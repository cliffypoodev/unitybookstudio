“Bullshit.” The word cracked out before he could stop it. He had played through worse. Freshman year, he took a hit that left his ankle purple for a month. Dad taped it up himself and sent him back in by halftime.

His uncle’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. “Language, Rylan.” He set the clipboard aside and leaned against the counter, arms crossed. His white coat was starched stiff. “You think the scouts will overlook a limp? The draft’s in six weeks.”

His stomach knotted. His uncle knew exactly where to press.

“What do you want?” The question hung between them, too loud in the sterile room.

His uncle’s thumb rubbed slow circles over the pen cap. “You’re not cleared yet. That’s all.”

The silence stretched. The AC kicked on, rattling the vent above them. He caught his own reflection in the glass-fronted cabinets—tense shoulders, jaw set. Quarterback posture even when he was sitting bare-legged on a fucking exam table.

His uncle’s hand landed on his thigh, just above the knee. Warm. Heavy. “Relax.”

He didn’t.

His fingers squeezed, deliberate. “I could reevaluate tomorrow. After… additional observation.”

The subtext coiled under his words, thick as the tape they would wrap around his ribs before the game. If there was a game.

His phone buzzed in his duffel by the door. Probably Dad, asking for an update. Always the team, always the win.

Dr. Shaw’s grip tightened. “Well?”

The clock ticked. The championship loomed. And his uncle’s eyes—dark, expectant—held the only answer that mattered now.

His uncle’s pace never faltered. Up. Down. Twist. His thighs shook. Shame burned hot under his skin, but worse—far worse—was the traitorous heat coiling low in his gut.

“Almost there,” his uncle replied softly. His wedding band caught the light as he worked him. “You’re doing so well.”

The praise shouldn’t have made his silence hitch. It did.

His uncle’s thumb pressed the slit. “Come for me.”

His stomach dropped.

The clock ticked. 11:13 PM.

The air in the room changed. It thickened, like the moments before a thunderstorm. His uncle’s hand stayed on him, a hot, heavy weight that had nothing to do with a medical exam. The paper gown was a joke now, a flimsy shred of dignity torn open down the middle. He stared at a crack in the ceiling tile, a fine black line that forked like a lightning bolt.

“Look at me.”

He didn’t want to. He looked.

Dr. Shaw’s face was a closed door. No anger, no lust, just a cold, surgical focus. The same expression he wore when he set a compound fracture on the field. “You understand this is the only way.”

It wasn’t a question. Rylan’s throat clicked when he stopped. He nodded.

“Verbal confirmation.”

“Yes.”

“Yes, what?”

The correction tasted like metal. “Yes, sir.”

His uncle’s other hand came up, cupped his jaw. The thumb smoothed over his cheekbone, a gesture almost paternal. It made his skin crawl. “Good. Now, get on your knees.”

The vinyl floor was cold and sticky under his bare knees. He focused on a scuff mark near the base of the cabinet, a black crescent of rubber from someone’s shoe. His own breathing sounded too loud in his ears. His uncle unbuckled his belt, the leather sliding free with a soft hiss. The zipper’s teeth separated. A sharp, clean smell of laundry detergent and something else, something musky and male, filled the space between them.

“Open them.” The voice was flat. “You watch. You understand what you’re doing.”

He forced his eyelids up. His vision blurred at the edges. His uncle took himself in hand, already hard. The sight was so foreign, so violently out of place in this room of gauze and ice packs, that his brain stuttered. This was a violation of physics. Of family. Of every fucking rule.

“Go on.”

He leaned forward. The first touch of his lips against the heated skin made his stomach twist. He gagged, pulled back.

His uncle’s hand fisted in his hair, not yanking, just holding. A steady, inescapable pressure. “Slowly. You’re an athlete. Control your chest rising.”

He tried again. His jaw ached with the strain of keeping it open. He took him in, inch by awful inch, until the head pressed against the back of his throat. His eyes watered. He said quietly through his nose, the antiseptic burn mixing with salt and sweat.

“Good.” The hand in his hair tightened in approval. “Now move.”

He did. A rhythm established itself, mechanical and grim. The wet sounds were obscene. He detached, a trick he’d learned somewhere in the back of his mind, a way to let his body work while his thoughts went somewhere else. The championship game. The playbook. The roar of the crowd. His father’s face in the stands, proud.

The hand guided his head setting a pace. His uncle’s stillness changed grew heavier. A low groan vibrated through him. He felt a jolt of something then—not pleasure, God no, but a sick, dark pride that he could do this, that he could make this happen, that even here he could perform. The thought disgusted him more than the act.

It ended abruptly. His uncle pulled him off by the hair, his grip unforgiving. He spilled onto Rylan’s face, hot and sudden. Some got in his eye, a burning sting. He blinked, tears clearing it.

“Clean it up.”

He used the torn edge of the gown, his movements clumsy. His mouth felt used, his jaw sore.

His uncle zipped his pants, buckled his belt. He picked up the clipboard, scanned the report. He didn’t look at Rylan. “Turn over. On the table. On your stomach.”

The second finger burned, a stretching, tearing sensation that had him digging his nails into the table’s edge. He thought of his knee, the phantom twinge. This pain was real, bright and specific.

“Almost there.” His uncle’s voice was a murmur now, almost gentle. The cruelty of that false comfort was worse than the roughness.

When he entered him, it wasn’t a push. It was a steady, steady invasion. Rylan gasped, the air punched from his lungs. He saw stars behind his eyelids, a constellation of white pinpricks against the dark. His uncle set a rhythm, deep and punishing, each thrust jarring his body against the table. The metal legs squeaked on the tile floor, a rhythmic, shameful counterpoint.

His own body betrayed him. A traitorous heat coiled low in his gut, a physical response divorced from his will. He hated it. He hated himself. He squeezed his eyes shut tighter, chasing the image of the field, the ball spiraling in a perfect arc, the cheers. But all he felt was the driving, claiming weight above him, the wet sound of skin on skin, and the cold, clear knowledge that his victory tomorrow was already a lie.

The stadium lights burned white-hot overhead. His knee held. Of course it held.

The trophy was heavier than he expected. Cold metal against his palms. The cameras flashed, the reporters shouted questions he answered on autopilot. How does it feel? What’s next? Any words for the scouts watching?

The locker room reeked of sweat, cheap body spray, and the sharp tang of adrenaline. Guys clapped him on the back, their voices too loud, their laughter too bright.
