The crypt air tasted of wet stone and something else, a sweetness gone sour. Myrrh, maybe, left to rot in a forgotten urn. Valerius Blackwood knelt on the flagstones, the chill leaching up through the wool of his trousers and into his bones. His grandfather stood before the oldest sarcophagus, a slab of black granite worn smooth by centuries of damp. The old man’s hands, parchment-thin and spotted, traced the carved family crest.

“The line ends with you,” his grandfather said. His voice was dry, a rustle of leaves in a sealed room. “No wife. No sons. The Blackwood name becomes dust.”

Valerius bowed his head. The accusation was old, a familiar weight. He had failed. Not in business, not in stewardship of the estate, but in this one primal duty. His blood was the last.

“There is a remedy,” the old man continued. He turned. His eyes, clouded with age, held a peculiar sharpness. “An oath. Buried in the earliest records. I found it.”

A scroll case lay on a stone pedestal. It was not ancient. The leather was cracked, but the stitching was modern, the brass cap dulled by fifty years of handling, not five hundred. Valerius noticed that. He noticed everything there. The way a single torch flickered in a draft that shouldn’t exist. The precise alignment of his knees on a specific worn patch of stone. Ritual was geography. He knew his place.

His grandfather unrolled the parchment. The script was not medieval Latin, but an awkward attempt at it, the letters too uniform, the ink too black. “In extremis sanguinis,” he read, the words rolling with a practiced reverence. “When the male line falters, the last son shall renew the pact with the patriarch. Through the flesh. Through the act of generation reversed. The patriarch’s breath shall not depart until the seed is returned to its source.”

Valerius stared at the torch shadow dancing on the wall. Generation reversed. The words sat in his stomach like a cold, heavy stone.

“It is a sacred duty,” his grandfather said. He let the scroll curl back into itself. “The family’s fortune, its very continuance, hinges on this. My life is tied to it. When my breath fails, so fails the Blackwood legacy. You must… receive me. You must harbor me. As a vessel harbors a relic.”

The silence after the words was absolute. The draft died. The torch guttered steady.

Valerius heard the meaning, not just the archaic phrasing. Receive. Harbor. The images came unbidden, crude and physical. His throat tightened. This was not prayer. This was not a chant over a relic. This was the body. The old body and his own.

“It is written?” Valerius asked. His own voice sounded strange, formal and hollow.

“It is law,” his grandfather said. He stepped closer. The smell of him changed—not just aged linen and camphor, but a keen, animal scent beneath. Anticipation. “Our ancestors demanded it. Their will is in these stones.”

Valerius looked at the sarcophagus. The crest was a tree, roots deep, branches bare. A motto carved below: Sanguis Unus. One Blood. He had lived by that. He had sacrificed his own desires for that. A wife would have been a distraction. Children would have diluted the line with another’s blood. He had kept himself pure, a vessel for the legacy. And now the legacy demanded this.

His grandfather’s hand came to rest on his shoulder. The touch was not comforting. It was proprietary. A claim.

“The ritual is precise,” the old man murmured. His fingers tightened. “It must be performed here, where the first Blackwood lies. It must be performed before dawn. My strength wanes with each hour. You can feel it, can you not? The air grows thinner.”

Valerius could feel it. Or he imagined he could. A tightening in the crypt, a suction on his breath. The torchlight seemed to dim.

“What must I do?” Valerius asked. He kept his eyes on the tree crest.

“You will remove your clothes. You will kneel, as you are now. You will open yourself to me.” The words were stripped of ceremony now, blunt. “You will take my essence into your body. You will hold it until my breath is secure. It is a transfer. A renewal.”

A knot formed in Valerius’s gut, hard and sour. He thought of the family portraits lining the manor stairs. Stern faces, proud faces. He thought of the land grants, the treaties, the wealth accumulated over eight centuries. All of it, balanced on this moment. On this act.

His grandfather’s other hand came to his chin, lifted his face. The old man’s eyes were hungry. Not for salvation, but for something else. Something that lurked behind the piety.

“You are the last son,” his grandfather whispered. “Your obedience is the last thread. Will you snap it?”

Valerius looked at the scroll case again. The modern stitching. The awkward script. A doubt, cold and needle-thin, pierced his devotion. But the weight of the stones, the weight of the name, pressed it down. Tradition was not truth. It was weight. And he had borne it all his life.

He stood. His movements were stiff, ceremonial. He unbuttoned his jacket, folded it, placed it on the stone pedestal beside the scroll. The air bit his skin. He removed his shirt, his trousers, his underclothes. The torchlight painted his body in long, wavering shadows. He knelt again on the flagstones, the cold now a sharp kiss against his bare knees.

His grandfather watched. A faint smile touched the old man’s lips. Not beatific. Triumphant.

“Good,” he breathed. “Now, turn. Present yourself to the ancestor. And to me.”

Valerius turned his back to the sarcophagus. He faced the darkness of the crypt’s deepest niche. He heard the rustle of his grandfather’s robes being shed. He heard the soft, labored breath coming closer.

A hand, dry and hot, settled on his hip.

“The oath binds us,” his grandfather said, close to his ear. “One blood.”

The words rang in the silent crypt. They sounded, to Valerius, less like a prayer and more like a decree.

The hand on his hip guided him forward, pressing his face toward the stone altar. Its surface was smooth, polished by something other than time. Valerius braced his palms against the cold edge. His breath fogged the dark granite.

“You understand the form,” his grandfather said from behind him. The rustle of fabric stopped. The old man’s bare body pressed against the back of his thighs. The skin was loose, papery, but the pressure was unyielding. “The patriarch gives. The son receives. Open your mouth.”

Valerius closed his eyes. He thought of the tree crest. Sanguis Unus. He parted his lips.

The old man’s fingers tangled in his hair, not gentle, and guided him down and back. Valerius met the soft, wiry hair of his grandfather’s groin. The smell was musky, sharp, utterly alien. His stomach clenched. The fingers tightened, pulling him forward until his mouth met soft, wrinkled flesh.

“Take it,” the old man commanded. His voice had shed its reverence. It was raw now, impatient.

Valerius obeyed. He opened wider, let the soft weight push past his lips. It filled his mouth, a taste like salt and age. He gagged, a reflex he couldn’t suppress.

His grandfather’s other hand slapped the back of his head, a sharp crack that echoed off the stones. “Do not resist. This is communion.”

Valerius forced his jaw slack. He focused on the chill of the stone beneath his palms, the grit against his knees. He breathed through his nose. The old man began to move, a shallow, rocking motion. Valerius’s throat worked against the intrusion. Tears pricked his eyes from the strain. He heard wet, obscene sounds inches from his own ears.

“Good,” his grandfather grunted. “Good. The vessel accepts the offering.”

The rhythm changed, grew deeper, more urgent. The hand in his hair became a vise. Valerius’s world narrowed to the stretch of his lips, the ache in his jaw, the overwhelming presence pushing down his throat. He choked. Spit dripped from his chin. His own body betrayed him with a traitorous, shameful heat coiling low in his gut. The violation was absolute, but his flesh responded to the crude dominance of it.

His grandfather pulled himself free with a wet sound. Valerius gasped, coughed, dragged in a lungful of crypt air that tasted of the act.

“Now the binding,” the old man rasped. He was breathing hard. He pushed Valerius’s torso down flat against the altar. The stone was freezing on his bare chest. Hands parted his legs. Something cold and slick—oil—drizzled down the cleft of his ass. He flinched.

“Be still.” A dry thumb pressed against him, insistent. “The pact is sealed in the flesh.”

Valerius buried his face against his own arm. He bit down on the wool of his folded jacket, still lying where he’d placed it. The irony was a bitter pill. The thumb pushed inside. It was a blunt, stretching burn. He hissed.

“It must be me,” his grandfather whispered, leaning over him. His breath was sour. “My essence. My seed. Into the source.”

The pressure changed. Thicker. Blunter. Valerius braced. He felt the old man’s weight settle over him, the brittle bones and sagging flesh. The push was slow, relentless. It was not pleasure. It was a tearing, a claiming. Valerius cried out, the sound muffled by the fabric in his teeth.

His grandfather sheathed himself fully with a low, shuddering groan. He stopped, buried inside him. Valerius felt every heartbeat, a frantic pulse in a place that should never feel such a thing. The pain was a bright, hot wire. The humiliation was a cold sea.

“One blood,” the old man chanted, beginning to move. Each thrust was a jagged shock. “One blood.”

Valerius clutched the edges of the altar. The stone was the only real thing. The rest was a nightmare of sensation—the brutal invasion, the slap of skin, his grandfather’s gasping breaths growing more ragged. The old man’s fingers dug into his hips, holding him open, using him. A part of Valerius detached. He watched from somewhere near the flickering torch as his own body was taken, as he was made a receptacle for this desperate, twisted ritual.

His grandfather’s movements grew frantic, stuttering. A final, deep thrust. A choked cry. Valerius felt a hot, sudden flood inside him. The old man collapsed onto his back, a dead weight, panting.

For a long moment, there was only the sound of their breathing. Then his grandfather rolled off him, sliding out of him with a sickening wetness. Valerius didn’t move. He lay on the altar, spent, hollowed out. The cold stone seeped into his bones.

His grandfather lay beside him on the crypt floor, staring up at the vaulted ceiling. His breath was a thin, reedy whistle. He turned his head. In the guttering torchlight, his eyes held no piety, no triumph. Only a weary, profound emptiness.

“The scroll,” he whispered, each word an effort. “I wrote it. Forty years ago. When I knew my own son… wouldn’t… continue the line.”

Valerius turned his head. He stared.

“No ancient oath,” the old man breathed, his voice fading. “Just… my own hunger. My own… fiction.” A last, rattling sigh escaped him. “Forgive me.”

The whistle of breath stopped. The silence that followed was different. It was just silence. Empty. The torch flickered once, twice, and went out.

Darkness was not absolute. A gray, watery light seeped down the stone stairwell from the world above. Dawn. The deadline had passed. The pact was sealed.

Valerius lay on the altar, the cold a second skin. The wetness between his legs was cooling, a viscous reminder. He could smell the oil, the musk, his own sweat. He could smell the sharp, coppery scent of blood. His. He pushed himself up, muscles protesting, a deep, bruised ache radiating from his core. He looked at his grandfather.

The old man stared at the ceiling, mouth agape, eyes clouded. The desperate hunger was gone. Only vacancy remained. The scroll case lay near his outstretched hand, a cheap, modern thing. A prop.

I wrote it.

The words echoed in the hollow of Valerius’s skull. He climbed off the altar, his legs unsteady. The flagstones were brutal on his bare feet. He found his clothes, folded neatly beside the lie. He dressed with mechanical precision. Each button was a mile. The wool of his trousers scraped against tender skin. He did not look at the body.

He picked up the scroll case. The brass cap was cold. He pulled the parchment free, unrolled it. In the weak dawn light, the awkward forgery was obvious. The uniform letters, the ink that hadn’t faded. It was a child’s idea of ancient. He had knelt for this. He had opened his body for this.

A sound escaped him. Not a laugh, not a sob. A dry heave of air.

He walked to the sarcophagus. The Blackwood crest. The tree. Sanguis Unus. He placed his palm flat on the carved roots. The stone drank his warmth. Eight centuries of weight. All of it, channeled down to this single, silent room. To a dying man’s lonely, ugly fantasy. His entire life—the discipline, the denial, the proud isolation—had been a runway built for this moment. A staging ground for a violation that served no god, no lineage. Only one man’s appetite.

He closed his hands into fists. The emptiness inside them was perfect.
