Chapter 3

The smell of old paper was a physical presence in the room, a dry, dusty odor that coated the back of Ethan’s throat. It mixed with the sharper, cleaner scent of antiseptic wipes from the lab annex, a smell that always made him think of mistakes being scrubbed away. He sat in the worn leather armchair that faced his uncle’s desk, a monolithic thing of dark oak that seemed to absorb the lamplight. His fingers traced a crack in the chair’s arm, a deep fissure in the hide that felt like a dry riverbed. He’d counted the tributaries branching off from it thirty-seven times since Professor Alistair Reed had told him to wait.

The clock on the mantelpiece ticked. Not a steady metronome beat, but a hesitant, sticky sound—Tick… silence… Tock… a longer silence. It was a quarter past eleven. The rest of the psychology department had emptied out hours ago, the building settling into a hollow, academic quiet.

Footsteps sounded in the hall, deliberate and unhurried. Ethan straightened, his spine pressing against the cracked leather. The door opened without a knock.

Alistair Reed carried the smell of the hall with him—cold stone and floor polish. He closed the door, the latch clicking with a soft, final sound. He didn’t go to his desk. He stood by the window, his back to the room, looking out at the darkened quad. He was a tall man, his frame still lean from the rowing he’d done decades ago at this very university. The grey at his temples was precise, distinguished. Everything about him was precise.

“Your literature review,” Alistair said, his voice a low, calm instrument. He didn’t turn. “It’s adequate.”

Adequate. The word landed in Ethan’s stomach like a stone. He’d spent six weeks on it. He’d bled into those pages. “Adequate” was the kiss of death.

“But,” Alistair continued, turning now. His glasses caught the lamplight, turning his eyes into two blank, reflective discs. “It lacks the necessary… empirical weight. The theoretical framework is sound. Elegant, even. But the committee, they want data. They want proof. Especially for a thesis challenging established attachment models with a somatic reciprocity theory.” He said the last part with a faint, dismissive wave of his hand, as if swatting at the ghosts of lesser minds. “Without novel data, it’s just philosophy. And the department does not grant doctorates in philosophy.”

“I’ve designed the twin studies,” Ethan said, his own voice sounding thin, reedy. A student’s voice. “The protocols are drafted. The ethics board submission is next week.”

“The ethics board.” Alistair’s mouth twitched, not quite a smile. He moved to his desk finally, not sitting, but leaning against its front edge, looming over Ethan’s seated form. He picked up a heavy, glass paperweight, turning it over in his hands. Inside, a fossilized fern was suspended, a delicate, dead thing. “A slow committee. Cautious. Your funding cycle ends in nine months. Do you think they will approve a study on the quantifiable, physiological resonance between dominant and submissive personalities in intimate, high-stress scenarios in time for you to gather, analyze, and write?” He set the paperweight down with a soft thud. “I do not.”

The air left the room. Or maybe it was just Ethan’s lungs failing. A cold numbness started in his fingertips, creeping up his wrists. “Then… what are you saying?”

“I’m saying the proof exists. The data set is… readily available.” Alistair’s gaze was unwavering now, the reflective glare gone, replaced by something dark and appraising. “The theory posits a measurable, somatic feedback loop. A transfer of autonomic control. The dominant personality, through specific, controlled stimuli can induce a verifiable physiological state in the submissive one—a state of hyper-awareness, of acute somatic compliance. The body, Ethan does not lie. Not like the mind. Not like a self-reported questionnaire.”

Ethan’s throat was parchment. He tried to swallow. “What stimuli?”

“The prescribed ones. In the methodology chapter you so carefully avoided writing.” Alistair leaned forward, just a little. The smell of his cologne, something with sandalwood cut through the dust. “Pain. Pleasure. The deliberate blurring of the two. It requires a participant. A subject. And an observer. A… measurer.”

“A human subject. For that?”

“This wouldn’t be for the board.” Alistair’s voice dropped became conspiratorial, intimate. “This would be for us. For the proof of concept. For your dissertation’s central argument. Once we have the data, the real data, the twin studies become a formality. A replication. The committee will see the preliminary findings, see the rigor, and the approval will be a rubber stamp.” He paused, letting the silence swell, filled only by the sticky clock. “You need this degree, Ethan. Your father… he asks about you. He wonders when you’ll stop being a permanent student. When you’ll produce something real.”

The mention of his father was a needle slipped between his ribs. It found the old, tender bruise of disappointment every time.

“I can’t… where would we even find someone to agree to that?” The question was a weak, desperate stall. He already knew the answer. He felt it in the way the room seemed to tilt, the shelves of books leaning in to listen.

Alistair didn’t blink. “We have a participant. One perfectly suited to the submissive profile. Intelligent enough to understand the process. Motivated enough to endure it.” He straightened up, looking down at Ethan. “You.”

The word was simple. Absolute. It didn’t feel like a question. It hung in the dusty air between them, a verdict.

“Me.” It wasn’t a question either. It was just a sound, a dry echo.

“The data requires authenticity. It requires a genuine power dynamic. Advisor. Student.” Alistair’s head tilted slightly. “Uncle. Nephew. The familial layer adds a necessary… thickness to the variables. A richer data set.” He finally moved to his chair, the leather sighing as he sat. He templed his fingers. “You provide the empirical evidence. I provide the analysis, the academic credibility. We publish. You graduate. A partnership.”

“And if I say no?” Ethan heard himself ask.

Alistair’s smile was thin, a paper cut. “Then your literature review remains… Adequate. And in nine months, you will have no data, no thesis, and no doctorate. You will have wasted six years of my time, and your father’s money. You will leave this university with a master’s degree and a theory nobody believes.” He opened a drawer, pulled out a thin file, slid it across the desk. It was Ethan’s funding application, the one Alistair had personally guaranteed. A large, red “REJECTED” stamp was slashed across the first page. It wasn’t from the university. It was from the private foundation Alistair chaired. “Your alternative path,” Alistair said softly, “just vanished.”

The crack in the leather under Ethan’s finger felt deeper now. A canyon. He stared at the red stamp. It looked like a wound. The clock ticked. Tock. Silence.

The examination table was cold through the thin cotton of the gown. It wasn’t a hospital gown, but something worse—a faded blue smock, the kind painters wore, open at the back. The starch in the fabric chafed the skin of Ethan’s shoulders. He stared at a water stain on the ceiling above him, a brownish bloom with tendrils like a dead neuron. The antiseptic smell was stronger here, undercut with the faint, sweet-metallic tang of the conductive gel Alistair had applied to his temples, his chest, the inside of his wrists. Wires snaked away from the adhesive pads, coiling toward a silent monitor on a wheeled cart.

“Baseline autonomic readings are stable,” Alistair said, his voice a dry murmur from somewhere near Ethan’s feet. He wasn’t looking at Ethan. He was looking at the graph on the screen, a green line crawling across a black field. “Elevated heart rate, of course. Elevated galvanic skin response. That’s the anxiety. We’ll account for it as noise.”

Ethan breathed in. The air tasted of dust and his own sour fear. He’d agreed. That was the worst part, the thing his mind kept circling back to like a tongue probing a cracked tooth. He had nodded, his throat too tight to speak, when Alistair laid out the parameters. The first session. A mapping of somatic response to controlled, aversive stimuli. To establish the lower threshold. The words were a clean, academic carapace over the rotten thing underneath.

“Procedure commences,” Alistair announced, as if speaking to a room full of observers. There was only the sticky clock, the silent books, the stain on the ceiling.

A hand, gloved in thin latex settled on his bare thigh. Ethan flinched, a full-body jerk that made the wires pull. The green line on the monitor spiked.

“Don’t,” Alistair said, not unkindly. A scientist chiding his instrument. “You must remain passive. Observation requires a still subject. Try to disassociate. Observe the sensations as data points. A pressure. A temperature. A localized nervous system response.”

The hand slid higher. The touch was clinical, exploratory. It wasn’t a caress. It was a palpation. Ethan squeezed his eyes shut. He tried to think of the theory, the beautiful, clean structure of it. The somatic feedback loop. The transfer of autonomic control. It was supposed to be about paired heart rates, synchronized breathing in dyads under stress. Not this. The latex fingers traced the crease of his thigh moved inward. His own body betrayed him, a traitorous, stupid animal—a flush of heat, a tightening in his gut that was not entirely revulsion. Shame followed, immediate and scalding.

“Interesting,” Alistair murmured. The green line jumped again. “Physiological arousal, concurrent with the spike in galvanic response. A conflict state. Precisely what the model predicts.”

The touch became more specific. Purposeful. Ethan bit the inside of his cheek until he tasted copper. The pain was an anchor, a fixed point in the swimming dread. He focused on it—the bright, clean sting. It was his. Not given, not part of the data set.

“Vocalization may be permitted,” Alistair said. His other hand came to rest on Ethan’s hip, holding him still. “It can be a useful metric. A measure of threshold breach.”

Ethan didn’t speak. He breathed in ragged hitches through his nose. The smock was rucked up around his waist now. The air in the room was cool on his exposed skin. The touch moved, intimate and utterly detached. It was the detachment that unmoored him. This wasn’t passion, or desire, or even cruelty for its own sake. It was methodology. He was a source of readings.

A sound escaped him then, a choked-off thing, when the probing became intrusion. His hands, lying limp at his sides, clenched into fists. The monitor beeped, a soft, polite sound.

“There,” Alistair said, a note of satisfaction in his voice. He wasn’t looking at Ethan’s face. He was watching the screen, where the green line had become a jagged mountain range. “The autonomic shift. See the cascade? Respiration, heart rate, dermal conductivity. A perfect storm.” He leaned closer, his breath a puff of coffee and mint against Ethan’s ear. “The body is so honest, Ethan. It tells me everything your mind is trying to hide.”

The act that followed was a slow, precise violation. It was measured in the hiss of the lubricant bottle, in the careful, almost thoughtful adjustments of angle and depth. Alistair narrated it quietly, like a man reading a protocol. “Increased resistance here. Note the corresponding tremor in the lower abdomen. Fascinating.” Ethan floated outside himself. He watched the stain on the ceiling. It did look like a neuron. Or a dead tree. He counted the main branches. Seven. One for each year of graduate school. A stupid, pointless thought. It was the only one he could hold onto.

It didn’t last long. It felt like forever. When it was over, Alistair withdrew, disposing of the glove with a soft snap. He made a note on a clipboard, then turned to the sink in the corner washing his hands with the thorough, surgical scrubbing of a man who had just completed a messy but necessary task.

“Session one concluded,” he said, drying his hands on a clean towel. He finally looked at Ethan, who hadn’t moved. “The data is promising. Very promising. We’ll reconvene Thursday. Same time.” He walked back to his desk sat, and opened a journal as if nothing had happened.

Ethan lay on the cold table, the wires still stuck to his skin, the smock damp and clinging. The green line on the monitor had settled back into a slow, steady crawl. A flatline of the mundane. He listened to the clock. Tick. Silence. Tock. A longer silence. He would have to get up. He would have to remove the sensors, get dressed, walk out of this room. He would have to do it again on Thursday. The theory, he realized with a clarity that was like falling through ice was just a story. A beautiful, logical story they had built a cage with. The data was the point. The taking of it was the point. The rest was just a lie they told so the numbers would feel like science.

The library archives smelled of wet wool and forgotten glue. Ethan sat at a carrel in the far corner, a fortress of silence built from towering shelves of bound periodicals. The single lamp cast a yellow circle over the open journal, the text swimming before his eyes. He’d been staring at the same paragraph for twenty minutes. His body felt hollowed out, a brittle shell. A specific, dull ache radiated from his lower back, a souvenir. His mouth still held the faint, chemical aftertaste of the antiseptic wipes Alistair kept in the lab annex.

He wasn’t reading. He was counting the lies.

The theory was Alistair’s pet, a radical re-framing of somatic reciprocity in dominant-submissive dyads. It posited a measurable, physiological synchronization—a quantifiable “entrainment” of autonomic responses under controlled stress. Ethan had built his entire thesis proposal around it. He’d defended its elegance in seminar, cited the preliminary studies, argued for its potential to revolutionize therapeutic approaches to trauma.

The journal in front of him was from 1998. Journal of Behavioral Psychophysiology. The article was a dry meticulous dismantling of a nearly identical hypothesis from two decades prior. The methodology was almost the same. The predicted outcomes were the same. The author had attempted to replicate the findings and failed. Spectacularly. The data showed no significant correlation. The conclusion was a single, damning sentence: “The proposed somatic feedback loop appears to be a theoretical construct unsupported by empirical evidence, and may be better understood as a post-hoc narrative imposed upon observed power dynamics.”

Ethan’s own proposal, word for word in sections was a ghost of this dead idea.

He hadn’t found it by accident. He’d come looking for it, a sick, compulsive drive that had started the moment he’d left Alistair’s study hours ago. He’d walked through the cold quad, his skin feeling two sizes too small, and instead of going home to his empty apartment, he’d turned toward the library. He needed to see it. To touch the proof of his own gullibility.

Alistair knew. Of course he knew. He’d curated Ethan’s reading lists, steered him away from certain databases, praised his “innovative synthesis” of the existing literature. He’d built a box of approved texts and told Ethan he was thinking outside of it. All the while, this journal, this specific volume had been sitting here. Waiting.

It wasn’t about the data. It never was.

The realization didn’t arrive as a shock. It was a slow, cold seep, like groundwater finding the cracks in a foundation. The “protocols,” the “measurements,” the latex gloves and the heart rate monitor stuck to his chest, the clinical detachment in Alistair’s voice as he gave instructions—it was all theater. An elaborate set dressing for something else. The theory was the bait. The degree was the leash. And he was the experiment, but not the one he’d signed up for.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and sharp. His uncle’s hand, steady and cool, adjusting the sensor on his ribs. The smell of sandalwood and dust. The look in Alistair’s eyes hadn’t been scientific curiosity. It had been hunger. A collector’s appraisal.

Ethan closed the journal. The sound was a soft, final thump in the silent archive. Outside the high, narrow windows, the sky was shifting from black to a deep, bruised grey. Dawn wasn’t far off. The deadline for his dissertation proposal was in nine days.

He could walk away. He could take this journal to the department head. He could burn the whole thing down.

His father’s voice, from their last strained phone call, slithered through the hollow space inside him. “A permanent student, Ethan. When are you going to produce something real?”

He looked at his hands, pale under the yellow light. They were clean. They shook, just a little. He thought of the degree. Three letters after his name. A door that would open. Validation. A thing he could hold up and say, See? I did it. I am not a fraud.

Even if he was.

He pulled his own notebook closer. He opened it to a fresh page. He picked up his pen—a cheap, plastic thing, the university logo faded from his grip.

He began to write. Preliminary Findings: Subject E. V. Session One.

The words were ash in his mouth. They meant nothing. They were the currency of the lie. He wrote them anyway. The pen scratched across the paper, a sound like insects chewing through dry wood. He wrote until the ache in his back was just a fact, and the taste in his mouth was just memory, and the thing he was building on the page looked, to anyone who didn’t know, exactly like science.
