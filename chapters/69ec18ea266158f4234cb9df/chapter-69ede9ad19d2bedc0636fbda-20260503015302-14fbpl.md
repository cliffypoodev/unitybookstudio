The air in the library tasted like dust and regret. Not just any dust—the kind that had settled on gilt-framed portraits and first editions no one had opened in fifty years. Finlay stood just inside the double doors, his scuffed boots on a Persian rug worth more than his studio’s annual rent. The scent hit him first: old paper, lemon oil, and beneath it, the ghost of his grandfather’s cologne, something spicy and expensive that had always smelled like money.

A grandfather clock ticked in the corner, each second a fat, deliberate drop of water into a silent pool. He counted them. Seven. Eight. His throat was tight.

“You’re late.”

The voice came from the high-backed leather chair facing the fireplace, cold and dry as the pages of the legal tome open on the desk beside it. Silas Rhodes didn’t turn. Finlay could only see a liver-spotted hand resting on the chair’s wing, the fingers long and unnervingly steady.

“The train was—”

“I didn’t ask for an excuse. Come where I can see you.

Finlay walked. The rug swallowed his footsteps. He focused on a water stain on the ceiling above the mantel, a brownish bloom shaped vaguely like a screaming face. His mind, always doing that—finding the art in the decay, even now. Especially now.

He stopped before the hearth. The fire was gas, fake logs glowing with perfect, cheerless orange. His grandfather finally turned his head.

Silas looked like a waxwork of himself. The famous Rhodes jaw had softened into jowls, the sharp eyes now sunken and filmed, but the intelligence in them was a cold, hard stone. He wore a burgundy smoking jacket. The velvet was worn at the cuffs.

“You look like a vagrant,” Silas said. No greeting. No ‘how have you been.

“It’s called having a job,” Finlay said. The sarcasm was automatic, a defense he’d honed since childhood. “Paint doesn’t come out of everything.

“A job.” Silas let the word hang. “Splashing color on canvas for rich dilettantes who wouldn’t know art from a hole in the ground. You call that a life?

“I call it mine.”

“And that’s the problem.” Silas shifted, a rustle of fabric. He gestured to the room with a slow, encompassing sweep of his hand. “All of this. The trust. The capital. The legacy. It isn’t yours. Not yet. Perhaps not ever.

Here it was. The hook. Finlay’s stomach did a slow, sick roll. He’d known this wasn’t a social call. The lawyer’s letter had been clear: Your presence is required to discuss the terms of succession. But the tone in the room—thick, performative, vaguely obscene—was something else.

“What do you want?” Finlay asked. His voice sounded smaller than he’d intended.

“I am dying.” Silas said it like he was commenting on the weather. “The doctors give me six months. Perhaps eight. The machinery is shutting down. I find I am… concerned with what remains. With what is worthy of remaining.

He picked up a small, ornate box from the side table. It was lacquered, black with mother-of-pearl inlay. He didn’t open it. Just held it, his thumb stroking the surface.

“The money, the properties, the collections—they are not a gift. They are a test. A final examination of character.” Silas’s eyes locked on Finlay’s. The firelight caught in them, two points of flat, reflected light. “You wish for freedom? For independence? To play the artist without the grubby need for patronage? I can give you that. A sum that would make your ‘job’ obsolete. A foundation that would fund your little rebellions for a lifetime.

Finlay’s mouth was dry. He could taste the dust again, at the back of his tongue. He wanted to spit.

“And the exam?” The question felt like pulling a tooth.

A smile touched Silas’s thin lips. It didn’t reach his eyes. “A series of evaluations. To prove you have the necessary… flexibility. The aesthetic sensibility. The strength of will to be a Rhodes. To hold what we have built.

“What kind of evaluations?”

“The kind I devise.” Silas placed the box back on the table with a soft, final click. “The first is simple. Look at this room. Tell me what is wrong with it.

Finlay blinked. He glanced around—the towering bookshelves, the heavy drapes, the marble bust of some forgotten ancestor on a plinth. It was a museum diorama. A mausoleum.

“It’s sterile,” he said, the artist in him overriding caution. “It’s a set. Everything is placed for effect, not for use. That chair isn’t for sitting, it’s for being looked at. That fire isn’t for heat. It’s all a performance.

Silas was silent for three ticks of the clock. Then he gave a slow, approving nod. “Good. You see the surface. The first test is passed.

Finlay didn’t feel relief. He felt a cold trickle between his shoulder blades.

“The next tests,” Silas continued, his voice dropping to a confidential murmur, “will not be about looking. They will be about doing. About embodying a certain… perspective. They will require a surrender of your modern sensibilities. A submission to a more classical understanding of form, and function, and legacy.

He picked up a small silver bell and rang it. The sound was shockingly bright in the muffled room. A moment later, a door hidden in the wood paneling opened, and a man in a dark suit entered. He was older, maybe sixty, with a composed, impassive face. He didn’t look at Finlay.

“Mr. Shaw will show you to your rooms,” Silas said, turning his chair back toward the false fire. “We begin tomorrow after breakfast. Do not be late.

The dismissal was absolute. Finlay stood there for another second, the screaming face on the ceiling his only witness. Then he turned and followed the silent man out, the weight of the unasked questions heavy as a cloak on his shoulders. What, exactly, did a classical understanding of legacy require you to do?

The door clicked shut behind him, sealing him into the hall. The air there smelled different—beeswax and something faintly floral, like dying lilies.

Silas’s bedroom was a cave of velvet and memory. The curtains, heavy damask, were drawn against the afternoon, leaving the air thick and amber-colored. Dust motes swam in the single shaft of light that escaped a pulled seam. Finlay’s eyes fixed on them. They were easier to watch than the bed.

The four-poster was a dark wood monstrosity, hung with faded burgundy drapes. Silas sat propped against a mountain of pillows, the burgundy smoking jacket gone. He wore a silk pajama shirt, open at the throat, the skin there thin and mapped with blue veins. The ornate lacquer box sat open on the nightstand. Inside, on a bed of black velvet, lay a single glass vial of clear oil.

“The second test,” Silas said. His voice was a dry rustle in the quiet. “Is about touch. About the artist’s hand. A true understanding of form requires… intimacy with the material.

Finlay’s tongue felt like it was coated in the same dust that filmed every surface. A cracked porcelain shepherdess smiled vacantly from the mantel. He counted the curls in her painted hair. Seven.

“You want me to paint you?” The question was stupid. He knew it was stupid.

Silas’s laugh was a short, airless sound. “No, boy. I am beyond portraiture. I want you to feel. To prove your commitment is not theoretical. That you will soil your hands, if the legacy demands it.

He gestured, a slow, deliberate motion toward the vial. “Bring it here.

The walk across the room was ten miles. The floorboards creaked a different protest under each step. Finlay picked up the vial. It was warm from the room. He unscrewed the cap. The smell was faint, herbal, almost like almonds.

“Your hands are steady,” Silas observed. “Good. Now. Apply it to me.

Finlay’s breath hitched. His mind split. One part was screaming, a white noise of revulsion. The other part was already calculating, the artist observing the assignment: the pallid skin of his grandfather’s chest, the sparse white hairs, the way the light from the seam in the curtain caught the ridge of a collarbone. A still life of decay.

“Where?” The word was a croak.

“Where your intuition tells you,” Silas murmured, his eyes half-lidded. “A test of aesthetic sensibility, remember? Show me you understand the focal point. The… vulnerability of the composition.

Something hot and sour moved in Finlay’s gut. Not quite nausea. Not quite rage. A sickening hybrid with no name. He poured a little oil into his palm. It was slick, cool.

He reached out. His fingers trembled. He made them stop by force, pressing his fingertips to the center of Silas’s chest. The skin was cool, papery. He rubbed the oil in, a small, circular motion. The scent of almonds rose between them, cloying.

“Deeper,” Silas whispered. “The muscle is beneath. Feel the structure.

Finlay pressed harder. He felt the unyielding bone of the sternum, the wasted flesh over it. His grandfather’s breath was shallow, rhythmic. A clock counting down.

“Lower.”

The command was soft. Absolute.

Finlay’s hand moved down the ridged plane of the abdomen. The silk pajama bottoms were loose. The waistband was just there. His knuckles brushed it.

“The legacy requires more than a superficial touch, Finlay. It requires a full… engagement. A surrender to the process.” Silas’s hand, surprisingly strong, closed over Finlay’s wrist. He guided him lower, beneath the silk. “Prove you want it. Prove you are a Rhodes.

The world narrowed to the feel of coarse hair, soft skin, the frail, shocking heat of another body. Finlay’s own breath came in short, sharp gulps. He was outside himself, watching his hand move, obeying the old man’s soft directives. This was the cost. This was the grotesque brushstroke required to buy the canvas. The oil made everything slick, soundless. His grandfather made a small, choked sound that wasn’t pain.

Finlay focused on the shepherdess. On the seventh curl. On a water stain on the ceiling in the far corner, a new one, shaped like a bird in flight. Anything but the heat in his hand, the terrible, rising proof of his grandfather’s arousal, the wet pearl of fluid that welled up under his circling thumb. The intimacy was a violation, but the performance was perfect. He was giving the performance of a lifetime, and the audience of one was shuddering toward its climax with closed eyes and a thin, satisfied smile.

He understood now. This wasn’t about worthiness. This was curation. Silas was adding a final, living piece to his collection.

The light in the breakfast room was a liar. It streamed through the tall, leaded windows, cutting through the dust motes that swirled like plankton in a dead sea. Everything looked cheap in that morning glare—the gold leaf on the picture frames was flaking, the velvet on the chairs thin and bald in patches. The smell of last night’s cold fireplace and old perfume had soured into something closer to a museum’s storage closet.

Finlay sat at the long mahogany table. A single place setting: white china, a silver spoon, a crystal glass of water. His body felt like a borrowed suit. A dull ache persisted low in his back, a souvenir. He kept his hands flat on the cool wood, watching the slight tremor in his left thumb. It wouldn’t stop.

The lawyer arrived at eight-fifteen. Not Silas’s man, but a younger one with a trim beard and a tablet instead of a briefcase. He smelled of mint and ambition.

Finlay didn’t touch the envelope. “Where is he?

“Your grandfather passed at four-seventeen this morning. Peacefully. In his sleep.” The lawyer’s voice was a practiced, respectful monotone. “His instructions were explicit. You were not to be disturbed. The bequest was to be delivered upon confirmation.

Peacefully. Finlay’s stomach turned. He saw the old man’s face in the firelight, the avid, hungry look in his milky eyes during the final test. The whispered command. Again. Slower. Let me see your father in you.

“There’s a letter,” the lawyer said. He produced a smaller, sealed envelope from his jacket pocket. Vellum, handwritten address. “A personal addendum. Not a legal instrument.

He left then, his polished shoes clicking on the parquet, the sound fading down the hall. Finlay was alone with the liar’s light and the envelope.

He drank the water first. It tasted of minerals and old pipes. Finally, he broke the wax seal—a stylized ‘R’—and pulled out a single sheet of the same heavy paper.

The handwriting was spidery, the ink a faded blue.

Finlay,

You performed admirably. Better than I dared hope. The vulnerability you showed in the third test—the genuine shock—was particularly convincing. Artistry, indeed.

You will no doubt believe this was about power. Or cruelty. It was not. It was about preservation.

The man you met in the city last autumn, the critic with the kind eyes who praised your use of ochre? His grandfather was mine. The winter of 1959. The boathouse. The tests you endured were not of my invention. They were my memories. My script. I was, for a time, a rather talented actor in such scenes. Life, however, demands a different performance.

I have paid you handsomely to be my understudy. To give flesh to the ghost. To let me watch the play one last time from the best seat in the house. The money is not for your worth. It is for my nostalgia. A final, extravagant purchase.

Do not squander it on guilt. Spend it on paint. Make something ugly and true. It is the one thing our bloodline has never had the courage to do.

Yours in legacy,
Silas

Finlay read it twice. The tremor in his thumb spread to his whole hand. A fly buzzed against the windowpane, a frantic, tiny drone. He looked at the water stain on the ceiling from a long-ago leak, now a permanent brown continent on the white plaster.

He picked up the thick envelope. He didn’t open it. He just held its weight.

He was free. He was rich. He could burn the studio and build a new one. He could tell every gallery and critic to go fuck themselves.

He sat there as the sun climbed higher, the light shifting from a liar’s gold to a flat, honest white. The envelope grew warm in his hand. He was an heir. He was a paid actor. He was a living museum for a dead man’s secret. He was all of it, and the money in his hand felt like the only real thing in the room.

He got up. He left the letter on the table, the empty glass, the spoon. He walked out of the breakfast room, down the hall, past the silent library. He didn’t look back. The envelope was a brick in his pocket. His footsteps on the marble floor of the grand foyer echoed, hollow and final, like applause for a curtain call he never meant to take.
