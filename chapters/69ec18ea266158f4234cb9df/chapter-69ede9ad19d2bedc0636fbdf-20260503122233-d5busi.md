Chapter 12

The atrium of the Whitlock Museum of History was a mausoleum of polished sound. Caspian’s footsteps clicked against the marble in a rhythm too loud, too present, for a place dedicated to the past. The air held the museum’s universal chill—a dry, preservative cold that seeped through his wool coat and settled against his skin. It smelled of lemon oil and dust, the lemon oil losing.

He looked up, as he always did, at the statue of Josiah Whitlock. The marble patriarch stood ten feet tall on his plinth, one hand resting on a plowshare, the other clutching a scroll meant to symbolize the town charter. The sculptor had given him a jawline you could break rocks on and eyes that gazed nobly into a future only he could see. A complete fabrication, Caspian thought, not for the first time. The man had probably had a weak chin and gout.

“Still communing with the family saint?”

The voice came from the shadow of the east colonnade. Uncle Alistair emerged, a thinner, greyer echo of the statue above. He wore a curator’s dark suit, impeccably tailored, but the fabric around the elbows shone with a faint, permanent sheen from leaning on display cases. He held a ring of old-fashioned brass keys, which he turned over in his palm with a soft metallic chime.

“I’m fact-checking his shoulder epaulets,” Caspian said. “The portrait from 1783 shows simpler trim.”

“Always the details.” Alistair’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. They were the same pale blue as Caspian’s, but where Caspian’s felt like they absorbed light, Alistair’s reflected it, hard and cool. “The archive arrives tomorrow.”

A jolt, hot and sharp went through Caspian’s gut. He’d been petitioning the board for access to the sealed Whitlock family archive for eighteen months. The cornerstone of his biography. The supposed cache of Josiah’s personal correspondence, his war diaries, the unvarnished truth behind the founding myth. “The board relented?”

“The board,” Alistair said, sliding the keys into his pocket, “has delegated custodial discretion to me. The archive is fragile. Unique. It requires… Supervised handling. Outside of public hours.”

The way he said it. Supervised. The word hung in the cold air between them, acquiring weight. Caspian’s throat tightened. He noticed a water stain on the marble floor near the base of a potted fern, shaped vaguely like Italy. He focused on it. “I can follow preservation protocols. You know my work.”

“I know your obsession.” Alistair took a step closer. The smell of his cologne, something with sandalwood and a bitter edge cut through the lemon oil. “This isn’t just papers, Caspian. It’s the family spine. You want to examine the vertebrae. To see if they’re cracked. That kind of examination… isn’t free.”

“My publisher has a research budget. I can—”

“Not that kind of price.” Alistair’s voice dropped, blending with the distant hum of the climate system. “The access is personal. Between you and me. A private viewing. It requires a certain… reciprocity.”

Caspian’s mouth went dry. He knew. A knowledge that arrived not as a thought but as a physical collapse, a hollowing out behind his ribs. He’d heard the whispers, of course, about Alistair’s tastes, his discreet companionships with young men from the university. Dismissed them as gossip. Now the whispers had shape, and temperature, and were standing two feet away, waiting.

“What are you saying?” The question was ash in his mouth.

“I’m saying the vault opens at ten o’clock tonight. Just us. You’ll get your truth.” Alistair’s gaze traveled over him, slow, appraising. Not as an uncle. As a curator assessing an artifact. “And I will get a demonstration of your commitment. To the family. To its history. Consider it a… hands-on tutorial in what it really takes to preserve a legacy.”

The statue of Josiah seemed to loom larger, the marble eyes now pointing directly at the crown of Caspian’s head. The hero. The fraud. The truth sat locked in a vault below their feet, and the only key was in his uncle’s pocket, next to the warm metal of those brass keys.

“Why?” Caspian heard himself ask.

Alistair’s smile finally touched his eyes, turning them icy. “Because nothing of value is given. It’s always traded. You of all people should understand that. History is a transaction.” He turned, his heels echoing. “Ten o’clock. Use the staff entrance. Come alone.”

He disappeared back into the colonnade’s shadow, leaving Caspian standing under the empty gaze of his ancestor. The cold of the atrium was inside him now, a core sample of ice. He stared at the water stain on the floor. Italy. A boot. A Rorschach blot of damp in a dead, dry place.

The staff entrance was an unmarked steel door at the end of a service corridor that smelled of floor wax and stale mop water. Caspian stood before it at five minutes to ten, the cold from the marble floors leaching up through the soles of his shoes. He’d changed into a fresh button-down, a pointless gesture of normalcy that now felt absurd, like polishing his shoes before an execution. The door buzzed, a sharp, insectile sound, and he pushed it open.

The corridor beyond was utilitarian, lit by humming fluorescent tubes that cast a sickly green pallor. It led to a freight elevator. The descent was slow, the machinery groaning like something in pain. When the doors opened, the air changed. It was colder, drier, a vacuum-sealed chill that made his sinuses ache. The smell was different too—not dust, but the sharp, clean scent of industrial dehumidifiers and the faint, sweetish tang of decaying paper.

The vault door was a massive, circular slab of brushed steel, like a bank safe from a different century. Alistair stood beside it, already in the vault’s antechamber. He wore a different suit, a charcoal grey that absorbed the low light. He held a single acid-free box, about the size of a shoebox, in his white-gloved hands.

“Punctual,” Alistair said. He said it, flat, absorbed by the soundproofed walls. “Come in. The environment is delicate. Close the inner door behind you.”

Caspian stepped through. The inner door was thick, with a heavy rubber seal. It shut with a soft, definitive thump that echoed in the silence of the room. The vault was smaller than he’d imagined—maybe twelve by twelve, lined with steel shelving units holding identical grey boxes. The only light came from a single LED panel in the ceiling, casting a stark, shadowless illumination. A single steel table and two chairs stood in the center. On the table rested a pair of white cotton gloves.

“The Whitlock correspondence, 1775 through 1781,” Alistair said, placing the box he carried on the table with a soft thud. “Josiah’s hand. Unredacted.”

Caspian’s silence caught. He took a step toward the table, his hand lifting almost of its own accord. The truth was right there, inches away, under a simple cardboard lid.

“The gloves,” Alistair said, nodding to the table. “But first.”

Caspian froze. His uncle’s gaze was on him, steady and expectant. The air hummed.

“First,” Alistair repeated, “we establish the terms of the transaction. A show of good faith. Your shirt, please.”

The words hung in the sterile air. They were so mundane, so ridiculous, and so utterly final. Caspian’s fingers went numb. He looked at the box. He looked at his uncle’s impassive face. The clock on the wall, a plain digital readout, glowed 22:01.

“You can’t be serious,” Caspian said. The words sounded childish.

Alistair didn’t answer. He just waited.

Caspian’s hands moved to his buttons. His fingers felt thick, stupid. The first button slipped free. Then the next. The sound of the cotton sliding through the buttonholes was obscenely loud. The cold of the vault kissed his chest as he opened the shirt, a shock that raised gooseflesh. He shrugged it off his shoulders, let it fall to the back of one chair. The air was a physical presence on his skin, raising the fine hairs on his arms. He felt a ridiculous urge to cover himself, like a patient in a doctor’s office.

Alistair’s eyes tracked over him, clinical. “Good. Now come here.”

Caspian didn’t move. His feet were bolted to the floor.

“The box stays closed until you do.” Alistair’s voice was still calm, a lecturer’s tone. “Every minute you hesitate is a minute you are not reading. The clock is running, Caspian. History won’t wait.”

He took a step. Then another. The floor was cold under his socked feet. He stopped an arm’s length away, the smell of his uncle’s cologne now mingling with the scent of old paper.

Alistair’s gloved hand came up, not to touch him, but to gesture downward. A simple, clear command.

The floor was grey linoleum, speckled with white. Caspian noticed a single, tiny black fleck near his left foot, a flaw in the pattern. He focused on it. His knees gave way, not a controlled descent but a collapse, the impact jarring his bones. The cold of the floor bit through his trousers. He stared at the black fleck. It looked like a misplaced period.

A hand settled on the back of his head, not rough, but inexorable. The leather of the glove was cool and smooth. The pressure increased, guiding him forward. He closed his eyes. The world narrowed to the scent of wool and starch, the hum of the climate control, and the terrifying, intimate proximity. He resisted for a second, a stiffening of his neck, and the pressure firmed.

“The truth has a cost, nephew,” Alistair said quietly from above him. “You’ve always known that. Now pay it.”

The pressure became a command he could not disobey. Not if he wanted what was in the box. Not if he wanted to know. His own need was the trap, sprung tight. He stopped fighting it. He let himself be moved. The first touch of warm skin against his lips was a violation so complete it emptied his mind of everything except a single, screaming fact: this was the price, and he was now paying it.

The cold lingered inside him, a deep settled thing, like a fossil in his marrow. Alistair had left, the heavy door sighing shut behind him, the click of the lock a final, soft punctuation. The silence in the vault was different now. Not the quiet of preservation, but the hollow after a storm.

Caspian stood by the conservation table, his trousers loose around his hips, the fabric rough against skin that felt scraped raw. The air tasted of leather and his own sour air. He focused on the neat stack of documents Alistair had pointed to before he left, his uncle’s fingers brushing the brittle paper with a tenderness he’d never shown his nephew.

The truth.

He pulled his clothes back into order with clumsy hands. The buttons on his shirt were a foreign language, his fingers thick and stupid. He finally got them, his gaze fixed on the papers. A single, dusty beam from a recessed light fell across the table, illuminating motes of ancient dust that danced like static.

He sat. The leather of the chair was cold through his trousers. He pulled on the white cotton gloves left for him, the ritual absurd now, a pantomime of propriety. The first document was a letter, the ink faded to a rusty brown. Josiah Whitlock’s hand was a tight, anxious scrawl, not the bold script of the museum’s facsimiles.

My Dearest Eleanor,

The rumors are true. I did not stand with the militia at the ridge. When the shots were fired, I was in the root cellar of the Pearson farm, my hands over my ears like a child. I could hear the screams through the earth. I soiled myself. The shame is a live coal in my gut. Pearson has agreed to say I was scouting the flank, for a price. We must sell the north field.

Caspian’s pause left him in a slow leak. He turned the page. A bill of sale for the north field, dated the week after the battle. A sworn affidavit from one Ezekiel Pearson, attesting to Josiah’s “valiant reconnaissance,” notarized for a fee of three pounds. Another letter, this one from Josiah to his brother: The monument is a folly. Do not build it. Let them forget me.

But they hadn’t. They’d built the statue. They’d written the schoolbooks. They’d hung his portrait in the damned atrium.

He read on. A military report, buried, listing Josiah Whitlock as absent without leave. A diary entry from a lieutenant, complaining of “the cowardice of certain gentlemen farmers who talk of liberty but hide from its cost.” Each document was a precise, surgical cut, severing the myth from the man. The hero was a fraud. The founder was a liar who bought his legacy with a field and a forger’s fee.

Caspian stared at the date. Twenty years. His uncle had known. He had always known.

A sound escaped him, not a laugh, not a sob. Something dry and broken, like a twig snapping underfoot. He looked at his gloved hands, pale and alien under the light. He had paid for this. He had opened himself, let his uncle’s cold methodical possession be the key to this box of lies. The transaction was complete. History was a transaction, Alistair had said.

He had his truth. It was worthless. It was ash.

He stood, the chair scraping on the stone floor with a shriek that made him flinch. He left the documents there, spread under the light. Let them rot. He walked to the vault door, his body moving with a strange, detached stiffness, as if he were piloting a machine that was starting to fail. The door was heavy. He leaned into it, the cool steel against his shoulder, and pushed.

The corridor outside was dark, the emergency lights casting long, monstrous shadows. He didn’t look back. He walked past the empty exhibits, the glass cases holding artifacts of a story that never happened. The grand atrium was grey with the first weak light of dawn filtering through the high windows. The statue of Josiah was a slab of featureless shadow now, the noble gaze lost in the gloom.

Caspian pushed through the staff door and out into the morning. The air was damp and cold, smelling of wet pavement and distant exhaust. A delivery truck rumbled past, its headlights cutting through the mist. He stood on the steps, the keys to his own apartment a cold weight in his pocket. He had nowhere to go. The past was a lie. The future was a blank, white page. He was a man built on a fault line, and the ground had just given way, leaving him standing over nothing at all.
