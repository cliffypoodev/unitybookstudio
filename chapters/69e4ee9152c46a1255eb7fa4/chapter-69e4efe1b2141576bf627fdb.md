Begin with the real case from Chapter Three: James, the man whose Individualized Service Plan listed “horticultural activities” as a goal, but whose deeper value was stewardship of living things.

His story is private. Your duty of confidentiality is absolute. The public account must be a translation, preserving the core truth while altering identifying details.

The process is surgical. First, you extract the universal principle from the specific person. James’s principle was the shift from passive activity to active stewardship.

His specific identity—his name, his specific disability, the exact layout of his greenhouse—is removed. The principle remains.

You replace the identifying markers with generic, composite details that cannot be traced. “James” becomes “a person in his fifties.” “The greenhouse at the community center” becomes “a small, south-facing patio.”

“Tomatoes and basil” become “container gardens of herbs and vegetables.” The medical specifics of his condition are replaced with a functional description: “he uses a wheelchair for mobility and requires intermittent support with daily tasks.”

The result is not a fictional person. It is an anonymized case profile, a vessel for the real methodology that was applied.

The vessel is neutral; the methodology inside is the precise documented intervention you performed. According to the ethical framework used by research institutions like the University of Minnesota’s Institute on Community Integration, this level of anonymization protects the individual while allowing the practice to be studied.

You are not inventing a story. You are de-identifying a real one to highlight a real process.

Next, you secure explicit, informed consent. This is non-negotiable. You present the anonymized profile to James—or to his legal guardian—and explain its intended use.

You show him the document you would publish or present. You ask: “Does this accurately reflect your experience and the outcome you value?”

And crucially: “Are you comfortable with us using this to help other people understand this approach?” His consent documented in writing, transforms the case from a private record into a sanctioned teaching tool.

Without this step, you are exploiting his story, no matter how well you anonymize it. With the anonymized profile and consent in hand, you now craft the narrative for external audiences.

The focus is never on the person’s deficits or diagnosis. It is on the friction point in the structure and your method for resolving it.

The open story begins not with James, but with the systemic problem. “A common friction point in ISP development is the translation of generic ‘community participation’ goals into meaningful, daily expression of personal values.”

Then you introduce the anonymized profile. “Our initial assessment showed low engagement with the activity. The standard response might be to discontinue the goal. We applied the Curiosity Protocol.”

Here you detail your method, not his life. “Staff were trained to ask: ‘What do you love about the process of gardening?’ The individual spoke not of produce, but of responsibility for life, of watching something grow from his care. This revealed the core value: stewardship.”

Then you show the operational shift. “We revised the ISP. Funding was not for ‘horticultural activities’ but for ‘stewardship resources.’ This allowed us to reallocate hours.”

A staff member’s role shifted from assisting with tasks to facilitating access to a wider gardening community and helping plan a small, container-based garden the person could manage independently.

You present the outcome in terms of individual and system efficiency. “Within six months, the individual initiated contact with a local gardening club. He designed his own planting schedule.”

Staff support decreased by 15%, as his own competence and network grew. The health plan saved funds. “The individual reported a measurable increase in life satisfaction.”

You must develop three distinct versions of each case for three different audiences: the operational, the strategic, and the public.

The operational version is for fellow providers. It is granular. It includes redacted copies of the actual ISP language before and after, the staff training prompts used, the shift in billing codes, and the outcome data.

This version says: “Here is exactly how we did it. You can do this too.” You share this at provider consortium meetings or in professional forums.

The strategic version is for payers and policymakers. It focuses on systemic alignment and return on investment.

It strips out most operational detail and highlights the friction in the Medicaid rulebook, the innovative application of an existing waiver clause, and the cost savings from reduced crisis intervention. This version says: “Your policy goals of community integration and cost containment are achieved through this method.”

The public version is for families and the community. It is the most anonymized and focuses on the human outcome. It uses simple language, emphasizes the restored sense of purpose and dignity, and presents your operation as a guide.

This version says: “There is a way to support your loved one that honors who they are and who they want to become.” This is for your website, newsletters, and community talks.

Crafting these versions requires you to know what each audience values. The provider values practical steps—the payer values data and compliance. The family values hope and respect.
