The official story is one of comprehensive evaluation.

You have an Individualized Service Plan. You have an intake form, a medical history, a behavioral assessment, and a quarterly review. The plan is updated annually—the case manager asks questions. The provider documents responses. In its view, it has already asked—it has already listened. It knows.

This belief is the first and most significant obstacle to the practice of Radical Stewardship. It is the quiet conviction that the map is complete and that the territory has been surveyed. It assumes the person’s needs—and by extension, their life—are understood.

The Managed Care Organization’s authorization system operates on this premise. The provider agency’s billing software is built upon it. The frontline staff’s daily task list is generated from it.

To suggest that something fundamental remains undiscovered is to imply a systemic failure. And systems are not built to acknowledge their own blindness.

Consider the architecture of the standard review. A 2023 content analysis of common Jefferson Empowerment Waiver intake tools, conducted by a disability rights consortium, reveals a pattern.

The questions cluster around deficits and risks. “What are the client’s limitations in Activities of Daily Living?” “List triggers for behavioral escalation.”

“Detail medication management needs.” “Identify fall risks.” The language is diagnostic, not aspirational. It is designed to catalogue what is broken, not to imagine what could be built.

The form has no field titled, “What makes this person feel most alive?” There is no checkbox for “moments of unscripted joy observed in the last month.”

A reflection of systemic intent, not an accident of poor form design. The managed care model is engineered for maintenance, not for discovery.

Its primary metric is the mitigation of costly adverse events—hospitalizations, injuries, crises. Its financial incentives align with containing risk, not with cultivating possibility.

Therefore, its questioning naturally bends toward the prevention of bad outcomes. A question “What are the symptoms?” will always take precedence over the question “What opportunity is hidden here?”

The former has a clear billing code and a risk-adjusted score. The latter is, in the system’s ledger, an unbillable abstraction.

One result is what Elena Rodriguez, Unity’s Director of Care Coordination, calls “the illusion of knowledge.”

In a 2024 internal memo to her team, she wrote: “We mistake the completion of a form for the completion of understanding. We have a document that tells us how to keep a person safe and medically stable. We do not have a document that tells us how to help a person thrive. We assume the absence of crisis data signifies the presence of contentment. This is our most dangerous professional error.”

The data supports her. Recall the Compass Audit from the previous chapter. In the review of 150 active plans, less than 3% contained proactive, detailed supports for community connection or skill development beyond basic ADLs.

Over 60% listed personal goals that were either generic (“will be happy”) or directly contradicted by restrictive clauses elsewhere in the same document. The plan became a permission slip for a narrowly defined existence, not a blueprint for a self-directed life.

The objection, then is not that the structure fails to ask questions. It is that it asks the wrong questions, from the wrong posture, for the wrong purpose.

It inquires to limit liability, not to expand horizon. It listens to categorize, not to comprehend. This is why the shift from case manager to Radical Steward requires a second, parallel discipline.

You must master the Compass of Intent to set a new direction. But to discover that true direction in the first place, you must adopt a new mode of questioning. You must move from passive data collection to active, structured curiosity.

A protocol, not a soft skill. It is the disciplined, systematic search for the life that exists beyond the checklist.

Recall James, the young man with cerebral palsy and a documented history of “self-injurious behavior” and “elopement risk.”

His initial ISP, authorized by HealthGuard Managed Care in the fall of 2022 is a textbook example of deficit-focused architecture. The 45-page document catalogs his needs under standard headings: “Mobility,” “Communication,” “Behavioral Health,” and “Safety.”

It stipulates two-person assists for all transfers. It mandates a behavior plan focused on de-escalation, listing specific staff interventions for when he becomes “agitated.”

It requires door alarms and a GPS monitoring device for community outings due to his “elopement risk.” The “Strengths” section contains three bullet points: “seems to enjoy music,” “responds well to a calm voice,” and “can use a switch-activated device for yes/no.”

The intake process that generated this plan is documented in the accompanying file. A nurse-practitioner conducted a physical health review.

A behavior specialist observed him for six hours over two days, tallying instances of “hand-biting” and “vocal outbursts.” A supports coordinator from the MCO interviewed his aging parents.

Exhausted and fearful, they emphasized his strength, his occasional frustration, and their worry that he might one day bolt from a car and be injured.

The questions followed the script: “What are his biggest challenges?” “What behaviors are most difficult to manage?” “What does he need help with?”

His parents, operating within the same framework of risk, answered in kind. They spoke of what he could not do, of what they feared. That system recorded their fears and codified them into restrictions.

At no point in the official record does anyone ask James what he likes to do. No one inquires about his preferences beyond choosing between two shirts or two snacks.

The official form has no prompt for “curiosity” or “emerging interest.” His silence—his communication method is primarily nonverbal—is interpreted as an absence of opinion, rather than an absence of an appropriate channel.

The plan that emerges is a masterpiece of risk mitigation. It is also a cage. It defines his life by what he might do wrong, not by what he might do at all.

It authorizes funding to prevent a bad life, not to construct a good one.

The discovery of his affinity for plants happens by accident eight months later, during a staffing crisis. A new Direct Support Professional, Maya is assigned to his home.

She is untrained in the specifics of his behavior plan. On her third day, during a tense afternoon, she takes him outside to sit on the porch simply to change the scenery.

She notices his eyes tracking a finch hopping between branches of a neglected rose bush. She points. He looks. She comments that the bush looks thirsty.

He makes a soft sound. On impulse, she gets a watering can, and places his hand on the handle with hers, guiding it to pour. His hand does not pull away. He leans forward.

The next day, she brings a packet of sunflower seeds. She shows him the picture. She places a seed in his palm. He holds it.

She models pressing it into a small pot of soil. She guides his hand to do the same. He allows it. He returns to the pot every day, leaning toward it.

When the first green shoot breaks the soil a week later, he lets out a sustained, quiet vocalization. This is a sound not listed on his behavior plan as a target for intervention.

Maya documents this as a “note” in the daily log: “James showed interest in sunflower seedling. Sat quietly for 20 minutes observing.”

This note triggers no official review. It is an anomaly in the data stream. A system is designed to flag incidents—falls, aggression, missed medications.

It does not flag moments of quiet engagement. The billing codes do not exist for “horticultural exploration.”

Still, Maya begins to shift her own approach. She starts bringing different seeds—marigolds, beans.

She finds that tasks involving soil, water, and sorting seeds by size consistently result in longer periods of focused attention. These activities lead to a complete absence of the behaviors his plan is designed to manage.

She observes that his muscle tone seems to improve during these activities, his hands more relaxed. She tells her supervisor.

The supervisor submits a request to amend the ISP to include “gardening” as a “therapeutic leisure activity.”

The MCO’s initial response is a request for clarification: “Is this a behavioral intervention? Please submit data showing reduction in target behaviors to justify service hours.”

One question, again is backward. It asks how the activity mitigates a deficit, not how it cultivates a strength.

Maya and her supervisor reframe the request, citing “improved fine motor engagement” and “calm focus,” which are approved.

A year after the initial ISP, James has a small raised bed. He chooses between seedlings by looking. He waters with an adapted long-spout can.

The behavior plan has been revised. The “elopement risk” protocols remain, but the primary focus of his support has quietly shifted from preventing outbursts to supporting a gardener.

Actually, the point is not the heartwarming ending. The point is the catastrophic blindness of the initial review.

Every tool deployed—the health review, the behavioral observation, the family interview—sought pathology. It successfully found it. It then built a fortress around it.

What it never sought was James’s latent capacity for nurturing, his potential for sustained attention, his possible identity as someone who cultivates life.

This capacity was not hidden. It was simply irrelevant to the questions being asked. The structure inquired into his brokenness so thoroughly that it never stumbled upon his wholeness.

Active questioning, therefore, begins with a suspicion: that the official record is a map of the cracks in the vessel, not a description of the water within.

Your first discipline is to learn to ask the questions the form forgot.

The consequences of this passive, checklist-driven investigation are not abstract. They are measurable declines in physical health, emotional well-being, and social participation.

The research on human factors in healthcare provides a framework. A 2019 systematic review in the Journal of Patient Safety concluded that “failures of diagnosis are often failures of information gathering.”

The authors noted that when protocols are rigidly task-based, support staff become “data clerks.” They prioritize the completion of fields over the synthesis of meaning. The person behind the form recedes.

In the context of supported living, this manifests as a cascade of unmet needs. When an ISP is built from a deficit inventory, its authorized supports are measured to prevent the listed risks.

They are not careful to promote holistic health. That result is maintenance, not optimization. An individual whose evaluation highlights “mobility impairment” and “fall risk” may receive funding for a wheelchair and staff assistance for transfers.

But if the process does not actively inquire into stamina, or interest in movement, the individual likely will not receive support for proactive physiotherapy, swimming, or adapted yoga.

These interventions could improve core strength, and potentially alter the trajectory of the impairment itself.

The need for these services is not identified because the question “What movement is still possible, and how could we nurture it?” is never posed. The body is kept safe, but it is also allowed to atrophy within the confines of that safety.

Emotionally, the cost is disengagement, often mislabeled as “behavior” or “apathy.” Human factors research emphasizes the concept of “latent errors”—flaws baked into the structure that set the stage for active failures.

A passive evaluation that catalogs triggers for “agitation” but never discovers sources of curiosity creates such a latent error. The environment it authorizes is one of boredom and reactive management.

The individual, lacking a conduit for their interests or agency may exhibit the very behaviors the plan seeks to suppress.

Following the plan, apply behavioral interventions. A cycle of escalation replaces a cycle of engagement.

The emotional decline is then documented as a validation of the original risk assessment, rather than seen as an indictment of the plan’s poverty of imagination. A system’s inquiry created a self-fulfilling prophecy.

The economic impact is perverse. Managed care operates on a capitated or risk-adjusted payment model; the incentive is to control costs.

A standard review, focused on containing obvious risks, appears financially prudent in the short term. It authorizes only the minimally necessary services.

But this myopia ignores the compounding cost of unmet needs. A decline in physical health due to undetected, unaddressed pain or deconditioning leads to more expensive acute care: doctor visits, hospitalizations, and prescriptions.

Emotional disengagement and behavioral escalation lead to crisis interventions, and potentially more restrictive placements.

Social isolation correlates with higher rates of depression and cognitive decline, triggering a need for more intensive mental health services.

The initial “savings” from a limited review are repeatedly undone by the downstream expenses of the life it inadvertently constricts. One system pays to put out fires it systematically arranged the kindling for.

Social participation is often the first casualty. The standard inventory asks, “Does the client have friends?” or “What community activities do they attend?”

If the answer is “none,” the form often moves on. Active questioning treats it as a critical mystery to be solved.

It asks the follow-ups the form omits: “What has been tried?” “What barriers were encountered?” “What topics light up their face when mentioned on TV?” “Is the absence of friends a preference, or a failure of support?”

Without this deeper probe, the ISP will not include the tailored, trial-and-error supports needed to bridge the gap between isolation and connection.

It will not fund a staff member to research niche clubs, to facilitate gradual introductions, or to provide the support to manage sensory overwhelm at a library or a cafe.

The individual remains lonely. The plan documents their loneliness as a fact, not a problem to be solved.

This is the evidence counter to the managed care narrative of comprehensive, efficient checks. The narrative claims the system asks, listens, and responds.

The evidence shows it asks narrow questions, hears only the answers that fit its risk categories, and responds with restrictions that stifle health and reinforce isolation.

The human factor is engineered out. Naturally, the person becomes a case study in managed decline.

The shift to active inquiry is therefore not a matter of adding a few nicer questions. It is a corrective to a system that, in its quest to avoid bad outcomes has made the cultivation of good ones operationally invisible.

You must become a counter-system. Your curiosity must be structured to detect the latent capacities, the hidden interests, and the quiet preferences that the official tools filter out. You are listening for the life that the paperwork has failed to describe.

Active questioning is not a personality trait. This isn't a vague sense of “being interested.”

It is a professional protocol with a defined objective: to systematically uncover the data the common review missed.

Where passive data collection accepts the existing map, active inquiry assumes the map is incomplete and sets out to survey the blank spaces.

Its goal is not to gather more information about deficits, but to discover evidence of latent capacity and hidden opportunity. This is the primary investigative tool of the Radical Steward.

Think of it as forensic curiosity. You are examining a scene—the person’s daily life—for clues the first responders overlooked.

The standard report documented the obvious damage and posted caution tape. Your job is to look past the tape.

You are searching for the intact wiring behind the shattered wall, the functional foundation beneath the cracked floor.

You are looking for what still works, what still sparks, and what still holds weight, even if it is currently buried or disconnected.

This requires a shift in posture from interviewer to investigator. An interviewer follows a script. An investigator follows a thread.

The script for the ISP intake is designed for coverage and closure. The thread of active investigation is pursued for depth and discovery.

One aims to fill a form. The other aims to solve a mystery. The mystery is always the same: Who is this person when they are not performing their diagnosis?

The protocol has three interlocking operations: Structured asking, Attuned Listening, and Intentional Observation. These are not sequential steps but concurrent disciplines, each reinforcing the others.

Structured asking means designing questions that bypass the defensive architecture of the “problem list.” You avoid questions that begin with “Can you…” or “Do you need help with…”

These queries automatically orient the respondent toward limitation. You build questions around action, preference, and memory.

“What did you do today that you enjoyed?” “What is something you used to do that you miss?”

“If you had a free Saturday with no limits, what would happen?” “What does a good day feel like?” “What irritates you the most about your current routine?”

These questions are not on the form. They require the respondent to access a different cognitive file—not the medical history, but the life narrative.

When working with nonverbal individuals or those with complex communication needs, structured asking becomes even more precise. You move from verbal questions to environmental probes.

You present curated choices that go far beyond the binary. You do not offer two shirts.

You offer ten images: a mountain, a city street, a garden, a race car, a paint palette, a musical note, and a blank square. You note which image holds the gaze.

You then drill down. If the garden image is selected, you later offer images of vegetables, flowers, trees, or watering cans.

You are using the environment to ask the questions your words cannot. Each choice is a sentence in a conversation you are learning to decode.

Attuned Listening is the practice of hearing the signal in the noise of official narratives. In a standard meeting, you listen for problems to solve and risks to document.

In active inquiry, you listen for three specific signals: energy, repetition, and contradiction.

You listen for the topic that injects energy into a person’s voice or body language—a quickening of speech or a shift in posture.

You listen for the subject that recurs unprompted across different conversations, with different people, over time.

You listen intently for contradictions—when a person’s stated goal is contradicted by every action or environmental choice they make. This signals that the stated goal may be what they think they should want, while their behavior reveals a deeper preference.

You also practice listening to silence and to complaint. Silence is often interpreted as absence or compliance. In active inquiry, silence is a data point. Is it peaceful? Is it resigned? Is it charged?

The quality of the silence matters. Complaint, similarly is not just a problem to be fixed. It is a direct communication about a boundary being crossed or a value being violated.

“I hate going to the day program” is not a behavioral issue. It is a clue.

Your next question is not “Why are you non-compliant?” It is “What part of it do you hate? The noise? The crowds? The crafts? The bus ride? Tell me about the worst minute of your last day there.”

Intentional Observation is the discipline of seeing what is not being reported. The daily notes in a support log typically record tasks completed and incidents occurred.

Intentional observation records patterns in energy, attention, and interaction that fall outside those categories. You become an ethnographer of one.

You note the time of day when the person seems most alert. You observe what they look at when the television is on.

You see how they touch objects—with curiosity, with ritual care, or with avoidance. You watch how they navigate space. Do they take the same path every day, or do they pause at a certain window?

You document their response to changes in routine, not just to label it “resistant” or “flexible,” but to understand what the change disrupted that they valued.

The 2024 internal memo from Elena Rodriguez at Unity included a directive for this practice: “For the next month do not write a single daily note about a task. Write only about one thing you saw that you had never noticed before. A glance, a reaction to a smell. A moment of patience. A choice made without prompting.”

Its result was not softer data. It was harder intelligence.

Staff began to identify sensory sensitivities no review had caught, a client who consistently positioned himself away from air vents, another who always touched a specific textured pillow before settling.

They noted nascent skills, a woman who would align remote controls on a table, demonstrating a capacity for ordering her environment. These were not deficits. They were data points for a new blueprint.

Together, these three operations form a continuous feedback loop. A question prompts a response you listen to, which leads you to observe a related behavior, which generates a new, more precise question.

This loop is your primary tool for navigating beyond the boundaries of the ISP.

It is how you discover that the man labeled “aggressive when frustrated” is, in a quieter moment, carefully organizing screws by size in a garage. This reveals a potential pathway for support through detailed manual activity.

It is how you learn that the woman described as “passive and withdrawn” spends forty minutes every afternoon watching a specific soap opera. She can recite the complex relationships of every character, evidence of retained narrative memory and social interest waiting for a conduit.

The ultimate purpose of active search is to generate what human factors engineering calls a “rich picture.” A standard evaluation produces a schematic, a diagram of interconnected problems.

A rich picture is a messy, multidimensional portrait of a person in context, replete with contradictions, potentials, history, and unspoken rules.

It contains the problems, but it surrounds them with the person’s resources.

This rich picture becomes the true substrate for the Compass of Intent. You cannot set a course toward a meaningful destination if your only map shows obstacles.

You need to know where the solid ground is, where the latent pathways lie, and where the person’s own internal navigation system is still functional.

This disciplined curiosity is the engine of discovery that makes Radical Stewardship possible. Without it, you are merely a more compassionate manager of a pre-defined, limited case.

With it, you become an investigator of a life, uncovering the evidence you need to argue for a different, more expansive plan.

You are not accepting the story as it has been documented. You are conducting the investigation that will rewrite it.

The final, critical synthesis is this: the “opportunity” you are seeking is always, ultimately, an opportunity for the person to exercise a greater degree of authorship over their own life.

The hidden potential in the behavior, the unarticulated need behind the resistance, the latent capacity beneath the silence, each is a thread the person can pick up and weave into their own narrative.

Your disciplined curiosity is the act of locating those threads and handing them back. You are not the weaver. You are the one who, by asking a different question, turns on the light so the weaver can see the loom.

The shift from “What are the symptoms?” to “What opportunity is hidden here?” is the shift from seeing a person as a system to be managed, to seeing them as a story waiting to be continued.
