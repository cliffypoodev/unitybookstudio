A 2022 internal survey of care coordinators across three states, conducted by the National Association of Disability Service Providers, asks a single question: “What is the primary driver for most decisions documented in your clients’ Individualized Service Plans?”

The top answer, selected by 68% of respondents is not “client preference” or “clinical best practice.” It is “risk mitigation.” The second most frequent answer is “MCO billing requirements.”

This is the reality you walk into. The Forward-Looking Compass from Chapter 6 is a powerful tool, but it is a blueprint for a new structure. You are standing in an old one.

Its walls are made of documents—thousands of pages of ISPs and behavior plans—that were drafted under a different set of laws. The law of fear. The law of compliance.

You cannot build the new by merely announcing it. You must first conduct an audit. An audit is not an evaluation of staff performance.

It is a forensic examination of the paper trail that currently dictates a person’s life. You are looking for the fingerprints of institutional anxiety. You are identifying where the ghost of the structure is still writing the plan.

A fear-based ISP has a recognizable architecture. Its goals are defensive. They are phrased as preventions: “Prevent elopement,” “Prevent self-injurious behavior,” or “Prevent nutritional deficiency.”

The language of support is passive and monitoring: “Staff will observe during meals.” “Staff will redirect using approved phrases.” The measured outcomes are negatives that did not happen, such as “Zero incidents of falls.”

The person exists in this document as a collection of hazards to be managed. A values-based plan is proactive. Its goals are declarative.

They are phrased as engagements: “Engage with the community at the weekly farmer’s market.” “Communicate preferences using a new tablet-based system.” “Develop a skill for preparing two preferred meals.”

The language of support is active and facilitative. “Staff will arrange transportation and provide visual schedule for market visits.” “Staff will program device with core vocabulary and model its use.”

The measured outcomes are positives that did happen: “Selected three items from vendors independently.” “Initiated request for break using device on five occasions.” The person exists in this document as a center of agency moving through the world.

The gap between these two documents is not a matter of wording. It is a chasm of intent. One document asks, “What must we stop?” The other asks, “What must we start?”

Your first implementation task is to bridge that chasm. You begin with the Compass Audit—the tool is simple. You take a current plan. You read every goal, every protocol. First: “Does this decision primarily serve the framework's need for safety, compliance, or convenience?”

Second: “Does this decision primarily serve the person’s expressed or observed priority for growth, connection, or autonomy?” You are not scoring. You are tracing a pattern.

You find goals that are purely systemic—the requirement for two staff on all community outings, not because of the individual’s needs, but because of a blanket agency policy born from a lawsuit five years ago.

You find goals that are purely personal—a client’s desire to learn to use a cash register, stemming from his belief in contribution and competence. You find the murky middle.

The goal for “increased social interaction” that is met by “attending day program,” where the principle of connection is acknowledged but the method is the organization's default solution, not the individual’s preferred path.

This is the granular, unglamorous work of revolution. It starts with a stack of binders in a quiet room, and the courage to see what you have actually been building all this time.

Unity Place in Minneapolis provides a documented example. In early 2023, under the guidance of its Director of Resident Services, the organization commits to a formal review of all current plans across its residences.

The initiative is not initially met with universal enthusiasm. Staff concerns center on two points: the volume of work involved in reviewing dozens of complex plans, and a noticeable anxiety about “opening doors” to requests the agency might not be equipped to fulfill.

The process they design is methodical. They do not attempt to rewrite plans in this first phase. Their goal is diagnosis.

A team comprising a lead coordinator, a direct support professional, and a family advocate—when available—takes each resident’s active plan. They apply the two diagnostic questions from the Compass Audit to every listed goal and support activity.

What the review group finds is a pattern that mirrors the national survey data. A significant majority of plans are architecturally defensive. Common systemic “red flags” emerge across multiple documents.

The phrase “prevent decline” appears in over 80% of plans to residents with dementia, with supporting interventions limited to “monitor for changes” and “report to nurse.”

Goals for community engagement frequently specify attendance at the facility’s own scheduled group activities, with no alternative options reflecting personal interests.

One plan for a resident who had previously been an avid gardener contains the goal “will participate in structured recreational activity twice weekly,” fulfilled by bingo in the common room.

The ideal of nurturing, of tactile connection to soil and growth is absent from the document. The assessment makes the abstract concrete.

They create a simple tally sheet for each document, categorizing goals as “System-Primary,” “Person-Primary,” or “Mixed.” The initial aggregate data is stark.

For the first twenty plans reviewed, 68% of all documented goals and supports are coded as System-Primary. Only 22% are Person-Primary.

The remaining 10% fall into the Mixed category, where a person-centered aim like “maintain social connections” is fulfilled by a systemic default like “attend weekly church service provided by facility van,” regardless of faith or preference.

The case of a resident we refer to as Mr. D illustrates the dynamic. His pre-review record is a model of risk management. Diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease and a history of falls, his plan is dominated by preventative protocols.

Goals include “prevent falls during ambulation” and “prevent aspiration during meals.” Supports are “staff will assist with all transfers” and “staff will puree all foods.”

The measured outcomes are “zero falls” and “zero choking incidents.” The document is, from a liability perspective, impeccable. It is also a blueprint for a life of heavy passivity.

Nowhere does it reflect what Mr. D prioritizes, nor his expressed wish to “feed myself as long as I possibly can.” The group, applying the lens, notes the disconnect.

The goal of safety is not wrong, but its exclusive dominance erases the person. The support of pureeing all foods is a systemic solution that ignores the principle of autonomy.

It is a decision made for the structure's convenience and liability mitigation, not for Mr. D’s stated goal of self-reliance. This is the review's critical revelation: the problem is rarely one of malicious intent.

It is one of invisible momentum. Plans are often updated annually through a process of replication—last year’s goals are copied forward with minor tweaks.

A support originally implemented after a single incident of choking a decade prior becomes a permanent, unchallenged fixture.

A blanket agency policy on staffing ratios for outings, instituted after a different resident’s elopement is applied universally, stifling spontaneous walks for individuals who pose no such risk.

The “ghost in the plan” is not a phantom; it is the accumulated weight of past anxieties, crystallized into standing policy and then mindlessly transcribed.

The initial resistance among some staff begins to shift when presented with these specific, line-item findings. It is harder to defend “puree all foods” as person-centered care when juxtaposed with the resident’s own stated desire.

The conversation moves from a theoretical debate about philosophy to a practical problem-solving session. “How can we support Mr. D to feed himself as long as possible while also managing aspiration risk?”

This question acknowledges both beliefs—autonomy and safety—and seeks a synthesis. It might lead to interventions like adaptive utensils, modified food textures, or specific mealtime positioning strategies.

The goal transforms from “prevent aspiration” to “maintain self-feeding skills with safe swallowing strategies.” The assessment report concludes with a pointed observation.

The most common Person-Primary goals they do find are often the weakest, most generic aspirations, such as “Increase happiness” or “Improve quality of life.” These are noble sentiments.

Without concrete, facilitated actions tied to them, they are merely decorative. They serve a different systemic need: the need to feel like the plan is person-centered without actually changing its operational core.

The review strips away this decoration, too. For Unity Place, the value is not in immediate solutions. It is in creating a shared, plain picture of the current reality.

The report does not cast blame on individual coordinators or staff. It shows them the structural output of the setup they had all been operating within.

The director’s summary notes, “We are not failing at person-centered care. We are succeeding, with high fidelity, at system-centered care. The audit shows us exactly what that looks like. Now we must choose if that is what we intend to build.”

This is the uncomfortable grace of the Compass Audit. It depersonalizes the critique while making the evidence intensely personal.

You are not telling your team they are doing a bad job. You are showing them the legacy code running in the documents they inherit and maintain.

You are proving that the gap between your mission statement and your operational plans is not a failure of character, but a function of design. The next step is the turn.

Consider the “No Unsupervised Kitchen Access” protocol. This is a common red flag found in plans for individuals with cognitive or mobility considerations.

The documented rationale is typically a past incident—a forgotten stove burner, a spilled pot—or a general risk assessment for fire or injury.

The support is clear: “Staff will prepare all meals and snacks. Client will not have access to kitchen without direct staff supervision.” The systemic driver here is unambiguous: liability mitigation.

A fear is of a reportable event, a licensing citation, or a lawsuit. The decision serves the setup’s need for safety and compliance. It is a clean, defensible line in the plan.

Now trace its impact on independence. The individual’s priority might be autonomy, contribution, or the simple sensory pleasure of making a favorite sandwich.

The protocol does not merely prevent a potential burn; it prevents the act of choosing, the ritual of preparation, the pride of “I made this.” It renders the person a perpetual guest in their own home.

Over time, it leads to skill atrophy. The knowledge of how to brew coffee or toast bread fades from disuse. The goal of “prevent kitchen accident” is achieved.

The unstated cost is the erosion of a fundamental domain of adult life. A values-based alternative does not ignore the risk. It engages with it through the lens of the person’s priorities.

If the priority is autonomy, the goal becomes “safely prepare simple, preferred meals and snacks.” The supports shift from prohibition to facilitation and skill-building.

“Staff will assess with OT for adaptive equipment (automatic shut-off kettle, pre-set microwave).” “Staff will co-create a visual recipe guide for two preferred snacks and provide graduated supervision during practice.”

The measured outcome is not “zero accidents,” but “independently prepared tea using adapted kettle on five occasions.” The risk is managed through strategy and support, not elimination of the activity. The person’s agency is centered.

Another pervasive red flag is the rigid, facility-centric daily schedule. The objective reads “will participate in structured daily activities,” and the support details a fixed roster: “9 AM: Group exercise. 10:30 AM: Arts & crafts. 1 PM: Current events discussion.”

The systemic driver is operational efficiency and staffing predictability. It is easier to manage one activity for twelve people than twelve activities for twelve individuals. The compliance need is met—the documentation shows “engagement.”

The plan is convenient, orderly, and defensible to an auditor. One impact on independence is a slow suffocation of preference and rhythm.

The early riser must wait for the 9 AM group. The person who finds group exercise overstimulating has no alternative but to decline, which may then be documented as “refusal of services.”

The individual whose principle is quiet contemplation or one-on-one connection finds no pathway for it in the daily architecture. Their internal clock and personal interests are subordinate to the institutional timetable.

A values-based alternative starts with the question of rhythm and choice. The goal might be “structure daily routines that align with personal energy patterns and interests.”

Supports become facilitative: “Staff will offer individual morning options (walk in garden, assist with breakfast setup, quiet time in room) prior to any group offering.”

“Staff will maintain a ‘menu’ of available activities each day and offer choice using preferred communication method.” The measured outcome shifts from “attended three scheduled activities” to “selected preferred morning activity on 80% of days.”

The structure's need for efficiency is not abandoned, but it is flexed to serve as a platform for choice, not a cage of compliance.

A subtler, but deeply significant red flag is the “Therapeutic Pass” restriction for community access. It isn't an outright ban, but a procedural barrier.

The document states, “Community outings require a Therapeutic Pass signed by the team nurse and program manager 72 hours in advance, detailing destination, purpose, and staffing plan.”

The systemic driver is, again, risk management and resource allocation. It ensures clinical “approval” and prevents spontaneous outings that could disrupt staffing ratios.

That impact on independence is the theft of spontaneity and the message of perpetual probation. A beautiful Tuesday afternoon invites a walk for ice cream.

The focus is joy, sensory pleasure, and normalcy. The protocol requires a form, and a wait. The moment passes.

The individual learns that their own desires are subject to a bureaucratic gatekeeping process that treats every outing as a potential clinical incident to be pre-authorized.

A world becomes something they visit only with permission, for pre-approved purposes. The underlying message is that their judgment, their simple want is insufficient and inherently risky.

A values-based alternative works to build protocols for supported independence, not pre-approval. It starts with a risk-benefit conversation focused on the individual, not the setup.

If the goal is community integration and spontaneity, the goal becomes “access the community for preferred activities with appropriate supports.”

The support might be: “Staff will develop a personal community safety plan with the individual, identifying preferred destinations and safety strategies.”

“Staff will establish a ‘ready kit’ for spontaneous outings (pre-loaded transit card, emergency contact list, communication aid).” The measure is “initiated a spontaneous community outing using safety plan on two occasions this month.”

The agency's need for safety is addressed through co-created planning and preparation, not through a permission slip that treats the individual as a risk to be contained.

In each case, the red flag is not an error in clinical judgment. It is a rational, system-optimal solution to the problem of liability, efficiency, and control.

But when applied universally, it solves the organization's problem by creating a human one: the systematic limitation of independence. The audit makes this trade-off visible.

It shows that the “safe” plan is often the one that most aggressively restricts the life it purports to serve.

The challenge is to move from a pattern of restriction to one of supported risk, where safety is not an end in itself, but a condition that enables the pursuit of a meaningful life.

The audit requires a method, not just a lens. You move from observation to operation—this is your protocol. First, you define the scope. You are not auditing a person. You are auditing a document—the Individualized Service Plan—and the operational protocols that flow from it.

Begin with one plan. Choose one that feels stagnant, or one where you sense a disconnect between the stated “person-centered” approach and the daily reality. This is a pilot, not an invasion.

Second, you assemble the review team. That isn't a task for a single coordinator behind a closed door. The multidisciplinary lens is non-negotiable.

The core team must include, at minimum: the lead care coordinator, a Direct Support Professional who provides daily support, and the individual at the center of the plan.

If the individual communicates through a guardian or advocate, that person is included.

This triad—coordinator, frontline staff, client/advocate—ensures three distinct perspectives: the systemic and the personal. Without all three, your assessment is blind in one eye.

Third, you prepare the document—print the current plan. Have a highlighter and pens ready. You need a copy for each team member. This physical act matters.

You are making the plan solid, something to be marked up, questioned, and held in the hand. Now, you conduct the review.

You use a structured set of questions, applied line by line to each goal, support activity, and restriction. This checklist is your surgical tool.

For each documented GOAL, ask: 1. Is this goal phrased as a prevention (“prevent X”) or as an engagement (“achieve Y”)? 2. Whose priority does this goal primarily reflect?

Is it a response to a past incident, a systemic regulation, or the individual’s expressed current interest? 3. If you removed all risk considerations would this goal still exist? Would the individual choose it?

For each documented SUPPORT or INTERVENTION, ask: 1. Does this action facilitate a skill, or does it substitute for a skill?

2. Does this protocol allow for graduated independence, or does it mandate perpetual assistance? 3. Is this the least restrictive way to achieve the stated aim, or is it the most convenient or liability-proof for the staff?

For each documented RESTRICTION or PROTOCOL, ask: 1. What specific incident or assessment triggered this rule? Is that trigger current and relevant to this individual today?

2. Does this rule apply to all individuals in a category, or is it individualized to this person’s specific risks and capacities?

3. What valued activity does this restriction prevent? Is the cost to independence proportionate to the actual, current risk?

One team discusses each question, marking the document. The coordinator might highlight a goal born from an MCO requirement.

The DSP might note that a support is impractical and fosters dependence. The individual or advocate might point to a restriction that feels infantilizing. The notes accumulate in the margins.

As patterns emerge, you categorize. Use a simple tally sheet with three columns: System-Primary, Person-Primary, Mixed.

Place each goal and its associated supports into one column based on the consensus of the team’s discussion. The act of categorization is not judgment. It is diagnosis.

You are creating a heat map of intent. You will encounter defensiveness.

A staff member might say, “But that rule is there because she burned herself once.” Your next question is critical. You ask, “What has she learned since then?”

“What supports could we put in place so she can cook safely, instead of not cooking at all?” You shift the conversation from defending a historical fear to solving for a current priority.

You also find ghost goals—aspirations like “improve quality of life” that have no concrete supports. Highlight these in a different color. They are placeholders.

Ask the team, “What would show us that quality of life has improved? What specific, observable thing would she be doing?” If you cannot answer, the goal is decorative.

It serves the agency's need to sound compassionate, not the person’s need for an actionable path. The final step of the audit meeting is to synthesize, not to solve.

Your output is not a new plan. It is a one-page “Audit Summary” for the document.

The summary lists the percentage of goals coded as System-Primary, Person-Primary, or Mixed. It identifies the top three “red flag” restrictions with their stated rationale and their impact on independence. It also notes the top three “ghost goals” that lack actionable supports, along with one to three primary beliefs of the individual currently absent from the architecture.

This summary is your compass correction. It is the shared, evidence-based answer to the question, “Where are we actually pointing?”

The power of this structured process is its neutrality. It uses questions, not accusations. The data comes from the team’s own analysis of their own document.

The disconnect between intent and implementation is revealed by the group, not dictated by management. This is how you build buy-in for the difficult work of change.

You are not imposing a new philosophy from on high. You are facilitating a discovery that the team makes together.

The examination is complete when you have that summary in hand. That room is quiet for a moment. The evidence is plain. You have successfully diagnosed the legacy code.

Now you must rewrite it. The work gives you the “why” for change. You turn the compass, one deliberate degree at a time.

The power of the Compass Audit lies not in what it finds, but in what it forces you to see. It is a mirror held up to your operational soul.

The red flags are not failures of your staff’s compassion; they are the logical output of a setup whose primary rewards are for avoiding bad outcomes, not for creating good ones.

When you present the Audit Summary—those stark percentages, those concrete examples of traded agency—you are not starting a blame game. You are initiating a re-education.

This re-education targets the deepest level: the subconscious question that drives daily decisions.

In a system-centered model, the implicit question hovering over every choice is, “What can go wrong?” This question generates the defensive architecture.

It leads to the blanket prohibition, the pre-approval form, and the pureed diet. It is a question of risk management. The values-based model requires a conscious shift to a different core question: “What can go right, and how can we support it safely?”

This is a question of possibility management. The first question seeks to eliminate variables. The second seeks to engage with them through strategy and support.

The findings make this shift necessary because it proves the cost of the old question. You show the team that “What can go wrong?” has been answered with great success.

Falls were prevented—choking incidents were avoided. Licensing citations were dodged.

But the tally sheet shows the bill for that success: a life narrowed, spontaneity extinguished. A team must confront that they have been expertly managing decay, not cultivating life.

This is the pivotal moment of the work's aftermath. The data creates a cognitive dissonance between their professional self-image as caregivers and the output of their care plans. This dissonance is the engine of change.

According to internal implementation notes from Unity Place, this moment is where the real work begins.

The Director of Resident Services reports that following the presentations, staff conversations fundamentally changed. “We stopped talking about ‘what the state requires’ or ‘what our insurance says,’ as the first and final justification,” the notes state.

“The findings gave us a shared vocabulary to challenge those defaults. We started asking, ‘Is this protocol serving Mr. D’s need for autonomy, or just our fear of a report?’”

It moved the fear from a hidden driver to an open variable we could weigh against a person’s stated desire.

This is the mindset shift from compliance to advocacy. The staff member is no longer merely an implementer of organizational directives. She becomes a translator and a negotiator.

Her role expands from executing the plan to interrogating it on behalf of the person she serves.

She must now look at a goal like “prevent wandering” and, remembering the findings, ask: “What choice is being restricted? Is it exploration, exercise, or solitude?”

“How can we support that choice safely instead of simply building a higher fence?” This turns the frontline worker from a rule-enforcer into a problem-solver and creative partner.

It invests her with a deeper professional purpose—not just to keep someone safe, but to help someone live.

The process thus functions as the foundational exercise for Radical Stewardship. Stewardship is not soft sentiment. It is hard, practical guardianship of another person’s potential.

A steward does protect an asset from harm; she nurtures its growth and ensures its legacy.

The process provides the initial inventory of that asset—the person’s principles, interests, and capacities—and reveals the systemic rust currently corroding it.

You cannot be a steward of a life if your primary tool is a manual for its containment.

This transformation requires leadership to model the new calculus. When a DSP brings a proposed, values-based support that carries more perceived risk than the old restrictive rule, the coordinator’s response cannot be a reflexive “no.”

It must be, “Show me your safety plan. Let’s analyze the actual risk versus the benefit to independence.” This moves the conversation from permission to collaboration.

It tells the staff that the agency will back their creative advocacy, not punish their deviation from the defensive norm.

The Unity Place notes highlight this, citing a specific case where a DSP proposed a supported community walk for a resident whose plan had a “therapeutic pass” requirement.

Instead of shutting it down, the team used the framework to co-create a safety and communication plan. The walk happened.

The documented outcome was not an incident, but a note from the DSP: “She smiled the whole way. Told me it was the first time she felt like a person in months.”

The Compass Audit, therefore is not a one-time project. It is the installation of a new operating system. It is the protocol that ensures your mission statement is not a plaque on the wall but a filter for every decision.

It builds a culture of continuous, critical reflection. One question, “Where is our compass pointing?” becomes a routine part of quarterly reviews, not just a crisis intervention.

It makes the invisible momentum visible and therefore interruptible.

You close the review not with a list of problems, but with a map of opportunities. The “System-Primary” percentage is not a score of failure; it is a measure of the work’s potential impact.

Each red flag is a locked door. The review hands you the keys.

The summary page is your mandate for the next phase: the deliberate, degree-by-degree turn of the compass.

It proves that the gap between the life being lived and the life that is possible is not a chasm of disability or decline.

It is a crevice carved by systems, and it can be bridged by intention. The review gives you the courage to build that bridge, because you have finally seen what lies on the other side.
