The Individualized Service Plan is the single most important document in your client’s life. It is also, in its current standard form, a legalistic trap.

Pull a typical ISP from any agency file in Jefferson. You find a document anchored in the past. Its primary function is compliance, and its structure is a catalog of deficits. The opening pages list diagnoses and areas of need defined entirely by what the person cannot do.

The goals are often generic and safety-obsessed. They often state that the client will not engage in self-injurious behavior or will take medication as prescribed. The supporting narratives read like incident reports. A risk-mitigation checklist written in the passive voice of institutional self-protection, not a plan for a life.

The problem is not the paperwork itself. One problem is the underlying assumption. The traditional ISP operates as a historical record. It looks backward documenting what has gone wrong and what must be prevented.

It treats the person as a case to be managed and a collection of risks to be contained. As Michaela Vance noted in her 2022 keynote to the National Council on Supportive Living, when your guiding document is a ledger of past failures, you are programming for limitation. You are building a ceiling, not a floor.

This backward gaze creates a perverse inertia. Staff are trained to reference the plan not for inspiration but for prohibition. A goal like “Will explore community activities with staff support” becomes a list of places the client cannot go because of a prior incident.

The document meant to outline support becomes a manual for constraint. The person’s present desires have no formal place in this archival record. This includes current interests or the simple wish for a different routine on a Tuesday. The person is frozen in the version of themselves that existed at last year’s annual review.

Gabriel Novak’s workshop begins by making this tension physical. He places two documents side by side on a projector. The first is a redacted file for a client named Leo, aged 28. Its goals are static, and its language is clinical.

The second is a single sheet of paper from Leo’s most recent support session. On it, in Leo’s own handwriting with staff transcription is a list of goals. He wants to learn to make pancakes without burning them and go to a baseball game. He wants to figure out how to use the bus to the library by himself and tell Mark he wants a different shower time.

The disconnect is immediate. The official plan speaks of community integration and daily living skills. The lived document speaks of pancakes, buses, and autonomy over shower schedules. One is a dead report. The other is a living pulse.

“Your work,” Novak tells the room of care coordinators and lead DSPs, “is to bridge that gap. Not by adding Leo’s list as an appendix to the old ISP. By making his list the foundation of a new one.”

He gestures to the projector screen. “We are not historians. We are cartographers — we do not record the terrain already crossed. We draw the map for the terrain the person wants to walk next.”

This is the core of the Forward-Looking Compass. It is a shift in reporting from what was to what could be. It moves from what the client cannot do to what he wants to do. The framework does not ignore history or risk.

It contextualizes them within a far stronger framework of the person's own stated values and immediate aspirations. Risk is managed for the sake of safeguarding the individual's journey toward his own north star, rather than for the sake of institutional liability.

The old model asks what must be stopped from happening again. The new model asks what this person wants to happen next. The difference is not semantic. It is existential.

Novak begins the workshop not with a lecture, but with a question. He asks the room of twenty-seven care coordinators and lead DSPs, their notebooks open and pens poised, “When you open a client’s ISP for the first time, what are you looking for?”

A silence hangs for a beat. Then answers come, scattered across the conference room at Unity’s Capital Metro office. One man in the back calls for the safety plan. Another care coordinator adds that he looks for behavior protocols. A third person mentions authorized hours and what he is supposed to do.

The instructor, writing each response on a whiteboard, turns back. “And when you open it on a Tuesday morning because you’re filling in for a call-out and you’ve never met this client before, what are you looking for then?”

The same voices are more certain now. They look for what to avoid, the do-nots, and the incident history. He nods, writing these down too. He draws a long, horizontal line beneath the lists.

“So the document functions as an operator’s manual. It is a troubleshooting guide. It tells you the human being's malfunction codes and the safety procedures to follow when an alarm sounds.” He lets that sit. “Is that wrong?”

Another silence follows, thicker this time — it isn't a comfortable question. These professionals pride themselves on their dedication. To imply they see human beings as machines is a provocation. The facilitator notes that Annette Dubois, a lead DSP with eleven years at Unity, shifts in her chair with her arms crossed.

“It’s not wrong to seek guidance,” he continues. “But it is a catastrophe if that is the only function of the document. You are being trained by its architecture to look for problems, not possibilities. You are being oriented toward prevention, not promotion.”

Novak says, “The standard ISP is a rearview mirror. It shows you where you’ve been. The potholes, the wrong turns. It is essential data. But you cannot drive forward by staring only at the rearview mirror. You will crash.”

He returns to the projector. That image shifts from Leo’s two documents to a simple graphic of a compass rose. He explains that the cardinal points are relabeled. North reads: What we are moving toward. East: Current Strengths & Assets. South: Historical Context (Not Destiny). West: Supports Needed for the Journey.

Novak instructs them to take out an active plan from their caseload. The rustle of folders fills the room. Annette Dubois opens the draft for a woman she supports, Mara. Her eyes skim the familiar sections for diagnosis and behavioral history.

“I want you to perform a simple audit,” Novak instructs. “With a highlighter, mark every phrase or goal that is forward-looking. Use a pen to circle every phrase that is backward-looking. That describes a problem or a past incident.”

For five minutes, the only sounds are the scratch of pens and the whisper of highlighting. Annette’s page for Mara becomes a battlefield of ink. The circles swarm with phrases like “prone to elopement” and “history of aggressive episodes.”

She scans, her highlighter hovering. She finds one forward-looking statement buried on page six. It mentions that the woman enjoys listening to music and may benefit from community concerts. Annette highlights it, a tiny yellow island in a sea of blue circles.

Novak walks the aisles, observing. He stops beside Annette and glances at her marked-up pages. He says nothing, but his presence is a question.

Annette says, “This is depressing.” Her tone is matter-of-fact, not defensive.

He moves on to the next desk. “It is data,” he replies. “Now you have a baseline. The tool is not magic. It starts with a clear-eyed assessment of where you are. And where you are, for most of these documents is stuck in the past.”

He returns to the front. “The backward gaze is not malicious — it is systemic. It comes from liability fears and a funding model that reimburses for mitigating risk, not for cultivating joy.”

“It comes from staff turnover so high that documenting problems feels more urgent than documenting dreams.” He looks across the entire room. “You are the ones who hold the line between that system and a human being. You can choose which document you bring to that meeting.”

He changes the slide. It shows the same ISP page for Leo, but now the backward-looking phrases are grayed out. “The second task is to reframe,” he explains. “Take every circled, backward-looking statement and ask one question. What would need to be true for this to no longer be the primary thing we say about this person?”

He points to a circled phrase on Mara’s plan about requiring full physical assistance for bathing. “That is a fact of the present. That isn't a prophecy. The forward-looking question is what adaptations would allow Mara to direct her own bathing routine?”

“The goal is no longer to provide full assistance. The goal becomes increasing autonomy and choice in personal care. The historical fact remains in his south point, but the direction of travel has changed.”

Annette uncrosses her arms. She picks up her pen and writes in the margin next to the circled phrase. She asks about choice in bathing, or using music to cue steps. She is no longer auditing a static document. She is beginning to draft a map.

The small groups reconvene. Novak calls for a volunteer to workshop a real file in front of the room. Elena Rodriguez, Unity’s Director of Care Coordination, raises her hand and opens a file on her laptop. She introduces James, who is 34 and has cerebral palsy. He is non-verbal, uses a power chair, and has lived in a group home for twelve years.

She projects his current goals onto the screen. The first goal focused on preventing skin breakdown through regular repositioning and skin checks. It included an objective ensuring he would not develop pressure ulcers.

The second goal aimed to ensure safe feeding and hydration through staff assistance and prescribed thickeners. The third goal sought to reduce agitation during personal care routines using consistent staffing and a calm environment. Finally, the fourth goal required him to participate in a structured day program activity three times a week.

Annette says, “James. These are nursing home goals — they’re about his body, not about him.”

Novak nods. “Exactly — they are maintenance protocols — necessary, but insufficient.” He turns to Elena. “What does James love? What does he choose when he can?”

Elena clarifies that he loves plants and spends hours in the group home’s sunroom. She shows a photo of him in his wheelchair with his hand near a basil plant. Last month, on a community outing to the botanical garden, he used his board to say “more” and “stay” repeatedly. He cried and hit his tray when it was time to leave.

Novak walks to the whiteboard and draws a diagram. At the north point, he writes about what James is moving toward. Novak says, “The greenhouse caused a strong behavioral response. The old framework reads that as a problem to avoid. What does the forward-looking framework read it as?”

A care coordinator from the Pine Ridge office leans forward. “It reads it as a signal. He was trying to communicate a strong preference.”

Novak says, “Yes.” He writes about deep engagement with horticulture under the north point. “The behavior is not the focus. The desire behind it is the focus. Our job is to decode the desire and build care around it. Now, we audit the existing goals through this lens.”

When asked about the goal to prevent skin breakdown, several voices identify it as backward-looking. Novak says, “Reframe it. Ask the model's question. What would need to be true for skin integrity to be a contextual fact, not the defining goal?”

Elena considers the equipment needed, such as a pressure-relieving cushion. James could also use his communication board to indicate discomfort. The goal transforms from preventing breakdown to increasing James's autonomy and comfort in managing his own health.

Novak says, “Let's draft a forward-looking goal for James.” He erases the old list and starts a new one on the whiteboard.

The primary goal states that James will direct and engage in horticultural activities of his choosing to cultivate joy, build communicative autonomy, and connect with a community of shared interest.

He emphasizes the language used. James will direct. The outcomes are joy, autonomy, and community — the medical necessities are embedded within the plan. He then writes several sub-points.

First, the team will collaborate with an occupational therapist to secure an adaptive gardening workstation. These items will maximize James’s physical range of motion and choice. Second, they planned to develop a personalized pictorial communication board centered on gardening actions and transition cues.

The third support involves partnering with the Green Roots Adaptive Horticulture Program to establish a weekly schedule. This includes a pre-visit orientation to co-create a transition plan with the help of his new board. Skin integrity checks will be integrated into the gardening routine as a natural pause point. He will use his communication board to provide input.

He steps back. “This is the same human being — the same diagnosis. The same risks. But the document is now a map toward a life he wants, not a manual for maintaining a body he inhabits. His south point informs the assistance in the west, but does not dictate the destination in the north.”

Elena asks about the MCO and their need for justification. She notes that the old goals are standard. The MCO will want to know why they are talking about greenhouses instead of pressure ulcers.

He explains the clinical outcomes achieved when treatment plans are aligned with intrinsic motivations. He pulls up a study showing that interest-driven programming reduces challenging behaviors for non-verbal individuals.

“You justify it with the evidence,” he says. “You frame the greenhouse as the therapeutic modality through which all his other goals will be advanced. You are not deleting medical necessity. You are demonstrating how to achieve it in a way that honors humanity.”

The energy in the room is a mixture of resistance and relief. For some, it feels like permission. For others, it feels like being asked to build a house without a blueprint. Both reactions are necessary. The unlearning has begun.

The workshop's whiteboard holds a prototype. The concept is clear. Now you need the tool. The Forward-Looking Compass is not a philosophy to admire. It is a device to build.

You begin not with a goal, but with a value. This is the magnetic north of the entire instrument. Core values are the fundamental principles that give an individual's life meaning. For James, a core value might be nurturing. For Leo, it could be self-reliance.

Your first task is to listen for the value beneath the stated want. Wanting to make pancakes points to mastery. Wanting to go to a baseball game might signal community belonging. You excavate using direct questions about what feels most important. For non-verbal individuals, you observe patterns of engagement. The value is the why behind the what.

Document this in the person's own words. In the draft, this becomes the central statement about what matters most. This is your true north.

The second component is Current Aspirations. These are the short-term, real goals that align with the core value. They must be specific, actionable, and owned by the person.

Learning to make pancakes or visiting the greenhouse are aspirations. Frame them in positive, active language. List them as person-centered directives. In the plan, this section is titled moving Toward: Our Current Landmarks.

The third component is Long-Term Vision. This is the desired future state that the aspirations lead toward. It is a vivid, descriptive picture.

For James, the vision might involve being known in a community of gardeners. For someone seeking self-reliance, it might involve managing a morning routine and using public transit. Use the person's own sensory details. Title this section on the Horizon: The Picture We’re Creating.

The fourth component is Present-Moment Clarity — this is the honest acknowledgment of current reality. It includes diagnoses, historical contexts, and risks.

This is the south point of the tool. The critical shift is to list these facts as parameters for designing better assistance. This section is titled our Starting Point: Current Reality & Context.

These four components interact. The core value pulls everything toward it. The current aspirations are tested against it to see if they move toward self-reliance or nurturing.

The long-term vision is illuminated by it. The present-moment clarity informs the assistance needed to move from the starting point toward the landmarks. This is done without losing sight of the horizon.

To build one, use a single page — divide it into four quadrants. In the top left, place What Matters Most and write the core value statement. In the top right, place moving Toward and list three to five current aspirations. In the bottom right, place on the Horizon and write the narrative vision. In the bottom left, place our Starting Point and list the key contextual facts.

Then, draw a circle in the center to synthesize. Ask what specific equipment or staffing is required to achieve the first landmark while honoring what matters most. This is your care design. It flows from the interaction of all four points.

The power of this tool lies in its use for daily decisions. When a choice arises about a new outing or adjusting a routine, you run it through the model.

Does this align with the core value? Does it advance a current aspiration? Given our present reality, what assistance must be in place to make this safe? If it passes, you proceed.

The tool works because it begins with a question the system has forgotten to ask. What matters most? An answer is the only true starting point for a journey worth supporting. Everything else is just maintenance. And maintenance, as Annette now sees through Silas’s window is a poor substitute for a life.
