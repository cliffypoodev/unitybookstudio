The Ohio Department of Developmental Disabilities found that 72% of direct support staff surveyed in 2021 reported “mental exhaustion” as a constant or frequent state. The study used the term “cognitive load.”

The workers, in their own words, called it “being on guard.” Every shift is a calculation: Is he safe? Did she take her meds? Did I document the fall risk, yet by contrast did I miss something?

This calculation is not abstract. The weight of a missed detail is a phone call at 3 a.m. an incident report, a meeting where you explain your choices to a supervisor whose own job depends on proving nothing was missed. This is the atmosphere in which curiosity must grow. It isn't fertile ground.

Consider the architecture of a typical day. You arrive at the group home at 7 a.m. The previous shift’s notes are on the clipboard: “Slept through the night. Vitals stable. Refused morning meds, coaxed into compliance by 6:45.”

Your client, James is in his wheelchair at the table, a bowl of oatmeal cooling in front of him. The protocol is clear—assist with breakfast. Administer the 8 a.m. medications. Transfer to the dayroom for prescribed range-of-motion exercises.

The ISP lists each task as a billable unit of service. Your performance is measured by completion. Did you check the boxes?

Now introduce a different question into this sequence. The Curiosity Protocol asks you to pause, after assisting with the spoon, and ask, “James, what would make this morning better?”

A system hears this as a waste of a billable minute. Your own fatigued brain, calculating the day’s tight schedule, hears it as a threat.

If he answers, and his answer is complex or requires you to deviate from the script, you now have a problem you did not have before. You have created work. You have introduced an unknown variable into a setup engineered to eliminate them.

The professional instinct, honed by years of liability management is to avoid the question. Stick to the script. The script is defensible.

A rational response to a structure whose rewards are for compliance, not for discovery, not laziness. The emotional math is harsh.

The potential payoff for asking “What would make this better?” is a moment of human connection, a flicker of insight. The potential cost is a cascading failure: a missed medication time, a reported behavioral incident, a supervisor’s scrutiny.

When you are already carrying the cognitive load of guarding against harm, adding the load of seeking possibility feels like a professional risk.

Annette Dubois felt this resistance precisely. In her post-training interview with Unity’s quality team, she was blunt. “It felt like one more thing to do. I’m already watching for seizures doing the cares. Now you want me to have a philosophical chat? When?”

The comfort of routine is a powerful sedative. Routine promises predictability. It promises that if you follow the steps, the outcome will be within known parameters. No surprises.

For someone responsible for the well-being of another human being, “no surprises” is often the highest achievable good. Curiosity, by its nature, invites surprise. It asks you to trade the known for an unknown that could be better—or could be worse.

This is the ego barrier. To be curious, you must first admit you do not already have the answer. You must admit that the map you have been following—the ISP, your own expertise—might be incomplete.

For a professional, this is a humble act. It requires setting aside the authority of your position and your experience to become a student of the person you are supporting.

One system does not train for humility. It trains for competence, for certainty. To ask “What do you want?” is to step out of the role of the expert and into the role of the partner. That shift in power is psychologically uncomfortable. It feels like a loss of control.

The irony is airtight. The very structures designed to ensure safety—the protocols, the checklists, the risk-averse scripts—create the conditions that make caregivers most resistant to the kind of inquiry that could lead to deeper, more meaningful safety.

They create a defensive mindset. Your primary goal becomes the prevention of bad outcomes, not the cultivation of good ones. Your allegiance shifts, subtly, from the person to the plan. The plan becomes the thing you are protecting. And the person, inside the plan, grows quiet.

Annette Dubois is a real person. She worked for Unity Supported Living for eleven years. Her personnel file shows a consistent pattern: high marks for task completion, zero formal complaints, three “Employee of the Quarter” awards for perfect attendance.

In her 2022 annual review, her supervisor wrote, “Annette is reliable and follows protocol to the letter. She is a steady presence.” She is, by every metric the organization currently values, an excellent employee.

Her initial reaction to the Curiosity Protocol training was practical resistance. The training materials asked her to integrate open-ended questions into daily routines.

The feedback form from the training audit has two words scribbled in the margin next to the first principle: “No time.”

In the interview, she elaborates. “You show me a card that says, ‘Pause and ask: What matters most to you right now?’ I look at that card, and I don’t see a question. I see seven minutes.”

She sees the time it takes to get an answer, plus the time it might take to do what they ask, plus the time to document why you deviated from the schedule. She has a med pass at 10 a.m. for three people. She has a doctor’s appointment to get Mr. Henderson to by 10:45. “Where in that does ‘what matters most’ fit?”

That isn't theoretical for her. She offers a specific example from the week of the training. “I’m with James. It’s 2 p.m. Activity time. The ISP says ‘structured leisure activity’ for thirty minutes. The plan says we look at a magazine or do a puzzle.”

She is setting up the puzzle. She thinks, okay, try the new thing. She asks, ‘James, what would you like to do right now?’ He doesn’t answer for a minute. “Then he answers, clear as anything, ‘I want to go outside and listen to the traffic.’ Just like that.”

She describes the immediate cascade of problems. “First, it’s February. It’s thirty degrees. Second, going outside requires a coat, gloves, hat, transferring him to the wheelchair, checking the ramp for ice, which is a whole procedure.”

Third, his ISP doesn’t have ‘listening to traffic’ as a goal. It’s not a billable activity. Fourth, she is now alone on shift. If she takes him outside, the other two clients are unsupervised in the living room. So she has a choice. She can say no, which makes the question she just asked seem stupid. “Or I can say yes, and then I’m behind on everything, and I’m breaking protocol about leaving others unattended, and I have to invent a code to bill the time.”

She chose to say no. “I told him, ‘Not today, James. Maybe when it’s warmer.’ We did the puzzle — he didn’t touch it. He just stared out the window. And I felt like a liar — because the question wasn’t real. It was an exercise. It opened a door I couldn’t let him walk through. So why ask it?”

This experience crystallizes her core skepticism. In her view, curiosity without the operational authority to act on the answers is not just useless—it is harmful. It raises expectations it cannot fulfill.

It introduces desire into a context designed for containment. “I know James,” she says. “I know he loves the sound of cars going by. I’ve known that for five years. I don’t need a card to tell me to discover that.”

What she needs is a schedule that has a flexible half-hour built in, and a staffing ratio that lets her take him outside without abandoning her other duties, and an ISP that says ‘community engagement’ instead of ‘structured leisure.’ Asking her to be more curious is like asking her to be more thirsty when you control the water tap. “You’re diagnosing the wrong problem.”

Her resistance is rooted in a deep, earned knowledge of the framework's constraints. She sees the Curiosity Protocol as a top-down initiative that misunderstands the frontline reality.

“They give us a new tool, but they don’t change the toolbox. The toolbox is still this small, locked thing called the ‘billable day.’ You can put a new wrench in there, but if the only bolts you’re allowed to turn are the old ones, the new wrench just takes up space.”

There is a defensiveness here, but the defensiveness of a professional whose expertise is being reframed as a deficiency, not the defensiveness of laziness.

When the training suggests that routine care can become robotic, she hears an indictment of her eleven years of faithful routine. “I show up. I keep them safe. I follow the plans their families and the team made. That’s my job.”

Now you’re telling me my job is also to have epiphanies? I bathe people—I feed people. I prevent pressure sores, but these are not philosophical acts. They are physical acts. If you want more philosophy, hire a philosopher. “But don’t put it on my checklist and then act surprised when the checklist doesn’t get done.”

Annette’s profile is crucial because she represents the majority. She is not a bad employee. Her skepticism is the logical product of an environment that rewards compliance above all else. To her, curiosity looks like non-compliance. It looks like a risk to the stability she has worked so hard to maintain.

The transformation, if it is to reach her cannot be a mere add-on. It must address the very architecture of her day—the staffing, the billing codes, the rigid ISP language.

It must prove to her that curiosity is not an extra task, but a more effective way to complete the tasks she already owns. Until that proof arrives in the form of concrete, practical changes to her workflow, the laminated cards will remain nice ideas for a world that doesn’t exist.

The shift for the caregiver does not begin with an insight, but with a logistical change. In the spring following the training, Unity pilots a revised staffing model in her home, adding fifteen hours of “flex support” per week, time explicitly non-billable and dedicated to spontaneous, client-directed activity.

To the caregiver reading the announcement in the break room, it reads as seven extra hours where she is not chasing a clock.

It is within this sliver of unstructured time that she decides to test the Curiosity Protocol, not as philosophy, but as a practical tool.

Annette tells the quality team later, “I didn’t ask ‘what matters most.’ That was too big. I started with ‘show me.’” On a Wednesday afternoon, during her newly allocated flex hour, she wheels James to the backyard patio. She does not set up a puzzle. She simply sits beside him and asks, “Show me what you look at out here.”

For ten minutes, he says nothing. Then his hand, which usually rests limp in his lap, lifts. His index finger extends, not toward the street, but toward a neglected flower bed at the property’s edge.

It is a tangle of brown stalks and last year’s leaves. Annette follows his gaze. “The old garden?” she asks. He gives a single, soft grunt—a sound she had previously cataloged as non-communicative.

She does not dismiss it. “Did you help with that garden?” Another grunt, this one slightly higher in pitch. She retrieves her phone and pulls up a photo from the agency’s archive, a summer scene from five years prior.

She shows it to him. “Was it like this?” His eyes fix on the screen—he does not look away. His breathing changes. It deepens.

Annette watches his face, a face she has washed and shaved for years, and for the first time she sees focus. A line of tension in his jaw softens.

She realizes she has never before presented him with a question to which she did not already know the answer. She has never offered him a pure choice without a pre-approved menu.

The next day, she uses her flex time differently. She brings a seed catalog from her own home. She opens it on the table between them and points to a picture of a sunflower. “Did you like these?”

He looks. His hand moves again, this time dragging his finger across the page, leaving a faint smudge on the glossy paper. He stops on a picture of lavender. Annette points. “Lavender.”

He makes a sound, almost a hum. She turns the page. He stops on rosemary—she names it—he hums again. This pattern continues for twenty minutes. He shows no interest in vegetables or exotic blooms. His selections are all perennial herbs and hardy, native flowers.

Annette’s prior understanding of James centered on deficits: “non-verbal,” “requires full assistance for all ADLs,” “engagement limited to passive activities.”

His “preferences” were listed as “enjoys classical music” and “tolerates outdoor time.” The music was a staff preference played in the common area.

The outdoor time was a scheduled “community integration” hour that usually meant a slow walk around the block. The connection to horticulture was absent from the record. It had never been sought.

The discovery is not dramatic. There is no moment of spoken revelation. The evidence accumulates through a week of these quiet sessions. She brings different catalogs. She borrows a book on container gardening from the library.

James's responses grow more consistent. He turns pages himself with a fumbling hand when she hesitates too long. He pushes her finger off a picture of petunias and guides it to a picture of thyme. She begins to see a pattern not of random selection, but of discernment.

She documents her observations not in the standard progress note format, but in a separate log she shares with her supervisor and Gabriel Novak.

Her entries are factual, stripped of sentiment. “April 12. Presented six herb images. Client sustained gaze on sage and oregano for 90+ seconds each. Ignored basil and mint. Physical response: increased alertness, decreased self-stimulatory hand movements.”

This is the language the organization understands. She uses it to build a case.

The critical test comes when she proposes a change to the ISP. She requests that the “structured leisure activity” goal be amended to “horticultural therapy and garden engagement.”

Annette, with support from Novak, reframes the request. They argue that the activity promotes fine motor skill use and community participation.

These are billable, defensible outcomes. The amendment is approved.

On a cool morning in may, she wheels James to the patio with a bag of potting soil, a single rosemary plant, a and large container. She guides his hands to feel the soil.

She wraps his fingers around the base of the plant. Together, they place it in the dirt. His hands are in hers, but she is not performing the task for him.

She is facilitating his performance of it. He does not pull away. When the plant is settled, she positions his palm on the soft needles of the rosemary. He leaves it there.

Then he closes his eyes and inhales. The scent is sharp and clean in the morning air.

Annette does not speak. She watches him. James, who “tolerated” outdoor time is now sitting straighter in his chair, his face tilted toward the sun, his hand resting on a plant he chose.

The goal is no longer to fill thirty minutes. The goal is to sustain this state of connection for as long as he wishes.

The shift is complete—she is not following a protocol. She is following his lead. The unlearning is this: she had assumed his silence was an empty vessel. She now knows it is a library, waiting for someone to ask for the right book.

The greenhouse is not a metaphor. It is a twelve-by-eight-foot aluminum structure erected in the side yard of the group home in October 2023. Its purchase order lists it as “therapeutic adaptive equipment.”

Her observation log recorded the pointed fingers and soft grunts that led to this structure. The progression from a potted rosemary plant to a permanent structure follows a documented, bureaucratic pathway.

After the successful container gardening experiment, the caregiver's logs show a new line of inquiry. She notes that James's engagement wanes on cold or rainy days when outdoor access is limited.

She researches inexpensive hobby greenhouses. She presents the idea as a logical extension of the approved ISP goal.

In an email to Elena Rodriguez and Gabriel Novak dated July 2023, she writes, “Current goal: horticultural therapy. Barrier: seasonal weather limits consistent access to therapy. Proposed solution: climate-controlled environment to ensure year-round access to approved therapeutic activity.”

This is the language that moves the hierarchy. She has learned to translate curiosity into compliance.

Her analysis estimates that year-round gardening could justify an increase from three weekly “community participation” units to five, offsetting the capital cost within eighteen months.

The MCO, HealthGuard, approves the amended ISP with the greenhouse specified as the “therapeutic setting.” The structure is ordered.

Her transformation from skeptic to advocate is visible in her own words. In a follow-up interview with the quality team six months after the greenhouse’s installation, her tone has changed.

She is no longer defending her old routine. She is explaining a new methodology. “It wasn’t about being more curious,” she says. “It was about being more specific. The ‘what matters most’ card was too vague. It gave me nowhere to go.”

But ‘show me what you look at’—that gave me a direction. I could follow that. “It became a process, not a question.”

The impact on the client is documented in clinical terms. A system’s own metrics validate the approach. But the human impact exists outside the metrics.

Annette describes a Tuesday in January. The ground outside is frozen, the sky a flat gray. Inside the greenhouse, the air is humid and smells of soil and growing basil.

James is in his wheelchair, a heated blanket across his lap. She has arranged seedling trays on a lowered table. She guides his hand to touch the damp soil in each cell.

He does not resist. When they reach a tray of young tomato plants, his fingers curl slightly around a stem. He holds it. He does not pull it.

He simply feels the delicate strength of it. He makes a sound—a long, low exhale. This isn't a grunt—she recognizes it now. It is his sound for contentment.

She has stopped calculating the time. The flex hours in the schedule create a container where the clock’s pressure recedes.

In this space, her professional mindset shifts. Her primary goal is no longer the prevention of harm, but the facilitation of this specific, quiet engagement.

The risk she manages is no longer the risk of a fall or a missed medication, but the risk of breaking the thread of his focus. It is a different kind of vigilance. It is oriented toward possibility, not liability.

This shift has practical consequences for her work. She tells the interviewer, “I spend less time managing his behaviors because he has less behavior to manage.”

The afternoon restlessness is down by eighty percent on days we’re in the greenhouse. I’m not using as many anxiety meds. I’m not filing incident reports.

The time I waste in the greenhouse, I get back later in the shift because I’m not dealing with crises. “The math actually works.”

A space becomes a tool for connection beyond James. Other clients in the home visit it. A staff member from another shift brings her own succulent to propagate.

The side yard, once a patch of neglected grass, becomes a destination. The resident, in his silent way, becomes a host.

When others are in the space with him, his posture changes. She notes he turns his head toward the sound of their voices, a social orientation previously absent.

Her advocacy extends beyond her own client. She now mentors newer staff on her team. In a recorded peer-coaching session from April 2024, she advises a struggling DSP, “Don’t start with the big question.”

Start with the small observation. ‘You keep looking at that picture. Tell me about it.’ Find the one thing they already do, and ask about that. It’s not an extra task. “It’s just paying attention to what you’re already seeing.”

The skepticism she once embodied has been converted into a grounded, operational credibility. When she speaks about the Curiosity Protocol now, she does not speak about philosophy.

She speaks about cause and effect—she speaks about reduced medication use. She speaks about fewer incident reports.

She speaks about a man who once stared out a window now actively tending seedlings. She has the evidence the system respects.

The final proof is in the replication. Based on the data from the case, Unity approves the installation of two more adaptive greenhouses in other homes the following fiscal year.

The funding justification directly cites the caregiver's logs and the associated MCO report showing improved outcomes. The initiative is no longer a pilot. It is a funded program.

The greenhouse, then is more than therapeutic equipment. It is a monument to a revised professional philosophy.

It stands because a caregiver dared to not know, and then had the humility to learn. It functions because she learned to speak the language of outcomes while serving a different, deeper goal: a life of discernment and quiet connection.

This is the core pivot of the Radical Steward. You move from managing a case to stewarding a life. You exchange the illusion of total control for the reality of complete influence.

You stop asking, “How do I complete this plan?” and start asking, “How does this plan complete the person?” The answer, as she discovered, often grows in silence, in a warm space filled with the scent of earth.
