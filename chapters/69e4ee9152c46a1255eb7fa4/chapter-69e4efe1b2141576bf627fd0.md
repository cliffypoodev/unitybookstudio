You will find codes for Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) and Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADLs). You will find exhaustive assessments of risk: falls, elopement, medication errors. You will find reams of compliance checklists for staff training, home safety, and documentation. The entire architecture is engineered to measure and mitigate deficit. It is a structure built to answer one question: “What can go wrong?”

It never asks, “What could go right?” This is the silent, central flaw—it isn't malice. It is a design feature. As the 2023 KFF analysis of Medicaid managed long-term services and supports (MLTSS) notes, the core incentive for the Managed Care Organization (MCO) is financial predictability.

Its contract with the state payer is a capitated rate—a fixed monthly sum per member. Every dollar spent on a “non-essential” service, every hour devoted to an “elective” community outing is a dollar not retained as profit or reserved for a catastrophic medical event. The MCO’s imperative is to manage risk downward, to keep costs within the actuarial forecast.

The provider agency operates under a different pressure. Its reimbursement is tied to delivering those very units of service—the billable hours of support documented in the Individualized Service Plan (ISP). Its survival depends on filling those hours efficiently, with staff who are trained to follow the plan. The plan serves as the map. And as we saw with James, the map shows only canyons and hazards.

The individual and their family are caught in the middle of this tense triad. The payer wants fiscal control—the MCO wants risk control—the provider wants operational control. All three are forms of containment. What slips through the cracks is the thing that cannot be contained: a human spirit.

This is the caregiving trap. It is a cycle that begins with the best of intentions. A family, exhausted and afraid, seeks help. The machinery responds with a safety net woven from protocols. A plan is written—staff are assigned.

Tasks are completed. Medications are administered—meals are prepared. The physical body is maintained. But the person inside that body—their curiosities, their latent capabilities, their desire for a Tuesday that differs from a Monday—remains unaddressed. A system sees a case to be managed. It does not see a life to be stewarded.

Stewardship is an active, forward-looking practice. Management is a reactive, containment-oriented one. The steward asks, “What is this person’s potential, and how do I help cultivate it?” The manager asks, “What are this person’s liabilities, and how do I minimize them?” The difference is not semantic. It is existential.

It determines whether James ever leaves his porch to feel the textured bark of a sequoia. It determines whether the Chen household feels like one home or two separate medical outposts. It determines if your role as a caregiver, whether you are a family member or a professional is that of a warden or a guide.

The prevailing model, for all its clinical precision, creates a profound stagnation. The individual’s world shrinks to the circumference of their approved services. Growth is not a billed code. Curiosity is not a reimbursable line item.

The spontaneous trip to a diner for pie, the unscheduled hour spent teaching someone to use a video call to see a grandchild—these are deviations from the plan. They are, in the strictest bureaucratic sense, irregularities. And so the trap springs shut—safety is achieved. Vitality is not.

The cost is paid in the quietest currency: the slow extinguishing of a person’s agency. You see it in the resigned shuffle of a daily routine that never varies. You hear it in the flat tone of a conversation that circles only medications and appointments. You feel it in the heavy air of a home that has become a clinical setting.

This is the problem the Radical Steward thesis is designed to solve. That isn't a call for incremental improvement—a better checklist, a kinder case manager. It is an argument for a fundamental shift. A move from managing cases to stewarding lives. The rest of this book is the blueprint for that shift. It is built on five core practices. They form a framework not of compliance, but of liberation.

Consider the genesis of this idea. It did not emerge from a policy white paper or an academic symposium. It was forged in the quiet, lamplit hours of a hospice room. Marisol Flores, in her testimony about her mother’s final months, described the moment the structure’s true nature became clear.

The nurses were proficient—the medications were timely. The paperwork was impeccable. Yet, as she told the interviewer, “Everything was done to her, not for her.” The distinction is everything. The administrative approach had achieved its goal: a medically controlled, compliant decline. Stewardship—of her mother’s dignity, of her remaining moments of connection—was absent.

This is the pivot point. Management is an external function—it operates on the individual. Stewardship is an internal orientation. It operates with the individual. Let us define our terms with surgical precision, for confusion here is fatal.

That method is the administration of constraints. Its primary tools are assessments, plans, schedules, and rules. Its goal is efficiency within defined parameters. It asks: Is the task complete, and is the rule followed? Is the risk documented?

The manager’s success is measured by adherence and avoidance—avoidance of error, of cost overrun, of liability. The individual under management is a subject, a case file. Their preferences are data points to be logged, not directives to be followed. Their deviations are problems to be corrected. The managed life is a contained life, but it is predictable. It is safe. And, as Marisol witnessed, it can be profoundly lonely.

Stewardship is the cultivation of potential. Its primary tools are curiosity, partnership, adaptation, and advocacy. Its goal is flourishing within authentic possibility. It asks: What is yearning to emerge, what strength is underutilized? What connection is waiting to be made?

The steward’s success is measured by expansion and engagement, the growth of agency, the depth of relationship, the richness of experience. The individual in stewardship is a partner, a co-author. Their preferences are the compass. Their deviations are information, often the most valuable kind. The stewarded life is an unfolding life. It is dynamic. It requires courage.

The shift from one to the other reframes every single action you take. Under management, helping someone get dressed is a task. You complete it correctly by following the proper technique for a sweater, ensuring no strain on a shoulder. You check the box. The goal is a dressed body.

Under stewardship, helping someone get dressed is a ritual. You offer two sweaters. You note which color they look at longer. You narrate the process, not as instruction, but as shared. “Let’s get this blue one over your head. There we go. Now, your left arm.”

The goal is a person who has exercised choice and participated in their own day. The dressed body is a byproduct. The administrative structure, as currently architected, trains and pays for the first. It often penalizes the second. That extra minute for choice is a minute not billed to another task. That noted preference is a datum with no corresponding billing code.

You work within the system’s framework of tasks while holding fast to the steward’s framework of intent. This isn't about adding “kindness” to this model. It is about replacing the foundational blueprint. A kind manager is still a manager. Their goal remains containment, just with a softer voice. A steward has a different goal entirely: liberation.

Your role transforms. You are no longer a service delivery mechanism. You are a bridge builder, a gardener. As a bridge builder, you connect the individual’s inner world of desires to the outer world of possibility. You translate “I miss feeling useful” into a trip to the community garden to deadhead flowers for fifteen minutes. You see the latent capability beneath the stated deficit.

As a translator, you interpret the clinical language of the bureaucratic machinery back into human terms for the family, and you advocate for the individual’s human terms back into the clinical language the machinery requires for approval. You rewrite “elopement risk” into “John has a strong desire for independent walks to feel the sun; a GPS tracker and a walking partner can support this safely.”

As a gardener, you do not manufacture growth. You create the conditions for it. You provide nutrients (opportunities), remove barriers (unnecessary restrictions), and offer support (encouragement) around the natural, organic trajectory of the person. You understand that your power is not to create the bloom, but to faithfully tend the soil so it can.

This is the Radical Steward thesis. Radical, because it returns to the root (radix) of caregiving: the unconditional belief in the worth and potential of the other person. Steward, because it accepts a sacred responsibility for nurturing that potential without claiming ownership over it.

The five practices that follow are the cultivation techniques for this garden. They are not sequential steps but simultaneous disciplines. The first practice, which we turn to next is the most critical.

It is the act of recalibrating your compass from fear to faith, from asking “What is the safest option?” to asking “What is the most enabling option?” This shift in your internal navigation system must happen before you touch a single policy, a single plan. It is the quiet revolution that makes all the others possible.

The model's default question is a reflex born of liability and loss. It is the question of management: “What could go wrong?” It echoes in every risk assessment, every cautious recommendation. It isn't a foolish question. It is a necessary one. But when it is the only question, it becomes a prison of hypotheticals.

The steward learns to ask a second question, one that must be given equal weight and often, primacy. It is the question of possibility: “What could go right?” What could go right if James visits the forest? He might feel overwhelmed. He might trip. The scent of pine might become a new anchor for his senses. One memory of the giant trees might become a reference point for his own strength.

The manager’s calculus stops at the risks. The steward’s calculus must encompass the potential gains, gains that are unquantifiable by Medicaid but invaluable to a human spirit. Your first discipline is to hold both calculations in your hands at once, the risk of action, and the certain, slow-motion risk of inaction.

The risk of a fall is real. The risk of a spirit atrophying from lack of novel experience is just as real, though it will never appear on an incident report. This is the core tension of Radical Stewardship: you are not dismissing safety. You are redefining it.

True safety is not the absence of all risk. It is the presence of adequate support to manage meaningful risk. It is the difference between a life padded against all stumbles and a life equipped with the balance and resilience to walk forward, knowing a stumble is possible but not catastrophic.

The five pillars of Radical Stewardship form a single, integrated architecture. They are not a linear checklist. You do not complete the Compass and then move on to Curiosity. They operate simultaneously, each practice reinforcing and informing the others. To neglect one is to weaken the entire structure. This framework is the operational blueprint for the shift.

The first pillar is the Compass: Values-Based Decision Making. This is your internal navigation system. As Marisol Flores’s experience reveals, the model's default settings are calibrated to risk-aversion. Your compass must be recalibrated to agency-maximization. The tool for this is the Forward-Looking Compass, a method we will build in the next chapter.

It replaces the historical, deficit-focused assessment with a dynamic document anchored in the individual’s current core values and desired future states. Instead of asking “What supports do you need because of your diagnosis?”, the compass asks “What matters most to you now, and what do you want your days to look like?”

This shifts the planning conversation from managing limitations to charting a course toward meaningful life. The compass ensures every subsequent decision—from daily routines to major care plan amendments—is tested against a simple question: “Does this move us closer to or further from the individual’s stated North Star?” Without this recalibrated compass, you will simply execute managed care with better intentions, drifting back into the safe harbor of protocol.

The second pillar is Curiosity: The Discipline of Active Listening. This is your primary data-gathering tool. The administrative model relies on standardized assessments and checkboxes. Stewardship requires a deeper, more nuanced source of intelligence: the individual’s own words, behaviors, and unspoken cues.

Curiosity is not passive waiting. It is an active, skilled practice of inquiry and observation. It means listening not just to the answer, but to the tremor in the voice that hints at an unmet need behind the stated request. It means observing not just that someone refuses an activity, but what they gravitate toward instead.

As documented in countless caregiver testimonials, including those from families working with Unity Home Care, the most effective supports often emerge from these subtle signals. This pillar fuels the direction with real-time, authentic data, ensuring the North Star remains true to the person, not to a care plan written six months prior.

The third pillar is Authority: Cultivating Empowered Partnership. This governs the power dynamics of the care relationship. The managed care model inherently centralizes authority with the structure, the MCO, the clinician. The individual is the subject of care. Radical Stewardship systematically decentralizes that authority, transferring it back to the individual and their chosen family.

This is about redefining it, not about abdicating professional responsibility. Your authority as a steward derives not from your professional title, but from your fidelity to the individual’s compass and your skill in facilitating their agency. As a 2022 Ohio legislative analysis on supported decision-making affirms, this shift is both ethical and practical; it increases engagement and reduces resistive behaviors.

This means you present options, not directives. You frame requests as invitations. You create opportunities for choice, even in seemingly minor domains. Naturally, you become a conduit for their power, not a wielder of power over them. This pillar ensures the values from the compass are enacted, not just documented.

The fourth pillar is Systems: The Art of Holistic Integration. This is your tactical skill for navigating the bureaucracy. You are not overthrowing the machinery; you are learning to harness its mechanics for your steward’s ends. This requires fluency in two languages: the human language of values and curiosity, and the clinical-administrative language of codes, plans, and justifications.

As David Chen, COO of Unity Supported Living, notes in operational memos, the most effective care coordinators are those who can translate a client’s dream into a defensible ISP amendment. This means you learn how to frame a desire for community gardening not as a recreational wish, but as a therapeutic intervention targeting goals of fine motor skill maintenance and nutritional education.

You understand the MCO’s incentives well enough to present requests in terms of their goals: preventing decline, reducing emergency utilization, improving quality metrics. You weave the threads of the individual’s life into the fabric of the bureaucracy so that the structure, in following its own rules, funds a more liberated life. This pillar is the practical bridge making the other three sustainable within real-world constraints.

The fifth pillar is Resilience: Building Adaptive Capacity. This is the meta-skill for both you and the individual. Caregiving, under any model is fraught with unpredictability, health crises, personal setbacks. An administrative mindset meets unpredictability with tighter control. A stewardship mindset meets it with cultivated resilience.

For the individual, resilience is built through successfully navigating small challenges and solving problems with support. It is the muscle of agency, strengthened through use. For you, resilience is built through a supportive practice community and the psychological flexibility to pivot when plans shatter.

You move from seeking perfect, static solutions to developing robust, adaptive responses. This pillar acknowledges that the path guided by the compass will not be a straight line. It ensures that when the inevitable storms hit, the entire structure does not collapse into crisis management, but bends and adapts, holding fast to its core intent.

Together, these five pillars form a self-reinforcing ecosystem. The orientation provides direction. Curiosity provides the ongoing map updates. Authority ensures the individual is at the wheel. Systems build the road under the wheels. Resilience ensures the vehicle can handle the terrain.

Remove Systems, and the entire becomes an isolated, unfunded philosophy. Remove Authority, and it becomes a benevolent dictatorship. And the compass points to a destination chosen for the individual. A complete re-skilling, not a gentle add-on to your current work.

Conventional wisdom in managed care is not wisdom at all. It is a series of operational assumptions, hardened into policy through repetition and the fear of liability. The Radical Steward framework does not build upon these assumptions. It dismantles them. To adopt this practice is to engage in a quiet, systematic demolition of the old logic, brick by bureaucratic brick.

The conventional wisdom of managed care dictates that planning begins with a historical assessment of deficits. The dominant tool is the Functional Needs Assessment. It catalogs what a person cannot do. The resulting Individualized Service Plan (ISP) becomes a legal document outlining the supports required to compensate for these lacks.

The future is extrapolated from the past. The goal is maintenance, to prevent further decline. As the Jefferson Department of Medical Assistance Services (JDMAS) 2023 provider manual states, the ISP must be “realistic, measurable, and focused on health and safety outcomes.” The horizon of possibility is the next annual review.

The Radical Steward’s guide operates on a contrary premise. Planning begins with a present-moment inquiry into values and a forward-looking vision for life. The tool is the Forward-Looking Compass interview. It catalogs what a person loves, misses, and wants to try. The resulting document is a dynamic guidepost, not a static contract.

The future is authored from aspiration. The goal is expansion, to cultivate further growth. As Michaela Vance of Unity Supported Living notes in her operational philosophy, “The plan is a living document that should feel too small in six months, because the person has outgrown its boundaries.” The horizon is the next meaningful experience.

The conventional orientation points toward risk mitigation. The steward’s direction points toward agency maximization. One asks, “How do we keep you safe from the world?” The other asks, “How do we equip you to engage with it?” Now examine the second pillar, Curiosity.

Conventional wisdom relies on standardized tools and scheduled reviews. Data collection is episodic and formal: the quarterly progress note, the annual reassessment. Listening is often transactional, aimed at confirming the existing plan or documenting a new problem.

The individual’s words are filtered through the clinician’s lens for “relevant” clinical data. A comment like “I’m bored” might be logged as “client reports low mood,” triggering a behavioral health referral, not a conversation about purpose. The model's curiosity is limited to the domains it is structured to fix.

The Radical Steward’s Curiosity is a continuous, skilled discipline of attunement. Data gathering is constant and informal. Listening is active and interpretive, aimed at uncovering the unmet need or latent desire beneath the surface statement. The steward treats the individual as the primary source of intelligence about their own life.

That same comment, “I’m bored,” becomes a line of inquiry. “What used to make a day feel good?” “What’s something you’ve seen lately that caught your eye?” As testimonials from families working with Unity Care Support LLC reveal, breakthroughs in care often stem from staff noticing a pattern, a client always brightening when a certain song played on the radio, and integrating that pattern into the day.

The framework's curiosity is boundless because its goal is understanding, not just coding. Conventional curiosity seeks to fit the person into the plan. Radical inquiry seeks to fit the plan to the person.

The managed care model centralizes authority. It resides with the MCO (which authorizes services), the prescribing clinician (who orders care), and the provider agency (which assigns staff and executes the plan). The individual and family are consultees, often supplicants. Power flows downward. Decisions are framed as professional recommendations or system requirements.

The doctor says you need this. The MCO won’t approve that. The relationship is inherently hierarchical. A 2024 analysis of MLTSS appeals in the Journal of Health Policy found that the most common reason for grievance filings was “feeling powerless in care decisions.”

The Radical Steward framework systematically decentralizes authority. It transfers legitimate decision-making power back to the individual and their trusted circle. The steward’s role is to facilitate the exercise of that power, not to wield it. Power flows outward. Decisions are framed as explored options and supported choices.

This is the model of Supported Decision-Making codified in Ohio’s 2022 statutes and advocated by the National Resource Center for Supported Decision-Making. That isn't about abdicating professional judgment; it is about placing that judgment in service to the individual’s autonomy. The relationship is a partnership. Conventional authority manages the individual. Radical authority empowers the individual to manage their own supports.
