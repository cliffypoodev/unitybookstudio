The standard Individual Support Plan opens with a section titled “Risk Factors.” This is a mandate from the Jefferson Department of Medical Assistance Services, not a suggestion.

The template, used by every Managed Care Organization in the state, requires a comprehensive list of all known medical, behavioral, and environmental hazards to the person. You are instructed to document them, to quantify them, and to create a mitigation plan for each one.

A fall hazard, a choking danger, a threat of elopement, and a hazard of financial exploitation are listed. A risk of non-compliance with medication is also included. The document becomes a ledger of potential harm.

The primary goal, implicitly and explicitly, becomes the management of these entries. A system’s success is measured by the absence of negative events—no falls, no complaints to the ombudsman. This is the architecture of security-first care.

It is a logical outgrowth of a system built on public funding, liability, and the political peril of a headline about a vulnerable person harmed under state care. Its origin is not malice, but a very specific form of diligence.

One problem is not the identification of a threat. That problem is what happens next. When hazard mitigation becomes the dominant organizing principle, choice evaporates.

Consider the physical environment. To mitigate fall hazards, you install grab bars, remove throw rugs, and prohibit the use of step stools. This is prudent.

But when you also disallow the person from hanging their own artwork because standing on a chair is prohibited, you have traded a minor, controllable danger for a major, intangible loss. The resident no longer controls the aesthetics of their own home.

The message is clear: your security is more important than your autonomy. We will make your world sterile to keep you secure. To mitigate nutritional danger, you ensure all meals are prepared by staff according to a dietitian-approved plan.

This is responsible. But when you also refuse the person’s request to learn to scramble eggs because the stove is “too dangerous,” you have traded a teachable moment for a permanent dependency.

The skill is never acquired. The confidence is never built. The message is concrete: your compliance is more valuable than your competence.

The stagnation is gradual. It is a thousand small vetoes. A man with mobility limitations wants to try a public bus route to the library.

The hazard assessment notes unfamiliar environments, potential for missed stops, and the possibility of fatigue. The mitigation plan suggests a staff-driven van transport instead. The bus trip is denied. A world shrinks by one mile.

A woman with a cognitive disability wants to manage a $40 monthly budget for coffee outings. The assessment notes potential for mismanagement or coercion. The mitigation plan suggests staff hold the funds and disburse them upon request.

The wallet remains empty. The dignity of a simple transaction is replaced by the mechanics of a permission slip. These are not hypotheticals.

They are the daily output of a security-first algorithm. The Individual Support Plan, in practice, becomes less a plan for support and more a manual for containment. The person’s life is parsed into a series of hazards to be neutralized.

The caregiver’s role subtly shifts from facilitator to guardian, from ally to enforcer of the hazard protocol. The consequence is a profound, slow-motion harm.

You create what researchers call “learned helplessness”—a psychological state where a person, after repeated experiences of having their choices overridden for their own good, simply stops trying to choose. Initiative atrophies.

Preferences blur into a background hum of resignation. The person becomes a passive occupant of their own life, perfectly protected and utterly stagnant. This is the central tension we must confront head-on.

A system designed to prevent physical and financial harm is, through its single-minded focus, inflicting a deeper psychological and existential injury. It is trading the acute, reportable crisis—a fall, a theft—for the chronic, unmeasured crisis of a life not lived.

The safety-first model operates on a false binary. It presents a choice: control or security. You can have freedom and danger, or you can have security and confinement.

This is a failure of imagination and a betrayal of the stewardship mandate. The radical steward rejects this binary. Your new goal is not the minimization of danger.

Your new goal is the maximization of choice within an honest understanding of threats. One question shifts. It is no longer “How do we eliminate this danger?”

It becomes “How do we support this person to manage this threat, to learn from it, to live fully despite its presence?” The difference is not semantic. It is everything.

One approach builds a cage of well-meaning precautions. The other builds a partnership for empowered living. One sees the client as a collection of vulnerabilities to be shielded.

The other sees the person as a person with the right to make informed choices, to experience natural consequences, and to grow, even when growth is not perfectly safe. The archival record of managed care is written in assessments and incident reports.

The human cost of that record is written in the quiet resignation of people whose worlds have been slowly, systematically made smaller. Your first act as a steward is to see that cost clearly.

To name it. And to decide that autonomy—the power to direct one’s own life—is not the enemy of security, but its necessary, life-giving counterpart. Choice is not a synonym for independence.

In the context of supported living, it is something more precise and more powerful. It is the sustained capacity for self-determination. It is the operational control over the decisions that shape your daily existence: what you eat, when you sleep, who you see, how you spend your time and your resources.

It is the authority to direct your support, not just to receive it. Agency requires two components: opportunity and capability. You must have real options from which to choose, and you must possess—or be supported to develop—the skills and confidence to choose among them.

The security-first model systematically restricts the first and undermines the second. It limits opportunities in the name of hazard management, and it refuses skill-building in the name of protection. That result is a double erosion.

True security, therefore cannot be a static condition of zero incidents. It is a dynamic state of resilience. You find it in a life of purpose and engagement.

A person who is meaningfully connected to their community, who exercises choice daily, who feels competent in navigating their environment is inherently more secure than a person who is isolated and passive. Their well-being is active, not defensive.

They have reasons to get up in the morning. They have relationships that watch out for them. They have a history of solving small problems, which builds the capacity to face larger ones.

This is the security of a lived life, not the security of a stored object. Research compiled by the National Council on Disability and longitudinal studies from states like Oregon and Washington show a consistent pattern.

People with intellectual and developmental disabilities who exercise greater choice and control experience higher self-reported quality of life, better mental health, and more community integration. Crucially, they do not show a corresponding increase in major adverse events.

The feared trade-off—more freedom, more harm, does not materialize in the data. A different trade-off appears: more control, less behavioral crisis, less costly emergency intervention.

The mechanism is practical. When you support someone to scramble their own eggs, you are not just teaching a cooking skill. You are also creating a natural supervisor, the person themselves, who now has a stake in managing the stove’s dials and timing.

The hazard is not eliminated; it is transferred into a context of learning and ownership. The same principle applies to budgeting that $40, or taking that bus ride.

The goal is not to remove the possibility of a mistake. The goal is to make the mistake survivable, instructive, and part of a larger narrative of growth. Maximizing individual control is a more effective pathway to long-term well-being and genuine security than a sole focus on hazard mitigation.

This isn't only a moral imperative, respecting the inherent dignity of the person; it is a superior operational strategy. It addresses the root causes of decline: boredom, helplessness, and social isolation.

It builds the person's own capacity as the primary bulwark against harm. The shift requires a fundamental reorientation of the caregiver’s role. You move from being a barrier between the person and hazard, to being a scaffold beside them as they engage with it.

Your expertise is not deployed to veto, but to enable. You are a coach, a partner in problem-solving. You ask different questions. Not “Is this allowed?” but “How can we make this work?”

Not “What could go wrong?” but “What does this person need to learn or have in place to handle what might go wrong?” This is the stewardship stance. It accepts that danger is an inherent condition of a meaningful life.

Your task is not to erase it, but to equip the person you support to manage it. The safety net you provide is not a cage of restrictions, but a web of taught skills, trusted relationships, and gradual, earned confidence.

The archival record of managed care holds countless support plans where the “Risk Factors” section is dense and detailed, while the “Goals and Preferences” section is vague and relegated to the final pages. This structural hierarchy is not an accident. It is a reflection of system priorities.

The radical steward inverts that hierarchy. You begin with goals and preferences. You derive the plan from them. Hazards are then considered not as prohibitions, but as logistical challenges to be solved in service of those goals.

Control, in this framework, becomes the measurable objective. You track not just the absence of falls, but the increase in self-initiated activities. You measure not just medication compliance, but the number of choices made within a day.

You document the expansion of the person's world, not just the maintenance of its secure perimeter. This is the transition from security to autonomy. It is the move from a defensive posture to a generative one.

You are no longer building a life defined by its limitations. You are building a life defined by its possibilities. The person you support is not a case to be managed, but a partner in an ongoing project of creation.

The dangers are real. But the cost of avoiding them—the cost of a life unlived, is far greater. Your mandate is to choose the harder, more human calculation. To weigh the hazard of action against the certainty of stagnation, and to decide, with clarity and courage, in favor of action.

The shift in goal—from minimizing danger to maximizing control, demands a new kind of document. The standard Individual Support Plan is a reactive record. It catalogs past deficits and present hazards.

It looks backward and inward, fortifying the status quo. To serve autonomy, the plan must become prospective. It must look forward and outward. It must be a blueprint for a future the person actively wants, not a manual for maintaining a present the system passively accepts.

This requires a concrete tool. You cannot simply will a new mindset into being. A replacement, not an addendum. It starts from a different first principle: the person’s values, not their vulnerabilities.

The evidence is not theoretical. It is recorded in the incident logs and cost overruns of the current system. When you prioritize hazard aversion over personal choice, you set in motion a predictable sequence of negative outcomes.

The damage is not a single catastrophic event, but a slow, corrosive decline across multiple domains of health. A standard protocol for a person assessed as an elopement threat or with limited safety awareness is to require one-on-one staff accompaniment for any community outing.

This is a logical security control. But when staffing levels are chronically low—a universal condition in the provider crisis, the control becomes a de facto prohibition. The outing does not happen; the person remains in the home. Day after day.

The clinical outcome of this restriction is documented. Research on psychosocial health for adults with disabilities shows a direct correlation between reduced community engagement and increased symptoms of depression and anxiety.

The person is not just bored. They experience a deep loss of social role and environmental stimulation. Deprived of this, mental health deteriorates.

This deterioration then manifests in what the system labels behavioral challenges. The person may become agitated, withdrawn, or verbally aggressive. These behaviors are not irrational.

They are the logical output of a trapped, under-stimulated life. The security protocol, intended to prevent the hazard of getting lost has created the certainty of psychological distress within the home.

A system then responds to this new behavior with further restrictions or the introduction of psychotropic medications, deepening the cycle. The cost shifts from a missing support worker to a psychiatric consultation and a more expensive, complex case file.

Next, the safety-first model is inherently conservative. It favors the known, staff-performed task over the unknown, person-attempted task. A common example is food preparation.

To mitigate hazards of burns cuts, or improper nutrition, policies often centralize all cooking with trained staff. The person may assist in limited, passive ways, stirring a pre-measured pot setting the table.

One result is skill atrophy. The fine motor coordination required for slicing vegetables dulls. The procedural memory for sequencing a recipe fades. The confidence to operate a kitchen appliance disappears.

What begins as a protective measure ends as a disabling one. The person loses practical competencies they may once have possessed, making them more dependent, not less.

This decline in functional ability is a direct outcome of the hazard-aversion strategy. The system's own caution makes the person more vulnerable to the very helplessness it fears. Financial choice presents another clear pathway to negative outcomes.

To mitigate the threat of financial exploitation or mismanagement, standard practice is to have staff control discretionary funds. The person must request money and justify its use.

This turns every personal purchase—a coffee, a gift for a friend, into a petition. The psychological toll is significant. It strips the transaction of its essential dignity and its educational value.

The person does not learn to budget, to compare prices, to make trade-offs. They learn that their desires are subject to review. The downstream effect is a stunted relationship with money and the world.

Without the freedom to make small mistakes with small amounts, they are never prepared to handle larger budgets. They remain perpetually in a state of financial infancy.

This lack of preparedness becomes a true hazard factor when larger financial decisions inevitably arise, but the system, focused on short-term liability, rarely connects the dots. It creates the long-term vulnerability it seeks to avoid.

The data from state quality reviews and family surveys consistently highlight these interconnected issues. They note the high frequency of choice-related complaints: people wanting more control over their schedules, their social lives.

They document the secondary crises that emerge from these restrictions: increased behavioral health service utilization, higher rates of polypharmacy for mood regulation, and escalating costs associated with managing the consequences of stagnation, not the activities of engagement.

The financial irony is acute. A system invests heavily in containing the hazards of an active life, but pays far more for the consequences of an inactive one.

Emergency psychiatric interventions, crisis team deployments, and hospitalizations for failure to thrive are exponentially more expensive than supporting a worker to teach bus navigation or cooking skills.

Yet the budgeting and reimbursement structures of Medicaid Managed Long-Term Services and Supports are often siloed. The crisis response budget is separate from the community inclusion budget.

This allows the short-term, hazard-averse decision to appear cheaper on one ledger, while its catastrophic long-term cost appears on another, disconnected ledger. One system is financially incentivized to create the very crises it then expensively treats.

Your role as a steward requires you to see this entire causal chain. The restrictive protocol is not the end of the story. It is the beginning of a downward spiral.

You must trace the line from the well-meaning rule to the measurable harm: from no unsupervised community access to increased depression; from staff-only kitchen use to lost cooking skills and decreased self-esteem; from staff-held funds to financial illiteracy and learned helplessness.

The research data provides a stark numerical counter to the security-first argument. It shows, repeatedly, that delays and denials in services that promote autonomy—like supported community activities or skill-building, do not save money or prevent harm.

They create expensive delays. They postpone the development of the person's own capacities, which are the most sustainable and cost-effective safeguards available.

The data argues that the greatest threat is not the possibility of a mishap during a bus trip. It is the certainty of decline if the bus trip is never taken. The physical, emotional, and social health of a person are not separate categories. They are a single integrated system.

A restriction in one area causes failure in another. The security-first model, by focusing myopically on preventing a physical or financial incident, disrupts the entire ecosystem of well-being.

It secures the body while impoverishing the mind and isolating the spirit. The outcome is a person who is, by clinical and qualitative measures, less healthy. Your evidence, therefore is not merely philosophical. It is operational and clinical.

The systematic prioritization of hazard aversion leads to measurable negative outcomes: higher rates of depression and anxiety, accelerated loss of functional skills, increased behavioral crises, and greater long-term financial cost to the system.

That path of maximum security is the path of maximum deterioration. To choose it is to choose a slower, more insidious form of harm, not to choose caution.

The radical steward’s first duty is to reject this false choice and to champion the harder, more generative path that builds a life, rather than merely defending an empty one. Values-Based Decision Making is the first pillar of Radical Stewardship.

You stop asking “What is the safest option?” You start asking “What option best aligns with this person’s core values and promotes their control, while mitigating unnecessary hazards?”

The distinction between hazard and unnecessary hazard is the operational pivot. All life involves some level of threat. Your task is to identify the portion of that danger that is gratuitous, that serves no value-aligned purpose, and to manage that portion intelligently. The rest is the inherent texture of a chosen life.

Consider Gabriel. His initial support plan contained seventeen separate hazard mitigation protocols. They covered everything from shower transfer procedures to a prohibition on using public transportation without a staff ratio.

His goals section listed two items: maintain current health status and participate in community activities. The disconnect is structural. The dangers are specified with concrete, enforceable actions. The goals are vague aspirations with no binding pathway.

When Gabriel’s support team applied a values-based process, they began with a different conversation. They used a simple protocol from the Supported Decision-Making models.

They asked Gabriel a series of open-ended questions about his typical week. Gabriel valued spontaneity. He valued being a regular at a place, not a client on an outing. He valued usefulness, specifically the ability to help someone else.

His frustration was the rigid scheduling of all his activities, the feeling of being a package being delivered. A team then took his existing plan and his assessment and placed them side-by-side with his stated values.

The exercise was not to discard the protocols. It was to interrogate each one through the lens of his values. The prohibition on independent bus travel was based on the danger of a seizure occurring en route.

The mitigation was a staff escort. The value affected was spontaneity and his desire to be a regular. The new question became: how can we support Gabriel to manage the seizure hazard while moving toward his value of independent community presence?

The solution was not the elimination of the escort, but its transformation. Staff would teach Gabriel to use the bus route in a series of graded steps, with a focus on what to do if he felt a seizure aura.

They would then, with his consent, discreetly follow him at a distance for several trips, moving to a check-in system via phone. The unnecessary hazard—being on a bus with zero preparation—was mitigated.

The necessary hazard—the residual chance of a seizure despite the plan, was accepted as the cost of pursuing his core value of autonomy. The escort protocol was redesigned as a teaching and fading tool in service of a value.

This is Values-Based Decision Making in practice. Imagine two overlapping circles. One circle is labeled Safety and Health Necessities. The other is labeled Individual’s Core Values and Aspirations.

The old model operates exclusively in the first circle. The steward’s goal is to operate in the overlap, the zone where security protocols and personal values align and reinforce each other.

When a proposed action falls outside the overlap, you do not automatically veto it. You engineer toward it. You ask: what supports, what skill-building, what environmental modifications would move this action into the zone of alignment?

The process requires a tangible tool. For Gabriel’s team, it was a one-page Values Compass. It listed core values like spontaneity, being a regular, and being useful. It identified barriers such as the escort requirement and fixed schedules.

It outlined alignment actions, including graded bus training and a flexible menu of weekly outing options. With this compass point set—that agency is the cornerstone of true well-being, the work turns practical.

A principle is meaningless without a process. A new stance is unsustainable without new tools. The revolution is not in the slogan. It is in the spreadsheet.
