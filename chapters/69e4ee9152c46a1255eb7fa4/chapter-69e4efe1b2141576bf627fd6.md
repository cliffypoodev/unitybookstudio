The training begins with a binder. It arrives in your inbox or appears on your desk, a PDF with a standard cover sheet. The title is something like “Jefferson Empowerment Waiver: Service Provision & Documentation Standards.” The date is current. The version number is 4.2. You are required to read it, to acknowledge its contents in the learning management system, and to pass a quiz. The document is not short. A 2023 review by the National Council on Disability found the average state waiver manual exceeds 800 pages. Jefferson’s is 1,047. The language is contractual, precise, and dense—it defines “medically necessary.” It lists “covered services.” It details “provider responsibilities,” “documentation requirements,” and “sanctions for non-compliance.”

Page 312, Section 8.4, Subsection C: “All direct support interventions must be documented contemporaneously within the electronic health record, utilizing the approved activity codes and noting any deviations from the planned schedule.”

Page 589, Section 22.1: “Risk assessments for community access must be completed annually, or following any significant change in condition, using Form JDMAS-441b. Mitigation strategies must be explicitly linked to each identified hazard.”

The binder does not contain a chapter titled “The Art of the Question.” It does not have a module on “Listening for Latent Desire.” Its primary concern is not the inner landscape of the person you support. Its concern is fidelity—fidelity to the service definition, to the billing code, and to the regulatory boundary. An engineered outcome, not an accident. The managed care system is a massive, distributed risk-management engine. Its financial and legal architecture transfers liability from the public payer to the managed care organization, and from the MCO to you, the provider agency, and ultimately to the frontline worker holding the binder. Every rule, every required signature is a circuit in that machine. It is designed to channel risk away from the state and into a documented, defensible process. Your training, therefore is predominantly training in that process. You learn to manage the electronic health record, to select the correct drop-down menus, and to write progress notes that align with the authorized hours. You learn the triggers for incident reports, the protocol for medication errors, and the chain of command for reporting a “consumer concern.”

You are taught to see the person through the filter of their plan. The ISP is the operating manual, and your job is to execute its directives. A culture forms from this daily practice. It is a compliance culture. In a compliance culture, the highest virtue is adherence. The primary question shifts from “What does this person need?” to “Is this service covered?”

The creative impulse—the wondering, the “what if”—becomes a liability. It introduces variance. Variance creates documentation gaps, then gaps create audit findings. Findings create financial recoupments. You hear it in the language of the seasoned staff, the ones who have internalized the system’s gravity. A client expresses a wish to visit the county fair. The response is not “Tell me what you love about fairs.” It is “Let me check your community access risk assessment.”

A new aide observes a client’s fascination with a neighbor’s dog and suggests exploring volunteer opportunities at an animal shelter. The supervisor’s reply is pragmatic: “Pet therapy isn’t an approved goal in his ISP. We can’t bill for that.”

The curiosity is still there in the hearts of the people drawn to this work. But the system’s architecture systematically discourages its expression. It redirects that human energy into the safe documented channels of task completion. A result is what researchers from the Human Services Research Institute called “the professional gaze”—a way of seeing that is trained to identify deficits and report anomalies, but not to discover potential. The cost of this is a slow, universal flattening. The relationship becomes transactional; the result is, the care plan becomes a checklist. The life being supported becomes a series of managed events, measured in units of time and documented with correct codes. The vibrant, messy, unpredictable humanity of the person is the very thing the system is designed to contain. This is the water in which you have been swimming. This is the system the Curiosity Protocol is built to dismantle. The training room at Unity’s Capital Metro office holds twelve people. Gabriel Novak stands at the front, next to a blank flip chart. The participants are lead Direct Support Professionals and new care coordinators. Annette Dubois sits in the second row, arms crossed. She has seen trainings come and go. “When was the last time you learned something surprising about someone you support? Not from their file. From them.”

The room is quiet, but a clock ticks. Someone shifts in their chair. Gabriel lets the silence hang; this is the first tool. Annette looks at the question on the flip chart. She thinks of Marcus, who she’s supported for five years in his apartment. She knows his routines and his behaviors. She knows he likes chicken tenders from the diner on Maple Street and that he gets agitated if the bus is late. She reported that agitation in last quarter’s progress notes. Learning something new isn’t in the service plan. Gabriel watches the room. He knows the silence. The average first response time to this question is forty-two seconds. The record is three minutes. “It’s a trick question,” a voice finally speaks. It is Ben, a lead DSP from a group home in the west region. “The file is them. The ISP, the assessments, the behavior plans. That’s the whole point.”

Novak flips to a new page. He draws a simple diagram: a circle labeled “The Record.” Arrows point inward toward it, labeled “Assessments,” “ISP,” “Progress Notes.” He draws a box around the entire diagram and labels it “The Professional Gaze.”

“This gaze,” Novak explains, “is what you’ve been trained to use. It is necessary. It keeps people safe; the key is, it ensures services are delivered. But it has a blind spot.” He draws a second circle, outside the box, and leaves it blank. “It cannot see anything that hasn’t already been documented as a need or a goal. It can only manage what is already known.”

The trainer continues to let them see the empty circle. “The Curiosity Protocol trains a second gaze. Its only function is to discover what isn’t in the record. Its primary tool is not an assessment form. It’s a question you don’t already know the answer to.”

A care coordinator named Priya speaks up. Her tone is practical, edged with the fatigue of prior authorizations. “How do we document that? What’s the billing code for a question?”

Novak does not dismiss it. He writes her question down. “There isn’t one,” he explains. “The Curiosity Protocol is not a billable service. It is a pre-service discipline. It is how you discover what services might actually be needed, beyond what the last assessor saw.”

He references a 2020 study from the Journal of Policy and Practice in Intellectual Disabilities. The researchers had analyzed 150 Individualized Service Plans from three states. They found that 73% of annual goal updates were minor variations of the previous year’s objectives. Only 12% introduced a goal reflecting a new interest or skill developed in the intervening year. The plans, the study concluded were largely historical transcripts, not forward-looking documents. “The system assumes stability, or decline,” Novak explains. “It rarely assumes growth. It doesn’t have a mechanism for detecting it. Why? Because growth is unpredictable; it’s messy. It creates new needs, new risks. From a compliance perspective, growth is a complicating factor.”

Annette uncrosses her arms. She thinks of Marcus again, of the agitation on the bus. Her report had focused on the behavior and its management. She had not asked him what he was so eager to get to. Gabriel sees the shift in her posture. He moves to the heart of the session’s argument. “What if the problem isn’t the client’s behavior, or their rigidity, or their so-called lack of motivation? What if the problem is the poverty of our questions?”

He flips back to the first page, to his opening question. “The compliance protocol trains you to ask questions that verify information. ‘Did you take your medication?’ ‘Are you ready for your appointment?’ ‘Did you have a bowel movement today?’ They are closed-loop questions. Their purpose is confirmation, not discovery.”

Novak clarifies that the Curiosity Protocol is a shift toward discovery. He turns to a clean page and draws two columns. The left he labels “Verification.” The right he labels “Discovery.”

He points to the board. “It is replacing ‘Did you have a good day?’ with ‘What made you smile today?’ It is replacing ‘Do you need help with that?’ with ‘What’s the hardest part of that for you?’ It is replacing ‘Are you ready for your shower?’ with ‘What would make getting ready easier?’”

Gabriel instructs the group as he writes the examples. The room is quiet, but now it is a thinking quiet. The questions are simple, deceptively so. But each one is a door. Each one assumes there is an interior answer, and a perspective that has not yet been recorded. Annette thinks of her own son. She asks him about his day, and he says “fine.” She asks what the best part was, and he tells her about a joke a friend told. The difference is the question. Novak gestures to the flip chart. “This isn’t about being nice—this is a technical shift in operational methodology. Verification questions maintain a known system. Discovery questions gather data about a living person. That data is what allows you to steward a life, not just manage a case.”

He lets that sit. A clock ticks. One system outside the room continues to operate on verification and on compliance. But in here, for this hour, they are mapping a different territory. Novak writes a goal statement common to dozens of plans across the state, drawn from his own agency’s records: Client will improve community integration by attending one scheduled community activity per week with staff support. “Look at the language,” he explains. “Improve community integration, but that’s a professional objective. Attending one scheduled activity—that’s a compliance metric. The entire goal is written from the outside in. It assumes integration is a skill to be improved, and attendance is the measure. Now. What’s the first question a DSP is trained to ask based on this goal?”

Ben answers from habit. “Is the activity on the schedule? Do we have the right staffing ratio? Did the client go?”

Gabriel acknowledges this by writing them under the goal. Verification: Schedule? Ratio? Attendance? “Exactly. These are verification questions; they ensure the service happened as planned. They are essential for documentation. But they tell you nothing about the person’s experience of ‘community’ or ‘integration.’ They cannot tell you if the activity is meaningful, or if there’s a different, undiscovered activity that would be more meaningful.”

Novak initiates a new page. He draws a line down the center. On the left, he writes closed/Verification Questions; on the right, Open/Discovery Questions. “The shift is grammatical,” Novak explains. “Verification questions often start with auxiliary verbs: Did is, can will. They can be answered with yes, no, or a piece of pre-existing data. Discovery questions start with What, How, When, Where, Who, Tell me about. They require a unique, personal response. They cannot be answered from the file.”

He provides a rapid series of examples, citing the common ISP categories. “Nutrition goal: Client will consume a balanced diet. The verification question is, ‘Did you eat your vegetables?’ The discovery question is, ‘What does a satisfying meal feel like to you?’”

“Personal care goal: Client will increase independence in hygiene tasks. The verification question is, ‘Did you brush your teeth?’ The discovery question is, ‘What part of your morning routine feels easiest? What feels like a chore?’”

“Social goal: Client will engage in appropriate conversation with peers. The verification question is, ‘Did you say hello?’ The discovery question is, ‘Who do you feel most comfortable talking to, and why?’”

Priya leans forward, her practical mind engaging. “But the ISP doesn’t ask for that. My progress note needs to document the skill, not the feeling.”

Gabriel calls them back to the reality of the system. “Correct. Your progress note for the MCO will still say, ‘Client attended community activity at library for 60 minutes.’ That is the billable service. But your private note to yourself—the steward’s note—should say, ‘Client reported feeling bored at library lecture, but lit up when discussing the antique maps in the reference room. Potential interest in cartography or local history?’ That discovery is the raw material for the next ISP review.”

He pairs them up. One person plays a DSP, the other plays a person receiving support. He gives each “DSP” a slip of paper with a standard ISP objective. Their job is to first ask the verification question they’ve been trained to ask, and then ask a discovery question based on the same goal. Annette is paired with Ben. Her slip reads: Client will demonstrate safety awareness by utilizing pedestrian crosswalks and checking for traffic 100% of the time. Ben, playing the “client,” sits facing her, his expression neutral. Annette begins, her voice taking on the familiar professional tone. “Did you use the crosswalk when you crossed Main Street today?”

Ben gives the expected answer. “Yes.”

The exchange is complete. The documentation box is checked. Annette feels the hollow efficiency of it. She takes a breath, shifting her mental posture. She thinks of Marcus, of the blank circle outside the box on the diagram. She asks a second question. Her voice is quieter, less formal. “What’s the most annoying part about crossing that street?”

Ben blinks—the script is gone. He improvises. “The light takes forever—and the button is sticky.”

It is a small, specific piece of data. It has nothing to do with safety awareness as a deficit. It has everything to do with the lived experience of navigating the community. That sticky button is a friction point, a tiny barrier to autonomy that no risk assessment would ever capture. Across the room, other pairs are stumbling into similar moments. A DSP tasked with a “money management” goal asks, “What did you buy with your allowance?” then follows with, “What’s something you’ve seen recently that you really wanted to save up for?” A DSP with a medication adherence goal asks, “Did you take your noon pills?” then tries, “What time of day do you usually feel your best?”

The discoveries are not dramatic; they are granular. A preference for afternoons over mornings, a desire for a specific brand of chips. A memory of a father who collected coins. This is the substrate of personhood. Novak calls them back and asks for observations. “It felt awkward,” one DSP admits. “Like I was prying.”

“It was,” Novak agrees. “You were asking a question without a predetermined answer. That creates relational risk—the compliance protocol exists to eliminate relational risk. But that elimination comes at the cost of relational depth. The Curiosity Protocol argues that some relational risk is necessary for growth.”

Annette speaks, her voice thoughtful. “When I asked about the crosswalk button, it wasn’t in the plan. But knowing it’s sticky… that’s actually useful. I could mention it to the city, or we could try a different route.”

The trainer points to her. “There it is. You moved from managing a behavior, using the crosswalk, to stewarding an experience, navigating the street with less frustration. The discovery question uncovered a hidden barrier. Your role is no longer just to ensure compliance with the safety rule. It is to partner in removing the barrier to its execution.”

He taps the blank circle. “A universe of undisclosed preferences and latent desires, not a void. The Curiosity Protocol is the method for its exploration. You begin by replacing one verification question per shift with one discovery question. You note the answer not in the official progress note, but in a separate log. You watch for patterns.”

Novak explains the goal is not to abandon the necessary gaze of compliance. It is to develop a second gaze, running in parallel. One eye on the plan, the other on the person. One question for the record, the next question for the life. He moves to the next flip chart page. The heading is already written: Listening for What Isn’t said. “A discovery question is only the first step,” he begins. “The second, and more difficult, step is to hear the answer. Not just the words. The answer beneath the words, and the answer behind the silence.”

Novak explains that healthcare providers interrupt patients within eleven seconds of them beginning to speak. The primary cause was not rudeness, but a trained efficiency, the provider listening for a specific keyword to slot into a diagnostic or documentation pathway. Once the keyword was heard, the listening stopped. “The compliance protocol trains you to listen for data points,” he explains. “The ‘yes’ or ‘no.’ The ‘did’ or ‘didn’t.’ It teaches you to extract the necessary information and move on. Active listening, in the Curiosity Protocol has a different objective. Its objective is understanding, not extraction.”

He draws a simple spectrum on the board. On the left end, he writes Passive Data Collection. On the right, Active Interpretation. “Most of your documented interactions live on the left. You collect a fact: ‘Client refused medication.’ ‘Client attended library.’ You report it. Active interpretation moves to the right. It asks: ‘What does that refusal mean?’ ‘What was the quality of that attendance?’”

Annette thinks of Marcus’s quiet days. Her notes often read: Client was cooperative but subdued. That was data collection. She had never written: Subdued posture began after phone call with sister. Spent rest of afternoon rearranging books on shelf, a self-soothing pattern. That would be interpretation. Novak lists three channels on the board. “One: The words spoken. Two: The body language accompanying them. Three: The environmental context surrounding them.”

He details the first channel with deliberate slowness. “The words. You are trained to record them literally. Active listening listens for repetition, for avoidance. If you ask, ‘What did you do today?’ and the person says, ‘Nothing. Just the usual,’ that is a verbal event. But if you ask, ‘What made you smile today?’ and after a long pause they say, ‘The bird… the little red one at the feeder,’ the particular, fragile detail is the signal. The ‘nothing’ is the noise of habit. The ‘little red one’ is the signal of a moment that actually mattered.”

Novak explains body language next. “You are not a therapist; you are not diagnosing. You are noticing discrepancies. Do the words ‘I’m fine’ match a clenched jaw and shoulders hunched toward the ears? Does the statement ‘I love going to the park’ coincide with a lethargic gait and a failure to choose which park? The body often contradicts the polite, rehearsed verbal script. Your curiosity should follow the contradiction.”

Finally, context. “The environment is a constant source of data. What objects are always within reach? What is worn, faded, cherished? What spaces in the home are avoided? A client’s insistence on wearing a specific worn-out jacket documented as a ‘behavioral rigidity’ in a support plan might be interpreted differently if you observe him patting its pocket every time he leaves the house. The pocket may hold a photo, a token of safety. The plan sees a problem to manage. Curiosity sees a meaning to understand.”

Priya raises a hand. “This sounds like we’re reading minds. It’s subjective. How do we document it without getting flagged for speculation?”

He does not flinch. “You don’t document it in the official EHR. Not yet. You document it in your steward’s log, the private running record we discussed. You write it as a hypothesis, not a fact. ‘Observation: Client becomes agitated when asked to choose from more than two dinner options. Hypothesis: Decision fatigue, or possible anxiety about making a “wrong” choice that will disappoint staff.’ This hypothesis then shapes your next discovery question.”

The trainer introduces a practical exercise. He asks them to close their eyes for thirty seconds and recall the entrance of the person they support most frequently into a room. “Don’t think of their name or diagnosis. Visualize the sequence—do they stride or shuffle? Do their eyes scan the room or fix on the floor? Do they go first to the window, the chair, the television? What do they touch first?”

The room grows still. Annette sees Marcus. He enters his apartment, keys jangling. He doesn’t look around. His eyes go directly to the thermostat on the wall. He adjusts it by one degree, every single time. She has documented this as “ritualistic behavior.” She has never considered what it achieves for him. It is the first act of control upon entering. It establishes his dominion over the environment. Novak pulls them back to the present. “That sequence is a text. It is a story they tell with their body every day. You’ve been reading it as a single, repeated word: ‘routine.’ But what is the chapter title? What is the theme? ‘Establishing control?’ ‘Creating predictability?’ ‘Checking safety?’ Your curiosity asks that thematic question.”

He offers a reflection prompt. “Tonight, after your shift, take five minutes. Write down one specific, non-verbal behavior you observed. Not a label like ‘agitated’ or ‘happy.’ A description: ‘She twisted the hem of her shirt between her thumb and forefinger for the duration of the doctor’s phone call.’ Then write a single interpretive hypothesis: ‘The texture provided a tactile anchor during a stressful auditory event.’ That is the seed of active listening.”

The difference is between seeing behavior as a symptom to be reported and seeing it as a communication to be decoded. The first ends with an incident report. The second begins a line of inquiry. The room is silent, but the silence is now charged with a physical, logistical energy. They are not just thinking about philosophy. They are calculating the keystrokes, navigating the EHR, envisioning the pushback from care managers accustomed to deficit reports. The revolution will not be filed under a new billing code. It will be typed, subversive narrative note at a time, into the small, open text box the system forgot to close.
