The first rule of the Curiosity Workshop: do not call it mandatory training.

You call it a pilot—you call it a collaborative. You call it a discovery session. You never issue a memo announcing a new protocol that staff must adopt by quarter’s end. That memo is a guarantee of silent resistance, of checked boxes, and of the exact compliance-thinking you are trying to dismantle.

Implementation begins in the breach between two meetings. According to internal planning documents from the agency's rollout in early 2023, Director of Care Coordination Elena Rodriguez schedules a thirty-minute slot following a routine budget review. The agenda item is listed as “Operational Efficiency – New Initiatives.” She does not distribute pre-reading. She brings coffee.

The documented exchange, summarized in meeting minutes is instructive. COO David Chen opens with the fiscal reality: “We’re at 112% capacity on DSP hours. Where do we find time for curiosity?” Rodriguez’s reply does not cite moral imperative. She cites risk.

“We’re losing James Chen. The family is one incident from filing a complaint with the insurer. Reactive care for him costs 18% more in unplanned nursing interventions than our average client. The time is already being spent. We’re just spending it on the back end.”

This is your opening argument. You do not lead with transformation. You lead with the concrete cost of the status quo. You present curiosity not as a soft skill but as a preventative measure.

The data from the agency's internal audit, referenced in Chapter 8, provides the ammunition: clients whose plans are purely safety-reactive incur 23% more in emergency service utilization. Show that number. Then ask the room where they would rather invest those dollars – in crisis response, or in the earlier discovery that might prevent the crisis.

Secure the thirty minutes. Secure the commitment to a test group of no more than five DSPs and one supervisor. This is critical. A large-scale launch is a funeral for innovation.

The trial group must be volunteers, but you curate the volunteer list. You select for the skeptical but respected Annette, not the perpetually enthusiastic new hire. The skeptic’s conversion is your most powerful testimony.

Your next step is resource allocation, not philosophical alignment. You need a room. Not a conference room with a projector, but a space that feels separate from the daily grind – a break room cleaned of posted memos, a quiet corner of a community center.

You need a budget line for coffee, for a small stipend for the DSPs’ time if it falls outside their shift. The stipend is not payment. It is a signal that their insights are valued labor. The 2023 Jefferson state guidelines for provider training reimbursements allow for this under “skills development for person-centered planning.” Cite that code. You identify your facilitator. This cannot be the CEO—it cannot be the director. Authority stifles confession.

At the agency, they use Gabriel Novak, an external consultant, for the initial study. Your facilitator must be someone perceived as outside the chain of command, someone who cannot write anyone up. If you must use internal staff, choose a retired nurse, a former DSP promoted to quality assurance, or anyone whose presence does not imply evaluation.

Finally, you craft the invitation. It does not mention “curiosity” in the subject line. The email subject line from the agency's trial reads: “James Chen Greenhouse Project – Input Session.”

The body text is three sentences. “We’re exploring new ways to support the client's goal of growing herbs. We’re holding a session to hear frontline perspectives on what the man might need to succeed. Your direct experience is crucial.”

You frame the workshop around a specific, actual client goal already uncovered – the greenhouse idea from Chapter 11. You make it about solving a puzzle, not learning a theory.

You hit send.

Then you wait. The success of the entire process hinges on who walks into that room, and the expression on their faces when they do.

A room is wrong before anyone arrives. You see it in the setup. A U-shaped table arrangement implies a presentation. A projector implies a lecturer—a sign-in sheet implies accountability. These are the architecture of compliance—you strip them out. The facilitator arrives first. Gabriel Novak’s method, described in his 2023 facilitator guide for the organization, dictates a circle of chairs—no table.

In the center, a small table holds only three items: a coffee urn, a plate of pastries, and a single, thick volume labeled “Greenhouse Notes.” The notebook is empty. The symbolism is deliberate. One space is for filling, not receiving.

The first staff member arrives. You watch her body language. Annette, the seventeen-year veteran, pauses in the doorway. Her eyes scan the circle.

She sees no podium, no agenda printout, but her shoulders drop half an inch. This is your first data point. She takes a seat. She does not choose the chair farthest from the door. She chooses one in the middle of the arc.

The facilitator does not open with welcome or objectives. Novak’s recorded sessions show he opens with a question, directed to the empty volume. “The client wants to grow basil. What’s the first thing that could go wrong?”

Silence. Then, a grunt from Annette. “He overwaters it. He loves watering. He’d drown the thing in a day.”

The facilitator writes this in the journal. He does not write “Annette – negative.” He writes: “Risk: Overwatering. Root rot.” He then asks the group, “Is that a ‘stop’ or a ‘solve’?”

This is the core mechanic of the Curiosity Workshop. Every identified barrier is immediately categorized. A “stop” is a legitimate safety or regulatory prohibition—a genuine halt.

A “solve” is a problem with a possible solution. That group’s first task is not to generate ideas, but to practice discrimination. According to the facilitator notes, the initial study group classifies seventy percent of their first-list barriers as “stops.” After three sessions, that ratio inverts.

You build the “safe-to-fail” environment through deliberate, graded exercises. The first exercise is always observational, never interactive. You show a two-minute video clip of James in his current day program, not a training video but a raw, unedited clip.

The prompt is: “Write down three things you see him doing with his hands.” No interpretation. No “he looks happy.” Just the concrete actions: he stacks cups, he taps the table in a rhythm, he folds a napkin corner. This forces a shift from assessment to perception. It grounds the room in the specific person, not the general case.

The second exercise introduces low-stakes role-play. The facilitator plays the client. A volunteer DSP must ask him about his day.

The only rule for the DSP is they cannot ask a question that can be answered with “yes” or “no.” The first attempts fail. “Did you have a good day?” is the instinct. A group groans, laughing.

The facilitator, as the client, answers “yes.” The conversation dies—this is the safe failure. The facilitator resets. One group brainstorms an open-ended question: “What was the loudest thing you heard today?” The facilitator, as James, answers: “The bus brake.” A new thread appears.

Peer feedback is governed by a single procedure, printed on a card placed on each chair: “Name the action, not the person. Suggest an alternative.” After the role-play, feedback does not sound like “You did badly.” It sounds like “The question was closed-ended. An alternative might be, ‘What did you have for lunch?'”

The workshop’s middle segment tackles the actual goal. The journal is open to the page titled “Basil.” The facilitator presents the group with a real, physical object: a small basil plant from a grocery store.

He places it in the center of the circle. “Your task is not to make a plan for him. Your task is to list everything you would need to know to help him keep this alive for one month.”

The list begins with technical needs: sunlight, pot size, watering schedule. But Novak’s notes show the critical pivot occurs when a DSP, usually the skeptic, voices the unspoken hurdle. “He’ll want to touch it all the time. He’ll pick the leaves off. He won’t understand it’s not ready.”

A long pause. “It’s a solve,” someone offers. “But we need to know… what does he get from picking the leaves? The smell? The texture?”

The conversation detours—it is no longer about horticulture. It is about sensory feedback. About how to provide that feedback without destroying the plant.

Someone suggests a dedicated “touch plant”—a hardy mint. Someone else suggests harvesting a single leaf for him each morning to crush and smell. The ideas are impractical, then refined. The binder fills not with bullet points from a manual, but with questions. “What does the texture of a leaf provide for him? Can that be replicated another way?”

This is the curiosity protocol in practice. A sustained inquiry into the function of a behavior, divorced from judgment, not a brainstorming session for solutions. The shift is tonal.

The final twenty minutes are dedicated to “fieldwork.” Each DSP is given a simple prompt for the coming week: “Observe one thing James does with a repetitive motion. Note the context immediately before he starts.”

No intervention required. Just data collection. This homework is not reported to a supervisor. It is brought back to the next workshop for the group to unpack. This breaks the cycle of observation-for-compliance and creates observation-for-discovery.

You close the session not with a summary, but with a next step. The facilitator points to the notebook. “We have a list of things we need to learn. Who learns which one?”

Annette volunteers to research touch-friendly plants. Another DSP will discreetly explore with the man what he knows about plants already. The tasks are small, specific, and owned by the group.

The workshop ends—the chairs are pushed back. Naturally, the coffee is gone.

The facilitator’s only assessment, noted in his report is a measure of participation equity: who spoke, who held back, and what the topic was when the quietest person offered an idea. The content of the ideas is secondary. The process is primary.

You do not get transformation in one session. You get a single, practiced skill: the reframe from stop to solve. You get a group of people looking at a basil plant and seeing not a risk, but a series of questions.

That is the architecture you are building. One circle of chairs, one plant at a time.

The volume from the workshop is not a plan. It is a collection of questions. Your next task is to forge those questions into a formal amendment to a legally binding document: the formal plan.

The transition from the circle of chairs to the payer's authorization desk is the most fragile point in the entire process. This is where curiosity meets compliance, where “James wants to smell basil” must be translated into a coded service with a unit cost and a measurable outcome.

Fail this translation, and the workshop becomes a therapeutic exercise for staff, not a change in the client’s life.

You begin with the “Greenhouse Notes.” The facilitator’s report to the care coordinator, as per the organization's 2023 trial does not recommend actions. It presents three structured outputs: the Reframed Barrier List, the Inquiry Map, and the Hypothesis Set.

The Reframed Barrier List takes every item initially marked as a “stop” and shows its reclassification to a “solve.” For the client, the original barrier is: “Client will damage plant due to repetitive picking.”

The reframed version reads: “Client seeks strong olfactory/sensory input from plants. Current plant choice (basil) is too fragile for his tactile exploration. Need to identify a durable, aromatic alternative or a structured harvesting ritual.” This is no longer a deficit. It is a need statement.

The Inquiry Map is a simple flowchart. At the center is the client’s stated value: “I want to grow something.” Branching out are the questions the workshop generated.

“What sensory feedback is primary (smell, touch, visual growth)?” “What existing daily routines can anchor plant care (morning meds, after lunch)?” “What does success look like to him (a full plant, a jar of dried herbs, the act of watering)?” This map does not go to the health plan. It is your internal guide for designing the support.

The Hypothesis Set is your actionable proposal. It is a series of “If-Then” statements grounded in the observations. “If James needs tactile and olfactory stimulation, THEN we will provide a hardy mint plant for daily touching and a basil plant for weekly harvesting.”

“If the client's motivation peaks in the morning, THEN we will schedule plant care as part of his breakfast routine.” “If success for him is the act of tending, THEN we will measure engagement, not horticultural yield.”

Now you must code these hypotheses into ISP language. The Jefferson Empowerment Waiver service definitions are your dictionary. “Community Integration and Support” (code H2015) is often the broadest category.

Your justification narrative does not start with the service. It starts with the valued outcome. The amendment draft from the agency for James reads: “To support the client’s valued outcome of increasing choice-making and purposeful daily activity through horticultural engagement, the following supports are needed…” You tether every requested support unit to that outcome.

You then break the valued outcome into measurable, but human, goals. Not “Client will not destroy plant.” The goals are active and client-centered.

“Client will select which plant to water each day from two options.” “Client will harvest one herb per week for use in his meal.” “Client will report satisfaction with the activity using his preferred ‘thumbs-up’ method.” These are payer-friendly—they are specific, measurable, and time-bound—but they measure agency, not compliance.

The budget impact analysis is non-negotiable. You must calculate the cost. The study at the agency revealed that supporting his goal requires an initial one-time allocation of $150 for supplies and a reallocation of 30 minutes per day from an existing “Community Navigation” hour to a “Horticultural Engagement” activity—a net zero increase in weekly service time.

You present this to the insurer not as a new cost, but as a reallocation of existing resources toward a more effective, person-centered goal. You include the internal audit data showing the higher cost of reactive care for him. You frame the amendment as a risk mitigation strategy.

Your submission package to the care manager has four parts, as outlined in the Jefferson DMA5 provider manual: the amended plan pages with tracked changes, the justification narrative linking goals to waiver outcomes, the budget reallocation summary, and the client’s own documentation of the goal.

This last piece is critical. For him, it is a photo of him holding the basil plant in the workshop, with a staff-written caption quoting him: “This smells like pizza.” You include that photo. It transforms the packet from a clinical request to a human story.

You anticipate the denial. The first reply from the payer—HealthGuard, in the provider's case—is often a form letter: “Requested service not medically necessary.”

The standard provider response is an appeal. Your response as a steward is different. You request a face-to-face meeting, and you bring the DSP from the workshop.

In the documented 2023 review meeting for the plan, Annette attends. When the HealthGuard nurse care manager asks, “How does this address his activities of daily living?”

Annette does not reference the manual. She says, “He hasn’t had a single episode of self-injury during the afternoon since we started the plant routine. He’s too busy smelling the mint. That’s a clinical outcome.” She presents the data the workshop’s “fieldwork” had collected. The care manager pauses. The conversation shifts from “is this covered” to “how did you get that data?”

Securing approval is often a negotiation, not a victory. The final approved plan for the client does not say “Greenhouse Project.”

It says “Structured Sensory Integration through Horticultural Activities,” and it approves the reallocation of the 30 minutes. The language is clinical. The reality in his home is a pot of mint on his windowsill and a small basil plant on the common table.

That system’s paperwork requires a diagnosis-linked framework. Your job is to ensure the lived experience matches the workshop’s intent, not the form’s title.

You update the plan. You disseminate the changes to the DSP team not through a memo, but by reconvening the workshop circle.

You show them the approved ISP language, side-by-side with the original notes. You demonstrate the translation. You show Annette that her observation about his need to touch became the clinical goal “will engage in tactile stimulation with a designated sensory plant.”

You give her the credit. This closes the loop. It proves the process has teeth—that their curiosity altered the legal record governing his care.

The final step is audit-proof documentation. Every note in the daily record must now reflect the new goals. But you train staff to document curiously.

Instead of “Client watered plant,” the note reads, “Client chose to water mint over basil. Smelled leaves for approximately two minutes. Thumbs-up given.” This documentation provides the proof for the next annual review, creating a positive feedback loop.

It shows the payer that the approved support is generating the valued outcome, making the next amendment easier to justify.

The integration is complete when the record is no longer a static record of deficits, but a living document that can be amended by the evidence of a client’s changing interests. The Curiosity Workshop is the engine of discovery.

This translation procedure is the drivetrain. Without it, the engine spins. The wheels never move.

You measure what you steward, not what you manage.

The Curiosity Protocol generates a new class of data. Your traditional compliance metrics—incident reports, medication errors, attendance logs—measure the absence of failure.

They tell you nothing about the presence of a life. After implementation, you must track a different set of signals. According to the organization's 2024 quality improvement dashboard, their post-pilot assessment focuses on three streams: client engagement and system fluidity. Each requires a different tool—client engagement is your primary outcome. You cannot measure “happiness.” You measure choice-making. The simplest metric is the Expansion of the Choice Catalog.

You maintain a running list—a literal catalog—of the choices a client has expressed and acted upon within their file. For James Chen, the pre-workshop catalog contains seven items, mostly related to meal and clothing selection.

Six months post-workshop, the catalog contains twenty-three, including which plant to water, which herb to add to his dinner, which garden center to visit for supplies, and whether to listen to music or a podcast while tending the plants. You track the growth of this list quarterly. The number is crude, but the trendline is everything.

You supplement this with direct participation rates. For each new, non-routine activity born from the protocol, you log the client’s voluntary initiation rate. Does the man go to the windowsill without prompting?

The data from the agency's test home shows that for the first two weeks, staff prompt the plant care 100% of the time. By week seven, he initiates the activity 60% of mornings. You graph this. The prompt-to-initiation shift is a concrete indicator of internal motivation, what researchers call “agency uptake.”

The third client metric is the most nuanced: the diversification of communication. For non-verbal clients, you track the modalities used. Does the person point more? Use a new symbol on their communication board? Introduce a novel gesture?

The agency's logs for a client named Leo show a 40% increase in the use of his “more” card after staff, guided by a curiosity session, begin offering him choices between two different textured fabrics during downtime. The data point is not about fabric. It is about the exercise of preference.

Staff behavior is your leading indicator. You do not measure staff satisfaction with a yearly survey. You measure observable changes in their documentation language.

The agency's quality team performs a textual analysis on DSP daily notes from the trial home versus a control home. They count the frequency of prescriptive language versus inquisitive language.

The test home shows a 35% increase in inquisitive language within three months. This shift in documentation is a proxy for a shift in mindset. You track it monthly with random audits of ten notes per DSP.

You also measure staff-led amendments. How many plan change requests originate from a DSP’s observation, rather than from a scheduled review or a crisis?

In the year following the Curiosity Protocol rollout, the organization sees DSP-initiated amendment requests rise from 2% to 18% of all amendments. This metric is a direct measure of frontline empowerment. It tells you if the curiosity is reaching the document.

The most critical staff metric is voluntary participation in subsequent workshops. After the initial test, the agency offers optional “curiosity rounds” on other clients.

The attendance rate among trial DSPs is 92%. The attendance rate among other staff is 41%. This gap shows you where the culture is taking root and where it isn't. You do not mandate attendance for the second round. The voluntary rate is the true metric.

System fluidity measures the protocol’s effect on the machinery itself. Your key performance indicator: amendment cycle time.

How many days elapse from the identification of a new client interest to the approval of a supporting plan change? Pre-protocol, the agency's average cycle time is 94 days.

Post-protocol, with the structured translation packet and the strategy of face-to-face insurer reviews, the average drops to 38 days. A radical increase in the system’s responsiveness to the person within it, not a soft outcome. You track it for every amendment.

You also track the denial rate. The Curiosity Protocol’s translation step is designed to reduce denials.

The provider's data shows a reduction in first-pass denials for person-centered goal amendments from 70% to 32%. But you track the reason for denials. Are they now about clinical wording rather than fundamental necessity? The change in the reason code is progress.

Qualitative feedback is gathered, but not with open-ended “how do you feel” questions. You use structured debriefs after each workshop, guided by three questions:

“What was one question you asked today that you wouldn’t have asked last month?” “What is one thing you now want to try with your client?” “What is one system obstacle that got in the way of your curiosity?”

The answers are anonymized and aggregated. You look for patterns, not compliments.

For families, you measure their engagement in the curiosity process. Do they contribute to the “Inquiry Map?”

The trial shows that when presented with the volume of questions, family participation in meetings increases by 50%. You measure this by their contribution of ideas to the client’s Choice Catalog.

You present this data internally not in a report, but in a “progress circle.” You reconvene the workshop participants every quarter. You show them the graphs.

You show Annette the line tracking the increase in inquisitive notes. You tell her, “This line is your handwriting.” You connect the data directly to the actions they recognize. This closes the feedback loop.

It proves that the new behavior is not just felt, but recorded and valued by the organization.

Sustaining the protocol, therefore is not about maintaining a program. It is about nurturing a particular type of attention until it becomes the default setting.

You know it has taken root when a new DSP, confused by a client’s behavior, turns not to the policy manual, but to a colleague and asks, “What do you think she’s getting out of that?”

A question is the cultural artifact. The circle of chairs, the basil plant—these are merely the tools that taught the organization how to ask.
