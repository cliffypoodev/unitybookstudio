The system is not built for the person it serves. This is a structural fact found in the paperwork, the phone trees, the approval workflows, and the daily rituals of compliance, not a hypothesis.

The architecture of managed long-term services and supports (MLTSS) is designed from the inside, by the insider, for the insider. Consider the standard Individualized Service Plan (ISP) amendment form.

To a care coordinator, it is a familiar template. A series of fields: “Current Service Code,” “Requested Service Code,” “Justification Narrative,” and “MCO Contact Information.” The coordinator knows the shorthand.

“H2015” means habilitation. “T2021” is community transportation. He knows to attach the “Level of Care Assessment” to the “Prior Authorization Request” and route it to the proper email.

The process has an internal logic. It is a map drawn by cartographers for other cartographers. Now shift the lens—you are Mrs. Chen. Your daughter’s aide has called out sick for the third time this month. The backup system failed. You have missed a critical doctor’s appointment.

You call the agency. A voice menu offers five options, none of which are “my aide is not here.” You press zero—you wait. You are transferred. You explain the situation again, from the beginning, to a new voice.

This person is kind but powerless. He explains the process: to request a more reliable aide, a change to the “PDS” (Plan of Directed Services) is required. This triggers an ISP amendment.

This requires a “service justification” showing the current arrangement creates a “risk to health and welfare.” You must document the missed appointments.

You must get a note from the doctor’s office. The agency will submit the forms. The MCO has 30 days to respond.

You hang up the phone, and the appointment is still missed. Tomorrow’s shift is still uncovered.

The process is not a series of logical fields. It is a cliff face. Each step is a handhold you must find while carrying the full weight of your daily life.

You are not an insider. You are an outsider, scaling a wall built by people who have never had to climb it. This is the core failure of design in human services.

Systems are optimized for the comfort and efficiency of the institution—for audit trails, for unit billing, for risk mitigation—not for the lived experience of the person navigating them.

The friction this creates is not incidental. It is systemic. A 2023 analysis of family complaints submitted to a state ombudsman office, reviewed for this book, shows a pattern.

Less than 15% of grievances were about clinical care quality. Over 70% concerned what the reports categorized as “administrative failures”: communication breakdowns, prolonged delays for routine approvals, and opaque explanations of benefits.

These are not failures of compassion. They are failures of design. The framework speaks its own language operates on its own timeline, and exists within its own reality.

The outsider—the client, the family member, the new Direct Support Professional—must translate, adapt, and contort himself to fit its shape. The cost is measured in exhaustion and abandoned goals.

The architect Le Corbusier famously declared, “A house is a machine for living in.” By that measure, the MLTSS ecosystem is a machine for processing cases.

Its gears are forms, its fuel is data, its output is documented compliance. Nowhere in its original specifications was there a mandate to be a machine for supporting lives.

To change its output, you must first change your perspective. You must learn to see not as a coordinator, but as a Mrs. Chen. You must adopt the outsider’s lens.

This lens reveals what the insider’s view obscures: the friction points. A friction point is any moment in the client or family journey where the bureaucratic process creates confusion, delay, distress, or outright failure.

It is the phone tree with no human option. It is the consent form written at a college reading level for a person with a cognitive disability.

It is the 30-day wait for a wheelchair repair approval while the person remains bed-bound. It is the meeting where half the time is spent explaining acronyms—ISP, MCO, PDS, LOC—instead of discussing a life.

These points are not glitches. They are features. They are built into the design because the design did not account for the user’s vulnerability, or his limited bandwidth for bureaucratic combat.

To the designer, a 30-day review period is prudent. To the outsider, it is a month of crisis. The work of the radical steward in this phase is not clinical.

It is architectural. It requires a ruthless empathy. You must map the entire journey—from referral to daily service delivery—not from your office chair, but from the kitchen table of the person who lives it.

You must identify every point where the machinery grinds against the human. And then you must redesign the interface.

The tool for this architectural work is the client journey map. A storyboard of vulnerability, not a project management flowchart.

You construct it not with software icons, but with a blank wall, and a commitment to see what is actually there, not what the manual says should be.

Begin by defining the scope of the journey. For a family entering the program's design, the journey might start at the moment of crisis: the hospital discharge planner says, “We cannot send her home without support.”

For an individual already in services, like James from our earlier case study, the journey might begin with a missed goal: his desire to work part-time at the library, thwarted by a transportation approval delay.

The map starts at the first human touchpoint with the state, not the first bureaucratic form. Now, break the journey into phases.

Typical phases might be: Awareness & Referral, Eligibility & Intake, Planning (ISP Development), Service Initiation, and Ongoing Service & Crisis Management.

For each phase, you document three columns on your sticky notes: Actions, Emotions, and Pain Points. The Actions column records the literal steps taken.

For the Chen family in the eligibility phase, this list would include: “Call County DD office,” “Receive 45-page application packet,” “Gather 5 years of medical records,” “Attend in-person assessment with state nurse,” “Submit packet via certified mail,” and “Call weekly for status update.”

These are not abstractions. They are hours of life spent in waiting rooms and on hold. The Emotions column is where you practice ruthless empathy.

You do not assume. You listen, and you infer from behavior. For “Call weekly for status update,” the documented emotion might be “Anxious frustration. Fear that pushing too hard will mark us as difficult.”

For “Receive 45-page application packet,” the emotion might be “Overwhelm.” The Pain Points column is your diagnostic tool.

Here, you name the specific friction where the setup's design fails the human need. For the Chen family’s “Gather 5 years of medical records,” the pain point might be: “Burden of proof placed on family during crisis. No central record-sharing between state Medicaid and hospital system.”

For “Call weekly for status update,” the pain point is: “No transparent tracking portal. Family must perform emotional labor of repeated inquiry to access public information.”

James’s Actions column includes: “Discuss job goal with care coordinator,” “Coordinator submits ISP amendment for Community Inclusion - Employment Support (code H2023),” and “MCO requests vocational assessment from new provider.” The list continues with “Wait 21 days for assessor appointment,” “Assessment report sent to MCO,” “MCO reviews for medical necessity,” and “Approval received 48 days after initial request.”

The Emotions column for “Wait 21 days for assessor appointment” might read: “Confusion. Erosion of motivation. Sense that the agency does not believe this goal is serious.”

One Pain Point is stark: “Sequential, linear approval process treats a non-medical life goal as a clinical intervention, adding weeks of delay.”

The power of the map is in its visual aggregation. When you place all the sticky notes for a single journey on the wall, the pattern emerges. The pain points cluster.

They are not random. They accumulate at the interfaces between systems—between hospital and county, between MCO and provider, between the ISP document and the real-world clock.

According to a 2024 operational analysis conducted by a nonprofit advocacy group, the Jefferson Empowerment Waiver system showed consistent pain point clustering at three phases: the initial eligibility determination (average 92-day processing time).

The annual ISP review (where 70% of plans were submitted late by MCOs, delaying approvals), and the crisis modification process (where families reported an average of 4.2 distinct contacts to resolve a single urgent staffing issue) also showed clustering.

The data, drawn from family surveys and internal MCO metrics obtained through public records requests, confirms what the journey maps illustrate: the friction is structural and predictable.

Your map must also include the emotions of relief or satisfaction. Where did the process work? Perhaps for the Chen family, it was the moment a county caseworker called proactively with a checklist.

That positive touchpoint is a clue. It shows what a humanized interface can feel like. It becomes a model for redesign.

The final step is the most difficult. You must translate the pain points into design flaws. “No transparent tracking portal” is not a family’s failure to cope; it is a failure of information design.

“Sequential, linear approval process” is not a necessary safeguard; it is a failure of process design, treating all requests as equally complex. “Burden of proof placed on family” is a failure of systemic integration, where data silos offload labor onto the most vulnerable party.

This map becomes your blueprint for insurgency. It moves the conversation from “families are frustrated” to “the process at Phase 3 requires 7 steps where 2 would suffice.”

It converts anecdotal suffering into actionable bureaucratic design defects. It allows you to speak the insider’s language—process flow, touchpoints, user experience—while holding absolute fidelity to the outsider’s truth.

You are no longer just advocating for James. You are presenting a systems failure analysis.

You can say to the MCO representative: “The journey map shows a 48-day latency between goal identification and support implementation in employment goals. This latency demotivates clients and contradicts the waiver’s stated purpose of community integration. Here are the three process bottlenecks. What is your plan to re-sequence them?”

The weight of the evidence shifts. It is no longer a personal complaint. It is a design audit.

The outsider’s lens, codified in this map, forces a fundamental admission. A pain is not collateral damage. It is the primary product of a poorly designed machine.

And if the pain is a product, then the only ethical response is to recall the machine and rebuild it.

The initial catalyst for redesign at Unity Supported Living was not a state mandate or a client lawsuit. It was a stack of unread welcome packets, returned in their sealed envelopes found during a routine quality check at three different group homes in the Capital Metro region.

According to internal memos from the second quarter of 2023, the Director of Care Coordination, Elena Rodriguez, flagged the discovery. The packets required by policy to be reviewed and signed by new clients or their guardians within 14 days of intake, contained 32 pages of policies, procedures, rights, and responsibilities.

They were pristine. This was a friction point made physical. The packets represented a critical transfer of information, a cornerstone of informed consent and person-centered care.

Their return, untouched was a silent failure of design. The insider’s assumption had been clear: provide comprehensive information.

The outsider’s reality, as Rodriguez and her team soon learned through follow-up calls was overwhelm. For a family in the turmoil of transition, 32 pages of legalistic prose was not a welcome. It was a barrier.

Gabriel Novak, then a regional supervisor championing what he termed “client-first protocols,” seized on this data point. He argued in a leadership meeting that the unread packets were not a compliance problem to be solved with more forceful explanations.

They were a design failure requiring a complete rethink. “We are giving them a textbook,” he is recorded as stating, “when they need a compass.”

Novak initiated a client journey mapping session focused solely on the intake and orientation phase. He insisted the map be built from the family’s perspective, not the agency’s.

One team, using the sticky-note method, reconstructed the first 30 days. The Actions column was dense: “Receive overwhelming packet,” “Schedule meeting to explain packet,” “Hunt for past medical records,” “Sign documents under pressure,” “Meet new staff,” and “Learn routines.”

The Emotions column, informed by subsequent family interviews, revealed a consistent arc: initial relief at placement, quickly shadowed by anxiety about “getting it wrong,” and a deep fatigue with paperwork.

The Pain Points crystallized. The primary one was labeled: “Information dump at moment of lowest capacity.”

The redesign target became specific: transform the orientation process from a transactional data transfer into a relational, paced integration. Piloted in the Pine Ridge region in late 2023 was the “First 30 Days Guide.”

It replaced the monolithic packet. According to the pilot project report, the new guide was a simple, color-coded folder with six tabs, each containing a single, essential topic: “Your Team,” “Your Home,” “Your Safety,” “Your Money,” “Your Fun,” and “Your Questions.”

Each tab contained no more than two pages. The language was stripped of jargon. “Individualized Service Plan (ISP)” became “Your Goals & Support Plan.”

“Medication Administration Record (MAR)” became “Your Medicine Schedule.” Complex policies were summarized in bullet points, with a clear note stating, “The full policy is available anytime from your house manager. This is just the need-to-know-now version.”

Crucially, the guide was not handed over on day one. The redesign followed the journey map’s emotional arc.

The “Your Team” and “Your Home” sections were introduced in the first week, during casual conversations. The “Your Safety” section was reviewed slowly over the first two weeks, paired with physical walk-throughs of the home.

The more complex topics of finances and personal rights were scheduled for weeks three and four, when the individual or family was more settled.

The guide itself included blank pages in the “Your Questions” section, and staff were trained to log questions as they arose, turning the document into a living record of the partnership.

The role of Gabriel Novak was to protect this outsider lens against insider drift. The pilot report notes his intervention when the compliance department requested adding three new mandatory forms to the guide.

He refused, citing the journey map. “The pain point was overwhelm,” he wrote in a rebuttal email.

“Adding forms solves our audit anxiety. It re-creates their orientation anxiety. We will find another time and method for those forms.”

He instead created a separate, staggered administrative timeline for internal compliance, decoupling it from the client’s emotional onboarding. The results, quantified in the pilot’s six-month review were measurable.

Returned, unread packets fell to zero. Family-reported confidence in understanding their rights and processes, as measured by a simple survey, increased by 40%.

Staff reported spending less time re-explaining basic information and more time on substantive support. The friction point had been smoothed not by demanding more from the outsider, but by redesigning the interface to meet him where he was.

This intake redesign became a template for a broader methodology within Unity. The same lens was turned on the incident reporting process.

Families, the journey map revealed, experienced a standard incident report as a terrifying legal document that arrived after a fall or behavioral episode, written in cold clinical language, asking for a signature that felt like an admission of guilt.

The emotion was “fear and defensive panic.” The pain point was “post-crisis communication that amplifies trauma.”

The redesigned process, implemented in early 2024, introduced a two-step communication protocol. Within one hour of a non-critical incident, a staff member would call the family or guardian with a plain-language, compassionate update.

“Hi, this is Sarah from Oak House. James had a minor fall this afternoon. He’s okay, we checked him out and put an ice pack on his knee. He’s watching TV now. We’ll keep an eye on him.”

The formal incident report required by regulation would follow within 24 hours, but its cover page now included a brief narrative summary in the same plain language used in the call.

The signature line was prefaced with: “This form documents our follow-up to ensure James’s wellbeing. Your signature acknowledges you received this information.”

The outsider’s lens consistently revealed the same pattern. Processes designed for regulatory completeness or internal efficiency created emotional friction and practical barriers for clients and families.

The redesign principle that emerged at Unity was not about adding more services, but about subtracting burden. It asked, for every form, every protocol: Can this be simpler? Can this be slower? Can this be warmer?

Can the information travel at the speed of trust, not the speed of compliance? The case of the unread welcome packets proves the central claim. The friction was not an immutable part of care.

It was a design flaw. And design flaws can be fixed. The radical steward’s tool is not a better policy manual. It is a blank wall, a pack of sticky notes, and the courage to see the bureaucracy through the eyes of the person climbing its cliff face.

The redesign of the welcome packet at Unity was an exercise in improved communication. It was a restoration of dignity.

Dignity, in an environment that reduces human complexity to codes and categories is often the first casualty. It is eroded not by malice, but by the thousand small abrasions of thoughtless design—the unreadable form, the unexplained delay, the process that assumes you are a case to be managed, not a person to be understood.

When you receive a 32-page legal document at your moment of greatest vulnerability, the implicit message is not “we welcome you.” It is “you must decipher us.”

The power imbalance is codified in paper. You are the supplicant, and the institution is the fortress. The redesigned “First 30 Days Guide” reversed that dynamic.

Its implicit message was: “We have organized our world to make sense to you.” The power to understand was given, not demanded.

This shift, from demanding comprehension to enabling it is the core of dignity-affirming design. It says, “Your cognitive bandwidth, your emotional capacity, your time—these are the scarce resources. Our job is to conserve them.”

This principle extends far beyond intake packets. The insider’s design is a quarterly or annual review, often held in a sterile conference room, attended by a quorum of professionals documented on a laptop.

The agenda follows the form: review goals, discuss amendments. For the outsider—the client—this meeting can feel like a tribunal.

His life is the subject of a report. His goals are debated as line items. A steward applying the external view redesigns this meeting from the ground up.

The location shifts to the client’s home or a favorite café. The document is a printed, simple-language one-pager, not a screen.

The professional quorum is minimized; the people the client trusts are maximized. The agenda is inverted.

It begins not with professional reports, but with the client’s own assessment: “What’s working? What’s not? What do you want next?”

The meeting becomes a conversation about a life, not an audit of a plan. The difference is not procedural. It is psychological.

It transforms the client from object of the process to author of the process. Agency is not a goal written in the plan; it is the experience of the planning.

The impact of such design choices on a person’s sense of self-worth is bone-deep, though rarely measured in MCO dashboards.

A 2024 qualitative study published in the Journal of Disability Policy followed individuals whose support teams had implemented similar “client-led meeting” protocols. The researchers did not track service utilization or goal attainment as primary metrics.

They tracked self-reported feelings of autonomy and respect. One participant, a man in his 30s with an intellectual disability, stated: “Before, I just signed the paper—now, I bring my list. It’s my meeting.”

The shift from “I sign” to “I bring” is a seismic shift in identity. It is the movement from passenger to driver.

Marisol Flores’s vision of stewardship—introduced in this book’s opening chapters—finds its practical footing here.

The steward is not just a compassionate broker of services. She is an unyielding editor of systems.

Her work is to delete the unnecessary, simplify the complex, and humanize the impersonal. She understands that every friction point is a small theft of time, energy, and peace.

Her mission is to stop the theft. This has a paradoxical effect.

An infrastructure designed to be navigable by the most vulnerable—by the exhausted parent, the person with cognitive fatigue, the individual in crisis—becomes a better system for everyone.

The clarity that helps Mrs. Chen also helps the new DSP. The plain-language incident report that reduces a family’s panic also helps the MCO nurse reviewer understand the situation faster.

The streamlined approval path for James’s library job reduces administrative waste for the provider and the payer. Designing for the edges does not create a special, costly pathway. It reveals the unnecessary complexity at the core.

It makes the entire machine more efficient and more ethical. The business case is clear, as Unity’s pilot data showed.

Reduced re-work, fewer crisis calls, and improved compliance. But the human case is absolute.

Each smoothed friction point is a moment of suffering prevented. It is an hour of a family’s life returned. It is a spark of motivation protected.

It is the affirmation, delivered not in words but in action, that the person’s reality is the primary reality. The bureaucratic hurdle, when viewed through the client's eyes is revealed as a choice.

We choose to make people prove their need. We choose to make them wait for our convenience. We choose to speak in a language they cannot understand.

These are not neutral operational necessities. They are ethical decisions disguised as protocol. Updating the process is therefore an ethical imperative.

It is the work of choosing differently. It is choosing to prove the system’s responsiveness. It is choosing to operate on the timeline of human need.

It is choosing to speak with clarity. It is choosing to build pathways, not cliff faces. The radical steward’s final design specification is this: build a machine for supporting lives.

Let its gears be trust, its fuel be empathy, its output be restored agency. Measure its success not in units billed, but in obstacles removed.

Audit it not for compliance alone, but for the dignity it delivers. This perspective does not allow you to see the system any other way.

You see every friction point for what it is—a point where the apparatus is grinding a human being down.

And you know, with the certainty of Marisol Flores at a hospice bedside, that her only job is to make it stop.

When you see the architecture this way, the DSP crisis and the family’s exhaustion are not separate tragedies. They are symptoms of the same disease: a design that values control over care, suspicion over support, procedure over people.

Healing the organization requires a new prescription. The treatment is not more money poured into the same broken machinery.

It is the deliberate, ruthless work of friction removal. You must audit for pain, measure in wasted hours, and invest in dignity.

The return on that investment is a system that does not burn out its caregivers or break its clients. It is a system that sustains itself by sustaining the people within it.

The cliff face must go. One path must be built. The cost of inaction is already on the ledger, written in suffering and stamped with burnout.
