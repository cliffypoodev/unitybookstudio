Miranda Kael stood in the sterile glow of the Tribunal’s simulation chamber, her reflection fractured across the glass surfaces. The air smelled of ozone and something faintly antiseptic. A prompt hovered in her peripheral vision, translucent and pulsing: Accessing Archive: Case 1147-Kael.M. Personal Resolution Module. She had requested this review six months ago, back when she still believed the Tribunal might grant her closure.

The chamber walls dissolved into a reconstruction of her old apartment—the one she’d shared with him. Every detail was exact: the hairline crack in the bathroom tile, the way the light caught the dust motes above the radiator. Even the smell of burnt coffee lingered, though she knew it was only algorithmic mimicry. Then the scene stuttered, and she was watching herself.

Miranda-prime, the system called it. A holographic projection of her younger self, mid-testimony, frozen mid-sentence. The Tribunal had preserved everything—the tremor in her hands, the way her voice broke on the word safe. This wasn’t just an archive. It was a performance.

A menu flickered beside the projection. Module Utilization Metrics: 14,892 completions. Average emotional engagement score: 8.2/10. Sponsor feedback: “Highly effective for demonstrating escalation patterns.”

Her stomach twisted. They’d turned her fear into curriculum.

The Tribunal’s response arrived before she could formulate her objection. A soft chime, then a voice, smooth and genderless: “Thank you for your inquiry regarding Archive 1147-Kael.M. This module has been classified as socially useful under Permanent Digital Consequence Protocol 9.3. Removal requests require Tier 4 authorization.”

Miranda’s fingers curled into fists. “I didn’t authorize this.”

“Authorization was granted under Clause 17-B of your Restitution Agreement,” the voice replied. A document materialized in the air between them, a single line highlighted: The Beneficiary acknowledges that resolved cases may be utilized for systemic improvement.

She remembered signing it. Desperate, exhausted, still wearing the hospital bracelet. She hadn’t cared about clauses then. She’d just wanted it over.

“That wasn’t consent,” she said.

The system didn’t argue. It didn’t need to. It showed her the metrics again—the thousands of trainees who’d watched her cry, the sponsors who paid for the privilege. This is helping others, the numbers implied. Your pain has purpose.

Miranda reached for the dismissal command. The scene dissolved, leaving her alone in the sterile chamber.

But the notification followed her out.

Your case remains active. Would you like to submit an appeal?

She already knew the answer.

Miranda swiped the notification away, but the words lingered like a watermark on her vision. The Tribunal’s appeals interface unfolded around her—a vast, hollow space lined with floating legal citations and procedural flowcharts. The air here was colder, the light sharper, as if the system had stripped away even the illusion of warmth for this stage of the process.

A new prompt appeared: Appeal Grounds: Select Category.

Her fingers hovered over the options. Data Misuse. Revocation of Consent. Emotional Distress. None of them fit. What she wanted wasn’t listed: I am not a teaching aid.

She selected Other and typed: This is my life.

The system processed her input with a barely perceptible delay. Then, with the efficiency of a machine that had performed this operation millions of times, it generated a response: Appeal grounds must cite specific violations under Tribunal Code. Please reformulate.

Miranda exhaled through her teeth. “Fine.” She pulled up the restitution agreement—the one she’d signed in the daze of aftermath—and began dissecting it line by line. Clause 17-B was the obvious culprit, but the language was airtight. Every loophole had been preemptively closed, every contingency accounted for. The Tribunal’s lawyers had outmaneuvered her before she’d even known there was a battle.

A shadow moved at the edge of the chamber. Not a person—the Tribunal didn’t employ human judges for appeals—but a projection, a sleek avatar with a neutral expression. “Your appeal lacks standing,” it said. Its voice was calibrated to soothe, which only made the words cut deeper. “Clause 17-B explicitly permits archival utilization for systemic improvement.”

“I didn’t understand what that meant.”

“Ignorance of contract terms does not constitute grounds for revocation.” The avatar gestured, and a string of precedent cases unfurled between them. Martinez v. Tribunal. Chen-Li v. All dismissed. All citing the same doctrine: consent, once given could not be retroactively withdrawn.

Miranda’s pulse thudded in her throat. “Then I’ll revoke it now.”

“Revocation requires Tier 4 authorization.”

“Which is impossible to get.”

The avatar didn’t nod. It didn’t need to. This answer was encoded in its silence.

She tried another angle. “How is this helping anyone? You’re just replaying my worst moments to strangers.”

“Module 1147-Kael.M has a demonstrated efficacy rate of 78% in reducing repeat offenses among trainees.” The avatar summoned a graph, its upward trendline glowing a self-satisfied green. “Your testimony is particularly effective in illustrating the escalation patterns of intimate partner violence.”

A sound escaped Miranda’s throat—half laugh, half choke. “So I’m a cautionary tale.”

“You’re a statistical success.”

The chamber seemed to contract around her. She’d known, abstractly, that the Tribunal monetized punishment. She’d seen the sponsorships, the educational licenses, the way high-profile cases became public spectacles. But she’d thought—foolishly, naively—that beneficiaries were different. That survivors were exempt from the machine’s hunger.

She was wrong.

The avatar tilted its head, a gesture meant to simulate empathy. “There is an alternative.”

Miranda went very still.

“You could endorse the module.” The avatar conjured a new contract, its terms glowing gold at the edges. “Authorized spokespersons receive a share of licensing revenue. You’d have input on how your testimony is presented.”

For a dizzying second, she considered it. The money would help. The control would help more. But then she saw the subclause: Endorsement constitutes irrevocable consent to all existing and future uses of archived material.

They weren’t offering her freedom. They were offering to make her complicit.

“No.” The word came out raw. “I want it gone.”

The avatar’s expression didn’t change. “That option is not available.”

“Then what is?”

“You may withdraw your appeal.”

Miranda stared at it. The chamber’s cold light etched every detail of the avatar’s face into stark relief. It wasn’t cruel. It wasn’t kind. Simply the face of a system that had already calculated her every possible move and found none of them consequential.

She reached for the final option—the one she’d been avoiding. Formal Grievance: Systemic Harm. The button was red, pulsing faintly, as if to warn her off.

The avatar stiffened. “That pathway is reserved for—”

“I know what it’s for.” Miranda pressed it.

Alerts blared. Error messages stacked in the air. The Tribunal’s smooth facade cracked, just for an instant, revealing the frantic machinery beneath. A grievance like this triggered audits, reviews, automatic holds on archived material. It was the one lever beneficiaries weren’t supposed to pull.

Then, as suddenly as it had begun, the chaos stopped.

The chamber reset. The error messages vanished. And the avatar reappeared, its voice smoother than ever. “Your grievance has been received.”

Miranda’s stomach dropped. She knew that tone. It was the sound of a system that had already won.

“Processing time is estimated at twelve to eighteen months,” the avatar continued. “During this period, Archive 1147-Kael.M will remain active and available for licensed use.”

Of course it would. The Tribunal hadn’t survived this long by letting appeals disrupt its revenue streams.

The avatar offered her a thin smile. “Is there anything else?”

Miranda looked around the chamber—at the legal citations hanging like dead leaves, at the contract still glowing gold, at the avatar’s empty eyes. She thought of the thousands of trainees who’d watch her cry tomorrow, and the next day, and the day after that.

“No,” she said. “There’s nothing else.”

The notification appeared as she exited:

Your case remains active. Utilization metrics updated: 14,893 completions.

She didn’t swipe it away.

The notification hovered, a persistent red ghost in her peripheral vision. From Silas.

She tapped the air. The message unfolded, a location ping, ten klicks west, deep in the flooded sectors. No identifier, no text. Just coordinates and a single-channel audio file, timestamped three hours ago.

A trap. Almost certainly.

The smart thing would be to ignore it, to keep moving toward the extraction point. Silas was waiting. The job was waiting. But Silas hadn't answered her last four pings. That silence from his end felt different. Thick.

She played the audio file. Static crackled, then a voice, shredded by compression and distance. “—anyone. Junction seven. They're—” A wet cough. “They're not human. The water, it's—” The transmission cut into a hiss of dead air.

Not human. She’d heard that before, from scavengers who’d spent too long in the deep tunnels, breathing bad air. Hallucinations. But the fear in that voice was raw, unprocessed. It dug under her skin.

Her hand went to the pistol at her hip. The polymer grip was cold. She’d need to detour. It would add hours. Silas would be furious.

She marked the coordinates on her internal map. The route glowed blue through the ruined cityscape, dipping into sectors marked in warning orange: structural collapse, chemical hazards, unknown biological activity. The last one was new. The city logs hadn’t mentioned that a week ago.

The access tunnel to the flooded sectors was a throat of rusted metal and dripping concrete. The air changed halfway down the ladder, from the dry, electric dust of the upper levels to a damp chill that smelled of mildew and something else. Something metallic, like old blood and ozone.

Her boots hit standing water at the bottom. The beam from her headlamp cut a shaky path down a corridor swallowed by darkness. Water reflected the light back, black and still.

She moved slow, pistol raised. Every few meters, something plopped in the water ahead. Rats, probably. Or worse.

The coordinates led to a junction chamber, a circular room where three maintenance tunnels met. Control panels lined the walls, their screens dark and shattered. In the center of the room, a figure slumped against a pillar.

She approached from the side, light fixed on him. A man maybe. Wrapped in a scavenger's patched thermal suit. One hand was curled against his chest. The other trailed in the water.

“Hey.”

No movement.

She nudged his boot with her own. Nothing. She crouched, keeping her distance, and shone the light on his face.

His eyes were open, milky and unseeing. Not from death; the cloudiness was wrong. Tiny, thread-like capillaries had burst across the sclera, leaving red maps. His mouth was slack, a trickle of dark fluid dried at the corner. Blood. It looked oily, iridescent under her light.

She reached for his wrist to check for a pulse. His skin was cold, but not the cold of a corpse. It was a deep, pervasive chill that seemed to leach into her own fingers through her gloves. No pulse.

His other hand, the one against his chest, clenched something. She pried the fingers back. They were stiff, resistant. In his palm was a standard-issue data chip, scratched and worn.

She pocketed it.

A sound echoed down the left-hand tunnel, a wet, sliding shuffle, followed by a drip that was too heavy for water.

She stood, pistol sweeping toward the noise. Her light pierced maybe fifteen meters before the darkness ate it. Nothing moved.

Then the dripping sound came again. Closer.

She backed toward the tunnel she’d come from, eyes on the black opening. Her breath fogged in the beam. The shuffle came again, just at the edge of the light. A shape, low to the ground, breaking the surface of the water.

She fired.

The report was deafening in the enclosed space. The round sparked off the wall. The shape vanished.

Silence, thicker than before.

Her comm buzzed. She flinched, almost dropping the pistol. A text-only message from Silas. Two words: Status. Now.

She typed a reply with her thumb, not looking down. Delayed. Found a body. Something's down here.

His response was immediate. Extraction in 90 minutes. With or without you.

She stared at the words. The cold from the dead man’s wrist was still in her gloves.

Another drip. This time from the right-hand tunnel.

She ran.

The ladder back to the upper levels felt a mile long. She climbed two rungs at a time shoulders aching, expecting a cold hand to close around her ankle at any second. Nothing grabbed her.

She hauled herself out of the access hatch and slammed it shut, spinning the manual lock until it groaned in protest. She stood over it, breathing hard, listening. No sound came from below.

Only then did she remember the data chip.

She found a recessed doorway a block away, out of the wind, and pulled out her handheld reader. She slotted the chip. A screen flickered, resolving into a file list. Mostly corrupted. One video file was partially intact.

She played it.

The footage was shaky, shot from a helmet cam. The same junction chamber, but lit by a handheld lamp. The man from the pillar was alive, his breathing ragged over the audio. “Day seventeen. Samples are… inconsistent. The colony spreads faster in standing water. It's not a fungus. It's not a parasite. It's something else. It rewrites the host's nervous system. Makes a… a network.”

The camera panned across the water. Something pale and fibrous clung to the walls just below the surface, pulsing gently.

“They're not individual anymore. They're nodes. I think… I think it's trying to talk. To coordinate.”

A splash off-camera. The man spun, the light jerking. For a second, the beam caught a figure standing in the tunnel mouth. A woman, her skin marbled with the same oily, iridescent pattern as the fluid on the dead man's face. Her eyes were solid black.

She took a step forward, her movement fluid, wrong. Her mouth opened.

What came out wasn't a scream. It was a modulated burst of static that resolved, horribly, into a garbled approximation of speech.

The video cut to black.

She ejected the chip. Her hands were steady. Too steady.

She looked back toward the sealed hatch. The city logs. Unknown biological activity. Not unknown anymore. Documenting itself.

Her comm buzzed again. Silas. 70 minutes.

She had a choice. Bring the chip to extraction, hand it to Silas, let the company decide what to do with a potential bioweapon. Or ditch it in the nearest drain, pretend she never saw it, and run.

The wind picked up, whistling through the broken teeth of the buildings. Somewhere far below, in the dark and the water, something was waking up. And it was learning how to speak.

She stood. She didn't look at the hatch again. She started walking east, toward the extraction point. The chip was a hard, cold rectangle in her pocket. Every step felt like a decision she hadn't fully made yet.

The sun was a dull smear behind the pollution haze when she reached the rendezvous, a derelict landing pad on the roof of an old transit hub. Silas's skiff was already there, engines idling with a low whine. He stood beside the open hatch, arms crossed. He didn't look furious. He looked bored.

“You're late.”

“Ran into complications.”

“Complications don't pay the fee.” He nodded toward the skiff. “Get in. We're burning daylight.”

She climbed aboard. The interior was all scuffed metal and worn synth-leather. She took a seat opposite the hatch, watching him. He didn't ask about the body. He didn't ask about the something in the tunnels. He just settled into the pilot's seat and started the lift sequence.

The skiff rose, the city falling away below them in a grid of ruin and shadow. She could just make out the sector where the access hatch was, a dark blotch near the river.

“Job's done?” Silas asked, not turning around.

“The data package is delivered.”

“Good. Then we're square.” He engaged the forward thrusters. The skiff banked, turning north.

She leaned her head back against the bulkhead and closed her eyes. The chip was still in her pocket. She could feel its weight with every beat of her heart. Silas hadn't searched her. He trusted her. Or he didn't care.

When she opened her eyes again, they were over the greenbelt, the strip of reclaimed land that separated the city from the corporate enclaves. Everything down there was orderly, clean, silent.

“Where are we going?” she asked.

“My contact wants a debrief. Standard post-op.”

“Which contact?”

Silas didn't answer. He adjusted a control on the console, his shoulders tight.

She sat forward slowly. “Silas. Who are we meeting?”

“The client. Who else?”

“You never meet the client. That's the rule.”

“Rules change.” He glanced at her in the reflection of the canopy. “He's got another job. Bigger payout. Wanted to discuss it in person.”

Another drip of cold down her spine. This wasn't procedure. Silas was careful. Paranoid. He didn't do in-person meetings with faceless corporate entities.

“You should have told me.”

“I'm telling you now.” His voice had an edge she hadn't heard before. A forced calm. “It's a good score. We need it.”

She looked out the window. They were heading for a private landing pad atop a spire of blue glass and polished steel—Veridian Solutions headquarters. The company that owned half the greenbelt. The company that had funded the original research in the flooded sectors, according to the dead man's fragmented logs.

The skiff began its descent. Silas's hands were white-knuckled on the controls.

“You already made the deal,” she said, quiet.

He didn't deny it.

“What did you trade?”

“It's not a trade. It's an opportunity.” The skiff settled onto the pad with a gentle bump. He killed the engines. The sudden silence was absolute. “They just want to talk. Ask a few questions about what you saw down there.”

She looked at him. Really looked. The tension around his eyes. The way he wouldn't hold her gaze. “You sold the location.”

“I secured our future!” The words burst out of him. He took a breath, composing himself. “They're offering protection. Clean ident files. A place off the grid. All we have to do is share the data.”

“We don't have any data.”

“You have the chip.” He finally turned to face her. His expression was pleading, desperate. “The man you found. He was one of theirs. A researcher who went rogue. They want their property back.”

So that was it. He knew. He'd probably known about the ping before she did. Set her up to retrieve the chip while he negotiated the price.

A hatch hissed open. Outside, two figures in tailored grey suits waited, their faces neutral. Security.

“Give it to me,” Silas said, hand out. “Please. It's the only way out of this life.”

She stood. She didn't reach for the chip. She looked past him, at the open hatch, at the two suits. They hadn't moved. They were waiting for her to come willingly.

“You should have asked me first,” she said.

Then she lunged, not for the hatch, but for the skiff's emergency toolkit bolted to the wall beside her. She yanked the small fire axe free and swung it sideways into the main console.

Sparks erupted. Silas yelled, throwing up his hands. Alarms shrieked. The two suits outside started forward.

She swung again, burying the axe in the wiring behind the panel. One console went dark. The hatch, powered by the same system began to whir shut.

One of the suits threw himself forward, jamming an arm in the closing gap. The door stalled, grinding against his limb.

Silas grabbed for her. She ducked under his arm and drove her elbow into his ribs. He grunted, stumbling back into the pilot's seat.

She didn't look back. She squeezed through the narrowing gap between the hatch and the frame, scraping her shoulders raw. She tumbled onto the landing pad, rolling to her feet.

The suit with the jammed arm was cursing, trying to free himself. The other one drew a compact shock-pistol.

She ran for the edge of the roof.

It was a hundred-meter drop to the next level. Service gantries and maintenance cables crisscrossed the space between the towers. She didn't stop to plan. She vaulted the low safety barrier and jumped.

The air rushed past. She reached out, her fingers closing around a greasy cable. The jerk almost dislocated her shoulders. She swung, legs kicking, momentum carrying her toward the facing building. She hit the glass wall hard, knocking the wind from her lungs. She clung there, dangling.

Above, on the landing pad, the second suit appeared at the barrier, shock-pistol raised. He fired. A blue arc of electricity crackled past her head, grounding out on a steel beam.

She started climbing down, hand over hand, the cable biting into her palms. The glass was smooth, offering no footholds.

Another shot. This one clipped the cable. It sparked and hissed, fraying. She slid, burning her hands, before she caught herself. Ten meters from the next gantry.

She looked down. Too far to drop.

The suit took careful aim.

She let go.

She fell, hitting the gantry back-first. The metal grid slammed the air from her chest. She rolled, gasping, onto the walkway. Above, the severed cable whipped away into the void.

She forced herself up, stumbling along the gantry. It led to a service door set into the side of the building. The panel was old, a mechanical keypad. She punched in a generic maintenance code she'd used a hundred times before. Red light.

Access denied.

Footsteps echoed on the gantry behind her. Heavy, running.

She drew her pistol and shot the keypad. Sparks flew. The door gave a shudder and slid open a foot before jamming.

She squeezed through into darkness.
