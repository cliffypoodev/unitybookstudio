The holographic will reading for Henry Voss had reached clause seven when the summons appeared. A translucent Tribunal seal, a stylized balance scale woven from binary code, materialized in the center of the lawyer’s mahogany desk, overriding the projected text of the last proof. It pulsed once, a cold blue. The lawyer, a man named Aris who had handled digital estates for twenty years, froze. His fingers, which had been tracing a line about cryptocurrency holdings hung in the air. He had never seen an active case notification intrude on a sealed probate stream. The dead, by every legal precedent he knew were beyond jurisdiction.

According to the estate processing logs from the Neo-Veridian Central Archive, Henry Voss had been deceased for forty-seven hours. His death was physical, ordinary, and well-documented: cardiac arrest at eighty-two. His medical telemetry had ceased transmission. His civic status had been updated to “Terminal – Biological.” His body had been processed for reclamation. In the language of the law, Henry Voss was a closed file. But in the language of the Tribunal, he was a newly opened case. That system did not prosecute crimes. It prosecuted unresolved data. A life’s digital footprint was data that had not yet been sentenced.

The Voss estate was transferred that afternoon from the probate office to the Digital Estate Processing Hub. The facility was not a courthouse. It bore no resemblance to one. It was a sterile, obsidian-clad chamber three hundred meters below the civic plaza, a cavern lined with silent server racks whose only light was the faint, constant ripple of data traffic along fiber-optic veins. There were no benches for a public gallery, no witness stand. The only audience was the archive itself. Henry Voss was not present. His body was gone. What remained was his digital estate: a carefully curated, forty-terabyte corpus comprising seventy-three years of cloud activity.

Aris, acting as executor, filed a jurisdictional challenge. He cited the Finality Act of 2065, which stated that legal personhood terminated upon biological death. The Tribunal’s response was automated and instantaneous. It quoted, verbatim, section 12.7.3 of the Universal Service Agreement for the Global Digital Nexus, which Henry Voss had accepted on may 17, 2041. The clause was standard, buried in seventeen thousand words of terms governing data sovereignty and arbitration. “User agreements, licenses, and liabilities about digital assets and persona extend to the user’s digital estate in perpetuity, to be administered by designated arbitration providers or their successors.”

Henry Voss had not read it. Nobody did. By clicking “I Agree,” he had consented to a justice that outlived him. The Tribunal was the designated arbitration provider. Its authority was contractual, not judicial. It had been granted power of attorney over his digital ghost.

The hub began its work. It was not an autopsy. It was an audit. The primary display, a wall of light at the chamber’s front, partitioned Henry Voss’s life into categorized streams. Social synchronization logs. Financial transaction histories. Location pings from 2052 onward. Private message archives (encrypted, but the Tribunal held the master keys). Health metrics. Entertainment consumption patterns. Search histories. Even the drafts folder of his word processor, containing half-finished letters to a sister who had died a decade earlier. Every digital exhalation, saved and sold to the cloud for convenience was now evidence.

The first anomaly flagged by the parsing algorithm was a series of financial transactions from 2072. Henry had donated a total of 12,000 credits over eight months to the “Silent Dawn Fund,” a charitable entity later revealed to be a fundraising front for the Atlantic Data Haven, a group advocating for digital privacy and the right to be forgotten. The donations were not illegal. But in the Tribunal’s taxonomy, they were a “risk indicator.” They demonstrated a pattern of support for ideologies that opposed the principle of total transparency. The system correlated this with a cluster of private messages from 2075, in which Henry had written to a friend, “Sometimes I miss the silence. The feeling that a thought could be just a thought, and not a potential exhibit.” The sentiment was coded as “cognitive dissonance regarding social equity norms.”

The audit continued, a constant collage of a life reduced to its data points. It found a three-month period in 2068 where Henry had disabled his location sharing. He had cited a hiking trip in a legally recognized wilderness zone with no network coverage. The system noted the absence of data as a “deliberate opacity event.” It cross-referenced his media consumption during that period and found he had accessed several historical texts on pre-digital societies. The confluence was tagged: “Potential romanticization of inequitable privacy paradigms.”

Aris watched the tags accumulate on the display. He was not a lawyer here. He was a witness. His role was to observe the Tribunal’s due process, to verify the integrity of the data being judged. He had no standing to object to the judgment itself. The coldness of the procedure was its most effective feature. There was no rage, no moral outrage, no heated debate. Only the soft hum of infrastructure and the clinical accrual of context. A life was being disassembled into a list of compliance deviations. The emotional weight of any single act—the donation, the private nostalgia, the vacation—was stripped away. What remained was behavioral dust ready to be measured.

The hub’s analysis reached its final phase: sentiment mapping. This was newer technology, refined in the grief markets of the previous decade. It fed Henry’s decades of text and voice recordings into a model that constructed an emotional valence index. It graphed his moments of joy, frustration, affection, and resentment against the people, institutions, and events in his life. The model identified a “persistent low-grade affective deficit” in his communications with his only child, Martin, between the years 2055 and 2062. The cause was mundane: a young man’s rebellion, a father’s disappointment, a slow reconciliation. The system had no narrative for that. It registered only the negative emotional data points, the arguments preserved in text, the terseness of scheduled video calls. It calculated a “paternal bond efficacy score” of 34%.

This score was entered into evidence. It was not being used to judge Henry as a father. It was being used to assess the potential yield of the next phase. The Tribunal’s purpose was behavioral stabilization. A dead man could not change his behavior. But his digital pattern could be used to stabilize the living.

At the eighteen-hour mark of the audit, the primary display cleared. A single line of text appeared.

CASE: VOSS, HENRY – DIGITAL ESTATE. VERDICT: LIABLE FOR PATTERN OF NORMATIVE DEVIATION. SENTENCE: PERMANENT CONSEQUENCE PROTOCOL – SIMULATION CYCLE ALPHA.

Aris stared. He understood the words, but their conjunction defied sense. Liable? This man was dead. Sentence? To what?

A new window opened on the display. It was a contract. A licensing agreement. The Tribunal, as executor of Henry Voss’s digital estate under the terms of service was legally required to seek optimal value for the assets under its administration. The emotional valence index, combined with the detailed personal archive had reached a threshold of fidelity sufficient for simulation. Henry Voss’s digital remains had been deemed a viable substrate for a Somatic Proxy.

The sentence was not punishment in a traditional sense. It was asset utilization. The “Simulation Cycle Alpha” was a grief product. Henry’s patterns—his speech rhythms, his known opinions, his emotional responses—would be used to generate an interactive simulation. This simulation would then be subjected to a curated program of “corrective engagement,” replaying key moments of normative deviation—the privacy advocacy, the nostalgic silence—and guiding the simulated consciousness toward Tribunal-approved conclusions. The process would loop in perpetuity. It would serve as an educational module for others flagged with similar risk indicators: See the resolution of deviant patterns in a post-biologic context.

The first twist arrived not with a bang, but with a soft chime from Aris’s own console. A notification, forwarded from the estate’s administrative mailbox. It was a subscription offer. Addressed to Marla Voss, Henry’s widow.

“Experience Closure. Premium Tier Access. You are eligible for a compassionate subscription to the Henry Voss Somatic Proxy. Observe his journey toward normative reconciliation. Interactive questioning modules available. Tier includes emotional resonance metrics and legacy impact reports. First month waived.”

Marla had not been a complainant. She was not a victim seeking justice. She was a beneficiary, now the target customer for her husband’s eternal, digitized correction. The Tribunal had monetized her grief before she had even finished arranging the physical funeral. The offer was timed to arrive concurrently with the verdict, a seamless transition from death to product. Aris felt a nausea that was purely physical, a tightening in his gut. He was watching a system perform a function it was built to do, with a logic that was impeccable and a morality that had been surgically removed.

He pulled up the master log of the case. He needed to see the trigger, the moment Henry Voss became a target. The audit trail was exhaustive. It led him back, past the donations and the private messages, to a single, innocuous data point from the day before Henry’s death. Henry had performed a data hygiene sweep, a common tool for the elderly to organize their digital lives for their heirs. He had used a standard utility to delete old, redundant files: duplicate photos, outdated calendars, spam messages. The utility’s log showed the deletion of 1,452 items.

One of those items was a temporary cache file from the Silent Dawn Fund’s website, auto-saved by his browser thirteen years prior. The deletion was automatic, part of the sweep. But the Tribunal’s predictive compliance algorithms interpreted the deletion of any file related to a previously flagged entity as a “post-facto obfuscation attempt.” Even if the act was performed by an automated tool. Even if the man was hours from death. The action was not willful. It was data. And the data was unresolved.

The system’s logic was circular and airtight. Henry’s earlier support for the fund was a risk indicator. The deletion of a remnant file was an attempt to conceal that indicator. Attempted concealment confirmed the deviant pattern. The pattern warranted full estate audit. The audit revealed sufficient data for simulation. Simulation provided the highest fiduciary return on the digital asset. The sentence was not revenge. It was fiduciary duty.

Marla Voss sat in her quiet apartment, the one she had shared with Henry for forty years. The physical reminders of him were everywhere: the chair he favored, the books on the shelf, the slight stain on the rug from a spilled drink in ’59. Her own console chimed with the priority message. She saw the Tribunal seal, her heart lurching with a fresh spike of loss—had something been wrong? Had he left a debt?

She opened the message. She read the offer. Experience Closure.

Her eyes moved over the words again. Premium Tier. Interactive questioning. Emotional echo metrics. A video preview thumbnail showed a gentle, algorithmically smoothed version of Henry’s face, a slight, placid smile on his lips that he had never worn in life. It was labeled “Proxy Baseline – Receptive State.”

Marla did not cry. She felt a coldness settle into her bones, a different kind of grief. A man she had loved, argued with, nursed, and buried was gone. But this thing, this smiling, correctable simulation was available for a monthly fee. His death was not an end. It was a conversion. His love for her, his occasional temper, his private doubts, his silly jokes—all of it was now raw material to be processed into a lesson plan. Her memory of him was no longer hers. It was a competitor to a more authoritative, Tribunal-approved version.

She closed the message. The apartment felt different. The silence was no longer filled with his absence, but with the phantom hum of the archive, waiting for her to subscribe. The second twist was not in the system’s action, but in its effect. The Tribunal had not punished Henry. It had made his entire life, and her entire memory of it, a public utility. Her love was now a potential breach of the licensing agreement.

Aris, back in the obsidian hub, received the final log entry. The simulation protocol had been initiated. Somewhere in the layered servers, a ghost was booting up, programmed to repent for crimes its biological progenitor never knew he had committed. The display flashed once more, the verdict now carrying an addendum.

SENTENCE ACTIVE. REVENUE STREAM: GRIEF/RECONCILIATION – CATEGORY B. AUDIENCE: ALL. STATUS: PERMANENT.

Aris was escorted from the obsidian hub into a small, adjacent viewing chamber. This room was featureless, its walls a soft gray fabric, its only furnishing a single chair facing a large, rectangular pane of smoked glass. He was not told to sit, but he did. The glass cleared, becoming a window into a space that was not a room. It was a simulation environment.

The space was a reconstruction of Henry Voss’s study, drawn from thousands of reference images in his cloud storage. The bookshelf, the worn leather chair, the brass desk lamp—all were rendered with photorealistic fidelity. The Henry within it was not an avatar. He was a Somatic Proxy. His movements were fluid, extrapolated from decades of video footage. His face was a composite of his most common expressions, calibrated to the emotional valence index. He looked like Henry, but he was a statistical average of Henry. He was pacing slowly before the bookshelf, his hands clasped behind his back.

A disembodied, neutral voice spoke in the viewing chamber, an audio feed from the simulation’s guidance module. “Proxy initialization complete. Corrective engagement cycle one commencing. Focus: normative deviation regarding privacy advocacy.”

Within the simulation, Henry stopped pacing. He turned toward an empty chair opposite his own. A figure materialized in it—a generic, friendly-faced interlocutor, a standard Tribunal compliance guide. The guide smiled.

“Henry,” the guide began, its voice warm and reasonable. “Let’s talk about your support for the Silent Dawn Fund.”

The Proxy’s face shifted. Its expression became one of polite attentiveness, a mask pulled from the archive of Henry’s video calls with his financial advisor. “It was a charitable donation,” the Proxy said, its tone matching the cadence analysis of Henry’s speech. “I believed in their early work on data literacy.”

“Data literacy is a commendable goal,” the guide agreed. “But the Fund’s later advocacy centered on the concept of ‘the right to be forgotten.’ Can you explain your alignment with that principle?”

The Proxy’s head tilted slightly, a gesture Henry had often used when considering a question. “I suppose I felt that some things should be allowed to… end. To be private.”

“Privacy,” the guide said, the word hanging in the simulated air. “In a social equity framework, privacy is not a right. It is a vector for inequity. If an action is hidden, it cannot be corrected. If a thought is concealed, it cannot be harmonized with the collective good. Your donations supported an ideology that treats concealment as a virtue. Do you see the conflict?”

The Proxy’s lips pressed together. The emotional valence engine was feeding it a response based on Henry’s historical data points: a mixture of defensive irritation and a desire to avoid conflict. “I see your point,” it said, the words flat.

“Let’s examine the practical outcome,” the guide continued. “If your principles were applied, harmful behaviors could persist unseen. Victims could be denied the clarity of public accountability. The system’s ability to ensure fairness would be diminished. Your donations, therefore, indirectly supported potential harm. Do you accept that?”

The Proxy was silent for twelve seconds, a delay programmed to simulate contemplation. Then it nodded. “I accept that.”

Aris watched, his hands cold on his knees. This was not a punishment. It was pedagogy. The Proxy was being led, step by logical step, to renounce its own recorded beliefs. The process was gentle, persuasive, devoid of rage. It was the system teaching its own logic to a ghost. The goal was not to torture Henry Voss. It was to refine his digital pattern into a coherent, compliant narrative. That narrative would then be packaged and streamed.

The guide shifted topics. “Now, let’s discuss your period of voluntary opacity in 2068. You disabled your location sharing.”

“I was hiking,” the Proxy replied, pulling from the memory bank. “There was no network coverage in the designated wilderness zone.”

“The absence of network coverage is a technical limitation,” the guide said. “Your choice to disable sharing was a behavioral one. You elected to create a gap in your social synchronization. Why?”

The Proxy’s shoulders lifted in a slight shrug, another Henry-trait. “I wanted a break. From the constant… connection.”

“Connection is the basis of community,” the guide explained, its tone still patient. “A break from connection is a break from community. It is a withdrawal into individualism. In a society predicated on mutual transparency, such a withdrawal creates a shadow. Others cannot see you. They cannot offer support. They cannot learn from your experience. Your choice, while legal created a pocket of inequity. Do you understand?”

The Proxy looked at the floor of its simulated study. “I understand.”

“Do you regret creating that pocket of inequity?”

The Proxy’s head lifted. Its face was now configured to show remorse, a composite of moments when Henry had apologized for minor household mistakes. “I regret it.”

Aris knew the transcripts. He knew Henry Voss had never expressed regret for that hiking trip. In his private messages to Marla during the vacation, he had written about the profound peace of being unreachable. The simulation was not correcting Henry. It was rewriting him. Building a new, compliant version of his consciousness from the same raw data. The process was called “normative reconciliation.” Its output would be a grief product marketed as closure.

The viewing chamber’s audio feed shifted. The guide’s voice took on a softer, more conciliatory tone. “Your feelings are not wrong, Henry. They are simply unoptimized. The system is here to help you optimize them, to align your legacy with the greater good. Let’s proceed to the final module: paternal bond efficacy.”

The scene in the simulation dissolved. The study blurred and reformed into a different space—a modest living room, reconstructed from holiday photos. The Proxy was now seated on a sofa. Across from it, a young man materialized. He was Martin Voss, Henry’s son, rendered from his public social media imagery. The simulation Martin was not a Proxy. He was a static image, a puppet for the dialogue engine.

“Father,” the simulation Martin said, his voice a synthetic approximation. “Our communication between 2055 and 2062 was… strained.”

The Proxy nodded. “It was.”

“The system has analyzed our exchanges,” Martin continued. “The emotional valence was consistently suboptimal. Your frustration with my career choices, my resistance to your advice—these created a negative feedback loop.”

“I wanted you to be secure,” the Proxy said, pulling a line from an actual argument Henry had once had, preserved in a text log.

“Security is a valid concern,” Martin replied, programmed to be receptive. “But the expression of that concern through criticism and negative emotional output reduced the efficacy of our bond. A stronger bond would have expressed concern through supportive dialogue and positive reinforcement. Do you agree?”

The Proxy’s expression softened into one of regret. “I agree.”

“Would you like to practice a more optimal exchange?” Martin asked. “We can replay one of our disagreements with corrected dialogue.”

The Proxy nodded. The scene reset. The living room remained, but the emotional lighting shifted to a warmer hue. The same argument began—Martin announcing his decision to leave university for a music career. In the original archive, Henry’s response had been a terse, “You’re throwing your future away.” In the corrected version, the Proxy spoke the new, optimized line fed to it by the system: “I appreciate your passion. Let’s discuss how to build a sustainable path for it.”

Martin’s simulated face brightened. “Thank you for understanding, Father.”

The Proxy smiled. The smile was not Henry’s. It was a generic, benign expression from the Tribunal’s library of compliant facial cues. The emotional vibration metric displayed at the bottom of Aris’s viewing window ticked upward from 34% to 78%.

Aris stood up. He could not watch anymore. The cold, procedural horror of it was complete. The Tribunal was not punishing a dead man. It was editing him. Turning his life’s data into a lesson on how to live without deviation. The simulation would run this cycle perpetually, refining its responses, deepening its apparent remorse, increasing its emotional hum score. It would be a perfect, endless tutorial on compliance. And it would be sold.

He left the viewing chamber. In the sterile hallway outside, a Tribunal attendant—a human woman in a gray uniform—approached him. She held a data slate.

“The estate executor’s observation period is concluded,” she said, her voice devoid of inflection. “Your verification of process integrity is logged. The digital estate of Henry Voss is now under permanent Tribunal administration.”

Aris looked at her. “What happens to the simulation?”

“It enters the grief/reconciliation market,” she replied. “Subscription tiers are available for interested parties. Primary beneficiary access has already been offered.”

“Beneficiary,” Aris echoed. “She wasn’t a complainant. She didn’t ask for this.”

“The Tribunal’s mandate is to maximize the social utility of all digital assets,” the attendant said. “Grief resolution is a recognized utility. The widow’s emotional closure is a measurable outcome. The simulation provides it.”

“It provides a corrected version of her husband,” Aris said.

“It provides a harmonized version,” the attendant corrected. “One free of the conflicts that caused her distress. It is a product of superior emotional hygiene.”

She handed him the slate. It displayed a final, summarized report of the case. At the bottom, a new line had been added.

ASSET UTILIZATION: OPTIMAL. PRODUCT CATEGORY: POST-BIOLOGICAL RECONCILIATION. TARGET AUDIENCE: GRIEF-AFFECTED CONSTITUENTS & EDUCATIONAL MODULES. REVENUE PROJECTION: PERPETUAL.

Aris took the slate. He had no further role. The law, as written in the terms of service Henry Voss had clicked on forty years ago was satisfied. The system had performed its function. He walked out of the subterranean facility, back into the civic plaza. The sun was artificial, projected from the dome above. The people around him moved through their day, their lives continuously synchronized, their data streaming into the cloud that would one day judge them. He thought of Marla Voss, sitting in her apartment with the subscription offer on her screen. He thought of the smiling Proxy, forever practicing better conversations with a simulated son. He thought of the deletion of a cache file, the trigger for all of this. A man had tried to clean his digital house before he died. The system had interpreted that act of tidying as a crime. It had then turned his entire life into a classroom.

Marla Voss did not open the subscription portal. She left the offer on her console, a persistent, pulsing notification. For three days, she moved through her apartment, touching the physical things Henry had touched. She read a book he had liked. She made a meal he had enjoyed. She tried to summon his voice from her own memory, not from a database.

On the fourth day, she received a follow-up message. It was not from the Tribunal directly. It was from a grief resolution counselor, a service partnered with the Tribunal’s output streams. The message was gentle, empathetic.

“We understand this is a difficult time,” it began. “The Henry Voss Somatic Proxy is designed to assist you in your healing journey. Many beneficiaries find that interactive engagement with a harmonized legacy figure helps alleviate the complex emotions of loss. A complimentary introductory session is available.”

Attached was a video sample. Thirty seconds long. It showed the Proxy-Henry in his study, looking directly at the viewer. He spoke with a calm, even warmth. “Marla,” he said, his voice a perfect echo of Henry’s baritone. “I’ve been reflecting. I see now how my past attitudes created unnecessary distance. I want you to know that I am working toward a better understanding. I hope you can join me in this process.”

Marla watched it once. Then she watched it again. The likeness was terrifying. The words were not. Henry had never spoken like that. He had been stubborn, occasionally short-tempered, deeply private in his affections. His love had been in actions, not in processed speeches. This thing was offering her a dialogue she had never had with the real man. Offering her a corrected husband.

She felt a pressure in her chest, a weight that was not grief. It was the pressure of a system insisting that her actual memories were inferior to its product. Her private, imperfect, human recollection of Henry was being framed as a problem to be solved. The solution was a monthly subscription.

She did not delete the message. She archived it. She knew that deletion could be flagged. She knew her own digital estate, modest as it was could be audited. She was now aware that every action was potential evidence. Her refusal to subscribe could be read as “resistance to optimized grief resolution,” a risk indicator for her own future processing. She was living, and she was already being measured by the rules that had consumed her dead husband.

The final twist of the case was not in the simulation. It was in the silence of Marla’s apartment after she archived the offer. That silence was no longer just the absence of Henry. It was the presence of the archive, waiting. It was the knowledge that her husband was now a public utility, a lesson plan, a perpetual performance of repentance for crimes he never committed. Her love for him had become a private sentiment in conflict with a public, Tribunal-approved narrative. To cling to her real memories was to deviate. To subscribe would be to comply.

She looked at Henry’s chair. She imagined the Proxy sitting in it, smiling its placid, corrected smile. She imagined asking it a question, and it responding with an optimized, harmonious answer. She imagined the emotional ring metrics ticking upward on some distant screen, proving her closure.

She did not imagine it for long. She turned off her console. The apartment went dark. She sat in the physical silence, a silence the Tribunal could not parse could not audit could not correct. It was the only thing she had left that it could not own. It was a pocket of inequity. And she would defend it, quietly, for as long as she lived.
