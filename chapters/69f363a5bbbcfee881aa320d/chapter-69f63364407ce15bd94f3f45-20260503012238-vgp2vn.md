Chapter 1

The morning inspection began with the scent of lemon polish and the low, steady hum of the climate enchantments. Corinne walked the central aisle of the barn, her boots silent on the scrubbed stone. To her left and right, the stalls stood open, each containing a man. The air held the clean, sharp odor of antiseptic undercut by something warmer, muskier—the smell of sleeping bodies, of skin and breath. A good barn. An ordered barn. The numbers on her ledger would match the reality before her, line for line, because she made certain they did.

Her eyes moved from one stall to the next, not lingering. She noted the rise and fall of a chest, the slackness of a hand draped over a cot’s edge. Inventory. She passed a stall where the occupant, a broad-shouldered man with close-cropped hair was already awake. He stared at the ceiling, his jaw working. A faint, silvery scar cut through his eyebrow. Corinne’s thumb found the smooth stone in her pocket, her index finger rubbing its cool surface once, twice. She did not pause. His yield had been consistent, if unremarkable. His compliance was adequate. He was not why she was here today.

The new one was at the far end, in the assessment stall.

She could feel the disruption before she saw it. A tension in the air, a slight change in the hum of the place, as if the barn itself had taken a shallow breath and held it. The two Handlers on duty—Reynard and the new girl, Lisbet—stood straighter as she approached. Reynard gave a curt nod. Lisbet’s eyes darted to Corinne’s face, then away, her fingers worrying the seam of her grey tunic.

“Matron,” Reynard said, his voice a low gravel.

Corinne ignored him. Her focus narrowed to the man in the stall.

He sat on the edge of the reinforced cot, back straight, wrists resting on his knees. They’d cleaned him up. Dark hair, still damp fell across his forehead. They’d given him the standard-issue trousers, loose and soft. His torso was bare. He was lean, but not starved; defined muscle corded his arms and stomach. A fighter’s build, or a laborer’s. Not the soft, pampered flesh of some of the stock acquired from the coast cities. His eyes were the color of wet slate, and they were fixed on the wall opposite, on a hairline crack in the mortar that ran from the ceiling down to the floor. He tracked its path with a concentration that felt like a deliberate insult.

She had read the acquisition report. Tomas. No family name offered. Taken from a Sunward Coast slaver’s caravan bound for the fighting pits. The slaver had been eager to sell, citing “attitude problems.” Corinne had purchased him for a song. Problem stock was often the most potent, once broken to the bit.

“Vitals?” she asked, her voice flat.

Lisbet jumped. “Stable, Matron. Heart rate elevated but within parameters. No signs of physical trauma from transit. He refused the nutrient broth.”

“He refused.”

“Yes, Matron.”

Corinne stepped into the stall. The space was exactly three paces by four. She took two paces in, stopping just outside his reach, not that he’d be fool enough to try. The air in the stall was different. Colder. It carried the faint, clean smell of the soap they’d used on him, and underneath, something else—a sharp metallic tang, like ozone after a lightning strike. It prickled at the back of her throat.

“Look at me.”

He didn’t. His gaze remained on the crack in the wall, as if it held the secrets of the universe. A muscle in his jaw feathered.

Reynard shifted his weight behind her. “Shall I—”

“Quiet.” Corinne studied the man. His breathing was too controlled, the inhale and exhale measured against some internal count. He was containing himself. Building a wall. She’d seen it before. The strong ones always built walls first. They thought it made them safe. It just made the eventual collapse more satisfying. “Your name here is Seventeen. You will answer to it. You will eat what you are given. You will comply with the schedule. Your previous life is over. Your function begins today.”

Nothing. Not a flicker.

Irritation, hot and fine as a needle, slid between her ribs. An old feeling, familiar. She let it sit there. She would not give him the satisfaction of seeing it. Instead, she noticed the way the pale dawn light from the high, barred window caught the dust motes swirling above his head. They moved in a lazy spiral, disturbed by the ventilation current. One landed on his shoulder. He didn’t brush it away.

“Your yield will be measured,” she continued, her tone clinical, a lecturer delivering facts. “Quality and quantity. High yield earns privileges. Private quarters. Better food. Low yield, or non-compliance, earns transfer to the Dreg pens. You do not want to be a Dreg. The harvest there is impersonal. Mechanical. It lacks the artistry we practice here.”

She waited. A full ten seconds of silence, broken only by the distant drip of water from a tap in the washroom down the aisle. The drip had been there for weeks. She kept meaning to have it fixed, but the rhythm of it had become part of the barn’s soundscape, a tiny metronome marking the passage of days.

Finally, he spoke. His voice was rough from disuse, but level. It didn’t match the defiance in his posture. “And if I yield nothing?”

Corinne allowed herself a small, cold smile. It was not a pleasant expression. “The body is not a fortress. It is a system of levers and pressures. We are experts in applied pressure. Everyone yields, Seventeen. The question is how much of you will be left when you do.”

His eyes left the crack. Slowly, they turned to her. The grey of them was not flat, she saw now. It was layered, like storm clouds, with flecks of darker charcoal near the pupil. There was no fear in them. No anger she could name. Just a vast, chilling assessment. He looked at her the way she looked at her ledger—evaluating, categorizing, finding the flaw in the column.

Something hot moved through her chest. Not quite anger, not quite the thrill of a challenge. Something between the two, tightening her throat and making the stone in her pocket feel suddenly too warm. The same feeling she got when a batch of Aethel began to crystallize too early in the vat, a beautiful, dangerous flaw in the process.

He saw it. She knew he did. His gaze dropped, just for a heartbeat, to the hand she had clenched at her side before returning to her face. A ghost of something passed over his features. Not a smile. A recognition.

He had found the crack in her mortar.

Corinne took a step back. The movement was sharp, a retreat she instantly regretted. She covered it by turning to Reynard. “Process him for the evening gathering. Full assessment. I want his baseline established.”

“The Hall of Reaping?” Reynard asked.

“No. The private chamber. The amber one. I’ll oversee the calibration myself.”

Reynard’s eyebrows lifted a fraction. The amber chamber was for Prime stock, for testing nuances, for Guests who paid a premium for a show. It was not for a defiant new acquisition on his first day. But he simply nodded. “Yes, Matron.”

Corinne didn’t look back at the man—at Seventeen—as she left the stall. She walked back down the aisle, her stride precise, her back straight. The numbers in her ledger blurred for a second. The drip from the tap seemed louder, each plink a tiny hammer on the anvil of the morning. She passed the stall with the man who had the scar through his eyebrow. He was still staring at the ceiling, but his hand was now clenched in a fist so tight the knuckles were bone-white.

She exited the barn into the misty grey dawn of the Sink. The silence here was different—a thick, damp blanket that swallowed sound. She took a deep breath, the air cool and heavy with the scent of wet earth and the ever-present, faintly sweet rot of the Sink fungus growing on the north wall. The tightness in her chest remained, a cold knot beneath her sternum.

The flaw was in the grist. She had brought it into her mill herself. Tonight, she would see just how sharp its edges were.

The fungal globes glowed a sickly, phosphorescent green, casting long, wavering shadows across the Hall of Reaping. Underneath lingered the sharper scents of sweat and arousal and expensive perfume.

They’d marched them in, a line of twenty men, all in the same soft trousers. Tomas kept his eyes on the back of the man in front of him, a broad-shouldered brute with a shaved head. The stone floor was cold under his bare feet. A memory flashed, unbidden: the cold flagstones of a monastery garden at dawn, the smell of wet rosemary. Gone before he could clutch it.

The Hall was a vast, circular chamber. Tiered benches rose along the walls, already filling with spectators. Guests. They murmured, their silks and velvets a rustle against the stone. Some wore elaborate masks of polished bone or dark wood. Others did not bother. Their faces were slack with anticipation.

In the center of the room, a wide shallow basin of polished black stone gleamed under the green light. Around it, low pallets were arranged like the spokes of a wheel, each with leather restraints at the corners.

A Handler—a woman with close-cropped red hair and a bored expression—gripped his arm, steering him toward one of the pallets. Her fingers were strong. “Here. Down.”

He didn’t move. The man to his left, a younger one with hollow cheeks was already lying back, his wrists and ankles being secured with efficient clicks. He stared at the ceiling, his breathing a rapid shallow pant.

“Down,” the Handler repeated, her voice flat. She gave his arm a sharp twist.

Something hot moved through his chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. He let her push him onto the pallet. The leather was cool against his back. She bent, her breath smelling of mint, and fastened the cuff around his left wrist. The mechanism was smooth, silent. It tightened just enough. Click. Then the right.

His heart was a frantic animal behind his ribs. He turned his head. On the highest tier, in a carved stone chair that was more throne than seat sat Corinne. She wore a gown of deep charcoal, severe and elegant. One hand rested on the arm of the chair, the fingers loose. The other held a thin ledger. She was not watching the preparations. She was reading.

The red-haired Handler moved to his ankles. Click.

He was spread-eagled, utterly exposed. The green light painted his skin a cadaverous hue. He focused on a single crack in the ceiling vault, a dark line that intersected with another, forming a lopsided T. He counted the beats of his own pulse in his ears.

A gong sounded, a deep, resonant vibration that hummed in the stone beneath him.

The murmuring ceased.

Corinne closed her ledger and set it aside. She did not stand. She simply lifted her chin. Her gaze swept the room, then settled on the men on the pallets. It felt like a physical touch, cold and assessing.

“Begin,” she said. The word was not loud, but it carried, clean and sharp as a scalpel.

Movement erupted from the edges of the hall. Handlers and Guests descended from the benches, flowing toward the pallets. Laughter, low and eager. The rustle of clothing being shed.

A man approached his pallet. Older, his body gone soft at the middle but with a cruel set to his mouth. He wore a mask of silver filigree that left his lips bare. He stood over Tomas, looking down. His eyes were a pale, watery blue.

“Prime stock, they tell me,” the man said. His voice was rough, amused. “Let’s see if the rumors are true.”

Tomas looked past him, back to the crack in the ceiling. He retreated inside the wall he’d built, stone by stone, in the assessment stall. He would not be here. He would be in the monastery garden. He would be anywhere else.

The man’s hands were on him then, confident, proprietary. They mapped his chest, his stomach, sliding over his skin. The touch was clinical at first, then possessive. Nails scraped lightly over a nipple. Tomas flinched. It was involuntary. A tiny fracture in the wall.

A sound escaped him, a choked-off gasp.

The man smiled. He unfastened his own trousers. He was already hard. “There we are.”

Around them, the hall filled with other sounds. Grunts. Moans. The wet, rhythmic slap of flesh on flesh. A woman’s high, delighted laugh. The air grew warmer, thicker, the fungal scent now layered with the salt tang of exertion, the coppery hint of blood from a split lip somewhere, the cloying musk of release.

The man mounted him.

There was no preparation, no pretense of care. It was an invasion, brutal and complete. Tomas’s body arched off the pallet, a silent scream locked in his throat. The leather cuffs bit into his wrists. He squeezed his eyes shut. The garden. The rosemary. The cold flagstones.

But the body was a traitor. It responded to the constant, grinding rhythm despite the searing pain, despite the revulsion that curdled in his gut. Heat pooled low in his belly, a sickening betrayal. A groan was torn from him, raw and ragged.

The man above him grunted, his pace becoming erratic, frantic. “Yes. That’s it. Give it up.”

Shame followed the heat, a black wave that threatened to pull him under. He was going to lose. The wall was crumbling. He could feel the pressure building, an unbearable tension coiling at the base of his spine. He fought it, teeth gritted, every muscle straining against the inevitable.

His eyes flew open, desperate for an anchor.

They found Corinne.

She was still in her chair, watching now. Her expression was unchanged, that mask of cool observation. But her hand on the arm of the chair was no longer loose. Her knuckles were white. She was leaning forward, just slightly, as if pulled by an invisible string.

Their eyes met across the reeking, heaving room.

The release was not surrender. It was a detonation.

It ripped through him with a violence that had nothing to do with pleasure. A raw, scorching flood that was pure, undiluted fury given physical form. He cried out—a sound that was less a human voice and more the crack of breaking stone. His back bowed off the pallet, the restraints creaking with the strain.

The man atop him shouted, a mix of pain and shocked euphoria, and collapsed.

But it didn’t stop. Tomas shuddered, convulsing, as the essence kept coming, far beyond the normal measure, hot and copious, spilling over the man’s stomach, soaking into the pallet beneath them. The metallic, ozone tang from the assessment stall filled the air around his pallet, sharp and clean, cutting through the hall’s miasma.

A strange quiet fell around him. The grunts and slaps from the neighboring pallets stuttered and died. He was aware of faces turning toward him, eyes wide behind masks, mouths slack.

Corinne stood.

She was halfway down the steps from her dais before she seemed to realize she’d moved. She stopped, one hand on the stone balustrade. The perfect composure was gone. Her chest rose and fell in a quick shallow rhythm. She stared at him, at the evidence of his yield still pooling on his skin, and for a fraction of a second, her face was utterly naked. Not triumph. Not calculation.

Hunger. A deep, ravaging thing that had nothing to do with ledgers or quotas.

Then it was gone, smoothed back into the familiar lines of control. But the mask was cracked. He’d seen it. She knew he’d seen it.

She turned, her gown swirling around her ankles, and walked back to her throne without a word. She did not sit. She stood beside it, her back to the hall, one hand pressed flat against the cold stone wall as if to steady herself.

The gong sounded again. The harvest was over.

Handlers moved in, unlocking cuffs, pulling men upright. The red-haired woman unfastened Tomas’s restraints with brisk, impersonal tugs. She avoided his eyes. She used a clean cloth to wipe the mess from his stomach, her touch hurried, almost nervous.

He could not stand. His legs were water. He sat on the edge of the ruined pallet, head bowed, the green light swimming in his vision. The ozone smell clung to him. It clung to the air.

Across the hall, Corinne remained facing the wall, her shoulders rigid, her head bowed. She did not look back.
