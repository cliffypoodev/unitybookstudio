The rain hadn’t stopped. It never did. But this morning, the Silence felt different. Not a muffling blanket, but a held breath. Mireille stood in the refinery, the air thick with the cloying sweetness of stabilizing vir and the underlying, fungal rot of the Sink moss packed into the clay urns along the walls. Her head throbbed behind her eyes, a dull, persistent ache the morning Aethel hadn’t touched. The taste of him—salt and over-steeped tea—still clung to the back of her throat, a ghost she couldn’t swallow.

Not the creamy opalescence of prime Aethel-in-waiting, but a murky, iridescent green, the sheen of a dying insect’s wing.

Cell Three.”

Kaelen. Of course.

The systemic instability. It wasn’t an accident. It was a beacon. His very presence, his uncontrolled somatic output, was screaming into the silent, damp air of the Sink. The Guild’s hunters would have ears for that frequency.

Her mind, usually a clean grid of protocols and quotas, fractured into a dozen panicked trajectories. Protect the Grist. Eliminate the threat. Hide the evidence. Run. The stone floor of the observatory was cold through the thin soles of her indoor slippers. A stupid detail to notice. The memory came, unwanted—the smell of burning hair and singed wool. A different fire. A different closed door. Her own hands, slick with someone else’s sweat, pounding on the oak until her knuckles split.

The question was a knife between her ribs. She’d spent twenty years building a fortress of ritual and control, and with five words, he reduced it to rubble. She saw it in his eyes—he knew. He’d pieced it together from the tremor in her hand, from the way she’d looked at the sketch, from the hungry, clinical precision of her work here at the Grist. She was a reflection of him. His hands came up, not to strike, but to frame her face. His thumbs brushed her cheekbones. You have to match the frequency.”
