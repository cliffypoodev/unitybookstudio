The observation gallery smelled of old velvet and lemon polish. Corinne stood in the narrow space, the brass eyepieces of the viewing ports cold against her skin. Below, in the room called the Gilded Cage, Lord Vane of the Sunward Coast was preparing his request. He’d paid triple the standard fee for a private, unrecorded session with a specific Prime. His money had bought the right to set the parameters. Corinne’s policy was non-interference with Guest privilege. Her policy was currently a tight, metallic taste at the back of her throat.

The room itself was an offense. Too much gold leaf, clashing with the deep plum of the carpets. A ridiculous crystal chandelier, unlit, its facets catching the low glow of the wall sconces. The centerpiece was a heavy, padded bench of dark-stained oak, fitted with leather restraints at the wrists, ankles, and across the chest. It looked like a piece of antique medical equipment. Vane liked the aesthetic.

Tomas stood beside it, flanked by two Handlers Corinne didn’t recognize—Vane’s own men, imported with him. They’d stripped Tomas of his linen trousers. He wore nothing. The light from the sconces carved the tension in his shoulders, the rigid line of his spine. He wasn’t shivering. His breathing was slow, too controlled. A deliberate calm. She could see the pulse hammering in his throat from here.

Vane circled him, a man of soft middle age dressed in a silk dressing gown. He held a thin, polished rod of dark wood. He tapped it against his own palm as he spoke, his voice a muffled murmur through the gallery’s sound-conduction tubes.

“… understand the commodity is more than the fluid,” Vane was saying. His tone was conversational, pedagogical. “It’s the yield under pressure. The moment the vessel attempts to refuse its own nature. I find most establishments skim the surface. They harvest pleasure. I wish to harvest the precise intersection of pain and capitulation.”

One of the handlers moved. A swift, practiced motion. Tomas’s arm was wrenched behind his back, a pressure applied to the shoulder joint. His body bowed forward, a gasp punched from his lungs. The handler forced him over the bench, his chest hitting the padded leather. The other man secured the ankle restraints. Then the wrist cuffs, pulled tight. The strap across the back. Tomas lay prone, his face turned to the side, pressed against the leather. His eyes were open. He stared at the leg of a nearby divan, its claw-foot brass ugly.

Corinne’s hand found the river stone in her pocket. Her thumb rubbed the smooth surface. Once, twice. The motion did nothing.

Vane discarded his robe. He was pale, soft. He took his time selecting a bottle of oil from a side table, pouring a generous amount into his palm. The smell of sandalwood, cloying and sweet, seemed to seep up through the viewing ports. He began at Tomas’s shoulders, his hands moving in slow, firm circles down the captive’s back. Not a caress. A kneading. Like working dough. Tomas remained motionless, his muscles locked.

“Resistance is a form of focus,” Vane murmured, his voice clear now. He leaned close to Tomas’s ear. “It gives the eventual release a certain… texture. A bitterness I quite enjoy.”

The memory arrived unbidden, irrelevant. Corinne was seven, standing on a stool in her mother’s perfume shop in Slake. A customer, a woman with feathers in her hat, had leaned over the counter and pinched Corinne’s cheek. Hard. “Such a serious little thing,” the woman had cooed, her breath smelling of anise. “You’ll learn to smile prettier if you want to eat, dear.” The woman had bought the most expensive jasmine oil. Corinne’s mother had smiled, the transaction complete. The cheek had ached for an hour.

Below, Vane’s hands moved lower. The oil gleamed on Tomas’s skin. The deliberate, invasive touch was not for arousal. It was a mapping. A claim. Vane’s fingers dug into the muscles of Tomas’s buttocks, then traced the cleft between with a clinical slowness that was worse than violence.

Tomas’s breathing hitched. A crack in the control. His fingers, visible where they were bound near the top of the bench, curled into fists.

Vane took the polished rod. He pressed the cool tip against Tomas’s entrance, applying a steady, insistent pressure. Not to enter. Just to press. To threaten. “The body opens long before the will,” Vane said, almost to himself. “A fascinating biological truth. We can negotiate with the flesh directly.”

A hot, sour flood filled Corinne’s mouth. Her own stomach clenched, a sympathetic spasm. She watched Tomas’s face. He had shut his eyes. A tremor ran through his trapped legs.

It’s just a different kind of harvest. The thought was her own, in her own voice, but it sounded hollow, a chant that had lost its meaning. Yield under pressure. A statistical event.

Vane dropped the rod. It clattered on the floor. He positioned himself behind Tomas, his hands gripping the captive’s hips. There was no preparation, no attempt at gentleness or even functional care. It was a brutal, forced entry, a taking designed to tear.

Tomas’s body jerked against the restraints. A raw, animal sound was ripped from him—not a cry, but a shredded gasp of pure, shocked agony. His eyes flew open, wide and unseeing, fixed on the claw-foot divan.

Corinne’s fingers were white on the brass eyepieces. The lemon polish smell was suddenly nauseating. She saw the exact moment Tomas left. His body was still there, held down, violated, but the light behind his eyes guttered. He went somewhere inside himself. A place with no doors. She knew that place. She had built her office there.

Vane began to move. A ruthless, punishing rhythm. Each thrust rocked the heavy bench. Tomas’s head lolled against the leather. He was passive now, utterly broken. This was the capitulation Vane had paid for. The harvest.

But it was wrong. It was a spoiled batch. A toxic yield. This wasn’t a Prime being milked. This was a transaction of pure cruelty, and she was the broker. The woman with the feathers in her hat, smiling as she sold the jasmine oil.

Her body moved before her mind caught up. Her hand left the stone and slammed against the brass communication lever on the wall. A sharp clang echoed in the gallery and, she knew, in a discreet bell in the room below.

Vane froze. He looked up, irritated, toward the mirrored wall that hid the gallery. Corinne didn’t use the speaker tube. She turned, her heels loud on the wooden floor, and wrenched the door open. She took the narrow servant’s stairs two at a time, the world narrowing to the burn in her thighs, the pounding of her own blood in her ears.

She burst into the Gilded Cage. The smell of sandalwood and sweat hit her like a wall.

“The session is concluded,” she said. Her voice was flat, colder than the Sallowmere in deep winter.

Vane had withdrawn, flushed with exertion and annoyance. “Matron. We are mid-contract.”

“Is forfeit. I will refund it double.” She didn’t look at him. Her gaze was on the Handlers. “Release him. Now.”

The men hesitated, looking to Vane. Corinne took one step forward. “You are on my land. Using my stock. Release him, or you will join him on the roster. I assure you, your yields would be negligible.”

That got them moving. They fumbled with the buckles. Tomas didn’t move as the restraints fell away. He slid from the bench, his legs buckling. He caught himself on the edge, head hanging, his back a landscape of oil and tension.

Vane sputtered, wrapping his robe around himself. “This is an outrage! I have patronage rights! I will see your license revoked!”

Corinne finally looked at him. She let him see the absolute void in her eyes, the one she usually reserved for balancing ledgers after a poor quarter. “Your patronage is ended. You have one hour to vacate your suite. If you are on my property after that, my security will escort you to the border. They are not gentle.”

She turned her back on him, a calculated and profound insult. She walked to Tomas. Up close, the damage was more specific. A bruise was already flowering on his hip where a hand had gripped too hard. His breathing was shallow, quick.

“Can you walk?” she asked, her voice low.

He didn’t answer. He didn’t look at her.

“Tomas.”

His head lifted slowly. His eyes found hers. There was nothing in them. No defiance, no fury, no storm. Just a flat, exhausted emptiness. It was worse than anything she’d seen before.

She reached for his arm. He flinched. A full-body recoil before he could stop it. Her hand hung in the air between them. Something sharp and nameless twisted behind her ribs—not pity, something more inconvenient. A recognition.

She lowered her hand. “Follow me,” she said, and turned, walking toward the door, not checking to see if he obeyed.

After a moment, she heard the soft, unsteady shuffle of his footsteps behind her.

The door to the private cell hissed shut behind her. The sound sealed them in a white, antiseptic silence. The air smelled of the pine-scented cleaner they used on the tiles and, faintly beneath it, of sandalwood oil. That cloying sweetness clung to Tomas, where he sat on the edge of the narrow cot. He’d been given a fresh pair of linen trousers. They hung loose on his hips. He was staring at the wall opposite, at a hairline crack in the plaster that ran from the ceiling corner down about a foot before it stopped. His hands rested on his knees, palms up. Open. Empty. He looked like a man waiting for a verdict he already knew.

Corinne stood just inside the door. Her thumb found the stone in her pocket. Smooth, cool. She didn’t rub it. She held it. The memory of the viewing gallery pressed against the back of her eyes—the gold leaf, the claw-foot divan, the way his fingers had curled into fists. A statistical event. Yield under pressure. The phrases were ash in her mouth.

“You interrupted a paying Guest.” Tomas’s voice was flat, scraped raw. He didn’t turn his head. “That’s a breach of contract. I read the boilerplate when I signed the intake forms. In the mine office. Before the tea they gave me made the words swim.”

“You didn’t read anything.” The words came out quieter than she intended. “The tea was laced with a mild somatic inhibitor. It impairs short-term memory and suggestibility. Standard procurement protocol.”

A flicker at the corner of his mouth. Not a smile. A tic. “So I signed nothing.”

“You consumed a beverage offered by a representative of a licensed enterprise. Implied consent.”

“Implied.” He repeated the word, testing its weight. He finally looked at her. His eyes were the same unsettling grey, but the defiance from the Orchid Room was gone. Something else lived there now. A hollowed-out knowing. It was worse. “You stood up there and watched.”

It wasn’t a question. She said nothing. The hum of the climate system filled the space between them.

“Why stop it?” He shifted on the cot, a slight wince tightening his features. He covered it instantly, his expression smoothing back into that awful neutrality. “The yield would have been substantial. Prime grade, given the stimulus. A bitterness he’d enjoy. You could have charged him extra for the narrative.”

Her stomach turned over. A sour, hot liquid burned at the base of her throat. She swallowed it down. “You are an asset. His methods risked damage to the asset. That is bad business.”

“Is that what this is?” He gestured vaguely around the cell. His movement was stiff, careful. “Business?”

“It is a system. It has parameters.”

“And you’re the parameter queen.” He leaned back against the wall, his head tipping to rest on the white plaster. He closed his eyes. The light from the overhead panel gleamed on the stubble along his jaw, caught the faint sheen of sweat at his temples. He looked exhausted. He looked like he’d been pulled apart and put back together slightly wrong. “So assess the damage, Matron. Write your report. Is the asset still functional?”

She took a step forward. Then another. The tiles were cold through the soles of her shoes. She stopped a few feet from the cot. He didn’t open his eyes. She could see the pulse in his throat, steady but too fast. A trapped bird beating against ribs. She could smell the sandalwood, and underneath it, the salt-sweat scent of him, and the faint iron-tang hint of blood.

“Show me,” she said.

His eyes opened it. He looked at the ceiling. A long, slow breath left his lungs. “What?”

“The session was terminated before completion. I need to evaluate for contusion, laceration, or soft-tissue stress that could impact future yield. Standard post-incident protocol.” The lie was smooth, automatic. The protocol existed for equipment failure, not Guest excess.

A sound escaped him. Part laugh, part choke. “You want to inspect the goods.”

“I want to see the damage.”

Their eyes met. The hollow knowing in his gaze sharpened, focused into a needle-point of understanding. He saw it. The flaw in her own grist. He pushed himself upright, a deliberate, pained motion. His fingers went to the tie at his waist. He didn’t look away from her as he undid the knot. The linen trousers pooled at his ankles. He stepped out of them, naked now, and turned his back to her.

The skin was mapped in violence. Angry red lines from gripping fingers bloomed across his hips and shoulders. Darker bruises were already forming along his spine. But it was the lower bruises that made her breath catch—deep, thumbprint purples on the backs of his thighs. And the evidence of the violation itself, a raw, inflamed redness that made her own body clench in a sympathetic, useless revolt.

Something hot and formless moved through her chest. Not anger. Not pity. Something between the two that had no name, a thick, choking sediment stirred up from a deep, still lake.

“Satisfied?” His voice was muffled, his face still turned toward the wall.

She didn’t answer. Her hand moved of its own accord, reaching out. She stopped her fingers a mere inch from the worst of the bruising on his hip. She could feel the heat radiating from the damaged skin. He flinched. A full-body shudder he couldn’t suppress.

“Don’t.”

The word was a cracked stone. She pulled her hand back, clenched it at her side. “You require a salve. A specific compound to reduce inflammation and prevent hematoma from affecting muscle compliance.”

“Just say it’ll help me perform better.” He turned around. Slow. Careful. He didn’t try to cover himself. The front of him was less marked, but tension corded every muscle of his abdomen. His face was pale, a fine tremor in his hands. “That’s the language you know.”

The overhead light caught a smudge of something on the floor by the cot leg—a fleck of gold leaf, escaped from the playroom. A tiny, stupid piece of glitter. It had no business here. She fixated on it. A flaw in the sterile field.

“Why did you fight him?” The question left her before she could cage it.

His brow furrowed. Genuine confusion. “What?”

“In the gallery. You fought. You knew it would make it worse. The protocols for submission are clear. Resistance escalates Guest behavior. It’s in your orientation.”

A bitter smile touched his lips. Gone in a blink. “You think I was following a manual?” He shook his head, looked past her shoulder at the blank door. “It wasn’t a tactic. It was just… me. The last piece he hadn’t bought. I couldn’t just hand it over. Even if it cost.”

The admission hung in the antiseptic air. It was the least transactional thing she’d heard in years. It made no sense. It was a rock in her stream, and the water was piling up behind it, pressure building.

Her throat tightened. Her mouth went dry. “That’s inefficient.”

“I know.”

Silence again, but different now. Charged. The hum of the vents was a distant insect. She could hear her own heartbeat in her ears. His gaze dropped from the door, traveled over her face, her neck, the line of her tailored jacket. It wasn’t the look of livestock assessing a handler. It was a man looking at a woman. A raw, unguarded appraisal that stripped her of her title.

He took a half-step closer. The space between them vanished. She caught the full scent of him—pain, sweat, that damned sandalwood, and underneath it all, something clean and male and vital. Her own breath hitched.

“You stopped him,” Tomas said, his voice low, a rough scrape of sound. “Why?”

All her prepared answers dissolved. Asset management. Business. Parameters. They were smoke. The truth was a live wire in her gut, sparking against her ribs. She saw the woman with the feathered hat, smelled the jasmine oil, felt the pinch on her cheek. A transaction. Always a transaction.

“I don’t know,” she whispered.

It was the first true thing she’d said to him.

He heard it. His eyes changed. The hollowed-out look filled with a dark, dangerous light. He lifted his hand, slow, giving her every chance to step back, to slap it away, to reassert the distance. She stood frozen. His fingertips brushed the back of her hand where it hung at her side. A feather touch on her knuckles. Her skin prickled, a shock that raced up her arm.

Then his hand closed over hers. His palm was warm, rough with calluses no amount of farm lotion could erase. He pulled her hand toward him, pressed her flattened palm against the worst of the bruise on his hip. The heat was a brand. The damaged flesh was a tragedy under her touch. She felt him shudder again, but he held her hand there, his own clamped over it, forcing her to feel the consequence.

“That’s the damage,” he breathed, his face inches from hers. His eyes held hers, a grey storm. “Not to your asset. To me.”

A tremor started deep in her core. Her fingers curled against his skin, not to push away, but to feel the contour of the bruise, the ridge of his hip bone beneath. A forbidden map. Her other hand came up, rested against his chest. The beat of his heart was a frantic drum against her palm. She could push him. She should push him.

She didn’t.

His head dipped. His forehead came to rest against hers. A gesture of exhaustion, of staggering intimacy. His breath fanned her lips. Sandalwood and salt. “What are you doing, Corinne?”

She had no answer. The system had none. Her lips parted. A helpless, silent gasp. The world narrowed to the heat of his skin under her hands, the solid reality of him, broken and defiant and here. The parameter queen was gone. In her place was only a woman, touching a man’s wound, and finding her own reflection in the bruise.

His mouth found hers.

It wasn’t gentle. It was a collision. A claim and a surrender all at once. The taste of him was bitter coffee and blood from a bitten lip. Her hands slid up to grip his shoulders, feeling the tense muscle, the ridge of fresh bruises. He groaned into her mouth, a sound of pain and something else, something hotter. His arms wrapped around her, pulling her tight against him, and she let him. The careful distance she’d maintained for a decade evaporated in the shared heat of their breath.

It was a catastrophic error. A breach in the hull. And as his tongue swept into her mouth and her back met the cold plaster of the cell wall, she didn’t care. The only truth was the press of his body and the shocking, silent scream of a long-dead thing waking up inside her chest.
