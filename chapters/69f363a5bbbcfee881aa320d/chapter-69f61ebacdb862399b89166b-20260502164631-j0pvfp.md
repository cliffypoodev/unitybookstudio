The rain had stopped. That was the first thing Mireille noticed, a wrongness so profound it pulled her from the fractured memory of Aric’s face. The observatory’s perpetual, soft hiss against the glass was gone, replaced by a damp, hollow quiet. It was the kind of silence that came before a shout. Or a scream.

Her hand went to the small vial at her belt. Empty. She’d drained it last night after Kaelen left, after the confession had hung in the air between them like a ghost. The craving was a physical hollow behind her ribs, a dry-mouthed insistence that made the edges of the room pulse faintly. She ignored it. The absence of rain was a louder alarm.

She crossed to the window. The world below the Pale Spires was a study in greys and dripping greens, the compound of The Grist laid out like a child’s grim toy. And it was wrong. Movement where there should be stillness. Not the orderly march of Harvesters to the Hall, not the trudging lines of Sources to the refectory. This was a scurrying, a scattering. Dark figures poured from the tree line beyond the eastern wall—not a handful, not a scouting party. A flood.

They moved with a terrible, synchronized purpose. No shouts. Just the wet thud of boots on mud, the metallic snick of blades being drawn. Their leathers were dark, but she caught the glint of polished buckles, the standardized cut of their gear. Not farmstead roughspun. Guild issue.

Her stomach clenched, a hard, sour knot. Vance.

The thought was a cold spike. She’d expected subterfuge, poison in the fungal vats, a bribed Harvester. Not this. Not a full assault in the washed-out dawn. It was inelegant. Blunt. It meant they weren’t here for stealth or acquisition. They were here for eradication.

A sound tore through the silence—the deep, wooden groan of the main gate giving way. It wasn’t a crash. It was a sigh of surrender. Then the shouts began, real and ragged. The clash of metal. Kael’s voice, somewhere down in the courtyard, bellowing an order that was cut short.

Her body moved before her mind caught up. The ledger on her desk, the one with the encrypted column detailing her shame, was just paper now. Meaningless. She left it. She took two things: the heavy iron key to the Source dormitory from its hook by the door, and a short, weighted harvesting knife from a display case. The knife’s handle was worn smooth, shaped to her grip from years of use. It felt alien in her hand now, a tool for a different world.

The stone stairs leading down from the observatory were slick with condensed damp. The air grew thicker, smelling of turned earth, of blood, of the sharp, coppery tang of released vir. She passed the entrance to the refinery. The door hung open. Inside, Elara lay sprawled across her workbench, a dark stain blooming across the back of her linen shirt. A broken vial of something amber and pungent glittered on the floor beside her outstretched hand. Mireille didn’t stop. Her breath sawed in her throat.

The corridor to the dormitory was a gauntlet. A Harvester—young Jerrik, his face still round with youth—was backed against the wall by two of the invaders. He swung a cudgel, wild. One of the dark-leathered figures darted in, a short sword flickering. Jerrik gasped, looked down at the red line opening across his tunic. He slid down the wall, leaving a smeared trail on the stone. The invaders turned. Saw her.

Their eyes were flat, impersonal. They wore no farmstead insignia, but on the breast of the nearest one, the leather had been worn smooth in a circular patch. A patch where a Guild insignia might have been pinned, day after day, until it left a ghost.

They advanced.

Mireille didn’t think. She threw the harvesting knife. It wasn’t meant for throwing; it tumbled, handle over blade, and struck the lead invader in the shoulder with a dull thud. He grunted, more surprise than pain. But it halted them for a second. She ran.

Past them, around the corner. The dormitory door was ahead, a slab of reinforced oak. She fumbled with the key, her fingers slick. The shouts were closer. A body slammed into the wall behind her, the impact vibrating through the floor. She didn’t look. The key turned. The bolt shrieked.

She shoved the door open and plunged into the long, low room.

The smell hit her first—unwashed bodies, straw, the ripe, sweet-salt scent of confined men. The Sources were on their feet, crowded at the far end near the barred windows, a wall of tense muscle and wide, white eyes. They’d heard the fight. Some held makeshift weapons: a torn plank, a chamber pot, a belt with a heavy buckle. In the center, taller than the rest, stood Kaelen. He had someone’s discarded food bowl in his hand, a crude ceramic shield. His gaze locked onto hers. No surprise. Just a grim, waiting assessment.

“They’re inside,” she said. Her voice was raw, stripped of its Matron’s cadence. “The Guild. They’re killing everyone.

A low murmur ran through the men. Fear, yes. But under it, a current of something else. A suppressed rage, humming.

“The gate’s gone,” she continued, forcing the words out. “The Harvesters are falling. They’re not here for a tithe. They’re here to burn it all down. To salt the earth.” She held up the empty key. “This is your choice. You can run. The tunnels behind the laundry—They might be clear. You might make the tree line.

Silas, the Prime Vein, shook his head. “They’ll hunt us in the woods. Like animals.

“Or,” Mireille said, and the word hung in the foul air. She looked directly at Kaelen. “You can fight. With me. Not as cattle. Not as Springs. As… something else.

Kaelen’s expression didn’t change. “Fight with what? Our bowls? Our righteous indignation?

“With what they came here for.” She took a step into the room. The men tensed. “They want vir? We’ll give them vir. Not in vials. Not refined. Raw. Uncontrolled. The way it was before the Harvesters, before the ledgers. The way it wants to be.

She saw the understanding dawn in his eyes first. Then in a few others. It was madness. It was heresy. It was the only thing left.

“You know the theory,” she said to Kaelen, throwing his own words back at him. “Aric’s work. Sympathetic resonance. A collective pulse. We channel it. We direct it. Not for harvest. For ruin.

A crash against the dormitory door. Wood splintered around the bolt.

Kaelen dropped the ceramic bowl. It shattered on the stone floor. “How?

“The same way we always have,” she said, her heart a frantic drum against her ribs. “But together. No restraints. No quotas. Just the flood.

He was silent for three heartbeats. The door shuddered again. Then he nodded, once. A sharp, decisive motion. He turned to the other men. “You heard her. Run and die tired. Or stand and die on your feet. Maybe take some of them with us.” He shrugged, a bleak, feral gesture. “The math seems simple.

No one moved toward the laundry tunnel. They tightened their grip on their pitiful weapons. Their fear was still there, a sharp scent in the air, but it had been fused with something hotter, darker.

The door exploded inward.

Two of the dark-clad Harvesters surged through, blades leading. Behind them, more crowded the corridor.

Mireille didn’t hesitate. She stepped forward, putting herself between the invaders and the Sources. It was an instinct she didn’t know she possessed. “Now,” she said, not a shout, but a low, clear command.

She closed her eyes. Not in prayer. In focus. She let go of the craving, the ledger, the title of Matron. She reached for the memory of Aric’s workshop, the scent of ozone and honey, the feeling of a connection that was more than transaction. She reached for the humming tension in the room behind her—Thirty-odd wells of potent, desperate energy, and one, Kaelen’s, that burned like a cold star.

She didn’t draw from them. She invited.

It began as a pressure in her temples. Then a vibration in the floor, a subsonic hum that climbed into the audible range. The air grew heavy, charged, like before a lightning strike. She heard one of the invading Harvesters curse, take a step back.

Kaelen’s hand found her shoulder. His touch was electric. Not a spark, but a circuit closing. The power wasn’t just in the room. It was the room. It was in the stone, in the damp air, in the collective, focused will of men who had nothing left to lose.

The lead Harvester lunged, sword aimed at her chest.

The air between them rippled. Visible, like heat haze off a forge. The man’s strike slowed, met resistance. His eyes widened. A force, unseen but palpable, slammed into him. It didn’t cut or pierce. It unmade his momentum. He flew backward as if yanked by a cable, crashing into his companion. They went down in a tangle.

A low, unified sound rose from the Sources behind her. Not a roar. A hum. The same resonant frequency that had shattered her calibration equipment. Now it was a weapon, focused through her, amplified by Kaelen’s anchoring presence.

The other invaders in the corridor faltered. This wasn’t in their manuals. This was the blasphemy they’d come to erase, given form.

Mireille opened her eyes. The world was overlain with a shimmering, painful clarity. She could see the individual threads in the invaders’ cloaks, the pores on their stunned faces, the rapid flutter of a pulse in a neck. The aftertaste of Aric’s honey was on her tongue. This was the memory. This was the connection.

“The Hall,” she said, her voice strange to her own ears. “We take the Hall of Reaping. It’s the heart. We fight there.

She turned. Thirty pairs of eyes looked back, not at their Matron, but at a conductor. At a focal point. Kaelen’s hand was still on her shoulder, a steady, burning weight.

“Lead,” he said.

And she did.

The Hall of Reaping was a cathedral of wrong angles and broken rhythm. Kaelen felt it in his teeth, a high, thin vibration that had nothing to do with the clash of steel and shouts echoing off the vaulted stone. The familiar fungal braziers, once pulsing with a steady, narcotic light, now stuttered like dying hearts. Their light threw jagged, leaping shadows that made the fighting seem like a puppet show staged by a madman.

He’d grabbed a harvesting sickle from a fallen Harvester—a woman whose name he’d never known, her eyes wide and empty—and the curved blade felt absurd in his hand. Too light. Meant for severing fungal stalks, not opening throats. He moved with the knot of other Sources, a bristling, desperate animal with too many limbs. They fought not with formation but with the raw, cornered fury of men who’d been counted and measured and drained. A man named Rourke, all thick shoulders and old scars, used a length of chain torn from a wall sconce, swinging it in wide, grunting arcs that cracked against Guild leather. Another, younger, just a boy really, fought with his bare hands and teeth, silent except for wet, tearing sounds.

The air was a soup of stinks. The coppery tang of blood. The sweet-rot of spilled, unstable vir from shattered collection urns. The ozone-sharp crackle of something else—raw somatic force, unrefined, leaking from panicked men and mingling in the damp atmosphere. It prickled against his skin, a static charge that lifted the hair on his arms.

He saw Mireille across the chaos. Not the Matron. A woman with wild, dark hair plastered to her temples, a short knife in her hand, moving with a feral economy he’d never seen. She ducked under a Guildsman’s swing, came up inside his guard, and drove her blade into the gap between chestplate and pauldron. She didn’t watch him fall. She was already turning, searching. Her eyes found his. A flicker. An acknowledgment. Then a shape lunged at her from the blind spot of a stone pillar.

Kaelen moved without deciding to. He wasn’t aware of crossing the space. The sickle was in his hand, then it was buried in the side of the Guildsman’s neck. The impact jarred his arm to the shoulder. A hot spray hit his cheek. The man made a wet, gurgling sigh and collapsed, pulling the sickle from Kaelen’s grip. He let it go.

Mireille was right there. Breathing hard. A cut on her brow leaked a thin line of blood down into her eyebrow. “They’re herding us,” she said, her voice a rasp. “Toward the central drain.

He followed her gaze. She was right. The Guild fighters weren’t trying to slaughter them indiscriminately. They pressed, disciplined, forming a tightening crescent that forced the defenders back toward the great iron grating in the hall’s center—the main runoff for the collection channels. Where everything flowed to be processed. Or disposed of.

A cold understanding settled in his gut. They didn’t want prisoners. They wanted a contained spill. A cleanup.

“The vir,” he said.

“It’s reacting,” she said. Her eyes were on the air itself, seeing what he felt. “All of it. The spilled batches. The men’s panic. It’s… resonant.

Not his own fear. It was external, a pressure in his sinuses, a taste of burnt honey and salt at the back of his throat. To his left, a Source screamed, clawing at his own face. His eyes were leaking a faint, phosphorescent silver. The unstable vir in the air, the collective terror and pain, it was coalescing. Seeking form.

A Guildsman stepped into the open space before the drain, lowering a short spear. Not aiming it. Planting the butt against the floor. He wore a commander’s gorget. Selene. Her face was sharp, austere, devoid of the fanatical glee he’d expected. She looked like an accountant reviewing a disappointing ledger.

“This is wasteful, Mireille,” Selene called, her voice cutting through the din. It wasn’t a shout. It was a statement. “Your master’s heresies always were. Unstable. Inefficient.

Mireille went very still. “You knew Aric.

“I knew his work. Sentimentality married to poor methodology.” Selene’s gaze swept over the cowering, injured Sources, the pooled vir with unnatural light. “This is the result. Chaotic potential. A hazard. The Guild harvests certainty. We prune variables.

It clicked then. The clinical assault. The standardized gear. The desire to corral, not capture. This wasn’t a rival farm stealing assets. This was the state-sanctioned Guild erasing a failed experiment. Aric’s legacy. Their legacy.

The hollow behind Kaelen’s ribs—the one that usually ached for freedom, for vengeance—filled with something else. A cold, clear fury. It had a taste. Like a penny on the tongue.

He didn’t look at Mireille. He reached for her hand. Her fingers were slick with blood, cold. They twitched, then closed around his with a desperate, crushing strength.

“You want efficiency?” Kaelen’s voice sounded strange to his own ears. Low, but it carried. “You’re standing in a lake of unrefined somatic charge. And you just cornered the sources.

Selene’s eyes narrowed. A flicker of professional assessment. “Contained charge is neutralized charge.

Mireille’s breath hitched. He felt the realization go through her like a current. Her head turned slightly. She was looking at him, but seeing past him, at the terrified men, at the swirling, reactive air. Her lips moved, soundless. The old master’s blasphemies.

Aric hadn’t been trying to create a better intoxicant. He’d been trying to weaponize connection. Symbiosis.

“Don’t control it,” Kaelen murmured, his mouth close to her ear. The words were for her alone. “Link to it. To them.

He felt her resistance. A lifetime of doctrine, of enforced detachment, of seeing power as something to be extracted and bottled. It was a wall inside her. He pressed his forehead against her temple, a brutal, intimate gesture. “You’re not a Matron. You’re a catalyst. So catalyze.

For a second, nothing. Then her hand in his went from cold to burning. Not a metaphor. Actual heat radiated from her skin. A sound tore from her throat—not a scream, not a word. A raw, open vowel of release.

The change in the air was instantaneous.

The swirling, chaotic pressure snapped into focus. The silvery mist rising from the spilled vir drew inward, coalescing into threads of light. They didn’t come from Mireille. They leapt from the Sources themselves—from Rourke’s heaving chest, from the weeping boy’s clenched fists, from every man in the hall who had been harvested, reduced, counted. The threads streamed toward Mireille, not to be taken, but to be woven. And through her, they connected to him.

It wasn’t pleasure. It wasn’t pain. It was a totality of sensation that obliterated the boundary between the two. He felt the ache in Rourke’s old shoulder, the acid burn of the boy’s terror, the cold, sharp grief of the dead Harvester outside the door. He felt Mireille’s own torrent—the shame of her addiction, the ghost of Aric’s approval, the terrifying, vertiginous freedom of letting go. And his own—the years of flight, the quiet fury, the unexpected, anchor-like weight of her hand in his.

The somatic charge built, a harmonic resonance. The fungal braziers flared, then went dark. The only light now came from the web of connections, a throbbing, silver-blue network that lit the hall in stark relief. Guildsmen faltered, raising hands against the sudden, oppressive pressure. It felt like walking into a wall of silent sound.

Selene took a step back, her professional disdain cracking into something raw. Alarm. “Suppress them!

A crossbow bolt cut the air toward Mireille.

Kaelen saw it in the web before he saw it with his eyes—a point of violent intention. He didn’t move. He focused. The threads of light around him flexed, a collective, unconscious flinch. The bolt shattered in mid-air, three feet from her chest, as if hitting an invisible pane of glass.

The action was a trigger.

The woven energy didn’t lash out. It pulsed. A single, deep, subsonic thrum that originated from the linked circle of Sources and rolled outward.

Stone didn’t crack. It moaned. The iron drain grating buckled upward with a shriek of tortured metal. Guildsmen were thrown from their feet, not by force, but by a sudden, violent disruption of equilibrium. They stumbled, retched, collapsed. Selene braced herself, her spear held crosswise, but her knees bent under the invisible wave. Her face was a mask of pure, incredulous fury.

The pulse passed. The web of light flickered, dimmed. The connection didn’t break, but it softened, settling into a low, steady hum in his bones. The effort left him gasping, his muscles trembling as if he’d run for miles. Mireille sagged against him, her breath coming in ragged, wet sobs. Her hand was still locked in his, their fingers fused by sweat and blood.

Silence, then. Not the dead quiet of the Sink, but the stunned, ringing silence after a colossal bell has been struck.

Across the hall, Selene pushed herself upright. One of her arms hung at a wrong angle. She looked at the shattered remains of her force, at the transformed Sources who now stood not in terror, but in a wary, electrified unity. She looked at Mireille and Kaelen, joined in the center of their own ruined world.

There was no grand curse, no final threat. Her lips peeled back from her teeth in a grimace of pure, professional contempt. Then she turned and limped toward a side archway, vanishing into the shadows without a backward glance.

The fight was over. The hall was theirs.

And nothing would ever be the same.

The current that ran through the men wasn’t hope. Hope was a clean, bright thing. This was darker, older—a rumble from a deep place, the sound a stone makes before the landslide. Kaelen felt it through the soles of his feet on the packed dirt floor. He watched Mireille’s face. The Matron’s mask was gone, stripped away by the coppery stink of the corridor and the raw panic in her voice. She looked like a woman who’d just set her own house on fire and was waiting to see if she’d burn with it.

“Fight with what?” a voice called from the huddle of men. Silas. His knuckles were white around a splintered bed slat. “They have swords.

Mireille’s throat worked. She had no answer. Her hand flexed around the empty space where a harvesting knife should be. Kaelen saw the calculation fail behind her eyes, the ledger-mind coming up blank. Then her gaze landed on him, and something else flickered there. Not a plan. A memory.

“The old master’s blasphemies,” Kaelen said, the words quiet but they cut through the murmur. He let the ceramic bowl drop. It thudded on the straw, didn’t break. “You remember the theory. The sympathetic resonance. One vein tuned to another, amplifying the flow.

“That was theoretical,” she whispered. Her eyes were too wide. “Aric never tested it.

“—would be catastrophic for a single vessel,” he finished. He took a step toward her. The other men shifted, a wave parting around him. “But we’re not one vessel. We’re thirty. And the Hall is a resonator. All that stone, all that shaped wood. It’s built to focus vir, to channel it. We turn their own tool against them.

She stared at him. The shouts from the corridor were closer. A body slammed against the door, making the heavy oak shudder. Someone whimpered.

“It will burn you out,” she said. Her voice was flat. “It could unmake you. All of you.

Kaelen smiled. It felt strange on his face, a crack in old stone. “We’re already unmade. What’s one more shape?

He turned to the men. Their faces were a blur of fear and a desperate, hungry anger. He’d seen that look in the mirror for years. “They’re not here for a harvest. They’re here to salt the earth. To leave nothing alive. You can run. Some of you might make the tree line. Or you can come with me into the Hall, and we will give them a harvest they cannot contain.

He didn’t wait for consensus. He moved to the door, put his shoulder against it. You looked back at Mireille. “You have to lead it. You’re the only one who knows the focusing patterns. The only one who’s ever handled raw flow without a conduit.

The admission cost her. He saw it in the flinch, the way her gaze skittered to the empty vial at her belt. The ghost of her addiction, her secret tally. Then her jaw hardened. She gave a single, sharp nod. “The basin. We need to reach the central basin.

He shoved the door open.

The corridor was a choked nightmare of sound and motion. A Harvester—Bren, his apron soaked red—was grappling with an invader on the ground. Two more dark-leathered figures turned from a doorway, their eyes impersonal as slate. Kaelen didn’t fight. He ran, a bull charge down the narrow space. He took the first in the chest with his shoulder, heard the breath explode from the man’s lungs as they crashed into the wall. He kept going, Mireille a shadow at his heels, the thunder of thirty men following.

The door to the Hall of Reaping was ajar. Inside, the scene was a perverse inversion of the harvest ritual.

Selene stood on the raised dais where Mireille usually presided. She was a tall woman, blade-thin, her hair the color of old ash bound in a severe knot. She held a long, needle-thin stiletto, its tip resting casually against the throat of Head Harvester Kael, who knelt before her, a line of blood beading along his jaw. Around the central stone basin, Selene’s harvesters had rounded up the remaining Grist workers. They knelt in rows, hands bound. The fungal braziers pulsed a sickly green, painting everything in underwater shades.

The assault stopped at the doorway. Selene looked up. Her eyes found Mireille first, a cold, professional assessment. Then they slid to Kaelen, and a spark of something hotter lit there. Greed. Recognition.

“Mireille,” Selene called, her voice carrying in the vaulted space with practiced ease. “You’ve brought the prize to the altar. How efficient.

Mireille walked into the Hall. She moved like someone walking into a cold lake, each step deliberate. She didn’t look at her captured people. She kept her eyes on the basin—a great, carved stone bowl at the room’s heart, its interior stained a deep, permanent purple. “This is Guild work, Selene. Vance’s signature is all over this blunt butchery. He always did prefer a hammer to a scalpel.

Selene’s smile was a thin slit. “The Grist is a tumor. Aric’s heresy given form. Vance wants it cauterized. I get to keep the most interesting cell.” Her gaze returned to Kaelen. “The last apprentice. The one who got away.

Kaelen felt the men crowd in behind him, a wall of heat and tension. He could smell their fear, a sharp, animal tang. Under it, the ripe, ready scent of vir, stirred by adrenaline and rage. It hung in the air, a thickening mist.

“You want to harvest me?” Kaelen said. He walked forward, away from the door, toward the basin. The invaders tightened their grips on their weapons but didn’t stop him. He was the prize. “You can try.

“Not harvest,” Selene corrected. She pushed the stiletto a little harder, and Kael grunted. “Dissect. Study. Your resonance is an anomaly. A flaw in the master’s flawed work. We will take you apart and learn why.

Mireille reached the edge of the basin. She placed her palms flat on the cold, slick stone. “Aric’s work wasn’t flawed,” she said, her voice dropping into a register Kaelen remembered from the old workshop. Lecturing. Certain. “It was just too big for small minds to hold. You think this is about control. About owning a resource.

She looked up, and her eyes were blazing with a light that had nothing to do with the braziers. It was the raw, un-refined Aethel in her blood, the memory of a sun on a beach she’d never seen. “It’s about symbiosis. You never understood that.

Then she did what no Matron should ever do. She opened herself.

It wasn’t a drawing-in. It was an expulsion. A renunciation of the careful walls she’d built for decades. The craving behind her ribs, the hollow ache, she didn’t fight it. She inverted it. She became a vessel not for taking, but for offering. A conduit.

And she began to hum.

It was a low, resonant note, felt more than heard. It vibrated in the stone, in the teeth. It was the fundamental frequency of the Hall itself.

Kaelen’s breath caught. His own vir, always a storm under his skin, answered. It surged, a painful, glorious tide. He didn’t try to calm it. He stepped into the basin, the cold stone under his bare feet, and he joined her.

He didn’t touch her. He stood across the bowl from her, and he matched her hum with one of his own, a fifth above. The harmonics clashed, warred, then braided together. The air between them shimmered—not with light, but with a pressure, a density that made the eyes water.

Selene’s smirk vanished. “Stop them.

Her harvesters moved. Two broke from the line, blades raised, heading for Mireille.

Kaelen didn’t look at them. He looked at the men still crowded at the door, their faces etched with terror and awe. “Now,” he said, the word a growl that rode the harmonic. “Give it to the basin. Don’t hold back. Don’t shape it. Just let it go.

Silas was the first. He threw his head back and roared, a raw, wordless sound of pure fury. And he released. It wasn’t the controlled spill of a harvest. It was a geyser. A visible ripple of heat and intent that shot from him, not toward the invaders, but into the stone bowl at Kaelen’s feet.

The basin drank it.

Then the others followed.

It was chaos. A torrent of raw, unfocused male essence, the compounded frustration, rage, and buried power of thirty captive lives. It flooded into the basin, and the harmonics Kaelen and Mireille were weaving seized it. The discordant noise focused, sharpened. The air in the Hall thickened to soup. Selene’s harvesters, halfway to Mireille, staggered as if hitting a wall. One dropped his sword, clawing at his throat.

Mireille’s hum became a wordless song. Her body was rigid, trembling, veins standing out in her neck. She was the lens. The power wasn’t hers to wield, but hers to direct. She turned her head, slowly, and her burning eyes found Selene.

Kaelen felt the shift. The collective power wanted a target. It was anger made manifest. He was the anchor, the grounding rod in the storm. He reached across the basin, not with his hand, but with his will. He found Mireille in the maelstrom. Not her body. The core of her, the wounded, brilliant, addicted woman who’d built a cage and called it mastery.

Their connection snapped into place. It was not gentle.

It was an invasion. A fusion. He felt the ghost-taste of Aric’s honey, the phantom sun on her skin, the acrid shame of the empty vial. She felt the cold of the cell, the sear of the calibration rune, the stubborn, unkillable ember of defiance he’d nursed for years. There was no privacy. No mercy. In that instant, they were one circuit.

And the power had its path.

It leaped from the basin, not as a wave, but as a whip-crack of concentrated will. It bypassed the struggling harvesters entirely. It struck Selene.

She didn’t scream. Her mouth opened, but no sound came out. The stiletto fell from her hand, clattering on the dais. Her eyes went wide, then blank. The power didn’t burn her. It didn’t cut. It filled her.

It was the opposite of harvesting. It was a forced infusion, a drowning in the raw, unchanneled essence of the men she’d come to destroy. Her body, trained to refine and control, had no defense against the flood. Her back arched. A thin, high sound finally escaped her lips, the squeal of a teakettle. She shuddered, violently, a series of jerking spasms. Then she collapsed, crumpling to the polished wood beside the kneeling Kael, a string of saliva connecting her mouth to the floor.

The harmonic collapsed. The pressure vanished. The sudden silence was deafening, a physical shock.

Kaelen’s legs gave out. He caught himself on the slick rim of the basin, his breath sawing. Across from him, Mireille slumped against the stone, her face grey with exhaustion, her hands bleeding where her nails had bitten into the carvings.

Around the Hall, Selene’s harvesters stood frozen, their purpose shattered with their Matron’s mind. The captured Grist workers stared, uncomprehending.

In the doorway, Silas slowly lowered his raised fists. He looked at his hands, then at the fallen Selene, then at Kaelen in the basin. A slow, dawning understanding smoothed the rage from his face.

Kael pushed himself up from the dais, wiping the blood from his neck. He looked at Mireille, then at the men. His expression was unreadable. Then he bent, picked up Selene’s stiletto, and walked calmly to the nearest invader. He placed the tip against the man’s chest. “Drop your weapon. Order your people to drop theirs.

The man let his sword fall. The clang was terribly loud.

One by one, the sounds of surrender echoed through the Hall.

The clearing took hours. They moved the dead to the refinery, a temporary morgue. The captured invaders were locked in the empty Source dormitory, under guard. The Grist’s survivors—Harvesters and sources, the old divisions meaningless now—worked side by side, hauling, cleaning, binding wounds. The rain started again, a soft, steady patter on the slate roof, washing the blood from the courtyard cobbles.

As dusk bled the color from the sky, they gathered in the Hall of Reaping. The braziers were lit again, but with ordinary fire, the clean smell of woodsmoke slowly overpowering the old scents of incense and essence. Everyone stood. No dais. No basin.

Mireille stood with them. She wore a borrowed tunic and trousers, her own clothes ruined. She looked smaller. Human. Kaelen stood beside her, not behind, not in front. His shoulder ached where he’d hit the wall. A deep, satisfying throb.

She didn’t address them as a Matron. Her voice was hoarse, stripped raw. “The Grist is finished. Selene’s people were the blade, but the order came from outside. From the Guild. They know this place exists now. They know what we can do. They will send more.

A murmur, weary, resigned.

“You have choices,” she continued. She looked at the faces, the harvesters she’d commanded, the men she’d catalogued. “There are ships in Slake. You can take them. Disappear. Start over somewhere this trade is just a sick rumor.” She paused. “Or you can stay.

Silas crossed his arms. “Stay and do what? Rebuild the farm?

“No.” The word was final. “The farm is dead. The ledger is burned. I’m talking about something else. A covenant. Not of ownership. Of mutual aid.” She glanced at Kaelen. He gave a slight nod. “The power we used today… it wasn’t mine. It wasn’t his. It was yours. And you directed it. Voluntarily. That’s the heresy. Not the somatic art. The choice.

Kael, his neck bandaged, spoke from the crowd. His voice was rough. “What are you proposing, Mireille?

She took a deep breath. “A collective. A sanctuary for those who can’t go back. We study Aric’s real work—the symbiosis. We learn to use what we have without exploitation. We protect each other. And we prepare. Because the Guild will come. Not for vir. For us. For the memory of what we did here today.

She fell silent. The only sound was the rain and the crackle of the fires.

Silas was the first to step forward. Then a harvester named Jeyne. Then Bren, limping from his wound. One by one, they moved. Not a rush, but a slow, deliberate convergence. They formed a loose circle around the cold, empty basin.

No one knelt. No one offered a vow. They just stood there, in the gathering dark, present.

Kaelen felt the truth of it settle in his bones, a quiet, solid thing. This was the new shape. Fragile. Terrifying. Alive.

Mireille’s hand found his. Her fingers were cold, but her grip was firm. She didn’t look at him. She watched the circle of faces, her own expression weary, uncertain, and utterly free of the old, calculating light.

Outside, the rain whispered against the stones, a sound like a beginning, or a long-forgotten name being spoken again into the dark.
