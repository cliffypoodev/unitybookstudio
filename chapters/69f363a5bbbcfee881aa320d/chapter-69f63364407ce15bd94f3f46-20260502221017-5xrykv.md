The Crimson Gallery smelled of old velvet and the faint, sweet decay of lilies left too long in a vase. Corinne stood at the one-way glass, arms crossed, the stone in her pocket a cool, familiar weight. The playroom below was a study in calculated excess. Plush crimson carpets, walls draped in gold-shot fabric, low couches arranged around a central platform. The air down there would taste of sandalwood incense and the sharp, clean scent of the antiseptic wipes the attendants used between guests.

Guest Armand was already there. He moved around the platform like a surgeon assessing his theater, adjusting the set of a velvet-lined restraint cuff, testing the give of the silk ties hanging from the overhead frame. He wore a dark robe, open at the front. His hands were precise, his movements economical. Corinne had approved his request for a private session with Seventeen. Armand paid triple the standard guest fee and had never caused procedural damage. He was a connoisseur, he said. He appreciated the craft.

The door to the playroom opened it. Evard led Seventeen—Tomas—inside.

He wore only the simple linen pants issued to the stock. No shirt. The supplements had done their work; his skin had a faint, healthy sheen under the warm light of the crystal sconces, the muscle definition of his shoulders and arms more pronounced than it had been that morning. His face was a closed door. He looked at the platform, at the restraints, at Armand. His eyes didn’t widen. His chest rising didn’t hitch. He just took it in, that flat, still pool in his gaze absorbing the details without a ripple.

“The central platform, please,” Armand said, his voice muffled by the glass but clear through the speaker grille set into the Gallery’s wall.

Evard guided Tomas forward. A flicker of something crossed the handler’s face—a tightness around the eyes, gone in a blink. He helped Tomas onto the platform. Tomas went, his movements fluid, cooperative even. He lay back on the padded surface, his head resting in the carved cradle. He let Evard secure his ankles to the lower rings, his wrists to the silken ties above his head. He didn’t struggle. He didn’t tense.

That was the first wrong thing.

Compliance was good. This wasn’t compliance. This was surrender. A total, hollow relinquishment. He was giving them nothing to push against, no friction to heat the process. Armand wouldn’t like that. Connoisseurs wanted a fight. They wanted to feel the moment of breaking, the crisp snap of will. This… this was just a body on a slab.

Armand circled the platform. He stopped at Tomas’s head, looking down. “You are the new prime. Seventeen. I’ve read your intake assessment. Potent. Resilient.” He reached out didn’t touch, just let his fingers hover an inch from Tomas’s temple. “Show me your resilience.”

He moved to a side table, picked up a small, brushed-steel cylinder. A diffuser. He pressed a button at its base. A fine, nearly invisible mist hissed out, carrying a compound Corinne knew well—aerosolized pheromones keyed to accelerate arousal, lower inhibitory thresholds. It was safe. Standard. Used in the communal rooms to synchronize the stock.

Armand passed the diffuser slowly over Tomas’s body, from head to foot, then back again. He set it down.

Nothing happened.

Tomas lay there, stillness slow and even, his body relaxed against the restraints. No flush climbed his neck. No telltale twitch or swell beneath the linen. His eyes stayed fixed on the gilded ceiling medallion, some distant, complex pattern of vines and fruits.

A knot, cold and small, formed under Corinne’s breastbone. She rubbed the stone through the silk of her pocket.

Armand’s pleasant expression didn’t change. He untied the silk cord of his robe, let it fall open. He wasn’t aroused either. This was business. He picked up a glass bottle from the table, poured a clear, oily liquid into his palm. It smelled of almonds and something metallic, even through the glass. Slick. He warmed it between his hands.

“Let’s begin,” he said.

His touch was clinical. He started at Tomas’s collarbone, hands spreading the oil in broad, smooth strokes across his chest. Down the sternum. Over the ribs. The friction was deliberate, firm. Tomas’s skin glistened. His muscles corded, a brief, involuntary tension at the invasion of touch, then went deliberately slack again. Armand’s hands moved lower, skirting the waistband of the linen pants, working the oil into the tense planes of his abdomen.

Corinne’s throat tightened. She remembered hands on her, not cruel, just… efficient. Assessing. A tutor’s hands when she was fourteen, measuring her posture for a dance she didn’t want to learn. Bend here. Arch there. Good. Now hold. The reward had been a sweet, a single honey-dipped almond placed on her tongue. The transaction complete.

Armand hooked his thumbs in the waistband of Tomas’s pants. He pulled them down, slow, revealing him. Tomas didn’t help. He didn’t resist. His body was limp, heavy. Exposed.

Still nothing. No physical response at all.

Armand’s jaw tightened. A faint line appeared between his brows. He poured more oil. This time his hands were not clinical. They were demanding. He worked with a rhythmic, insistent pressure, his technique shifting from assessment to coercion. It was a method Corinne knew—persistent, overstimulating touch meant to trigger a reflexive response regardless of mental state. The body could betray the mind.

Tomas’s moment hitched. A sharp inhale. His hips jerked, a tiny, aborted movement against the restraint. His eyes squeezed shut. A flush, dark and angry, bloomed across his chest.

“There,” Armand murmured, a note of satisfaction in his voice. He increased the pace, his grip turning brutal, focused solely on forcing a reaction. It wasn’t pleasure. It was mechanics. A pump being primed.

Tomas turned his head to the side, face pressing into the velvet padding. His shoulders trembled. A sound escaped him—not a moan, not a gasp. A choked, ragged thing, bitten off at the end.

The cold knot under Corinne’s ribs sharpened became a needle of ice. She watched Armand’s hands, the ruthless efficiency. She watched Tomas’s face, the way his teeth ground into the velvet, the vein pounding in his temple. This was yield. This was what she’d wanted to see that morning, the break, the proof. But it was empty. It was just a body being forced to perform a function, like a stubborn engine cranked until it sputtered to life. There was no victory in it. There was only the transaction. The almond on the tongue. entry.

He understands the cage, he’d said. And this was the cage. This precise, gilded, perfumed violence.

Armand changed tactics again. He reached for a different bottle, something with a deeper, muskier scent. He spoke, his voice low, pitched to carry through the speaker. “You will provide. It is your function. Your only value. You are a vessel. Empty yourself.”

The words landed in Corinne’s gut like stones.

Your only value.

A memory, unbidden: her mother’s voice, cool and disappointed, the morning after a party. You didn’t smile enough, Corinne. The Baron asked after you, and you gave him numbers about the harvest. He wanted a flower, not an accountant. The punishment had been a week confined to her rooms, reviewing social protocols until she could recite them in her sleep. The lesson: perform correctly, or you have no worth.

On the platform, Tomas’s body finally, traitorously began to respond to the chemical and physical assault. A purely biological reaction. His face was a mask of agony, not ecstasy. Tears leaked from the corners of his shut eyes cutting tracks through the oil on his temples.

Armand smiled. He moved to mount the platform.

Corinne’s hand slammed down on the brass intercom button on the wall. The sound was a harsh crackle in the Gallery. “Stop.”

Armand froze, halfway onto the platform. He looked up, directly at the glass where he knew she stood. Confusion, then a flash of irritation, crossed his face. “Matron? We are nearing culmination.”

“The session is concluded.” She lowered the words to strange in her own ears, thin and tight.

“The yield is not yet—”

“I said concluded.” She pressed the button again, this time for the handler’s channel. “Evard. Extract Seventeen. Return him to his stall. Now.”

Armand stood up, his robe gaping. He glared at the glass. “This is a breach of contract, Matron. I paid for a full session. I paid for the break.”

Corinne released the button. She didn’t answer. She watched as Evard, moving with stunned haste, unfastened the silk ties, the ankle cuffs. Tomas didn’t move. He lay there, shaking, curled slightly onto his side as soon as his hands were free, arms crossing over his chest. Evard pulled the linen pants back up, then draped a plain gray blanket over him. He helped him sit up, then stand. Tomas’s legs buckled. Evard caught him, half-dragged him toward the door.

Armand snatched his robe from the floor, tying it with violent jerks. He stared after them, then up at the glass once more, his mouth a hard, flat line.

Corinne turned from the window. The lilies in the vase on the side table were brown at the edges, one petal floating in the cloudy water. The velvet drapes were faded along the folds where the sun hit them each afternoon, a stripe of pale crimson. She walked out of the Crimson Gallery, her heels silent on the runner in the hall. The stone in her pocket felt suddenly too smooth, too worn. Like it had never been anything but a pebble.

The stone in her pocket was warm from her grip, the smoothness of it now an irritant against her palm. Corinne stood outside the cell door, her knuckles inches from the steel. The scent of antiseptic from the Orchid Room still clung to her clothes, a sickly sweet ghost over the sharper smells of hay and damp stone from the barn aisle. A memory surfaced, unbidden—the honeyed almond on her tongue at fourteen, the dry, papery feel of the tutor’s hand on her spine. She blinked it away.

She opened the door without knocking.

The cell was a cube of white tile and pale light from a single, recessed panel. Tomas sat on the edge of his cot, a blanket pooled around his waist. He’d been given a basin of water, a cloth. He held the cloth in one hand, suspended over his thigh, not moving. The oil from Armand’s session still glazed his skin in patches, catching the light on the swell of a shoulder, the dip of a collarbone. Red marks circled his wrists—not from the silken ties, but from her own hands when she’d pulled him off the platform. A thin, straight cut, clean and precise, bisected his lower lip. Armand’s ring, probably, during the struggle she’d terminated.

He didn’t look up.

Corinne closed the door. The click of the latch was loud in the sterile quiet. She leaned against the steel, crossing her arms. The cold seeped through the silk of her blouse.

“Your yield was zero,” she said. She spoke — flat, a recitation. “Your compliance, as measured by physiological response was negative. You absorbed the compound. You metabolized it. You gave nothing back.”

He lifted the cloth, dabbed at a patch of oil on his sternum. The movement was slow, exhausted. Water dripped from the cloth onto the tile floor. Plink. A tiny, maddening sound.

“Armand is a platinum-tier guest. His displeasure has a quantifiable cost. It will be deducted from your caloric allocation. From your light allowance. From the thickness of your blanket.” She gestured to the wool around his hips. “That one is a loan. It goes back to the linen stores tomorrow. You’ll get a thinner one.”

Finally, he raised his head. His eyes were the same flat pools, but the depths were different now—not still, but spent. A hollowed-out calm. “Are you waiting for an apology?”

The question hung between them. She hadn’t expected him to speak. The sound of his voice, raspy from disuse or suppressed cries was a physical thing in the small room.

“I am waiting,” she said, pushing off from the door, taking one step into the space, “for you to explain the failure.”

A faint, bitter twist touched his mouth, pulling at the cut. It wasn’t a smile. “There was no failure. The system worked. You put a body in a room. The body was touched. The body did not respond. The system recorded the data. The data was clear.”

“The body is not supposed to have an opinion.”

“The body is all opinion,” he said, so low she almost missed it. He looked past her, at the blank wall. “It says yes or no with every silence. You just don’t like its vocabulary.”

She took another step. The distance closed to six feet. She could smell the almond oil, the metallic undertone of the slick, the faint copper of the blood on his lip. Underneath it, the clean, salty scent of his skin. He was right. The body had a language. Hers was speaking now—a tightness across her shoulders, a dry-mouthed awareness of the space between them, of his nakedness under the blanket, of the red marks on his skin that were her fingerprints.

“You think this is a victory,” she said. “This… silence. This hollowing out. You think by feeling nothing, you win.”

“I didn’t feel nothing.” He met her eyes then, and the hollows filled with something dark, something hot and weary. “I felt every second. I counted the threads in the ceiling medallion. I remembered the pattern of cracks in the wall of a room I lived in when I was nine. I felt the oil. I felt his hands. I felt the weight of your gaze through the glass. I felt it all. I just didn’t give it to him.”

The admission was a violation. It was too much truth. It stripped the transaction bare. He wasn’t resisting; he was hoarding. He was taking every sensation, every humiliation, and locking it away inside that quiet, defiant frame. Making it his.

Her own air felt short. The clinical air of the cell was suddenly insufficient. She needed the smell of the rain, of the turned earth outside, anything but this closed-box intimacy.

“Why?” The word left her before she could stop it. It was the wrong question. It was personal.

He looked at her for a long moment, his gaze traveling over her face—the careful knot of her hair, the line of her jaw, the unyielding set of her mouth. It was the same assessment he’d given her in the stall, but softer now, blurred at the edges by exhaustion. “You watched,” he said simply. “You always watch. But today you stopped it. Why?”

A trap. A reversal. The cold knot under her breastbone tightened. She couldn’t answer. To say she saw the tutor’s hands, the honeyed almond, of her own childhood where affection was a column to be filled—that was a currency she wouldn’t spend. Not here. Not with him.

“Guest privilege is sacrosanct,” she recited, the doctrine dry as dust on her tongue. “Interruption incurs a penalty equal to three times the session’s value. I have compromised my own operational integrity.”

He dropped the cloth into the basin. The wet slap echoed. “Liar.”

The word hung in the air, simple and absolute.

Her hand was in her pocket, fingers clenched around the stone. Her pulse beat hard in her throat. She took the last steps, crossing the space until she stood over him. The top of his head was level with her ribs. She could see the faint, whorled pattern of his hair, a tiny scar like a crescent moon near his temple. An old thing.

“You want to know why?” She spoke, quieter now, a private thing for the white walls. “Because he was enjoying it. Not the touch. The failure. The breaking. He wanted to document the exact moment you ceased to be a problem and became a thing. And I…” She stopped. The truth was a live wire in her chest. “I have a ledger for that. I don’t need his notes in my margins.”

His head tilted back. He looked up at her, his neck exposed. A gesture of vulnerability, or challenge. “So it wasn’t mercy. It was territorial.”

“There is no mercy here.”

“I know.” He didn’t look away. His eyes held hers. The spent calm was gone, replaced by a focused, weary intensity. “Your hand is shaking.”

She glanced down. It was. The faintest tremor in her fingers where they rested against her thigh. She made a fist drove the tremor inward.

He moved then. Not fast, but with a deliberate, fluid grace that belied his position. He reached out, his hand hovering near her clenched one. Not touching. An offer. A question.

Every protocol screamed. Every rule written in the cold logic of the Farm’s ledgers. She did not let them touch her. She was the hand that turned the page, not a word upon it.

Her fist uncurled. Her fingers, stiff settled against his open palm.

The contact was a jolt of pure, unmediated heat. His skin was warmer than she’d imagined, the calluses on his palm rough against her softer flesh. A working hand, once. A memory of a life before the barn. He didn’t close his fingers around hers. He just held them there, letting the heat bleed between them.

The silence in the cell changed. It wasn’t empty anymore. It was thick, charged, like the air before a lightning strike. She could hear her own pause shallow and quick. She could see the slow rise and fall of his chest beneath the blanket. The cut on his lip was a dark, precise line.

She was the one who closed the distance. Her other hand came up, not to strike, not to punish. Her thumb brushed the blood from his lip. The touch was clinical at first. Then it wasn’t. Her thumb traced the swell of his lower lip, feeling the split in the skin, the surprising softness around it.

His breath hitched. A small, sharp intake.

That sound undid her. A crack in his perfect control. An answer, in the body’s vocabulary.

She bent, her free hand coming to cradle the back of his head, her fingers tangling in his hair. It was coarse, thick. She pulled his face to hers and kissed him.

It wasn’t gentle. It was a claim, a contradiction, a war fought on the border of their mouths. The taste of blood—copper and salt—mixed with the faint, cloying remnant of almond oil. He went utterly still for a heartbeat. Then his hands came up, one still holding hers, the other gripping her hip through the silk of her trousers. His mouth opened under hers, not in surrender, but in a kind of furious, desperate answer. He kissed her back like a man pulling air into starved lungs, all hunger and ragged edges.

She forgot. She forgot the glass, the scent of lilies, the tutor’s hands. There was only this: the heat of his mouth, the scrape of his stubble against her chin, the solid reality of his shoulder under her hand. A transaction, but one with no terms, no columns. Just this wild, unbalanced exchange.

When she broke away, her lips felt bruised, alive. They were both silence between them hard. The blanket had slipped further, baring him to the waist. The marks on his skin were livid in the pale light. Her marks.

He looked up at her, his eyes wide, dark, stripped of all their careful flatness. Raw.

“See?” he whispered, his voice rough. “The body has an opinion.”

She stepped back, releasing him. The cool air of the cell rushed into the space between them, a shock. She straightened her blouse, a useless, automatic gesture. Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic, traitorous drum.

She turned for the door. Her hand on the latch was steady now. Dead steady.

“The thinner blanket arrives at dawn,” she said, and The words came out Matron Corinne’s voice again, cool and final. “Your caloric reduction begins with the morning meal.”

She left without looking back. The click of the latch behind her was the loudest sound she’d ever heard.
