Chapter 3

The morning harvest bell sounded wrong. Not the clean, resonant chime that cut through the valley’s perpetual drizzle, but a choked metallic clatter. Corinne stood on the observation platform overlooking the central barn’s main floor, her knuckles white on the polished railing. The scent hit her first, overriding the usual antiseptic and hay: ozone, sharp and electric, from the perimeter fences. Something had tripped them. A tremor started in her left hand, a fine vibration she pressed into the cold steel.

Below, the scheduled harvest was a stalled machine. The Primes stood in their designated stalls, but the Harvesters weren’t moving to their stations. They clustered in tense knots by the equipment lockers. Linden, her most compliant Prime, stared straight ahead at the wall, his jaw working. He was usually a study in placid readiness. Not today. Today his shoulders were hunched, a defensive curve she’d never seen. A crack in the ceiling above his stall, a long, jagged line like a lightning bolt frozen in plaster, held his gaze. He was counting the tributaries. She knew the look. She’d done it herself as a child, tracing the water stains on the ceiling of her mother’s sitting room, trying to find a pattern that meant something.

“Report.”

The word left her throat flat. Kael, her Head Harvester, detached himself from a group and mounted the steps to the platform. His face was a closed door. He didn’t meet her eyes, instead looking past her shoulder at the main entrance. “The eastern gate is compromised. The failsafe engaged, but the secondary power coupling is fried. It’s offline.”

“How?”

“Sabotage. Clean cut on the conduit.” He finally looked at her. The loyalty she’d taken for granted was gone, replaced by a cold, professional assessment. “Lord Vane’s retinue left before dawn. Took three of the junior Handlers with them. They also emptied the strongroom in the main house.”

The words landed, one after another, like stones dropped into still water. She felt each impact in her stomach, a heavy, sickening plunge. Vane. Of course. The humiliated guest, his privileges revoked, his cruelty checked. He hadn’t just left. He’d gutted her on his way out. The strongroom held the refined Aethel, the product, the currency. But the gate. The gate was a message. An invitation.

“He’s coming back,” she said. It wasn’t a question.

“He’s not alone.” Kael’s voice dropped. “Scouts saw dust on the Slake road. A lot of it. Wagons. Armed men. Not just his. Others.”

A coalition. Rivals she’d kept at bay with quality and exclusivity, now smelling blood in the water. They wouldn’t just take the Aethel. They’d take the infrastructure. The system. The Livestock. Tomas.

Her hand went to the smooth stone in her pocket. Her thumb found the familiar groove. The chaos below had a sound now—a low, anxious murmur, the shuffle of boots on concrete, the sharp click of a safety being disengaged on a Harvester’s shock-rod. They were choosing sides. She saw it in the way they avoided her gaze, in the calculated distance they kept from Kael. Her control, the detailed clockwork of compliance and consequence she’d built over a decade, was seizing up, grinding its own gears to dust.

She thought of the ledgers in her study, the perfect rows of numbers that defined every life under this roof. Yield, compliance, nutritional intake, punitive measures. It was all a lie. A story she’d told herself to make the machinery seem inevitable. The real ledger was in the bruise on Tomas’s hip, in the hollowed-out look he’d carried last night, in the way her own breath had caught when his forehead touched hers. A column of damages with no corresponding credit.

“Where is he?” she asked. The words shifted, strange to her. Thin.

Kael knew who she meant. “Cell Block B. As ordered after last night’s incident.”

“Is he secure?”

A flicker of something like pity crossed Kael’s face. “The cell is secure. The man inside it?” He shrugged. “You broke the protocol, Matron. You showed a flaw. The whole system’s built on the idea that you don’t have any.”

The truth of it was a physical blow. She’d shown a crack, and the entire valley was rushing in to widen it. She looked down at her hands on the railing. The tremor was gone. A cold, clear stillness had taken its place. It wasn’t calm. It was the moment before the leap, when the world shrank to a single, irreversible choice.

“Gather the Primes,” she said. “Linden, Tomas, the two others from the north stable. Bring them to the equipment bay. Not in restraints.”

Kael stiffened. “Matron—”

“Do it.” She turned from the railing, from the view of her crumbling kingdom. “And unlock the armory. Distribute the shock-rods and the bolt-cutters. To everyone. Harvesters and Livestock alike.”

He stared at her as if she’d spoken in a dead language. The protocol was carved in stone: Livestock never touched tools. Never held weapons. It was the first, inviolable law.

“You’re arming them?”

“I’m freeing them,” she said, and the words, once spoken, became true. A weight she hadn’t known she carried lifted from her shoulders, leaving only a terrifying, giddy lightness. “The Farm is a target. Anyone here when Vane’s coalition arrives will be property. I am dissolving the operation. They can flee into the Sink, take their chances with the silence and the fungus. Or they can stand with me and fight for the right to walk out of here as something other than inventory. Their choice.”

Kael’s mouth was a hard line. “And you think they’ll fight for you?”

“No.” She met his gaze. “I think they’ll fight for themselves. And I am going to give them the means. Now move.”

He moved, his boots a sharp tattoo on the metal steps. The orders he barked below were met with stunned silence, then a surge of chaotic motion. Corinne didn’t wait to watch it unfold. She descended the opposite staircase, her heels echoing in the suddenly vast space of the barn. The air tasted of panic and galvanized metal. She passed a spilled bucket of antiseptic, the sharp pine scent clawing at her throat. A memory flashed, unbidden: her mother dabbing jasmine oil on her wrists before a client arrived, the smile that never touched her eyes. A transaction. Always a transaction.

She reached Cell Block B. The guard at the door—Evard, young, anxious—flinched at her approach.

“Open it.”

He fumbled with the key. The lock turned with a heavy clunk. She pushed the door open.

Tomas stood at the back of the small cell, facing the wall. He’d heard the bell, the commotion. His posture was rigid, braced for the next blow. The bruise on his hip was a livid purple in the weak light from the high, barred window.

“Tomas.”

He didn’t turn. “Come to check on your investment?”

“The Farm is under attack. By people who will make Lord Vane look like a gentle patron. They’re minutes away.”

That made him turn. His eyes were the same grey storm, but the exhaustion was gone, burned away by a fierce, alert intensity. He searched her face for the lie, the manipulation. He found only the stark, plain truth.

“So you’ve come to hide the silver?” he asked, his voice rough.

“I’ve come to give you a weapon.” She stepped fully into the cell, leaving the door open at her back. The gesture was not lost on him. His gaze flicked to the open doorway, then back to her. “I’m unlocking every cell. I’m arming anyone who will take a tool. The ones who want to run can run. The ones who want to fight can fight.”

He was silent for a long moment. The sounds of the barn—shouted orders, the clatter of metal—filtered into the small space. “Why?”

The question she’d been asking herself since she left the platform. She could give him the strategic reasons. The untenable position. The tactical advantage of a desperate, armed resistance. They were all true. They weren’t the reason.

“Because I am tired of ledgers,” she said, the words raw. “Because that bruise is on my ledger now. And the only way to settle the account is to burn the whole damn book.”

He took a step toward her. Not the slow, forced step of a prisoner, but the deliberate step of a man crossing a threshold. He stopped an arm’s length away. The air between them crackled, charged with the chaos outside and the quieter, more dangerous chaos within.

“You’re asking me to fight for you.”

She studied him, her eyes tracing the lines of his face as if reading a new text. Then, slowly, she nodded. Not a bow. Not submission. A pact.

“Then we fight,” he said.

Outside, the first warning shout echoed through the barn, followed by the distant, obvious crack of a shock-rod firing. The battle for the Farm had begun.

The chaos had a texture. It wasn’t just the shouts, the wet smack of flesh on flesh, the high whine of a shock-rod discharged into the air. It was the way the light caught the dust they’d kicked up from the barn floor, turning the beams from the high windows into solid, golden shafts you could almost lean against. Tomas saw it all from a strange, flat distance. His body moved—he ducked a swing from a Harvester whose name he’d never learned, drove his shoulder into the man’s gut, felt the air leave him in a sour puff of panic and cheap beer—but his mind was elsewhere. It was tracing the grain of the wood on the stall door beside him, the deep, whorled pattern like a frozen river. He’d stared at that door for weeks. He’d never noticed the river.

Then Corinne was there. Not the Matron from the observation platform, a silhouette of control. This was a woman with her hair coming loose from its knot, a dark streak of something that might have been blood or dirt across her cheekbone. She held a shock-rod like she’d been born with it, her movements economical, vicious. She didn’t shout orders. She just moved, and the space around her cleared, a pocket of terrible, focused silence in the noise.

She caught his eye. Just a flicker. Her gaze went to the double doors at the barn’s far end, the ones that led to the private playrooms. Then back to him. A question. No, a statement. With me.

He went. It was that simple. The rock in the stream had decided to move, and the current changed. They fought back-to-back for a handful of seconds, a clumsy, spent dance. He felt the heat of her body through the thin linen of her shirt. He smelled her sweat, sharp and human, cutting through the barn’s antiseptic stink. A man lunged at her from the side—Evard, the nervous Handler from the first day. Tomas moved without thinking, his arm hooking around Evard’s neck, pulling him off balance. Corinne’s rod jabbed forward, not to stun, but to crack against his temple. Evard folded. The look she gave Tomas then wasn’t gratitude. It was recognition. An animal acknowledgement of a shared kill.

They broke for the doors. A bolt of energy from a misaimed shock-rod scorched the wood beside Tomas’s head, filling his nostrils with the scent of burnt pine. Then they were through, slamming the heavy doors behind them, throwing the bolt. The sound of the fight became a muffled, roaring ocean.

The Orchid Room was a ruin. A guest’s chaise lounge was overturned, its silk slashed. A crystal decanter lay shattered, the expensive perfume it had held now a cloying, dying sweetness in the air. The frescoes of entwined bodies seemed to leer down at the wreckage. Corinne leaned against the door, her chest rising and falling. A thin line of blood welled from a split in her lower lip. She touched it, looked at the red on her fingertips.

“They’ll break through,” she said, ragged.

“I know.” He was across the room, his own breath raw in his throat. The bruise on his hip throbbed in time with his heartbeat. A counter-rhythm.

She pushed off from the door. Walked toward him. Not the slow, predatory stalk of the Matron assessing livestock. This was different. Direct. Necessary. “Vane’s out there. He’ll want you back. He’ll want to finish what he started.”

“I know that, too.”

She stopped an arm’s length away. The dangerous light he’d seen in her eyes last night was back, but it was hotter now, fed by violence and the crumbling of walls. “I have nothing left to trade,” she said. The words were stripped bare. “No Aethel. No system. No authority. It’s just this.” She gestured between them, a sharp, frustrated slash of her hand. “Whatever this is.”

He understood. The old order was ash. All that remained was the damage column, and the two of them standing in it. The realization was a cold clarity, washing the last of the distance away. He closed the space between them.

His hand came up, cupped the side of her face. His thumb brushed the streak on her cheek. It was dirt. Her skin was warm, alive under his touch. She didn’t flinch. She leaned into it, her eyes closing for a second, a shudder passing through her. When she opened them, the grey was storm-dark, pupils wide.

“This,” he said, the word rough, “is mine. I’m not a transaction.”

“Then take it.”

It wasn’t a surrender. It was a challenge. A gauntlet thrown down on the ruins of her empire. He answered it with his mouth on hers.

The kiss was nothing like the forced performance of the harvests. It was all teeth and desperation, a clash of heat and the copper taste of her blood. She met him with equal ferocity, her fingers tangling in his hair, pulling him closer. There was no technique, only need—a raw, starving thing that tore a groan from his chest. Her hands went to the fastenings of his trousers, his to the buttons of her shirt. Fabric tore. Buttons skittered across the floor, lost in the perfume and dust. There was no time for finesse, no room for the slow, curated seduction of the Farm. This was a claiming.

He backed her against the wall, the cool plaster a shock against her bare skin. She wrapped one leg around his hips, pulling him into the cradle of her body. The first thrust was a blunt, searing shock of connection that made her cry out, a sharp, broken sound that was nothing like the staged sighs of the playroom. Her head fell back against the wall, her throat a pale arc. He drove into her again, setting a rhythm that was pure instinct, each movement shaking a gasp from her lips. The pain in his hip was a distant echo, drowned out by the roaring in his ears, by the feel of her, tight and hot around him, by the way her nails scored his back.

Her hands were everywhere—gripping his shoulders, sliding down the sweat-slick plane of his back, finding his hips to pull him deeper. Her eyes were open, locked on his, and in them he saw the last vestiges of the Matron burn away. What was left was feral, glorious, and utterly his. “Look at me,” she gasped, the command frayed at the edges. “Don’t you dare look away.”

He didn’t. He watched her come apart. He felt the tension coil in her belly, heard her breath hitch, saw the moment her world narrowed to the single, shattering point of their joining. Her climax tore through her with a silent, shuddering violence, her body clamping around him, pulling him over the edge with her. His release was a white-hot fracture, a surrender that felt like victory, pouring into her as he buried his face in the sweat-damp hollow of her neck.

For a long moment, there was only the sound of their ragged quiet between them, the pound of blood in his ears. The muted chaos from the barn was a world away. He stayed inside her, his weight braced against the wall, her leg still hooked around him. Her forehead rested against his collarbone. Her breath was warm on his skin.

Slowly, feeling returned to the edges of the world. The scent of sex and shattered perfume. The gritty feel of plaster dust under his forearms. The solid, real weight of her in his arms.

She shifted, and he eased back, letting her slide down the wall until her feet found the floor. She didn’t let go of him. Her hands stayed on his arms, holding him there. She looked up, her face naked in the ruined light. The stone-cold Matron was gone. The defiant prisoner was gone. What stood before him was a woman, stripped to her essence, and for the first time, he saw no cage behind her eyes.

“Tomas,” she said. Just his name. It sounded different in her mouth. Not an inventory number. A fact.

A heavy thud hit the bolted doors, making the wood shudder. A shout, muffled but close. Vane’s voice, angry and slurred.

The world rushed back in. But it was a different world.

He bent, retrieved her shock-rod from where it had fallen. He pressed it into her hand, his fingers closing over hers for a second. Then he turned, picking up a length of broken chair leg from the floor. It was solid oak, heavy in his grip.

She looked from the rod in her hand to the makeshift club in his, then back to his face. A ghost of her old, dry smile touched her lips. “Not a very balanced arsenal.”

“It’ll do,” he said.

He took his place beside her, facing the shuddering doors. Not in front. Not behind. Beside.
