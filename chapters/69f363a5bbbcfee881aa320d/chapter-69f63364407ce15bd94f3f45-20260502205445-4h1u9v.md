The morning air in the barns tasted of wet hay and the sharp, chemical tang of the floor disinfectant. Corinne moved through the long, white-tiled aisle between the rows of pens, the hem of her linen dress whispering against her calves. A handler, a young man named Evard, trailed behind her, tablet in hand, but his presence was a formality. The data was already in her head—Yield volumes from the night’s automated collections, hydration levels, resting heart rates. She saw the numbers superimposed over the physical forms of the men behind the polished steel bars. Stall Seven: a slight fever in the subject, likely a minor infection. She noted it without breaking stride, a flick of her fingers toward Evard the only command needed. The man would be isolated, dosed, his fluids collected separately until the readings cleared.

Her own private rhythm governed this walk. Left foot, right foot, a measured pace that allowed her eyes to sweep each stall in a three-count visual inspection. Posture. Eye contact, or lack thereof. The state of the provided water bowl. She passed a man—Stall Twelve, a Prime they called Linden—who was on his knees, forehead pressed to the cool bar, his breathing a controlled, audible pattern. Preparing. Good. Her gaze slid over him like oil over water, registering the compliance, filing it away. A crack in the ceiling tile above Stall Fifteen snagged her attention for a half-step too long. It was shaped like a lopsided star, edges feathered with old moisture damage. It served no purpose. It was just there. She looked away.

Then she reached Stall Twenty-Two.

The new one was standing. They were usually sitting, or lying down, the shock of the transition still heavy on them. This one stood at the back of the enclosure, shoulders squared against the white wall, arms hanging loose at his sides. He was tall, with the lean, ropey build of someone who’d done hard labor, not the softened bulk of long-term confinement. Dark hair, cropped short. Eyes the color of silt. He watched her. Not with the blank terror of the freshly broken, nor the calculated blankness of the resigned. He just watched. As if she were a weather pattern. Interesting, possibly inconvenient.

Something tightened under her ribs. Not anger. A kind of alertness, a tuning-fork hum.

“This one,” she said. Her voice was low, meant for Evard, but it carried in the hushed space. The only other sounds were the distant hum of the ventilation and the drip of a faucet from the washroom. “The intake from Slake.”

“Yes, Matron. Processed last evening. Designation Tomas. No yield history. The slavers said he was a miner from the Sunward foothills. Strong. Resilient.” Evard’s voice was a nervous murmur. He was new himself.

Corinne stepped closer to the bars. The man—Tomas—didn’t retreat. His eyes tracked her, then flicked to Evard, then back to her. Assessing the hierarchy. A stupid thing to do. A smart animal kept its head down.

“Resilient is not a yield metric,” Corinne said, still looking at Tomas. “It is a problem to be solved. Has he been fed?”

“The standard morning ration, Matron. He refused the supplement.”

She’d known that. The refusal was logged in the intake report she’d reviewed at five a. m. with her bitter, lukewarm tea. She’d tasted the tannins, felt the ghost of a headache behind her left eye, and noted the anomaly. Now she saw the untouched protein paste in its shallow bowl by the gate.

“Open it,” she said.

Evard fumbled with the keycard. The magnetic lock disengaged with a solid thunk. Corinne entered the stall. The space was eight by ten, containing a cot, a toilet, the water bowl. It smelled of new plastic and underlying bleach. And him—a scent of cold stone and male sweat, not the cloistered musk of the long-term livestock.

He didn’t move as she approached. She stopped an arm’s length away. Up close, she saw the faint scar through his left eyebrow, the shadow of stubble along his jaw. His hands were curled loosely, the knuckles scarred.

“The supplement accelerates acclimation,” she said. Her tone was clinical, a physician explaining a treatment. “It regulates your system for optimal production. Refusal extends the discomfort. It is inefficient.”

He was quiet for a long moment. His eyes dropped to the stone in her hand—she hadn’t realized she’d taken it from her pocket, was rubbing its smooth surface with her thumb. She stilled her fingers.

“I’m not a machine,” he said. His voice was rougher than she expected, graveled by disuse or dust. “You can’t just prime a pump.”

A metaphor. They always reached for metaphors, the new ones. Pumps, beasts of burden, harvested grain. It was a way to frame the horror in something familiar. She found it tedious.

“You are a biological resource housed within a controlled environment,” she corrected, her words precise as surgical cuts. “Your comfort is a variable we manage for output stability. Your defiance is a statistical error. It will be corrected.”

She expected fear then. Or fury. A flash in those silt-colored eyes.

He breathed in, a slow, deep pull of air that expanded his chest. He held it. Let it out. “Okay.”

That was all. Okay.

Not submission. Acknowledgment. As if she’d stated a fact about the weather—rain is wet—and he’d simply concurred. The hum under her ribs sharpened, narrowed to a fine point of pure, unclassifiable irritation. It was a hot, dry feeling in her throat. She wanted to see him break, just a little. Just enough to prove the system worked.

“Tonight is a communal harvest in the Orchid Room,” she said, taking a deliberate step closer. She was shorter than him, but her presence filled the sterile space. “You will attend. You will participate. Your performance will be graded. Your rations, your privacy privileges, the hardness of your cot—all are functions of your yield and your compliance. Do you understand the operational causality?”

His gaze drifted over her shoulder, to the open stall door, to the freedom of the aisle that led only to another locked door. A muscle in his jaw feathered. He was calculating the distance, the odds. He was smart. He saw the cameras in the upper corners, the silent panic button on Evard’s belt. The calculation died behind his eyes, leaving a flat, still pool.

“I understand the cage,” he said, finally looking back at her.

It wasn’t the right answer. It was, in fact, the most dangerous kind of answer—one that saw the bars for what they were but chose to stand inside them anyway. A passive resistance that was harder to grind down than outright revolt. Revolt had handles. This was just weight.

Corinne turned. She walked out of the stall, the stone a hard, smooth pressure in her clenched fist. “Double his supplement in his evening meal,” she told Evard, her voice betraying nothing. “And put him on the central roster for tonight. Prime station.”

“Prime? But, Matron, his baseline is untested, the protocols for a first harvest suggest a private—”

“Prime station,” she repeated, the words leaving no room. “I want him where the guests can see. Where everyone can see.”

She needed to see what happened when pressure was applied to something that refused to be a pump. Would it shatter? Or would it simply hold its shape, a rock in a stream, diverting the current around it? The data was incomplete. She hated incomplete data.

As she moved down the aisle toward the next stall, she caught the faint, incongruous scent of lemon polish from a handler’s boot, mingling with the ever-present antiseptic. It reminded her of the main house’s grand staircase, buffed to a high gleam every morning. A clean, sharp smell that meant order. Control.

She did not look back at Stall Twenty-Two. But the space between her shoulder blades itched, as if those silt-colored eyes were still on her, watching, measuring the length of her own chain.

The air in the playroom tasted like heated silk and salt. Corinne stood by the marble wet bar, a glass of mineral water sweating in her hand, and watched the geometry of desire unfold. Low, amber light pooled in the corners, leaving the center of the room a stage of shifting shadows and slick skin. The scent was a layered thing—sandalwood incense from a burner on the mantel, the cloying sweetness of a Guest’s perfume, the raw, animal musk of effort and release. A thread had come loose from the hem of the velvet chaise nearest her. She focused on it, the tiny loop of crimson fiber, while the sounds washed around her. Grunts. The wet slap of flesh. A man’s choked-off gasp that might have been pain or something else.

Her Harvesters moved among the Livestock with practiced efficiency. They weren’t rough, not tonight. Tonight was about display, about proving the quality of the product to the three Guests who lounged on divans, their eyes glittering with avarice and arousal. A handler named Jax had a Prime, Linden, bent over a padded bench, working him with a steady, piston-like rhythm. Linden’s face was turned to the side, cheek pressed to leather, his expression smooth and empty as a still pond. Good. Yield was optimal when the subject achieved a detached, meditative state. The clinical part of her mind noted the angle, the leverage, the way Jax’s thumb pressed just so against the base of the spine to stimulate full release.

Then they brought in the new one.

Tomas entered between two Harvesters, his arms held loosely at his sides. They’d cleaned him up, dressed him in the simple linen trousers all the Livestock wore for these events. The fabric was thin. It hid nothing. He walked with that same unsettling awareness, his gaze sweeping the room, cataloging exits, assessing the other men, the Harvesters, the Guests. His eyes found her by the bar and stuck for a second. A hot, dry feeling spread under her sternum. Not anger. An alert. A tripwire plucked.

One of the Guests, a woman with silver hair coiled tight at her nape, pointed a languid finger. “That one. I haven’t seen him.”

Corinne set her glass down. The condensation left a cold ring on her palm. “Fresh stock. From the Sunward mines. His first showing.”

“Mmm. He has a spine. I like that.” The woman’s smile was a thin curve. “Does he perform?”

“All stock performs.” Corinne’s voice was flat, automatic. She watched as the Harvesters guided Tomas to a clear space near the center rug. One of them, a woman named Pella with hands like a sculptor’s, placed a palm on his chest. A directive. Lie down.

He didn’t.

He stood there, a rooted thing, while Pella’s confident smile faltered. She murmured something Corinne couldn’t hear. Tomas shook his head, once. A minute motion. The other Harvester, a broad-shouldered man called Gren, moved in closer, a warning in his posture.

The silver-haired Guest laughed, a sound like breaking glass. “Oh, he’s spirited.”

The hum under Corinne’s ribs sharpened into a needle-point. Inefficiency. Public disruption. A statistical error manifesting in flesh and bone. She moved then, her own steps silent on the thick rug. She crossed the space, the other couplings blurring at the edges of her vision—a tangle of limbs, a sheen of sweat on an arched back, the rhythmic glide of a hand. Her world narrowed to the defiant line of Tomas’s shoulders, the tight set of his jaw.

She stopped before him. Pella and Gren stepped back, yielding the space.

“You are required to participate,” Corinne said, her voice low enough that only he and the Harvesters could catch it. It was not a shout. It was colder. “This is not a negotiation. Compliance is measured. Non-compliance has consequences you have not yet imagined.”

He looked at her. Really looked. His silt-colored eyes held hers, and in them she saw no terror, no blankness. She saw a calculation. A stubborn, stupid fire. “I’m not a show dog,” he said, the gravel in his voice grinding the words rough.

“You are whatever I say you are.” She reached out then, not to strike him, but to place her fingertips against the warm skin of his chest, just over his heart. A clinical assessment. His pulse hammered against her touch, a frantic, trapped rhythm. Good. The body was responding, even if the mind rebelled. “Your physical responses are already engaged. The rest is a formality. Lie down.”

For a stretched second, he held. The muscle in his jaw jumped. The sounds of the room seemed to recede—the wet noises, the moans, the low music from hidden speakers all fading to a distant murmur. There was only the heat of his skin under her fingers, the violent drum of his heart, and the crack in the ceiling above his head she hadn’t noticed before, a jagged line like a lightning bolt frozen in plaster.

Then he let out a breath, a slow, deliberate exhalation that seemed to drain the resistance from his frame. He lowered himself to the rug, his movements stiff, his eyes never leaving hers.

It was not surrender. It was a tactical retreat. She knew the difference.

She nodded to Pella. The Harvester moved in, her hands sure, her touch transitioning from directive to coaxing. Gren took position, his role to stabilize, to apply pressure where needed. Corinne did not move away. She stood as an overseer, a witness. Her role was observation, quality control. That was all.

Pella worked with a methodical artistry. Her hands traced the tense lines of Tomas’s abdomen, her mouth following the path of her fingers. She was trying to gentle him, to lull the animal into compliance. Tomas kept his head turned away, his gaze fixed on the leg of a nearby chair. His body remained rigid, a bowstring drawn too tight.

The Guest with the silver hair had leaned forward, her drink forgotten. “He’s fighting it. How delicious.”

Corinne’s thumb found the stone in her pocket. She rubbed it. The smooth surface was warm from her skin.

Pella’s efforts became more direct, more insistent. Her mouth, her hands, leaving no room for ambiguity. Tomas’s breath hitched. A flush crept up his neck. His hands, which had been fisted at his sides, slowly uncurled. His resistance was a wall, but biology was a siege engine. She watched, detached, as the signs manifested. The quickening of his breath. The faint tremor in his thigh. The inevitable, betraying swell of arousal beneath the thin linen.

He made a sound then. Not a moan of pleasure. A gritted-teeth exhale, ragged and furious. His eyes squeezed shut. His hips gave a single, abortive jerk, a movement he clearly tried to stifle.

And something in Corinne’s clinical detachment cracked.

It wasn’t the physical reaction. She’d seen thousands. It was the raw, unfiltered conflict on his face—the shame warring with involuntary pleasure, the fury at his own body’s betrayal. It was human. Messy. It was not the smooth, empty pond of Linden’s acceptance. This was a storm contained in skin. A hot, sudden twist low in her own gut mirrored his tension. It was an echo, unwanted, a sympathetic vibration in an instrument she’d thought long muted.

Pella sensed the tipping point. She shifted, her rhythm becoming relentless, a focused assault on the last of his control. Gren held his shoulders firm. Tomas’s back arched, a bow pulled to breaking. A tendon stood out in his neck like cable. He turned his head, and his eyes, blown dark with a chaos that wasn’t just arousal, locked onto Corinne’s.

He didn’t look away. He held her gaze as his body was forced to its climax, as the wave he’d fought so hard broke over him. It was silent. His mouth opened on a soundless gasp, his body seizing not with ecstasy but with a kind of violent surrender. He watched her watch him fall apart.

For a three-count of his pounding heart, the rest of the room ceased to exist. The orgy was a painted backdrop. The only real things were the heat in her belly, the stone grinding into her palm, and the naked, devastating acknowledgment in his eyes. He saw her. Not the Matron. Not the system. Her. And in that seeing, he imprinted his defeat—and his furious, enduring self—onto her.

Then it was over. His body went slack, spent. Pella moved away, wiping her mouth with the back of her hand, a job well done. Gren released him.

Tomas rolled onto his side, turning his face away from her, from everyone, his breathing harsh and uneven.

The silver-haired Guest applauded, a slow, soft clap. “Magnificent. The struggle makes the finish so much sweeter, don’t you find, Matron?”

Corinne’s mouth was dry. The taste of her mineral water was a ghost on her tongue. She forced her fingers to unclench from the stone. She gave the Guest a shallow, correct nod. “The yield will be exceptional.”

She turned and walked back to the bar. Her legs felt steady. Her face was calm. But beneath her ribs, the alert had settled into a dull, persistent ache. A flaw had been introduced into her perfect, sterile system. It had a name. It had silt-colored eyes. And it had, in its brutal, silent climax, forged a connection that felt less like a ledger entry and more like a fresh, unhealed wound.
