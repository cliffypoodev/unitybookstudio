The windmill blades sliced through the humid air with a sound like a guillotine made of plastic flamingos. Mary had her shotgun out now, scanning the reactivated mini-golf course.

A scream cut her off. Female. Angry. Coming from the direction of the eighteenth hole.

“Lola,” Rex said. His tail twitched twice, his tell. “She wasn't supposed to be here until noon.”

Jesus pinched the bridge of his nose. The headache that had been building since the blood drive came roaring back. “Of course she's here early. Of course she's screaming. Why would today be any different?”

They found her at 'Noah's Water Hazard,' wrestling with a seven-foot-tall fiberglass gorilla. The gorilla was winning. Lola had one arm hooked around its neck and was trying to pry open a panel on its back with a screwdriver. Her other hand held what looked like a car battery with Eden World's logo stenciled on the side.

“Put it back,” Jesus said.

Lola kicked the gorilla in the knee. It made a sad hydraulic hiss. “It's a prototype!”

A siren whooped. Red and blue lights strobed across the peeling mural of the Ark. Two deputies stepped out of a golf cart painted to look like a squad car. The taller one adjusted his belt. “Ma'am, step away from the religious experience.”

Mary sighed and holstered her shotgun under her jacket. Rex's reactor cycled through three colors before settling on a low, non-threatening hum. Jesus considered, very seriously, walking into the Everglades and letting the gators have him.

Lola let go of the gorilla. It immediately hugged her. The servos in its arms whined as it squeezed. “Oh come on,” Lola wheezed.

The shorter deputy pulled out his notepad. “That's vandalism of a themed attraction, theft of intellectual property, and”—he squinted at the gorilla's name tag—“assault on Brother Bananas.”

“Brother Bananas is assaulting me!”

The gorilla patted her head. A recorded voice boomed, “BLESSINGS ARE NEVER FORCED, BUT ALWAYS AVAILABLE WITH THE PURCHASE OF A SEASON PASS.”

Jesus stepped forward. “Officers, there's been a misunderstanding—”

The tall deputy held up a hand. “Sir are you this young lady's spiritual advisor?”

“I'm her… bail bondsman.” The lie tasted like week-old communion wafers.

“Uh-huh.” The deputy eyed Lola's outfit, black jeans, black tank top, and a jacket that said 'THIS MACHINE KILLS FASCISTS' in peeling letters. “She doesn't look like the usual rapture-ready crowd.”

Lola finally pried herself free of Brother Bananas. “You wanna see my rapture face?” She grabbed the Eden battery and shook it at them. “This is part of a mind-control network!”

The short deputy sighed. “Ma'am are you currently under the influence of any substances?”

“Just the crushing weight of late-stage capitalism!”

“That's a yes.” The deputy pulled out his cuffs. “Turn around, please.”

Lola looked at Jesus. “You're just gonna let them—”

“Turn. Around.” Jesus pinched the bridge of his nose again. The deputies didn't know about the resurrection slab. About the signal. About any of it. And explaining would involve words like 'miracle' and 'Judas-9' and probably end with all of them in matching jackets with very long sleeves.

Mary leaned in as the cuffs clicked. “We need that prototype.”

“Then bail me out fast,” Lola hissed. “The processing center's on Twelfth. They'll inventory the 'stolen goods' there.” She jerked her head at the Eden battery. “You've got maybe six hours before some clerk realizes what it really is.”

The tall deputy guided her toward the golf cart. “You have the right to remain silent.”

“Yeah, yeah, I know the drill.” Lola shot Jesus a look over her shoulder. “This is why nobody likes organized religion.”

As the cart puttered away, Rex's tail thumped the fake grass. “She stole from a theme park.”

“Technically,” Mary said, “she stole from Eden World using a theme park as the middleman.”

Jesus stared at the fading taillights. The headache had migrated behind his left eyeball. “We need cash. Fast.”

Mary patted her pockets. “I've got thirty bucks and a coupon for half-off confession.”

Rex's reactor dimmed. “I know a guy.”

Jesus groaned. “Of course you do.”

The 'guy' turned out to be a sunburned retiree named Earl who operated a pawn shop out of a converted ice cream truck parked behind a Hooters. He took one look at Rex and said, “Y'all ain't cops are ya?”

Jesus rubbed his temples. “Do we look like cops?”

Earl squinted. “You look like a guy who got hit by a bus full of Bibles.” He popped the truck's serving window. “Whatcha need?”

“Bail money,” Rex said. “Fast.”

Earl scratched his belly. “I don't do personal loans. But I do buy unique items.” His eyes lingered on the Roman nail keychain dangling from Jesus's belt. “That's real iron, ain't it?”

Jesus covered it instinctively. “Not for sale.”

“Uh-huh.” Earl leaned out the window. “How 'bout the dinosaur? He's got some interesting hardware.”

Rex's tail went rigid. “I am not collateral.”

Mary stepped forward. “We've got something better.” She pulled out the VHS tape from the blood drive, the one with the encoded obedience formula. “Rare televangelist collectible. Contains Benny Hinn's greatest hits.”

Earl took the tape. Held it up to the light. “This the one where he knocks over that old lady with the holy wind?”

Mary blinked. “You… know your televangelists.”

“Honey, I'm Florida born and raised. This stuff's our state sport.” He popped the tape into a player under the counter. The screen flickered to life—Benny in his white suit, arms outstretched. Earl fast-forwarded. “Yep, there's the wind incident. Classic.” He ejected the tape. “I'll give you two grand.”

Jesus's eyebrows shot up. “For a VHS?”

“For this particular VHS.” Earl tapped the side of his nose. “Collector's market. The wind incident's when he lost the TouchFlo patent lawsuit. Nasty business.”

Mary opened her mouth. Closed it. “We'll take it.”

As Earl counted out bills, Rex's reactor pulsed. “You're telling me we had two thousand dollars this whole time, and we've been sleeping in a moldy motel?”

“Priorities,” Mary muttered.

Jesus pocketed the cash. The keychain felt heavier suddenly. He wondered, not for the first time, what exactly they were bargaining with, and who was really setting the price.

The clerk barely looked up from her crossword. “Name?”

“Lola Garrity,” Jesus said.

The clerk flipped through her clipboard. “Ah. The heretic.” She checked a box. “Bail's set at fifteen hundred.”

Jesus slid the cash across the counter. The clerk counted it with the enthusiasm of someone who'd rather be anywhere else. As she stamped the forms, she said, “You know she tried to bite Deputy Wilkins.”

“That tracks,” Mary said.

The clerk shrugged. “Honestly? We get worse from the Holy Land Experience crowd.” She handed Jesus a receipt and a plastic bag containing Lola's personal effects. “She's being processed out back.”

A crash echoed from the holding area. A familiar voice yelled, “I said I don't want your damn pamphlet!”

The clerk sighed. “Or you can go supervise.”

They found Lola in a cinderblock room, arguing with a volunteer from the Jail Ministry. The volunteer—a grandmotherly type in a 'Jesus Loves You' sweatshirt—was holding out a tract with a picture of a flaming golf course on the cover.

“I don't need salvation,” Lola snapped. “I need a lawyer who doesn't think 'objection' is something you say at a wedding!”

Jesus cleared his throat. Lola spun. Her eyes lit up when she saw the bag of her belongings. “Took you long enough.”

“You're welcome,” Jesus said.

The volunteer tutted. “Young lady, sarcasm is a poor substitute for—”

Lola snatched the tract. “Fine.” She folded it into a paper airplane and launched it at the wastebasket. It banked left at the last second and hit the volunteer's shoe. “Oops. Guess I missed the rapture.”

The volunteer's smile didn't waver. “We'll pray for you.”

“Don't bother,” Lola said, grabbing her stuff. “I'm allergic to incense.”

As they walked out, the deputy at the desk handed Jesus a clipboard. “Sign here for the confiscated property.” He tapped a box containing the Eden battery. “We're holding it as evidence.”

Jesus's fingers tightened on the pen. “How long?”

“Depends on the DA. Could be weeks.” The deputy leaned closer. “Between you and me? That thing's got Eden World's logo on it. Their lawyers move fast. Probably get it back by Tuesday.”

Jesus froze. “Their lawyers?”

“Yeah. They got a whole department for intellectual property.” The deputy chuckled. “Last month, some kid stole a prototype from Holy Land. Eden had a cease-and-desist on him before he finished his mug shot.”

Mary's grip on Jesus's arm was the only thing that kept him from breaking the pen. “Thanks for the heads-up.”

The deputy winked. “No problem. Y'all have a blessed day.”

Outside, the Florida sun hit like a judgment. Lola stretched her arms. “So. We breaking into evidence lockup tonight or what?”

Rex's tail twitched. “We need to move faster than that.”

“They're not just tracking it,” Jesus said quietly. He looked at the processing center, then at the battery in the evidence box. “They're expecting it.” The headache was back, worse than ever. Because the deputy was right—Eden's lawyers would move fast. But not to get the battery back. To make sure it ended up exactly where it was supposed to be.

In the system. Waiting for them.

The bail bondsman’s office had the aesthetic charm of a DMV designed by someone who’d only heard of DMVs secondhand. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, flickering in time with Jesus’s worsening headache. A plastic ficus in the corner had given up on life sometime during the Reagan administration.

The man behind the counter, name tag reading “Chaz,” though Jesus would have bet money that wasn’t his real name, smiled like a used car salesman who’d just spotted a sucker with bad credit. “You here for the Garrity girl?”

Jesus nodded. “What’s the damage?”

Chaz tapped at a keyboard with the enthusiasm of a sloth on sedatives. The monitor flickered, displaying Lola’s booking photo, glaring, mid-snarl, with what looked like a smear of gorilla greasepaint on her cheek. “Ah. The heretic.”

Mary leaned against the counter. “That’s the second time someone’s called her that today.”

“First time was free,” Chaz said. “This time, it’s gonna cost you.” He spun the monitor around. The bail amount blinked red: $150,000.

Jesus stared. “That’s a typo.”

“Wish it was, pal.” Chaz sucked his teeth. “Judge bumped it up special this morning. ‘Flight risk,’ ‘violent tendencies,’ blah. Also, apparently she threatened to ‘expose the divine-industrial complex’ during processing, which, in Misty Palms County is like yelling ‘bomb’ in an airport.”

Rex’s tail thumped against the linoleum. “She was arrested for stealing a car battery.”

“Correction,” Chaz said, holding up a finger. “She was arrested for stealing a sacred car battery. Big difference in these parts.” He leaned forward, lowering his voice. “Between you and me? Someone really doesn’t want her walking out of here.”

Jesus’s fingers twitched toward his keychain. “Who?”

“Anonymous benefactor.” Chaz shrugged. “Filed a motion to increase bail ‘in the interest of public safety.’ Judge signed off on it before breakfast.”

Mary crossed her arms. “That’s not how bail works.”

“Sweetheart,” Chaz said, “this is Florida. Bail works however the guy with the biggest lawyer says it works.” He tapped the screen again. A logo popped up—Eden World Legal Services, gleaming in corporate gold. “And these guys? They’ve got armies of lawyers.”

Jesus exhaled through his nose. The headache had officially upgraded itself to a migraine. “So what are our options?”

Chaz grinned. “Well, for a modest ten percent fee, I can—”

“We don’t have fifteen grand,” Jesus said flatly.

“Ah.” Chaz’s grin didn’t waver. “Then I’d recommend prayer.”

Rex’s reactor cycled through three shades of irritated purple. “We could break her out.”

Mary pinched the bridge of her nose. “No.”

“It’s a jail, Rex,” Jesus said. “Not a theme park.”

“Same security budget, though,” Rex muttered.

Chaz cleared his throat. “Look, if you’re really strapped, there’s always the Work Release Miracle Program.” He slid a pamphlet across the counter. The glossy cover featured a beaming elderly woman holding a check next to a man in a white suit. Eden World Legal Services: For All Your Post-Miracle Needs.

Jesus didn’t touch it. “What’s the catch?”

“No catch!” Chaz said, too quickly. “Just sign a temporary service contract with Eden, perform a few minor miracles—water into wine, healing the occasional leper, standard stuff—and they’ll cover the bail. Think of it as… divine Uber.”

Mary picked up the pamphlet like it might bite her. “This is indentured servitude with better branding.”

“Hey, I don’t make the rules,” Chaz said. “I just profit from them.”

Jesus stared at the pamphlet. The fine print at the bottom read All miracles subject to quality assurance review. Failure to meet performance metrics may result in additional penalties.

Rex’s tail flicked. “We could always let her sit in jail for a few days.”

“Rex,” Mary and Jesus said in unison.

“What? It’s not like she’s dying in there.”

Jesus rubbed his temples. “She’ll be dead to us if we leave her.”

Chaz clapped his hands. “So! Miracle gig or cash?”

Jesus looked at Mary. Mary looked at Rex. Rex looked at the ficus like he was considering eating it out of spite.

Then the door burst open.

A woman in a pantsuit strode in, heels clicking like gunshots. Her hair was pulled into a bun so tight it looked painful. She dropped a business card on the counter. “I’m representing Ms. Garrity.”

Jesus blinked. “Who are—”

“Sister Mercy,” the woman said. “Pro bono division of Eden World Legal.” She smiled, all teeth. “And we’re waiving bail.”

Chaz’s eyebrows shot up. “Since when does Eden do pro bono?”

“Since approximately,” Sister Mercy said, checking her watch, “thirty seconds ago.” She turned to Jesus. “You’ll want to come with me.”

Mary’s hand drifted toward her hidden shotgun. “Why?”

Sister Mercy’s smile didn’t reach her eyes. “Because the Serpent Engine just activated. And it’s asking for you.”

The migraine spiked. Jesus knew then that getting Lola out was just the beginning of a much larger, more expensive headache.
