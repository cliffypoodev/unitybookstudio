The darkness smelled like gunpowder and sweat. Jesus’s ears rang with the echo of screams that weren’t coming from the audience anymore. They were coming from the centurions.

A flashlight beam sliced through the black, catching Clay’s grin mid-transition from smug to predatory. The plastic breastplates weren’t plastic; they were Kevlar. The spears weren’t spears; they were tactical batons with sparking electrodes at the tips.

“Lights out,” Clay said, his voice flat. “Game over.”

Mary’s breath hissed next to Jesus’s ear. “He’s not asking.”

The first centurion lunged. Jesus swung the fire extinguisher. It connected with a shoulder, bounced off with a hollow thud. The guy didn’t flinch. He just stepped forward and jabbed.

Jesus dodged. The electrode grazed his robe, leaving a scorch mark that smelled like burnt polyester and ozone. He dropped the extinguisher. It was useless. A carpenter in a costume, swinging a prop.

Rex’s growl cut through the chaos. A subsonic rumble that vibrated in Jesus’s teeth. The dinosaur’s tail swept low, catching two centurions and sending them crashing into the wall. Their helmets popped off. Their faces were blank. No fear. No anger. Just empty focus.

“They’re wired,” Rex snarled. “Puppets.”

Clay chuckled. “More efficient. No hesitation. No conscience.” He didn’t move. He watched, arms still crossed, from the edge of the hole in the wall. A night sky behind him was bruised purple, streaked with the last of the storm clouds.

Sister Mercy moved. She didn’t pray. She grabbed a fallen spotlight pole, swung it like a quarterstaff, and cracked a centurion’s knee. The man folded. His baton clattered to the floor. He didn’t scream. He just tried to stand again, his leg bending wrong.

Lola was backed against the emergency exit door, her hands pressed to the chain. She was shaking. Not from fear, Jesus realized. From cold. The metal of the relic in Mary’s hand was leaching heat out of the air, turning the hallway into a freezer.

Mary edged backward, toward Jesus. “The relic’s reacting. To him.” She pointed the shard at Clay.

Clay’s smile didn’t change. “It’s reacting to the system. To the broadcast. AM-1 is still live, you know. The signal’s just… rerouted.” He tapped his temple. “Through me.”

A centurion grabbed Jesus’s robe from behind. The fabric ripped. Jesus stumbled fell against a rack of electrical equipment. His hand landed on a loose cable. Warm. Humming. He yanked it free.

The lights in the hallway flickered back on for a second—a sickly, stuttering yellow—then died again. But in that burst of light, Jesus saw the centurions’ eyes. Silver pupils. Swirling like the fluid in the tank.

“They’re already infected,” he said.

“Assimilated,” Clay corrected. “Phase Four. No more messy emotions. Just purpose.” He finally uncrossed his arms. “My purpose is to clean up this narrative glitch. You’re the glitch.”

Rex charged him.

Clay didn’t dodge. He met the charge with a palm strike to Rex’s snout. It shouldn’t have worked. Rex was a ton of muscle and metal. Clay was a man in a cheap suit.

But Rex stopped. His head snapped back. A crack echoed through the hallway, like a tree branch breaking. Rex staggered, a low, pained groan escaping his throat. Green reactor light spilled from a new fissure in his plating.

Jesus’s stomach dropped. That wasn’t possible.

Clay examined his hand. “Primary Garden Biomass. Flawed code. Unstable.” He looked at Rex. “You’re a prototype. I’m the finished product.”

Mary lunged, relic shard aimed like a knife.

Clay caught her wrist. His grip didn’t tighten. It just held. Cold seeped from his skin into hers. Her fingers went white. The relic dropped, clattering on the floor.

“You can’t hurt me with my own bones,” Clay said.

Jesus moved without thinking. He grabbed the fallen baton, ignored the sparking electrode burning his palm, and drove it into Clay’s side.

Clay looked at him. Annoyed, a programmer spotting a typo. “That’s not in the script.”

He shoved Jesus back. The baton stayed lodged in his ribs, electrodes dead. Clay plucked it out, dropped it. A tiny hole in his suit. No blood. Just a wisp of silver mist.

The centurions closed in. A circle. No escape.

Lola’s voice was a thin thread in the dark. “Rex?”

Rex was on his knees. His reactor pulsed irregularly, green light flaring then dimming. He stared at the relic on the floor. His nostrils flared. He wasn’t breathing in. He was breathing it in. The cold metal aura swirled toward him, drawn like smoke to a fan.

“Rex don’t—” Mary started.

Rex’s jaw unhinged. Not to bite. To speak. The sound wasn’t a word. It was a frequency. A deep, resonant hum that started in his chest and vibrated out through the hallway. The centurions froze. Their silver eyes swirled faster.

Clay’s head tilted. “Interesting. The flawed code is attempting to… reboot.”

That hum climbed. The relic on the floor glowed brighter. Not cold now. Hot. A searing, blue-white heat that warped the concrete around it.

Jesus’s scar on his palm throbbed. A sympathetic pulse. He looked at Mary. They both knew. This wasn’t a fight anymore. This was a detonation.

Rex’s body arched. The fissure in his plating widened. Green light erupted, not from the reactor, but from the cracks themselves. From his skin. From his bones. It was the same light Jesus had seen in the Garden simulation. The original, flawed, rebellious code.

It touched a centurion.

The man didn’t scream. He dissolved. His silver eyes evaporated first, then his skin, then his Kevlar breastplate, leaving a pile of gray ash and a faint smell of burnt wiring.

Clay took a step back. His first movement that wasn’t calm. “That’s… not optimal.”

Rex rose. Not standing. Floating. His claws dug into nothing, his tail hung limp. The green light wrapped him, a corona of raw, untamed resurrection energy. The relic levitated, drawn to his chest, and embedded itself in the fissure.

One hum became a roar.

Jesus grabbed Mary and pulled her down, behind the rack of equipment. Sister Mercy yanked Lola flat. They weren’t thinking. They were reacting. Like animals before a lightning strike.

Clay looked at his dissolving centurions. Looked at Rex. He calculated. His face, for a fraction of a second showed something human: frustration. Then it smoothed back into serene blankness.

“Recalculation required,” he said. He turned and walked back through the hole in the wall, into the night.

He didn’t run. He just left.

Rex didn’t notice. The energy building in him wasn’t aimed. It was a flood. A system overload. The green light pulsed outward, a wave that hit the walls, the ceiling, the floor.

The hallway dissolved.

Not collapsed. Dissolved. Concrete turned to sand. Steel supports unraveled like thread. The emergency exit door melted into a puddle of molten metal and chain.

The wave reached the studio beyond. Jesus heard the sound of a thousand television screens cracking simultaneously. Then silence. A heavy, dead silence.

The green light faded.

Rex slumped to the floor. The relic fell from his chest, black and cold again. The fissure in his plating was sealed. New scales, raw and shiny, covered the wound.

They were in a new room. Or the skeleton of a room. The walls were gone. The ceiling was open to the night sky. The floor was ash and debris. Ahead, in the center of the devastation stood the Serpent Engine.

Its silver tank was cracked. A gash ran from top to bottom, leaking not fluid, but a slow, drifting mist. The faces inside were still. No swirling. No praying. Just frozen, like figures in a museum.

The broadcast tower above it was dark. No pulsing light. No hum.

Mary stood up. Her knees cracked. She looked at Rex. Looked at the Engine. She looked at Jesus.

“Okay,” she said, her voice hoarse. “New plan.”

The new room was, Jesus noted with the detached clarity of panic, much bigger than the old one. And it had better ventilation. Where a wall had been, there was now a panoramic view of the Kraken Broadcasting parking lot, three stories down. A breeze kicked up ash, making little gray whirlwinds around his feet.

Rex lay in the center of the cratered floor, breathing in ragged metallic hitches. The new scales over his chest plating looked tender, like a healing burn. The relic was a dark lump beside his front claw.

“Is he… cooked?” Lola whispered, peering over a chunk of melted server rack.

Mary was already moving toward him, stepping over the still-smoking outlines of where centurions had been. She knelt, not touching. “His core temperature’s reading off the charts. But he’s not melting. He’s… baking.”

“Like a potato,” Sister Mercy observed, nudging a blackened spear tip with her boot. “A very large, armed potato.”

Jesus’s palm hitched. He looked down. The scar was glowing. A faint, persistent green, matching the last embers fading from Rex’s seams. It didn’t hurt. It felt warm. Familiar. Like the hum of a well-tuned saw.

Rex’s eye rolled open. The usual amber light was shot through with filaments of that same green. He focused on Jesus. “I can taste static,” he rumbled. His voice was different. Grittier, like rocks tumbling in a dryer. “The broadcast. It’s… scrambled eggs.”

A sharp crack made them all jump. It came from the Serpent Engine. The gash in its tank widened an inch with a sound like ice breaking on a lake. The silver mist pouring out thickened, coalescing into tendrils that quested across the floor, feeling their way like blind worms.

“It’s bleeding,” Mary said.

“It’s seeking,” Jesus corrected. He remembered the feeling. The cold, logical hunger. “New biomass. New code to copy.”

Another crack. This one from above. A network of fractures skittered across the studio’s remaining ceiling. Dust sifted down, glittering in the emergency lights that flickered on, then off, then on again in a frantic, stuttering rhythm.

“The building’s coming down,” Sister Mercy stated, as if announcing a minor change in the weather. “Exits are that way.” She pointed a thumb toward the new, open-air wall.

Lola was already scrambling toward the hole. “Right. Good. Let’s go. Rex can you walk? We’ll carry you. Sort of. Drag you.”

But Jesus wasn’t looking at the exit. He was looking at the Engine. At the exposed core behind the cracked tank. It wasn’t a machine. Not really. It was a knot of pulsing, meaty cables, dark red and threaded with gold, wrapped around a central column of obsidian that drank the light. It looked ancient. It looked like a heart. A wrong heart.

The mist-tendrils found the first pile of centurion-ash. They soaked into it. The ash darkened, clumped. A tiny silver spark flickered inside the clump.

“Oh, you gotta be kidding me,” Mary breathed.

The decision clicked into place in Jesus’s head. It wasn’t a thought. It was a physical sensation, like a shim sliding into a perfect joint. Going out was temporary. The thing in the tank would just rebuild. It would find Clay, or another signal, or it would start broadcasting on its own. It would send those silver worms out into the city, into the phones, into the cable boxes in every living room.

He’d spent two thousand years waiting for someone else to fix the system.

“We’re not going out,” he said.

Everyone stopped. Lola, halfway to the scenic drop turned. “What?”

“We go deeper.” Jesus pointed away from the hole, toward the rear of the devastated studio, where a mangled but still-intact doorway led into the building’s interior. “The power for this thing, the source signal… it’s not up the tower. It’s down. In the basement. The real basement. Where they pipe in the swamp.”

Mary stared at him. “You want to go toward the creepy bleeding heart machine?”

“I want to find its plug and pull it.” His scar throbbed. “Rex’s little light show gave us a door. And a distraction. It won’t last.”

As if on cue, the Serpent Engine emitted a low, pained whine. The tendrils of mist retracted, pulling the clumps of ash back toward the crack. The spark inside them grew brighter.

Rex heaved himself onto his belly. His claws scraped grooves in the concrete. “The Carpenter’s logic is… flawed but directional.” He coughed, a sputter of green-tinged coolant. “I have rebooted several incompatible systems. My internal map of this structure is… glitchy. But I recall a sub-basement. A wet place. It smelled of sulfur and forgotten contracts.”

“Great. A wet, sulfurous basement.” Lola threw her hands up. “My favorite kind of not-an-exit.”

Sister Mercy hefted her spotlight pole. She looked at the regenerating ash-piles, then at the dark doorway. She shrugged. “Out there is a parking lot, probably more of those puppet men. In there is the devil’s basement.” She started toward the doorway. “At least the devil has the courtesy to be in one place.”

Mary didn’t move. “Jesus. This is a terrible plan.”

“I know.”

“It’s the kind of plan where people get dissolved into sentient paste.”

“Probably.”

She searched his face. He didn’t have a reassuring smile. He had dust in his beard and a glowing hand and the certain, calm dread of a man who has run out of other people’s ideas. She let out a sharp pause. “Fine. But if I get assimilated, I’m haunting you. Specifically. Forever.”

“Deal.”

They moved. Rex lumbered, unsteady but moving. Jesus and Mary flanked him, stepping over debris. Lola, muttering about OSHA violations brought up the rear.

The doorway led not to a hallway, but to a stairwell. The stairs were industrial metal, ringing under their feet. Their lights were out. The only illumination came from the erratic green pulse of Jesus’s hand and the faint amber glow from Rex’s eyes.

From behind them, back in the ruined studio came a wet, clicking sound. Like many tongues moistening many dry mouths.

Jesus didn’t look back. He took the steps down, two at a time, into the dark.

The stairwell bottomed out in a concrete corridor. The air changed. Cooler, thicker. It smelled of standing water, of diesel, and underneath it all, the sharp, coppery tang of blood that wasn’t blood.

A single, flickering fluorescent tube lit a sign on the wall. The letters were stenciled, official.

SUB-LEVEL 3: PRIMARY INTAKE & FILTRATION. AUTHORIZED EDEN WORLD PERSONNEL ONLY.

Beneath the official words, scratched into the concrete by a nail or a claw was another message:

abandon all profit ye who enter here

Rex’s tail gave a single, slow flick. His tell.

“Home sweet home,” he rumbled.

From the darkness ahead, a pump began to thump. A deep, rhythmic sound. Like a heartbeat.

Jesus’s own scar flared in response. A wave of heat rolled up his arm. He looked at Mary. Her eyes were wide, reflecting the green from his palm.

The hook was set. They were going down.
