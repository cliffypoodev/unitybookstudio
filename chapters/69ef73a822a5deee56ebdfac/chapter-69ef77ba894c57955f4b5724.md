The air in the swamp tasted like rotten eggs and burnt wiring. Mary crouched in the bow of a stolen airboat, the aluminum hull vibrating under her knees. She held a jury-rigged signal sniffer—a converted metal detector with a crucifix antenna—that whined like a dying mosquito. It pointed toward a shanty boat listing in the black water fifty yards ahead. The shanty was a Frankenstein of corrugated tin, driftwood, and stolen satellite dishes. A single yellow bulb glowed in its one window, and a homemade broadcast tower made from old TV antennae and fishing poles wobbled from its roof.

“That’s it,” she whispered. “The static’s coming from that floating dumpster fire.”

Jesus leaned against the airboat’s fan cage, picking at a splinter in the rail. He wore a distressed denim jacket over a T-shirt that read ‘I Got Nailed in Miami.’ “You sure it’s not just a guy who really likes AM radio? Could be a Barry Manilow fan.”

“The signal’s riding a UHF carrier wave. It’s piggybacking on the Kraken frequency, but it’s… raw. Unfiltered.” She adjusted a dial. The sniffer screeched. “It’s the same spectral signature as the Elvis broadcast. And the St. Bartholomew’s resonator.”

“Great. So we’re about to interrupt a pirate broadcast of ‘Copacabana.’ My favorite.”

A low groan sounded from the water beside them. Rex’s head surfaced, brackish water sluicing off his metal-plated snout. He’d insisted on swimming ahead as a scout. “The vessel is structurally unsound. The starboard pontoon is a repurposed septic tank. I can smell the failure.”

“Can you smell the transmitter?” Mary asked.

“I smell despair and mold. Also literal sewage.” He blinked, his ocular lens clicking as it zoomed. “There is one life sign inside. Humanoid. Elevated heart rate. He is speaking.”

They listened. A tinny, fervent voice drifted across the water, amplified by a cheap PA system.

“—and the static, brothers and sisters is the voice of the un-made! It is the sigh of the clay before the Potter’s hand! Can you hear it?”

Mary’s jaw tightened. Her coping mechanism was a specific, almost imperceptible tap of her right thumb against her palm—a ghost keystroke from a keyboard that wasn’t there. Tap-tap-tap. “He’s not just hearing it. He’s preaching it.”

“This is a terrible sermon,” Jesus observed. “No parables. No lightning bolts. Two out of ten.”

“We need to get on that boat and fry his transmitter.”

“Or,” Jesus said, gesturing to Rex, “we let the dinosaur sneeze on it. One good electromagnetic pulse from his reactor and that shack becomes a toaster.”

Rex’s tail, floating just above the water like a periscope gave a dismissive flick. “My bio-reactor is at 31%. A directed pulse would drain the capacitor and require me to nap for twelve hours. I am not napping in this effluent.”

“Fine. Quiet approach.” Mary stowed the sniffer and pulled a multi-tool from her belt. “You two, create a distraction. Something low-key.”

Jesus nodded. “Low-key. Got it.” He turned to Rex. “Hey. Think you can tap that septic tank pontoon? Gently?”

Rex sank below the surface. A moment later, a series of dull metallic thumps echoed through the hull of the shanty boat. The preacher’s voice stuttered.

“—the sigh of the clay—what in the name of—”

The boat rocked.

Mary was already moving. She slid over the side of the airboat into the tepid water, wincing as it soaked her jeans. She paddled the short distance, grabbed a slimy rope ladder hanging from the shanty’s deck, and hauled herself up. The whole structure tilted under her weight. Up close, the place was held together with baling wire and prayer. The broadcast antenna was guyed with rosary beads.

She could hear the preacher inside, muttering now. “—probably just a gator. A big one.”

Mary edged to the window. The interior was a single room, a nest of electronic equipment surrounding a folding chair. One man in the chair was skeletally thin, wearing a stained white suit. He faced a microphone, his back to her, a pair of oversized headphones clamped over his ears. Banks of old radios and reel-to-reel decks blinked around him, all feeding into a central console where a single, ugly black box pulsed with a familiar cold blue light. An Eden relic. It was smaller than the one from the cruise ship, a different model, but the same make. Serpent static hissed from every speaker.

Her tell fired. A tiny, involuntary twitch at the corner of her left eye. They were mass-producing these things.

The preacher leaned into his mic, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. “The chosen frequency is 88.7 megahertz. The dial of the divine. There, the static forms words. It tells us of the coming clay. The living clay. It says… it says a name. Judas. Judas-9. The final proof is not ink, friends. It’s a signal.”

Mary’s blood went cold. Not a metaphor. A designation.

The boat lurched violently. A loud, wet crunch sounded from below, followed by a bubbling groan. Rex’s head appeared outside the window next to her, his snout dripping muck. “The septic tank pontoon is no longer a pontoon. It is more of a suggestion.”

The preacher yelped, spinning in his chair. His eyes, wide and bloodshot, locked on Mary’s face in the window. Then they darted to the dinosaur head filling the other one. He didn’t scream. He just sagged. “Oh. Auditors. Already?”

“We’re not auditors,” Mary said, pushing the rickety door open.

“You’re with the lizard. That’s an audit team. The gator’s probably around here somewhere.” He sighed, a defeated sound that crackled through his live mic. “Fine. Just… let me sign off. The listeners need closure.”

He turned back to his microphone as the shanty boat listed another ten degrees to starboard. Equipment slid across the table. “My faithful friends. It seems our broadcast is experiencing… technical difficulties. But remember! The static is eternal. The message is in the medium. Until we meet again on the dial, keep… keep listening in the dark.”

He flipped a switch. The blue pulse from the Eden relic died. The serpent static cut off, leaving only the buzz of the yellow bulb and the lapping of water.

The preacher took off his headphones and set them down neatly. He looked at Mary, then at Jesus, who was now climbing in through the door, and finally at Rex, who was trying to fit a shoulder through the window. “You’re not very good auditors. Where are your clipboards?”

“We’re not from Eden World,” Jesus said, picking up a discarded circuit board. “We’re more of an independent review board.”

“What were you broadcasting?” Mary asked, her eyes on the dead relic.

“The truth. The raw feed. Kraken filters it, processes it, turns it into that smooth, easy-listening spiritual slop. I give it to them pure.” He smiled, but it didn’t reach his eyes. They were flat. Glassy. “The static tells stories. Lately, it’s been telling one story. About Project Clay. And the nine betrayals.”

Mary’s thumb tapped against her palm. Tap-tap-tap. “Who gave you the transmitter?”

“A friend. A technician with a crisis of conscience. He said the people had a right to hear the unedited word.” The preacher’s head tilted. His smile remained fixed. “He was terminated. I upgraded his equipment.”

The boat groaned. Water sloshed over the deck outside.

“We need to go,” Jesus said. “This thing is going down.”

“I’m not leaving my post.” The preacher folded his hands in his lap. “The broadcast is my life.”

Mary stepped closer. The man’s pupils were too wide. His breathing was too even. She reached out and touched the side of his neck. No pulse. Cold.

“He’s not alive,” she said.

The preacher’s fixed smile twitched. His voice flattened, losing its preacherly cadence, becoming a monotone recording. “Primary directive: broadcast signal. Signal source: Judas-9 archive. Mission parameters: test resonance in uncontrolled environment. Test complete. Containment breach detected. Initiating shutdown.”

His body went rigid. Then his head lolled forward onto the console.

A final, rasping sentence hissed from his lips, the words skipping like a damaged record. “The… … is… a… frequency… Find… the… ninth… wave…”

He powered down.

The only sound was the water rising in the hull.

“Well,” Jesus said, looking at the dummy slumped over its microphone. “That’s a new one.”

Rex finally pulled his head back from the window. “The vessel is sinking. And I believe I see actual auditors. Two hundred yards and closing. They are, indeed, alligators. With clipboards.”

Mary grabbed the black box Eden relic. It was inert, cold. She shoved it into her bag. “We go. Now.”

They abandoned the shanty boat just as its roof antennae touched the black water. The preacher-dummy sat silently at his console, a slack-jawed captain going down with his ship. From the swamp, the steady, purposeful glide of two large shapes moved toward the wreckage.

Mary hauled herself back into the airboat, soaked and smelling of failure and swamp. Jesus gunned the engine. The giant fan roared, pushing them away into the dark.

She looked back. The yellow bulb flickered once, then went out, swallowed by the hungry water and the advancing, clipboard-toting dark.

The shanty boat groaned like a stepped-on coffin. Jesus braced himself against a tilting wall of soggy particle board. The preacher-dummy sat motionless, a discarded marionette with a smile stitched onto its face by someone who’d never seen a real one.

“We’re taking it with us,” Mary said. She was elbow-deep in the console, pulling wires. Her hair stuck to her forehead with sweat and swamp film.

“Why?” Jesus asked. “It’s a toaster that talks heresy. Let the gator-auditors have it.”

“It’s evidence. It mentioned Judas-9 by name. That’s not in any public log.” She yanked a cluster of cables free. A spark fizzled. “This thing was a relay. A repeater. Which means there’s a source. Help me get it loose.”

Rex’s head reappeared at the window, blocking the sickly dawn light. “The auditors are circling. They are… taking notes. This is profoundly undignified.” His tail, visible over the submerged deck gave a slow, warning sweep. A tell. He was anxious.

Jesus grabbed the dummy under its arms. It was lighter than it should have been, a husk of plastic and cheap metal under the stained suit. Its head lolled back, glassy eyes reflecting the dying yellow bulb. A faint odor of ozone and decay leaked from its neck seam. He dragged it toward the door, its shoes scraping twin trails in the silt and mold.

Water poured over the threshold. The deck outside was a shallow, murky pond.

“The airboat is ten feet away,” Rex reported. “The water contains six species of parasite that would enjoy your particular biology.”

“Fantastic.” Jesus heaved the dummy over his shoulder in a fireman’s carry. The movement was awkward. His palms, where the phantom cross-prints ached in damp weather, burned with a fresh, sharp pain. He ignored it. Mercy for a machine. What a joke.

Mary cursed behind him. “The core memory is sealed. Eden tech. It’s a black box, literally.” She slammed her multi-tool against the console. The whole boat shuddered.

“Can you crack it?”

“Not here. I need a clean room, a spectrometer, and a prayer.” She pried at the black box’s faceplate. “But there’s a data port. Standardized. Which is weird. Like they want someone to plug in.”

“Or like it’s a trap,” Jesus said, wading into the knee-deep water off the deck. The dummy’s cold plastic ear dug into his neck.

“Everything’s a trap. That question is who set it.” Mary gave a final, violent twist. A small, crystalline chip popped into her hand. She stared at it. “Got it.”

A low, resonant bellow rolled across the water. Not a gator. Deeper. More deliberate.

The two circling alligators, each the size of a canoe, paused in their patrol. They turned their armored heads toward the sound, then looked back at the sinking boat. One of them lifted a clipboard in its mouth, a waterproof tablet stylus clutched in its claws. It made a precise checkmark.

“They’re grading our salvage operation,” Jesus said. “We’re probably violating some swamp-code ordinance.”

“Just get to the boat,” Mary snapped, wading past him, the chip held high like a communion wafer.

Jesus slogged after her. The water was thick, warm, alive with unseen things brushing against his legs. Rex swam alongside, a dark, hulking shadow, his reactor emitting a low, worried hum.

They reached the airboat. Mary clambered in and reached back for the dummy. Jesus shoved the limp form up and over the gunwale. It landed on the deck with a hollow thud. He hauled himself up after it, dripping foul water onto the aluminum.

The engine coughed to life under Mary’s hand. The giant fan began to spin.

From the deep channel beside the now-half-submerged shanty, the water bulged. A wake spread, smooth and V-shaped, aimed directly at them.

The alligator that surfaced made the auditors look like geckos. Its head was as broad as a church pew, its hide a landscape of moss-crusted scales and ancient scars. One milky eye was blind. The other was a sharp, intelligent yellow, and it fixed on Jesus with the focus of a bank manager reviewing a suspicious withdrawal.

It didn’t roar. It didn’t charge. It simply surfaced and floated, its massive tail moving just enough to keep it stationary. In the crook of one foreclaw, it held not a clipboard, but a sleek, waterproof tablet. Its other claw tapped the screen.

Mary froze, her hand on the throttle. “That’s not a field auditor.”

“That’s management,” Jesus whispered.

And it was saying his name.
