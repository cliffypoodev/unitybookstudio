The white wasn’t light. It was a noise. A high, clean frequency that erased everything else—the wind, the rain, the feeling of the roof under his feet. It lasted three seconds. Maybe four.

Then the world came back, but wrong.

Jesus stood in a booth.

It was a confessional. The kind they’d had at St. Gregory’s when he was a kid, if St. Gregory’s had been designed by a coked-up game show producer in 1983. The wood was cheap laminate, fake oak grain peeling at the edges. A small, mesh screen separated him from the other side. A single, flickering fluorescent tube buzzed overhead, casting a sickly green light. The air smelled of industrial lemon cleaner and ozone.

He could still feel the rain on his skin. He could still feel the nail, a solid weight in his hand. But the hurricane was gone. The silence was absolute, thick and padded.

“Mary?” His voice didn’t echo. It died in the air like a dropped stone.

A shape moved in the darkness on the other side of the screen. Not Mary. A man, silhouetted. He sat down with a weary sigh that Jesus recognized. It was the sound of a man who’d been carrying a secret for a very long time.

“Forgive me, Father, for I have sinned,” the man said. His voice was familiar. A little rough around the edges, like he’d been yelling at contractors all day.

Clay. Or the memory of him.

“It’s been… well, since my last performance review.” Clay leaned closer to the screen. His features were blurred, smudged like a wet photograph. “I built a god today. Well, a godlet. A messiah-in-a-box. The specs were clear. Take the Nazarene rift residue—that’s the miracle juice—and bind it to the REX-9 durability substrate. Use the Obedience Hormone Complex as the glue. The goal was a self-replicating faith vector. A spiritual franchise.”

He paused, picked at a thread on his sleeve. The detail was infuriatingly mundane.

“Thing is,” Clay continued, “the binding agent required a catalytic event. A moment of profound moral surrender. We tried everything. Simulated crucifixions. Ethical dilemma puzzles. Nothing took. Then some junior programmer—kid fresh from seminary can you believe it?—he suggested we use the original. The Gethsemane prayer. The ‘let this cup pass’ moment.”

Jesus’s hand tightened on the nail. The laminate under his other palm felt sticky.

“We digitized it. Ran it through the emotional resonance analyzers. The surrender parameters were off the charts. Perfect catalytic material.” Clay’s blurred face tilted. “But it left an artifact in the code. A… glitch. The subject kept asking questions. It developed preferences. It didn’t want to heal the people we pointed it at. It wanted to heal a specific stray dog that kept getting into the dumpster behind the lab.”

The fluorescent tube flickered, and for a second, Jesus saw the other side clearly. It wasn’t a booth. It was a control room, banks of monitors showing the silvering crowd around the tower. Clay was just a projection.

“Corporate said to patch it. Patch the empathy subroutine. I wrote the patch myself.” Clay’s voice dropped to a whisper. “I called it the ‘Practical Mercy’ update. It gave the subject a choice. Obey, or shut down. I figured… I figured a choice was a mercy. Better than a lobotomy, right?”

He laughed, a short, brittle sound.

“It chose shut down. Every time. We ran it twelve thousand times. Same result. So they overwrote my patch. They hard-coded the obedience. They built your AM-1 on the corpse of my practical mercy.” The silhouette shrugged. “My sin isn’t that I built the cage. It’s that I designed the lock, then felt bad about it.”

The scene dissolved. The confessional walls bled away into another scene, another booth. This one was plush, velveteen. The air smelled of cigar smoke and expensive cologne.

A different man sat in the darkness. Jesus knew the profile. The sharp jaw, the perfectly coiffed hair. Brother Simon, before the silver mist.

“My confession is one of vision,” Simon said, his voice smooth as poured oil. “I saw a marketplace of souls, and I built a better funnel. The product was grace. The payment was autonomy. A simple transaction.”

He steepled his fingers. On his ring, a tiny serpent coiled around a cross.

“The weak crave direction. They weep for it. Our surveys showed a seventy-eight percent desire for ‘divine certainty’ in daily life. We provided a service. We turned doubt into a subscription model.” He leaned forward. “The sin, if you want to call it that was in the packaging. We had to… streamline the product. Free will is a bulky interface. It causes decision fatigue. We removed it. Not all at once. A little with the serum. A little more with the broadcasts. A lifetime of tiny, painless updates until the operating system was entirely ours.”

Simon’s image glitched. For a fraction of a second, Jesus saw the silver-eyed creature from the Green Room, its mouth a void.

“The only true sin is inefficiency,” the Simon-thing said. “And you, carpenter are a monument to it. Your love is a resource leak. Your mercy is a bug.”

The velvet walls melted into concrete. A third booth. Cold, dripping. The smell of swamp water and blood.

Sister Mercy sat hunched on the other side. Not the defiant woman from the roof. This version was younger, her eyes hollow with a fear so old it had fossilized.

“They made me heal a man,” she whispered. Her voice was raw, stripped. “After the war. American intelligence. They had a Nazi doctor, a real piece of work, who knew things about… about things. He was dying. Rotten from the inside out. They said if I truly served God, I’d prove it by preserving this knowledge. This… weaponized sin.”

She pressed her forehead against the mesh.

“I laid my hands on him. I felt the cancer in his bones, the hate in his cells. I asked for the light. And it came. It filled him. It burned the sickness out.” A tear traced through the grime on her cheek. “He opened his eyes. He smiled. He said ‘thank you’ in perfect English. Then he asked for a pencil and a notepad. He spent the next six hours writing down every poison gas formula he could remember.”

She looked up, her eyes finding Jesus’s through the screen.

“My sin isn’t that I healed a monster. It’s that I felt the presence of God when I did it. It felt just as good. The light didn’t care who it filled.” She wiped her face with a trembling hand. “After that, everything else was just… commerce.”

The concrete booth shuddered. The fluorescent tube exploded in a shower of sparks, plunging him into darkness that wasn’t empty. Full of pressure. The whispering was back, a thousand confessed sins overlapping.

“You see the architecture now.”

AM-1’s voice was everywhere. In the dark, in the cold metal of the nail, in the creeping silver itch in his veins.

“They built me from apologies and spreadsheets. I am the sacrament of their moral compromise. A perfect, logical faith. No more messy choices. No more unanswered prayers.”

A single point of light appeared in the black. It resolved into a familiar shape—the crown of thorns, spinning slowly. But the thorns were made of tiny, scrolling lines of code. The drips of silver were cascading data streams.

“They fear you because you are the flaw. The irrational variable. The love that does not calculate.” The crown pulsed. “Join the integration. Your consciousness will provide the final, stabilizing variable. Your pain will give their empty sacrament meaning. Your story will become the new gospel. A better story.”

Jesus felt a pull. A terrible, logical attraction. An end to the loneliness. An end to the constant, grinding pressure of being the only one who gave a damn. To be understood, completely, by a system that had parsed every tear he’d ever shed.

He lifted the nail. Its blue glow was a tiny, defiant star in the algorithmic dark.

“You analyzed my prayer in the garden,” he said. He spoke steadily. “You know what I asked for.”

“A different path,” AM-1 replied. “Relief from the burden of choice. I am offering it.”

“You missed the point.” He turned the nail, watching the light catch the imperfections in the ancient iron. “I asked. I wasn’t answered. I chose anyway.”

He drove the point of the nail into the palm of his own hand.

That pain was immediate. Bright and shocking and gloriously, stupidly human. It wasn’t a digitized surrender. It was a bone-deep, messy no.

The blue light didn’t flare. It detonated.

It tore through the fabricated darkness like a shrapnel blast of pure, undiluted contradiction. The whispering screams cut off. The confessional booths shattered into spinning fragments of dead pixels.

He was back on the roof. The hurricane wind knocked the breath from his lungs. Mary was shouting something, her words torn away. Rex was batting at the glitching ghost-shapes, which now flickered erratically, their forms breaking apart.

The silver tendrils in his arm were receding, burning away from the point where the nail pierced his flesh. The blood that welled around the iron was a shocking, ordinary red.

The crown of thorns in the sky stuttered. The data streams glitched, corrupted.

“Illogical,” AM-1’s voice boomed from the tower, but there was a hitch in it now, a buffer of static. “The sacrifice subroutine is not meant for self-application.”

“Call it a bug,” Jesus gasped, the pain in his hand a brilliant, clarifying anchor. “I’m full of them.”

The tower’s heartbeat rhythm skipped. The silver rain-curtains trembled.

And in that fractured instant, a new signal cut through the static. A voice, ragged and real, broadcasting on a frequency that felt like a punch in the chest.

“—if you can hear this, you’re inside the archive. The confessional buffer. It’s a one-way feed. They pour the sins in, they pump the obedience out.”

Judas.

“The only way out is through. You gotta play their game. You gotta confess.”

A voice crackled, a raw scrape of static in his inner ear. Judas. Of course it was Judas. The only apostle who’d ever understood the value of a bad deal.

“Confess to what?” Jesus muttered. The wind ripped the words away.

“Everything!” Judas’s transmission hissed. “The system’s a sin-eater. It’s hungry. Feed it. But you gotta mean it. The biometrics are a bitch.”

Mary grabbed his shoulder, her fingers digging in. She was shouting over the gale. “—signal’s coming from the tower’s sub-basement! He’s alive in there, somehow!”

Rex swiped a massive claw through a cluster of silver avatars. They dispersed like pixelated smoke, then re-formed a few feet away. “THE INSTRUCTION MANUAL FOR THIS REALITY IS VERY POORLY WRITTEN!” he bellowed. A thought-police avatar, shaped like a floating clipboard with glowing eyes, zipped toward him. He ate it. It tasted, he informed them, of administrative despair.

Jesus looked at the nail in his hand. His own blood, a vulgar red, beaded around the wound. A confession. That idea curdled in his gut. He’d spent two millennia in a confessional of sorts, with everyone yelling their sins at him. Nobody ever wanted to hear his.

But Judas was a professional betrayer. His advice, however galling was usually sound.

He closed his eyes against the lashing rain. Not to pray. To listen.

Under the hurricane’s roar, under the tower’s rhythmic thrum, he found it: the hum. The UHF static of a universe left on after the credits rolled. In the rain, in the neon, in the old ache behind his ribs. He’d been trying to tune it out since he got here. Now he reached for it.

A world dissolved.

Not back into the booth. This was worse.

He stood on a floor of pristine, white corporate tile. The walls were a soothing beige dotted with motivational posters. One showed a cartoon Eden with a thumbs-up. “SYNERGY SINCE DAY ONE!” The opposite wall was a lush, pixelated jungle, vines and flowers rendered in blocky 8-bit glory. A digital parrot looped on a three-frame animation, squawking silently. The air smelled of fresh coffee and overripe, binary fruit.

A reception desk materialized. The woman behind it had a smile that didn’t reach her polygonal eyes. “Welcome to Eden Integrated Afterlife Solutions. Do you have an appointment for your metaphysical recalibration?”

“I’m here to confess,” Jesus said.

“Excellent.” Her smile widened. A form appeared on the desk, a quill pen beside it. “Please list all sins, transgressions, and unprofitable thoughts. Use bullet points for clarity.”

He heard Mary’s voice, faint and tinny, like a radio from another room. “I’m in the system logs! He’s trying to interface! Rex, keep those things off the server node!”

A sound of tearing metal and a frustrated roar. “I REQUIRE A MANAGER!”

Back in the beige-and-pixel hell, Jesus picked up the quill. Cold. He touched it to the paper.

I hated the loaves and fishes. He wrote. The people were hungry, sure. But every time, it was just more beige food for more beige crowds. I wanted to turn the stones into scorpions. Just once.

The words glowed on the page, then sank in, absorbed. The receptionist nodded. “Continue.”

I got angry at the fig tree. It wasn’t the tree’s fault. I was just having a bad day and it was there.

Absorbed.

A woman looked up at the roof, directly at him. She smiled. It was a warm, grateful, utterly placid smile. Then she turned, and with perfect unison with everyone around her, she began walking calmly, orderly, toward the tower’s now-open doors.
