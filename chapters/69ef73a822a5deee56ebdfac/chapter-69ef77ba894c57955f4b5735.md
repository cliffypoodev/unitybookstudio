The crash behind them sounded like a church collapsing in slow motion. Metal screamed. Glass rained onto asphalt. The horn’s “Amazing Grace” choked off after the second note.

Mary kept her foot flat on the accelerator. A station wagon’s engine whined like a choirboy being castrated. “Anyone want to check if they’re dead?”

“No.” Jesus stared at the flash drive in his lap. It wasn’t just warm. It pulsed with a faint, sickly light, a greenish throb that matched the rhythm of a slow heartbeat. He picked it up. The plastic casing felt wrong—slippery, like skin after a fever breaks. “This thing’s alive.”

“It’s data,” Mary said, swerving around a possum that was probably already dead. “Data doesn’t have a pulse.”

Rex leaned forward from the backseat. His reactor cast a cold blue wash over the drive. “It’s not just data. It’s a schematic for a consciousness. A recipe for a soul. Of course it’s alive. It’s hungry.”

The wagon hit a pothole. Jesus’s head slammed against the window. A crack spread through the glass, a spiderweb starting from the spot where his temple connected. He saw his own reflection, distorted and bisected by the fracture. He looked exhausted. He was exhausted. The migraine from the motel had settled into a dull, familiar throb behind his eyes—the ghost of a crown. “Where are we going?”

“Away,” Mary said.

“That’s not a plan.”

“It’s the only plan we’ve got that hasn’t gotten us surrounded by razor-wire choirboys.” She glanced in the shattered rearview. Headlights pierced the swamp mist behind them. Not just one pair. Three. “Speaking of.”

Jesus turned. The SUVs were back. They moved with an unnatural coordination, keeping perfect distance, their headlights cutting identical cones through the gloom. “They’re herding us.”

Rex’s tail thumped against the seat. “The road dead-ends at Gator Gospel Gardens in eight miles. They know it.”

Of course they did. Jesus closed his eyes. The flash drive’s pulse beat against his palm. A recipe for a soul. Judas had wanted a clean, efficient messiah. No messy free will. This blueprint was the shopping list. His rift residue. Rex’s DNA. The obedience hormone as binding agent. He imagined the thing it would build—a smiling, hollow thing that healed by erasing. A messiah that offered peace by deleting the self.

His stomach clenched. Not anger. Not fear. Something older. A grief so deep it felt like geography.

“They’re closing,” Rex said, his voice a low grind.

Mary cursed and yanked the wheel. The wagon skidded onto a narrower service road, crushed shell and gravel spraying up against the undercarriage. Palmetto fronds slapped the windshield. The headlights followed, unwavering.

“We need to lose them,” Jesus said.

“You think?” Mary fumbled in the center console with one hand. Cassette tapes, gum wrappers, a fossilized french fry. She pulled out a cassette. The label was handwritten in the same messy scrawl as the flash drive: DEMON-SMASHING MIXTAPE. DO NOT PLAY AT FUNERALS. “Only one thing left to try.”

“What’s that?”

“An exorcism.” She shoved the cassette into the deck.

The wagon’s speakers, already half-blown from years of Mary’s questionable taste in music, emitted a low, distorted hum. Then a voice crackled through—not a singer. A preacher. Jimmy Swaggart, mid-sob, pleading for forgiveness. A sound was layered over a pulsing, synthetic bassline.

The effect on the pursuing SUVs was immediate.

The lead vehicle swerved violently, its headlights jerking across the road like searchlights in a panic. The second SUV braked, then accelerated, then braked again, as if the driver couldn’t decide between chase and prayer.

“What is this?” Jesus shouted over the unholy noise.

“Samples!” Mary grinned, wild in the dashboard glow. “I recorded hours of televangelist breakdowns—Swaggart, Bakker, that guy who cried about the gay agenda while wearing mascara. Slowed them down, pitched them, layered them over a drum machine I built from a broken confessional. It creates a cognitive dissonance loop! Their brains can’t process the contradiction!”

Rex’s reactor flickered in time with the beat. “You weaponized hypocrisy.”

“I repurposed it!” Mary cranked the volume. Tammy Faye Bakker’s tearful warble, distorted into a demonic chipmunk squeal, filled the car. “The Serpent Engine runs on ordered, compliant faith. This is faith having a nervous breakdown. It’s kryptonite!”

The third SUV suddenly veered off the road entirely. It plowed through a stand of sawgrass and into a murky canal with a tremendous splash. Its headlights glowed under the water for a moment, then died.

Jesus watched, a laugh caught in his throat. It wasn’t funny. Absurd. This was his defense against the architects of a new creation—a mixtape of televangelist meltdowns on a cassette from 1985.

The lead SUV wasn’t giving up. It accelerated, drawing alongside them on the narrow road. Jesus could see the driver. Not a robed enforcer. A man in a cheap suit, his face slick with sweat, his eyes wide and unblinking. A vein throbbed in his temple. He was mouthing words, trying to pray over the noise flooding his cab.

The passenger window rolled down. A hand emerged, clutching not a gun, but a small, silver device shaped like a crucifix. It emitted a high-pitched whine that cut through the mixtape’s chaos.

Rex snarled. “Signal purifier. They’re trying to cancel the noise.”

“Oh, hell no.” Mary slammed her palm against the dashboard radio. The station wagon’s ancient equalizer lights, long dead, suddenly flickered to life. She twisted the bass knob all the way to the right.

The speakers blew.

The sound that erupted wasn’t music. It was a physical force—a wall of distorted, subsonic rage that made the wagon’s windows vibrate. Jesus felt it in his teeth, in the bones of his chest. It was the sound of a thousand collection plates being crushed in a compactor.

The purifier in the SUV driver’s hand shattered. The man screamed, clutching his ears. His vehicle slewed sideways, tires screaming, and clipped the rear quarter-panel of the wagon.

Metal shrieked. The wagon lurched. Mary fought the wheel, her knuckles white. “Hold on!”

They weren’t going to hold on. The SUV was too big, the road too soft. Jesus saw the ditch coming—a dark, watery gash lined with cypress knees.

He did the only thing that made sense in a moment that made no sense at all.

He leaned across Mary, grabbed the wheel, and yanked it toward the SUV.

“What are you doing?!”

“Mercy,” he said, though the word was lost in the roar.

The wagon’s front bumper kissed the SUV’s side panel. A gentle nudge. A suggestion.

The SUV, already unbalanced took the suggestion. Its tires lost their purchase on the shell road. It slid, graceful as a sinner giving in, into the waiting ditch. It didn’t crash. It settled. The murky water rose to its door handles. The man in the suit stopped screaming. He just sat there, blinking at the swamp rising around him.

Mary stared. “You… helped him.”

“I inconvenienced him.” Jesus let go of the wheel. His hands were shaking. “There’s a difference.”

The wagon limped on, one headlight dead, the rear end dragging. The mixtape had ended, leaving a ringing silence in its wake. The only sounds were the groan of damaged metal and the chorus of frogs from the ditch.

Rex was quiet for a long moment. Then he said, “The Serpent Engine cannot process irrational acts of mercy. It’s a logic error. You just introduced a bug into their pursuit algorithm.”

Mary killed the engine. They rolled to a stop in the middle of the dark road. The only light came from the moon and the sputtering glow of Rex’s reactor.

She looked at Jesus. “That was stupid.”

“Yes.”

“It might have worked.”

“Maybe.”

She picked up the flash drive from where it had fallen to the floor. Its pulse was stronger now, a persistent green beacon in the dark. She didn’t grin. Her smile was tired, real. “So. We’ve got the recipe. And we’ve got a demon-smashing mixtape. What’s step two?”

Jesus took the drive from her. Hot now. Almost burning. He could feel the blueprint inside, not as data, but as intent. A wanting. He knew what they had to do. It was the only irrational choice left.

“We don’t build a god,” he said. The words tasted like copper and certainty. “We un-build one.”

The No-Tell Motel clung to the side of Alligator Alley like a barnacle on a rusted hull. The vacancy sign buzzed, the ‘V’ flickering on and off so it read “acancy” in the pre-dawn gloom. Mary paid cash to a clerk who didn’t look up from his wrestling magazine. One room smelled of mildew, industrial lemon cleaner, and the ghost of a thousand smuggled cigarettes.

Jesus sat on the edge of the bed, a floral-print comforter scratchy under his hands. The flash drive lay on the cheap particleboard table beside a broken lamp. It pulsed. A slow, patient green. Rex stood by the window, peering through a slit in the blinds. His tail made a soft, rhythmic thump against the shag carpet. Mary was at the sink, trying to get the rust-colored water to run clear.

“They’ll triangulate the drive’s energy signature,” Rex said, his voice a low rumble. “We have perhaps six hours.”

“Then we look at it now.” Jesus picked up the drive. It felt like a living tooth. He plugged it into Mary’s battered laptop, a machine held together with duct tape and atheist bumper stickers.

The screen flickered. Instead of file directories, a three-dimensional double-helix spiral appeared, rotating slowly. Beautiful. Horrible. Sections glowed with familiar gold—his rift residue, the divine fingerprint he’d left on reality. Woven through it were strands of deep, reptilian crimson—Rex’s base code. Binding it all together was a lattice of sickly silver, a structural substrate that throbbed with a rhythmic, demanding pulse. The obedience hormone.

“It’s not a copy,” Jesus whispered. His migraine was back, a hot nail behind his left eye. “It’s a collage.”

Mary leaned over his shoulder. Her damp hair smelled of swamp and cheap motel soap. “They didn’t have enough of your… stuff. So they used Rex’s DNA as a structural filler. Like using sawdust in sausage.”

“Thank you for that image,” Rex muttered, his tail giving one sharp, irritated flick.

Jesus zoomed in. The gold strands weren’t just material. They were memories. Fragments. He saw the Upper Room, the taste of wine and dread. The garden, the sweat like blood. These weren’t recorded events. They were sensations, stripped of context and labeled as “voluntary submission templates.”

His hands went cold. They hadn’t just taken his power. They’d scavenged his agony.

“The silver lattice,” Mary said, pointing at the pulsing substrate. “It’s a delivery system. The miracles it performs—the healings, the prophecies—they’re just carriers. Like a virus. Every act deposits more of the obedience hormone into the recipient. It doesn’t just ask for faith. It installs compliance.”

If host consciousness experiences ethical doubt > initiate neurotransmitter flush > trigger memory of crucifixion sensory data (pain index 9.7) > reinforce outcome: obedience equals cessation of pain.

Mercy requires obedience.

The lie he’d been fighting wasn’t just Judas’s. It was the foundational logic of the thing they were building. They’d taken the worst moment of his existence, the moment he’d chosen surrender, and turned it into a fucking user manual.

His vision narrowed. The room smelled suddenly of cedar and stone dust. The phantom weight of the cross pressed on his shoulders. He wasn’t remembering. He was reliving. The blueprint was pulling him in.

“Hey.” Mary’s hand was on his arm. Her fingers were cold. “Don’t go down the rabbit hole. That’s what it wants. It’s designed to trigger empathetic feedback.”

He jerked his arm away. “It’s not feedback. It’s a mugging.”

Made of nightmares, fine. But it’s the only one that doesn’t end with us as gator snacks in a drainage canal.”

They’d stolen a patient. And they were about to perform brain surgery with a mixtape and a parable.
