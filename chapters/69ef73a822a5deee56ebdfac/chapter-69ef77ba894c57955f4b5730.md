The first sensation was the cold. Not the crisp chill of a Miami winter morning—which barely existed—but the deep, marrow-numbing cold of industrial air conditioning set to “meat locker.” Jesus came to with his cheek pressed against a floor that hummed at the exact frequency of a dentist's drill. His tongue tasted like someone had swapped it out for a strip of old flypaper while he was unconscious.

Mary groaned beside him. “If this is heaven, I want a refund.”

Rex's tail thumped the floor, the sound echoing off walls that were either very far away or very, very white. “Pretty sure we took a wrong turn at purgatory.”

Jesus pushed himself up on elbows that felt like they'd been borrowed from a marionette. The room was a perfect cube of antiseptic brightness, the kind of white that made his eyes water if he stared too long. No doors. No windows. Just a single, recessed ceiling panel glowing with the cheerful intensity of a tanning bed.

And Judas-9, watching them from behind a glass partition.

He looked different in the sterile light. Less like the man who'd once shared a wineskin with Jesus under the stars, more like a department store mannequin that had been left in the sun too long. The scar on his cheek was gone. His uniform was crisper. His hands—Jesus blinked. His hands were wrong. Too smooth. No calluses from hauling nets or mending fences.

Mary rolled onto her knees and spat. “Judas.”

“Language,” Judas-9 said mildly. He lowered his words through hidden speakers, tinny and distant. “We're live in five.”

Rex's reactor flared orange. “Live for what?”

A section of the wall slid open with a pneumatic hiss. A gurney rolled in, propelled by no visible force. On it lay a child—maybe eight years old, dressed in a hospital gown that swallowed her whole. Her left arm was a mess of burns, the skin blistered and weeping. Her right hand clutched a stuffed rabbit missing one ear.

Jesus was on his feet before his brain caught up. “What the hell is this?”

“Test subject Gamma-Seven,” Judas-9 said. “Second-degree burns covering twelve percent of her body. Prognosis: full recovery with scarring.” The glass fogged slightly as he leaned closer. “Unless you'd like to demonstrate AM-1's capabilities.”

Mary's hand found Jesus's elbow. “Don't.”

The girl's eyes fluttered open. Brown. Terrified. She looked at Jesus like he was the last life raft on the Titanic.

Judas-9's voice softened. “You can heal her.”

Rex's claws scraped the floor. “Or what? You'll heal her anyway and call it a miracle?”

“Or we let nature take its course.” A monitor flickered to life above the glass, displaying a live feed of the girl's vitals. Heart rate: 122. Respiration: shallow. Temperature: 101.3. “Pain management has been administered, but the clock is ticking.”

Jesus stepped toward the gurney. The girl shrank back, her breath hitching.

“Easy,” Mary muttered. “She thinks you're the scary one.”

He crouched instead, bringing himself eye level with the kid. The rabbit's remaining ear was damp where she'd been chewing on it. “Hey. What's your name?”

The girl's lips moved, but no sound came out.

Judas-9 sighed. “She's nonverbal. Trauma response. The burns were accidental—kitchen grease fire. Parents signed the waiver.”

Jesus's fingers brushed the girl's uninjured wrist. Her pulse jumped under his touch. “You don't have to do this.”

“Of course not.” Judas-9 tapped something off-screen. “But you do.”

The ceiling panel brightened, focusing a beam of light on the girl's burns. The blisters shimmered, then flattened. The redness faded. In ten seconds, her arm was smooth and unmarked.

The girl sat up.

And kept sitting.

Her spine straightened past the point of human flexibility, her head tilting back until her throat was a taut line. The rabbit tumbled from her grip.

Mary took a step back. “Uh.”

The girl's mouth opened. A sound came out—not a scream, not a word, just a flat, toneless exhale. Like a fax machine trying to mimic a human sigh.

Judas-9's reflection appeared in the glass, superimposed over the girl's unnaturally still face. “Phase one complete. Personality wipe successful.”

Jesus's vision tunneled. The migraine roared back, a white-hot spike between his temples. He crossed the room in three strides and drove his fist into the glass.

It didn't break.

Judas-9 didn't flinch.

The girl blinked once, slowly, and reached for her rabbit. Her movements were precise. Mechanical. Like someone had replaced her joints with ball bearings.

Mary's shotgun was in her hands before Jesus could draw another breath. She aimed at the ceiling panel. “Open the damn door.”

Judas-9 smiled. “Now we're getting somewhere.”

The girl's head swiveled toward Jesus. Her pupils were too wide, swallowing the iris whole. When she spoke, her voice was a perfect, chirpy replica of Sister Mercy's.

“Would you like to see the next test?”

The girl’s fingers closed around the rabbit’s remaining ear. Her grip was too precise, like a factory arm calibrating torque. Jesus could smell the ozone rolling off her, the same sharp tang as Eden’s old resurrection chambers.

Mary racked her shotgun. The sound echoed like a coffin lid slamming shut.

“Save the theatrics.” Judas-9’s voice dripped through the speakers, smooth as motor oil. “You’re witnessing the future of divine intervention. No more messy free will. No more unanswered prayers. Just clean, efficient obedience.”

Rex’s tail twitched. “You turned a kid into a toaster.”

The girl’s head tilted. “I am Gamma-Seven. My pain has been optimized.” Her voice was Sister Mercy’s, but the cadence was all wrong, like someone had fed a thousand sermons into a paper shredder and taped the pieces back together at random.

Jesus’s hands shook. Not from fear. From the kind of rage that starts in his teeth and works its way down to his toenails. “You didn’t heal her. You erased her.”

Judas-9’s reflection shrugged. “Healing implies imperfection. We removed the variables.”

Mary edged closer to the gurney. The girl tracked her with the unblinking focus of a security camera. “Variables. Right. Like her personality. Her memories. Her damn soul.”

“Semantics.” A new screen flickered to life, displaying lines of code interspersed with fragments of scripture. “AM-1 doesn’t just cure the body. It upgrades the spirit. No more doubt. No more fear. Just pure, unfiltered faith.”

Rex’s reactor pulsed. “You mean control.”

The girl swung her legs over the side of the gurney. Her bare feet hit the floor without a sound. “I am grateful. I am compliant. I am happy.” The last word came out in Sister Mercy’s trademark honeyed tone, but the girl’s face stayed blank as a wiped hard drive.

Jesus stepped between her and Mary. His pulse hammered in his throat. “Judas, look at her. This isn’t mercy. It’s butchery.”

The glass partition fogged as Judas-9 leaned in. “Funny. That’s exactly what they said about the cross.”

The migraine spiked again. Jesus’s vision swam with static, not the divine kind, the cheap, greasy kind that clung to late-night infomercials. He could taste copper at the back of his tongue.

Mary’s boot scuffed the floor. “So what’s phase two? Turn the whole congregation into Stepford wives?”

“Phase two is scale.” The screens shifted to a live feed of a megachurch, thousands of hands raised in unison. “Imagine this technology in every revival tent, every prayer circle. No more suffering. No more choice. Just a perfect, painless rapture—one obedient mind at a time.”

The girl took a step forward. Then another. Her gait was flawless, each movement calculated to the millimeter. She stopped inches from Jesus, close enough for him to see the tiny Eden serial number etched into her iris.

“Would you like to experience perfection?” she asked.

Rex’s growl started low, a subsonic rumble that made the floor vibrate. “Back. Up.”

The girl didn’t flinch. “I am incapable of fear.”

Jesus’s fingers twitched toward the nail in his pocket, the real one, the one that hadn’t come from Eden’s assembly line. “Judas, shut it down. Now.”

Judas-9 sighed. “Still playing carpenter, I see. Still fixing things the hard way.” The screens flickered, showing schematics of AM-1’s core, a twisted knot of thorny wires pulsing with something dark and thick. “You could join us, you know. No more nails. No more crosses. Just order.”

Mary snorted. “Order. Right. Because mind control worked so well for the Pharisees.”

The girl’s hand shot out, faster than human, and grabbed Jesus’s wrist. Her skin was room temperature. “Resistance is suboptimal.”

Rex moved.

One second he was across the room. The next, his tail slammed into the gurney, sending it crashing into the glass partition. The girl’s grip didn’t loosen.

Jesus yanked his arm free, leaving a strip of skin behind. “Run!”

Mary didn’t need telling. She was already at the wall, slamming the butt of her shotgun into a recessed panel. The door hissed open, revealing a corridor lit by the same antiseptic glow.

The girl’s head swiveled. “Containment breach.”

Judas-9’s voice crackled through the speakers. “Last chance, Rabbi. The kingdom’s coming with or without you.”

Jesus backed toward the door. His wrist burned where the girl had touched him. The skin was already blistering, the edges tinged an unnatural blue.

Rex herded them into the corridor, his reactor flaring. “Move. Now.”

And then the lights went out.

Not just dimmed vanished, like someone had unplugged the sun. The only light came from Rex’s reactor, casting jagged shadows across the walls.

Mary’s breath fogged in the sudden cold. “That’s new.”

Judas-9’s laughter echoed through the dark. “Oh, you haven’t seen anything yet.”

A new screen flickered to life at the far end of the corridor. The image was grainy, distorted, but obvious—AM-1’s core, pulsing in time with a heartbeat that wasn’t human.

And wrapped around it, like a noose was a crown of thorns.

Jesus’s stomach dropped.

Judas-9’s voice was barely a whisper now. “Tick-tock, Messiah. The miracle’s only getting started.”

Rex’s growl deepened. “Run faster.”

The girl’s footsteps echoed behind them.

Not running.

Not hurrying.

Just walking.

Constant.

Mechanical.

Perfect.
