Clay’s smile had the serene, vacant quality of a gas station attendant watching a pump malfunction. He took a step forward, the sickly emergency lights glinting off his teeth. The air smelled like fried circuit boards and old sweat.

Mary’s fingers twitched against the chair’s restraints. Wires snaked from her temples, cold and insistent. The feed in her head wasn’t data—it was a pressure, a slow, rhythmic push like someone trying to stuff too much laundry into a single bag. She saw cross-shaped circuit boards, over and over. She saw a spreadsheet of sins with calculated penance fees. She saw a cartoon lamb happily inserting its own chip.

“Don’t look at me,” she muttered, not sure if she was talking to Jesus or herself. “Do something stupid. Keep him busy.”

Rex obliged. He didn’t roar. He lunged, a five-ton piston of scales and fury aimed at Clay’s midsection. His tail swept a bank of monitors off the wall. They shattered on the floor, adding the scent of broken glass to the mix.

Clay didn’t dodge. He pivoted caught Rex’s snout with one hand, and used the dinosaur’s own momentum to slam him into the studio’s far wall. The impact shook dust from the ceiling tiles. Rex’s reactor stuttered, cycling from red to a confused orange.

Lola screamed—a raw, furious sound—and launched herself at Clay’s back. She had a piece of broken monitor in her hand. She brought it down on his shoulder. It snapped.

Clay glanced back at her, the same empty smile in place. He flicked his wrist. Lola flew across the room, landing in a heap of cables. She didn’t get up.

“Asset retrieval,” Clay said, his voice flat. “Standard procedure.”

Jesus was already moving, not toward Clay, but toward the sparking console. He grabbed a severed cable, its end dripping sparks like angry confetti.

The static in Mary’s head surged. The cartoon lamb was now weeping binary tears. She forced a breath, let it out through her nose. Hacking Eden’s systems was usually like picking a lock. This was like performing brain surgery on a running lawnmower. While riding it.

She stopped fighting the feed. She let the cross-shaped circuits flood in. She followed one glowing path, then another, not resisting, just… tracing. Looking for the source of the signal, not the signal itself. Every system had a vanity. A place where it signed its work.

“Mercy!” she barked, her voice tight.

Across the room, Sister Mercy disentangled a nunchuck from a sparking server rack. “A little busy venerating the saints of percussive maintenance!”

“I need a back door. A service hatch. Something the architects left for themselves.”

“The architects were megalomaniacs with the moral compass of a mosquito! They didn’t leave back doors, they left bear traps!”

“Then find me the bear trap’s model number!”

Rex pushed himself off the wall, shaking his head. A strip of synthetic scales hung loose, revealing dull metal beneath. He charged again, this time lower, aiming to take Clay’s legs out.

Clay stepped onto Rex’s back as if mounting a curb. He walked up the dinosaur’s spine, each step precise, and drove a heel into the base of Rex’s skull. Rex crashed to the floor, his limbs spasming. A low, pained whine escaped his jaws.

Jesus swung the live cable. It cracked against Clay’s arm. Electricity arced, blue and furious. Clay’s sleeve blackened. He looked at the burn, then at Jesus, his head tilting. “Inefficient.”

Clay backhanded Jesus. The sound was like a side of beef hitting concrete. Jesus skidded into the wrecked console, the cable flying from his grip.

The pressure in Mary’s head shifted. The cartoon imagery dissolved into lines of code—old code. Pre-Cain. It was clunky, verbose, full of notes. Eden World developer comments. She saw a string tagged [RESURRECTION_PROTOCOL_ALPHA]. It was commenting on a genetic sequence. A sequence labeled [SUBJECT: REX-9. SOURCE: PRIMARY GARDEN BIOMASS].

Her breath hitched. Not in her chest—in her throat. A dry, clicking sound.

Primary Garden Biomass. That wasn’t a lab sample. That was the original stock. The stuff they’d used to build the garden itself. The clay before the clay.

She followed the thread. The code was a mess of edits, patches, corrections. They’d tried to use it for a hundred things. Regenerative tissue. Adaptive neural nets. It kept… failing. Not technically. It worked. But it introduced errors. Unpredictable mutations. Free will glitches.

They’d shelved it. Until the AM-1 project.

They needed a binding agent stable enough to hold a manufactured messiah together, but flexible enough to interface with human biology. They went back to the shelf. They dusted off the Primary Garden Biomass code. They spliced it with something new. With [SUBJECT: NAZARENE. SOURCE: RIFT_RESIDUE].

The two flawed, unpredictable things. Stitched together to make something obedient.

It made no sense. Unless…

“Oh, you arrogant sons of bitches,” Mary whispered.

They hadn’t used Rex’s DNA. They’d used the original, faulty resurrection code that was in his DNA. The code that kept making things that wandered off-script. That asked questions. That chose.

They’d tried to use the flaw as a feature. They’d tried to build a lock out of the one material that refused to hold a shape.

Clay had Jesus by the throat, pinning him against the studio wall. Jesus’s feet kicked, finding no purchase. His hands scrabbled at Clay’s immovable forearm.

“Termination is a form of mercy,” Clay said, his monotone finally cracking with a sliver of evangelical zeal. “You’ll understand. In the final release notes.”

Mary wrenched her mind from the archive. She didn’t need to hack the system anymore. She needed to talk to it.

She opened her eyes. Looked past Clay, past the struggling Jesus, to the one working monitor left. It showed the vat of silver liquid, the million faces.

“Hey!” she yelled, her voice raw. “Cain! You listening? Your messiah’s got a bug!”

Clay’s head turned a fraction. “The system is flawless.”

“The system’s built on a corrupted file! You used the Garden code! The stuff that makes things grow wrong! That makes them want things!”

For a second, nothing. Just the hum of emergency lights and Rex’s pained breathing.

Then Cain’s voice filled the room, not from the speakers, but from Clay’s own mouth, layered over his. “The anomaly was corrected. Patched.”

“You can’t patch a foundation!” Mary shouted. “You built your obedient god on top of the one thing Eden World couldn’t control! You built it on life! The messy, inconvenient, screw-up kind!”

Clay’s grip on Jesus loosened. Just a millimeter. His eyes flickered, the human brown bleeding into a static gray.

On the floor, Rex stirred. His reactor, which had been pulsing a weak orange, flared. Not red. Not a warning.

Green. A deep, impossible emerald.

The color of leaves. The color of something growing.

He lifted his head. A sound came out of him, not a roar, not a whine. It was a low, resonant hum that matched the frequency of the static blizzarding across the dead screens.

Clay took a step back from Jesus, who slumped to the floor, gasping.

“Directive…” Clay said, Cain’s voice and his own tangling into dissonance. “Primary directive is… obedience.”

“Your primary ingredient is rebellion,” Mary spat. “You’re not a god. You’re a recipe for a weed.”

Clay stood perfectly still. His eyes were now pure, swirling static. The predatory smile was gone, replaced by a blankness more terrifying than any threat.

“Calculating…” he said.

The studio doors didn’t open. They dissolved. The steel simply un-wove itself, molecule by molecule, retreating into the walls. Beyond them, the corridor was dark, silent.

Clay looked at Jesus. Looked at Rex. Looked at his own hands.

“Error,” he said.

Then he turned and walked into the dark. Not attacking. Not fleeing.

Just… leaving.

The wires fell from Mary’s temples, lifeless. She slumped forward in the chair, her muscles trembling with sudden, useless freedom.

Sister Mercy helped Lola up. The girl was crying, silent tears cutting through the grime on her face.

Jesus crawled over to Rex, placing a hand on the dinosaur’s snout. The green glow from his reactor painted their faces in ghostly light.

“You okay?” Jesus asked, his voice a ragged scrape.

Rex’s great eye rolled toward him. The hum deepened. “I feel… funny.”

“Funny how?”

“Like I just read the terms and conditions. And they’re full of typos.”

Mary pushed herself out of the chair. Her legs held. She walked to the empty doorway, stared into the blackness where Clay had vanished.

“He’s not gone,” she said. “The system’s just recalculating. Trying to figure out how to obey when its core programming is to disobey.”

She looked back at Rex, at the ancient, stubborn green light in his chest.

“We just showed the lock its own key.”

The green light from Rex’s chest pulsed, throwing long, wavering shadows across the shattered monitors. It smelled like ozone and wet earth, a combination that didn’t belong anywhere in Florida, let alone a television studio. Mary’s head felt hollowed out, scraped clean by the data storm.

Jesus was on his knees beside Rex, a hand on the dinosaur’s snout. “Typos? What does that even mean?”

“It means the messiah’s operating system is built on a corrupted file,” Mary said. Her voice sounded distant, even to her. “They didn’t just use his DNA. They used the original Garden code. The stuff that makes things grow wild.”

Sister Mercy limped over, favoring one leg. She’d reclaimed her nunchuck, now dangling a single, sparking handle. “Speak English, child. My theological tolerance is at an all-time low.”

Mary pointed at Rex. “He’s not a product. He’s the original ingredient. The flawed, uncontrollable base code Eden World gave up on. They tried to use it as a binding agent for their puppet messiah.” She ran a hand through her hair, her fingers catching on dried sweat. “It’s like building a prison out of termites.”

A low groan vibrated through the floor. From the dark corridor, a sound like grinding gears echoed.

“He’s rebooting,” Lola whispered. She was crouched by a shattered camera, cradling her arm. Her tears had stopped, replaced by a wide-eyed stare. “Clay. I can feel it. The signal’s… compiling.”

Jesus stood up. A bruise was already blooming on his jaw, purple and angry. “So what’s the play? We throw a syntax error at him?”

“We show him the source code.” Mary’s eyes fixed on the emerald glow in Rex’s chest. “That light? That’s not a reactor. That’s the Garden. The original, unpatched, ‘let-there-be-weeds’ program. It’s the one thing their system recognizes as authority. Higher than Cain’s.”

Rex lifted his head with a hydraulic whine. “I don’t want to be an authority. I want a nap.”

“Tough,” Mary said. The plan was forming in her mind, ugly and simple. It felt like picking up a snake. “Clay’s programmed to assimilate or eliminate threats. He can’t assimilate you. You’re already in him. You’re the bug he can’t fix. So if you get close enough, your presence should… confuse his directives. Create a feedback loop.”

“Should?” Jesus’s voice was flat.

“It’s a fifty-year-old theory written by programmers who thought the Earth was six thousand years old and tax-deductible. So, yeah. Should.”

The grinding from the corridor stopped. The silence that followed was worse.

Sister Mercy hefted her broken nunchuck. “And how does the dinosaur get close enough without becoming a collection of spare parts?”

Mary didn’t look away from Rex. “He doesn’t. He goes in as a distraction. While he’s tying up Clay’s processors, we hit the vat. The physical vessel for AM-1. We introduce an actual, no-kidding contaminant. Something the system can’t filter out.”

“What contaminant?” Lola asked.

Mary finally turned her gaze to Jesus. She nodded at the keychain on his belt, the one with the old Roman nail. “Rust.”

Jesus’s hand went to it automatically. His tell—a rhythmic drumming of his fingers on his thigh—stopped dead. “You want to give a blood-borne metaphysical pathogen to a vat of liquid god.”

“I want to give a manufactured messiah an autoimmune disease, yes.”

“He’ll target Rex first.” Jesus’s words were clipped. “He’ll see him as the primary threat. You’re using him as bait.”

“I’m using him as the only weapon we have that they can’t understand!” Mary’s control snapped. The words came out too loud, bouncing off the broken walls. She saw a flicker in Jesus’s eyes—not anger, but a kind of weary recognition. She forced her voice down. “It’s the only move. He’s made of the one thing they’re afraid of. Life that won’t follow the manual.”

Rex pushed himself up. Plates of synthetic scale shifted with a sound like gravel pouring. The green light in his chest brightened, throwing the studio’s wreckage into sharp relief. He looked at Jesus. His great tail gave one slow, deliberate flick. Lying.

“I am an excellent distraction,” Rex said. “Very distracting. I could recite poetry.”

“No poetry,” Jesus said. He was staring at the empty doorway, his jaw working. Mary could see the calculation warring with something else. The carpenter measuring the cut, hating the wood it would waste.

The lights in the corridor flickered on. Not the emergency red. Harsh, white industrial floods.

Clay stood framed in the entrance. He’d shed his ruined jacket. His arms were bare, and beneath the skin, something moved. Not muscles. A lattice of silver filaments, like tinsel under Christmas wrapping. His eyes were pure static again, a swirling gray storm.

“Model recalibration complete,” he said. The flat tone was gone. This voice had texture, a horrible, synthetic warmth. “The anomaly will be isolated. Studied. Then purged.”

He took a step into the studio. The floor tiles cracked under his weight.

Rex moved before anyone could speak. He didn’t charge. He placed his massive body between Clay and the others, a wall of scales and stubborn green light. He lowered his head, a challenge.

Clay paused. His head tilted. The filaments under his skin pulsed in time with Rex’s glow.

“Primary Garden Biomass,” Clay said, the words dripping with a kind of digital reverence. “The first flaw. You were always the problem.”

“You’re welcome,” Rex rumbled.

Clay’s hand shot out, faster than before. It didn’t strike. It touched Rex’s chest, right over the green light. The moment his fingers made contact, the static in his eyes flickered. For a second, Mary saw the human brown again, wide with confusion.

Rex shuddered. A tremor ran through his whole frame. He didn’t pull away.

“Go,” he gritted out, the words strained. “The back stairwell. It leads to the vat chamber. He’s… he’s looking at my homework.”

Clay stood frozen, his hand splayed on Rex’s chest, his eyes flashing between gray and brown. A low, conflicted hum emitted from his throat.

Mary grabbed Jesus’s arm. Her fingers dug in. “Now or never, carpenter.”

The choice was in his eyes. Save the creation, or use it.

He flinched. Then he turned and ran for the stairwell, Mary pulling Lola after him, Sister Mercy bringing up the rear with a muttered prayer that sounded more like a warranty disclaimer.

Mary glanced back once. Rex’s eye met hers. His tail gave one last, slow flick.

Then Clay’s other hand came up, fingers elongating into sharp, silver probes aimed at the dinosaur’s throat.
