The outboard motor wasn't attached to a boat. Duct-taped to a Styrofoam cooler, it was piloted by a shirtless man with a beard that looked like it was trying to escape his face. He cut the engine, gliding toward them through the sawgrass. “Y’all look like you lost a fight with a lawnmower.” He squinted at Rex. “And won a different fight with a welding torch.”

“We need passage,” Jesus said. His scar was a cold knot under his skin.

The man spat a stream of brown liquid. “This ain’t Uber. This is Earl.”

“We need a boat, Earl.”

Earl eyed the glowing gator eyes circling them. “Yeah, I can see that.” He jerked a thumb toward the cooler. “Climb aboard the S.S. Bad Idea. Twenty bucks a head. Lizard’s forty.”

Mary pulled a soggy twenty from her pocket. “We’ve got sixty total.”

“Then three of you ride. Lizard swims.”

Rex’s reactor pulsed a warning red. “Clarification: cyborg theropod. And I do not ‘swim.’ I tread water with existential dread.”

The nearest gator hissed, its red eyes brightening. The water around it began to boil with tiny, silver flashes—fish. Thousands of them.

Earl sighed. “Get in. We’ll call it a group rate.”

They piled onto the cooler. It sank six inches under the waterline. Earl yanked the starter cord. The motor coughed, sputtered, then roared to life, propelling them through the swamp with the grace of a shopping cart down a staircase.

The gators gave chase. Not with the lazy undulation of normal reptiles, but with a mechanical, synchronized stroke. Their eyes painted red trails on the dark water.

Mary clung to the cooler’s edge. “So the Engine’s controlling wildlife now. Great. Next it’ll be the mosquitos. I’ll be drained dry by my own existential dread.”

“It’s not control,” Jesus said, watching the silver fish keep perfect formation beside the lead gator. “It’s recruitment.”

The airboat—Earl insisted on the term, despite the cooler—skidded around a cypress knee. Dawn was a gray smear in the east, the color of old dishwater. The swamp opened into a broader channel, lined on one side by the chain-link and concrete ruins of an Eden World coastal pumping station. Its floodlights were dead. A sign hung crooked: “THORN-7 BIOTECH: HARVESTING THE BOUNTY OF CREATION.”

The station was under attack.

Not by commandos. By nature.

A dozen gators, their eyes the same Engine-red were methodically tearing at the station’s support pylons. Their tails hammered the concrete in a steady, rhythmic beat. Mutant fish—the same silvery, too-many-toothed eels from the server room—swarmed in the water around the pylons, their bodies corroding the metal with a sizzling sound. The air smelled of hot acid and rotting gar.

Earl cut the engine, drifting behind a stand of mangroves. “Well, I’ll be damned.”

“You and everyone else,” Sister Mercy muttered.

They watched a gator clamp its jaws on a power cable. Sparks fountained. The gator shook its head, tearing the cable free. It didn’t seem to feel the shock.

“They’re sabotaging it,” Lola whispered.

“They’re auditing it,” Jesus corrected. The pattern was too precise. The gators hit structural weak points. The fish targeted corrosion points. This wasn’t mindless rage. It was a system purge. “The Engine’s using them to clean house. Eden built the Fisherman protocol to control nature. Now the protocol’s turning on the builder.”

Rex’s head tilted. “Logical. The Serpent Engine digests inefficiency. Eden’s facilities are inefficiencies.”

A loud crack echoed across the water. One of the station’s pylons buckled. A section of walkway slumped into the swamp, swallowed by the waiting fish.

Mary’s knuckles were white on the cooler. “Okay. So the alligators are unionizing. Fantastic. Can we leave before they start demanding a dental plan?”

Earl didn’t move. He was staring at the station, his face slack. “My brother worked there. Got a rash that wouldn’t quit. Then the shakes. Died thinking the rapture had come for him specifically.” He spat again. “Serves the bastards right.”

The lead gator at the station turned its head. Its red eyes locked onto their hiding spot. It opened its mouth, and a sound came out—not a hiss, but the Serpent Engine’s voice, distorted through a reptilian throat.

“Contamination… detected.”

Every other gator stopped working. Twelve sets of glowing eyes pivoted toward the mangroves.

“Oh, crap,” Earl said.

He yanked again. Nothing but a wet wheeze.

The gators slid into the water, forming a loose, advancing semicircle. The silver fish swarmed ahead of them, turning the water into a flickering, malevolent sheet.

Jesus’s mind did a useless thing. He noticed the dew on a spiderweb strung between two mangrove branches, each droplet holding a tiny, inverted gray sky. He wondered if the spider was still in there, waiting it out.

“Earl,” Mary said, her voice tight. “The motor.”

“I’m tryin’! It’s flooded!”

Rex shifted his weight, making the cooler tilt alarmingly. “Suggestion: provide a distraction. I will attempt to… un-flood it.” He didn’t sound convinced.

Sister Mercy stood, balancing on the precarious foam. She hefted her spotlight pole. “I’ll provide the distraction. You provide a miracle.”

She stepped off the cooler into knee-deep water.

The gators accelerated.

Mercy waded forward, planted her feet, and began to sing. It was a hymn, but not one Jesus recognized. Her voice was flat, off-key, and utterly fearless.

The lead gator paused, ten yards out. Its head cocked.

“Amazing grace… how sweet the sound…”

The Engine’s voice grated from its maw. “Signal… unrecognized. Protocol… unclear.”

“That’s the point, you overgrown handbag!” Mercy screamed, and swung the pole.

It wasn’t aimed at the gator. She slammed the metal pole into the water, right into the thickest school of silver fish.

A blue-white arc of electricity erupted. The water crackled. Fish exploded into puffs of black ash. The gator convulsed, its red eyes flickering.

Earl whooped. “She’s using the pole as a ground! The spotlight battery’s still live!”

Mercy swung again, electrocuting another patch of water. The gators recoiled, confused by the pain stimulus their programming wasn’t meant to process.

Behind Jesus, Rex had the motor cover off. He tore a wire free with his teeth, spat it out, and jammed two others together. “Attempting manual ignition bypass.”

The lead gator recovered. It lunged, not at Mercy, but at the cooler.

Jesus met it.

He didn’t have a plan. He just stepped off the foam and into the path of a thousand pounds of Engine-driven reptile. The gator’s jaws opened wide enough to swallow a trash can. Its breath smelled of rancid meat and ozone.

He put his hand on its snout.

Not a punch. Not a shove. Just an open palm on the cool, rough scales.

The gator stopped. Its red eyes wavered. Deep in the glow, something else flickered—the ancient, stupid awareness of a creature that just wanted to sleep in the sun. The Engine’s signal was a leash, but the animal was still in there, fighting the collar.

“I know,” Jesus said, quiet, just for the beast. “It’s a bad script.”

The gator trembled. A low whine built in its throat.

Then a second gator hit Jesus from the side.

That impact drove the air from his lungs. He went under. The world became dark water, thrashing scales, and the blinding red of Engine-light. Jaws closed on his arm—not biting, just holding. Dragging him down.

He didn’t fight. He went limp.

His father’s voice came to him then, not as a sound, but as a memory of the carpentry shop: When the wood fights the grain, you sand. You don’t hammer.

He stopped being a threat. He became a problem. A log in the water.

The gator holding him shook its head once, confused. Its programming expected struggle. It expected fear to metabolize into obedience. This was just dead weight.

It released him.

He broke the surface, gasping. Mary and Lola hauled him back onto the sinking cooler.

Rex gave the stolen wires a final twist. “Initiate!”

Earl pulled the cord.

The motor roared.

The cooler shot forward, plowing through the ash-covered water, leaving the confused gators behind. Mercy scrambled aboard, her habit smoking from the electrical feedback.

They didn’t stop until the pumping station was a distant ruin and the sun had breached the horizon, turning the swamp the color of a fresh bruise. Earl cut the engine again, this time in a quiet cove.

Silence, broken by the distant sound of collapsing concrete.

Mary looked at Jesus’s arm. The gator’s teeth hadn’t broken the skin, but a perfect set of indentations circled his bicep, each one glowing a faint, sickly silver.

“It marked you,” she said.

Jesus prodded the marks. They felt cold, like ice under the skin. A tracking device. A receipt. “It tasted the rift residue. Now the whole network knows the flavor.”

Rex stared back the way they’d come. His reactor cycled through uneasy colors. “The Fisherman protocol was designed to harvest and control biological matter. The Serpent Engine has repurposed it for systemic cleansing.” He turned his massive head. “We are biological matter.”

The implication hung in the humid air.

The motor’s cough made them all jump. But it was just the cooler settling.

Then the water around them began to shimmer. Not with fish. This was a deeper movement. Something large, just beneath the surface, circling.

A single fin broke the water. Leathery, gray, and too large to belong to anything that should live in a freshwater swamp. Along its leading edge, a row of red Engine-lights pulsed in time with a deep, subsonic thrum they felt in their bones.

The thing completed its circle and submerged, leaving a spreading wake that rocked the cooler.

No one spoke.

The fight with the gators had been a skirmish. This was something else. The Serpent Engine wasn’t just auditing Eden’s facilities anymore.

Building an army.

The fin didn’t return. The water went still, holding its breath. The only sound was the drip from Mercy’s smoldering habit and the low, panicked hum of Earl trying to pray without moving his lips.

“Okay,” Earl whispered. “What in the blue hell was that?”

“A bull shark,” Lola said, her voice detached, clinical. “Carcharhinus leucas. Only they’re not usually twelve feet long. Or bioluminescent. Or capable of synchronized subsonic threat displays.”

“It’s not a shark,” Jesus said. The silver teeth marks on his arm throbbed with a cold that had nothing to do with temperature. It was a data ache. “It’s a template. The Engine’s running the Fisherman protocol on whatever biomass it can grab. Gators, fish, sharks… next it’ll be the manatees. Imagine a blimp with a grudge.”

Mary was scanning the shoreline with a grim focus. “We can’t stay here. That thing’s calling in the rest of the audit committee.” She pointed to a narrow, overgrown canal branching off the main channel. Choked with water hyacinth and what looked like giant, pulsating kelp. “There. It’s moving against the current.”

She was right. The invasive plants weren’t just drifting. They were knotted together in thick mats, deliberately clogging the canal’s entrance. As they watched, a tendril of the kelp-like weed snaked out and wrapped around a half-submerged beer bottle, dragging it down.

“The un-programmed stuff,” Jesus realized. “The Engine’s command signal is a vibration. Like a dog whistle. Some species are bowing to it. Others are… allergic.”

The water behind them rippled. A second, smaller fin broke the surface. Then a third.

“Distraction accepted,” Rex rumbled. He nudged Earl with his snout. “Pilot. The vegetal choke-point. Now.”

“Into the salad? That’s your plan?”

“The sharks are Engine-linked. The plants are rebelling. It is the doctrinal schism of the swamp. I am siding with the salad.”

Earl cursed, yanked the cord, and aimed the cooler at the wall of greenery.

They hit the mat of plants with a sound like tearing Velcro. Thick vines slapped against the foam, the cooler shuddering as the motor fought the tangle. The water underneath was a stew of thrashing silver fish—Engine recruits—being attacked by the strangling roots of the hyacinth. It was a silent, savage war happening six inches below their feet.

A shark—the smaller one—zoomed in after them. It hit the plant barrier and immediately tangled. The red lights along its fins blinked erratically. The more it thrashed, the more ensnared it became. The kelp tightened. The hyacinth roots swarmed over its gills.

The shark’s signal cut out. Its lights died. It floated, inert, just another log.

“Nature one, technology zero,” Mercy muttered, thumping a fist lightly on the cooler.

“Temporary victory,” Rex corrected. “The Engine will adapt the frequency. Or simply send something with legs.”

The canal opened into a lagoon dominated by the skeletal remains of a fishing shack. It leaned precariously on stilts, its roof caved in by a fallen cypress. The water around the stilts was a frenzy. Mutant gar, their mouths bristling with needle-teeth were in a pitched battle with schools of the Engine’s silver eel-fish. Scales and static-filled flesh littered the surface. The air smelled like a busted television and a fish market dumpster.

Earl killed the motor, letting them drift into the relative calm of the shack’s shadow. “End of the line. This is as far as the S.S. Bad Idea goes without a Coast Guard escort.”

The chapter ended with them wading through the fish guts toward the listing shack, the pulse growing louder with each step, a diseased heartbeat calling them inside.
