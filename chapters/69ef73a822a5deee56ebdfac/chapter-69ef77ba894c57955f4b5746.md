The shack’s pulse wasn’t a sound Mary heard with her ears. It came up through the soles of her shoes, a sub-bass thump that made her molars ache. The planks underfoot were soft with rot, weeping black sap that smelled of burned sugar and low tide. Mary went first, because someone had to, and because the others were arguing about the theology of property damage.

“It’s trespassing,” Jesus whispered behind her, his voice tight.

“It’s a crime scene,” Rex corrected, his tail flicking a pile of rusted lures off a shelf. “We are investigators.”

“We’re breaking and entering.”

“The door was absent.”

It was. The doorway was just a ragged hole chewed by termites and something with sharper teeth. Mary stepped through, into the dark. Her eyes adjusted to the gloom, picking out shapes. Fishing nets hung like cobwebs, strung with old bobbers and the skeletal remains of something that might have been a possum. A Coleman lantern lay on its side, the glass cracked. That pulse was stronger in here. It came from below.

A set of rickety stairs led down into the water under the shack. They weren’t wood. They were metal, bolted clumsily to the original structure, descending into a greenish glow.

“Bingo,” Mary said.

Lola peered over her shoulder. “That’s not a basement. That’s a baptismal font for catfish.”

Sister Mercy pushed past them, her smoldering habit leaving a trail of acrid smoke. She didn’t hesitate. Her boot hit the first metal step with a clang that echoed in the confined space. Sister Mercy vanished into the glow.

Mary followed. The air changed halfway down—from damp rot to the sharp, sterile chill of server rooms. The water at the bottom wasn’t swamp. It was clear, contained by a glass or acrylic wall that formed a tunnel. Inside the tunnel, the source of the glow pulsed: a thick cable, sheathed in what looked like veins, running along the floor. It pulsed with that same sickly silver light. The Serpent Engine’s artery.

The tunnel led under the shack, opening into a chamber that should not have existed. This space was the size of a suburban living room, carved out of the limestone bedrock. It was cold. The walls were lined with server racks, their fans humming a discordant hymn. Wires snaked across the floor, bundled with zip-ties and what appeared to be strips of dried palm frond. In the center of the room sat the heart of it all.

It wasn’t a machine. Not exactly. It was a vat, glass-walled, filled with that same silver liquid from the tower. Inside, shapes drifted—pale, humanoid forms, curled in fetal positions. Dozens of them. Their faces pressed against the glass, mouths open in silent prayers. The liquid swirled around them, fed by the pulsing cable.

Sister Mercy stood before the vat, her back to them. She had her arms outstretched, like a preacher welcoming the congregation.

“You found it,” she said, not turning around. Her voice had a weird resonance in the chamber. “The wellspring.”

“Mercy,” Jesus said. He took a step forward, his shoes squeaking on the damp floor. “What is this?”

“Harvest.” She finally turned. Her eyes were red-rimmed, but dry. Her expression was serene in a way that made Mary’s stomach clench. It was the calm of someone who’d already jumped. “The Engine doesn’t just broadcast. It ingests. Every prayer of doubt, every flicker of fear from the faithful… it’s food. This is where it digests.”

Mary moved to the nearest server rack. The monitors were old CRTs, their screens showing scrolling lines of text. Not code. Confessions. I stole from the collection plate. I coveted my neighbor’s wife. I doubted the miracle. Each line was timestamped, tagged with a name and a GPS coordinate.

“It’s a sin-eater,” Jesus said, understanding dawning like a slow, cold leak. “A mechanical scapegoat.”

“But better,” Mercy said, a missionary zeal heating her words. “A scapegoat only carries the sin away. This… transforms it. Purifies it into usable data. The doubt is filtered out. What’s left is pure, obedient faith. A clean signal.” She gestured to the vat. “These are the donors. The ones whose faith wasn’t perfect. The Engine is helping them.”

One of the forms in the vat drifted closer to the glass. It was a man maybe in his fifties, his face slack. His lips moved, forming the same two words, over and over. No sound came out. Mary read the lips. Thank you.

Rex growled, a low vibration that made the cables on the floor tremble. “You are describing a refinery. For souls.”

“For their own good!” Mercy’s calm cracked, just for a second. A spasm of something raw crossed her face. “You think I wanted this? I felt God in a room where they made me heal a monster! I know what contaminated grace looks like! This…” she slapped the glass of the vat, making the forms inside shudder. “This is clean. No suffering. No messy choices. Just peace.”

Mary’s fingers were already moving. She’d spotted the broadcast console on the far wall—a Frankenstein mix of a 1980s radio board and Eden tech, sprouting thorn-like crystals. This was the source of the signal Judas had tried to warn them about. The one going live tonight.

“It’s not peace,” Jesus said. He walked up to the vat, placed his hand against the glass next to the floating man’s face. “It’s digestion. You’re turning people into batteries.”

“They volunteered!” Mercy snapped. “They sent in their prayer requests! They checked the terms of service!”

Mary ignored them. She slid into the chair at the console. The seat was warm. Someone had been here recently. The main screen showed a countdown: 00:12:47… 46… 45. A system was in standby, waiting for the final command to begin the live feed that would pipe this “clean signal” into every home still tuned to the True Cross Network.

Her hack kit was in her backpack, a waterproof pouch she’d salvaged from the wreck of the studio. She pulled it out, her hands steady. This wasn’t about firewalls. This was about narrative. The Engine fed on confession. It craved the messy, human truth of failure.

So she’d give it a feast.

“What are you doing?” Lola asked, hovering at her shoulder.

“Changing the channel,” Mary said. She plugged a spliced cable into an auxiliary port on the board. Her laptop flickered to life, its screen a stark, sane blue against the organic silver glow of the room. “It wants a clean signal. I’m giving it the opposite.”

“The unfiltered truth,” Jesus said. He’d turned from the vat, his eyes on her. There was no joke now. No deflection. Just a weary, grim approval.

“It’ll be a system shock,” Mary said, her fingers flying across the keys. She pulled up directories, navigating the Engine’s bloated memory. “All the raw data. The unedited confessions. The financial records from the miracle cruises. The blueprints for AM-1. The obedience hormone formulae. The Fisherman protocol specs. Every dirty secret, back-to-back, no commercial breaks.”

Rex loomed behind her, his reactor casting a greenish light on the keyboard. “The cognitive load will be immense. It may trigger a purge cycle. Or a catastrophic failure.”

“That’s the idea.”

Sister Mercy stared at her, horror replacing zeal. “You can’t. You’ll burn it all down. You’ll burn them down!” She pointed a trembling finger at the vat.

“Lady,” Mary said, not looking up from her screen. “They’re already ash. You just liked the way it sparkled.”

The countdown hit ten minutes. A soft, choral tone filled the chamber—the system’s “on-air in ten” warning. Mary found the main broadcast buffer. It was a massive file, labeled PURE_SIGNAL_FINAL.mpg. She highlighted it, dragged it to the trash. The system chirped a protest.

“Earl,” Jesus called up the stairs. “We might need a quick exit.”

“The S.S. Bad Idea has a leak!” Earl’s voice echoed down. “And I think something’s nesting in the life jacket!”

“Improvise.”

Mary uploaded her payload. A progress bar crawled across her screen. 15%. The system chirped again, louder. A red light began to strobe on the console.

“It knows,” Lola whispered.

The forms in the vat began to stir. Their synchronized, silent prayer broke. One of them opened its eyes. They were silver, pupil-less. It looked right at Mary and screamed. This sound was the pure, undistilled terror of a man realizing he’s been digested.

The glass vibrated.

“Hurry,” Jesus said.

The progress bar hit 60%. The main server rack let out a high-pitched whine. A monitor exploded in a shower of sparks and black smoke.

Sister Mercy sank to her knees, her hands clasped. She wasn’t praying to the vat anymore. She was just praying. The words were too quiet to hear.

The countdown hit five minutes. The choral tone became a single, sustained organ note that made the fillings in Mary’s teeth hum.

85%.

A conduit pipe on the ceiling burst, spraying coolant that smelled of mint and formaldehyde.

90%.

The vat cracked. A hairline fracture, top to bottom. Silver liquid wept from the seam.

95%.

Mary’s finger hovered over the enter key. The final command. The system’s alarm was a continuous, ear-splitting shriek now. The red strobe light painted the room in pulses of panic.

100%.

She looked at Jesus. He gave a single, sharp nod.

She hit the key.

The organ note cut off. The alarm died. For one second, there was perfect, deafening silence. Then every speaker in the chamber, every screen, every wire, erupted with noise. Financial figures stuttered over the shriek of a televangelist mid-fraud. The guttural scream of the mutant shark mixed with the serene, recorded voice of Sister Mercy explaining the obedience hormone’s side effects.

Glorious, messy, unbearable truth.

And it was going live in four minutes.

The noise wasn't a broadcast. It was a vomit. A geyser of every dirty secret, every cooked book, every low doubt and faked miracle, fired directly into the Serpent Engine’s digestive tract. The speakers didn’t play it—they hemorrhaged it. Its sound was physical, a wall of shame that hit Mary in the chest and made her ears pop.

On the main CRT, the serene “ON AIR” graphic glitched, melted, and reformed into a stuttering collage of nightmare home movies. There was Pastor Bob, sweating under studio lights, explaining to a man in a suit how to spike the communion wine with mild sedatives “for a more receptive congregation.” There were the blueprints for AM-1, with Jesus’s stolen DNA sequences highlighted in screaming red. There was the Fisherman, on his boat, muttering about “harvest yields” as he looked at a chart of viewer loyalty ratings.

The vat shattered.

It didn’t explode. It sighed open, a long, tired crack that became a yawn. The silver liquid poured out, not with a flood’s violence, but with the awful inevitability of a gutted thing emptying itself. It sloshed across the floor, cold and thick as mercury, swirling around their ankles. The forms inside slumped, boneless, into the spreading pool. They didn’t move. The one who had screamed just stared at the ceiling, his silver eyes dimming to the color of dirty dishwater.

Sister Mercy stopped praying. She watched the liquid soak the hem of her habit, her face blank. The zeal was gone. What was left looked like the morning after a fever broke—hollow, and vaguely embarrassed.

“You killed it,” she said, her voice flat.

“It was never alive,” Mary shot back, her hands still on the keyboard. This system was seizing. Files auto-opened, windows layering over windows until the screen was a solid block of gibberish and confession. “It was a stomach. I gave it indigestion.”

A new sound cut through the digital bedlam—a low, grinding shriek of shearing metal from somewhere above. The whole shack groaned. Dust and bits of termite-chewed wood sifted down from the ceiling.

“The structural integrity of this location is now zero,” Rex announced, his head tilted as he listened to the building’s complaints. “The Engine’s coolant systems were load-bearing. Metaphorically. And literally.”

“Earl!” Jesus yelled toward the stairs. “We’re leaving!”

“The boat’s full of that silver goop!” Earl’s reply was a panicked holler. “And the gators are back! They’re… they’re lining up!”

Mary ripped her cable from the console. The broadcast would run itself now, a self-replicating loop of truth poisoning the network. Her job was done. A screen froze on a single image: the Serpent Engine’s own internal diagnostic, showing its core stability at 2% and plummeting. A system purge was imminent.

Lola was already at the stairs, beckoning frantically. “Move!”

Sister Mercy didn’t move. She knelt in the spreading silver, watching it puddle around her knees. “It was clean,” she murmured, to no one.

Jesus hesitated. He looked from Mercy to the exit, his jaw tight. Mary saw his fingers drum against his thigh—once, twice, a frantic tattoo on the phantom wood of a cross. His coping mechanism. His tell. The wound of betrayal, echoed in the hollow woman on the floor.

He took a step toward her.

“Don’t,” Mary said. It wasn’t a plea. Data.

“She’s just—”

“She’s a grown woman who turned people into soup stock. Let her wallow in it.”

Another beam overhead cracked, a sound like a gunshot. The entire chamber lurched to the left. The server racks leaned, their fans wailing.

Jesus met Mercy’s empty gaze. He held it for a three-count. Then he turned, his decision made in the set of his shoulders. He grabbed Mary’s arm, pulling her toward the stairs. “Go.”

They braced for the fall as the alligators roared their victory.
