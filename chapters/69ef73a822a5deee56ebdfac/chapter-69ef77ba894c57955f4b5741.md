The stairwell door slammed shut behind them cutting off the studio's green glow and the sight of silver probes aimed at Rex’s throat. The silence was immediate, thick, and smelled of stale cigarettes and industrial cleaner. Jesus’s lungs burned. He hadn’t taken a breath since he’d started running.

“Move,” Mary hissed, shoving Lola ahead of her down the concrete steps.

The steps were steep, narrow, and painted a chipped institutional green. Emergency lights buzzed overhead, casting a jaundiced pallor. Jesus’s hand went to his pocket, to the nail. Its familiar, rough-hewn shape was the only solid thing in a world coming unglued.

Sister Mercy brought up the rear, her steps heavy. “If that overgrown iguana gets himself disassembled, I’m not filling out the paperwork.”

“He’s buying us time,” Mary snapped, not looking back. Her voice echoed off the cinderblock walls. “So use it. The vat chamber should be two levels down. Sub-basement.”

“How do you know?” Lola asked, her voice small.

“Because that’s where you keep the thing you don’t want anyone to find. Basic evil architecture.”

They hit the next landing. One door here was metal, riveted, with a small, wire-reinforced window. Jesus peered through. A long, low-ceilinged corridor stretched away, lit by the same sickly yellow lights. Pipes ran along the ceiling, dripping condensation. Empty.

He pushed the door open. The air changed. Cooler, damper, and it carried a new sound underneath the hum of the lights—a wet, rhythmic sloshing, like a giant’s bathtub.

“That’s it,” Mary whispered. She pointed. At the far end of the corridor maybe a hundred yards away, a heavier door stood ajar. A pale, silvery light pulsed from the gap.

They started down the corridor at a jog. Jesus’s sneakers squeaked on the damp concrete. His mind was a broken record: Rex’s tail flick. The silver probes. The green light. He’d left him. Again. The old wound, the Judas-shaped one, throbbed like a rotten tooth.

A new sound joined the sloshing. A high, chittering skitter. Like a thousand tiny claws on concrete.

They all froze.

The sound came from the vents. From the pipes overhead. From the grates in the floor.

“Rats?” Lola breathed.

“Big rats,” Sister Mercy said, hefting her broken nunchuck.

A grate to their left popped open. Not from force. The screws just… unscrewed themselves. They fell to the floor with tiny metallic pings. Something silver and wet flopped out.

It was a fish. A largemouth bass maybe ten inches long. Its scales gleamed with an unnatural, mercury-like sheen. One eye was milky white, blind. The other was a perfect, accusing black orb. It flopped on the concrete, gills flaring.

Then it arched its body and jumped. Not back toward the grate. Toward them.

It landed with a wet slap at Jesus’s feet, twitched once, and lay still. The silver sheen on its scales seemed to bleed into the damp concrete, leaving a faint, iridescent stain.

“Okay,” Mary said slowly. “That’s new.”

Another grate clanged open ahead of them. Then another. Silver fish poured out—bass, bluegill, even a small, iridescent carp. They didn’t flop helplessly. They propelled themselves across the floor in violent, jerking arcs, leaving slick trails behind. Their mouths gaped open and shut, not for water, but with a purpose. A hunger.

The chittering skitter became a cascade. From the open door of the vat chamber at the end of the hall, a wave of them spilled into the corridor. Not dozens. Hundreds. A flowing, squirming carpet of silver.

“The Fisherman protocol,” Jesus said, the memory clicking into place with cold clarity. The river. The mutated fish attacking the contractors. “Project Lazarus. They dumped the substrate. It’s in the water table.”

“And the facility’s coolant systems,” Mary finished, her face grim. “They’re in the pipes. They’re everywhere.”

The advancing tide of fish parted around a thicker pipe running along the wall. As they touched it, the silver sheen on their scales transferred to the metal. The pipe began to corrode, flaking with orange rust at an impossible speed. A seam burst with a hiss, spraying warm, chemical-smelling water.

An alarm began to blare overhead, a different tone than the studio—a deep, pulsing klaxon that vibrated in Jesus’s molars.

“They’re not attacking us,” Mary said, watching the fish chew through the infrastructure. “They’re attacking the building.”

A bass launched itself at a junction box. It stuck to the metal, sizzling, its body dissolving into a corrosive paste that ate through the casing. Sparks fountained, and the lights in their section of the hallway flickered and died, plunging them into near-darkness. Only the pulsing silver light from the vat chamber door remained.

“Well, hallelujah for small miracles,” Sister Mercy muttered. “The apocalyptic fish army is on our side.”

“They’re not on anyone’s side,” Jesus said. Water from the broken pipe was ankle-deep now, swirling around their feet. It was warm, and it carried the same metallic tang as the obedience serum. “They’re just… un-building. Like we wanted to do.”

Lola shrieked. A silvery eel, thin as a cable had dropped from a pipe overhead and wrapped around her calf. It didn’t bite. It just clung, its body searing her jeans with a smoking, acidic burn. Mary lunged, grabbing the thing and yanking. It came free with a tearing sound, thrashing in her grip before she hurled it against the wall. It stuck there, writhing, eating into the concrete.

“We need to move!” Mary yelled over the klaxon and the growing roar of rushing water. “This whole level is going to flood!”

The fish army was between them and the vat chamber door. The corridor behind them was now a churning, knee-deep stream of water and flashing silver scales. A fire suppression sprinkler head shorted out above them and unleashed a torrent of rusty water.

Jesus looked at the seething mass blocking their way. He looked at the nail in his hand. A plan, stupid and simple, formed. He didn’t like it.

“The water’s conductive,” he shouted to Mary. “And the fish are basically swimming batteries of corrupted Garden code.”

“So?”

“So we give them a common enemy.” He pointed the nail at the sparking junction box the fish had just destroyed. “A bigger signal.”

Mary’s eyes widened. She understood. “You’ll fry every circuit in the hall! Including the door locks!”

“You got a better idea?”

She didn’t. She grabbed Lola and Sister Mercy. “Back to the stairwell door! Now!”

They sloshed backwards, fighting the current. Jesus waited, the water rising to his thighs. The nail felt hot in his palm, humming with a low-grade resonance. He focused on the broken box, on the live wires spitting sparks into the flood. He thought about the river, about the fish turning on the men who poisoned them. He thought about Rex, a flawed, stubborn piece of original code holding back a manufactured god.

He didn’t pray. He just threw the nail.

It wasn’t a good throw. It tumbled, end over end, and slapped into the water about five feet short of the junction box.

For a second, nothing happened.

Then the world turned white and loud.

Every fish in the corridor convulsed at once, a synchronized seizure. The silver light from their scales flared, then inverted into a deep hungry black. The water around the nail erupted, not with heat, but with a shockwave of silent, negative energy. It raced through the flood, a dark ripple.

Where it touched the fish, they dissolved. Not into goo. Into a fine, black ash that swirled on the water’s surface.

Where it touched the pipes and wires, the corrosion accelerated explosively. Metal screamed as it twisted and failed. The klaxon died mid-wail. The remaining lights blew out with a series of pops, plunging the corridor into absolute blackness save for the eerie, ashen glow of the dissolving fish.

The shockwave hit Jesus. It didn’t burn. It was a cold so profound it felt like a void opening in his chest. It stole his breath. He stumbled, the current taking his legs out from under him.

A hand hooked under his arm, yanking him upright. Mary. “Dramatic,” she coughed, her face pale in the gloom. “Now can we please go?”

The way to the vat chamber was clear. The fish were gone, leaving only a swirling, ashy sludge on the water’s surface. The door at the end was still ajar, that silver light pulsing like a diseased heart.

But the stairwell door behind them, their only retreat was gone. Not blocked. One wall around it had partially collapsed, submerged under a churning waterfall from the broken floor above. The corridor had become a cul-de-sac, flooding fast.

They were trapped between a rising tide and the source of everything.

The water was up to Mary’s waist, warm as blood and stinking of ozone and fish rot. The black ash from the dissolved army coated the surface like industrial glitter. It stuck to her skin, itching.

“Okay,” she said, her voice tight. “New plan.”

“The old plan was ‘don’t get eaten by fish,’” Sister Mercy grumbled, trying to wring out her habit’s sleeve. “I liked the old plan.”

“The old plan is floating face-down.” Mary pointed toward the pulsing silver light. “That’s the only door left. We go through it.”

“Into the room that’s making the fish?” Lola’s eyes were huge in the gloom.

“They’re not making them. They’re attracting them. There’s a difference.” Mary started forward, pushing through the water. It dragged at her legs. Every step stirred up more of the ash, which swirled and then seemed to pull toward the vat chamber, like iron filings to a magnet.

Jesus followed, silent. He kept flexing the hand that had thrown the nail. Mary knew the look. He was counting exits in a room with no doors. His tell—that absent drumming of his fingers on his thigh—was going a mile a minute.

The water deepened as they neared the door. The sloshing sound was louder, a rhythmic glug-glug-thump that vibrated through the floor. The silver light pulsed in time.

Mary reached the door first, peered around the jamb, and felt her stomach drop.

It wasn’t a vat chamber. It was a server farm, or it had been. Row upon row of tall, black server racks stretched into the darkness, their status lights dark. But in the center of the room, where the raised floor had been torn up sat a massive, cylindrical tank. Glass, or something like it, cracked in a spiderweb pattern. Silver fluid churned inside, bubbling like a geyser.

The fluid was alive. Fish shapes—some recognizably bass or snook, others twisted into things with too many fins or eyes—boiled to the surface, pressed against the glass, and fell back into the churn. Each time the light pulsed, a dozen or more would vomit out of the top of the tank through a broken pipe, sliding down the side in a silver rush to join the flood on the floor.

The room was a foot deeper in water than the hall. The fish here didn’t dissolve. They schooled in lazy, menacing circles around the submerged server racks.

“It’s a relay,” Mary whispered. “Not a source. It’s amplifying the signal from the main substrate dump. Calling them home.”

A bluegill with a jagged, crystalline spine bumped against her leg. She jerked back.

“So we break the tank,” Jesus said.

“The tank is already broken. The signal’s coming from somewhere else. This is just the… the church bell.” She scanned the room. There had to be a terminal, a control node. Her eyes landed on a workstation, partially submerged, on a slightly higher platform near the far wall. Its monitor was dark, but a single, green power light blinked.

“There. I can maybe scramble the frequency, turn the call into noise.”

“You can ‘maybe’?” Sister Mercy said.

“It’s the only ‘maybe’ we’ve got.”

They waded in. The fish parted around them, uninterested. They were all swimming toward the tank, touching it, and then turning to swim toward the room’s other exit—a wide, arched conduit, once probably for cables, now vomiting a steady stream of water and silver shapes out into the deeper facility.

Mary reached the workstation. The keyboard was underwater. She sighed, plunged her hands in, and yanked it free. Water poured from the ports. She slammed it onto the desk, pulled a multi-tool from her belt, and started prying off the keycaps.

“What are you doing?” Lola asked.

“Shorting the trace lines. Making a static loop.”

A server rack three rows over groaned, then toppled. It fell in slow motion, hitting the water with a colossal splash. The fish near it scattered, then converged on the new wreckage. They didn’t chew the metal. They just… touched it. Where their silver-scaled sides brushed the rack, the metal pitted and softened, like salt on a slug. In seconds, the solid steel frame was Swiss cheese, collapsing in on itself.

“They’re not eating the building,” Jesus said, understanding dawning in his voice. “They’re un-making it. De-rezzing it. Back to base code.”

“Great. Poetic.” Mary popped off the last keycap. She used the tip of her tool to bridge two contacts on the circuit board. The monitor flickered. Lines of static scroll rolled across the screen.

A bass the size of her arm shot past her face, so close she felt the slipstream. It arrowed toward the cracked tank and didn’t stop. It slammed into the glass at full speed.

The crack didn’t spread. The glass around the impact point just… vanished. A perfect, smooth hole appeared. The pressurized silver fluid exploded outward in a thick, ropey jet.

Then it turned, and with a powerful flick of its tail, surged toward the remaining support column holding up the ceiling above them.
