The parking lot asphalt bubbled under AM-1's bulk like a bad toupee in a sauna. Jesus backpedaled, his heel catching on a pothole that smelled like old fryer grease and regret. The monster's face flickered through a dozen televangelists before settling on a disturbing hybrid—Billy Graham's jawline, Tammy Faye's mascara, and the unblinking stare of a department store mannequin.

“Obey,” it repeated, the word vibrating Jesus's fillings.

Mary racked her shotgun. “Eat holy lead, you overgrown karaoke screen!”

The blast tore through AM-1's chest, scattering shards of cathode-ray glass. The monster looked down at the hole, then at Mary. The screens across its skin switched to a clip of Jimmy Swaggart fake-sobbing.

Rex's tail twitched. “Told you we should've gone with the eating-people option.”

A shadow moved in the strip mall's busted-out window. A man stepped through the jagged glass like it was a bead curtain, holding what appeared to be a garage door opener duct-taped to a car battery. He had the sunburned complexion of a tourist who'd mistaken SPF 5 for a suggestion and the posture of someone who'd spent years ducking under low-flying bad decisions.

“Y'all mind?” He nodded at AM-1. “I got a warranty claim on this unit.”

Jesus blinked. “Clay?”

Clay pressed the garage door opener. AM-1 spasmed, its screen-face glitching through a rapid-fire montage of televised faith healers mid-scandal. The monster clutched its head and emitted a sound like a dial-up modem drowning in holy water.

Clay adjusted the battery pack. “See, the problem with you corporate messiah types is the planned obsolescence. Built-in failure points.” He tapped a sequence into the keypad. “Like this little subroutine I found in the Eden servers. Call it… practical mercy.”

AM-1 froze mid-roar. Its screens flickered once, twice, then displayed a single phrase in Comic Sans: REBOOTING FOR YOUR SPIRITUAL CONVENIENCE.

Mary kept her shotgun trained on the monster. “What the hell did you do?”

“Gave it a choice.” Clay wiped sweat off his forehead with the sleeve of his “I ❤️ Judas” t-shirt. “Turns out even factory-default messiahs got enough free will to pick 'cancel subscription.'”

The monster's shoulders slumped. Its screen-face switched to a test pattern. When it spoke again, the voice was softer, like a late-night infomercial host trying to sell you salvation on easy payments.

“Would… would you like to hear about our lord and savior's extended warranty?”

Rex's eye twitched. “I hate everything about this.”

Clay grinned. His left canine was capped with what looked like a tiny chrome cross. “Stick with me, big guy. I got a biotech lab to hit before happy hour.”

Jesus stared at the docile AM-1, now absently picking at its barbed-wire stitches. The nail in his pocket throbbed, not a warning, but a question.

Mary lowered her shotgun by degrees. “This your idea of divine intervention?”

“Nah.” Clay patted the monster's pixelated knee. AM-1 whimpered and displayed a screensaver of tropical fish. “This is just good customer service.”

A shopping cart rolled past, pushed by the swamp breeze. Somewhere in the distance, a car alarm played the chorus of “Jesus Take the Wheel.”

Rex sighed. “We're gonna regret this.”

Clay winked. “Probably. But think of the story you'll tell.”

The monster hiccuped and started playing “Amazing Grace” on a loop through its static.

Jesus pinched the bridge of his nose. The smell of burning ozone mixed with the lingering stench of deep-fried heresy. Some days, it was hard to tell the difference between a miracle and a really bad decision.

“Lab first,” he said. “Then we talk about your shirt.”

Clay's grin widened. The chrome cross caught the sunlight, flashing like a holy strobe light.

AM-1 belched out a cloud of static that formed the shape of a thumbs-up.

The engine coughed to life. The radio tuned itself to a station playing nothing but Creed songs.

Rex groaned and thunked his head against the window. “Worst. Rescue. Ever.”

Clay adjusted the rearview mirror, which was framed by a plastic crown of thorns air freshener. “Stick around, lizard king. The fun's just starting.”

The van peeled out, leaving AM-1 waving goodbye in the parking lot, its screens now showing nothing but a serene loop of puppies in heaven.

Jesus leaned his head against the window. The glass vibrated against his temple like a poorly tuned church organ.

He had just acquired the world's most heretical ally.

Rex was absolutely right, they were going to regret this.

The van hit a pothole. The crown of thorns air freshener swung wildly, dripping tiny plastic blood droplets onto the dashboard.

Mary caught Jesus's eye and mouthed a single word: “Why?”

He shrugged. The nail in his pocket hummed in agreement.

Some questions didn't have answers. Just really, really bad decisions.

The van's suspension bottomed out as Clay swerved onto the overgrown service road, crushing a NO TRESPASSING sign under tires that had last seen proper tread during the Reagan administration. The place looked less like a cutting-edge research facility and more like a meth lab designed by someone who'd watched too much Nova.

“Practical mercy,” Rex muttered. His tail twitched, knocking over an empty can of sacramental wine rolling around the floorboards. “That's what we're calling it now?”

Clay adjusted the crown of thorns air freshener with the reverence of a bishop adjusting his mitre. “Better than your plan, which as I recall involved biting its head off and—what was the phrase?—'seeing if it still preached with a spine in its teeth.'”

Mary kicked open the passenger door before the van fully stopped. “If we're doing this, we're doing it quiet.” She tossed a glare over her shoulder.

“Poetic justice?” Rex offered.

“—setting anything on fire.”

The lab's front doors were sealed with a chain and a padlock the size of a hymnal. Jesus squinted at it. “You got a bolt cutter, or are we praying this open?”

Clay produced a key from his pocket. It had “PROPERTY OF EDEN WORLD” stamped on the bow in Comic Sans.

Rex's eye ridges lifted. “You just carry that around?”

“Found it in a Cracker Jack box.” Clay jiggled the key. “Also, I may have looted a supply closet in '79. Details are fuzzy—there was a lot of sacramental wine involved.”

The lock clicked open. The chain slithered to the ground with a sound like a snake shedding its skin. Inside, the lab was a time capsule of bad decisions, yellowed safety posters, a coffee mug labeled WORLD'S OKAYEST BIOCHEMIST, and a faint hum of electricity that set Rex's teeth on edge. The air smelled like ozone and the sharp metallic tang of bad ideas.

Mary swept her flashlight beam across a bank of monitors. “Let's make this quick. Find anything about AM-1's core programming and get out.”

Rex's claws clicked against the linoleum as he moved toward a filing cabinet labeled MIRACLES: REFURBISHED/NON-RETURNABLE. His tail flicked twice—a tell he'd never admit to, as he yanked the drawer open. Inside were folders labeled in the same cheerful font as a Sunday school workbook: LOAVES & FISHES v3.2 PATCH NOTES, WATER-TO-WINE CONVERSION RATES (Q2 1986), and, inexplicably, JONAH'S WHALE: LEASE AGREEMENT.

Jesus held up a floppy disk labeled SERMON ON THE MOUNT: DIRECTOR'S CUT. “These people are heretics.”

“Heretics with dental,” Clay said, prying open a panel in the wall. Behind it, a nest of wires pulsed with a faint blue light. “Bingo. Mainframe access.” He wiggled his fingers like a televangelist warming up for a healing session. “Somebody get me a paperclip and a bad decision.”

Rex's vision narrowed. The wires hummed at a frequency that made his jaw ache, a sound just shy of a hymn, just this side of a scream. His programming twitched in response, some subroutine he'd buried deep kicking like a fetus in a womb. He drummed his claws against his thigh. “This is a mistake.”

Mary didn't look up from the monitor she was hacking. “Noted. Now hand me that screwdriver.”

Clay whistled as he worked, the tune suspiciously close to “Nearer my God to Thee.” The panel sparked. A monitor flickered to life, displaying a login screen with a pixelated Jesus winking. The password field blinked.

“Anybody know the name of Judas's childhood pet?” Clay asked.

Rex's tail slammed into a chair, sending it skidding across the room. “We're not doing this.”

Everyone turned.

Jesus raised an eyebrow. “Doing what?”

“This.” Rex gestured at the room, the wires, the floppy disks full of sacrilege. “Sneaking around like thieves. Playing with systems we don't understand. That thing back there—” He jabbed a claw toward the parking lot, where AM-1 was presumably still watching puppy videos. “—it's not fixed. It's just confused.”

“Like what?” Clay tilted his head. “Like somebody did to you?”

The air left Rex's lungs.

An alarm blared, a sound like Gabriel's trumpet filtered through a kazoo. Red lights strobed. Mary cursed and yanked a cable from the monitor.

OBEDIENCE HORMONE SYNTHESIS: 78% COMPLETE

“Time to go,” Jesus said, already moving toward the door.

Clay hesitated, his fingers hovering over the keyboard. For a second, Rex thought he might try to type something, a prayer, a curse, a final middle finger to Eden World. Then the ceiling tiles rattled, and whatever moment Clay was having passed. He grabbed a handful of floppy disks and bolted.

Rex was the last out. As he turned, a radio on the desk crackled to life.

“—serpent static confirms linkage to primary assets. Repeat, THORN-7 and REX-9 protocols are synchronized—”

The words hit like a punch to the ribs. REX-9. He'd never heard the designation before, but it settled into his bones like a hymn he'd known all his life.

Mary grabbed his arm. “Move!”

They ran. The van's engine roared to life as they piled in, tires spitting gravel. Rex twisted in his seat, watching the lab shrink in the distance. The radio, still tuned to Creed cut out for a second.

“With arms wide open… under the sky… of AM-1…”

Clay white-knuckled the wheel. “Okay. That's new.”

Rex stared at his claws. They trembled, just slightly, just enough to notice. Somewhere in the static, in the hum of wires, in the echo of that voice, something had shifted.

And for the first time in his existence, Rex wasn't sure if it was his programming or his soul.

He didn't shrug. He didn't joke. He just watched the lab disappear into the swamp, the word REX-9 ringing in his skull like a bell.
