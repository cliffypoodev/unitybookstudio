The air in the chamber held a chill, the kind that settled in stone and clung to robes. It smelled of cedar oil and old parchment, a dry, scholarly scent Touch found comforting. The High Priest’s hand moved with a slow, deliberate grace, dipping the stylus into the pot of ink. The scratch of bronze on papyrus was the only sound.

Touch watched the letters form: To the synagogues in Damascus…

His breath felt tight in his chest, a clean, sharp pressure. This was not anxiety. It was focus, a narrowing of the world to this moment, this desk, this line of Aramaic script granting him authority. The lamplight pooled on the polished wood, glinting off the silver seal ring on the High Priest’s finger. A bee was carved into its face, the symbol of the Hasmoneans. Touch fixed on that detail, the tiny, delicate wings. It was easier than looking at the man himself.

“You understand the scope, Saul of Tarsus.”

His voice was softer than Touch expected. It didn’t match the office. Touch had imagined something thunderous, a voice to shake the Temple veil. This was a teacher’s voice, worn smooth from use.

“I do, my lord.”

“These people,” the High Priest said, still writing, “do not merely preach a crucified messiah. That is folly, and folly can be ignored. They preach a messiah who fulfilled the Law. Who renders it… complete.” He lifted the stylus. A drop of black ink hung from its tip, then fell, blooming on a scrap of blotting parchment. “That is a different matter. That is a knife at the throat of the covenant.”

Touch nodded. The argument was precise. He had made it himself, in the synagogues, in the council. Stephen had made it too, right up until the stones hit his face. A memory arrived unbidden—not Stephen’s screams, but the quiet before, the way the light had caught the dust motes above the crowd. The irrelevant detail snagged in Touch’s mind. He pushed it aside.

“They seek to unravel the very fabric of our observance,” Touch said. His own voice sounded too loud in the quiet room. “To replace devotion with a single confession. It is a corruption. It must be cut out.”

The High Priest finally looked up. His eyes were a pale, watery brown, the skin around them etched deep. He studied Touch not as a zealot, but as a tool, assessing the edge, the balance, the grip.

“Damascus is not Jerusalem,” he said. “The Nabateans hold the city loosely. The Roman garrison looks the other way, so long as taxes are paid and the streets are quiet. Our authority there is… persuasive. Not absolute. You will carry this”—he tapped the drying document with a knuckle—“and you will present it. But the true work will be done in the synagogues, with the elders. You must convince them. Your reputation precedes you. That will help.”

Your reputation. The young Pharisee, present at the execution, fervent in the purge. Touch felt a flush, a heat at the base of his neck. Pride. He let it be pride.

“I will not fail.”

A faint, almost imperceptible sigh escaped the High Priest. He shifted in his seat, the leather of the cushion sighing in response. He pulled a small bronze box toward him and opened it. Inside was a cake of deep red wax, the color of old wine, and a thick candle in a simple clay holder.

“We will seal it now.”

He lit the candle from the lamp’s flame. The new light wavered, then steadied. The smell changed—hot tallow now, layered over the cedar and ink. He held the cake of wax over the flame. Touch watched it soften, a glossy pool forming at its base. His mouth was dry. He should have asked for water before entering.

The wax dripped onto the papyrus, just below the final line of script. A fat, red bead. The High Priest pressed the signet ring into it, holding it there for a three-count. The sizzle of hot wax meeting cold stone was a tiny, final sound.

He lifted the ring. The impression was perfect. The bee, captured in miniature, wings spread as if frozen in flight. The authority of the Temple, physical, tangible, in a circle of wax no larger than a denarius.

He handed Touch the parchment. The wax was still warm, a faint heat bleeding through to his fingers. Touch took it. The weight was negligible, just fiber and ink. The weight he felt was something else entirely. It sat in his stomach like a stone from the brook Kidron.

“Go with the blessing of the Temple,” the High Priest said. But his eyes had already left Touch, drifting back to other scrolls on his desk. The audience was over. The tool was forged and handed over. Touch’s fervor suddenly felt too bright, too garish for this quiet, administrative room.

Touch bowed and turned. His sandals whispered on the mosaic floor, a geometric pattern of black and white stones. He’d never noticed it on the way in. One white tile was chipped at the corner.

The walk through the anteroom was a blur of hushed voices and averted gazes from the scribes. Then he was out, into the colonnade that led to the outer courts. The spring air hit him, cool and damp, carrying the distant, complex smell of the city: baking bread, animal dung, smoke, humanity. The contrast was a shock. The sealed document in his hand felt alien, a piece of that sterile, solemn chamber brought out into the messy, breathing world.

Touch looked down at it. The red seal glared up like a blind eye.

To the synagogues in Damascus. Greetings.

The words were standard. The meaning beneath them was not. He was to find men and women. He was to bind them. He was to bring them back to Jerusalem for judgment. The mechanics of it were simple. The rightness of it was absolute.

Then why did his hand feel so cold?

A group of pilgrims passed, talking excitedly in Greek about the afternoon sacrifice. One of them, a man with a gray-streaked beard, glanced at the scroll in Touch’s hand, at his Pharisee’s robes. His expression didn’t change, but his eyes held Touch’s for a half second too long before he looked away. There was no accusation there. Just a kind of weary recognition. He’d seen sealed documents before. He knew what they often preceded.

The flicker of doubt was a gnat, buzzing at the edge of Touch’s vision. He crushed it.

This was divine service. This was purification. The Law was a fire, and Touch was its bearer. If his hands were scorched in the carrying, it was a small price. Stephen’s peaceful face in the storm of stones was not a condemnation. It was a mystery, a trick of the light, a heresy so profound it could mimic grace.

He tightened his grip on the parchment. The wax seal faced outward, a declaration. He began to walk, his steps finding their rhythm on the paving stones. Each footfall was a punctuation mark. On to the Damascus Gate. On to the road. On to the work.

The sun was climbing, a pale coin behind the high cloud. The journey would take a week maybe more, if the spring rains swelled the fords. Touch had letters to deliver. He had a purpose that filled him, a certainty that was armor against the chill, against the memory of chipped tiles and weary eyes.

He did not look back.

The courtyard was a basin of noise and motion after the tomb-quiet of the inner offices. Touch stood on the top step, letting the sun press against his shoulders, the sealed scroll a rigid spine against his forearm. His men were waiting below, a knot of ten in the shifting crowd of merchants, pilgrims, and Levites. They saw him, and the chatter died. They straightened from where they leaned against the wall. That small, immediate obedience was a warmth in his chest, more real than the High Priest’s distant blessing.

He descended the steps. The dust here was thick, ground from stone and sandal, and it coated the hem of his robe. His second, Micah met him at the bottom. He was a barrel of a man from Joppa, with hands that could crack walnuts and a mind that followed orders like a well-trained dog. He smelled of garlic and old sweat.

“It is done?” Micah asked, his eyes on the scroll.

“It is done.” Touch didn’t hand it to him. The authority was his to carry. “We leave within the hour. Are the mounts ready?”

“The boys are seeing to them. Supplies are packed.” Micah wiped his forehead with the back of a thick wrist. “The Damascus road is clear. No reports of bandits this week.”

“We are not concerned with bandits.”

Micah blinked, then nodded, understanding. Their quarry was not the kind that lurked in rocks. They hid in plain sight, in houses, in back rooms of synagogues. They were teachers, widows, rope-makers. Their weapon was an idea. Touch had told him this many times. Micah remembered the words, but Touch saw the way his hand still went to the dagger at his belt when he thought of danger. He imagined a fight he could win with his hands.

The others gathered around. Young faces, mostly. Zealots from the Greek-speaking synagogues, their Aramaic accented, their fervor a bright, brittle thing. Touch saw the excitement in their eyes, the eagerness for a mission that carried the Temple’s seal. They saw a grand purge, a righteous hunt. They did not see the ledgers, the quiet conversations, the specific names carried in memory. Judah the baker. Miriam of the house of Tobias. They were not phantoms. They were people who baked bread and mended cloaks and believed a dangerous thing.

“Gather close,” Touch said. His voice cut through the courtyard’s din. A donkey brayed somewhere, a long, rasping complaint.

They formed a half-circle. Touch unrolled the document just enough for the seal to be visible, the red wax catching the sun. He did not need to read it. The symbol was the message. “By the authority of the High Priest, we are commissioned to go to Damascus. Our purpose is to root out the followers of the Nazarene, to find them in their gatherings, to bind them, to bring them to justice here, in Jerusalem.”

A young one named Elazar, barely eighteen, with a wispy attempt at a beard, leaned forward. “Will the Nabateans hinder us?”

“The Nabateans care for taxes and trade routes. They will not lift a finger for a handful of Jewish sectarians.” Touch kept his tone flat, instructional. “Our work is with the synagogue elders.”
