The courtyard was his. The fig tree, the pale dust, the slanting light. That silence was immense. Not peaceful. A silence of a house after the mourners had left. A silence full of absence. Touch was alone. Not just alone in the courtyard. Alone in the world. One wall he had lived inside all his life had just shown him its outer face. Smooth, high, without handhold. On the other side, he could hear the life of his people continuing—the prayers, the debates, the shared certainties. He was outside. He had been put there with careful, reasonable words. A traitor. A madman. Unclean. The heat in his chest had cooled into a hard, heavy lump. It sat just below his ribs. It had no name. It was the weight of being unknown. Of having a truth inside him that, when spoken made him a stranger to everyone who had ever known him. A breeze stirred the fig leaves. It carried the scent of baking bread from a nearby street. An ordinary smell. A human smell. Not yet. Touch would not be silenced.

The sun dipped lower. Long shadows stretched across the packed earth. Touch moved from the cistern, the water still cool on his fingertips, and found the door to his small room. Less a room than a cell. Four walls, a straw mat, a clay lamp he had not lit since Ananias left. The window was high, a slit in the stone, letting in the last, fading light. Dust motes danced in the slanted beams, oblivious to the day’s wreckage.

His tunic lay crumpled on the mat. Touch picked it up, the rough wool familiar against his skin. It still smelled faintly of the road, of sweat and desert and something else, something sharp and metallic he couldn’t place. His fingers brushed against the inner pocket, where he had tucked the parchment. The reason he had come to Damascus. The instrument of his zeal.

He pulled it out. The papyrus crackled, dry and brittle. The High Priest’s seal, a crimson smear of wax was broken. It had shattered when he fell on the road, a tiny prophecy he had ignored. The parchment was thin, translucent in the weak light. His own handwriting covered it in tight, precise Hebrew script. A list.

Each name was a hammer blow.

Touch held it, a dead thing in his hand. It felt heavier than stone, lighter than ash. He walked to the window, the faint breeze a cool whisper against his face. The street below was quiet. A dog barked once, far off. The scent of baking bread was fainter now, replaced by the dry smell of dust and sun-baked stone.

The names. Touch traced the first with his thumb. Jorah bar Judah. A weaver from the Jewish quarter in Jerusalem. He remembered him. Jorah had a limp, a quiet man who always sat near the back of the synagogue. He spoke little, but his eyes, Touch remembered had held a steady, unsettling light when he spoke of the Messiah. Touch had seen it as defiance.

His stomach tightened. What had he intended for Jorah? To drag him from his loom? To shame him before his neighbors? To bring him back to Jerusalem in chains? The questions clawed at his throat. His zeal had been so pure, so unwavering. It had been a fire that consumed everything in its path, sparing no one, not even him.

Touch moved down the list. Miriam bat Eleazar. A widow. Her husband had died two years ago. Stephen had visited her often, bringing her food, sitting with her in the evenings. Touch remembered seeing him there, the quiet presence of his grace a sharp contrast to his own righteous fury. Miriam, who had nothing left but her faith. What right had he to take that from her?

His breath hitched. The parchment trembled in his hand. Each name on the list was a person, a life, a family. Not just a follower of the Way, not just a heretic. A human being. Touch had reduced them to a legalistic problem, a stain to be removed from the purity of the Law.

His gaze drifted to the street below. A woman passed, her head covered, a basket on her arm. She hummed a low tune. A child chased a stray cat. Life continued, oblivious to the small, dark piece of papyrus in his hand, oblivious to the names etched into its surface. Names Touch had once meant to destroy.

The light faded further, painting the room in shades of gray and bruised purple. The air grew cooler. Touch thought of the synagogue, of Malchus's eyes, cold and certain. Malchus had understood. He had seen the infection, the rot Touch now carried. He had seen Touch as unclean. He was right.

Touch had been so certain then. So utterly, blindingly certain. The Law, a shield. His actions, a sword. He had wielded them both with a conviction that brooked no argument, tolerated no dissent. And now? The shield lay shattered, the sword broken. All that remained was the wreckage.

The parchment felt like a living thing, squirming in his grip. Touch could burn it. Tear it. Bury it in the desert. But the names would still be there, burned into the tablets of his memory. They were part of him now. Part of the man he had been, and part of the man he was trying to become.

Touch lowered the list. His eyes closed. The faces of the people on the parchment swam before him, replacing the dancing dust motes. Jorah, the quiet weaver. Miriam, the grieving widow. And Stephen, his face illuminated not by a fire of hatred, but by something else entirely. Something Touch had failed to see.

A different kind of fire now burned in his chest. Not the cleansing flame of the Law, but a hot, searing shame. It tasted like ash. It smelled of regret. It felt like a new burden he had to carry.

Touch opened his eyes. The room was almost dark. Only a sliver of light from the high window remained, a thin blade cutting across the wall. He looked at the parchment again. One name, near the bottom stood out. Simon bar Jonah. A fisherman from Galilee. Touch remembered him too. He had been a loud man, full of laughter, always arguing. He had followed Jesus from the beginning. Touch had seen him in Jerusalem, listening to Stephen preach. He had stood near the front, nodding, his face alight.

His thumb traced the letters of his name. Simon. A fisherman. One of the first. Touch had meant to bring him to justice. To silence him. Now his name on this list was a question. A challenge. An invitation, perhaps.

The oil lamp was a small, sputtering heart in the gloom. Touch had lit it without thinking, his hands moving on the memory of a thousand evenings spent studying. The flame guttered, painting the walls with frantic, leaping shadows. It made the names on the parchment seem to move.

Simon bar Jonah. The fisherman's name was a hook in Touch's mind. He could hear his voice, a Galilean accent thick as tar, arguing with a Temple guard near the Dung Gate. “You look for life among the dead!” Simon had shouted. One guard had shoved him back, but Simon had laughed, a rough, barking sound that held no fear. Touch had watched from across the street, his robes marking him as a scholar, a man of authority. Their eyes had met for a second. Simon's were a startling blue, crinkled at the corners from squinting at sun on water. He hadn't looked away. He'd given Touch a slow, deliberate nod, as if they shared a secret. Touch had taken it as insolence. Now, in this darkening room, it felt like a warning he'd been too blind to read.

The lamp smoked. A thin, black thread of it rose and twisted in the still air. It smelled of rancid fat and forgotten things.

Touch's finger moved down the list. Lydia, seller of purple. From Thyatira. A businesswoman with a household. Touch had never met her, only heard reports. She had opened her home to the followers. A practical woman, they said. She would have calculated the risk. Touch had calculated it for her. The warrant authorized the seizure of her goods, the dissolution of her household. A financial ruin, neatly justified by scripture. His script.

A cold knot settled in his gut. It was a precise, legal cold. This was the work Touch had excelled at. Not the violence in the streets—that was for others. His violence was cleaner. It happened on parchment. It used the Law's own language to strip a person of community, of livelihood, of dignity. He had been so proud of its precision. A surgeon's knife. Now the blade was turned, and he was feeling its edge. The cold spread upward, tightening his throat.

A memory came unbidden. Not of Lydia, but of another house. A smaller one, in the lower city. The smell of cumin and burnt lentils. A child's toy, a carved wooden bird left on the threshold.

They had come at first light. Touch was there to observe, to ensure the procedure followed protocol. One man's name was Ezra. A potter. His crime was hosting a gathering. He stood in the doorway, his hands covered in dry gray clay, as the guards read the warrant. His wife hovered behind him, her fingers clutching at the edge of her shawl. She wasn't weeping. Her face was a mask of furious confusion. “On whose testimony?” Ezra had asked, his voice low.

“The testimony is sealed,” Touch had replied. His own voice sounded foreign to him now, a flat, administrative instrument.

“You take my home on a secret word?”

“We uphold the Law.”

Ezra had looked at Touch then, really looked. His eyes traveled from Touch's phylacteries to his face. He didn't see a zealot. He saw a clerk. A man who destroyed lives with ink. His shoulders slumped, not in defeat, but in a profound, weary disgust. He turned and walked back inside, leaving them at the door. A guard looked to Touch. He gave a curt nod. They went in.

Touch had stayed outside. His job was done. From within came the sounds of a quiet, efficient disaster—the scrape of a table being moved, the clatter of a cooking pot, the sharp cry of the wife, quickly stifled. Touch had focused on the child's toy on the threshold. One wing was chipped. He remembered the grain of the wood, the clumsy attempt at feathers. He had nudged it with his sandal. It rolled into the dust.

The lamp flame shuddered. A wave of heat, then a retreat. A shadow of his own hand on the parchment looked like a claw.

Ezra's face merged with Simon's, with Jorah's. They were all here, in this room. Their silence was louder than Malchus's condemnation. Touch had made them homeless in the name of a God he thought demanded purity. Now he was the one outside the walls. The irony was a physical taste, coppery and foul, at the back of his tongue.

Touch scanned further. Tabitha. A name near the bottom. From Joppa. A disciple known for good works and acts of charity. She made tunics for widows. That was her entire entry in his notes. Widows' seamstress. Influential.

His breath stopped.

The parchment slipped from his fingers. It didn't fall. It seemed to hang in the air for a moment, a pale leaf, before fluttering to the mat beside the lamp.

Not Tabitha.

Dorcas. The Greeks called her Dorcas.

Touch knew her. Not from reports. From the street.

In Jerusalem, weeks before the business with Stephen. A minor disturbance near the Pool of Siloam. A group of them were there, helping a crippled man. She was among them, a small woman with hair the color of iron, pulled back in a severe bun. She was arguing with a Levite who was trying to move them along. Her voice was low, but it carried. It wasn't the strident tone of a prophet. It was the firm, unyielding voice of a woman used to managing a household, used to getting things done.

“The man needs water,” she was saying. “Your law permits mercy on the Sabbath does it not? Or have you bound the compassion of the Almighty with your own cords?”

The Levite sputtered. Touch had stepped forward. His presence quieted the scene. The Levite deferred. The followers shrank back, all but the crippled man and the woman. Dorcas. She turned to look at him.

Her eyes were a calm, clear gray. She looked at his robes, his bearing. She saw the authority. And she did not look away. She did not cower. A smudge of soot, or perhaps charcoal from a tailor's chalk, marked her cheekbone. She held a folded tunic in her arms, plain linen.

“Saul of Tarsus,” Touch had said. “You are causing a disruption.”

“The only disruption is to his thirst,” she said, nodding to the man. “We will move when he is able.”

Her defiance was quiet, absolute. It infuriated Touch. Rooted in a practicality that dismissed his theological authority as irrelevant. Here was a woman who believed her God was concerned with cripples and tunics, not with the fine points of debate in the shade of the colonnades. Her faith was a tool, like her needle. It mended things.

“You test the patience of the Council,” Touch said, his voice dropping into a warning.

She studied his face then. Not with hatred, not with fear. With a piercing, sorrowful scrutiny. As if she were measuring him for a garment and finding the dimensions profoundly sad. She saw the zeal, the rigid certainty. And she pitied it.

“The Council does not own God's patience,” she said softly. Then she turned, dismissing him, and bent to help the man drink.

The memory was a shard of glass in Touch's mind. He had ordered her name added to the list that very afternoon. A footnote. Dorcas (Tabitha). Joppa. Incorrigible. Influence among the poor. He had meant to shatter that practical, mending faith. To still those hands that sewed for widows.

Where was she now? In Joppa, still sewing? Had she heard of his conversion? Would she believe it? Or would she hear his name preached in their gatherings and remember the proud young Pharisee by the pool, his soul a clenched fist? Would she pity that, too?

This thought was unbearable.

Touch looked at the parchment where it had fallen. The lamplight caught the edge, making it glow like a brand. Dorcas's name seemed to pulse in the shadow. That smudge of chalk on her cheek. The weight of the tunic in her arms. The unassailable, quiet certainty in her gray eyes.

His hands began to shake. A tremor started deep in his wrists, a vibration that traveled up his arms. It was as if the memory had a physical current. The shame was no longer a hot coal in his chest. It was a live wire, sparking under his skin.

Touch had not just persecuted a sect. He had targeted a woman who clothed widows. A potter who lost his home. A fisherman who laughed in the face of guards. He had traded in lives, and his currency had been holy script.

The room tilted. The shadows on the wall swarmed, coalescing into shapes—a chipped wooden bird, a Galilean's crinkled eyes, a smudge of chalk on a weathered cheek. The air grew thick, difficult to pull into his lungs. It tasted of smoke and dry papyrus and the ghost of cumin from a threshold he had defiled.

His gaze locked on Dorcas's name. That single word, written in his own meticulous hand became a window. Through it, he saw not just her face, but the chain of faces behind her, a procession of the damned stretching back to Stephen and forward into a future he had meant to carve with warrants and seals. Their eyes were all on him. Not accusing. Just present. A cloud of witnesses to his former certainty.

A sound escaped him—a sharp, choked gasp. His hand jerked back from the parchment as if the papyrus itself had burst into flame. The recoil was violent, instinctive. His elbow struck the clay lamp.

It toppled. The flame died midair, swallowed by darkness. The hot oil spilled across the mat with a hiss, the smell suddenly overwhelming.

In the absolute black, the afterimage of her face remained. Carved in defiance. Cut in pity.

Touch was on his feet. He couldn't see his own hands in front of him. The darkness was complete, a tangible pressure. He stumbled back, his shoulder hitting the cold stone wall. The only sound was the ragged saw of his own breath and the slow, final drip of oil onto the dirt floor.

The dark was a solid thing. It filled his mouth, his lungs. Touch pressed his back against the wall, the rough stone digging through his tunic. The smell of spilled oil was a greasy curtain. His breath came in shallow, useless pulls. Dorcas's face wouldn't fade. That smudge of chalk. The pity in her eyes—it was worse than hatred. Hatred he understood. It was a mirror. Pity was a window into a room he could never enter.

They would all look at him like that, if they ever saw him again.

Jorah the weaver. Miriam the widow. Simon the fisherman. They were not abstract heresies. They were people with memories, with eyes that had seen him in his robes, his certainty, his power. They had felt the shadow of his authority fall across their doorways. Would they see a converted man? Or would they see the shadow?

His knees gave. Touch slid down the wall until he was sitting in the grit, the cool of the packed earth seeping through his wool. The dark was a mercy. It hid his hands, which wouldn't stop trembling. It hid his face. But inside, the dark was a theater, and the play was every encounter he had ever forced.

Simon's laugh by the Dung Gate. That nod. He had seen the clerk in Touch, too. Had he seen the fear underneath? The frantic need to be right that fueled all that precision? Simon was a fisherman. He knew about hooks. Had he sensed the barb in Touch's own soul, the one he was caught on?

A sliver of moonlight found the high window. It cut a pale blade across the floor, missing the spilled oil by a hand's breadth. It illuminated motes of dust, still dancing. The world went on, indifferent. In Jerusalem, Malchus was probably asleep, content in his purity. In Joppa, Dorcas had likely put away her needle for the night. And here Touch was, a man dissolving in the dark of a borrowed room, undone by a list.

He had thought his confession to Ananias was the end of it. He had spoken his crimes to a stranger who spoke with God's voice. He had been baptized. The scales had fallen from his eyes. A neat story. A closed ledger.

But the ledger was open. It was here, a scent of rancid oil and a phantom weight in his hand. Forgiveness from heaven was one thing. Vertical, a column of light. But the earth was horizontal, a network of faces, of memories he had poisoned. Could grace travel that route? Could it reach the potter's ruined threshold, the widow's silence, the chalk smudge on a seamstress's cheek?

His throat tightened. A dry, soundless sob racked his chest. It was a child's feeling, this helpless terror. Touch was seven years old again in Tarsus lost in the cedar market, surrounded by the legs of strangers, unable to speak their language. The same hollow panic. The same certainty that he would never be found.

Touch was lost. More lost than on the Damascus road. Then, he had only been blind. Now he could see, and the landscape was unrecognizable. Every path led to a face that knew his former self.

The moonlight crept, slow as a thief. It touched the edge of the fallen parchment. The papyrus glowed, a sickly white. Touch could make out the dark smear of spilled oil creeping toward it. In a moment, it would soak the fibers, blur the ink, ruin the names. A part of him screamed to let it happen. Let the oil consume it. A fitting end. Let the evidence be destroyed.

His hand moved without his consent. It shot out, fingers scraping on the gritty floor, and snatched the parchment back just as the oil's leading edge touched the corner. Touch held it away from the spill, his arm trembling with the effort. The paper was cool. Dry. Safe.

Why? Why save it?

The answer came not as a thought, but as a sensation. The weight of it in his hand was the only real thing in the room. It was an anchor. To burn it would be a lie. A second violence. An attempt to murder the memory. Touch could not heal a wound by pretending it had never been made. The scar was part of the flesh.

He brought the list closer to the moonlight. The oil had stained one corner, a dark bloom like a bruise. It framed Dorcas's name. Touch traced the letters with a filthy finger. The ink did not smudge. Permanent. As permanent as the look in her eyes.

They would know him.

The fear crystallized, sharp and cold. Fear of violence, though that was possible. Fear of that look. The recognition. The moment when their eyes—Jorah's, Miriam's, Simon's—would widen, then narrow, then shutter closed. The moment the message of a risen Messiah would hit the wall of his face and shatter. Touch would be a walking contradiction. A poison trying to act as an antidote. His very presence would taint the good news.

Touch could stay here. In this room, in this city. He could live in the shadows of the Nabatean quarter, learn a trade, vanish. Ananias had given him a place, not a mission. He could let the story of Saul of Tarsus end in a silent room with a spilled lamp. Let Paul—whoever he was—be stillborn.

The silence of that thought was worse than the dark. It was the silence of the cistern again, but deeper. A surrender. It would mean Malchus was right. The infection would be quarantined. The Law would be satisfied. The man who knew too much would quietly disappear.

A cockroach skittered across the moonlit strip. Its shell glistened. It moved with purpose, from one shadow to the next.

Touch was not a cockroach.

The anger that came then was a clean, hot spike. Not the old, righteous fury. Directed at himself, at the cowardice of the thought. Stephen had not hidden. He had stood before the Council, his face like an angel's, and spoken until the stones flew. Dorcas had not hidden. She had bent to give a cripple water while a Pharisee loomed over her. They had faced the consequences of their truth. Who was Touch to slink away from the consequences of his lie?

His lie. That was the heart of it. His entire life had been a performance of a truth he did not truly possess. He had persecuted them not from strength, but from a terror of the peace they seemed to have—a peace that mocked his endless striving. His zeal was the shrieking of a door hanging off one hinge, pretending to be a wall.

Touch opened the door.
