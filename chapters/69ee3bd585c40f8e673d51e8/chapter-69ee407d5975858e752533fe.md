The room smelled of dust and old leather. Not the clean, tanned scent of a workshop, but something stale, abandoned. A dry tickle at the back of his throat. Touch sat on a pallet of rough wool laid over a packed-earth floor, his back against a wall of crumbling mudbrick. His fingers traced the texture, the grit catching under his nails.

A shape moved in the darkness to his left. A rustle of fabric, the soft click of a ceramic bowl set down.

“You should drink.” Micah’s voice came from too low—he was crouching.

Touch turned his head toward the sound. The blackness didn’t change. “I don’t want it.”

“Your lips are cracked. You haven’t taken water since yesterday.”

The concern in his voice was a new kind of humiliation. Touch was now the object of his care, a burden to be managed. The letter in his inner tunic felt like a lump of baked clay against his ribs. Useless. A joke. He shook his head, a sharp, futile motion. The world tipped, a sickening lurch in the void. He braced his palms flat against the cool earth to keep from listing over.

Silence. Micah didn’t move. Touch could feel his stare on the side of his face.

“Leave it,” he said.

Micah stood. The floorboards near the doorway creaked under his weight. “Judas will return soon. He has gone to make inquiries.”

Inquiries. The word hung in the stuffy air. About a doctor? About the followers of the Way he had come to arrest? Touch’s skin prickled. He was a blind man in the house of a stranger, his purpose in this city now a secret he himself didn’t understand. His two companions were the only tether he had to a world he could no longer see. One of them, Elazar hadn’t spoken since they’d entered the city gate. Touch could feel his disgust like a heat on his skin, radiating from the corner where he’d slumped after depositing Touch’s pack.

“Where is Elazar?” Touch asked.

“By the window. Watching the street.”

“Tell him to stop. He draws attention.”

“He’s keeping watch, Saul.”

“A blind Pharisee and two Judean toughs in a leatherworker’s house on Straight Street.” Touch’s voice was a dry scrape. “What exactly is he watching for? The spectacle we’ve already become?”

A snort from the corner. Elazar. “I’m watching for the men you promised we’d drag out of these streets. Remember them? Or did the light burn that list from your mind too?”

“Elazar.” Micah’s warning was low.

“No. He needs to hear it. We left Jerusalem with a commission. With authority. Now we’re nursemaids in a stinking room hiding. What is the plan? Do we sit here until his sight returns? Or do we admit this is finished?”

The plan. Touch had maps in his head. Synagogue layouts, names, the house of a man called Judas where the local ringleaders might gather. All of it was still there, etched behind his eyes. But the map had no legend now. The names were just sounds. Judas. Ananias. Mary. Were they heretics or... something else? The voice on the road had rewired the meaning of every word.

I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting.

“It is finished,” he said, the words ash in his mouth.

Elazar barked a laugh, sharp and bitter. “Good. Then we go home. We tell the High Priest his chosen instrument broke in the sun.”

“We stay.” Touch forced the words out. “Until I understand.”

“Understand what? You heard a voice. You fell. Your eyes are cooked. What more is there?”

His hands curled into fists, the grit from the wall grinding into his palms. The physical pain was an anchor. “You didn’t hear it. You heard a noise.”

“I heard enough. I heard you ask its name. I heard you call it ‘Lord.’” He lowered the words to a venomous whisper. “You named the demon, Saul. You gave it a title.”

The air left the room. Micah’s intake of breath was a sharp hiss. “Enough. The man is sick.”

“He is apostate.”

The word spoken aloud in this confined space had a finality. A verdict. In the silence that followed, Touch heard the distant cry of a street vendor hawking melons. The ordinary world, going on.

“Get out,” he said.

“Gladly.” The shuffle of him standing, the angry stride across the creaking boards. The door opened—a rectangle of slightly less-solid blackness, a brief wash of cooler air smelling of dung and cooking oil—then shut with a firm thud.

The silence he left behind was thick and suffocating.

“He’s frightened,” Micah said after a moment. His voice was nearer. He’d moved back to the bowl. “We all are.”

“You should go with him.”

“My duty is to you.”

“Your duty,” Touch said, “was to assist an agent of the Temple. That agent no longer exists.”

Micah didn’t answer. Touch heard the slosh of water, the soft sound of him drinking from the bowl himself. His own thirst, a parched knot in his throat, clenched. He ignored it.

He slid down the wall until he was lying on the pallet, the rough wool scratching his neck. The black was absolute. It wasn’t like night. Night had depth, variation, distant pinpricks of light. This was a void. A seal pressed over his eyes. He brought his hand up, passed it a finger’s width from his face. Nothing. Not a shadow, not a blur. A perfect, meaningless dark.

His mind, unmoored from sight began to spin.

It went to Stephen. Not to the theology, the trial, the charges. It went to his hands. They had been clean. A teacher’s hands, stained with ink perhaps, but clean. As the first stone struck his shoulder, spinning him, those hands had come up. Not to shield his face. They’d opened, palms out, fingers spread. As if catching something. Or releasing it.

His face had been turned upward, away from the crowd, away from Touch, where he stood holding the cloaks. The sun was on his face. He’d been saying something. Touch hadn’t listened. He was watching the arc of the next stone, judging its weight, its velocity. A good throw. It caught him on the temple. A wet, wrong sound.

But now, in the black, Touch heard it. Not the sound of the stone. The sound of Stephen’s voice. Just before that stone.

It wasn’t a scream. It was a sigh. A name.

“Lord Jesus, receive my spirit.”

Then, later, crumpled, blood masking his features: “Do not hold this sin against them.”

The memory wasn’t a thought. It was a physical invasion. It unfolded behind his sealed eyes in perfect, terrible detail. The gray of the dust on Stephen’s tunic where he fell. The particular way a lock of his hair stuck to the wet streak on his cheek. The sandal of the man next to Touch, split at the sole. These useless, irrelevant details flooded in, vivid and unstoppable. He had not forgotten a single one. His mind had recorded it all, a scribe taking notes, while his heart approved the verdict.

Them. The throwers. The shouters. Me.

The sin was his. Stephen had forgiven it. He had forgiven Touch, even as Touch’s presence, his approval, his held cloaks made the next stone possible.

A sound escaped him—a choked gasp, like a man punched.

“Saul?”

Micah was at his side in an instant. His hand touched Touch’s shoulder.

Touch flinched away from the contact, rolling onto his side, facing the wall. His body curled tight. The sheer tonnage of what he had done pressed down, collapsing his lungs. This wasn’t guilt. Guilt was a ledger, a list of debits you could work to repay. This was a precipice. This was the ground giving way beneath every step he had ever taken.

“I’m blind,” he whispered, not to Micah, to the void.

“It may pass. The sun—”

“Not the sun.” The words were ragged, torn from a place deeper than his throat. “I am blind.”

Micah didn’t understand. He patted Touch’s shoulder, an awkward, helpless gesture. “Rest. I will be here.”

He retreated. Touch heard the scrape of a stool, his weight settling onto it by the door. A sentry for a ruined man.

The image of Stephen’s hands, open, palms out would not leave. They weren’t defending. They were receiving. And giving.

Touch had come to Damascus to bind hands. To close them. To shackle that dangerous, forgiving spirit in chains and drag it back to Jerusalem. The voice on the road had asked why. The answer was here, in this memory. He persecuted him because that forgiveness was a fire that would burn down every wall he’d built. The Law was a wall, stone by careful stone. His grace was a flood. Touch had to stop it.

And he had failed.

A new sound seeped into his awareness. Not the street. A low murmur of voices, just outside the door to his room. Micah’s stool creaked as he stood. One voice was his, tense, questioning. The other was unfamiliar. Older. Softer. The words were indistinct, but the tone was calm, measured.

Touch’s body went rigid. He pushed himself up to sit, his ears straining.

The conversation lasted a half-dozen heartbeats. Then the door opened.

“Saul,” Micah said, his voice strangled with a confusion Touch had never heard in him before. “There is a man here. Ananias. He says... he says he was sent to you.”

A cold stillness settled in Touch’s veins. That name. From the list.

The stranger’s voice spoke from the doorway, gentle, carrying no threat at all. “Brother Saul, the Lord Jesus, who appeared to you on the road has sent me so that you may see again and be filled with the Holy Spirit.”

The air vanished from the room. Touch could not move. He could not breathe. One of the very men he had traveled here to arrest had walked through the door and called him brother.

And he could not see the knife in his hand, or the pity on his face, or whatever it was he truly carried.

A hand, warm and surprisingly gentle settled on Touch’s shoulder. He flinched, a small, involuntary jerk. The touch was not forceful, not threatening, but it was intimate. Too intimate. It bypassed his armor, his defenses, and went straight to the raw, exposed wound of his being. He wanted to recoil, to lash out, to demand to know by what authority Ananias touched him. But he could not move.

“Let go,” Touch managed to whisper.

“Not until you let go,” Ananias replied, his voice a murmur. “Let go of the anger. Let go of the fear.”

The anger. The fear. They were all Touch had left. They were the familiar currents that had driven him, the fuel for his zeal. Without them, what remained? Only the void. The blackness. The memory of Stephen’s open hands, his upward gaze, his words of forgiveness.

His breath came in short shallow gasps. His chest tightened, a vise clamping down on his ribs. His hands, still curled into fists from Elazar’s taunts began to tremble. He felt the grit from the wall digging into his palms, a small, sharp pain that barely registered against the rising tide of internal chaos.

“He forgave me,” he whispered, the words barely audible. A confession. A plea.

Ananias’s thumb stroked his shoulder, a light, rhythmic motion. “He forgives us all, Brother Saul.”

The tears came then. Hot, stinging, unexpected. They cut trails through the grime on his face. Touch had not cried since he was a child. Not when his father disciplined him, not when he left Tarsus, not even when he had seen a man die at his feet. But now, in the presence of this stranger, this man of the Way, the dam broke.

His body shook, wracked by a grief he hadn’t known he carried. The grief for Stephen. The grief for the man he thought he was. The grief for the God he thought he served.

“I was wrong,” he choked out, the words raw, tearing at his throat.

“Yes,” Ananias said, his voice still gentle. “But now you can be right.”

Touch’s head throbbed. A dull ache behind his eyes intensified. It wasn’t the gentle pressure of tears. It was a sharp, burning pain, as if tiny needles were pricking the surface of his eyeballs. His hand flew to his face, pressing against his closed eyelids. The pain flared, a blinding, internal agony. It was not just physical. Terror that this was it. This was the punishment. A permanent, inescapable darkness, a living tomb for his eyes, for his soul.

A soft knock sounded at the door.

It hung in the air after his tears had dried to salt tracks on his cheeks. Ananias’s hand left his shoulder, and the absence of its warmth was a sudden, cold shock.

“Come,” Judas’s voice called from the corridor.

The door swung inward on protesting hinges. Touch smelled it before he heard the bowl set down—a thin, savory steam carrying the scent of cumin and coriander, something sharper underneath maybe rue. It was a poor man’s broth, but complex. A woman’s touch. Judas’s wife, perhaps, moving in the shadows of her own home, tending to the unwanted guest.

“Some broth,” Judas said. He spoke in a careful neutral, the tone you’d use for a skittish animal. “You must eat, Saul.”

Touch’s stomach twisted, a hollow clench of protest. The smell was both tantalizing and repulsive. It spoke of a normalcy, of a hearth, of a kindness he had forfeited. “No.”

“You’ll die of stubbornness before the Lord takes you any other way.” Judas didn’t move to leave. Touch felt his presence lingering in the doorway, a solid, patient block of silence.

“Let him die, then.” The words were out before Touch could cage them. They hung in the spice-laden air, ugly and true.

Judas exhaled, a long, slow sound. “That is not the way of this house. Nor of the One who sent my brother Ananias to you.”

Ananias was still here. Touch could feel his quiet attention from somewhere near the wall. A witness to his petulance. Shame, hot and fresh washed over the earlier grief. He was a child refusing supper.

“I am not worthy of your bread,” Touch said, his voice low.

“Worth isn’t the currency here,” Ananias said. No judgment. Just a fact.

The smell of the broth coiled into Touch’s memory, a key turning in a rusted lock.

It was a different room. A cellar near the Dung Gate. Damp stone, the reek of mildew. The man’s name was Reuben. A linen-weaver. His hands were bound behind him with a length of rough cord. Touch had questioned him for an hour, pressing him on his allegiance to the crucified magician. He would not recant. He just kept repeating the same mad phrase: “The pierced one reigns.” His breath had smelled of onions and cheap wine. But just before Touch’s men dragged him away to the hold, his wife had burst in, weeping. She’d thrust a small clay bowl at him. “Drink, husband. Please.” It was a broth. Touch remembered the scent—cumin, coriander. Rue for bitterness. Reuben had drunk it, his eyes on hers over the rim. Then he looked at Touch, calm as a millpond. “He will judge the judges, young Pharisee. Remember that.” Touch had struck the bowl from his hands. It shattered on the stones, the broth a dark stain spreading between them. “Take him.”

The memory was a physical blow. His breath caught. The scent in this room was identical. A common enough combination of spices, but in this context, in this darkness, it was a ghost. Reuben’s calm face floated in the black behind Touch’s eyes. Not angry. Not afraid. Resigned. Certain.

He will judge the judges.

At the time, Touch had heard a threat. The desperate bravado of a beaten man. Now, lying blind in the house of a follower, the words slithered into a new shape. They weren’t about earthly power. They were about a reversal. An inversion. The persecutor laid bare.

A cold sweat broke out across his back. His hands were trembling again. He pressed them flat against the wool pallet to still them.

“Saul?” Micah, from his post by the door. Always watching the ruin.

“Take it away,” Touch managed to say. The words were thick.

Judas didn’t argue. Touch heard the scrape of the bowl on the floorboards as he lifted it, the soft shuffle of his sandals as he retreated. The door closed, leaving the three of them in the thickening dark. The scent lingered, a taunt.

Time lost its shape. It became a slow, suffocating crawl measured only by the sounds of the city dying outside. The melon-seller’s cry ceased. The clatter of cart wheels grew sporadic, then stopped. Distinct voices melted into a low murmur, then into silence, broken only by the occasional bark of a dog or the distant, lonely call of a watchman. The room cooled. The day’s heat, stored in the mudbrick, bled away into the night air seeping through the shutters.

Touch didn’t sleep. His mind was a churning pit. Fragments of faces, voices he had silenced rose and fell like debris in a flood. A woman in Joppa, her eyes fierce as she told him the kingdom was within. A young Levite in Antioch who had smiled as Touch listed the charges. They had all shared that same damnable calm. It wasn’t defiance. It was something worse. Pity. They had pitied him.

And Stephen. Always Stephen. His open hands.

Ananias began to pray. Not aloud, not in words Touch could grasp, but a low, rhythmic murmur that was more pause than speech. It was a sound like water over stones. It filled the corners of the room, a gentle, persistent pressure. Touch wanted to rage against it, to order him into silence. This was the sorcery, the whispering heresy that seduced simple minds. But he had no rage left. Only a vast, aching exhaustion. His body was a hollow vessel, drained of tears, of fury, of purpose.

The hunger and thirst he had denied became living things, gnawing at his edges. A dry fire in his throat. A cramp deep in his belly. His weakness was absolute. He could not stand without aid. He could not find the water bowl three feet away. He was an infant. A corpse that hadn’t finished dying.

“Micah,” he said, low.

Micah was at his side instantly. “Here.”

“The water.”

He guided Touch’s hand to the bowl. His fingers brushed Micah’s, and Touch recoiled at the warmth of living skin. He fumbled found the rim, lifted it. He drank greedily, water slopping down his chin, soaking the front of his tunic. Lukewarm, tasted of clay, and it was the finest thing he had ever consumed. He drained it.

When he lowered the bowl, gasping, the world tilted again. A wave of dizziness, deep and swaying. His head felt too heavy for his neck.

“You need to eat,” Micah said, his voice close to his ear.

“No.” But the refusal was a reflex, empty of conviction.

“You will break.”

And Touch, the judge of so many, lay utterly helpless, blind and bound by his own pride, awaiting it.
