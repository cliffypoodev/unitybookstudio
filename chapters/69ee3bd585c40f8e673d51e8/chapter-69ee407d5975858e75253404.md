The dog’s bark still echoed in Saul’s ears when the knock came. Not the sharp rap of a merchant or the tentative tap of a beggar. Three quick strikes, a pause, two more. Ananias froze mid-step, his hand hovering over the chest.

Saul sat up, straw prickling through the pallet. “Who—”

“Stay down.” Ananias mouthed the words more than spoke them. He slid the chest lid shut without a sound and moved to the door, pressing his ear against the wood. The knock came again—three, pause, two. Ananias exhaled and lifted the latch.

Miriam slipped inside, her sandals dusty, the hem of her robe damp with sweat. She didn’t look at Saul. “They’ve posted men at every gate. Reuben’s paying the guards to look the other way.”

Ananias closed the door. The latch clicked like a bone snapping. “When?”

“Tonight.” Miriam finally turned to Saul. Her lips were chapped, her eyes red-rimmed. “They know you’re still here. They know you’ll try to leave.”

Saul stood. The room tilted for a heartbeat, the taste of old bread sour in his mouth. “Let me talk to them.”

Miriam laughed, a short, sharp sound. “Talk? You think this is a debate over Torah?”

“I know Reuben. He’s a reasonable man—”

“He held the cloak at Stephen’s stoning.” Her voice didn’t rise, but the words landed like stones. “He watched you approve it. You think he’ll listen now?”

Saul’s fingers found the scar on his palm. The cut had reopened, the blood dried to a crust. He pressed until the pain cleared his head. “Then I’ll go to the synagogues.”

“They’ll hear the heretic,” Miriam said. “The traitor. The man who came to drag them to Jerusalem and now preaches the opposite of everything he swore to uphold.” She stepped closer. The scent of cumin and fear clung to her. “They won’t debate you, Saul. They’ll drag you to the stoning pit and make sure you don’t get up.”

Ananias rubbed his forehead. “The caravan to the Decapolis—”

“Gone. Elisheva warned them. They left at dawn.”

Saul’s throat tightened. The coins in the pouch at his belt suddenly felt absurdly light. “Then I’ll walk out another way.”

Miriam and Ananias exchanged a look.

“There is no other way,” Ananias said quietly.

Silence stretched. Somewhere outside, a child shrieked, the sound slicing through the afternoon heat. Saul crossed to the narrow window. The street below was empty except for a stray dog nosing at a pile of refuse. The wall of Damascus rose beyond it, the stones bleached white by the sun.

He had entered this city with letters of authority, a band of armed men, the full weight of the Temple behind him. Now he stood in a borrowed room, hunted, with nothing but a handful of silver and the clothes on his back.

Miriam’s voice cut through the stillness. “There’s one option.”

Saul turned.

She held up a length of rope, the fibers frayed but strong. “The house backs against the wall. There’s a gap where the stones have shifted—just wide enough to lower a man through.”

Saul stared at her. “You’re suggesting I crawl out like a thief.”

“I’m suggesting you live.”

Ananias took the rope, testing the weight. “It’s not just the drop. The Nabateans patrol the outer perimeter.”

“Then it’s a race.” Miriam’s gaze didn’t waver. “You reach the rocks before they spot you, you might make it to the trade road.”

Saul’s pulse hammered in his temples. The indignity of it burned—lowered in a basket, scrambling through the dirt like a fugitive. But the alternative was worse. He thought of Stephen again, the peaceful set of his face as the stones rained down.

“Tonight,” he said.

Miriam nodded. “At midnight. The guards change shifts then.”

Ananias coiled the rope, his fingers moving methodically. “I’ll find a basket. Something sturdy.”

Saul flexed his hand. The scar split open again, a thin line of red welling up. He smeared it across his palm, watching the blood darken in the creases.

Some debts couldn’t be repaid. Some roads only led one way.

Light through the window turned the color of old bruises. Saul sat with his back against the wall, watching Ananias knot the rope around a roof beam. The fibers creaked under tension.

“You’ll need to keep your arms tucked,” Ananias said. “If the basket tips—”

“I understand the mechanics.” Saul flexed his hand. The scar had stopped bleeding, but the skin around it was raised and angry.

Miriam crouched by the door, grinding something in a mortar. The scent of myrrh and something bitter filled the room. She dipped a cloth into the paste and held it out. “For your face.”

Saul didn’t take it. “I’m not disguising myself.”

“Not a disguise.” She pressed the cloth to her own cheek, leaving a dark smear. “The Nabateans track by torchlight. This dulls the shine of skin.”

He took the cloth. The paste was cold, gritty with crushed herbs. He rubbed it over his forehead, the bridge of his nose, the hollows beneath his eyes. The smell clung to the back of his throat, like damp earth and rust.

Ananias tested the basket, his weight making the reeds groan. “It’ll hold.”

Saul looked away. The basket was the kind women used for market—wide shallow, meant for figs or lentils. Not a man. Not a Pharisee. He thought of the letters he’d carried into Damascus, sealed with the High Priest’s insignia.

Miriam tied a waterskin to her belt. “You’ll need to move fast once you’re over. Head southeast—there’s a wadi that cuts through the rocks. Follow it until the second bend, then climb the western ridge.”

“And if the Nabateans are waiting?”

“Then you’ll wish you’d taken the stoning.” She said it flatly, without malice. A fact, like the coming night.

Ananias shot her a look. “There are caves along the wadi. Hide until dawn if you have to.”

Saul stood. His knees ached from sitting too long. The room felt smaller, the walls pressing in. He paced to the window. The street below was emptying, merchants shuttering their stalls. A pair of Temple guards stood at the corner, their spears glinting in the fading light.

He turned abruptly. “Give me a knife.”

“You won’t fight your way out,” Ananias said.

“I know that.” Saul’s voice was too sharp. He forced it lower. “If they take me, I won’t be dragged back to Jerusalem.”

Silence thickened. Somewhere in the house below, a child cried out in its sleep.

Miriam unbuckled the sheath at her waist. The blade was short, curved, the edge honed to a wicked gleam. She held it out. “Don’t use it unless you mean to.”

Saul took it. The hilt was warm from her body. He slid it into his belt, the weight unfamiliar against his hip.

Ananias cleared his throat. “We should pray.”

Saul almost laughed. Prayer. As if words could smooth this ragged edge. But he bowed his head anyway, out of habit more than faith.

Ananias’s voice was steady. “Lord, guide his steps. Make his path straight.”

Miriam didn’t join in. She was checking the knots again, her fingers quick and sure.

The prayer ended. The room was darker now, shadows pooling in the corners. Ananias lit a single lamp, the flame guttering in the still air.

Saul rubbed his palms together. The paste had dried, tightening his skin. “How long until midnight?”

“Three hours,” Miriam said.

Three hours. The last moments of Saul of Tarsus, Pharisee, scholar, enforcer of the Law. By dawn, he’d be a fugitive, a rumor, a man who vanished into the desert.

He sat on the pallet, the straw crackling under his weight. The knife pressed against his thigh. He could feel the shape of it through the fabric, a hard line against his flesh.

Ananias sat across from him, his hands clasped. “You could stay. Wait for a better chance.”

“And how long would that be?” Saul stared at the lamp flame. “A week? A month? Until the High Priest sends more men?”

“You’re safe here.”

“Am I?” Saul looked up. “Or are you just better at hiding your fear?”

Ananias didn’t flinch. “Both.”

Miriam snorted. She was wrapping strips of cloth around her hands, the fabric rough and stained. “Fear keeps you alive. Pride gets you killed.”

Saul’s jaw tightened. He wanted to argue, to quote Scripture, to remind them both of the weight of his learning. But the words stuck in his throat. What good were arguments now?

The lamp flickered, a draft maybe, or the oil running low.

Miriam stood. “I’ll scout the route.”

Ananias caught her wrist. “Be careful.”

She pulled free. “Always am.”

The door opened, then shut. The latch clicked softly.

Ananias exhaled. “She’s right, you know. About the pride.”

Saul said nothing.

“You think this is beneath you.” Ananias leaned forward, the lamplight carving deep lines in his face. “But the Messiah didn’t ride into Jerusalem on a warhorse. He came on a donkey.”

“And look what happened to him.”

Ananias smiled, just a little. “He changed the world.”

Saul looked down at his hands. The paste had cracked between his fingers, revealing patches of bare skin. He rubbed at it, but the stain wouldn’t come off.

Time passed. The lamp burned lower. Outside, the sounds of the city faded—a last cart rattling over stones, a drunk singing off-key, then silence.

Ananias dozed, his chin dipping to his chest. Saul stayed awake, watching the shadows creep across the floor.

A scratch at the door. Ananias jerked upright.

Miriam slipped inside, her hair damp with sweat. “It’s clear. For now.”

Saul stood. His legs felt heavy, as if the earth itself resisted his movement.

Ananias handed him a cloak—coarse wool, dyed the color of dust. “Pull the hood up.”

Saul draped it over his shoulders. The fabric itched.

Miriam checked the knife at her belt. “Stay close. Don’t speak. If we’re stopped, let me do the talking.”

Saul nodded. The humiliation burned, but beneath it, something colder. Clearer.

Ananias touched his arm. “Go with God.”

Saul almost said something then—a thank you, an apology, a plea for understanding. But the words wouldn’t come. He squeezed Ananias’s wrist instead, a gesture he’d seen soldiers use.

Miriam opened the door. The alley beyond was black as pitch.

Saul took a breath and stepped into the dark.

The night air was thick with the smell of cooking fires and sewage. Miriam moved like a shadow, her steps silent on the packed earth. Saul followed, his sandals scuffing against the stones.

They turned down a narrow passage, the walls so close Saul could touch both sides without stretching. A cat yowled somewhere overhead.

Miriam paused at a corner, pressing flat against the wall. Saul froze. Footsteps echoed nearby—slow, deliberate. A guard on patrol.

The footsteps faded. Miriam gestured sharply. They slipped across an open courtyard, past a sleeping dog that didn’t even lift its head.

The city wall loomed ahead, its stones pitted with age. A section near the base had crumbled, leaving a gap just wide enough for a man to squeeze through.

Miriam crouched, her fingers probing the mortar. “Here.”

Saul stared at the hole. It was smaller than he’d imagined. He’d have to crawl.

The basket lay nearby, the ropes already secured. Miriam checked the knots one last time, her hands moving with practiced ease.

Saul’s throat tightened. This was it. No turning back.

Miriam straightened. “Ready?”

He wasn’t. But he nodded anyway.

She grabbed his arm, her grip like iron. “Listen. Once you’re down don’t look back. The desert doesn’t forgive hesitation.”

Saul met her eyes. In the faint starlight, they were black pools, unreadable.

He turned to the wall. The stones were rough under his palms, still warm from the day’s heat. He knelt, dust coating his knees.

The basket creaked as he climbed in. It sagged under his weight, the reeds digging into his thighs.

Miriam took the rope. “Hold on.”

Saul gripped the sides. The world tilted as the basket lifted off the ground.

For a heartbeat, he was suspended—between the city and the desert, between the man he’d been and the man he’d become. Then the basket began to lower, inch by inch, into the dark.

The last thing he saw was Miriam’s face, outlined against the stars. Then the wall swallowed him whole.

The basket swayed, the reeds groaning under his weight. Saul clutched the sides, his knuckles white. The wall scraped against his back through the basket’s weave, rough as a cat’s tongue.

The night air cooled as he descended, the stink of the city giving way to the dry, mineral scent of the desert. Above, Miriam’s silhouette shrank against the stars. No torchlight. No voices. Just the whisper of rope through her hands and the occasional skitter of pebbles dislodged by his passage.

His sandal slipped. The basket lurched. Saul bit back a curse, his stomach lurching with it. For a heartbeat, he hung at an angle, one side dipping perilously low. Then the rope steadied. His breath came in short, sharp bursts.

A sound below—metal on stone. Saul froze. The Nabateans. He strained to hear over the hammer of his own pulse. Nothing. Just the wind maybe, or some night creature.

The wall was pocked with handholds, gaps where the mortar had crumbled. He could almost climb it, if not for the drop, if not for the knife at his belt, useless against height.

His thighs ached from crouching. The reeds dug into his flesh, leaving patterns he knew would linger. A Pharisee, curled like a babe in a market basket. The irony burned, but the shame burned hotter.

Another sound. Closer this time. A boot scuffing dirt. Saul held his breath.

A voice murmured in Nabatean, low, bored. A guard making his rounds. The basket swayed as Saul’s hands trembled. He willed himself still. The guard’s footsteps paused. Saul could picture him squinting into the dark, hand on his spear.

Then the footsteps moved on, fading into the night.

The rope jerked. Saul’s grip tightened. Miriam was lowering him faster now, the basket spinning slightly. The wall blurred, pale stone, darker patches where lichen clung. His palms were slick with sweat.

A sudden drop. The basket fell a hand’s breadth, the rope rasping. Saul’s teeth clicked together. He tasted blood where he’d bitten his tongue.

Then the ground rose to meet him. Not the hard impact he’d braced for, but a gradual settling as the basket touched earth. The ropes went slack.

Saul didn’t move. The desert stretched before him, a vast, formless dark. Behind him, the wall loomed, its height suddenly monstrous. He’d never seen it from this angle, not as a free man. Always from within, always with the weight of authority at his back.

A whisper from above: “Go.”

Miriam’s voice, barely audible. The rope slithered down the wall, coiling at his feet like a dead snake. Saul stepped out of the basket. His legs buckled, the muscles stiff from crouching. He caught himself on the wall, the stone rough and warm against his palms.

The wadi. Miriam had said southeast. He turned, scanning the darkness. The stars were sharp, indifferent. No moon to betray him.

A scrape of metal. Saul spun.

A guard stood ten paces away, his spear glinting faintly. He hadn’t seen Saul yet; he was turned slightly, adjusting his belt. Saul’s hand went to the knife.

The guard turned.

Saul dropped into a crouch, his heart pounding. The guard’s gaze swept past him, lingered on the basket. A frown. He took a step toward it.

Saul edged along the wall. One step. Two. The guard bent to examine the basket, his spear propped against the wall.

Now.

Saul bolted.

The guard shouted. Saul didn’t look back. The desert rose to meet him, the ground uneven, studded with rocks that twisted underfoot. His sandals skidded on loose gravel. Behind him, the guard’s cries grew louder, joined by others.

The wadi. Where was it?

He veered left, his lungs burning. A torch flared to life behind him, casting his shadow long and spindly across the rocks. Arrows of light probed the darkness.

Then the ground fell away. Saul stumbled, his foot catching on nothing. He slid, the slope steep, his hands scrabbling for purchase. Stones bit into his palms. The wadi. He’d found it.

He half-ran, half-fell down the incline, the shouts fading behind him. The wadi walls rose on either side, shielding him from view. His breath came in ragged gasps.

At the bottom, he crouched behind a boulder, listening. No footsteps. No torches. Just the wind hissing through the rocks.

His hands shook. Not from the chase, but from the realization. He was outside. Alone. No letters of authority. No men at his back. Just the knife and the clothes he wore and the dust coating his tongue.

He touched the hilt. The metal was cold.

A sound made him stiffen. Not pursuit, a scorpion maybe, or a desert hare. Something small and unconcerned with him.

Saul stood. The wadi stretched before him, a pale ribbon in the starlight. Southeast. Follow it to the second bend, then climb.

He took a step. Then another.

Behind him, Damascus was a darker shadow against the night, its walls impenetrable. A city he’d entered with power. A city that had unmade him.

He didn’t look back again.

The desert swallowed his footsteps.
