The dream broke like a clay jug on stone. Touch woke with the taste of dust in his mouth, his neck stiff from leaning against the wall. The barking dog had fallen silent. The lamp was a dying ember in its dish.

Barnabas was already moving, rolling a sleeping mat with quick, efficient tugs. “Up. There’s a gathering at Matthias’s house, near the Essene Gate.”

Touch pushed himself upright, his tongue thick. “A gathering?”

“He said you couldn’t join our gatherings. This isn’t ours. It’s... different.” He wouldn’t meet Touch’s eye. He tied the mat with a frayed cord, his fingers working too fast. “They’re Hellenists. Widows, some freedmen. They meet separately. James thought... it might be a place to start.”

Start. The word was a hook in his ribs. A place to be useful. To earn a square of floor that wasn’t borrowed on Barnabas’s strained credit. He stood, his knees popping. “Will they have me?”

Barnabas finally looked at him. His face was the same open map, but the roads were tired. “I spoke to their elder. A man named Philip. He agreed to let you come and listen. Just listen.”

The house was a single room stacked atop a dyer’s workshop, reached by an exterior staircase that groaned underfoot. The air stank of stale urine and woad, a sharp, ammoniac tang that clung to the back of the throat. Inside, the room was packed, bodies sitting shoulder to shoulder on reed mats. The heat was a wet wool blanket. A single high window let in a bar of afternoon light cutting through the motes of lint and dust, illuminating the anxious tap of a woman’s finger on her knee.

Every face turned as they entered. The conversation didn’t stop so much as fracture, pieces of sentences left hanging.

“...and the distribution was unfair, I told him—”
“—if the Romans ask, we say nothing—”
“—who is that with Barna—”

Silence pooled, heavy and complete. Not the quiet of people waiting. The silence of a held breath, of a room recognizing a snake in its midst.

Philip was a compact man with ink-stained fingers. He stood near the window, his smile tight as a drumhead. “Barnabas. Welcome. And... Saul.” He said the name like testing a fig for rot.

Barnabas guided Touch to a space against the far wall. They sat. The floorboards were gritty with tracked-in dye powder, a faint blue dust. The man to his left, a gaunt fellow with a baker’s pallor, shifted his mat an inch away.

Philip resumed speaking about the daily distribution of food to widows. He spoke, the words careful, administrative. He listed names, measures of grain. Touch’s mind snagged on a splinter in the floorboard beside his thigh. Long and pale, lifting from the wood. He wondered how many feet had caught on it, if it had torn a robe. A stupid thing to notice.

A woman across the circle asked a question about a widow from Cyprus. Her voice was reedy, strained. As Philip answered, her eyes cut to Touch. Not a glance. A direct, measuring look. Then away.

It happened again. And again. Eyes would find him, hold for a heartbeat too long, then dart back to Philip. The room’s attention was split—part on the business of survival, part on the monument to their past terror sitting against their wall. He became an object. A ruin. The air grew thicker, hotter. The smell of unwashed wool and sour breath condensed into a paste on his skin.

The baker beside him finally spoke, his voice a low mutter meant for his neighbor. “Heard he had letters for Damascus. Had names.”

His neighbor, a younger man with a scar across his knuckles, grunted. “Heard he watched them stone Stephen. Held the cloaks.”

“Quiet,” the baker hissed.

But the words were out. They hung in the air, more real than Philip’s lists of barley. A woman near the front drew her shawl tighter around her shoulders, as if a chill had entered. The room’s focus had crystallized. Not on Touch, but on the ghost he brought with him. The ghost of Stephen, of warrants, of a knock at midnight.

Philip finished his report. He cleared his throat. “Barnabas has asked if his guest might offer a word. A testimony of... recent events.”

All pretense of other business vanished. The silence pressed his lungs flat. Barnabas gave him a slight nod. His eyes said, Now. Prove it.

Touch stood. His height felt obscene in the low room. He had to stoop. He looked at the circle of faces—some curious, some hostile, most simply closed, shuttered like houses in a storm.

“I...” The word cracked. He tried again. “On the road to Damascus, I met the Lord.”

A few blinked. No one moved.

“He asked me why I persecuted him. The light... it blinded me.” He was reciting facts. Giving a report, just as he had to Peter. It sounded hollow, even to him. A story told by a stranger. He searched for the feeling, the scorching certainty of that moment. All he found was the memory of the grit under his knees, the terror. “Ananias, a disciple in Damascus, laid hands on me. I received my sight. I was baptized.”

A man in the corner, his beard threaded with white spoke without raising his head. “We know the story. It’s all over the city. A fine story.”

“It’s true,” Touch said, the heat rising in his neck.

“Many things are true,” the old man said. “A viper’s bite is true. It still kills.”

A murmur rippled through the room. Agreement. The baker nodded once.

Touch floundered. His hands hung useless at his sides. He had prepared arguments, scriptures, prophecies from Isaiah. They were ash in his mouth. These people didn’t want prophecy. They wanted to know if their children would eat, if the Romans would come, if the man who had hunted their friends was now a wolf pretending to shepherd.

“I want to help,” he said, the sentence weak, pathetic.

“Help?” a woman echoed. It was the one who had asked about Cyprus. She had a narrow, intelligent face, eyes dark as olives. “How?”

“I can teach. I can write letters. I have... I know the Law.”

“We know the Law too,” she said. No malice. Just a flat statement. “It didn’t protect my husband when the Temple guards came asking about his associations.”

Her husband. Another ghost. Touch didn’t know his name. He was one of the faceless, one of the ones swept up in the net Touch had helped weave. He looked at her, really looked. Her grief wasn’t a fresh wound. It was something worn smooth by time, a stone carried in a pocket until it took the shape of the body.

“I’m sorry,” he said. The words were gravel.

She didn’t acknowledge them. She just held his gaze until he had to look away down at the blue-dusted floor.

The gathering dissolved after that. The life went out of it. People stood, conversations resuming in hushed tones as they filed toward the door. They gave him a wide berth, a circle of empty floor. Barnabas was talking with Philip by the window, their heads close together. Philip was shaking his head, a small, firm motion.

Touch was a stone in a stream. The water simply flowed around him.

Then he saw her. She stood alone by the doorway, waiting for the press of bodies to clear. She was older, perhaps fifty, her hair mostly gray under a simple mantle. Her hands were clasped in front of her, the knuckles swollen, the skin cracked from work. She wasn’t looking at the door. She was looking at him.

Not the sharp, judging stare of the others. A steady, absorbing gaze. She took him in. The new robe Barnabas had found for him, still too loose at the shoulders. His hands, empty. His face.

He knew her. Not her name, but her kind. The wife of a craftsman, perhaps. A weaver. A potter. One of the quiet ones who kept the world turning while men debated divinity and law. Her eyes were the color of a winter sky, a pale washed-out blue. They held no anger. No accusation. No curiosity.

They held nothing at all. Just a vast, silent recognition.

His mouth went dry. He took half a step toward her, some insane impulse to speak, to explain, to beg a forgiveness he had no right to name. Her gaze didn’t waver. She didn’t frown. She didn’t smile. She just... saw him.

And in that seeing, he saw the ghost of her husband. Not as he was in life, but as he was in her memory—a presence now defined by its absence. A space at her table. A cold place in her bed. A silence where his voice should be. Touch had caused that silence. Had authored that absence.

She didn’t need to say a word. Her silence was the indictment. It filled the room, louder than any shouted curse. It said: You are the reason my world is quiet. You are the why.

He stopped. His breath caught, a physical knot under his breastbone. He couldn’t speak. Any word would be a violation, a crude noise thrown into her sacred, terrible quiet.

She held his gaze for a moment longer. Then, as if he had ceased to exist, she turned and stepped through the doorway, melting into the dim stairwell.

The room was almost empty. The bar of sunlight from the high window had crawled up the opposite wall, leaving the space where he stood in shadow. The blue dust was just dust.

Barnabas approached, his footsteps heavy on the creaking boards. He didn’t ask what happened. His face was etched with a new, grave concern, the lines around his mouth deep. He had seen the woman. He knew.

“We should go,” he said quietly. “There’s... someone else. Someone who demands to see you. He’s been waiting.”

The way he said it—demands—sent a cold trickle down Touch’s spine. This wasn’t a hopeful meeting. It was an unavoidable confrontation. Another door, swinging shut behind him.

Barnabas led the way through the thinning crowd, his broad shoulders parting the last few stragglers. The air still carried the tang of woad and the collective scent of too many bodies in too small a space, but now it felt thinner, stretched. Touch’s stomach coiled. He watched Barnabas’s back, the worn fabric of his tunic, the way the muscles shifted under it. Barnabas didn’t look back. He didn’t need to. He knew Touch followed.

They stopped in a narrow alcove near the back, tucked between a stack of empty grain sacks and a pile of discarded reed mats. The light there was poor, a single oil lamp flickering low on a shelf. A man stood waiting. He was tall, his frame lean and sharp under a dark cloak. His face was obscured by shadow, but Touch recognized the set of his jaw, the arrogant tilt of his head.

Jonah.

Touch’s breath hitched. Not a confrontation. A reckoning. Jonah’s eyes, when he turned them on him were colder than any winter sky. They held all the accusation the woman’s silence had not.

“So, the great Saul of Tarsus,” Jonah said, his voice a low rasp, like sand on stone. “Or should I call you Paul? The convert. The transformed.” He spat the word “transformed” as if it were a curse. He didn’t look at Barnabas, his gaze fixed on Touch’s face. “Heard you were in Damascus. Heard you were preaching the very name you swore to eradicate.”

Touch felt the heat rise in his cheeks. “I preach the truth. The Messiah has come.”

Jonah let out a short, humorless laugh. “Truth. You speak of truth, you who turned your back on everything you once held sacred? On the Law? On the Temple? On your own people?” He stepped closer, invading the small space. Touch could smell the sharp scent of his expensive perfume, a cloying sweetness that made his gorge rise. “You were a lion, Saul. A defender of the faith. Now you are... what? A mewling lamb?”

Touch’s hands clenched at his sides. He wanted to strike him. To wipe that sneer from his face. But the memory of the woman’s silent accusation still clung to him, a shroud. His zeal, his conviction had brought nothing but sorrow.

“The Lord appeared to me,” he said, his voice tight. “He called me. Just as he called you, Jonah, to witness to his power.”

Jonah scoffed. “He called me to Jerusalem, yes. To the Temple. To the traditions of our fathers. Not to a desert hallucination that turns a man into a blathering fool.” He took another step, his shadow falling over Touch. “Tell me, Saul, when you preach this... gospel... do you speak of Stephen? Do you tell them how you stood by, approving, as they crushed the life from him?”

The name hung in the air, a bell tolling. Stephen. His face, serene even in death, burned behind Touch’s eyelids. He saw the stones, the blood. He saw his own face, younger, harder, full of a righteous fury that now tasted like ash. His stomach twisted.

“I... I have repented,” he managed. The words felt thin, inadequate.

“Repented?” Jonah’s laugh was louder this time, devoid of mirth. “Of what? Of serving God? Of upholding the Law? You repent of righteousness, Saul, and embrace... what? Blasphemy? Sedition?” He leaned in, his voice dropping to a venomous whisper. “You think you can simply walk away from who you were? From what you did? You think a few days of blindness and a convenient vision absolve you?”

He paused, letting the words sink in. He ran a hand over his jaw, a tell Touch remembered from their days in the Sanhedrin—a gesture of contempt, of dismissal. “You preach in the synagogues, they say. You argue with the Hellenists. You twist the scriptures to fit your new heresy.”

“I explain the scriptures,” Touch corrected him, his voice raspy. “I show how they point to the Messiah. To the fulfillment of prophecy.”

“Prophecy?” Jonah sneered. “You think this... carpenter... this crucified criminal... is our Messiah? The one who will restore Israel? You are a fool, Saul. A deluded, dangerous fool.” He shook his head, a slow, deliberate movement. “And a traitor.”

The word hit Touch like a stone. Traitor. It cut deeper than fool, deeper than blasphemer. It went to the core of his identity, the one he had tried to shed, the one that still clung to him like the scent of dried blood.

“I am no traitor,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “I serve the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. I simply serve him in a new way.”

“A new way,” Jonah echoed, his tone dripping with sarcasm. “A way that spits on generations of tradition. A way that undermines the very foundation of our faith. You are a cancer, Saul. A disease that threatens to consume the body of Israel.” He paused, his eyes narrowing. “And like any cancer, you must be cut out.”

Barnabas stepped forward, placing a hand on Touch’s arm. His presence was a solid, anchoring weight. “That is enough, Jonah. He has made his choice.”

“His choice will lead to ruin,” Jonah retorted, his gaze still fixed on Touch. “For him, and for all who listen to his lies. You think you can escape your past, Saul? You think you can simply become someone new? You carry your past with you. It is carved on your soul. And there are those who will never forget.”

He took a step back, his eyes sweeping over Touch one last time, a look of utter disgust. He didn’t say another word. He simply turned, his dark cloak swirling around him, and walked out of the alcove, disappearing into the dim light of the doorway. The scent of his perfume lingered, a sickly sweet reminder of his presence.

Touch’s shoulders sagged. The confrontation had drained him, leaving him hollowed out. Jonah’s words, sharp and precise had sliced through the fragile shell of his new identity. They had ripped open the old wounds, the ones Touch tried to ignore, the ones that still festered beneath the surface.

Barnabas squeezed his arm. “He’s gone.”

Touch nodded, unable to speak. The air in the alcove felt heavy, stifling. Jonah’s words echoed in his ears: You carry your past with you. It is carved on your soul. And then the woman’s face, her silent, unyielding gaze. Two faces of the same truth. One shouted, the other whispered, but both condemned him.

“We should go,” Barnabas said again, his voice softer this time. “There’s nothing left for us here.”

Touch didn’t argue. His feet felt heavy as lead. They walked out of the house and down the groaning exterior stairs. The street was almost empty now, the late afternoon light fading to a bruised purple. The smell of woad was still strong, but now it mingled with the faint scent of charcoal from a nearby cook fire, the promise of an evening meal for others.

He felt none of it. His senses were dulled, his mind a tangle of Jonah’s accusations and the woman’s silent judgment. He had come to Damascus seeking to serve, to atone, to build something new. But everywhere he turned, the past rose up to meet him, a wall he couldn’t breach. The city had offered no refuge. Only mirrors.

Barnabas walked beside him, his silence a comfort now, not a judgment. He didn’t try to reassure him. He didn’t offer platitudes. He just walked.

Touch’s throat felt tight. His chest ached. The world seemed to shrink, the familiar streets of Damascus becoming a maze of shadowed alleys, each turn leading him back to himself, to the man he had been, the man he still was. He couldn’t outrun him. He couldn’t bury him. He was here, in the dust, in the air, in the eyes of everyone who looked at him.

Barnabas put a hand on his shoulder. “We will find another way,” he said, his voice quiet but firm. “There is always another way.”

Touch shook his head. The words wouldn’t come. The weight of her silence, of Jonah’s scorn, pressed down on him, heavy and suffocating. He felt adrift, a ship without a rudder, tossed on a sea of his own making. The path he thought he had found was crumbling beneath his feet. He had hoped to build a bridge from his past to a new future, but all he had done was dig a deeper grave.

The street lamps flickered on, one by one, casting long, distorted shadows. Night was falling, and with it, a cold dread settled in his bones. He was a stranger in this city, a stranger to himself. And the truth, the horrible, clear truth was that he deserved it. Every bit of it.

He looked at Barnabas, his face now a blurred outline in the encroaching gloom. He was Touch’s only anchor, and Touch was dragging him down with him. He couldn’t ask Barnabas to carry this burden any longer. Not for him.

“I need to be alone,” Touch said, the words a raw whisper torn from his throat. His voice was not his own. A stranger’s voice.

Barnabas looked at him, his eyes filled with a familiar sadness. He didn’t argue. He just nodded, a slow, solemn gesture.

He walked away, leaving Touch standing in the deepening twilight, a solitary figure on a deserted street. The silence of the city pressed in, louder than any clamor. Touch felt the cold stone of the wall behind him, the rough texture a small comfort against the chaos in his mind. His past was not a shadow to be outrun. It was a part of him, a limb he couldn’t amputate. The question was, what did he do now with such a monstrous thing attached?

The street wasn’t deserted for long. A pair of women hurried past, baskets balanced on their heads, their sandals slapping a quick rhythm against the stones. They glanced at him, then away, a flicker of caution in their eyes. A stranger, standing too still. He pushed off from the wall and began to walk.

He had no destination. His feet moved because standing still felt like an invitation for the ground to swallow him. The purple dusk deepened into true night, the sky a bowl of indigo pierced by the first sharp stars. Oil lamps bloomed in windows, squares of honeyed light that made the spaces between houses darker, more secret. The smell of frying fish and cumin drifted from an upper room, a mundane, taunting promise of normal life. His stomach turned over, empty and sour.

He walked without seeing. His mind was a broken pot, spilling the same images again and again. The woman’s hands. The cracked knuckles, the swollen joints. Work-worn hands that had touched a husband’s face, mended his clothes, prepared his meals. Hands that now worked alone. Then Jonah’s jaw, the contemptuous set of it, the way his perfume had clung to the air—a rich man’s smell, a smell of councils and power. Two different condemnations. One silent, one shouted. Both true.

The street curved, narrowed. The buildings leaned in, their upper stories nearly touching, shutting out the sky. He was in the Lower City, he realized. The air smelled different there—less spice, more damp stone, refuse, the close scent of too many people in too little space. A child cried behind a shuttered window, a weary, rhythmic sound. A door opened ahead, spilling a bar of yellow light and the rumble of male laughter. It cut off as the door shut, leaving the alley in deeper silence.

Touch stopped. Where was he going? Back to the upper room? To face Barnabas’s pity, the unasked questions in his eyes? To the house of Ananias in Damascus? That door had been closed for years. To the desert? He’d already fled there once, to the emptiness of Arabia, and the silence had nearly unmade him. There was no to. There was only this from.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and cruel. Not of Stephen, but of a man whose name he’d forgotten. A linen-seller from Cyrene, arrested in a synagogue in Joppa. He had a mole on his left cheek, shaped like a tiny fig. When they took him, he hadn’t looked at the guards. He’d looked at Touch. His eyes hadn’t been pleading. They’d been... puzzled. As if trying to solve a difficult sum. As if he couldn’t reconcile the scripture-quoting Pharisee with the man who signed his ruin. Touch had looked away, busied himself with the scroll of the warrant. Why remember his face now, here, in this stinking alley? Why not let the memory stay buried?

Because the woman had dug it up. Her silence was a spade, turning over the soil of every life he’d touched.

He started walking again, faster now, a panicked energy driving his legs. He needed to get out from under those leaning houses. He took a turning, then another, his sandals skidding on slick stones. The alley opened onto a slightly broader street, lined with shuttered workshops—a potter’s wheel visible through a broken lattice, a carpenter’s bench. At the far end, a weak lamp burned outside a small shrine to some Roman household god, a crude stone figure smeared with soot.

This was wrong. He was going in circles. He turned to retrace his steps, and his foot kicked something soft.

In the absolute stillness, he listened to the city breathe. For the first time, he heard his own place in its rhythm—not as a heartbeat, but as a scar. A silence where a sound should be.
