Barnabas did not suggest the inn. He turned and walked, a silent command to follow. The hen scratched in the dust, indifferent. Touch’s legs moved, stiff from days of standing, nights on hard ground. They left the courtyard through a different gate, into an alley that smelled of wet clay and cumin.

They walked for what felt like half the city. Not toward the Upper City, not toward the Temple. Into the warren of the Essene Quarter, where the streets narrowed until the whitewash on opposite walls brushed his shoulders. The light fell in thin, dusty blades. Barnabas moved with a purpose that knew every turn, every uneven stone underfoot. He didn’t look back. The silence between them was a third person, keeping step.

Barnabas stopped at a door so plain it was invisible—unpainted wood, a lintel worn smooth by generations of shoulders. He knocked a pattern: two quick, a pause, three slow. The sound was swallowed by the stone. A slot slid open at eye level, then closed. A bolt scraped.

The room inside was low-ceilinged, lit by a single high window. The air was cool and still, thick with the smell of old scrolls and lamp oil. Men sat on mats along the walls. They stopped talking when Touch and Barnabas entered.

Touch knew some of their faces. James, the brother of the Lord, his features a softer echo of the man Touch had never met, his hands resting on his knees like tools he didn’t know how to use. John, son of Zebedee, whom they called a Son of Thunder—he looked older than Touch remembered from the trials, his thunder banked to a watchful simmer. And Peter.

Peter sat in the corner, his back to the wall. He was bigger than Touch had pictured, with shoulders that had hauled nets, a beard streaked with grey like tarnished silver. He didn’t stand. His eyes found Touch’s and held. They were the color of flint, and just as hard.

Barnabas moved to the center of the room. “I have brought him.”

“We see what you have brought.” The voice was James’s, measured, without warmth. “The wolf asks to lie with the lambs.”

“He is not the same man.” Barnabas’s voice held a calm Touch couldn’t fathom. “He was struck down on the Damascus road. He was blind. He saw the Lord.”

A ripple went through the room, a shifting of cloth, an intake of breath. Peter’s expression didn’t change.

“A story,” said a man Touch didn’t know, his face pinched with suspicion. “A clever story to open our doors. To finish what he started.”

“He preached in their synagogues,” Barnabas continued, as if the man hadn’t spoken. “He called Jesus the Son of God. The Damascenes tried to kill him for it. They lowered him from the wall in a basket to escape. These are not the actions of a spy. A spy does not make himself a target.”

“Or it is the deepest deception,” James said. His fingers traced a seam in the mat. “To suffer, to be persecuted, to gain our trust utterly. Then the strike comes.”

Touch wanted to speak. His throat was a dry cistern. The words he’d carried from the desert—I am a fool, forgive me, use me—crumbled to powder. They weren’t listening to words. They were reading his body, his face, the set of his shoulders, looking for the ghost of the Pharisee who had signed the order for their friend’s death. He could feel him there, in the line of his jaw, in the way he held his hands. He couldn’t dislodge him.

“Why should we believe you, Barnabas?” John’s voice was quiet, but it cut. “You vouch for him with your life. But your life is not the one he threatened.”

Barnabas nodded, accepting the blow. “My life is not. But my judgment is. I sold my field. I laid the money at your feet. You trusted me with that. Trust me with this.”

“A field is not a soul,” Peter said.

His first words. The room went still. He leaned forward, his elbows on his knees. The light from the high window caught the web of lines around his eyes. “Tell me,” he said, and he was looking only at Touch now. “When the light struck you, when you heard the voice, what did it sound like?”

The question was a trap. A test. If Touch said it sounded like trumpets or choirs, he was a liar. If he said it was like nothing on earth, he was vague, unreliable. His mind scrabbled. He remembered the pressure, the heat, the obliterating whiteness.

“It was... inside my bones.” The words came out rough, unpracticed. “Not in my ears. In my teeth. In the marrow. It wasn’t a sound you hear. It was a sound you... are.”

Peter’s gaze didn’t waver. “And the words. ‘Why do you persecute me?’ Did he say anything else?”

Touch saw Stephen’s face, peaceful, a man looking through a window at a better country. “He said... ‘It is hard for you to kick against the goads.’”

A sharp breath from James. Peter’s eyes narrowed, just a fraction.

“That is not in the common telling,” John murmured.

“It is what he said,” Touch whispered.

Peter sat back. Looked at the floor between his feet, at a crack in the stone packed with ancient dirt. The silence stretched. A fly buzzed against the high window, a frantic, tiny metronome. Touch counted its impacts. Seven. Eight.

“I denied him,” Peter said to the crack in the floor. “Three times. While they kindled the fire in the courtyard, I said I never knew the man. The words came out of my mouth like vomit.” He looked up, and his flint eyes were wet. “The cock crowed. And I went out and wept like a child.”

He wiped his cheek with the back of a broad, calloused hand. The gesture was brutal, final. “This man... he persecuted. Not in fear. In zeal. He hunted our brothers and sisters from house to house. He stood and approved while they crushed the life from Stephen.” He pointed a finger at Touch, not in accusation, but in bleak statement. “His sin has a different shape than mine. But the same grace that found me in my cowardice must find him in his certainty. Or none of it is real.”

James opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked troubled.

The man with the pinched face wasn’t convinced. “You ask us to risk everything. On a feeling. On a story.”

“I ask you to risk on the pattern,” Barnabas said, his voice rising for the first time, edged with a passion that made the air hum. “The pattern is always the same. The lost one is found. The dead thing is made alive. It is the only pattern he knows. Why would he change it now?”

More silence. It wasn’t agreement. It was a stalemate. Touch could see it in their postures, the way they looked at each other, then away. Fear was a colder, more rational thing than faith in that room. It had better arguments. It spoke of survival.

The hope that had twisted in Touch’s gut when Barnabas spoke his name in the courtyard shriveled. It wasn’t going to happen. They would debate, they would pray, they would send him away with a blessing for his journey and a warning never to return. He would be a ghost forever, haunting the edges of the life he was meant to join.

His knees felt loose. The smell of the oil lamp was suddenly nauseating, thick and sweet. He needed air. He needed to be out of this room where his very presence was a poison.

He took a step back toward the door.

Barnabas saw it. He saw the surrender in the hunch of Touch’s shoulders. He moved, not toward the apostles, but toward Touch. He put a hand on his arm. His grip was firm, anchoring.

“Then let him stay with me,” he said to the room, to Peter, to James. “In my house. Under my eye. Let him work with me. Let you watch him, not as a prisoner, but as a man. If he is a wolf, I am the first he devours. My blood is on my own hands, and you are free of it. But if he is a brother...” He looked at each of them in turn. “If he is a brother, then we have gained more than we have lost.”

The offer hung in the close air. A hostage. A guarantee. Barnabas was betting his life, his standing, everything, on the man Touch might become.

Peter looked from Barnabas’s face to Touch’s. He searched for something: the ghost of the Pharisee, or the shape of the grace that could bury him. He sighed, a long, weary exhalation that seemed to come from the stones of the floor.

“He stays with you,” Peter said, the words a verdict. “He does not come to the gatherings. Not yet. We will watch. We will pray.” His eyes locked on Touch’s, and the weight in them was immense. “And you will work.”

The fly stopped buzzing. It found the patch of fading light on the floor and sat there, cleaning its legs. Touch watched it, a stupid, ordinary thing. His knees held.

Peter’s verdict wasn’t acceptance. It was a stay of execution. He stays with you. Not with us. The space between those words was a canyon Touch couldn’t cross.

Barnabas’s hand was still on his arm. He gave a slight squeeze, then let go. The warmth of his grip lingered, a brand. He turned back to the circle, his posture easing from pleading to practical. “Thank you.”

James stood. He moved to a low table against the wall where a clay jug and several cups sat. He poured water, the sound loud in the quiet. He brought a cup first to Peter, then to John, then to Barnabas. He brought one to Touch. Their fingers brushed as Touch took it. James’s were dry, cool. He didn’t look at him.

The water was lukewarm, tasting of the clay vessel and the deep cistern. Touch drank it all. His throat still felt raw.

“The work?” John said. He had a way of speaking that turned a word into a question without inflection. “What is he to do?”

Barnabas answered before Touch could. “He can write. A clear hand, Aramaic and Greek. He can copy letters, keep records of the distributions. He can help me with the Gentile inquiries.”

“He is a scholar,” James said, not looking up from his cup. “A Pharisee. This is work for a scribe, a servant.”

“It is work that needs doing,” Barnabas said, simple as stone. “Let him serve.”

The man with the pinched face—Touch learned later his name was Matthias—shook his head. “He listens. He hears our plans, our names, our meeting places. He carries them in his head back to the Temple.”

“Where will he carry them?” Barnabas’s voice remained patient, but a thread of steel ran through it now. “He has no audience with Caiaphas. The Sanhedrin would stone him on the steps if he showed his face. He is as hunted as we are, for the same confession. His safety is tied to ours. Betraying us is betraying himself.”

The logic was sound. It left no room for argument. Matthias looked away, dissatisfied.

Peter hadn’t touched his water. He stared into the cup as if reading dregs that weren’t there. The lines around his mouth were deep grooves. Touch found himself tracing the grey in his beard, the way it clustered at the corners of his jaw like frost. A fisherman’s beard, salted and wind-tangled. Touch’s was trimmed, a Pharisee’s beard. The difference felt obscene in that room.

“You spoke of his preaching in Damascus,” Peter said, his eyes still on the cup. “Tell it. Not the signs. The words. What did he say of the Christ?”

Barnabas glanced at Touch, a flicker of permission. Touch gave a slight, desperate nod. Tell it. Please.

Barnabas began. He spoke not as a storyteller, but as a witness reporting facts. He told of Touch’s first stumbling attempts in the synagogues, how he had not started with miracles or prophecy, but with the puzzle of the Suffering Servant. “He took the scroll of Isaiah,” Barnabas said, his hands shaping the air. “Chapter fifty-three. ‘He was wounded for our transgressions, bruised for our iniquities.’ He asked them—the Damascene Jews—how the rabbis reconciled this with the conquering Son of David. He led them through the thread, the scarlet thread from the Passover lamb to the Temple sacrifice to... to the man outside the wall.”

Barnabas was good. He stripped the emotion from it made it sound like a legal argument, which it had been, at first. A cold, brilliant proof. He left out the nights of shaking, the dreams of stones, the taste of blood in Touch’s mouth from biting his own cheek to stay silent during the rebuttals.

As he spoke, Touch watched Peter. He saw no softening. Peter’s brow furrowed, not in concentration, but in a kind of interior struggle. He was measuring Barnabas’s account against something in his own memory. A private weight.

Barnabas reached the escape, the basket, the darkness, the rough weave scraping Touch’s skin as they lowered him. “He preached the Messiah he came to destroy,” Barnabas concluded, his voice dropping. “And for it, he lost his place, his reputation, his safety. He gained nothing but a price on his head. What spy pays such a price?”

The room was silent again, but a different silence. The heated resistance had cooled into a weary, watchful tension. They were listening now, not to Barnabas, but to the shape of the story. The pattern, as he’d called it.

James rubbed a thumb over his lip. “And his time in Arabia? What of that?”

Barnabas hesitated. A fraction of a second. He didn’t know. Touch had told no one of the empty silence of the desert, the screaming arguments with God that left him hoarse and weeping, the visions that were not visions but a terrible, wordless knowing. He had called it prayer. More like being dismantled.

“He went into the desert,” Barnabas said carefully. “As our father Elijah did. As the Lord did. To be tested. To listen. He returned with the same confession, but... settled. Like a jar that has been fired a second time.”

It was a good answer. Poetic. It satisfied them. It wasn’t true. The truth was no one returns from the desert the same. You came back less, not more. A scar where your certainty used to be.

Peter finally moved. He set his untouched cup on the floor with a soft click. He leaned forward, his big hands dangling between his knees. He looked at Touch, and for the first time, it felt like he was seeing him, not the ghost at his shoulder.

“When you were blind,” he said, “after Ananias laid hands on you, what was the first thing you saw?”

Touch’s breath hitched. Not the light. Not the face of a man. “Scales,” he whispered. The word was ash. “Like... fish scales. Falling from my eyes. They drifted down, thin as onion skin. And then I saw his feet. Sandals. Dusty. The strap on the left one was frayed.”

He had never told anyone that. Too small, too stupid. A frayed strap. The first fragment of the world God gave back to him.

Peter’s eyes closed. He held them shut for a long moment. When he opened them, something had shifted. Not acceptance. Recognition. He had seen something small and true once, too, the way the charcoal fire lit the servant girl’s face.

He nodded once. A bare dip of his chin.

It wasn’t forgiveness. It wasn’t welcome. It was an acknowledgment that Touch’s memory was a real place, with dusty sandals and frayed leather. That his wound was specific. Peter’s own wound echoed in it, a different note in the same terrible chord.

“The work will be hard,” Peter said, his voice gravelly. “It is not study. It is counting coins. Writing lists. Listening to widows argue over portions of barley. It is your hands getting dirty with the world’s need.”

“I understand,” Touch said. His voice cracked.

“I do not think you do.” He held Touch’s gaze. “But you will.”

He stood then, unfolding his height in the low room. He seemed to fill it, his shadow swallowing the patch of light where the fly had been. The meeting was over. James and John rose. Matthias shot Touch a last, lingering look of distrust before turning away. They began to speak in low tones about other matters—a sick brother in the Lower City, a shipment of grain from Cyprus.

Touch was dismissed. A project. A conditional burden.

Barnabas touched his elbow, guiding him toward the door. As they reached it, Peter’s voice stopped them.

“Barnabas.”

They turned. Peter hadn’t moved. He stood in the center of the room, a rock in a slow current.

“You carry him,” Peter said. The words were heavy, final. “His weight is yours now. Do not stumble.”

Barnabas met his gaze. “I will not.”

Peter’s eyes moved to Touch. The flint was still there, but behind it, a bleak compassion. The look a man gives another who is about to walk a road he knows is long, and steep, and lined with stones. He said nothing more.

Barnabas opened the door. The alley air rushed in, cool and smelling of night coming on. They stepped out. The door closed behind them with a solid, muffled thud. The bolt scraped home.

They stood in the narrow passage. The last blade of sun cut across the whitewash high above, painting it bloody orange. From a nearby courtyard came the sound of a woman calling a child. A normal sound. It felt like something from another creation.

Barnabas let out a long, slow breath. He leaned against the wall, his head back, eyes on the strip of darkening sky. He looked tired down to his bones.

Touch wanted to thank him. The words piled up behind his teeth—a torrent of gratitude, of apology, of shame. They died there. Thank you was a pebble thrown into the chasm Barnabas had just bridged with his own body. It meant nothing.

Barnabas pushed off the wall. “Come,” he said. “My house is this way.”

He started walking. Touch followed. The silence between them was different now. It held Barnabas’s risk, Touch’s debt. A living, breathing thing, heavier than before.

Touch glanced back once at the plain, worn door. It gave no sign of what had just happened inside, of the verdict passed, the temporary reprieve. It was just a door. He wondered if Peter was still standing in the center of that room, in the gathering dark, listening to the echo of his own words: You carry him. He wondered if he regretted them already. The doubt was a cold stone in Touch’s gut. It sat there, familiar. He knew he would carry it with him, along with the weight of Barnabas’s faith, into whatever work awaited. Into the counting of coins, the writing of lists. Into the long, silent watch of those who were waiting for him to prove them right.

That night, a dog began to bark again, frantic, persistent. It barked at the night, at the moon, at some shadow only it could see. It barked as if its noise could keep the darkness at bay. Touch listened to it until his eyes grew heavy, until the barking became another thread in the fabric of the night, and he slipped into a sleep where he dreamed of ledgers and falling scales and a frayed strap that would never hold.
