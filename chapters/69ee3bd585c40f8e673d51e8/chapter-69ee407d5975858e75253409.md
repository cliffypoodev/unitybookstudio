The synagogue air smelled of old parchment and cedar oil, the benches worn smooth by generations of argument. Touch gripped the edge of the reading desk, his knuckles pressing against the grain. The faces before him were a mosaic of disbelief—furrowed brows, lips pressed thin, fingers drumming against thighs. A man in the front row kept cracking his knuckles. That sound was a tiny hammer on Touch’s nerves.

“Isaiah saw the Lord high and lifted up,” he said. His voice didn’t sound like his own. It was too loud, too sharp, the way it had been in Damascus when he first stood before a crowd like this. “The seraphim cried, ‘Holy, holy, holy.’ But the prophet said, ‘Woe is me, for I am undone.’” He paused. The knuckle-cracker stopped mid-pop. “Why undone? Because he saw the King—and knew himself unclean.”

A woman near the back snorted. “And you’re clean now, Saul of Tarsus?”

That question hung in the air like smoke. Someone coughed. A child kicked the bench legs, the rhythmic thud underscoring the silence.

Touch exhaled through his nose. The scar on his palm itched, the one from the Damascus wall, where the basket’s rope had burned him. “No,” he said. “But the coal touched my lips anyway.”

A man with a silver beard—Rabbi Eliezer, who’d once praised Touch’s zeal—shook his head. “You quote scripture like a man who’s found a new toy. But we remember how you wielded those same words like a lash.”

“I wielded them wrong.”

“Or you wield them wrong now.”

A fly buzzed against the high window, desperate for the fading light. Touch tracked its frantic path. He’d prepared arguments, prophecies about suffering servants, Psalms about pierced hands, but they felt like stones in his mouth. These people didn’t need convincing. They needed to spit in his face.

A young man near the door stood abruptly. “You dragged my cousin to the Sanhedrin for saying less than you’re saying now.”

Touch’s gut tightened. He didn’t ask the cousin’s name. He didn’t want to know. “Then let them drag me.”

The room erupted.

Outside, the street was eerily quiet. The sun had dipped behind the rooftops, stretching the shadows long and thin. Touch’s sandals scuffed the stones. His throat was raw. He’d shouted more than he’d intended.

A figure leaned against a wall ahead—Barnabas, arms crossed, his face unreadable. “They didn’t throw anything this time.”

“They considered it.”

Barnabas pushed off the wall and fell into step beside him. “You can’t keep doing this.”

“I have to.”

“Not like this. Not—” Barnabas cut himself off, rubbing his temple. “You’re not debating. You’re provoking.”

Touch stopped walking. A donkey brayed in the distance, the sound lonely and sharp. “They need to hear it.”

“They need to hear it from someone they don’t want to stone.”

The words landed like a slap. Touch turned away, studying the cracks in the street. A line of ants marched single-file through a fissure, carrying crumbs twice their size.

Barnabas sighed. “There’s a gathering tonight. At Miriam’s.”

“The widow?”

“She asked for you.”

Touch’s chest constricted. The last time he’d seen Miriam, her silence had been a blade. “Why?”

“I don’t know. But she didn’t look like she wanted to kill you.”

“That’s an improvement.”

Barnabas didn’t laugh.

The muttering started as they turned the corner. Touch didn’t need to look back to know who it was, the Hellenists from the synagogue, clustered in a tight knot. Their voices were low, but the tone carried.

“…dangerous…”

“…twisted the Law…”

“…should’ve stayed blind…”

Barnabas tensed beside him. “Don’t turn around.”

Touch didn’t. But the words slithered down his spine anyway, cold and familiar. He’d heard that tone before, in his own voice, in the halls of the Sanhedrin, in the moments before the stones flew.

The chill was the same.

That room smelled of lamp oil and the faint metallic tang of the city’s water. Touch sat on the edge of a low bench, his fingers tracing the rough grain of the wood. The house was too quiet, no voices from the courtyard, no footsteps in the hall. Just the occasional creak of the roof beams settling in the cooling night.

Peter stood by the window, his silhouette cutting a sharp line against the pale rectangle of moonlight. He hadn’t spoken since Touch finished recounting the day. James, seated across from him, picked at a loose thread on his sleeve. That silence was worse than the shouting in the synagogue.

“You’re certain you didn’t provoke them?” James finally said.

Touch’s jaw tightened. “I quoted Isaiah.”

“Isaiah provokes,” Peter said, his voice flat. “You know that.”

A moth fluttered against the lamp, casting frantic shadows. Touch watched it batter itself against the clay rim. “They were provoked before I opened my mouth.”

James exhaled through his nose. “And the Hellenists following you after?”

“They followed Barnabas too.”

“Barnabas didn’t used to hunt them.”

The words landed like a stone in still water. Touch’s fingers stilled on the bench. He could recite every prophecy about the Messiah’s suffering, every Psalm that foretold the piercing of hands and feet. But this wasn’t about scripture. It was about the space between his ribs where their distrust lived, sharp and unyielding.

“You think I’m still that man.”

Peter turned from the window. The moonlight caught the lines around his eyes, the weariness Touch hadn’t noticed before. “I think you don’t know what you look like to them. To us.”

The moth gave up and spiraled to the table, wings twitching. Touch focused on the way its antennae trembled. “Then tell me.”

James leaned forward, elbows on his knees. “You walk into a room and people see the man who dragged their brothers to prison. You open your mouth and they hear the voice that condemned their sisters. You can’t—” He broke off, rubbing his forehead. “You can’t preach like the others. Not yet.”

Touch’s pulse thudded in his throat. He wanted to argue, to list every hour he’d spent in Arabia wrestling with the Law and the Prophets until the pieces fit. But the words wouldn’t come. He heard Elisheva’s voice from Damascus, soft and terrible: You don’t get to be the hero of this story.

Peter sighed. “We’re not saying stop.”

“Be quieter,” James finished.

The lamp flickered. Outside, a dog barked, the sound echoing down the alley. Touch stood abruptly, the bench scraping against the floor. “I’ll take the roof.”

Neither man objected.

Cooler air waited above the city, the stars sharp through the haze of smoke and dust. Touch lay back on the flat clay tiles, his arms folded behind his head. The ache in his chest was familiar now, not the blinding pain of Damascus, but a dull, persistent weight, like a stone sewn into his tunic.

Footsteps sounded on the stairs. He didn’t turn.

“You’re not subtle,” Lucius Valerius said, leaning against the parapet. The Roman’s armor gleamed dully in the starlight, the leather straps creaking as he shifted. “Half the city knows where you are by now.”

Touch closed his eyes. “Then why haven’t they come?”

“Maybe they’re waiting for you to do their work for them.”

A breeze carried the scent of charred bread from a nearby oven. Lucius had been shadowing him since Jerusalem, a silent observer with no apparent orders. Touch had stopped asking why.

“Your friends don’t trust you,” Lucius said.

“They’re not my friends.”

“No?” Lucius tilted his head. “Then why do you care what they think?”

Touch breathed out through his nose. The stars blurred above him. “You wouldn’t understand.”

“Try me.”

Below, a door creaked open. Voices floated up—James and Peter, speaking in hushed tones. Touch held his breath.

“…too much like before…”

“…dangerous for everyone…”

“…if he’s wrong again…”

Lucius raised an eyebrow. “Sounds like a lively debate.”

Touch rolled onto his side, facing the city. The rooftops stretched like a jagged sea, the Temple Mount looming dark against the sky. He’d stood there once, certain of everything. Now the certainty was gone, but the weight of it remained, the choices he couldn’t unmake, the faces he couldn’t forget.

Lucius pushed off the parapet. “For what it’s worth, I think you’re wasting your time.”

“Why?”

“Because no one believes a convert.” Lucius’s sandals scuffed against the tiles as he walked away. “Especially not one who used to burn their holy books.”

The stairs creaked under his weight. Touch didn’t watch him go.

A cat yowled somewhere in the dark. The voices below had faded. Touch sat up, rubbing his palms against his thighs. The scar from the Damascus rope was rough under his fingers.

He’d thought the blindness was the hard part.

A room below was empty when he descended. The lamp had burned low, the wick drowning in its own wax. A single cup sat on the table, the dregs of wine dark as old blood.

Touch picked it up. The clay was cool against his palm. He could pour it out, smash it against the wall, watch the shards scatter like the pieces of his old life.

He set it down carefully.

The courtyard was silent. The dog had stopped barking. Even the insects had gone quiet. Touch stood in the doorway, the night pressing against his back.

Somewhere in the city, the Hellenists were whispering. The apostles were doubting. The Romans were watching.

And he was here. Alone.

The weight in his chest shifted settling deeper.

He stepped into the dark.

The Temple courts swarmed with pilgrims and merchants, the air thick with the smell of roasting meat and crushed herbs. Touch wove through the crowd, his pulse loud in his ears. The morning sun glared off the white stone, sharp enough to make his eyes water. Or maybe it was the lack of sleep. He’d spent the night on the roof, chasing fragments of dreams that dissolved like smoke when he reached for them.
