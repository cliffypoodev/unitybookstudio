The door creaked shut behind Saul cutting off the alley's noise—donkeys braying, a potter shouting prices, the metallic tang of hot iron from the blacksmith's stall. A dozen faces turned toward him. The silence that followed wasn't the quiet of anticipation, but the held breath of a room waiting for a scorpion to strike.

A woman near the hearth—Miriam, he remembered from the list—dropped her ladle into the stew. The clatter made a boy flinch.

“Brother Saul.” Ananias stood, wiping his hands on his tunic. The title was a rope thrown across a chasm.

Saul stepped forward. His sandals stuck to the floor where wine had spilled. The room smelled of fish and charred bread, the scent of people who worked until dusk and ate in haste. He opened his mouth, but the words tangled. What did you say to the people you'd come to shackle?

A man in the corner—Jorah, broad-shouldered, with a scar across his knuckles, leaned toward the woman beside him. The whisper was meant to carry. “He stood by when they stoned Ezra. Held the cloaks.”

That woman—Lydia, her hair streaked with gray, didn't look up from her mending. “And now he knows the Messiah? Like a dog learns the shepherd's voice?”

Ananias shot them a look, but the damage was done. Saul's fingers found the edge of the table. The wood was rough, splintered. He pressed until a sliver bit his palm. Its pain was clean, sharper than the stares.

“I didn't come to argue Torah,” Saul said, keeping his voice low. “Only to break bread.”

Miriam snorted. “You came to arrest us last month.”

“Last month,” he agreed. The admission cost him nothing. They already knew.

A child tugged at Lydia's sleeve. “Is that the bad man?”

The room froze.

Lydia tucked the boy behind her. “Hush.”

Saul exhaled through his nose. The oil lamps flickered, stretching shadows like accusatory fingers across the walls. He could recite every prophecy about the suffering servant. He could parse the legal implications of a crucified Messiah until dawn. None of it mattered here. The only text that mattered was written in the set of Jorah's shoulders, the way Miriam's hand hovered near the knife at her belt.

Ananias passed him a cup. The wine was thin, sour. “They'll see in time.”

Saul drank. The dregs gritted against his teeth.

Across the room, a girl no older than twelve studied him. Her eyes were dark, unblinking. Not fear, calculation. She whispered to the boy beside her, who giggled into his fist.

The stew pot bubbled. Someone coughed. The normal sounds of a shared meal, strained through a sieve of distrust.

Jorah stood abruptly, his stool scraping. “I need air.”

The door slammed behind him.

Miriam wiped her hands on her apron. “He lost his brother to your warrants.”

Saul set down the cup. “I know.”

“You don't.” She jabbed the ladle at him. “You knew names on a list. Not the man who sang off-key at Sabbath. Not the way he patched my roof when the rains came.”

The words landed like stones. Saul had no defense. His warrants hadn't asked about singing or roofs.

A hand touched his elbow—Ananias, steering him toward the courtyard. “Come.”

Outside, the night air was cooler. The stars were sharp, indifferent. A cat yowled from a nearby rooftop.

Ananias leaned against the wall. “They need time.”

Saul rubbed his palm. The splinter was gone, but the sting remained. “What if time isn't what they need?”

From inside, Lydia's voice carried through the window. “—sent to spy. Mark my words. The high priest doesn't give up his hounds so easily.”

A murmur of agreement.

Ananias winced.

Saul stared at the cracked clay of the courtyard tiles. A line of ants marched toward a fig pit, oblivious to the giants above them. He envied them. Their purpose was simple. Carry. Survive.

A girl from inside appeared in the doorway. She held out a crust of bread. An offering or a test, he couldn't tell.

Saul took it. “Thank you.”

She darted back inside before he could say more.

The bread was stale. He chewed slowly. It tasted like dust and regret.

Ananias clapped him on the shoulder. “We'll try again next week.”

Saul nodded. The lie sat between them, patient as a spider.

Later, in the alley, he heard them.

“—watched us tonight. Counting. Remembering.” Lydia's voice.

Jorah's reply was a growl. “If he's still here by the new moon, we vote.”

A third voice, unfamiliar, asked, “And if the vote goes against him?”

The pause was answer enough.

Saul pressed his back to the wall. The stones were cold through his tunic. Above him, a bat flitted between the rooftops, swift and silent.

He'd been a hunter once. He knew the sound of a closing trap.

The souk swallowed him whole. Saul kept his head down, letting the current of bodies carry him past stalls heaped with saffron and cumin, past copper pots clanging in the wind like discordant bells. A merchant shouted about Syrian silk, another about Nabatean frankincense. The air was a fist of smells: cardamom, dung, the metallic bite of blood from the butcher's stall.

He adjusted the fold of his cloak, the wool scratchy against his neck. Three days since the house meeting, and the memory of their stares still prickled his skin. He'd come for bread and olives, nothing more. But the market was a bad place to hide. Too many eyes.

A child darted past, nearly colliding with a basket of pomegranates. The vendor cursed, grabbing for the boy's arm. Saul sidestepped, his sandal slipping on a smear of rotten fruit. The stench rose like a wave, overripe figs, vinegar, something sweet and putrid beneath. His stomach clenched.

A voice cut through the din. “—not just the Nazarenes. The Romans won't tolerate it either.”

Saul froze. The accent was Galilean, the tone too sharp for casual barter. He ducked behind a stack of woven reed mats, the fibers rough against his fingertips.

Two men stood by a spice merchant's stall, their backs to him. The taller one—broad through the shoulders, his beard streaked with gray, tapped a coin against his palm. “Three days. That's all we need.”

The other, younger, with a livid scar across his forearm, shook his head. “And if he's warned before then?”

“Who would warn him?” The older man snorted. “Even his own kind don't trust him.”

Saul's breath hitched. He knew that voice. Reuben ben Levi, a member of the synagogue council. A man who'd once clasped his shoulder and called him “brother” when Saul presented his letters of authority.

The younger man leaned in. “The Romans won't care about one more dead Jew.”

“Not if it's quiet. Not if it's done in the alleys, where they don't have to look.” Reuben pocketed the coin. “The Damascenes have long memories. They won't forget what he did here.”

Saul's pulse hammered in his throat. He'd walked these streets with warrants in hand soldiers at his back. He knew the alleys they meant, the ones where the walls leaned close, where a cry could be swallowed by the wind.

A donkey brayed, too loud. Reuben turned, his gaze skimming the crowd. Saul bent over a basket of lentils, pretending to inspect the quality. The grains were tiny, pebbled with dust. He let them trickle through his fingers.

The younger man muttered something about the Nabatean garrison. Reuben waved him off. “Let the Arabs play at kingship. This is our matter.”

Their footsteps faded into the din of the market. Saul straightened, his knees stiff. The sun was a white-hot coin overhead, pressing down. He needed to move. Needed to think.

But the crowd had other plans. A woman shoved past, her elbow jabbing his ribs. A porter balancing a crate of dates cursed as Saul stumbled into his path. The crate wobbled. One date tumbled to the ground, landing in the muck. The porter's glare was a brand.

“Watch where you're going, blind man.”

Saul didn't correct him. Blindness had been simpler. At least then the threats were honest.

He edged toward the dyer's stall, where bolts of fabric hung like banners. Indigo, madder red, the yellow of desert blooms. The dyer—a wiry man with stained fingers, was haggling with a customer. Saul traced the edge of a blue cloth. The color reminded him of the sky over the Damascus road, right before the light struck him down.

A snatch of laughter made him turn. Three children crouched near a puddle, poking at something with a stick. A scorpion maybe. Or a beetle struggling in the mud.

He should warn Ananias. A hand gripped his arm.

Saul jerked back, his free hand curling into a fist.

The dyer's apprentice, a boy no older than fifteen, blinked at him. “You're bleeding.”

Saul looked down. A splinter from the reed mats had lodged in his palm, welling a thin line of red. He hadn't felt it.

The boy offered a rag. “Here.”

Saul took it, pressing the rough fabric to his skin. The boy watched him, curious. “You're him aren't you?”

“Thank you.” Saul shoved the rag back too hard. The boy flinched.

He shouldn't have done that.

But the boy's stare was too much like the girl's from the house meeting, weighing, measuring. Saul turned away, pushing deeper into the souk. A crowd thickened near the silversmiths, where the air rang with the ping of hammers. A merchant called out to him, waving a necklace. “Finest work in Damascus! A gift for your wife?”

Saul kept walking.

The alley between the leatherworkers and the potters was narrower, shadowed. A good place to catch his breath. He leaned against the wall, the stone cool through his tunic. The splinter stung. He picked at it, his fingers clumsy.

A voice drifted from the next street. “—found the house?”

Saul stilled.

“Not yet.” The reply was muffled. “But the weaver's daughter talks too much. She'll lead us there.”

The first voice chuckled. “Good. The sooner he's dealt with, the better.”

Footsteps approached. Saul pressed himself flat against the wall. Two men passed the mouth of the alley, their silhouettes sharp against the sunlight. One carried a club.

Saul waited until their shadows vanished. Then he counted to ten. Twenty. His mouth was dry, his tongue sticking to his teeth.

He had to get back. A flash of movement at the edge of the market caught his eye. A woman stood by the well, her shawl slipping as she lifted a water jar to her shoulder.

Elisheva.

Nathaniel's widow.

Saul's breath left him in a rush. He'd seen her once before, at the trial. She hadn't wept. Hadn't screamed. Just stood there, her hands folded, while the sentence was read.

Now, across the press of bodies, her gaze locked onto his.

He couldn't look away.

The market noise faded. The smells, the heat, the ache in his palm, all of it narrowed to that single point: her eyes, dark as the ink on his old arrest warrants.

She didn't blink. Didn't frown. Just watched him, her expression unreadable.

Then she turned, vanishing into the crowd.

Saul exhaled. The splinter came free, trailing a bead of blood. He flicked it away, his hands steady now.

The synagogue council had set a date.

The widow had marked him.

And the alley walls, once his hunting ground were closing in.

The oil lamp guttered as Saul shut the door behind him. The wick needed trimming, but Ananias hadn't touched it. His fingers drummed the table, a rapid, uneven rhythm. The sound filled the small room like a second heartbeat.

Saul unbuckled his sandals. The leather was damp with sweat, the straps biting grooves into his ankles. He set them by the threshold, lining them up precisely. A habit from his Pharisee days, when order was armor.

Ananias pushed a cup across the table. The wine was darker tonight, thicker. “You're late.”

“I was followed.” Saul didn't touch the cup. His palms were still gritty from the souk, the cut stinging. “Two men near the dyer's stall. Another pair by the potters' quarter.”

Ananias's fingers stopped drumming. “Reuben?”

“And others.” Saul peeled a splinter from the table's edge. The wood was soft, yielding. “They've set a date. Three days.”

A moth battered the lamp. The shadow of its wings fluttered across Ananias's face, tightening the lines around his mouth. “You're certain?”

“I heard them.” Saul flicked the splinter into the flame. It caught, flared vanished. “They weren't hiding it.”

Ananias breathed out through his nose. The sound was heavy, weighted. He reached for the wine but didn't drink. “They'll watch the gates.”

“I know.”

“The desert roads—”

“Patrolled.” Saul's knee bounced under the table. The tremor ran up his thigh, restless. “Nabatean mercenaries. Paid to look the other way.”

Ananias rubbed his thumb over a stain on the table. The mark was old, darkened with wine and time. “There's a cellar beneath Jonah's workshop. The entrance is hidden behind a grain bin.”

Saul shook his head. “They'll tear the city apart, starting with the houses I visited.”

The unspoken truth hung between them. The cellar wouldn't save him. And if they found him there, Jonah's family would pay the price.

Ananias's fingers found their rhythm again, slower now. “You could go to the Romans.”

Saul barked a laugh. It came out too sharp, too loud. “With what proof? A rumor overheard in the souk?”

“You're a citizen. They're obligated—”

“To what? Protect a man half the city wants dead?” Saul leaned forward, the table digging into his forearms. “I signed my name to their arrest warrants. Used their roads to drag prisoners to Jerusalem. Now I'm asking for shelter?”

Ananias didn't flinch. “You're asking to live.”

The moth hit the lamp again. It stuck to the clay, wings searing. The smell was acrid, faintly sweet.

Saul pushed back from the table. The stool screeched against the floor. He paced the narrow room, his bare feet slapping the packed earth. Three steps to the wall. Turn. Three steps back. The rhythm matched the pulse in his temples.

Ananias watched him. “What will you do?”

“I don't know.” The admission tasted like ash. He'd always known. Always had a plan, a scroll, a list. Now his mind was a dry riverbed, cracked and empty.

A knock at the door.

They froze.

Ananias stood slowly, his stool scraping. He motioned Saul toward the sleeping alcove, then called out, “Who is it?”

“Miriam.” Her voice was low, urgent. “Open up.”

Ananias lifted the latch. Miriam slipped inside, her shawl damp with night air. She didn't greet Saul. Didn't look at him. Her fingers twisted the edge of her shawl, working a loose thread. “They're gathering at Reuben's house. Jorah's with them.”

Ananias shot Saul a glance. “How many?”

“Enough.” Miriam's gaze flicked to Saul, then away. “They're calling it a purification. For the blasphemer.”

Saul's gut clenched. The word was a stone in his belly. Blasphemer. Not apostate. Not traitor. The charge was specific, ritual. A sentence carried out by the faithful, not the state.

Ananias touched Miriam's shoulder. “You shouldn't have come.”

She shrugged him off. “Lydia sent me. Said if you've got any sense, you'll get him out tonight.”

Saul crossed his arms. The cut on his palm throbbed. “And where would I go?”

Miriam finally met his eyes. Her stare was flint. “That's your problem.”

The silence that followed was brittle. Ananias broke it first. “Thank you, Miriam.”

She turned for the door, then paused. “There's a caravan leaving at dawn. Syrian merchants. Headed for the Decapolis.”

Saul frowned. “How do you—”

“My cousin drives their mules.” She yanked the shawl tighter. “They don't ask questions.”

Ananias reached for her hand. “You're risking—”

“I know what I'm risking.” She pulled free. “But I won't have his blood on our walls.”

The door shut behind her with a soft click.

Ananias sagged onto the stool. The lamp flickered, stretching his shadow up the wall. “You should go.”

Saul rubbed his palm. The cut had reopened, smearing red across his lifeline. “And if they track the caravan?”

“Better than staying.”

Saul studied the blood on his fingers. It was darker in the lamplight, almost black. “What about the others? The ones who've accepted me?”

Ananias's jaw worked. He didn't answer.

Saul understood. Acceptance was one thing. Martyrdom for his sake was another.

The oil lamp sputtered. The wick was drowning in its own wax. Ananias didn't move to fix it.

Saul flexed his hand. The sting was fading, replaced by a dull ache. “I'll need supplies.”

Ananias nodded. “I'll pack them.”

“No.” Saul stood. “You've done enough. Tell me where to find what I need, and I'll go alone.”

Ananias looked up. His eyes were shadowed, the lines around them deeper than before. “You'll need water. A knife.”

“I know how to survive.”

The words hung between them, heavy with all they didn't say. Saul had survived prisons, riots, the stoning pit. But this was different. This was the slow unraveling of every thread he'd woven.

Ananias pushed to his feet. He moved to the chest by the wall, lifting the lid with a groan of wood. Inside were folded tunics, a rolled blanket, a small leather pouch. He took the pouch and pressed it into Saul's hands. “Silver. Not much, but enough.”

Saul weighed it. The coins shifted, clinking softly. “You were prepared.”

Ananias didn't smile. “I've been preparing since the day you arrived.”

The truth of it settled over Saul like a shroud. Ananias had known. They all had. This was always the end.

Saul tucked the pouch into his belt. “I'll leave before dawn.”

Ananias hesitated, then gripped Saul's forearm. His fingers were warm, calloused. “You'll write? When you're safe?”

Saul covered Ananias's hand with his own. The blood smeared between them, sticky and dark. “If I can.”

Ananias nodded. He didn't ask for promises.

Outside, a dog barked. The sound was sharp, sudden. They both tensed.

Ananias dropped his hand. “You should rest while you can.”

Saul moved to the alcove. The pallet was thin, the straw poking through the fabric. He lay down, boots still on. The ceiling was low, the beams cracked. He traced the lines with his eyes, counting the fissures like sheep.

Ananias blew out the lamp. Darkness was immediate, thick.

Saul closed his eyes. One image burned behind his lids: Elisheva's stare, unblinking, marking him in the crowd.

The widow hadn't spoken. Hadn't needed to.

Some debts couldn't be repaid.

Some roads only led one way.
