The sun hung low over the Temple Mount, casting long fingers of shadow across the portico's worn stones. Saul's sandals scraped against the pavement as he turned to face the gathering crowd, his voice raw from hours of debate. The air smelled of burnt offerings and sweat, the scent of piety and desperation mingling like old wine.

“Isaiah wrote of a suffering servant,” he said, his fingers tracing the scroll's edge. “Not a conqueror on horseback, but a lamb led to slaughter. You know the words. You recite them every Passover.”

A man in a patched cloak spat at Saul's feet. “We see a traitor.”

The spittle gleamed on the stone. Saul inhaled, the back of his neck prickling. He knew that voice. One of the Cyrenian pilgrims, one he'd debated last week in the lower city. The man's face was all sharp angles, his beard streaked with gray like tarnished bronze.

Saul tightened his grip on the scroll. “If the Law is a wall, you've built it so high you can't see over it.”

Murmurs rippled through the crowd. A woman near the back clutched her shawl tighter, her eyes darting toward the Temple guards. Saul recognized the look: fear, but not of him. Fear of being seen listening.

“You quote Isaiah while standing in the shadow of the Temple?” A younger man pushed forward, his tunic too fine for a laborer. “The same mouth that condemned Stephen now preaches his heresy?”

Saul's pulse jumped. Stephen's name was a hook in his ribs. He could still see the man's face, serene as the stones struck.

“I condemned what I didn't understand.” The admission tasted like grit.

“Voices now?” The Cyrenian barked a laugh. “First you hunt us, now you hear voices?”

A boy near the front snickered, then clapped a hand over his mouth. Saul's vision narrowed, the edges darkening like a scroll held too close to a flame. He forced his fingers to uncurl from the scroll.

“You want signs? Look at the cripple at the Beautiful Gate. Look at the healed lepers in the Kidron. The power isn't in the Temple's gold, it's in the name you refuse to speak.”

The fine-tunic man stepped closer, close enough for Saul to smell the myrrh on his breath. “You're a sword turned against its wielder. A dog that bites its master's hand.”

Saul didn't flinch. “Better a dog than a hypocrite.”

The crowd hissed. A woman gasped. The Cyrenian's face purpled.

“I dare because I was you.” Saul's voice dropped, low and rough. “I stood where you stand, clutching the Law like a shield, never seeing it was a mirror. Look into it. Tell me whose face you see.”

Silence gathered weight, like storm clouds before the first drop fell.

Then the fine-tunic man turned on his heel and walked away. Others followed, muttering. The Cyrenian lingered, his eyes black with something hotter than anger. “This isn't over, persecutor.”

The word hung in the air long after he'd gone.

Saul exhaled, his shoulders aching. The sun had dipped lower, staining the portico's columns the color of old blood. He rolled the scroll carefully, his fingers trembling, not from fear, but from the aftertaste of battle. He'd won nothing lost nothing. Only planted seeds on stony ground.

A hand touched his elbow. Barnabas, his face grim. “They're talking near the money changers. Not just grumbling. Planning.”

Saul nodded. He'd expected this. “How many?”

“Enough.” Barnabas's grip tightened. “We should go. Now.”

Saul glanced toward the Temple's inner courts, where the priests would be preparing the evening sacrifice. The smoke curled skyward, a pale thread against the darkening blue. He thought of the lamb, bound and silent.

“Not yet.” He turned back toward the dispersing crowd, catching fragments of conversation:

“—never should have let him in—”
“—Barnabas vouched for him—”
“—silence him before—”

The last whisper slithered past, too clear to ignore. Saul's gut clenched. He'd heard that tone before, in the voices of men discussing how best to drag Stephen outside the city walls.

Barnabas tugged his sleeve. “Saul.”

He blinked. The portico was nearly empty now, the shadows stretching long across the stones. Somewhere beyond the Temple walls, a dog barked, sharp and insistent.

“All right,” Saul said. “We go.”

But as they turned toward the stairs, a figure detached from the columns, a lean, dark-eyed man Saul didn't recognize. The stranger didn't speak or threaten. He only watched as they passed, his gaze tracking them like a blade drawn slow from its sheath.

Barnabas quickened his pace. Saul matched it, his sandals slapping against the steps. The evening breeze carried the scent of baking bread from the lower city, a cruel reminder of ordinary life.

At the base of the stairs, Barnabas hissed, “Don't look back.”

Saul looked anyway.

The dark-eyed man was gone. But near the portico's edge, three others stood clustered, their heads bent together. One glanced up met Saul's eyes, and smiled.

It wasn't a friendly smile.

Barnabas grabbed Saul's arm and yanked him into the press of pilgrims heading for the city gates. The crowd swallowed them, a current of bodies too thick for pursuit.

Saul's breath came fast. His palms were slick.

Silence him.

And beneath that, quieter but no less insistent, the old doubt:

Maybe they should.

The synagogue's oil lamps flickered against the soot-stained walls, casting uneven light over the gathered men. Saul ducked through the low doorway, the scent of hot wax and unwashed wool thick in his throat. The room fell quiet, not the hush of reverence, but the sharp silence of a snare tightening.

Barnabas nudged him toward a bench near the back. “Stay close to the door.”

Saul sat, his knees pressing against the rough-hewn wood. The scrolls in their niches seemed to watch him. A man across the room leaned to his neighbor, whispering behind a cupped hand. The neighbor's eyes darted to Saul, then away.

The leader, a broad-shouldered man with ink-stained fingers, cleared his throat. “We gather to discuss the portion from Deuteronomy.” His voice was too loud, too deliberate. “The dangers of false prophets.”

Saul's spine stiffened. A bead of sweat traced the hollow between his shoulder blades.

A boy passed around cups of watered wine. When he reached Saul, his hands shook, sloshing liquid over the rim. The stain spread dark across the bench.

Barnabas took the cup instead. “Peace be with you,” he murmured.

The boy fled without replying.

The leader unrolled a scroll with exaggerated care. “'If a prophet speaks in the name of the Lord but the thing does not take place or come true—'”

“Then he is false,” interrupted a gaunt man near the front. His beard was trimmed short, Roman-style. “And the penalty?”

“Death,” said another voice from the shadows.

The word hung in the air. The leader's jaw worked, but he didn't correct it. He read on, slower now. “'You shall not be afraid of him.'”

Saul's fingers dug into his thighs. The room was too warm, the air thick with the press of bodies and something else, a current like the moment before a storm broke.

A man near the front stood abruptly. “We all know why he's here.” He didn't turn or point, but the accusation was a blade laid bare on the table. “To turn our children from the traditions. To defile the Law with his new doctrines.”

Barnabas shifted, his shoulder brushing Saul's. “Let it pass,” he muttered.

But Saul's pulse roared in his ears. He stood. “I defile nothing. I uphold what Moses wrote, what the prophets foretold. The Messiah had to suffer.”

“Do not quote scripture at us,” the Roman-bearded man snapped. “Not with Stephen's blood still fresh on your hands.”

The name was a spark to tinder. Murmurs erupted, faces twisting. A woman near the doorway drew her child closer.

Saul's vision blurred at the edges. He forced air into his lungs. “I was wrong about Stephen. But I'm not wrong about this.”

“Convenient,” someone sneered.

The leader raised his hands. “Brothers—”

“No.” The Roman-bearded man stepped forward. “Let him answer. How many did you drag from their homes? How many did you watch flogged? And now you stand here, preaching their poison?”

Saul's throat burned. The memories rose unbidden: the screams, the pleading, the way the synagogue floors smelled after a scourging. Copper and salt.

“I did those things,” he said, low and raw. “And if that makes my testimony worthless in your eyes, so be it. But it doesn't make the truth a lie.”

A brittle laugh cut through the room. “Hear him! The persecutor turned preacher. Next he'll say the dead rise!”

“They do,” Saul said.

Silence.

Then the room erupted.

Barnabas grabbed Saul's arm, hauling him back as men surged to their feet. Spittle flew. A cup overturned, wine bleeding across the floor. The leader shouted, but no one listened.

“Enough!” The Roman-bearded man slammed his fist against the wall. The room stilled. He turned to Saul, his smile thin as a knife's edge. “You want to preach resurrection? Fine. Let's see if yours is as convincing as Stephen's.”

The threat coiled in the air.

Barnabas pulled Saul toward the door. “We're leaving.”

No one stopped them.

Outside, the night air was a slap. Saul gulped it down, his hands shaking. The street was empty, the moon a sliver overhead.

Barnabas's grip was iron. “That was a mistake.”

Saul wiped his mouth. “They needed to hear it.”

“They heard. And now—” Barnabas cut himself off, listening.

Footsteps echoed from an alleyway. Not the random shuffle of pilgrims, but the deliberate tread of men moving with purpose.

Barnabas cursed under his breath. “The house near the Essene Gate. Go. Now.”

Saul hesitated. “What about you?”

“I'll draw them off.” Barnabas shoved him toward a side street. “Move!”

Saul ran.

The alley twisted, narrow and reeking of urine. His sandals slipped on the uneven stones. Behind him, voices rose—Barnabas's, loud and mocking, then others, angry, fading as he led them away.

Saul ducked behind a crumbling wall, his breath ragged. Cold seeped through his tunic. Somewhere nearby, a dog growled.

Then voices again—closer this time.

“—not the one we want.”

“Barnabas is a fool. But the other—”

A pause. The crunch of gravel underfoot.

Saul pressed himself flat against the wall.

“The Sanhedrin won't act. Not openly.” The voice was familiar, the Roman-bearded man from the synagogue. “But a zealot's death... well. Who's to say who struck the blow?”

A chuckle, dark and wet. “A knife in the dark. Or a fall. The rocks are sharp in the Kidron.”

“Make it swift. And make it soon.”

The footsteps receded.

Saul's knees gave out. He slid down the wall, the stones scraping his back.

A zealot's death.

The irony choked him. He'd orchestrated such plots, whispered in corners, arranged the “accidents,” the convenient mobs. Now the script had flipped, and he was the heretic to be silenced.

A gust of wind rattled the loose tiles above him. The moon vanished behind a cloud, plunging the alley into blackness.

Somewhere in the dark, a cat yowled.

Saul closed his eyes. The faces of the men he'd arrested flickered behind his lids, not as he'd last seen them, bruised and defiant, but as they might look now, hearing the news: Saul of Tarsus, dead in an alley.

Would they mourn? Or laugh?

He pushed himself up, his palms gritty with dirt. The house near the Essene Gate was still a mile away, through streets that no longer felt like sanctuary.

He couldn't stay here.

He stepped into the open, the night air sharp against his sweat-damp skin. The city loomed around him, its walls suddenly foreign, its shadows alive with unseen watchers.

Somewhere ahead, a shutter banged in the wind.

Like the sound of a trap snapping shut.

The hidden room smelled of damp wool and lamp oil, the air thick enough to chew. Saul pressed his back against the rough plaster wall, his fingers finding the grooves where the mortar had cracked. A single clay lamp guttered on the low table, its flame throwing jagged shadows across the faces of the four men hunched around it.

Barnabas leaned forward, his knuckles white around the edge of the table. “You can't stay. Not after tonight.”

Saul exhaled through his nose. The taste of the synagogue's wine still clung to the back of his tongue, sour and metallic. “Running won't prove my innocence.”

“It's not about proof.” The oldest of them, a potter named Reuben with hands like gnarled olive roots, rubbed at a stain on the table. “It's about blood. And they're eager to spill yours.”

A shutter rattled somewhere in the house. Saul's shoulders twitched. He hadn't heard footsteps, but the night was full of sounds, real and imagined.

Barnabas pushed a wineskin across the table. “Drink. You look like death.”

Saul ignored it. “If I go, they'll say I fled because my message couldn't stand scrutiny.”

“Scrutiny?” A younger man—Joel, one of the Cypriots—snorted. “They weren't debating theology in that alley. They were measuring your throat for a blade.”

The lamp flickered. Saul watched the shadows leap, his pulse a dull throb in his temples. He knew the arguments could recite them in his sleep: the danger, the necessity, the greater good. But the thought of slipping away like a thief curdled in his gut.

“I didn't come back to Jerusalem to hide,” he said.

Reuben's laugh was a dry rasp. “And yet here you are.”

Barnabas shot the old man a look, then turned back to Saul. “You want to stand firm? Fine. But not here. Not now. Go to Tarsus. Wait. Let this blow over.”

“Things don't 'blow over' when the Sanhedrin's involved.” Joel drummed his fingers against his thigh. “They'll send word to every synagogue between here and Damascus. You'll be a marked man.”

Saul's jaw clenched. He'd been a marked man since the road, since the light and the voice and the scales falling from his eyes. The difference was, now the mark was his own.

A knock at the door: three quick raps, a pause, then two more. Joel rose, his chair scraping against the packed earth floor. He cracked the door, exchanged murmured words with someone outside, then turned back, his face grim.

“They're searching the lower city. House by house.”

Barnabas stood so fast his cup overturned. Wine seeped into the wood like old blood. “Then we move. Now.”

Saul didn't budge. “And if they find you harboring me? What then?”

“Then we'll answer for it.” Barnabas yanked a cloak from a peg on the wall and thrust it at him. “But not tonight. Tonight, you're going.”

The wool was coarse against Saul's palms. He stared at it, the reality of the moment pressing down like a millstone. He'd walked into Jerusalem with Barnabas's arm around his shoulders, the believers' wary acceptance a fragile shield. Now he'd leave under cover of darkness, a fugitive again.

The irony tasted bitter.

Joel edged toward the door, peering through the crack. “Clear for now. But we need to hurry.”

Reuben pushed himself up with a grunt. “I'll take him. My nephew's got a cart waiting near the Essene Gate. Load of pottery for Hebron.”

“Hebron?” Saul shook his head. “I need to go north.”

“You'll go where we send you,” Barnabas snapped. “At least as far as Caesarea. After that, you're on your own.”

Saul opened his mouth to argue, but the words died in his throat. Barnabas's face was set, his usual patience worn thin. This wasn't a debate. It was an extraction.

He stood, the cloak dragging at his arms like a weight. “You're making a mistake.”

Barnabas's smile was thin. “We'll see.”

Joel handed Saul a small pouch. “Bread. Cheese. Enough for two days.”

The pouch was lighter than it should have been. Saul tucked it into his belt, his fingers brushing the hilt of the knife he wasn't supposed to have. A gift from a Syrian trader in Damascus, its edge honed to a whisper. He hadn't told Barnabas about it. Some instincts died hard.

Reuben shuffled to the door, his joints creaking. “Stay close. And for God's sake, keep your head down.”

The alley behind the house was a slit of darkness, the moon obscured by clouds. Saul followed the old man's hunched silhouette, his sandals whispering against the stones. The night air was cooler here, laced with the scent of damp clay from the potter's quarter.

Somewhere to the east, a shout echoed, too distant to decipher, but close enough to tighten Saul's shoulders. Reuben didn't react, his pace steady as he led them through a maze of backstreets, past shuttered workshops and sleeping courtyards.

At the corner of a tannery, the stench of urine and lime sharp in his nose, Saul caught Barnabas's sleeve. “You're not coming?”

Barnabas shook his head. “I'll draw their eyes. Make them think you're still in the city.”

“And if they catch you?”

“Then I'll remind them I'm a Levite.” Barnabas's grin was all teeth. “Even the high priest thinks twice before striking a temple servant.”

Saul's throat tightened. The lie was obvious—Barnabas had no such protection, not anymore. Not since he'd thrown his lot in with the Nazarenes.

He gripped Barnabas's arm. “Don't be a fool.”

“Too late for that.” Barnabas pulled him into a rough embrace, his beard scratching Saul's cheek. “Go. Preach. Just not here. Not yet.”

Then he was gone, melting into the shadows with the ease of a man who'd spent a lifetime navigating Jerusalem's seams.

Reuben tugged Saul's cloak. “Move.”

The cart was where he'd promised, a rickety thing piled high with jugs and bowls, their unglazed surfaces pale in the dim light. A young man, Reuben's nephew sat hunched on the bench, his face half-hidden by a hood.

“Get in,” Reuben muttered. “Under the straw.”

Saul hesitated. The space between the cart's slats was barely large enough for a child, let alone a grown man.

Reuben shoved him. “You want to live? Get in.”

The straw prickled against Saul's skin as he wedged himself beneath the pottery, his knees drawn to his chest. Reuben tossed a blanket over the load, plunging him into near darkness.

A whip cracked. The cart lurched forward, the wheels grinding against the stones.

Saul closed his eyes. The rhythm of the cart was uneven, jolting him against the rough wood with every turn. He focused on the pain, the sharp edges digging into his ribs, the sweat trickling down his spine. Better than thinking about the road ahead. Better than remembering the last time he'd fled Jerusalem, the letters in his pack still warm from the high priest's seal.

The cart slowed. Voices filtered through the straw, gruff, official.

“Where to?”

“Hebron.” The nephew's voice was calm. “Market day.”

A pause. Saul held his breath.

“Open the back.”

The blanket shifted. A hand groped through the straw, close enough for Saul to smell the garlic on the man's fingers. He pressed himself deeper into the cart's belly, his pulse hammering in his ears.

Then, miraculously, the hand withdrew.

“Move along.”

The cart creaked into motion again. Saul didn't exhale until they'd rounded two more corners, until the sounds of the gate faded behind them.

The road north was long. The night longer.

And somewhere in the dark, a knife waited.
