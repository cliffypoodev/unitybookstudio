The road was a pale scar across the brown hills. Dust, fine as ground bone, coated Touch's tongue. It settled in the creases of his knuckles, the sweat-damp collar of his tunic. The sun was a white hammer on the back of his neck. They had walked since before first light, the rhythm of their sandals a dry percussion against the stone paving. Micah walked ahead, a silent, broad-shouldered shadow. Elazar and the others trailed behind, their conversation a low murmur about the price of wine in Damascus compared to Jerusalem. The world had narrowed to heat, dust, and the constant forward press of his own will.

The letter was a live coal against his chest, wrapped in oiled cloth inside his tunic. He felt its corners with every stride. Authority. Purpose. Each step was an argument against the woman's stare, against the cold shiver under the stars. The Law was a straight line. His path was its echo.

A lizard darted across the stones, a blur of ochre. It vanished into a crack. His eyes tracked the empty space. The High Priest's voice surfaced, unbidden. A messiah who fulfills the Law renders it complete. A finished thing. Heresy. A door slammed shut on a room still full of people. His fist clenched. The dust gritted between his fingers.

“How much farther?” Elazar's voice was edged with a whine. “My water's half gone.”

“You drank it too fast,” Micah said without turning. “The city is there when it is there.”

Touch didn't answer. The hills shimmered in the heat. Damascus was out there, a knot of streets and synagogues and hidden believers. He rehearsed the names on his list. He saw the arrests in his mind—the confrontation, the citation of scripture, the firm application of Temple authority. Clean. Surgical. A purification.

A donkey brayed in the distance, a rusty-hinge sound. The smell of thyme rose from a scrubby bush beside the road, crushed under someone's foot. These details were irrelevant. He pushed them aside. They were static, interference in the clear signal of his mission.

His heel throbbed where the blister had broken. The pain was a pinpoint of focus. Good. The body's complaints were petty. He welcomed them. They were a sacrifice offered on the altar of conviction.

“Saul.” Micah had fallen back beside him. He held out his waterskin. “Drink.”

“I have my own.”

“You haven't touched it. Your lips are cracked.”

Touch took the skin and drank. Its water was warm, tasting of leather. It did nothing to slake the dust. He handed it back. Micah watched him, his eyes squinted against the glare. He had that look again—the one from the inn. As if Touch were a clay vessel he was checking for hairline cracks.

“The men are talking,” Micah said, his voice low.

“Let them talk.”

“They talk about Stephen. They saw the family at the gate too.”

A hot needle twisted behind Touch's ribs. “What of it? A heretic died. His family grieves a lie. Their grief does not sanctify the error.”

Micah was silent for a dozen steps. The road began a gentle ascent. “I only report what I hear.”

“I did not ask for a report.”

He nodded, falling behind again. The friction was a low heat between Touch's shoulder blades. Micah was loyal, but his loyalty was to a man, not the mission. He worried about Touch's state. The mission required no state, only action.

The sun climbed. The light changed. It wasn't the gentle gold of morning, or the white fury of noon. It thickened. The air grew heavy, pressing down on the crown of his head. The dust motes hung suspended, glowing. His breath came shorter. Not from the climb.

Something was wrong with the sky.

Too blue. A depthless, polished blue, like the glaze on a ceramic bowl. The hills lost their detail, becoming flat cutouts against it. No birdsong. The chatter of the men behind him faded, swallowed by the strange, dense silence. Even their footsteps seemed muffled.

Touch stopped.

Micah stopped a pace ahead. He turned. “What is it?”

Touch couldn't name it. The air hummed. Not a sound, but a vibration in the teeth, in the marrow of his wrists. The light wasn't just bright. It was accumulating, pooling around them. The shadows of the rocks bled away, erased.

“Do you feel that?” His own voice sounded distant.

Micah looked around, wary. “Feel what? The heat?”

Not heat. A pressure. A presence, vast and focused, condensing in the atmosphere. The letter against Touch's chest burned. The straight line of the Law wavered in his mind's eye, a scribe's hand suddenly unsteady.

Then the world dissolved.

No. Not dissolved. Replaced.

From the center of that impossible blue, light erupted. It was not sunlight. It had no source, no direction. It was simply there, all of it, everywhere at once. A whiteness so absolute it was neither color nor void. It scoured the hills, the road, his companions' startled faces. It bleached everything to a single, searing fact of illumination.

It hit him in the chest. A physical force.

It threw him backward. His body left the ground. For a suspended instant, he was floating in pure, silent white. No heat. No cold. A terrifying, total clarity.

Then he struck the stones.

The air blasted from his lungs. The world snapped back—but only to sound. The roar was inside his skull, a tidal wave of noise that was also a voice. It was not heard with ears. It etched itself directly onto the fabric of his being.

SAUL.

The stones dug into his back. He couldn't breathe. The white filled his eyes, his nose, his mouth. He was drowning in light.

WHY DO YOU PERSECUTE ME?

The question was not words. It was a universe of meaning delivered in a single stroke. It contained the smell of blood on stones, the peace on Stephen's face, the dry wax of the Temple seal, the weight of every warrant he had ever signed. It contained the woman's grief and Micah's worry and the dusty taste of the road. It was an accusation that was also an invitation, a demolition that was also a revelation.

Touch's mouth worked. “Who are you, Lord?”

The title was ripped from him. Instinct. Protocol before a power so absolute it made the High Priest a guttering candle.

I AM JESUS, WHOM YOU ARE PERSECUTING.

The name landed like a second impact.

Jesus. The Nazarene. The crucified fool. The dead heresy.

But he was not dead. The voice was alive. It was the source of the light. It was the thing Touch had been hunting, and it had been hunting him. His whole life—his scholarship, his zeal, his righteous fury—was a frantic flight from this, from the living center of everything he thought he defended.

The light did not fade. It changed. It poured into him, through the cracks the question had opened. It filled the dark, armored places where he kept his certainty. It showed them to him. Not as fortresses, but as tombs.

A sound escaped him. Not a word. A fracture.

The white began to recede from the world, but it did not leave his eyes. It stayed there, pooled behind his lids. When he forced them open, nothing changed. The world was gone.

Blackness. A perfect, seamless void.

He pushed himself up on his elbows. Grit pressed into his palms. He heard gasping, shouting—his men. Their voices were smeared, coming from the end of a long tunnel.

“Saul!” Micah's hands were on his shoulders, shaking. “What happened? What did you see?”

Touch turned his head toward Micah's voice. He saw nothing. Not shadow. Not shape. An absence so complete it was a presence in itself.

“I see nothing,” he whispered. The words were ash.

His hands came up and touched his face. His eyes were open. He could feel the blink, the scrape of lid on eyeball. But the blackness remained. The brilliant, world-ending light had burned out his sight and left this.

One voice echoed in the emptiness it had created.

Why do you persecute me?

The question was now inside the blackness with him. It was the only thing left.

“Lead me,” Touch said. His voice was not his own. It was a stranger's, cracked and thin. “Lead me into Damascus.”

Micah's hands tightened on his arms. He pulled Touch to his feet. His legs held, but they were someone else's legs. The world under his sandals was an abstraction. The road, the city, the mission—all of it was gone. There was only the black void, and the question reverberating in its endless depths.

Micah began to guide him forward. One blind step. Then another.

* * *

The hands on Touch's arms were anchors. Without them, he would have drifted away, a piece of ash on a thermal. Micah's grip was all that tethered him to the earth. The ground under his feet was a rumor. He heard the scrape of their sandals, the crunch of grit. He felt the pressure against his soles. But it meant nothing. His body was a hollow reed, and the black wind blew through it.

Voices swirled around him, sharp with panic.

“—like a thunderclap, but no sound—”

“—threw him like a doll—”

“What did he say? What did he say to it?”

They were children's voices, high and thin. These were the men who had followed him from Jerusalem. They had clutched their warrants like holy relics. Now they were clutching each other.

“Keep moving.” Micah's voice was close to his ear. Strained, a rope pulled taut. He was not speaking to Touch. He was herding them. “Elazar, take his other arm. Don't stand there gawking.”

Another set of hands, hesitant and clammy, gripped Touch's left elbow. Elazar. He could smell the sour fear on his breath. Elazar pulled, out of sync with Micah. Touch stumbled. His knee hit a stone. The pain was a bright, specific point in the formless dark. He welcomed it. A landmark.

“Sorry, sorry,” Elazar whispered.

They moved again. The rhythm was wrong. Three-legged, staggering. Touch was a burden between them. His sandals dragged. The glorious, purposeful stride of the morning was a memory from someone else's life.

The blackness was not empty. It was full of the afterimage. Not light, but its ghost. A negative of the world. When he turned his head, he could almost sense the bulk of the hills to his right, a greater density of nothing. The road was a slight hollow feeling, a worn path in the void. His mind painted pictures on the inside of his skull. The lizard's ochre flash. The thyme bush. The cracks in the stones. Each memory was a tiny, desperate stitch in the unraveling fabric of reality.

But the voice was the only real thing.

It wasn't repeating. It was residing. It had taken up space in the newly blasted chambers of his mind. It echoed not in sound, but in consequence. Every breath he took was an answer. A wrong one.

He had spoken to it. Who are you, Lord? The courtesies of the courtroom, automatic. His tongue had betrayed him. It had granted sovereignty to the accused.

The words were not a sound. They were a key. They turned in the lock of his life, and every door swung open at once. Stephen's face, not in death, but in the moment he looked up and said, Lord, do not hold this sin against them. The peace there. It wasn't stoicism. It wasn't defiance. Recognition. Stephen had seen this same light. He had heard this same voice. And Touch, standing there holding the coats, had called it demonic inspiration.

His stomach clenched. A spasm, dry and violent. Nothing came up. There was nothing inside him but dust and the dregs of warm water.

“He's retching,” Elazar said, his voice tight with disgust.

“He's not,” Micah snapped. “Keep walking.”

Touch wasn't. It was just his body trying to expel the understanding. It wouldn't go. Its truth was not in his stomach. It was in his bones. It had rewritten the marrow.

The sounds of the road began to filter back. The distant donkey again. The buzz of flies. The world was continuing. Obscene. How could there be flies? How could there be donkeys? The axis of creation had just been sheared in two, and the insects hummed their same oblivious song.

His blindness was a separate terror. It was a prison built inside the ruins of his soul. He tried to force his eyes to work. He squeezed them shut until colors sparked in the pressure—meaningless swirls of ochre and violet—then opened them wide. The result was the same. A black so complete it had texture. A velvet shroud pressed against his face.

I am blind.

The thought arrived plain, factual. Then it unfolded.

He was blind on a road in the desert. He could not read the Law. He could not write a warrant. He could not recognize a heretic's face. He could not find his way to the synagogue. He could not see the stars he used to navigate by. He was a child. Worse than a child—a newborn, helpless, led by the hand.

A noise leaked from his throat. A whimper.

Micah's hand on his arm tightened. “Not far now. I can see the cypress grove at the spring. The city gate is beyond it.”

His words were a map drawn in the air. Touch couldn't see it. The cypress grove was a story. A city gate was a myth. His Damascus was gone. The Damascus of warrants and arrests and righteous purification had vanished in the light. What lay ahead was an unknown city, a maze of streets he would never navigate.

“The letters,” Touch croaked. His first words since the command to lead him.

“What?” Micah leaned closer.

“The letters. In my tunic.”

Micah's hand hesitated, then moved. Touch felt the pressure of his fingers through the wool, probing, finding the oiled cloth packet. He drew it out. The slight weight of it leaving Touch's chest felt like the removal of a vital organ.

“I have them.”

“Destroy them.”

A pause. The shuffling of their feet. The other men had gone quiet, listening. “Saul, this is the High Priest's authority. Sealed.”

“Burn them.” The words were grit in Touch's raw throat. “Tear them. Scatter the pieces. Now.”

It was not an order. It was a confession. The authority was counterfeit. The seal was ash. It had been burned away by a brighter fire.

Touch heard the rustle of cloth, the sharp, definitive tear of papyrus. Once, twice, again. A sound like a small death. Elazar made a faint, protesting sound. Micah grunted, a sound of effort, and then a handful of scraps, light as moth wings, brushed across Touch's face and chest, caught in the wind of their movement. They fell away behind them.

The last tether snapped. He was adrift.

The mission was over. It had been over before it began. He had been hunting a ghost, and the ghost was alive, and it had been standing behind him the whole time, watching his frantic, murderous pursuit with a patience that now felt like the most terrifying thing of all.

They reached the spring. Touch knew it by the sudden coolness washing over his skin, by the smell of wet stone and damp earth, a shocking richness after the dust, by the sound of water—a quiet, chuckling melody over rock. A sound of profound indifference. The world did not care about his blindness. The water flowed.

They lowered him to sit on a flat stone. The chill seeped through his tunic. Micah guided his hands to the water's edge. His fingers touched liquid cold. He cupped them and brought water to his face. It was a shock. It ran down his neck, into his tunic. He did it again. He couldn't wash the light away. It was inside.

“Drink,” Micah said.

Touch drank from his hands. The water was sweet, alive. It made the dust in his throat into mud. He coughed.

The men were huddled nearby. He heard their whispers, no longer trying to hide.

“A stroke. The sun—”
“—epilepsy. My cousin had fits. Saw lights.”
“He spoke to it. He called it ‘Lord.’”
“What does that mean for us? We carried the warrants too. Are we... cursed?”

Their fear was a tangible thing, a sour odor in the clean air. They were not afraid for him. They were afraid of him. He was the contaminated one. He had touched the live wire of heaven and it had burned out his eyes, and now they were afraid the charge might jump to them.

Touch wanted to laugh. The sound would have been madness.

They didn't understand. The curse was not in the punishment. The curse was in the answer. I am Jesus. The curse was the glorious, unbearable truth. They were still worried about Temple reprisal. Touch had just met the Temple's founder, and he had called him a persecutor.

Micah settled beside him. His shoulder pressed against Touch's. A solid, warm pressure. He didn't speak. He just sat. He was waiting for Touch to be Saul again. The Saul who knew what to do.

That Saul was dead. He had died on the stones back there. This was a new creature, blind and gasping in its birth sac of darkness.

“We cannot enter the city like this,” Touch said. His voice was flat. “Not with me like this.”

“We'll find the house of Judas on Straight Street. As planned.”

“And say what? That the Pharisee with the letters is blind? That God struck him down on the road? They will think it a trick. Or a judgment. They will bolt. Or they will stone us as impostors.” The practical problems rose up, a pathetic distraction from the cosmic ones. They were the only problems his mind could grasp. Logistics. A broken tool.

Micah was silent for a long moment. “Then what would you have us do?”

Touch had nothing. No plan. No authority. No sight. The helplessness was a weight, pressing him into the stone. He was a leader who could not lead, a scholar who could not read, a zealot whose zeal had been turned inside out and shown to be the engine of his own ruin.

“I don't know,” he said, low.

The admission left him hollow. It was the truest thing he had said.

They sat by the water. The men's whispers died down. The only sounds were the spring and Touch's own breathing, too loud in his ears. The black void was no longer just before his eyes. It was in his future. An endless corridor of nothing. No Law to guide him. No mission to define him. No enemy to fight. Just this dependent, stumbling existence.

The voice did not speak again. It didn't need to. Its one question was a plow. It had torn up the field of his life, and now he was lying in the furrow, waiting for a seed he could not see and did not deserve.

Micah stood. His knees cracked. “The day is wasting. We need shelter before night. Can you walk?”

Touch held up a hand. Micah took it and pulled him to his feet. His legs held. They would do that much. They would carry this ruin toward a city he could not see, to a people he had come to destroy, for reasons he no longer understood.

“Yes,” Touch said.

One blind step. Then another.

The city announced itself long before they reached the walls. It started as a hum underneath the whisper of the spring, a vibration in the soles of Touch's feet. Then the smells came, layer upon layer, a confusing assault. Woodsmoke, sharp and resinous. Animal dung, sweet and rotten. The metallic tang of a forge. Spices—cumin, coriander—carried on the warm pulse of the city. The clean desert air curdled, thickened with the press of humanity.

His grip on Micah's arm became a vise. Every sound was a potential collision. A cart rumbling by, the axle squealing at a pitch that sawed at his nerves. A merchant's shout, too close. The bleat of a goat. Laughter from a doorway. The world was a chaotic symphony played by unseen musicians, and he was standing in the middle of the orchestra, waiting to be struck.

“Gate's ahead,” Micah muttered. “Roman guards. Say nothing.”

The sounds changed. Their footsteps echoed briefly on stone, then were swallowed by a deeper, resonant din. They were under the arch. The air grew cooler, damp. It smelled of urine and old mortar. Touch heard the clink of armor, the bored mutter of Latin. A hand shot out and grabbed his chest, halting him. Rough wool, the smell of old sweat.

“State your business.” The voice was a gravelly baritone, inches from his face.

Micah answered, his tone carefully neutral, the voice of a man used to borders. “Merchants from Jerusalem. Seeking the house of Judas, a leatherworker, on Straight Street.”

A pause. The guard's hand remained. “He's blind?”

“A sun-sickness. It will pass.”

One guard grunted. Touch could feel his eyes on him. Assessing. A blind man was a beggar, a thief, a liability. He stared into the blackness where the man's face must be, his own face a rigid mask. Shame, hot and liquid, pooled in his gut. He, Saul of Tarsus, Pharisee, Roman citizen, was being inspected like a dubious piece of luggage.

The hand withdrew. “Move along.”

They moved. The noise exploded.

Damascus was a beast, and they were in its belly. The din was a physical thing, a wall of sound that pressed the air from Touch's lungs. A hundred conversations in Aramaic, Greek, Nabatean, a babbling torrent. The hammering of a coppersmith. The shriek of a saw. Children shrieking as they darted through the crowd. Donkeys braying in protest. Directionless, sourceless. A sound from every angle, a world of activity he could not manage or parse. He flinched at a sudden clatter to his right—a potter's stall collapsing? Just a cart hitting a rut. His heart hammered against his ribs.

Micah and Elazar guided him, but it was a clumsy, harrowing dance. They pulled him left to avoid a jostling crowd, then right, then stopped short as something blocked their path. His shoulders bumped into unseen people. Muttered curses followed him. “Watch it, blind man!” The words were a lash. His face burned.

The smells intensified, a nauseating fabric. Roasting meat from a street vendor. The pungent reek of a tannery. Perfume from a passing woman, cloying and floral. The stench of an open sewer. Touch's empty stomach clenched. He let the air out through his mouth, tasting the city's foulness.

This was the prize. This bustling, stinking, pagan-infested hive was the jewel he had come to cleanse. He had imagined marching through these streets with the authority of the Temple, the warrants a sword in his hand. He would have seen the fear in the faces of the followers. He would have noted the architecture, the alleyways, the safe houses. Now he was a piece of driftwood in its torrent, blind and helpless. His great mission had been reduced to the desperate focus of not falling, not vomiting, not crying out.

They turned a corner. The noise muted slightly, the press of bodies lessened. The ground underfoot felt smoother, more even.

“Straight Street,” Micah said, his voice taut with relief.

The question still hung in the darkness, unanswered, unanswerable, the only signpost in a country where all the maps had burned.
