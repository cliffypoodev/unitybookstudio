The cart jolted over a rut, slamming Saul's ribs against the pottery. He bit down on a groan. The nephew up front hissed something to the donkey, a low, steady sound that did nothing to smooth the road.

Dawn light seeped through the straw. Not gold—gray. The kind of light that made everything look half-dead. Saul shifted, trying to ease the cramp in his thigh. His sandal scraped against a jug. The nephew muttered a curse.

"Quiet," Reuben said.

Saul closed his eyes. The air under the blanket was thick with dust and the sour tang of unfired clay. He could taste it, gritty on his tongue.

The road climbed. The cart slowed. Voices again—not soldiers this time. Merchants maybe. Pilgrims. Someone laughed. The sound was bright, careless. It made Saul's teeth ache.

Reuben's hand brushed the straw near Saul's shoulder. A warning. The cart stopped.

"News from the city?" a stranger's voice asked, rough with morning.

"Nothing worth hearing," Reuben said.

"Nothing ever is." A pause. The cart creaked as someone leaned against it. "You're early for the Hebron market."

"Pottery cracks if you let it dry too fast."

The stranger grunted. The cart lurched forward again.

Saul exhaled. His fingers found the edge of the blanket, pinched it back just enough to see slivers of the road. Pale dust. Scattered stones. The shadow of the cart stretching long and thin ahead of them.

He'd walked this road before. Not hiding. Not like this.

One memory came sharp—letters in his pack, the high priest's seal heavy against his hip. The dust then had been the same. The stones. The sun hammering down. He'd kicked a pebble, watched it skitter ahead of him, and thought nothing of it.

Now the pebble would have been a sign.

The cart hit another rut. Saul's elbow struck a bowl. A sound rang loud in the close space.

"Damn it—" The nephew twisted around, yanked the blanket tighter. "You want to get us all stoned?"

Saul didn't answer. The boy's fear was a live thing, twitching in his voice. Let him have it.

The road curved. The air changed—drier, thinner. They were leaving the city's shadow. Saul could feel it in the way the ground tilted, the way the cart rolled a little faster.

Reuben leaned down. "We'll stop at the next cistern. You can ride upright from there."

Saul nodded. His throat was too dry to speak.

The cistern was a crumbling circle of stone, half-buried in the hillside. The nephew unharnessed the donkey and led it to the water. Reuben stood watch, his back to the road.

Saul crawled out. His knees cracked. His tunic was damp with sweat, stuck to his back in patches. He rubbed his hands over his face, scraping away straw and dust.

The water was greenish, scummed with algae. He cupped it anyway and drank fast. It tasted like mud and old metal.

Reuben tossed him a fig. "Eat. You look like death."

Saul caught it. The skin was split, oozing syrup. He bit down. Too sweet. Cloying. He forced himself to swallow.

The nephew watched him, arms crossed. "You're trouble."

"Yes."

The boy blinked. He'd expected denial maybe. Justification. Saul wiped his hands on his thighs. Let him chew on that.

They repacked the cart. Saul sat up front now, legs dangling over the edge. The road unspooled ahead, pale and endless. Behind them, Jerusalem was a smudge on the horizon.

Reuben flicked the reins. The donkey plodded on.

"You'll go to Caesarea," Reuben said. "There's a ship there, bound for Tarsus."

Saul stared at the road. "Barnabas arranged this."

"Barnabas has sense."

The sun climbed. Dust rose in little puffs where the cart wheels bit into the road. Saul's sandals were coated with it, his toes gritty.

He should say something. Argue. Explain why he needed to stay, why running was worse than dying. The words sat in his chest, weightless.

The nephew hummed under his breath. A psalm maybe. Something with a steady rhythm. Saul tuned it out.

A hawk circled overhead. Saul watched it tilt on the wind, wings spread wide. It didn't flap. It just rode the air, effortless.

He'd seen hawks like that outside Tarsus, perched on the cliffs, watching the sea.

The road dipped. Jerusalem vanished behind a fold of hills.

Reuben cleared his throat. "You'll write. Let us know you arrived."

Saul nodded. The motion felt hollow.

The donkey's ears twitched. Flies. Always flies.

They passed a shepherd with a handful of scraggly goats. The man raised a hand in greeting. Reuben returned it. Saul kept his eyes on the road.

Silence stretched. Not peaceful. Not even quiet, really—just the absence of words. The cart creaked. The donkey's hooves scuffed the dust. The nephew's humming had stopped.

Saul flexed his hands. His knuckles were cracked, the skin rough. He'd washed them in the cistern, but the dust was ground in.

Like the blood maybe.

He closed his eyes. The sun pressed against his lids, red and hot.

The cart rolled on.

* * *

The harbor stank of fish and tar. Saul stood at the edge of the quay, his sandals gritty with crushed shells. Reuben had vanished into the crowd—gone to find the ship's captain, gone to pay whatever bribe would secure passage without questions. The nephew lingered a few paces back, arms crossed, watching the gulls wheel over the water like scraps of dirty linen tossed into the wind.

Caesarea was too bright. The white stone of Herod's buildings glared under the sun, the sea a sheet of hammered bronze. Everywhere, movement: slaves hauling amphorae up gangplanks, merchants haggling over bolts of Syrian silk, soldiers lounging near the customs house with their shields propped against their knees. A pair of legionaries passed close enough for Saul to catch the sour tang of their sweat. One of them eyed him—the dust-streaked tunic, the unkempt beard—and smirked.

Saul turned away. His ribs ached where the pottery had bruised them.

A ship's boy darted past, barefoot, his arms full of rope. He shouted something in a dialect Saul didn't recognize. The words dissolved into the clamor of the docks—the creak of rigging, the slap of waves against pilings, the rhythmic thud of a mallet somewhere down the quay.

Reuben reappeared, his face slick with sweat. "The Dioscuri. Berth seven." He jerked his chin toward a broad-beamed merchant vessel. Its hull was streaked with salt, the painted eyes on its prow chipped and faded. "Captain's a Lycian. Doesn't care who he carries as long as the coin's good."

Saul nodded. The motion made the sun flare behind his eyes.

They picked their way through the crowd. A camel balked, its load of cedar logs swaying dangerously. The handler cursed, yanking on its nose ring. Saul sidestepped, his shoulder brushing against a stack of crates. The wood was rough under his fingers, splintered at the edges.

The Dioscuri loomed over them, its mast a stark line against the sky. The gangplank sagged under Saul's weight as he climbed aboard. The deck was cluttered with coils of rope, stacked amphorae, a chicken coop lashed near the rail. The air smelled of wet wood and old wine.

The captain stood near the helm, his tunic stained with grease. He held out a hand without looking at Saul. "Passage paid?"

Reuben passed him a small leather pouch. The captain weighed it in his palm, then tucked it into his belt. "You'll work your way. We're short-handed."

Saul opened his mouth, then closed it. Arguing would mean nothing here.

The nephew shifted behind him. "How long to Tarsus?"

"With fair winds? Ten days. Maybe twelve." The captain spat over the rail. "If the gods are pissing on us, could be twenty."

Reuben gripped Saul's arm. His fingers were calloused, the nails rimmed with dirt. "You'll write."

It wasn't a question. Saul nodded again.

The nephew shoved a bundle at him—bread, a wedge of cheese, a waterskin. His expression was unreadable. "Don't drown."

Saul took the bundle. The bread was already stale.

Reuben hesitated, then turned and descended the gangplank. The nephew followed, his sandals slapping against the wood. Neither looked back.

Sailors moved around Saul, their voices rough with commands. He stepped aside, pressing himself against the rail. The harbor sprawled below him, a chaos of color and noise. A priest of Poseidon walked the quay, sprinkling something from a bowl. The wind caught the grains—incense maybe, scattering them over the water.

The ship's boy scrambled up the rigging, nimble as a monkey. Saul watched him until his eyes burned.

A hand clapped his shoulder. "You. Help with the lines."

Saul turned. A grizzled sailor thrust a coil of rope into his hands. The fibers were rough, smelling of salt and old fish.

He worked without speaking. His palms stung by the time the last knot was secured. The sailors moved around him as if he were part of the ship's furniture, something to step over, something to ignore.

The captain bellowed an order. The gangplank creaked as it was hauled aboard.

Saul leaned against the rail. The quay was a blur of faces now. Somewhere in that crowd, Reuben and the nephew were walking away. Somewhere beyond the city walls, Jerusalem smoldered under the same sun.

The ship lurched as the oars bit into the water. The deck tilted under Saul's feet. He gripped the rail, the wood rough against his palms.

The wind picked up, carrying the scent of salt and foreign spices, cumin maybe, or myrrh. Something sharp and unfamiliar. The sails snapped taut overhead.

The sea stretched endless, a vast plain of shifting blue. Saul gripped the rail until his knuckles whitened. The ship rolled beneath him, each swell a reminder of how far he'd fallen, from the high priest's court to this: a fugitive hidden among cargo and chickens. The salt wind stung his face, carrying with it the reek of tar and unwashed bodies.

A sailor shoved past, muttering in Lycian. Saul didn't move. He stared at the horizon where land had been, where Jerusalem still stood, though he could no longer see it. The letters in his pack weighed heavier than the high priest's seal ever had.

"You'll write."

Reuben's words clung like burrs. What would he say? That he'd made it aboard without incident? That the captain hadn't asked questions? None of it mattered. The truth was written in the empty space where his name should have been, no longer Saul of Tarsus, no longer a Pharisee of Pharisees. Just a man with no place to return to.

The deck creaked. The captain stood beside him, squinting into the sun. "First time at sea?"

Saul didn't answer.

The man spat over the rail. "You'll puke before noon."

A gull screamed overhead. Saul watched it dive, wings tucked tight, and emerge with something silver flashing in its beak. The fish writhed once before vanishing.

He'd seen executions like that. Quick. Efficient. No wasted movement.

The memory rose unbidden—Stephen's face, upturned, not flinching as the stones struck. The way his voice had carried even as the blood dripped into his eyes. Lord, do not hold this sin against them.

Saul's stomach twisted. He gripped the rail tighter.

The captain smirked. "Told you."

Hammocks swayed with the ship's motion, empty for now. Saul found a space near the hull, where the timbers groaned loudest. He sat with his back against the wood, feeling each wave shudder through him.

The bread Reuben had given him was hard as brick. He gnawed at it anyway, the taste of dust and barley thick on his tongue. The cheese had gone oily in the heat. He ate it all.

A rat scuttled past, its eyes gleaming in the dim light. Saul watched it vanish into the shadows.

He'd slept in worse places.

The ship rolled. A loose barrel crashed against the far wall. Someone shouted above deck, the words lost in the creak of ropes and timbers.

Saul closed his eyes. The darkness behind his lids was no different from the blindness on the road to Damascus. Three days of it. Three days to sit with the weight of what he'd done.

And then, light.

Ananias' hands on his face, the shock of sight returning. The first thing he'd seen: a stranger's eyes, wide with fear and something else. Pity?

No. Not pity.

Brother.

The word had undone him.

The ship pitched sharply. Saul's head struck the hull. Pain bloomed bright behind his eyes. He gritted his teeth, waiting for it to fade.

Above, footsteps pounded across the deck. The captain's voice roared commands. The ship heeled hard to starboard, timbers screaming.

Saul staggered to his feet. He climbed the ladder, emerging into chaos.

The sky had darkened. Clouds massed on the horizon, their bellies bruised purple. The sailors scrambled, tying off lines, reefing the sails. Wind howled through the rigging.

A wave broke over the bow, drenching Saul to the skin. The cold shocked the breath from his lungs. He coughed, salt burning his throat.

The captain spotted him. "You, help them!" He jerked his chin toward a group of men wrestling with a loose sail.

Saul moved without thinking. The deck tilted beneath him, slick with seawater. He grabbed a fistful of canvas, his fingers numb with cold. The sail fought like a living thing, snapping in the wind.

A sailor cursed, his words ripped away by the gale. Together, they hauled the fabric down, tying it off with hands that shook from effort and fear.

The storm raged. The ship climbed each wave only to plunge into the trough, the bowsprit spearing the dark water. Saul clung to the mast, his arms burning.

Lightning split the sky. For an instant, the sea was frozen in stark relief, the waves like mountains, the ship a toy in their grasp.

Then darkness.

The thunder that followed shook the deck. Saul's ears rang with it.

Another wave hit. He lost his footing, skidding across the deck. His shoulder struck the rail. Pain lanced down his arm.

He gasped, the wind stealing the sound.

The sailor beside him grinned, his teeth a flash in the gloom. "Still standing, Jew?"

Saul didn't answer. He pushed himself up, gripping the rail.

The storm passed as quickly as it had come. The clouds broke, revealing a sky streaked with pink and gold. The sea gentled, the waves rolling rather than crashing.

The sailors slumped where they stood, exhausted. The captain clapped Saul on the back hard enough to stagger him. "Not bad for a landsman."

Saul wiped seawater from his face. His hands trembled.

The sailor who'd grinned at him tossed a wineskin. "Drink. You've earned it."

The wine was cheap, sour as vinegar. Saul swallowed it anyway. The burn in his throat grounded him.

The captain studied the horizon. "We'll make Cyprus by dusk tomorrow. Then north to Tarsus."

Saul handed the wineskin back. The sailor took it, his expression unreadable.

The sun dipped lower, painting the sea in hues of fire. Saul turned east, toward the distant haze where land would be.

Tarsus.

A city of his birth, where he'd studied under Gamaliel, where he'd first learned to parse the Law with razor precision.

Now it would be his prison.

The wind shifted, carrying the scent of rain and something else, olive groves, perhaps, or the distant hint of cedar.

Saul closed his eyes. The sun warmed his face.

When he opened them again, Jerusalem was gone.

Only the sea remained.
