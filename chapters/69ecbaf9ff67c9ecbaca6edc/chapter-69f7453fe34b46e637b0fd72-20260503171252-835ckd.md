The knock was the sound of a knuckle on hollow wood, three precise taps that cut through the last of Blaze’s words and left the room feeling thin, like the air had been stretched too tight. Zonk’s shoulder still held the ghost-warmth of Blaze’s hand. He let out a breath he’d been holding since the guy stood up. It tasted of dust and the faint, cloying perfume from the fake flowers woven into Pip’s hair.

“Showtime,” Blaze said, his voice dropping into something low and steady. The shift was immediate. The guy who’d just been talking about broken GPS systems was gone, replaced by a set jaw and shoulders that squared themselves against whatever was on the other side of the door. Zonk envied the switch. His own insides felt like a bag of wet cats, all claws and panicked motion with no direction.

Pip stood, her small hands patting down the ridiculous layers of her Gala performer’s skirt. It was mint green and covered in sequins that threw little sickly dots of light on the walls. She caught him looking and offered a smile that was too wide, the corners of her eyes pinched with a fear she was trying to swallow. It didn’t work. He saw it anyway. That was new, too—noticing the details of someone else’s fear instead of just his own. It was a shitty superpower.

The door opened before any of them could move. A silhouette filled the frame, tall and slender, armored in polished shadows that seemed to drink the light from the antechamber’s single crystal sconce. A Night Guard. The helmet was a stylized crescent moon, the face beneath it invisible.

“The Court assembles,” the guard intoned, voice filtered into a metallic hum. “The vessels will proceed.”

“Vessels,” Zonk muttered. “Charming.”

He pushed off from the wall he’d been leaning against. His legs felt loose, unbolted at the knees. He followed Blaze and Pip into the corridor, the Night Guard falling in behind them like a silent, malevolent shadow. The hallway was all white marble veined with threads of silver, lit by floating orbs of soft blue light that cast no warmth. Their footsteps echoed, a messy tap-tap-scuff rhythm against the perfect, eerie silence. Zonk fixated on a crack in the marble baseboard, a hairline imperfection that ran for ten feet before disappearing under a plinth. He wondered who’d installed it, if they’d been fired for the mistake, if they were still alive. The thought was pointless. It was better than thinking about what came next.

They were led upward, through a spiraling staircase that opened onto a vast, circular chamber. The Canterlot Spire’s ritual room. The ceiling was a dome of clear crystal, open to a sky the color of a deep bruise, purples and blacks bleeding into the first pinpricks of stars. In the center of the room, a massive crystal array rose from the floor—a geometric nest of interlocking prisms, currently inert and dark. Around the perimeter, figures stood in pools of shadow.

Princess Luna waited beside the array. She’d forgone her usual armor for a gown of deep blue shot through with silver threads that mimicked a starfield. She looked less like a military commander and more like a particularly elegant undertaker. Her smile was a thin curve of approval.

“Our instruments of change,” she said, her voice carrying in the hushed space. “The stage is set. The audience gathers.”

Zonk followed her gaze to the shadows. He picked out shapes now. A handful of the Old Herd survivors, their forms slight and draped in simple grey. A contingent of Luna’s personal guard, six of them, standing at rigid attention. And in a darker archway across the chamber, the distinct, hulking silhouettes of Solar Sentinels, their gold-and-white armor glinting dully. Celestia’s people. Watching.

A cold knot tightened in his gut. This wasn’t a covert operation. This was theater.

“You said this was a recalibration,” Zonk said, the words out before he could filter them. “Not a… public broadcast.”

“All meaningful change requires witnesses, Ezekiel,” Luna replied, gliding closer. She stopped before Pip, reaching out to adjust a stray curl of hair from the girl’s temple. Her touch made Pip flinch, just a tiny jerk she tried to hide. “The old magic must be seen to return. Its light must shame the false sun.”

Blaze shifted his weight, a low grumble in his throat. “Feels like we’re the main course at a dinner party.”

“You are the conduit,” Luna corrected, her eyes still on Pip. “The catalyst. Your unique bond—chaotic, unscripted, genuine—is the key the old locks have been waiting for.” She finally looked at Zonk. “You understand the theory. The Elements are tuned to a frequency of coercive control. You must retune them, using the weight of your own connection. It is not about power. It is about… Harmony.” She said the word like it was a private joke.

“Right. Harmony.” Zonk’s mouth was dry. He remembered the smell of his apartment, the stale weed and dust, the safe, predictable grime of his old life. This room smelled of ozone and cold stone.

Luna gestured to the center of the crystal array. Three shallow depressions formed a triangle there. “The primary Elements. You will hold them. Focus through them. The others are already placed in the secondary nodes.” Zonk saw them then—the pink gag ball, the yellow ointment jar, the purple flogger—each sitting in a smaller crystal cradle around the array’s edge, glowing with a faint, sickly light.

Pip moved first, drawn toward the center like a moth. She knelt in one of the depressions, her skirts pooling around her. After a second’s hesitation, Blaze took the spot to her right. Zonk was left with the position facing the main archway, where the Solar Sentinels watched. Great.

He knelt. The floor was freezing, even through the fabric of his costume. Luna approached, holding a velvet cushion. On it rested the three remaining Elements: the tiara of Magic, the silver tongue depressor of Honesty, and the red cuffs of Loyalty.

“Place them,” she instructed.

Pip took the tiara with reverent hands settling it on her head. The six-pointed star at its center flickered, then held a steady, pale violet light. Blaze took the cuffs. He didn’t put them on, just held them in his big palms, his thumbs rubbing the unbreakable metal. Zonk was left with the tongue depressor. It was cold and smooth, ridiculously small in his hand. A medical instrument. A truth-telling thing. He almost laughed.

“Join hands,” Luna said, stepping back into the ring of her guards. “Begin.”

Zonk reached out. Pip’s hand was tiny, trembling. Blaze’s was calloused, solid, and gripping his like a lifeline. The moment their skin connected, a jolt went through him—not electricity, but a sudden, vertiginous sense of connection, like a circuit closing. The crystal array beneath them began to hum, a low thrum he felt in his teeth.

“Think of your bond,” Luna’s voice floated from the periphery. “The unforced loyalty. The absurd kindness. The shared laughter.”

Zonk squeezed his eyes shut. He thought of Blaze covering for him at work when he was too baked to function. He thought of Pip trying to make their hideout “cozy” with stolen throw pillows. He thought of the three of them sharing a single greasy pizza on the floor of a storage locker, Pip trying to explain the political structure of the Sun Court through a mouthful of pepperoni. It wasn’t epic. It was stupid. It was theirs.

The hum deepened. A warmth spread from his clasped hands up his arms. He cracked an eye open. The tiara on Pip’s head was shining brighter, a clean, violet-white. The cuffs in Blaze’s hands glowed like heated iron. The tongue depressor in his own grip felt warm, almost alive.

Around the edge of the room, the secondary Elements lit up in sequence. A bubblegum pink pulse from the whoopee cushion. A gentle gold from the kindness salve. A deep amethyst from the flogger. Light began to travel through the crystal lattice of the array, connecting the points, building a geometric web of radiance above their heads.

It was working. The stupid, beautiful, genuine thing was actually working.

He looked at Pip. Tears were cutting clean tracks through the glitter on her cheeks, but she was smiling, a real one, full of wonder. Blaze was grinning like an idiot, his eyes wide. “Hell yeah,” Blaze said, low.

The light grew, painting the whole chamber in washes of color. The watching figures were silhouettes now. Zonk felt a wild, impossible hope crack open in his chest. They could do this. They could actually fix something.

Then Luna’s voice cut through the hum, clear and cold and utterly changed.

“Now,” she said.

The warmth in Zonk’s hands turned to a searing cold. The light from the Elements didn’t just brighten—it sharpened, beams focusing into laser lines that lanced out from the array, not toward the ceiling, but toward the Solar Sentinels standing in the archway. The violet beam from the tiara struck the lead Sentinel in the chest. There was no explosion, only a silent, horrific dissolution. The gold-and-white armor didn’t shatter; it unraveled, melting into motes of light that winked out, leaving the stunned, ordinary man inside to crumple to the floor, gasping.

“What the fuck?” Blaze yelled, trying to pull his hand back.

He couldn’t. Their hands were locked held by the very energy flowing through them. The circuit was complete, and they were the battery.

Luna stood with her arms raised, her starfield gown drinking the reflected light. “The old magic returns!” she proclaimed, her voice booming, amplified. “But order must be preserved! A new order! One of clarity, of contracts written in starlight and shadow! Not the tyranny of the sun, but the sovereignty of the moon!”

The beams from the Elements tracked, finding the other Solar Sentinels. They didn’t fight. They stood, frozen, as the coercive magic they’d served was turned against them, stripping their authority, their power, and their very uniforms away.

Zonk understood. The understanding was a physical blow. This wasn’t a recalibration. It was a coup. They weren’t tuning the Elements back to a benign frequency. Luna was using their genuine bond as a pure power source to reforge the Elements, imprinting them with her own signature, her own flavor of control. Secrets instead of spectacle. Fear instead of pageantry. A different cage.

“You’re hijacking it,” Zonk gasped, the words ripped from him. The cold from the tongue depressor was crawling up his arm, a frostbite ache.

“I am redirecting it,” Luna corrected, her eyes blazing with triumph. “Your bond is the perfect, unsullied catalyst. So beautifully earnest. It will make the new contracts unbreakable. The power of friendship,” she said, and this time the sarcasm was a razor, “harnessed at last.”

She nodded to her Night Guards.

They moved. Not toward the defeated Solar Sentinels. Toward the array. Toward them.

The ritual wasn’t for saving the dimension. It was for stealing it. And they were right in the goddamn middle.

The air in the chamber tasted of lightning and wet stone. Blaze’s palms were slick. He wiped them on the stupid velvet of his costume pants, the fabric rough against his skin. The crystal array hummed, a sound that started in his molars and vibrated down into his gut.

Luna’s voice cut through the low drone. “Begin.”

Pip stepped forward first, her sequins throwing frantic green dots across the floor. She placed a hand on the central prism. It flared, a gentle pink. The whoopee cushion gag-ball set—Laughter—floated from the satchel at Zonk’s feet and settled into a notch in the array. A low, ridiculous farting sound echoed in the vast space, followed by a wave of genuine, giddy warmth. Blaze felt the corners of his mouth twitch. He bit the inside of his cheek.

“Your turn, big guy,” Zonk muttered, not looking at him. Zonk’s face was pale, his eyes fixed on the array like it was a bomb. He nudged the red cuffs—Loyalty—with his toe.

Right. Blaze crouched, the old bandana tied around his wrist brushing the cold floor. He picked up the cuffs. The metal was warmer than it should be, almost body temperature. He remembered the feel of them in the apartment, the click of the mechanism, the way Zonk had tapped out that safeword rhythm. He carried them to the array. The notch for them glowed a dull crimson. He slotted them in.

A jolt went through him. Not pain. Recognition. It was the feeling of catching Zonk’s eye across a crowded room, the unspoken you ready to bail? It was the solid weight of the guy’s shoulder against his after a shitty day. It was simple. It was his. The crimson light brightened, pulsed once in time with his heartbeat.

He stepped back, his breathing coming easier.

Zonk was moving now, placing the silver tongue depressor—Honesty—with a look of deep distaste. “Feels like putting a thermometer in a god’s mouth,” he grumbled. A clear, sharp light bloomed. Next, the purple flogger—Generosity. Its light was deep, velvety. Then Pip, her small hands trembling, placed the yellow jar of salve—Kindness. A soft, buttery glow spread from it, carrying the scent of chamomile and clean linen.

Only the tiara remained. Magic. The master key.

Luna glided to the array’s edge, her starfield gown whispering against the floor. “The conduit,” she said, her voice swelling to fill the chamber. “The catalyst must now link them. Your bond. Your… chaotic harmony.” She held out a hand toward the three of them. “Join hands.”

Blaze reached for Pip’s. Her fingers were ice-cold. With his other hand, he grabbed Zonk’s wrist. Zonk’s pulse hammered against his thumb.

“Now,” Luna commanded, her eyes on the tiara. “Focus the connection. Pour your… affection… into the lattice.”

For a second, nothing. Then Blaze felt it—a pull, gentle at first. It was like a thread tugging behind his sternum. He saw a flash behind his eyes: Zonk, sixteen, passing him a poorly rolled joint behind the bleachers. Pip, her first day in the apartment, carefully folding a blanket. The thread tightened. The array began to sing, a chord of six different notes weaving together. The light from the Elements didn’t just glow; it bled into the air, colors twisting and braiding into a single, brilliant strand that lanced upward toward the crystal dome.

He looked at Luna. She wasn’t watching the light. She was watching them, her expression hungry. Her lips were moving, shaping silent words.

The strand of light touched the dome. The clear crystal fractured into a million rainbows. The chord of notes rose to a piercing, beautiful frequency.

It wasn’t a joyful sound. It was a crack of triumph, dry and cold.

She raised her arms. “Now!” she cried, not to them, but to the shadows.

Her Night Guards moved. But not to protect the array. They turned, bat-winged helmets swiveling, and raised their weapons—curved blades that crackled with black energy—Toward the archway where the Solar Sentinels stood.

Chaos detonated.

Gold-and-white armor clashed with polished shadow. The beautiful chord shattered into a screech of metal and snarled spells. A blast of solar energy seared past Blaze’s head, close enough to singe his hair. He smelled burnt sugar and ozone.

“What the fuck!” Zonk yelled, yanking his hand free. The brilliant strand of light flickered, dimmed.

Luna stood at the center of the storm, untouched. Her gown swirled around her. “The old magic returns!” she proclaimed, her voice magically amplified, echoing off the dome. “But a garden untended grows wild. It requires a new gardener. One who understands the necessity of shadow. The power of secrets kept.” She looked directly at them, her smile widening. “The recalibrated Elements will not usher in an age of pointless ‘friendship.’ They will forge contracts of perfect compliance. Fear is a more reliable bond than love is it not?”

The truth landed in Blaze’s gut like a heavy stone. She didn’t want to fix it. She wanted to own it. The theater wasn’t for witnessing change. It was for a coup.

A Solar Sentinel, half its helmet melted away, lunged toward the array, its weapon aimed at the tiara. A Night Guard intercepted it, their blades locking with a shower of violet sparks. They were fighting over the prize. Right here. Right now.

Pip screamed, a short, sharp sound lost in the din.

Blaze’s body moved before his brain caught up. He shoved Zonk back, away from the clashing guards, and grabbed Pip by the arm, hauling her behind the bulk of a secondary crystal column. Shards of light and dark magic ricocheted around the room, pocking the marble.

“We’re the fucking conduits!” Zonk shouted, crouching beside him. His eyes were wide, his sarcasm burned away by pure adrenaline. “They’re going to kill us trying to get to the stuff!”

The array was the epicenter. The fighting swirled around it, a vicious ballet of gold and black. The Elements still glowed in their slots, the woven strand of light now frayed and lashing like a live wire.

Blaze’s mind raced, but not with battle tactics. It went to the basics. The first thing you learn. Before you play, you make the space safe. His old instructor’s voice, a gruff woman named Marta with knuckle tattoos. You check your gear. You negotiate your scene. You establish your circle. Nothing enters the circle without permission.

This was the most fucked-up scene he’d ever been in.

“We need a circle,” he said, the words coming out flat.

Zonk stared at him. “A what?”

“A safe circle. A boundary. They can’t cross it.”

“Blaze, they have magic swords!”

“So do we!” He pointed at the array. “The Elements. They’re not just glow-sticks. They’re the rules. Luna said it—They’re tuned to us right now. To our… whatever.” He didn’t have better words. He had instinct. “We made the connection. We’re still in it. I can feel it.” That thread behind his sternum was still there, taut and humming.

He looked at Pip. “The Kindness salve. What does it do?”

She was shaking, but she spoke, the sound surprisingly clear. “It… it soothes. It creates a space of non-aggression. A sanctuary.”

“And Loyalty?”

“It binds. It protects what is inside.”

“Okay,” Blaze said out loud. “Zonk, the cuffs. Pip, the salve. We’re not powering the whole dome. We’re drawing a fucking line.”

Another explosion rocked the chamber. A chunk of marble from the ceiling crashed down ten feet away. Time was up.

He didn’t wait for agreement. He lunged from behind the column, low and fast, toward the array. A Solar Sentinel turned, raising a blade. Blaze didn’t stop. He slid the last few feet on his knees, the marble tearing his pants, and grabbed the red cuffs from their slot. The metal burned his palms, a searing promise. He scrambled back to the column.

“Here!” He thrust one cuff toward Zonk, keeping the other. “Think about the apartment. Think about not letting anything in.”

Zonk’s hand closed around the metal. His jaw was clenched. He gave a sharp nod.

Pip was already unscrewing the jar of yellow salve. The scent of chamomile grew stronger cutting through the acrid battle-smell. She dipped her fingers in.

“Mark the boundary,” Blaze said. “Around us. Now.”

He didn’t watch her. He focused on Zonk, on the cuff in his own hand, on that thread of connection. He thought of closed doors. Of the stupid deadbolt on his apartment that he’d fixed three times. Of standing between Zonk and a drunk asshole in a bar parking lot. Of a line in the sand. This far. No farther.

He clicked his cuff onto his own wrist. Zonk, his eyes locked on Blaze’s did the same.

A pull shivered through the air, a sub-audible thrum. The crimson light from the cuffs didn’t flash. It bled out across the floor in a wavering red line that connected them, then curved away as Pip, moving with frantic grace, smeared a glistening streak of salve on the marble. She traced a rough circle around their column, her fingers leaving a trail of soft yellow light that merged with the red.

The circle closed.

A Night Guard, breaking off from its fight, charged them. It hit the boundary where the red and yellow lights met.

It didn’t bounce. It didn’t shimmer. It just… stopped. The guard froze, its weapon arm raised. It took a step back, confused. It tried again, pushing against the air. Its boot slid as if on ice. It couldn’t cross.

Blaze let out a shuddering breath he hadn’t known he was holding. It worked. The goddamn kindergarten magic of sharing and safety protocols worked.

Inside the circle, the sounds of battle became distant, like a TV in another room. The air felt still. Warm.

Outside, Luna saw. Her elegant composure fissured. Rage twisted her features. “You dare?” she shrieked, her amplified voice cracking. “You make a playpen with the instruments of destiny?”

She raised her hands, gathering a vortex of dark energy between them. “The contract is mine! The power is mine! You are vessels! Empty yourselves!”

She hurled the vortex at their circle.

It struck the boundary and splintered into a hundred shards of dissolving shadow. The red and yellow light flared, brightened held.

But Blaze felt the impact in his bones. A deep, sickening ache. Zonk grunted doubling over. Pip cried out. The circle was holding, but it was drawing from them. From their connection. He could feel it thinning.

Luna gathered another, larger vortex. The fighting around the array was dying. Both Solar and Night Guards were turning, realizing the real prize was now inside the stupid, glowing circle on the floor. They were surrounded. Out of time.

Blaze looked at Zonk, who was panting, sweat beading on his forehead. He looked at Pip, her face streaked with tears and salve. The thread between them felt frayed, stretched to breaking.

“We can’t just hide in here,” Zonk gasped.

“We’re not hiding,” Blaze said. The way he said it was rough. “We’re finishing the ritual.”

“How? She’ll just take it!”

“Then we don’t let her have it.” Blaze looked at the tiara, still sitting in its slot at the heart of the array, just outside their circle. The key. “We tune it to us. For real. Not for her. Not for Celestia. For… this.” He gestured between the three of them, to the circle, to the shared, desperate ache in his chest. “For our fucked-up, ride-or-die, safe-word-having thing. We make it ours.”

He met Zonk’s eyes. Saw the fear, the doubt, and underneath it, the stubborn, smart-ass refusal to quit. Zonk nodded.

“Pip,” Blaze said. “Get the tiara.”

She didn’t hesitate. She broke from the circle.

The moment she crossed the boundary, the world rushed back in—the roar, the heat, the violence. Luna saw her move. “The child! Take her!”

Pip was small and fast. She darted between two clashing guards, a flash of mint green and sequins. She reached the array, her hand closing around the cool metal of the tiara.

A Night Guard lunged for her.

“Pip, now!” Blaze roared.

She turned and threw it. Not to him. To Zonk.

The tiara spun through the chaotic air, a silver star. Zonk snatched it out of its arc, fumbling it for a heart-stopping second before gripping it tight.

“Do it!” Blaze yelled, extending his cuffed wrist.

Zonk’s hands were shaking. He looked from the tiara, to Blaze, to the closing guards, to Luna gathering another killing blast. His face went blank for a second, pure unscripted panic.

Then he did the only thing that made sense.

He put the fucking tiara on his own head.

The world dissolved into a mess of color and noise.

Something hot and wet streaked Zonk’s cheek. He touched it, fingers coming away smeared with something that wasn’t quite red, more like liquid rust. Not his. The air tasted of shattered crystal and burnt ozone, a metallic tang that coated his tongue. A Solar Sentinel lay three feet away, the childlike faceplate of its helmet cracked, one glassy eye staring at nothing. The gold armor was dented inward, a perfect horseshoe shape. Blaze’s work.

“Move!” Blaze’s voice was a raw scrape, right in his ear. A hand clamped on his collar and yanked.

He stumbled backward, his boots slipping on glittering shards. The central crystal array wasn’t inert anymore. It was a storm of wrong light, jagged bolts of magenta and sickly green lashing out from its heart where they’d begun the ritual. Luna’s betrayal had slammed into the process like a hammer on a tuning fork. Now the energy had nowhere to go but out.

He caught a glimpse of her across the chamber, backlit by the chaos. Her starfield gown was torn at the shoulder, but she stood straight, one hand raised as if conducting the violence. Her Night Guard clashed with Celestia’s Sentinels in a grinding, brutal scrum of shadows and gilded armor. The Old Herd survivors were either stopped by the fray or smart enough to flee. It was a three-way war now: Sun, Moon, and the unstable magical bomb the three of them had accidentally primed.

Pip was a small, trembling weight against his side. He hadn’t even realized he’d grabbed her hand. Her sequined skirt was ripped, one mint-green frill hanging by a thread. She wasn’t crying. Her face was pale set in a mask of concentrated terror, her eyes fixed on the whirling crystal death machine they’d helped create.

Blaze herded them toward the chamber’s curved wall, away from the main doors, which were a churning bottleneck of combat. He moved with a frantic, scanning efficiency, shoving aside a toppled marble plinth with a grunt. Behind it was a shallow alcove—a decorative niche meant for a statue that had probably been vaporized in some previous magical disagreement. It was maybe four feet deep, six feet wide. A coffin with a view.

“In. Now.” Blaze didn’t ask. He physically turned Zonk and pushed him into the shadowed space. Pip scrambled in after, pressing herself against the cold stone back wall.

Blaze didn’t follow. He stood at the alcove’s mouth, his broad back blocking the worst of the light show. He was breathing hard, his stupid velvet costume torn at the sleeve, revealing the corded muscle of his forearm. He held the Element of Loyalty—the red, unbreakable cuffs—in one hand. They weren’t connected. Just two separate bracelets.

Outside was a banned word. It was the scream of metal on crystal, the wet thud of bodies, Luna’s amplified voice chanting something that made Zonk’s fillings ache.

“Making a circle,” Blaze said, his voice low and focused. He dropped to one knee, placing one cuff on the marble floor. He reached into a hidden pocket of his costume—Since when did it have pockets? —and pulled out a small, worn leather pouch. Zonk recognized it. Blaze’s “kit.” The one he brought to every dungeon session, real or virtual. Nipple clamps, a spare blindfold, lubricant packets, a tiny pair of medical shears.

“You are fucking kidding me.” The laugh that bubbled up Zonk’s throat tasted like bile. “Now? You’re doing aftercare now?”

“Safety circle,” Blaze grunted, not looking up. He poured a thin line of white powder from the pouch onto the floor, connecting the first cuff to the second, which he placed about three feet away, forming the start of an arc. “Rule one. When the scene goes sideways, you secure a safe space. No magic, no fighting, no fucking drama. Just pause.” His hands were steady. Zonk’s were shaking so hard he had to clench them into fists.

“That’s salt,” Zonk said, dumbly.

“Sea salt. For purification. Also, it’s what I had.” Blaze completed the arc, then connected the ends with two more lines, forming a rough, lopsided circle around the alcove’s entrance. He placed the small shears in the center of the line, blades open. “Scissors in the circle. Universal symbol for ‘pause.’ Anyone who knows the rules sees this, they stop. They have to.”

“Blaze. Those are murder-ponies and a treasonous moon princess. They don’t know the rules!”

“Then it’s a fucking salt line and some scissors,” Blaze snapped, finally looking at him. His eyes were wide, the whites visible all around the iris. He was terrified. But his hands kept moving, precise, ritualistic. “It’s something. It’s a thing I can do. So I’m doing it.”

He sat back on his heels, placed his palms flat on the floor just inside the salt line, and took a deep, shuddering breath. He closed his eyes.

For a second, nothing happened. A Sentinel was hurled past the alcove, crashing into the wall with a sound like a bag of wet gravel. A shard of crystal the size of a dinner plate embedded itself in the marble a foot from Blaze’s knee.

A hum. Low, vibrational. It started in the cuffs. The red metal glowed, not with the array’s violent light, but with a warm, pulsing crimson. The glow traveled down the salt lines, turning the white powder into a thin, glowing thread. The light climbed, forming a faint curtain across the alcove’s opening. It wavered like heat haze.

The noise didn’t stop. But it muted, as if someone had thrown a thick blanket over the world. The colors beyond the curtain blurred, smearing into a watery mess of conflict. The air inside the circle lost the ozone sting. It just smelled of cold stone, sweat, and the faint, sweet scent of Pip’s hair.

Blaze opened his eyes. He looked drained. “It won’t hold long. It’s not meant for… for this. It’s a time-out. A safeword made physical.” He crawled into the alcove, the curtain parting for him like water before settling back into place. The space was suddenly, oppressively intimate. Three of them, silence between them, each other’s air.

Pip slid down the wall until she was sitting, her ruined skirt pooling around her. She hugged her knees. “She was going to use our bond. Like a key. To open the lock and then… keep the key for herself.”

“Yeah,” Zonk said. He leaned his head back against the stone. It was freezing. “Turns out the cool, rebellious alternative to a fascist is just a different kind of fascist. Who knew.” He wanted a cigarette. He wanted his shitty apartment. He wanted the boring, predictable agony of his old life back.

“I believed her,” Pip said.

“We all did, Pipsqueak.” Blaze sat beside her, not touching, just close. His big hands dangled between his knees. One knuckle was split open, oozing a slow bead of blood. “She had better PR.”

Zonk watched the blood well up, cling to his skin for a moment, then fall. It left a tiny, perfect dark spot on the grey marble dust. He couldn’t look away from it. A meaningless detail. His brain latched onto it like a life raft. That spot. That specific spot.

“My mom had a dress that color,” he said. The words fell into the quiet like stones.

Blaze turned his head. “What?”

“The blood. That rust color. She had a dress. For, like, church or some shit. I don’t think she ever went to church. But she had this dress.” He could see it. Polyester. Cheap. A pattern of little white flowers. She’d worn it the day she left. She’d stood in the doorway, a suitcase in one hand, and she’d looked back at him. Not with sadness. With an expression he’d spent years trying to name. It wasn’t regret. It was more like… calculation. Like she was checking a box. “She said it was terracotta. I thought that was a kind of pot.”

He hadn’t thought of that dress in a decade. Why now? Why here hiding in a magic circle from pony fascists? His brain was a broken museum, throwing random exhibits at the wall.

Blaze was silent for a long moment. “You never talked about that.”

“Nothing to talk about. She left. The dress was terracotta. End of story.” His throat felt tight, raw. “I just… I don’t know what I’m doing. I’m not a distinct voice. I’m a guy who remembers his mom’s ugly dress while the world ends.”

Pip reached out. Not to him, but to Blaze’s injured hand. She took it gently turned it over. She pulled a clean, lace-trimmed handkerchief from somewhere in her layers—of course she had a handkerchief—and pressed it to the split knuckle. The white lace bloomed red.

“You remembered the salt,” she said softly, to Blaze. “You remembered the rules of safety.” She looked at Zonk, her eyes huge in the dim, crimson-tinged light. “And you remember the lore. The real lore, not the… the contract they made of it. You knew what the Elements were supposed to be. Before.” She dabbed at the cut. “That is a distinct voice. It is just… out of tune. Like the crystals.”

Outside their fragile bubble, the battle raged. A prismatic explosion lit the curtain for a second, painting their faces in carnival colors. The curtain shivered, the hum dipping in pitch.

“It’s failing,” Blaze said, his voice flat.

Zonk looked from Pip’s small, earnest hands on Blaze’s, to the terror held just barely at bay in Blaze’s eyes, to the tiny terracotta spot on the floor. A hot, sharp pressure built behind his sternum. It wasn’t courage. It was simpler, more desperate. It was the absolute, gut-deep knowledge that if that curtain fell, the two people in this stone coffin with him would die. And he would have to watch. And after that, he would be alone with the ugly-dress memory forever.

“Fuck harmony,” he said. The words were quiet, but they cut through the hum. “We’re not tuning a goddamn piano. We’re breaking it.”

Blaze met his gaze. “The plan.”

“The plan was Luna’s. The plan is ash. You made a safe circle.” Zonk pushed himself off the wall. His legs held. “Now we need to make a weapon.”

He looked at the Element of Magic, the silver tiara, hanging from Pip’s belt where she’d clipped it. It was dark. Dormant. Waiting for a master.

“We finish the ritual,” Zonk said. “But not for her. Not for any of them. We pour our distinct, fucked-up, out-of-tune voices into it until it breaks. Until it can’t be used as a key or a lock or a contract ever again.”

Pip’s hand tightened on the bloody handkerchief. “That could destroy it. The feedback could… it could unmake the connection. The real one. Between us.”

“Yeah,” Zonk said. He felt strangely calm. The bag of wet cats in his gut had settled, frozen. “Might. But if we don’t, they get it. One of them. And then they own the concept of friendship. Forever. And I’d rather have nothing than let that happen.”

The curtain flickered. A hairline crack of violent magenta light split the haze from top to bottom.

Blaze looked at Pip, then at Zonk. A slow, grim smile touched his mouth. It didn’t reach his eyes. “Okay. How?”

Zonk took a beat. The air still smelled of Pip’s hair. “We do what we were going to do. We just… mean it differently. And we don’t stop when it hurts.”

He held out his hand. For the tiara.

Pip looked at his outstretched hand. She looked at the failing light of the safety circle, at the blood on her own lace, at Blaze’s waiting face. Her lips moved, soundless. Then she unclipped the tiara.

She didn’t give it to him.

She placed it on her own head.

The silver band settled into her curls. The central star, dark for so long gave a single, weak pulse. A heartbeat of light.

“It’s my distinct voice, too,” she said, and her voice didn’t shake. “You’re not the only one who’s scared.”

Outside, the curtain shattered.

The air above the safe circle shimmered—not the pretty, magical shimmer of the game, but the greasy, heat-haze ripple of a bullet’s wake. Another blast from a Solar Sentinel’s energy baton impacted the barrier Blaze had conjured. The sound was a wet thump, like a giant heart beating once against the inside of a drum. The circle, defined by the red Loyalty cuffs buried in the marble at four points held. For now.

Zonk’s knees pressed into cold stone. Pip huddled beside him, her sequined skirt a puddle of mint and nervous light. The rest of the Elements were scattered just outside the perimeter, where Blaze had shoved them when Luna’s betrayal turned the chamber into a shooting gallery. The pink whoopee cushion of Laughter quivered near a fallen Night Guard’s boot. The purple flogger of Generosity was half under a shattered crystal shard. The tiara, the crown jewel of Magic, glinted five feet away, next to a dark, spreading stain that used to be someone from the Old Herd.

“We have to get them back in the array,” Pip said quietly. She spoke like a frayed thread. “The connection is… it’s a thread. It’s breaking.”

He could feel it. Not in his mind, but in his bones—a low, discordant hum that made his fillings ache. The ritual had been a live wire, a circuit completed by their three stupid, messy hearts. Luna cutting the power had been like snipping the cord, but the current was still there, leaking, grounding itself in chaos. It felt like the ghost of a headache, the kind you get from bad fluorescent lights.

“Yeah. No shit.” He peered over the low, illusory wall of the safe circle. The chamber was a riot of color and violence. Bolts of gold solar magic clashed with arcs of deep blue lunar energy. The Old Herd survivors were trying to shield the inert crystal array with their bodies, pathetic and brave. Luna stood atop a plinth, directing her shadows with sharp, graceful flicks of her wrist, a conductor in an evening gown. Celestia’s Sentinels fought with silent, brutal efficiency, a wall of white and gold advancing.

None of them were looking at the three idiots in the bubble. They were the prize, not the players. For the moment.

“Okay,” Zonk said. “Okay. The cuffs are anchors. The circle stays. We go out, grab the stuff, come back. Simple.”

“It is not simple,” Pip said, but she was already shifting her weight onto the balls of her feet, a tiny fighter poised to spring. She had a smudge of soot on her cheek. He had the sudden, irrelevant thought that he’d never seen her dirty before. She’d always been weirdly pristine, like a doll kept in a box.

“Since when are you the tactical genius?” Zonk asked, the old reflex firing on fumes.

“Since you were busy having an existential crisis. Go.”

The plan was terrible. It was a three-part grocery run in the middle of a war. Zonk’s mouth was full of the taste of pennies and ozone. He met Pip’s eyes. She gave a tight, desperate little nod.

He broke from the circle.

The air outside was different—thicker, charged. It crackled against his skin. He kept low, a shuffling crouch that made his thighs burn. The tiara glinted, a taunt. Four steps. A Solar Sentinel went down three yards to his right, a lunar spear of darkness punching through its chest plate with a sound like cracking ice. Zonk didn’t look. He focused on the way the starlight from the domed ceiling caught the tiara’s central gem. It was a purple so deep it was almost black. He’d always thought it looked cheap, like plastic from a party store.

His fingers closed around cold metal.

A shadow fell over him.

He looked up. A Night Guard stood there, the crescent moon helmet tilting down. The guard’s weapon, a polearm tipped with a wickedly curved blade was leveled at his chest. No time for a safeword. No time for a clever quote.

“The vessel will return to the circle,” the guard droned.

Zonk’s brain offered nothing. No lore, no joke, no sarcastic comment. Just a white, silent blank. His body moved anyway. He threw himself sideways, not away, but toward the guard’s legs, a clumsy tackle he’d last attempted in seventh-grade gym class.

He connected with a shin. The guard grunted, stumbled. The polearm blade swept over Zonk’s head, close enough he felt the wind of it ruffle his hair. He hit the floor, the tiara clutched to his stomach like a football. He scrambled, knees scraping on rough stone, back toward the border of the safe circle.

A hand grabbed the back of his costume jerkin and hauled. He tumbled across the line, the discordant hum in his bones softening back to that persistent ache. Blaze stood over him, breathing hard, a long scratch bleeding down one arm. He’d retrieved the whoopee cushion. It looked absurd in his big hand.

“You get it?” Blaze asked.

“Yeah.”

“Cool.” Blaze turned and flung the whoopee cushion like a frisbee. It sailed out of the circle, bounced off the helmet of a Solar Sentinel who was advancing on a cowering Old Herd member, and emitted a sad, deflating pfffrrrrt sound. The Sentinel stopped, confused. The old pony scuttled away.

“Did you just… use the Element of Laughter as a distraction?” Zonk asked, pushing himself up.

“It was a whoopee cushion. Seemed right.”

Pip slid back into the circle a second later, the purple flogger clutched to her chest. She’d also grabbed the yellow jar of Kindness salve. Her breathing came in quick shallow gasps. “The connection,” she panted. “It is like… a song with missing notes. We have all but Honesty.”

The silver tongue depressor and scales. Zonk scanned the battlefield. He saw them, over by the base of the crystal array, gleaming in the reflected magic. Twenty feet of open, murderous ground.

“That’s the worst one to be missing,” he muttered. “Irony’s a bitch.”

“We go together,” Blaze said. “All three. Quick in, quick out. They’re focused on each other. We’re ants.”

“Ants get stepped on,” Zonk said.

“Not if they’re fast ants.”

Pip reached out and took Zonk’s hand. Her fingers were small, cold, and shaking. Then she took Blaze’s. She didn’t say anything. She just held on, her grip tighter than he thought she could manage. The hum in Zonk’s bones changed pitch, warmed a degree. It wasn’t power. It was a tuning fork, vibrating because they were touching.

Blaze looked at their linked hands, then at the silver scales glinting in the distance. He nodded once. “On three. One.”

Zonk’s heart hammered against his ribs, a trapped bird.

“Two.”

The air smelled of burned sugar and hot metal.

“Three.”

They ran.

Not as individuals, but as a single, three-headed creature. They burst from the circle and cut diagonally across the chamber, a tight, desperate pack. Zonk saw flashes—a Lunar guard turning, a Solar bolt searing past his ear with a hiss that left it ringing. Pip let out a sharp cry, but she didn’t slow. Blaze was a wall on their left, his free arm up as if to block projectiles.

The scales were right there. Zonk dropped to his knees, his free hand outstretched. His fingers brushed cool metal.

Something heavy and dark slammed into his side.

The world upended. He lost Pip’s hand. He was rolling, the tiara flying from his grip, skittering across the floor. Pain, bright and hot, flared along his ribs. He came to a stop on his back, the wind gone from his lungs.

Princess Luna stood over him, her starfield gown now torn at the hem, her face a mask of elegant fury. She held the scales of Honesty in one hand. “Sentimental,” she spat, the word dripping with contempt. “You think your little bond is a weapon? It is a vulnerability. A lever.” She looked past him, to where Blaze was helping Pip up. “The connection is yours. Good. You have done the hard work of tuning it. I will simply… plug it in.”

She raised the scales. The other Elements, scattered nearby began to vibrate. A low, pulling thrum started in the center of Zonk’s chest. Luna wasn’t trying to break their connection. She was trying to hijack it. To use their retuned signal, broadcast through her own will.

From her shadowy archway, Princess Celestia’s voice cut through the din, not a roar, but a plea raw with exhaustion. “Luna! You fool! You don’t understand what you’re unleashing!”

Luna didn’t even glance her way. Her eyes were fixed on the gathering energy, a hungry, possessive light in them.

Zonk pushed himself up on his elbows. His ribs screamed. He saw the tiara, five feet away. He saw Pip’s terrified face. He saw Blaze, standing protectively in front of her, looking from Luna to the tiara to Zonk, his expression not confused, but calculating. The guy was actually thinking.

The safe circle was broken. The ritual was corrupted. Luna held the last Element. They had nothing.

Nothing except each other, lying in the dirt between two armies.

Blaze met his eyes across the chaos and gave a slow, deliberate shrug, as if to say, Well, this is a new kind of fucked.

Zonk almost laughed. It was that or scream. He started crawling toward the tiara.
