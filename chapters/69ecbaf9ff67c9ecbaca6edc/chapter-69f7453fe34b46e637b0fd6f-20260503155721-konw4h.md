The key-crystal was a dead weight in Zonk’s fist, warm and useless. The Inspector’s face, a perfect study in bureaucratic outrage, purpled. He took a step, the stylus in his hand looking suddenly less like a writing tool and more like a shiv.

“Give. It. Back.”

Pip’s shoulder brushed Zonk’s arm. A tiny, solid pressure. Her voice, when she spoke again was different. Not the shrill, legalistic whine of a moment before, but something lower, deliberate. She wasn’t looking at the Inspector anymore. She was looking at the stylus in his hand, at the way his knuckles had gone white around it.

“Red,” she said.

The word hung in the velvet-draped air. It didn’t echo. It just sat there, small and red and final.

The Inspector blinked. “What?”

“Red,” Pip repeated, clearer now. She took a small, precise step backward, pulling Zonk with her by the sleeve. “Safeword. Session termination protocol, subsection gamma, paragraph. Oh, I forget the number. But you’re familiar.”

The man’s mouth opened, then closed. The furious intelligence in his eyes glitched, just for a second. It was like watching a search engine hit a dead link. The stylus lowered a fraction of an inch. “That. That is a client-subject protocol. This is an administrative detention. The forms are completely different.”

“Are they?” Pip’s head tilted. The torn lace on her cuff fluttered. “Because the binding arbitration clause in the Standard Inter-Court Extradition Agreement, which you cited three minutes ago, specifically defaults to subject-protection protocols in cases of ambiguous jurisdiction. Which this is. Since we haven’t been formally charged by a Solar magistrate, only detained for ‘consultation’ by a Lunar agent. Ambiguity. Red.”

Zonk’s brain, usually a swamp of sarcasm and half-remembered game lore was a blank, white sheet. He stared at Pip. Where had this come from? The kid who’d tried to negotiate with armored thugs using please-and-thank-you was gone. In her place was this. This little lawyer. Her eyes were wide, but not with fear. With focus. A terrible, calm focus.

The Inspector sputtered. “Ambiguous—I am a duly appointed auditor of the Lunar Compliance Bureau! My jurisdiction is perfectly clear!”

“Then produce the warrant.” Pip’s voice was a scalpel. “The physical, stamped warrant for our detention, not a notation on your slate. Show me the clause that overrides safeword invocation during pre-charge holding. Show me.”

She held out her hand, palm up. It didn’t tremble.

The man stared at her empty palm. His own hand twitched toward the crystal slate on his belt, then stopped. A vein pulsed in his temple. The silence in the room changed. It wasn’t the quiet of before, the heavy, velvety quiet of a fancy cell. This was the brittle, humming quiet of a system error. Of a rule hitting a rule and both of them breaking.

He was stuck. Zonk saw it. The guy lived in a world of paragraphs and sub-clauses, and Pip had just quoted the holy scripture back at him in a way he couldn’t process. His authority was a house of cards built on everyone agreeing the cards were stone. Pip had just pointed out they were paper.

“Red means stop,” Pip said, softer now. Almost gentle. “All activities cease. The detained are to be released to a neutral location immediately to prevent psychological trauma and breach of contract. That’s the rule.”

“The rule,” the Inspector murmured, his voice strangled.

“Your rule,” Zonk heard himself say. The words felt thick in his dry mouth. He hefted the key-crystal. “You gonna follow it, or do we need to file a grievance? I hear the paperwork on those is a bitch.”

The man flinched as if struck. He looked from Pip’s steady, waiting hand to Zonk’s face, to the open doorway behind them. The math in his head was visible—career repercussions versus procedural violation. The sheer, unthinkable scandal of a safeword being invoked in a holding room. His shoulders slumped, defeated not by force, but by his own beloved bureaucracy.

“The west anteroom,” he muttered, not meeting their eyes. He gestured vaguely with the stylus. “It leads to a service corridor. It is. Neutral territory. For the mandated cooling-off period. You will find the door unlocked.”

“Thank you,” Pip said, with devastating politeness.

She didn’t run. She turned, smooth as a clockwork doll, and walked toward the arched doorway they’d come in through. Zonk scrambled after her, his heart trying to punch its way out of his ribs. He expected a shout, a tackle, the blare of an alarm. Nothing. Just the soft crush of velvet underfoot and the smell of old incense—Sweet, with a sour note underneath, like forgotten fruit.

The anteroom was just as described: small, windowless, furnished with a single ugly pouf the color of a bruised peach. A plain, unadorned door was set in the opposite wall. Pip went straight to it. The handle turned under her hand with a click that sounded like a gunshot in the quiet.

Then they were through, into a narrow, dim corridor. The walls here were bare stone, the floor scuffed linoleum. The air smelled of bleach and stale pastry. A service corridor. Real. Ugly.

Pip pulled the door shut behind them. The click of the latch engaging was the sweetest sound Zonk had ever heard.

For a full ten seconds, they just stood there in the dim, buzzing light of a single overhead fixture. Zonk’s knees felt loose, unbolted. He leaned against the cool stone wall. The key-crystal was still clutched in his hand, sweating against his palm.

“What,” he replied softly, “the actual fuck was that?”

Pip was looking at her hands, turning them over as if she’d never seen them before. The calm, terrifying focus was gone. Her lower lip quivered. “I don’t know,” she said quietly. “It just. Came out. All those lessons. All the contract recitations. It was just there.”

“You broke him with paperwork.”

“He broke himself.” She wrapped her arms around herself, a sudden, small movement. “The rules are all they have. If you use the rules better, they have nothing to stand on. It’s. It’s all they have.”

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite pride, not quite a sickening awe, something between the two that tightened his throat. He looked at her, really looked. The torn lace, the smudge of dirt on her cheek, the way she held herself so tight she might fracture. The kid wasn’t naive anymore. The kid was dangerous. And she was shaking.

“Hey,” he said. He lowered the words to out rough. “You’re okay. That was. That was the most insane thing I’ve ever seen. And I once watched Blaze try to microwave a burrito still in the foil.”

A hiccup of a sound escaped her. Not quite a laugh. “Did it work?”

“It made a light show and set off the smoke alarm. So, kind of?” He pushed off the wall. The corridor stretched in both directions, identical and empty. “Which way is out?”

Pip pointed left. “The pastries smell stronger that way. Kitchens usually have delivery accesses.”

Of course she’d notice that. He hadn’t smelled anything but his own adrenaline. “Lead on, Counselor.”

They moved, sticking close to the wall. The corridor was a long, featureless tube, broken occasionally by closed, unmarked doors. The hum of industrial refrigeration grew louder. The bleach-and-pastry smell intensified, mixing unpleasantly. Zonk’s thumb, the skin around the nail shredded and raw from his own nervous picking, throbbed in time with his heartbeat.

He kept waiting for the shout, the pounding of boots. Nothing. Just the hum, the buzz of the lights, the soft scuff of their feet. The Inspector was probably still in that room, trying to find the correct form to file for having his worldview dismantled by a pony-girl quoting his own manuals.

“He’ll call it in,” Zonk said, more to break the silence than anything. “The second we’re out of his ‘neutral zone.’”

“I know.” Pip’s voice was small again. “We’re fugitives from both Courts now.”

“We were already fugitives.”

“From the Sun Court. Now we’re fugitives from the Night Court, too. Princess Luna will be. Displeased.”

“Understatement.” Zonk remembered the look in Luna’s star-flecked eyes when she’d offered her deal. It wasn’t kindness. It was the look of a chess player spotting a useful, temporary piece. “She wanted us to break her sister. We broke her deal instead. She’s gonna want her pieces back.”

A door up ahead on the right was propped open with a dented metal bucket. Yellow light and a wave of hot, sugary air spilled out. The clatter of pans, a burst of swearing in a language that sounded like someone gargling gravel. A kitchen.

Pip slowed, peering around the edge of the doorway. Zonk looked over her head. It was a cavern of stainless steel dominated by a massive oven. A creature with the torso of a man and the head of a. Was that a cow? A very disgruntled cow. He was pulling a sheet of rainbow-frosted cupcakes from the oven. He didn’t look at them.

At the far end of the kitchen, past racks of hanging pans was a wide metal door. A delivery exit.

Pip didn’t hesitate. She walked in, not sneaking, just moving with a purposeful, quiet directness, as if she had every right to be there. Zonk followed, trying to mimic her. The cow-man slammed the cupcake tray onto a cooling rack, muttered something about “fondant shortages,” and turned his back to rummage in a lowboy refrigerator.

They were halfway across when Zonk’s foot caught the leg of a stool. It screeched against the floor.

The cow-man turned. His large, liquid eyes blinked. “Who’re you?”

Pip stopped. She looked at the cow-man, then at the cupcakes, then back at the cow-man. A smile touched her lips, fragile and real. “Quality inspectors,” she said. “The, uh, frosting consistency on batch seven was reported as suboptimal. We’re here to check.”

The cow-man’s bovine face fell into an expression of deep horror. “Suboptimal? Batch seven? But I recalibrated the sugar stream! The humidity logs were within tolerance!” He wrung his hairy hands on his apron. “You have to tell them, it wasn’t the mixer! It was the seasonal flour blend, I said it was too coarse!”

“We’ll note that in our report,” Zonk said, nodding with a gravity he did not feel. “Carry on.”

He and Pip walked the last twenty feet to the metal door as the cow-man descended into a panicked monologue about gluten development. Zonk hit the horizontal bar. The door opened with a sigh of compressed air, revealing a dim alley stacked with garbage bins and smelling of damp cardboard and something sweetly rotten.

The night outside was the same perpetual, twilit purple of this dimension. They slipped out, letting the door swing shut behind them cutting off the cow-man’s lament.

The alley was empty. In the distance, the spires of the Lunar city glittered under their false stars. Somewhere, in a pastel room, an Inspector was filling out a form. Somewhere, in a star-flecked chamber, a princess was learning her pawns had walked off the board.

Zonk looked at Pip, standing small and resolute in the alley grime. They had no allies, no plan, and two entire courts of terrifying, kink-themed gangsters who wanted them dead or enslaved.

All they had was each other, a bag of magic sex toys, and a newly discovered talent for bureaucratic warfare.

“Okay,” Zonk said, letting out a long breath that fogged in the cool air. “Now we find Blaze.”

The corridor was narrow, dim, and smelled of industrial cleaner with a faint, sickly-sweet top note of cotton candy. It was the kind of smell that got stuck in the back of your throat. Zonk’s heart was still doing a frantic tap-dance against his ribs, but the silence behind them—the significant, unbroken silence of a bureaucrat deciding to look the other way—was starting to feel real. Pip walked ahead, her steps quiet on the scuffed linoleum. She didn’t look back.

“So,” Zonk said. The word bounced off the close walls, too loud. “Red, huh?”

Pip’s shoulders tightened, just a fraction. “It was the appropriate protocol.”

“Yeah, no, it was genius. Where’d that come from? You were quoting paragraphs. You sounded like you wrote the damn manual.”

She stopped and turned. The dim light from a flickering overhead panel cut across her face, making her look older, hollowed out. The earnest, wide-eyed confusion was gone. In its place was a flat, weary expression he’d never seen on her before. “I was trained to,” she said. The way she said it was matter-of-fact, empty. “Comprehensive knowledge of standard contracts, arbitration procedures, and subject-rights protocols. It’s a core module for companion-caste aspirants. How else would you ensure optimal service and dispute resolution?”

Zonk just stared. The kid who’d tried to thank a guard for kidnapping her was explaining legal theory. His brain, still scrambling to catch up, fixated on a stupid detail: a tiny, peeling sticker on the wall beside her head. It was a smiling cupcake. Someone had drawn a frowny face over it in ballpoint pen.

“Optimal service,” he repeated.

“Yes.” She turned and started walking again. “It wasn’t. It wasn’t supposed to be used like that. It’s for client relations. Not for. ” She trailed off, her hand lifting to touch the torn lace at her cuff. A useless, fussy little gesture. The old Pip, peeking through. “It felt wrong. Using it to bully him. But it was the only tool we had.”

“Hey, bully away. My ribs thank you.” He caught up to her, the key-crystal a dull, warm lump in his pocket. “So, you’re a lawyer-sub. Good to know. What else you got in that training? Lock-picking? Poison detection? The secret to a perfect soufflé?”

She didn’t smile. “De-escalation techniques. Non-verbal cue recognition. Basic first aid for common restraint injuries. The soufflé module was elective. I didn’t take it.”

The corridor ended at a heavy metal door with a push-bar. A faded, stenciled sign read: SVC. ACCESS – SUGAR CUBE VENDORS ONLY. The sweet, cloying smell was stronger here, mixed with grease and something sharper, like ozone.

Zonk put his shoulder to the bar. The door opened onto noise.

It wasn’t a sound, it was a physical thing—a wall of synthesized carnival music, shrieks of laughter, and the sizzle-pop of deep fryers. Light, garish and unending, spilled over them: neon pinks, electric blues, eye-searing yellows. They stood in an alley, but the alley was carpeted in astroturf the color of key lime pie. Across the way, the mouth of a covered market yawned, a cave of glowing stalls and shifting crowds.

Sugar Cube Corner. The name clicked into place from Blaze’s earlier, frantic ramblings. The black market’s cheerful, psychotic front door.

“Right,” Zonk muttered, squinting. “Intel. Disguises. And probably a fucking aspirin.”

Pip hovered at his elbow, her earlier steel seeming to melt in the sensory onslaught. She looked small again, overwhelmed. A vendor trundled past pushing a cart stacked with glowing blue lollipops the size of dinner plates. The vendor himself was a barrel-chested man in a rainbow-striped apron and a horsehair tail clipped to his belt. He whistled a tune that was almost, but not quite, the theme song to a very old, very sugary cartoon.

The market was a nightmare of enforced joy. Every stall was a pastel explosion. Candy apples glistened under hot lights next to displays of brass knuckles molded into cute little hoof-shapes. A kiosk sold balloons and pre-paid burner communication crystals. Another offered “authentic Ponyville-style hooficures” and, according to a smaller sign, discreet tattoo cover-ups.

Nobody looked at them twice. That was the most unnerving part. In this sea of curated madness, two fugitives—one in a torn lace bodysuit, one in a stained graphic tee and the thousand-yard stare of a man who needed a joint so bad he could taste it—were just part of the scenery.

“We need to blend,” Zonk said, mostly to himself.

“Blend how?”

“I’m thinking less ‘fugitive’ and more ‘aimless tourist.’” He steered her toward a stall overflowing with cheap clothing. The rack was a riot of neon spandex, faux-fur vests, and hats with ear holes. Everything looked like it had been designed by a hyperactive toddler on a pixie-stick bender. “Pick something. Something that doesn’t scream ‘I just broke out of a Lunar holding cell.’”

Pip fingered a shirt covered in sequined butterflies. “These are all. Very bright.”

“That’s the point. We’re not hiding, we’re camouflaging. In a forest of neon, be neon.” He grabbed an oversized hoodie for himself. It was a violent shade of purple with a glittery unicorn head on the back. It smelled like stale incense and regret. Perfect. He shrugged it on over his tee. The fabric was weirdly static, clinging to his arms.

Pip, with a look of serious resignation, selected a pink-and-white tracksuit. The pants had a stripe down the side. The jacket zipper was shaped like a carrot.

“Change in there,” Zonk said, nodding to a curtained-off alcove labeled “Fitting Stable.” He turned his back, scanning the crowd. His eyes skipped over the obvious—the families with wide-eyed kids hoisted on shoulders, the couples feeding each other fried dough—and landed on the edges. The ones loitering in the shadows between stalls, not buying. The ones with watchful eyes and hands kept near their waistbands. His gaze snagged on a stall at the far end, darker than the others. No flashing lights, just a single, pulsing purple bulb. The sign above it was a simple, stylized cannon with a party hat on it.

The Party Cannon. It had had to be.

Pip emerged. The tracksuit drowned her, the sleeves hanging past her fingertips. She’d rolled the pants up. She looked like a child who’d raided a much larger, much tackier sibling’s closet. The effect was somehow more disarming than the lace. She fiddled with the carrot zipper.

“It’s. Soft,” she offered.

“You look like a walking stick of bubblegum. It’s great.” He fished a few crumpled bits of pastel-colored scrip—the local “bits” they’d scavenged days ago—from his pocket and tossed them to the vendor, a bored-looking woman with electric blue hair and a crystal embedded in her forehead. She caught the money without looking, her eyes glued to a small screen showing what appeared to be competitive cupcake-decorating.

“Now for the intel,” Zonk said, lowering his voice. “See that stall with the purple light? That’s our target. We need to know how to get in, what the deal is. You’re going to go buy. I don’t know, a glowing lollipop or something. From the stall right next to it. Listen. Look casual.”

“Casual,” Pip repeated, as if trying out a foreign word.

“Yeah. Relaxed. Uninterested. Like you don’t have a care in the world besides that future diabetes you’re about to purchase.” He gave her a gentle push. “Go on. I’ll be right here, blending.”

Pip took a deep breath, squared her shoulders inside the giant jacket, and marched toward the lollipop cart with the grim determination of a soldier heading into a trench. He was sending a kid who thought soufflé was an elective into a den of literal cutthroats. To buy candy. This was his plan.

He leaned against a support beam that was painted to look like a giant candy cane. The paint was chipped. Underneath, the metal was rusted a dull brown. He focused on that—the rust, the flake of red against the white and pink spiral. A tiny fracture in the perfect, sickly-sweet world.

Pip reached the lollipop cart. She pointed at something, her voice lost in the din. The vendor, a hulking guy with a neck tattoo of a smiling sun, said something back. Pip nodded. She was doing it. She was just a girl in a stupid tracksuit buying a stupid lollipop.

Then her head turned, just slightly, toward the dark stall with the purple light.

A man stepped out from its shadows.

He was tall and lean, dressed in a tailored suit the color of burnt sugar. His hair was a shocking, impossible pink, swept up into a chaotic pompadour. He held a clipboard. He wasn’t looking at Pip. He was looking at a point over her shoulder, his expression one of mild, professional annoyance. But as Pip turned back to complete her purchase, the pink-haired man’s eyes—a bright, robin’s-egg blue—flicked down. They took her in. The too-big clothes, the awkward posture, the face that was trying too hard to look blank.

His gaze lingered for a half-second too long.

Then he smiled. It wasn’t a friendly smile. It was the smile of a cat that had just calculated the trajectory of a canary. He made a small, neat checkmark on his clipboard turned, and disappeared back into the gloom of his stall.

Pip returned, clutching a massive, galaxy-swirl lollipop. She held it out to Zonk like a trophy. “I got the ‘Nebula Dream’ flavor. The vendor said it induces a mild sense of cosmic awe for approximately twenty minutes.”

“Great. Did you see him? The pink guy?”

She nodded, her earlier focus returning. “He came out. He looked at a schedule. He saw me. He made a note.” Her brow furrowed. “He had a pin on his lapel. A little cannon with a party hat. Just like the sign.”

“Yeah.” Zonk’s mouth was dry. The key-crystal in his pocket felt like it was burning a hole through the horrible purple hoodie. “That’s the guy. Or one of them.” He took the lollipop from her. It was heavier than it looked. “What’s the play here, Pip? You’re the protocol expert. Do we walk up and ask for a tour? Try to sneak in a back door?”

She was quiet for a moment, her eyes on the dark stall. The pulsing purple light seemed to beat in time with the synth-carnival music, a slow, diseased heart. “The training,” she said slowly, “for approaching an unknown entertainment venue with possible hostile elements. It recommends gathering observable data first. Patterns of entry and exit. Security posture. If possible, acquiring an invitation from a verified patron. Forced entry has the lowest success rate and highest probability of. ” She trailed off, swallowing. “Of injury.”

“An invitation.” Zonk barked a laugh that had no humor in it. “Right. We’ll just find someone coming out of the secret fight club and ask them to pretty please bring us to the next one.”

A commotion erupted three stalls down—a shouted argument about correct change. A cluster of people turned to look. In the shift and stir of the crowd, Zonk’s eye caught a flash of familiar colors. Red and yellow. A bandana, knotted around a thick wrist, disappearing into the throng near a fried dough stand.

Blaze’s bandana. The one from the concert. The one he never took off.

Zonk’s breath hitched. He grabbed Pip’s arm. “Did you see that?”

“See what?”

He didn’t answer. He was already moving, shoving through the crowd, the giant lollipop held before him like a clumsy battering ram. The smell of hot grease and powdered sugar grew overpowering. He reached the dough stand, his heart hammering a new, frantic rhythm.

No bandana. No Blaze.

But leaning against the counter, licking glossy pink frosting from his fingers was a huge man in a leather vest. His arms were a mural of faded tattoos—hearts, daggers, a poorly rendered dragon. And tied around his left bicep, a clean, bright strip of red-and-yellow cloth.

Blaze’s bandana.

The man finished his doughnut, crumpled the wax paper, and tossed it into an overflowing bin. He wiped his hands on his jeans turned, and ambled away from the stall. He moved with the loose, confident gait of someone who owned the space around him. He headed not for the market exit, but deeper into the maze, toward a section of stalls that seemed darker, quieter.

Toward the alley behind the purple-lit stall.

“We have our invitation,” Zonk said, low.

Pip stared at the bandana on the man’s arm. Her face went pale. “He took it from him.”

“Yeah. Which means he knows where Blaze is. Or was.” Zonk’s hands clenched. The static from the stupid hoodie made the hair on his arms stand up. “We follow. We see where he goes. And then we get it back.”

He didn’t wait for her agreement. He just started walking, keeping a dozen people between them and the man in the vest. The neon carnival blurred at the edges of his vision, all its forced cheer now just a garish backdrop for the single, stark detail ahead: a stolen piece of cloth, moving steadily toward the dark.

The service corridor smelled of damp stone and the ghost of a thousand boiled cabbages. It was a relief after the cloying incense. Zonk’s lungs burned. He kept waiting for the shout, the pounding of boots, but the only sound was the scuff of their own feet and a distant, rhythmic thump-thump-thump that seemed to come up through the floorboards. Like a heartbeat. Or a bass line.

Pip stopped at a junction where the corridor split. Left, right, or straight into a wall hung with a faded web of a unicorn vomiting a rainbow. It was moth-eaten. One of the unicorn’s eyes was missing.

“Which way?” Zonk’s voice came out a croak.

She didn’t answer. She was staring at her hands, turning them over as if she’d never seen them before. The torn lace flapped.

“Hey. Pip. Geography. Now would be good.”

“Red,” she said.

“Yeah, you said. It worked. Let’s go.”

“I said red.” She looked up at him. Her eyes were huge in the gloom. “I’ve never. You’re not supposed to. Not to an auditor. It’s like using a fire alarm to get out of a boring meeting.”

“Seemed like a great meeting to get out of.” The thump-thump-thump was getting louder. It vibrated in his molars.

“They’ll report it. It’ll go on my permanent record. I’ll be. Unplaceable.” A weird little laugh hiccupped out of her. “My finishing mistress would have a thrombosis. A literal thrombosis.”

Zonk grabbed her shoulder. It was bony under the soft fabric. “Kid. Listen. The guy with the stylus was gonna let Blaze get turned into party confetti. Your record is the least of our problems. Which way to the Party Cannon?”

The word “cannon” seemed to snap her back. She blinked, shook her head, and pointed left. “Down. Always down. The Party Cannon isn’t a place you find. It’s a place you fall into.”

They went left. The corridor sloped downward, the stone walls giving way to rough-hewn brick, then to damp, untreated earth held back by wooden beams that looked like they’d been chewed into place. The thump-thump resolved into music—a frenetic, off-key carnival tune played at twice the normal speed, punctuated by a deep, subsonic boom. The air grew warmer, thicker, smelling of sugar, sweat, and something coppery.

A sign appeared, nailed to a beam. It was pink, with loopy, cheerful letters: The Party Cannon! Fun for Everypony! An arrow pointed down a set of rickety stairs. From below, a flash of strobe light pulsed, bleaching the shadows for a split second, then plunging them back into near-darkness.

Zonk’s stomach did a slow, unpleasant roll. This wasn’t a VR dungeon. There was no respawn point, no health bar ticking down in the corner of his vision. This was a basement where bad things happened and the decor was by someone who’d read about joy in a manual and gotten it all wrong.

He took the stairs first, each step groaning under his weight. The music hesitated them whole—a wall of sound so physical it felt like pressure against his skin. At the bottom, a heavy velvet curtain, this one a violent magenta, blocked the way. A bored-looking pegasus-kin with a clipboard and a collar of jingle bells stood guard.

“Invite?” she yelled over the din.

“We’re with the, uh, the entertainment,” Zonk yelled back, jerking a thumb at Pip. “The conga line coordinator. She’s here for the pre-fight morale sync.”

The pegasus-kin looked Pip up and down, her eyes lingering on the torn cuff. She shrugged, the bells on her collar tinkling a flat, cheap sound. “Whatever. Ten-bit cover charge each. Or you can work it off in the glitter pit after the main event.”

Zonk patted his pockets, coming up empty. He’d left his wallet in another dimension. Pip, however, reached into a small hidden pouch at her waist and produced two thin, iridescent chips. She handed them over without a word. The mare took them, bit one to test it, and jerked her head toward the curtain.

“Try not to get blood on the streamers. Celestia hates the stain.”

Then they were through, and the Party Cannon hit Zonk in the face.

The room was a seizure in three dimensions. Strobe lights cut through a fog of what looked like pink cotton candy, but smelled like burnt hair. The walls were painted with giant, lopsided cartoons of smiling ponies holding balloons that were also, on closer inspection, very detailed maces. The floor was sticky. Confetti swirled in the air, but when a piece landed on Zonk’s arm, it stung—tiny, sharp edges catching the light. Razor glitter.

In the center of the room was a ring, not of rope, but of giant, interlocked licorice whips. Inside, two figures circled each other. One was a massive earth-pony kin, his coat a dull brown, his hooves wrapped in sparking brass knuckles. The other was a slender unicorn-kin, darting and weaving, her horn glowing as she flung handfuls of what appeared to be multicolored thumbtacks.

The crowd around the ring was a heaving, screaming mass of color. They wore party hats. They blew noisemakers that sounded like dying geese. They threw handfuls of the sharp glitter at the fighters, cheering when it stuck in their coats or drew a thin line of red.

“Sweet mother of. ” Zonk said.

Pip was pressed against his side, her fingers digging into his arm. She wasn’t looking at the ring. She was scanning the periphery, her eyes darting past a concession stand selling “Punch!” in cups that dripped a suspiciously viscous purple liquid, past a “Photo Booth!” where a dazed-looking colt was having his picture taken with a pony in a blood-stained Pinkie Pie costume.

“There,” she said, her voice barely audible. She pointed.

In a shadowed alcove behind the photo booth, barred with more of the licorice whip was a holding pen. A few figures slumped inside on piles of dirty hay. And one large, familiar shape sat with his back against the wall shoulders squared, head up.

Blaze.

Even from across the room, Zonk could see the damage. One eye was swollen shut, a lurid purple and green. A cut on his cheek had been clumsily bandaged with a strip of polka-dot fabric. But he was watching the fight, his expression not one of fear, but of cold, clinical assessment. He was counting punches. Timing the unicorn’s throws. Planning.

Something hot and sour flooded Zonk’s throat—not quite anger, not quite panic. It was the feeling you got when you saw a cockroach skating across the surface of your soup—a intense, raw wrongness.

A roar went up from the crowd. The earth-pony had landed a blow, a sparking brass-knuckle punch to the unicorn’s jaw that spun her around. She went down, her horn sputtering. The earth-pony raised his arms, and the carnival music swelled into a triumphant, discordant fanfare. A mare in a sequined vest vaulted into the ring, grabbing the earth-pony’s wrist.

“And the winner, by total sparkle-conversion! Give it up for Mister Pummice!” she shrieked into a lollipop-shaped microphone. “Who’s next? Who’s brave enough to party with the Pummice?”

This was their shot. The announcer’s attention was on the crowd, the guards were focused on dragging the unconscious unicorn from the ring. Zonk’s mind, that useless swamp, finally coughed up a plan. It was terrible. It relied entirely on the specific, pedantic insanity of this world.

“Stay close,” he muttered to Pip, and started pushing through the crowd toward the ring.

The smell up close was worse—body odor and sugar syrup and the sharp, clean scent of fresh blood. Zonk vaulted over the licorice rope, his shoes sinking into the soft, stained canvas of the ring floor. The announcer turned, her smile faltering for a second at the sight of a human in street clothes.

“Well! A bold new challenger! And what’s your name, sweetie?”

Zonk ignored her. He walked to the center of the ring, pointed directly at the announcer, and raised his voice.

“I invoke clause seven-dash-twelve of the Canterlot Combat Carnival Codex! The right of. Of champion substitution!”

The announcer blinked. The crowd’s cheers dipped into confused murmurs. “What?”

“Clause seven-dash-twelve!” Zonk repeated, pouring every ounce of bullshit authority he could muster into his voice. He’d read something like this in the game’s lore, a throwaway line about ceremonial stand-ins. “It states that any registered combatant may, prior to the commencement of a bout, nominate a substitute if the original champion is. Medically indisposed. Or suffering from emotional fatigue. I’m here for the guy in the pen. The big one. He’s my substitute.”

“That’s for sanctioned charity matches!” the announcer sputtered, her sequins rattling. “This is a Party Cannon throw-down! The rules are ‘no biting’ and ‘pretty colors win extra points’!”

“Are they?” Zonk took a step closer. He could see the doubt in her eyes. This whole world ran on rules nobody fully understood but everyone was terrified of breaking. “Because the Codex’s jurisdiction extends to all public spectacles of martial contest within the city limits, unless specifically exempted by royal decree. Got a decree? A signed, notarized exemption from Celestia herself? No? Then it’s Codex rules. And the Codex says I can call a sub.”

He was making it up as he went, weaving game trivia with half-remembered legal drama plots. But the jargon was the key. It was the magic word. The announcer’s face did the same glitch the Inspector’s had—the system error. She looked frantically to a stallion at the edge of the ring wearing a referee’s shirt. He just shrugged, helpless.

Meanwhile, Pip had sidled up to the concession stand. With a deliberate, clumsy motion, she knocked over a giant glass bowl full of the purple punch. It hit the sticky floor with a crash and a splash, the liquid spreading in a viscous tide toward the crowd’s feet. A few ponies yelped, jumping back.

“Oops,” Pip said, loud enough to carry. “My goodness. What a mess.”

It wasn’t much. But it was a distraction. The announcer, already flustered, snapped her attention to the spill. “Somepony clean that up! The sugar attracts glitter-rats!”

In that second of chaos, Zonk caught Blaze’s eye across the room. He gave a sharp, barely perceptible nod toward the licorice bars of the pen.

Blaze understood. He was already moving, his big hands wrapping around two of the candy bars. They weren’t metal. They were licorice, hardened to a tough, woody consistency, but still organic. Still susceptible to force applied in the right way. Zonk saw the muscles in Blaze’s back and shoulders bunch. He didn’t roar didn’t strain theatrically. He just leaned his weight into it, a steady pressure.

There was a loud crack, like a tree branch snapping in winter. One of the bars splintered. Then another.

The guard at the pen, a pegasus-kin picking his teeth with a glitter-shard turned. His eyes went wide. He fumbled for a whistle.

“The contract!” Zonk yelled, pointing at the announcer again, pulling her focus back. “You’re in breach! Failure to adhere to the Codex invalidates your insurance waivers! You’ll be personally liable for any spectator injuries! Do you have liability insurance? ”

The announcer paled. “Liability? Nopony said anything about liability!”

From the corner of his eye, Zonk saw Blaze kick the broken bars aside and step out of the pen. He didn’t run. He walked, with a limping, deliberate stride, straight toward the ring. The crowd, confused by the legal argument and the punch spill, parted for him almost without thinking. He looked like he belonged there—a battered gladiator answering the call.

He reached the licorice ropes and hauled himself over, dropping into the ring beside Zonk. He was breathing hard, and the polka-dot bandage was seeping a dark red. But he clapped a heavy hand on Zonk’s shoulder. The grip was solid. Real.

“Took you long enough,” Blaze said, his voice rough. “Your legal thing—”

Zonk’s knees felt weak. The hot, sour feeling in his throat melted into something else, something shaky and light. “You’re welcome. Now what’s the plan for the part where they kill us?”

Blaze looked past him, at the announcer who was now arguing with the referee, at the guard blowing his whistle to no effect in the cacophonous din. He cracked his neck, a series of pops that were audible even over the music.

“I’m thinking we leave,” he said.

The service corridor smelled of damp stone and the ghost of a thousand spilled drinks, something sticky-sweet and fermented. Zonk’s knees felt like they’d been replaced with overcooked noodles. Pip walked ahead of him, her steps measured, the torn lace on her cuff a tiny, fluttering white flag. She didn’t look back.

“Kid,” he managed, his voice a dry rasp. “What the hell was that?”

“Protocol,” she said, not slowing. The word was flat, a stone dropped in a well.

“Since when do you know protocol?”

“Since always.” She stopped at a junction where the corridor branched. One way sloped downward, the rough stone walls giving way to painted drywall covered in garish, looping graffiti of balloons and cupcakes. A distant, rhythmic thump vibrated through the floor. “That way.”

“Pip.”

She finally turned. In the dim light, her face was pale, her eyes dark hollows. The terrible calm was gone, cracked open. Something raw and shaky lived underneath. “They teach you. In training. All the protocols. All the clauses. In case a patron. Oversteps. Or in case you need to terminate a contract without penalty.” She stopped. “I just never thought I’d use it for. For this.”

The confession hung between them. Zonk’s brain, the white sheet, finally got some text. Not a realization—those were done, he’d already realized Pip wasn’t just a kid—but a confirmation. A sick, solid weight in his gut. Her designed naivete had a manual. A troubleshooting guide.

“Right,” he said, because nothing else fit. He looked down the garish corridor. The thumping resolved into a bass line, punctuated by sharp, collective shouts. “The Party Cannon?”

“It’s the only underground venue with holding pens on this level. The Inspector’s slate had a dispatch notice. ‘One large human contaminant, for disposition at PC.’ It’s where they’d take him.”

“Disposition.” The word tasted like battery acid.

Pip just nodded and started down the slope.

The noise grew. The bass wasn’t music, not really—it was a physical pressure, a fist squeezing the air in his lungs. The graffiti got louder too, neon spray-paint depicting grinning ponies wielding comically large mallets, party hats tipped at violent angles. A sign, hand-painted in dripping pink read: PARTY CANNON - NO SPOILSPORTS - NO REFUNDS.

The corridor ended at a heavy velvet curtain, black, stained with something that glistened under the single bare bulb. The smell shifted—sweat, cheap candy, blood, and the sharp, astringent bite of industrial cleaner. Pip didn’t hesitate. She pulled the curtain aside a crack.

Light and sound vomited out.

Zonk looked over her head.

It was a nightmare birthday party. The room was a vast, low-ceilinged basement, strung with drooping streamers and flickering fairy lights. A central ring, not a proper boxing ring but a circle of scuffed linoleum surrounded by bales of hay was the focus. In it, two figures circled each other. One was a mountain of a pony in a torn tank top, his muzzle a mask of dried blood. The other was a scrawny unicorn-kin with a glittery horn-helmet, wielding a pool noodle wrapped in barbed wire. The crowd, a seething mass of ponies in party hats and little else, roared. They weren’t cheering for a winner. They were screaming for the next hit, the next spray of crimson on the linoleum.

To the left of the ring raised on a rickety platform was a pony in a pink sequined vest. He held a kazoo to his lips, blasting a shrill, off-key note into a microphone. “AND LADIES, GENTS, AND NON-BINARY PALS! GUMDROP’S GOT SOME SPRING LEFT IN HIS STEP! WILL TWINKLE-STAR’S BARBED BEAUTY FINALLY POP HIS BALLOON? PLACE YOUR LAST-MINUTE BETS! REMEMBER, FRIENDSHIP IS MAGIC, BUT VIOLENCE IS A MORE LIQUID ASSET!”

Zonk’s eyes scanned the periphery. Cages. Rusty makeshift things, lined the far wall. Most held dejected-looking ponies, some bruised, some just staring into space.

“Blaze.”

He was sitting with his back against the bars, head down. One eye was swollen shut. His lucky bandana was gone. But his shoulders were set, his hands resting on his knees, not trembling. Just waiting.

A guard, a hulking earth-pony with a security shirt straining over his barrel chest, leaned against the cage, watching the fight.

Plan. They needed a plan. Zonk’s mind scrabbled, a rat in a slick-walled bowl. He couldn’t fight that crowd. He couldn’t reason with the kazoo-announcer. The contract-law gambit was spent.

Pip’s hand closed around his wrist. Her fingers were ice-cold. “Cause a distraction,” she murmured, her voice barely audible under the roar.

“What? How?”

“You’ll know.” And then she was gone, slipping through the curtain and into the edge of the crowd, a small, pale ghost in the strobe-light chaos.

Shit.

Zonk stood there, heart hammering against his ribs. He looked at Blaze in the cage. He looked at the announcer on the platform. He looked at the guard. The kazoo shrieked again, a sound that drilled directly into his molars.

Okay. Distraction.

He took a deep breath of the foul candy-coated air and pushed through the curtain.

He didn’t try to blend. He walked with a slouch he didn’t feel, aiming for the platform. The crowd jostled him, a pony spilling something neon-green and sticky on his shoe. He didn’t stop. He climbed the three wobbly steps to the announcer’s platform. The pony in the sequined vest glanced over, kazoo dropping from his lips. “Hey, pal, authorized personnel only. You wanna bet, use the drones.”

Zonk leaned close to the microphone. He didn’t shout. He used his dry, rasping voice, the one that carried just enough.

“Article Seven, subsection B of the Canterlot Municipal Entertainment Code,” he said.

The announcer blinked. “What?”

“Governing voluntary combatant status and the ‘knowing assumption of risk.’” Zonk plowed on, making it up as he went, weaving in half-remembered phrases from video game EULAs and the inspector’s rant. “I’m here representing the, uh, the non-affiliated participant in cage seven. There’s a discrepancy in his liability waiver. Specifically, the clause regarding dimensional provenance and its impact on insurance underwriting. This entire event’s coverage is now in question.”

He gestured vaguely toward Blaze’s cage. The guard there was now looking over, confusion on his broad face.

The announcer stared. “The waiver is standard. He was processed.”

“Was he?” Zonk leaned in. “Did you verify his plane of origin? Because if he’s a planar contaminant, which the Solar Crown has officially designated, then your standard waiver is null under the Extra-Planar Contaminant Act of. Last Tuesday, I think. You’re not insured for inter-dimensional incidents. One bad punch and the whole operation is liable. Personally liable.” He let that hang. “I’d need to see your insurer’s rider. Now.”

The announcer’s smile was gone. He looked from Zonk to the guard, to the crowd. This was his language, but spoken in a bastard dialect he couldn’t quite parse. “This is a shakedown.”

“This is compliance,” Zonk said, pouring every ounce of weary, bureaucratic menace he could muster into the words.

Down by the cages, movement.

Pip had reached the edge of the crowd near Blaze’s cage. She wasn’t looking at Blaze. She was looking at the guard, who was now distracted, arguing with someone via a crackling earpiece, his head turned away.

Then she did the stupidest thing Zonk had ever seen.

She straightened her shoulders, put on a bright, vacant smile, and started to clap. A steady, rhythmic clap. Then she began to sway. With a little hop, she started a conga line. By herself.

She snaked through the edge of the crowd, clapping, humming a tuneless approximation of the bass line. A drunk pony near her hooted and fell in behind her, grabbing her waist. Then another. In ten seconds, she had a wobbly, six-pony conga line shuffling between the crowd and the cages, a ridiculous, snaking barrier of flailing limbs and off-beat clapping.

The guard, finishing his call turned back and threw his hands up in disgust as the line wobbled past, blocking his view of Blaze’s cage.

Now.

Zonk abandoned the sputtering announcer and half-fell, half-ran down the platform steps. He ducked under a stray elbow, sidestepped a puddle of something unidentifiable, and reached the back of the conga line as it passed the cage.

Blaze was already on his feet, his good eye locked on Zonk. No words. Zonk fumbled with the simple latch on the cage door—a padlock, but the hasp was just hooked, not clicked shut. Sloppy. He yanked it open.

The door squealed on rusty hinges.

The guard heard it. He bellowed, shoving a conga-ing pony aside. Blaze erupted from the cage. He didn’t tackle the guard. He grabbed the bale of hay next to the cage and shoved it, all his weight behind it, into the man’s legs. The guard went down with a grunt, tangled in hay and his own security shirt.

“Go!” Blaze barked, his voice rough but familiar.

They went. Zonk grabbed Pip, pulling her from the head of her now-disintegrating conga line. The three of them bolted for the opposite side of the room, where a glowing red EXIT sign hung over a metal door.

The crowd’s roar shifted, turning from bloodlust to confusion, then to a new, gleeful anger. Something flew past Zonk’s head—a plastic cup, then a horseshoe. The kazoo was screeching again. “PARTY FOUL! SECURITY! SOMEONE’S TRYING TO LEAVE THE PARTY EARLY!”

They hit the metal door shoulder-first. It burst open into a cold, dark alley piled with reeking garbage bags. They stumbled out, the door swinging shut behind them, muffling the chaos.

For a second, they just stood there in the dim light from a distant streetlamp, gulping the cold, dirty air. The bass was still a vibration in their bones.

Blaze leaned against the brick wall, breathing hard. He touched his swollen eye, winced. Then he looked at Zonk, then at Pip.

A grin split his bruised face. It was lopsided, painful-looking, and utterly genuine. “Dude,” he said quietly. “A conga line?”

Pip’s calm was utterly shattered. She was trembling, her eyes huge. “It was all I could think of.”

Zonk looked from one to the other. The weight in his gut was still there, but it had changed. It wasn’t just sickness. It was something else, something hot and tight and terrifying. Not quite pride. Not quite fear. Something without a name.

Blaze pushed off the wall, clapping a massive hand on Zonk’s shoulder. The grip was solid, real. “Let’s not do that again.”

“Yeah,” Zonk said, his voice still unsteady. “Let’s.”

They moved down the alley, a trio of shadows. United. Rescued.

The cost became clear twenty feet later. Zonk patted his pockets, a habit. His fingers closed around the warm, hard shape of the key-crystal from the Inspector. He pulled it out. In the alley’s gloom, it was dark. Not just inactive. Cracked. A fine, hairline fracture split its surface from top to bottom. He must have crushed it in his fist during the run.

It was useless now.

He didn’t say anything. He just closed his fist around the broken thing and kept walking.
