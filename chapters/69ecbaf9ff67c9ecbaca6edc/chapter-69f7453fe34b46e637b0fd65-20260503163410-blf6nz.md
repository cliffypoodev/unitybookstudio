Chapter 3

The apartment smelled of stale weed and fresh panic. Zonk’s thumb found the ragged skin beside his nail again, a familiar groove he’d worried raw over years of bad decisions. The six objects from the game sat in the middle of the coffee table, pushing aside a stack of unopened bills and a petrified slice of pizza. They didn’t belong. They were too shiny, too deliberately shaped, like props from a fetish shop that had gotten lost on its way to a much weirder party.

Blaze loomed over the table, his shadow swallowing the pink gag ball. “So. Magic sex toys.”

“Sacred Elements,” Pip whispered from the corner of the sofa. She’d pulled her knees to her chin, making herself small. Her eyes, though were fixed on the items with a reverence that felt like a physical weight in the room. “They are the foundation of all contractual harmony.”

“Right.” Blaze picked up the silver tongue depressor. It was cold, heavier than it should be. One end was shaped like a little set of scales. “And this one?”

“The Element of Honesty.” Pip’s voice gained a rehearsed cadence, like a kid reciting a pledge. “It compels truthful testimony during arbitration. The scales balance only when the speaker’s words align with their heart’s intent.”

“A lie detector spatula.” Zonk didn’t look up from the tiara. The thing was a delicate silver filigree set with a single, star-shaped violet gem that seemed to drink the gray afternoon light from the window. It felt… aware. He set it down carefully, as if it might bite. His fingers came away smelling like ozone and vanilla. A completely useless detail. The crack in the ceiling above the TV looked more like a lopsided smile than a question mark today.

“It is not a spatula,” Pip said, a flicker of indignation breaking through her fear. “It is a sacred instrument. Used by Solar Magistrates in covenant disputes.”

Blaze swapped the tongue depressor for the red cuffs. They were simple, brutal-looking things, a dull matte metal with a single hinge and a locking mechanism that looked like a stylized sun. “And these bad boys?”

“The Element of Loyalty.” Pip uncurled a little. “They cannot be broken. Once locked, they open only for the one who holds the key, or through the fulfillment of the sworn oath.”

“Cool.” Blaze grinned, the same grin he got before attempting a backflip off a questionable surface. He slapped one cuff around his own wrist. It clicked shut with a sound like a vault door sealing.

The grin froze.

He tugged. The cuff didn’t budge. He pulled harder, his bicep straining against the strap of his tank top. Nothing. The metal didn’t even dig into his skin; it just sat there, cold and final.

“Uh.” Blaze’s eyes darted to Zonk. “Little help?”

“You’re an idiot.” Zonk stood, a hollow feeling opening in his gut. He grabbed the free end of the cuff and pulled. Blaze yelped as his arm was jerked forward. The lock didn’t so much as scratch.

“It’s not funny, dude.”

“Who’s laughing?” The panic was a dry metallic taste at the back of Zonk’s throat. Not a metric. A taste. He fumbled with the lock, looking for a release button, a keyhole, anything. The sun symbol was smooth, featureless. “Pip. How do they open?”

She blinked. “I… I do not know the specific key. It is unique to each contract. But there is always a secondary release. A fail-safe. For safety.”

“A safeword.” The words left Zonk’s mouth before the thought fully formed. It was lore, stupid game lore mixed with the half-remembered pamphlets from that one awkward BDSM club they’d stumbled into years ago. “It’s a safety protocol. There’s a sequence.”

He grabbed Blaze’s imprisoned wrist, ignoring his friend’s flinch. He tapped the cuff twice with his index finger. Nothing. He tried three times. A rhythm. Tap-tap. Pause. Tap.

The lock emitted a soft, musical chime. The hinged side popped open with a sigh of released pressure.

Blaze snatched his arm back, rubbing the wrist where the cuff had been. No mark. “What the hell was that?”

“Red, yellow, green.” Zonk’s heart hammered against his ribs. “Two taps for ‘check in,’ three for ‘slow down,’ one for ‘stop.’ It’s a… it’s a communication system. For when you can’t speak.” He stared at the open cuff in his hand. The metal was warming to his touch. “These aren’t just props.”

Pip was watching him, her head tilted. The practiced wonder was back, but it was edged with something else. A sharp curiosity. “You know the Old Protocols.”

“I know game mechanics.” He threw the cuff onto the table. It clattered against the tiara. “This is all just… coded rules. Someone took BDSM safety stuff and baked it into their magical world-building. It’s not real.”

“The cuff locked,” Blaze said quietly. “On my actual arm. In our actual apartment.” He pointed at the purple flogger with its diamond-shaped falls. “What’s that one do? Generously whip someone?”

“The Element of Generosity facilitates equitable exchange,” Pip recited, but her eyes were still on Zonk. “It ensures no party leaves a transaction feeling diminished.”

“A flogger that makes sure everyone gets a fair cut.” Zonk let out a breath that was almost a laugh. It hurt. “Great. Perfect. And the whoopee cushion?” He nudged the pink rubber ball gag set. It emitted a faint, sad squeak.

“Laughter relieves contractual tension and resets negotiation timers.” Pip finally looked away, her cheeks coloring. “It is considered… undignified. But vital.”

Silence settled, thick and uncomfortable. Blaze picked up the small yellow jar, unscrewed the lid, and sniffed. “Lemon Pledge and… cayenne?”

“Healing unguent,” Pip said. “For aftercare. Kindness.”

Zonk’s gaze drifted back to the tiara. The star gem seemed to pulse, once, with a light that had no source in the room. He remembered the unicorn mare in the game, her question. Do the real contracts bind the heart, or just the flesh? A shiver walked down his spine, a centipede of pure dread. This wasn’t a game glitch. This was a translation. Someone had taken concepts—Loyalty, honesty, magic—and turned them into physical, functioning… things. Tools. Weapons.

“Pip,” he said, his voice too calm. “What happens if the Elements are gone from where they’re supposed to be?”

She wrapped her arms around herself. “The contracts weaken. The balance fails. The Solar Sentinels… the Royal Guard… They will be looking. They track the harmony echo.” She said it like it was obvious, like mentioning that water was wet. “They will find the dissonance. They will find us.”

Blaze sat down heavily on the floor. The pizza box crumpled under him. “So we stole the magical foundation of a dimension, and the magic cops are coming.”

“They are not ‘cops.’ They are the enlightened will of her Solar Majesty made manifest to correct aesthetic and contractual deviations.” Pip’s correction was automatic, parroted. Then her lower lip trembled. “They are very thorough.”

Zonk looked at the tiara. He looked at Pip, small and scared on his stained sofa. He looked at Blaze, who was staring at his own hands as if they’d betrayed him. The crack in the ceiling grinned its lopsided grin.

A key scraped in the apartment door lock.

All three of them froze. It wasn’t the smooth turn of Blaze’s key. It was a slow, deliberate metallic probing.

Then a heavy, rhythmic knocking shook the door in its frame. Not a request. An announcement.

Thump.

A voice followed, deep and filtered, like someone speaking through a helmet made of polished candy. “Open in the name of the Solar Crown. We sense unauthorized arcana. We smell… dissonance.”

Blaze’s eyes met Zonk’s. Wide. White-rimmed.

The knocking came again, harder. The doorjamb splintered with a sound like a snapping bone.

“Okay,” Zonk whispered, the word dust-dry in his mouth. “So they’re thorough and punctual.”

The silence after the cuff popped open was a different animal than the one before. Not the tense, waiting kind. This one was hollow, like the air had been sucked out of the room and replaced with something colder. Blaze’s wrist looked pale and ordinary. The cuff lay on the table, a harmless-looking piece of scrap metal. It was the most terrifying thing Zonk had ever seen.

Pip broke the quiet. “You do know the Old Protocols.” Her voice had lost its breathy reverence. It was flat. Assessing.

“I know the difference between a red light and a green one,” Zonk snapped. The words came out sharper than he meant. His own pulse was a frantic drumbeat in his ears. “It’s not magic. It’s just… good design. Safety first, even in kink-land.” He gestured at the flogger. “So what, the sparkly whip makes sure everyone gets a fair cut? How’s that work? You beat the generosity into someone?”

“It is not for beating,” Pip said, a flicker of her old indignation returning. She pointed a delicate finger. “The falls are imbued with a vibration crystal. When applied with intent during a negotiation, they… harmonize the emotional field. They ensure both parties perceive the exchange as equitable. It prevents resentment.”

“A mood-whipper.” Blaze had recovered enough for a weak grin. He nudged the jar of yellow salve with his knuckle. “And this?”

“The Element of Kindness. A restorative unguent. It heals minor physical abrasions and soothes psychic fatigue after intense contractual alignment.” She said it like she was reading a product label.

“Psychic fatigue,” Zonk repeated. He picked up the jar. It was warm, like skin. The glass was thick, the salve inside the color of old butter. He unscrewed the lid. The smell hit him first—not medicinal, but like his grandmother’s linen closet, lavender and cedar and something faintly sugary. A completely useless memory: sitting on the floor of that closet hiding, the scent of folded sheets and mothballs a blanket around him. He blinked it away. “So it’s… magical Neosporin for your soul.”

“For the aura,” Pip corrected, as if that clarified everything.

He set the jar down next to the pink gag ball. Laughter. It was a simple silicone ball, attached to a leather strap, but the pink was an aggressive candy-store hue. “And this one? You put it in and suddenly everything’s hilarious?”

Pip’s expression tightened, the first real shadow crossing her face. “The Element of Laughter… facilitates release. It is used when a submissive cannot safeword, when the intensity of the covenant becomes… cognitively binding. The compulsion to vocalize distress is redirected. The resulting sounds often resemble laughter. It breaks the feedback loop of panic.”

The apartment felt smaller. The petrified pizza on the table seemed like an artifact from a simpler, dumber world. Zonk’s thumb found its groove again. The raw sting was a grounding wire. “Right. So it’s a panic button that makes you giggle instead of scream. That’s not creepy at all.”

“It is a mercy,” Pip said, but she looked at the gag ball like it was a spider.

Blaze picked up the tiara. He didn’t put it on, just turned it over in his big hands. The violet gem caught the light and seemed to swirl, a tiny galaxy trapped in stone. “And the crown jewel. Literally.”

“The Element of Magic.” Pip’s voice dropped to a whisper. “The master key. The final arbiter. It can, with the proper authority, override any other contract. Lock or unlock any binding. It is the will of the Crown made manifest.”

“The will of Princess Celestia,” Zonk said.

Pip nodded once, a sharp, fearful jerk of her chin.

“And we stole her master key.” Blaze said it slowly, the weight of it landing in the space between them. He looked from the tiara to Zonk. “Dude. We stole the magical, BDSM master key from the pony mob boss.”

“We didn’t know!” The protest sounded pathetic, even to Zonk. “It was a quest! A glowing exclamation point! You don’t say no to the glowing exclamation point!”

“The… quest?” Pip tilted her head. “You were contracted to retrieve them?”

“No. Yes. I don’t know.” Zonk ran both hands through his hair, pulling until his scalp hurt. “It just appeared. No NPC, no dialogue tree. Just a marker on the map and a description that said ‘legendary loot.’ We thought it was an event.”

Pip was silent for a long moment. She studied them, her violet eyes moving from Zonk’s face to Blaze’s, then to the clutter of their apartment—the dirty socks, the stacked pizza boxes, the bong with water the color of weak tea. Her expression shifted through confusion, dawning comprehension, and finally, a pity so profound it was like a physical blow.

“Oh,” she said softly. “You were not contractors. You were… players.”

The way she said it. It wasn’t a question. It was a diagnosis. A death sentence.

“What’s that supposed to mean?” Blaze’s voice had an edge.

“It means you took a test you did not know you were taking.” She folded her hands in her lap, the picture of formal composure, but her knuckles were white. “The Crown is always watching the… the game. For promising candidates. Those with an intuitive grasp of the Protocols. The quest for the Elements is the final trial. Those who succeed are offered prestigious contracts in the Solar Court. As consultants. As… companions.” A faint blush colored her cheeks. “You were supposed to be intercepted at the vault. Greeted. Offered terms. But you… you left. You logged out. That is not possible.”

“We crashed,” Zonk muttered.

“You transited.” Pip’s eyes were huge. “You pulled the Sacred Elements across the Barrier. And you brought me. A trainee. I was… I was in the staging area. I felt the pull. I thought it was my assignment beginning.” She swallowed. “I am a high-value asset. My retrieval will be their highest priority. After the Elements.”

The words hung in the stale air. High-value asset. Zonk looked at her—the slight frame, the too-big eyes, the brand on her flank just visible through the bodysuit. A sun symbol. Not a school mark. A property stamp.

Blaze set the tiara down with a clink that was too loud. “Okay. Okay, new plan. We put all this shit in a box. We find the nearest… portal node, whatever. We shove it back through. We send you home. Apology note. No harm, no foul.”

Pip shook her head, a slow, mournful movement. “It is not so simple. The Elements have been removed from their foundational contracts. The Barrier is destabilizing. Returning them now requires a formal reconciliation ritual. It must be performed at a major node, with a sanctioned arbitrator from one of the Courts. Otherwise, the energy feedback could…” She gestured vaguely. “Tear the seam wider.”

“Great.” Zonk’s laugh was a dry crack. “So we need to walk into pony court with the shit we stole and ask for a do-over. I’m sure that’ll go well.”

“The Royal Guard will already be looking for the signature of the Elements.” Pip’s voice was barely audible. “And for my hum. They track such things. They will trace the… the rip you made. They will come here.”

A cold knot tightened in Zonk’s stomach. Not a metric. A physical fist of ice, low and heavy.

Blaze stood up abruptly, pacing the short length of the room. His footsteps were too loud on the creaking floorboards. “How long?”

“I do not know. The Barrier is strange here. Thin. But they are thorough. And the Solar Sentinels… They do not knock.”

The light from the window was fading, painting the room in long, gray shadows. The crack in the ceiling was just a dark line now. Zonk looked at the six objects on the table. A gag, a spatula, cuffs, a flogger, a jar of cream, and a crown. The sacred foundations of a world. Worthless, terrifying junk cluttering up his coffee table. He had the sudden, overwhelming urge to sweep them all into a trash bag and throw them down the building’s incinerator chute.

He didn’t move.

“We need to hide these,” he said. His own voice sounded distant. “And we need to not be here.”

“Where?” Blaze stopped pacing. “My mom’s? She’ll ask about the pony.”

“Anywhere. A motel. A storage unit. I don’t know.” Zonk’s mind was racing, skidding on empty. All his usual exits—sarcasm, withdrawal, getting blitzed—were dead ends. This was a real problem with a real, probably violent, deadline. The ice in his gut spread.

Pip was watching him again. That same sharp curiosity. “You are afraid.”

“You’re damn right I’m afraid.”

“But you are not running.”

“The door’s right there. I’m thinking about it.”

She shook her head. “You would have already. You are assessing. Like a contractor.” She said it like it was a compliment.

It made him want to scream. He wasn’t assessing. He was freezing. The ancient animal part of his brain was just listing all the ways this could go wrong, a scrolling marquee of disaster. He wasn’t a contractor. He was a guy who forgot to buy toilet paper half the time.

Blaze crouched in front of the coffee table, his face grim. “Okay. We hide the stuff. We take Pip. We go to ground. We figure out who this Luna is—you mentioned a Night Court. Rival business, right? Maybe they’d like to stick it to Celestia. Help us do the return.”

“Consorting with the Night Court is high treason,” Pip whispered, but there was a spark in her eyes. Not fear. Something else. Ambition?

“We’re already treasonous, Pip.” Blaze gave her a lopsided smile. “We’re treason pioneers.”

Zonk stared at his friend. Since when did Blaze come up with plans that weren’t “hit it” or “run away?” The hollow feeling in his gut got deeper. He was supposed to be the brains. The one who thought things through. And all he could think about was the smell of that salve, and his grandmother’s closet, and the specific way the dust motes danced in the dying light over Blaze’s shoulder. A useless, irrelevant observation.

“We need a bag,” he said, the words dragging him back into the room. “Something without a trace. Cash. Burner phones.”

“I’ll hit the ATM,” Blaze said, standing. “You pack the magic sex toys. Pip… Just… try to look less like a magical pony.”

She drew herself up, insulted. “This is my skin.”

“Then put a coat on it.”

As Blaze shrugged into his jacket, a low hum began to vibrate through the floorboards. It was a sub-audible thrum, more felt than heard, like a massive engine idling deep underground. The lightbulb in the ceiling fixture flickered once, twice. The violet gem in the tiara on the table flared, a single pulse of light that painted the room in brief, shocking purple.

All three of them froze.

The hum faded. The light steadied.

From the street below, seven stories down came the sound of a car alarm whooping once, then dying abruptly.

Nobody moved. Nobody said quietly.

Blaze looked at Zonk, his hand still on the doorknob. “Was that… ”

“I don’t know.” Zonk’s mouth was dust-dry. “Maybe a brownout.”

Pip was trembling again, her composure shattered. She stared at the tiara with pure, undiluted terror. “They are scanning,” she said softly. “That was a wide-spectrum ring pulse. Looking for the key.”

She looked from the tiara to the window, to the door.

“They are closer than I thought.”

The silence after the cuff popped open was the wrong kind of quiet. It wasn’t peaceful. It was the quiet of a held breath, of a bomb squad staring at a wire they’d just cut, waiting to see if they’d guessed right. Zonk’s ears rang with it. The ozone-and-vanilla smell from the tiara was stronger now, clinging to the back of his tongue like the ghost of a bad cupcake.

Blaze flexed his freed hand, staring at his unmarked wrist. “Okay,” he said, voice too loud for the room. “Okay. So. Magic safeword cuffs. That’s a thing we own now.” He picked up the pink gag ball, the one Pip had called Laughter. It was a perfect sphere with tiny, delicate straps. He gave it an experimental squeeze. It didn’t squeak. It emitted a low, melodic giggle that seemed to come from inside his own skull.

He dropped it like it was hot. It bounced once on the rug and rolled under the coffee table, the giggle fading into a faint, echoing chuckle.

“Stop touching things,” Zonk said. His own voice sounded thin. The hollow feeling in his gut had solidified into a cold, hard knot. This wasn’t a glitch. A glitch was pixels misbehaving. This was physics taking a sick day. The pink gag ball shouldn’t have bounced like that—it was too light, the wrong material. It shouldn’t have sounded like that.

Pip uncurled fully from the sofa. She moved with a new, unsettling grace, her earlier terror shelved. She knelt by the table, her eyes tracing the items with the focus of a jeweler appraising stolen gems. “The Old Protocols are forbidden knowledge,” she said, not looking at him. “Only the Archivists of the Old Herd are permitted to study them. The Solar Court purged most of the records. How do you know them?”

“I told you. Game mechanics.” He needed to sit down. His legs felt disconnected. He sank into the armchair, the one with the spring that always poked his thigh. The familiar discomfort was an anchor. “Some nerd on the dev team was into kink. They put the real stuff in as an Easter egg. A joke.”

“A joke.” Pip picked up the yellow jar, Kindness. She unscrewed the lid. The scent that wafted out was clean and medicinal, like aloe and lemon verbena. “This unguent heals contractual breaches of the spirit. It soothes the psychic abrasion left by broken promises.” She dipped a finger in came out with a dollop of pale salve. “Is that the joke?”

“Maybe it’s just nice lotion.” Blaze sniffed the air. “Smells like my grandma’s bathroom.”

Pip touched the salve to the inside of her wrist, where the skin was chafed red from her earlier bonds. The redness faded, not slowly, but all at once, as if someone had clicked an undo button on a photo. The skin was smooth, unmarked.

Blaze’s mouth hung open.

Zonk’s thumb found its groove again. The raw skin burned. Specificity creates humor, he thought, the writing rule surfacing from some useless corner of his brain. This wasn’t specific. This was a category error. The world had skipped a track.

“The Elements are attuning,” Pip said softly, screwing the lid back on. She looked at Zonk, her violet eyes clear and sharp. “They have been inert, tools for the Court’s ceremonies. But you activated the Loyalty fail-safe. You spoke the Old Language to them. They are… waking up.”

“I tapped a cuff.”

“You communicated intent. That is the foundation of the magic.” She gestured to the collection. “They are listening now.”

The tiara’s violet gem pulsed, a slow, lazy flash like a dormant eye opening for a second. Zonk stared at it. He remembered the quest text, the florid, stupid description: The Crown of Magic, key to the Covenant of Harmony, through which all intent is focused and made manifest.

“Oh, you have got to be kidding me,” he whispered.

The doorbell rang.

It wasn’t their doorbell. Their doorbell had been broken since the Great Hot Sauce Incident of last November. This was a clear, melodic chime, two perfect notes that hung in the air.

Blaze broke first, moving to the apartment’s peephole in two silent strides. He put his eye to it, his body blocking the dim hallway light. He went very still.

“Blaze?” Zonk’s throat was tight.

Blaze didn’t turn. “There’s… a pony. Out there. Like, a full-on, cartoon pony. But… not.” He said it, a strained hush. “It’s got armor. Gold armor. And it’s holding a… is that a halberd?”

Pip made a small, choked sound. She scrambled behind the armchair, her breath coming in fast shallow hitches. “The Solar Sentinel. They found the weight.”

The doorbell chimed again. The same two notes. Polite. Patient.

“It’s just one,” Blaze said, his face still pressed to the peephole. “Maybe we can… talk to it?”

“You cannot talk to a Sentinel,” Pip hissed from her hiding spot. “They are extensions of the Sun’s will. They do not negotiate. They assess aesthetic deviation and enforce contractual compliance.”

“What’s it doing?” Zonk asked. He didn’t get up. His body refused the command.

“Just… standing there. Looking at the door. Its eyes are glowing. Like, literally glowing, dude. Little suns.”

The Sentinel knocked. Not a fist. It was a single, solid thump, as if something heavy and metallic had tapped the wood. The door shuddered in its frame. A fine crack appeared in the cheap paint, running from the deadbolt down to the floor.

“Okay,” Blaze said, stepping back from the door. “Not talking.”

Zonk’s mind was a white blank. This was the part in the game where you fought, or you ran, or you triggered the dialogue tree. But his controller was gone. The health bar was his actual, fragile body. The thought was so stupid, so profoundly obvious, that it circled back around to being funny. A wet, desperate snort escaped him.

Blaze looked at him like he’d grown a second head. “What?”

“Nothing.” Zonk pushed himself out of the chair. His legs held. “It’s just… we’re about to get murdered by my Little Pony. My mom always said video games would rot my brain. She never said they’d send a pastel fascist to kill me in my own living room.” He was babbling. The words felt loose, unmoored.

The Sentinel knocked again. Thump. Another crack spiderwebbed out from the first. Dust sifted from the doorframe.

The items on the table seemed to hum, a vibration just at the edge of hearing. The tiara’s gem flashed in time with Zonk’s rabbit-quick pulse.

Pip peeked over the arm of the chair. “The Crown,” she whispered. “It is responding to your distress. Your intent is fear. It will amplify that. You must focus.”

“On what? Dying politely?”

“On a solution.” She lowered the words to a thread of steel he hadn’t heard before. “The magic follows command. Give it one.”

Thump. The deadbolt groaned. The metal bent inward, just a fraction.

Blaze looked from the door to the table. His gaze landed on the purple flogger, the one with the diamond-shaped falls. Generosity. “You said this one makes fair trades, right?”

“It facilitates equitable exchange,” Pip corrected, automatic.

Blaze grabbed it. “Right. So I’m gonna go generously offer this guard a fair trade of not breaking down our door.” He hefted the flogger. It was lighter than it looked, the falls swaying like metallic seaweed.

“Blaze, no—” Zonk started.

But Blaze was already moving. He yanked the door open.

The thing in the hallway was a monument to dissonance. It was, as Blaze had said, a pony—a tall, muscular equine form covered in pristine white fur. But its face was a polished gold helmet, featureless except for two slits where eyes of molten light burned. It wore complex gold barding, sun motifs etched everywhere, and it held a polearm that ended in a wickedly sharp, crescent-shaped blade. It smelled like hot metal and cinnamon.

It did not speak. It tilted its helmeted head, the glowing eyes sweeping past Blaze to land on the coffee table behind him. On the Elements.

“Hey,” Blaze said, his voice straining for casual. He held up the flogger like a crossing guard holding a stop sign. “We’re closed, man. Private party.”

The Sentinel’s head swiveled back to him. It took a single step forward, its hoof clacking on the linoleum with the finality of a judge’s gavel.

Blaze swung the flogger.

It wasn’t an attack. It was a warning shot, a crack of the falls in the air between them. But as the falls snapped, a pulse of deep violet light erupted from the diamonds. It hit the Sentinel in the chest plate.

Nothing happened.

For a second, Zonk thought it had failed. Then the Sentinel looked down at its own armor. The gold was… changing. Flaking. Tarnishing at an impossible speed, spreading out from the point of impact like rust in a time-lapse video. A chunk of the chest plate crumbled away, hitting the floor with a dull clang. Underneath was not fur or flesh, but a lattice of glowing, crystalline filaments.

The Sentinel looked back up at Blaze. If anything, the glow in its eyes brightened.

“I don’t think it liked that trade,” Zonk said.

The polearm came up. It moved faster than anything that big should move. Blaze stumbled back, tripping over the threshold, and the crescent blade whistled past his face, embedding itself in the doorframe with a splintering crunch. Plaster dust snowed down.

Zonk didn’t think. He lunged for the table. His hand closed around the first thing he touched—the silver tongue depressor, Honesty. It was freezing, like holding an icicle. He spun.

The Sentinel wrenched its weapon free, stepping into the apartment. It filled the doorway, a nightmare of light and polished decay. It raised the halberd again, aiming for Blaze, who was scrambling backward on his hands and heels.

“Hey!” Zonk shouted.

The Sentinel ignored him.

What was in his heart? Panic. A raw, screaming need for it to stop.

He didn’t swing it. He pointed it, like a remote, at the golden helmet. “Stop!” he yelled, the word tearing from his throat.

The tongue depressor flared, a searing white light that left afterimages on the air. The scales on the end chimed, a clear, ringing note.

The Sentinel froze. The halberd halted in midair. The glowing eyes fixed on Zonk. A voice issued from it, not from a mouth, but from the air itself, resonant and hollow. “COMMAND: CEASE HOSTILITIES. QUERY: STATE DESIGNATION AND CONTRACTUAL AUTHORITY FOR ELEMENT BEARING.”

Zonk’s mouth was desert-dry. His heart hammered against his ribs. The cold from the implement was creeping up his arm, a numbness. He had no authority. He had a stolen sex toy and a lifetime of bad choices.

The truth. The scales wanted the truth.

“My name is Ezekiel Kincaid,” he said, the full name feeling foreign on his tongue. “I don’t have a contract. I stole this. I don’t know what I’m doing. I just want you to leave.”

The scales on the depressor trembled. They balanced, perfectly level.

The Sentinel stared at him. The glow in its eyes flickered, dimmed. “STATEMENT: RECEIVED. TRUTH: CONFIRMED. CONTRADICTION: BEARER HOLDS ELEMENT OF HONESTY WITHOUT COVENANT. PARADOX.” It lowered the halberd, the point clicking against the floor. “DIRECTIVE: CONTAINMENT AND APPREHENSION PRIORITIZED. REPORT TO SOLAR MAGISTRATE FOR RESOLUTION.”

It took another step forward.

The truth hadn’t saved them. It had just confused the program.

From behind the chair, Pip moved. She didn’t run. She darted to the table, her small hand closing around the pink gag ball. She didn’t throw it. She held it up, and she blew across its surface, as if cooling a spoonful of soup.

The giggle that poured out wasn’t melodic. It was a shrieking, hysterical cackle, a sound that clawed at the inside of Zonk’s skull. It filled the apartment, bouncing off the walls, multiplying.

The Sentinel jerked as if struck. It took a staggering step back, its helmeted head shaking from side to side. The crystalline filaments under its damaged armor pulsed erratically. “ERROR. AUDITORY PROTOCOL… CORRUPTION.”

Its words dissolved into static. It stumbled back into the hallway, one hoof missing the floor entirely and punching through the cheap linoleum. It righted itself with a clatter of metal, then turned and clopped away down the hall, moving in a jerky, uncoordinated gait, the cackling sound clinging to it like a swarm of bees.
