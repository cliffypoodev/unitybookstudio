The alley behind the apartment building smelled of dumpster juice and wet cardboard. Zonk’s back was pressed against cold, gritty brick, the rough texture biting through his thin T-shirt. His heart was a frantic animal trying to chew its way out of his ribs. To his left, Blaze was breathing through his mouth, loud and ragged. To his right, Pip’s small hand was clamped around his wrist, her fingers cold as river stones.

From the shattered window three stories up, the sounds of their apartment being systematically destroyed drifted down—the crunch of drywall, the snap of cheap furniture, a low methodical voice giving orders.

“Move,” Zonk whispered. The word tasted like copper.

They moved. A shuffling, stumbling retreat down the alley, away from the orange pool of a streetlight. Zonk’s mind was a white-noise machine. Every instinct screamed to run in a straight line, to put miles between them and the pounding, but a deeper, weirder knowledge surfaced through the panic. The aesthetic is law. They looked wrong. Two humans in jeans and band T-shirts, one small pony-girl in a pastel bodysuit, fleeing under the garish neon of a city that punished visual dissonance with batons that had looked plastic and felt like steel.

A trash can lid clattered ahead. They froze. A shape detached itself from the deeper shadows—a tall, slender figure in a dark, tailored coat, its face obscured by a high collar and a wide-brimmed hat. It paused seemed to consider them. Zonk’s hand went to his pocket, to nothing. His pipe was still on the coffee table, probably in pieces now.

The figure tipped its hat, a gesture that managed to be both polite and deeply threatening, and melted back into the dark.

“What was—” Blaze started.

“Don’t,” Zonk cut him off. His voice was steadier than he felt. “Just don’t. We need to not be us.”

“How do we do that?” Blaze’s whisper was strained. “You got a closet full of pony-play gear I don’t know about?”

“Shut up and look.”

The alley was a graveyard of commerce. Behind a Korean barbecue place, they found a stack of discarded boxes smelling of rancid grease and soy. Next to a shuttered psychic’s storefront, a dumpster overflowed with black plastic bags. And there, behind a club with a pulsating pink neon sign that simply read “HOOVES,” was a small, fenced-off service area. Hanging on the fence were three dark, shapeless bundles.

Zonk approached. They were costumes. Damp costumes, smelling of sweat and cheap detergent. One was a matted brown fur suit, missing a headpiece. Another was a glittery leotard with a ripped fishnet sleeve. The third was a set of guard gear—not the full Solar Sentinel armor, but the simpler, dark blue tabard and vinyl leggings of a night-watch pony, the kind that might direct traffic or check permits at a low-rent club. The helmet was a basic plastic dome with ear holes.

“Jackpot,” Zonk said. He pulled the tabard off the fence. It was cold and slightly slimy.

“You cannot be serious,” Blaze said, staring at the brown fur suit with undisguised horror.

“You got a better idea? They’re looking for two humans and a pony. They are not looking for three… whatever these are.” He thrust the leotard at Pip. “Put this on over your suit. Hide the tail in the… the… I don’t know, figure it out.”

Pip took the garment, her nose wrinkling at the smell. “This is a ‘rave pony’ uniform. It is not for public thoroughfares.”

“Tonight it is.” Zonk was already shrugging into the tabard. It was too short, ending above his waist, and tight across the shoulders. He looked ridiculous. He felt a hysterical laugh bubble up in his throat and choked it down. The plastic helmet smelled of old hairspray and anxiety. He put it on. The world narrowed, the sounds of the alley becoming muffled and hollow.

Blaze, with the expression of a man facing a firing squad, picked up the brown fur suit. “It’s damp, dude.”

“It’s camouflage. Put it on or get flogged. Your choice.”

While Blaze wrestled with the damp, clinging fur, Zonk focused on Pip. She had pulled the glittery leotard on. It was too big, hanging off her small frame, but it covered her distinctive pastel bodysuit. The fluffy blue tail was a problem. She tried tucking it between her legs, but it popped out.

“Here,” Zonk said. He grabbed a length of frayed twine from the ground. “Tie it down. To your leg or something.”

She gave him a look of profound betrayal but did it, wincing as she secured the twine around her thigh, flattening the tail against her leg. The effect was lumpy and uncomfortable, but from a distance, in bad light, it might pass.

Blaze emerged from the fur suit. He was a monstrous, shaggy brown silhouette, the headpiece missing so his own bewildered face peered out from a mound of matted synthetic fur. “I feel like a Muppet that died in a swamp.”

“You look like a local,” Zonk said. It was mostly true. “Now walk. Don’t run. Walk like you belong here, like you’re just three weirdos going to a weird job after a weird night.”

“That’s a lot of weird,” Blaze muttered.

“It’s the baseline. Move.”

They moved out of the alley and onto a side street. The city was a sensory assault. Buildings painted in candied hues of mint, peach, and lavender rose into a night sky the color of a faded bruise. Neon signs in curlicue script advertised “Sugarcube Corner Confectionaries,” “Carousel Boutique,” and “Sparkle Spa & Sorcery.” The air smelled of cotton candy, ozone, and the underlying acrid tang of industrial cleaner. Ponies—or people dressed as ponies with an unsettling degree of physiological accuracy—walked the sidewalks. Some wore simple work harnesses. Others were in elaborate, elegant outfits, their flanks marked with detailed, glowing cutie marks. A unicorn with a flickering mane carried a stack of scrolls in a telekinetic glow, the parchment rustling as she passed.

And everywhere, the eyes. Glances that lingered a second too long on Zonk’s ill-fitting tabard, on the lump at Pip’s thigh, on Blaze’s faceless fur. His skin prickled under the cheap vinyl. He focused on a crack in the sidewalk ahead, a zigzagging fault line filled with iridescent glitter. He counted his breaths. In. Out. The helmet made it sound like he was breathing inside a tin can.

They turned a corner onto a wider plaza. A crowd had gathered around a raised, circular stage lit by harsh white spotlights. Music, a jaunty, upbeat tune played on what sounded like glockenspiels and accordions, tinkled from unseen speakers. The crowd was quiet.

“What’s this?” Blaze whispered, his voice muffled by fur.

“Don’t look,” Zonk said. But it was too late.

On the stage, a pegasus in a simple grey smock was kneeling, her wings bound behind her with satin ribbons. A tall earth pony in the crisp, gold-trimmed uniform of a contract adjudicator stood beside her, holding a slender, flexible cane. His voice, amplified by a charm was calm, pedagogical.

“—breach of Clause Seven, subsection B, regarding the timely delivery of cloud-wool shipments. The aggrieved party represented by the Sun Court has accepted this public arbitration. The penalty, as per the standard Friendship Agreement is ten strokes. Let this be a lesson in the magic of accountability.”

The adjudicator raised the cane. It whistled faintly in the silent plaza. It came down across the pegasus’s back with a sound like a wet towel snapping. She jerked but made no sound. The crowd watched, still and silent. Some wore expressions of mild interest, others of boredom. A foal on its parent’s shoulders sucked on a lollipop shaped like a butterfly.

Zonk’s stomach clenched. He looked away, his gaze landing on a poster glued to a lamppost. It advertised a new musical, “Heartswarm: A Tale of Two Herds.” The smiling pony faces seemed to leer at him.

Another whistle-crack. Another muffled jerk from the stage.

This wasn’t a game. The realization wasn’t a lightning bolt. It was a slow, cold seep, like water filling his shoes. The stupid costumes, the stolen fetish objects in the bag over his shoulder, Pip’s earnest terror—none of it was a glitch, or a joke, or a bad trip. This was a place with rules, and the rules had teeth. The stakes were not points or reputation. They were the difference between walking away and kneeling on a stage.

“Zonk,” Pip whispered, her voice trembling. She was staring at the stage, her face pale under the glitter of the leotard.

He grabbed her arm, gently, and turned her away. “Don’t look. Walk.”

Blaze was already moving, a shambling brown mass putting distance between them and the plaza. The jaunty music faded, replaced by the hum of the city and the rhythmic click of their own footsteps. Zonk’s mind, usually a maze of sarcastic commentary and stoned tangents was suddenly, painfully clear. They needed to get rid of the Elements. They needed to get Pip somewhere safe. They needed to find the way back to their world. Three objectives, simple and impossible.

He spotted a sign hanging over a recessed doorway. It was a simple wooden plaque carved with a crescent moon and a stylized, winking eye. No words. The windows were dark, the glass frosted.

“There,” he said, nodding toward it. “The Night Market front. Pip said look for the moon.”

“It looks closed,” Blaze said.

“Everything here looks like something it’s not.” Zonk adjusted his stupid plastic helmet. The smell of old hairspray was making him nauseous. “We go in. We find someone who knows about portals. We make a deal.”

“With what?” Blaze asked. “We got no money. We got nothing but trouble in a bag.”

Zonk hefted the duffel bag. The Elements inside shifted with a soft, discordant clatter. “We have these. Maybe someone here thinks they’re worth something.”

Pip made a small, distressed sound but said nothing.

They crossed the plaza, skirting the edge of the watching crowd. The third stroke landed just as they reached the recessed door. The sound followed them inside as Zonk pushed the door open, a tiny, sharp punctuation mark to the end of their old debate.

The door swung shut behind them, muffling the city sounds. They stood in a small, dark vestibule that smelled of sawdust and stale beer. A single, low-wattage bulb in a tin shade cast a weak yellow light over a battered wooden bar. The place was empty except for a massive, scarred minotaur polishing a glass with a rag. He didn’t look up.

Zonk’s heart was still pounding, but the target was clear now. The path was narrow and dark and probably led to more terrible places, but it was a path. He took off the plastic helmet. His hair was damp with sweat. He set the helmet on the bar with a definitive click.

The minotaur looked up. His eyes were the color of old pennies. He looked at Zonk’s human face, at Blaze’s fur-suited bulk, at Pip’s lumpy, glitter-covered form. His expression didn’t change.

“We’re here for the Market,” Zonk said. His voice didn’t shake.

The minotaur stared for a long moment. Then he gave a slow, deep nod toward a heavy velvet curtain at the back of the room.

“Through there,” he rumbled. “Mind your step. The floor’s uneven.”

It wasn’t an invitation. It was an observation. Zonk picked up his helmet, slung the duffel bag back over his shoulder, and walked toward the curtain. Blaze and Pip fell in behind him. Their footsteps were the only sound in the quiet, dark room.

The debate was over. This was the plan.

The main thoroughfare was a punch in the face. A candy-colored, saccharine-scented punch. Harmony Boulevard stretched before them, a canyon of impossible architecture: buildings shaped like giant frosted cupcakes, lollipop lampposts casting pools of pink and yellow light, storefronts with awnings striped like peppermint sticks. The air itself smelled of spun sugar and ozone, with an underlying note of disinfectant, like a dentist’s office that had been hosed down with cotton candy spray. Zonk’s stolen helmet felt like a fishbowl, the world outside it with a fever-dream clarity.

People—Ponies—moved in a steady, orderly stream. Earth ponies in simple smocks pulled small, ornate carts. Pegasus couriers darted between balconies overhead, their wing-suits rustling with a sound like stiff parchment. Unicorns in tailored coats walked with a precise, unhurried grace, horns gleaming. Everywhere cutie marks flashed on flanks and sleeves—a balloon, an apple, a lightning bolt—tiny badges of purpose. It was a living cartoon, rendered in sickening detail.

“Holy shit,” Blaze breathed from inside his matted fur. The words were muffled lost in the general hum. His shaggy brown form shuffled next to Zonk, a swamp monster adrift in a parade of pastels.

Pip, sandwiched between them in her glittery leotard kept her head down. Her bound tail gave her walk a stiff, unnatural hitch. “Do not stare,” she whispered, the command tight with fear. “Staring is a challenge. Or an admission of unfamiliarity.”

Zonk forced his eyes forward. His heart was still doing that frantic, caged-bird thing against his ribs, but the sheer sensory overload was a weird kind of anesthetic. He noticed a crack in the cobblestone beneath his boot, a perfect little zigzag filled with something that glistened like melted blue jelly. He had the sudden, irrelevant memory of a grape lollipop he’d dropped on a hot sidewalk when he was seven, watching it melt into a similar, sticky fissure. His mother had been annoyed. The memory had no point. It just arrived, sharp and useless.

They moved with the flow of pedestrian traffic. The rules of the street were immediately, violently clear. The unicorns took the center, the cleanest part of the pavement. Earth ponies kept to the edges, near the gutters. Pegasi owned the air and the higher walkways. Guards in polished armor—Some gold, some blue—stood at intervals, their expressions bored, their eyes constantly scanning. Not for crime, Zonk realized. For aesthetic deviation.

A pegasus courier with a bright pink mane cut too close to a unicorn lordling. A flick of a guard’s baton—rainbow-colored, ridiculous—and the pegasus flinched back, dipping her head in a swift, practiced bow before darting away. The unicorn didn’t even break stride.

“This is fucked up,” Blaze muttered.

“It’s order,” Pip replied, her voice small. “It’s how things are.”

“It’s a theme park run by angry mall cops,” Zonk corrected, his own voice sounding hollow inside the helmet. The tabard chafed under his arms.

Ahead, the flow of the crowd slowed, then stopped, forming a loose circle around a small plaza dominated by a fountain—a statue of a rearing alicorn, water spraying from its horn in a perfect, glittering arc. The water smelled faintly of lavender. In the center of the circle, a scene was unfolding.

An earth pony stallion in a flour-dusted baker’s apron knelt on the cobbles. His head was bowed. Before him stood a unicorn in a severe gray suit, holding a scroll that glowed with a soft, silver light. A Solar Sentinel in full gold armor stood to the side, impassive.

“—breach of Clause Seven, Subsection D,” the unicorn intoned, his voice carrying, crisp and without warmth. “The agreed-upon delivery of sucrose-confections was eighteen dozen. You delivered seventeen dozen and eleven. This constitutes a deficit of five confections. A failure of generosity. A fracture in the contract.”

“The last batch burnt,” the baker pleaded, his voice trembling. “The oven’s element glitched.”

“The contract accounts for equipment failure. You did not file a Form 44-B in the proscribed window. The deficit stands.” The unicorn rolled up the scroll. The silver glow vanished. “The penalty is clear.”

The Sentinel stepped forward. He didn’t draw a weapon. From a loop on his belt, he unclipped a short, flexible rod. It was a creamy white, like ivory, and it ended in a soft-looking pink tuft. It looked like a child’s toy.

The baker shuddered, a full-body tremor, but he didn’t resist. He bent forward, resting his forearms on the cobbles, presenting his back. The crowd was silent. A pegasus child on a nearby balcony looked away, guided by a parent’s wing.

The Sentinel brought the rod down. It didn’t thwack. It made a sound like a wet towel snapping, a dense, meaty impact that echoed off the cupcake facades. The baker jerked. He made no sound. Again. The pink tuft left no mark, but the force behind it was real, brutal. Each strike drove the baker’s breath out in a sharp, choked gasp. Ten strokes. Twelve. The rhythm was methodical, almost lazy.

Zonk’s stomach clenched. His mouth was full of cotton. This wasn’t a game mechanic. There was no health bar ticking down. The baker’s shoulders shook with suppressed sobs. The smell of lavender from the fountain suddenly seemed nauseating, cloying. He felt Blaze go rigid beside him. Pip had turned her face into Zonk’s tabard, a small, muffled sound escaping her.

The Sentinel stopped at twenty. He clipped the rod back to his belt. The unicorn produced a new scroll, a quill. “Do you accept the penalty as just, and reaffirm your commitment to your contractual obligations, including punctual Form 44-B filing?”

The baker, pushing himself up on trembling arms, nodded. A tear dripped onto the cobblestone, darkening the gray stone. He took the quill. He didn’t sign with ink. He pricked his own fetlock with the tip, a bead of crimson welling, and pressed it to the parchment. The scroll glowed briefly, a harsh satisfied gold, then faded.

“Harmony is restored,” the unicorn announced. The crowd began to move again, the momentary pause in the day’s business concluded. The baker stumbled to his feet, clutching his back, and shuffled back toward a side alley, his head low.

The trio stood frozen, a stupid island in the resuming current. The violence had been quiet, bureaucratic, and utterly routine. The pink tuft. Zonk couldn’t get the image out of his head. The cheerful color of it.

“We need to get off this street,” he said. He pulled the words back, strange to his own ears.

“No kidding,” Blaze rasped.

Pip just nodded, her face pale. She pointed a shaky finger down a narrower avenue that branched off the boulevard. The sign above it was a painted wooden board depicting a cracked horseshoe. “The Gilded Pony is that way. The music is… louder.”

They broke from the main flow, slipping into the shadow of the narrower lane. The buildings here were less confectionary, more worn. The paint was chipped. The smell shifted from sugar and ozone to stale beer, fried food, and the sharp, animal tang of real sweat. The ponies here were different too—fewer unicorns, more earth ponies and pegasi in patched clothing, their eyes darting, their movements quicker. A griffon with molting feathers leaned in a doorway, sharpening a claw with a file. He watched them pass, his gaze lingering on the ill-fitting guard tabard, the ridiculous fur suit, the lumpy leotard. It wasn’t a friendly look.

The music Pip had mentioned grew from a throb to a pounding beat. Up ahead, a neon sign flickered: a cartoon pony holding a microphone. The ‘G’ in ‘Gilded’ was dark. The door beneath it was painted a chipped, desperate pink. The bassline from inside was a physical thing, vibrating in Zonk’s teeth.

This was it. A karaoke bar fronting a portal. A door out of this pastel nightmare.

Blaze let out a long, slow breath that stirred the fur around his face. “Okay. Plan. We go in, we find the portal, we dump the magic BDSM gear, we get Pip somewhere safe, we go home. Right?”

“That’s the plan,” Zonk said. The words felt flimsy.

“And if it’s not a portal? If it’s just a bar?”

“Then we get very, very drunk and figure it out tomorrow.”

Pip looked between them, her large eyes wide. “You are leaving?”

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite guilt, not quite the old, familiar urge to flee, something between the two that tightened his throat. He didn’t answer. He just reached for the pink door, the metal handle cold under his palm, and pushed.

The noise and the smell hit him like a wall.

The door to the Prancing Pony didn’t so much open as yield, swollen wood scraping against a warped floorboard with a sound like a tired sigh. The air inside was a solid thing—warm, damp, and thick with the smell of stale beer, old leather, and something sweetly chemical, like cheap cherry syrup. Zonk’s first impression was of a theme pub that had given up halfway through construction and decided to just lean into the failure. Exposed beams crossed the low ceiling, but instead of antlers or vintage farm tools, someone had hung an assortment of well-used bondage gear: a set of leather cuffs, a flogger with faded purple tails, a gleaming bit gag dangling from a hook. He shuffled in behind Blaze, the damp vinyl of his stolen tabard sticking to his back. The plastic helmet was a sauna. A bead of sweat traced a hot, itchy path from his temple down to his jaw. He wanted to rip it off. He kept it on.

The bar ran along the left wall, a slab of dark wood stained with countless ring-shaped ghosts of glasses past. Behind it, a massive figure polished a tankard with a cloth. Not a pony—at least, not in a suit. This was a minotaur, or something close: broad shoulders straining a clean white shirt, a great bull’s head with horns that had been filed down to dull nubs, eyes like chips of obsidian set deep under a heavy brow. He moved with an economical grace that was terrifying.

The place was maybe a third full. A cluster of figures in simple work-tunics huddled around a table, speaking in low tones. In a shadowy booth, a unicorn—a real one, the horn a spiraled bone rising from her forehead—sipped something clear while studying a scroll. Her ears twitched at their entrance. At the far end of the bar, a pegasus with neatly folded wings nursed a drink, his feathers preened to a military crispness. Every single one of them fit. The aesthetic was intact, a cohesive picture of rustic-fantasy-meets-dungeon. Zonk, Blaze in his dead-swamp-Muppet fur, and Pip with her lumpy, tied-down tail were a smudge on the canvas.

“Don’t stare,” Zonk muttered, elbowing Blaze, whose head was swiveling like a tourist in a museum of the bizarre.

“Table. In the back. Now.”

Safe. Zonk’s lungs didn’t believe it. Every muscle was a coiled spring. He noticed a crack in the plaster wall beside him. It’s giving me a headache behind my left eye.”

Blaze shifted on the divan. The damp fur suit made a wet, peeling sound.

“The Elements,” Zonk said. The words were out before he could run them through the usual filters of irony, deflection. His throat was tight. “We’ve got them.”

Maud didn’t move. “Define ‘have.’”

“In a bag. A gym bag. From our world.” He gestured vaguely toward Pip, who sat rigid beside him, her hands clasped in her lap. The glitter on her leotard caught the low light in cheap, desperate sparkles. “She’s got it. The… leakage. Is that what that is?”

“You stole the foundation stones of reality and put them in a polyester sack.” Maud stated it. No judgment, just a fact, like noting the weather. “And you brought them here. To my establishment.” She pushed off the sideboard took two steps toward Pip. Pip flinched, a tiny movement she tried to hide by smoothing the leotard over her knees. “Show me.”

Pip looked at Zonk, a flicker of panic in her wide eyes. He gave a tiny, numb nod. What was the alternative? Run? They were in a velvet box with a woman who looked like she could disassemble a clock and put it back together wrong without changing expression.

With trembling fingers, Pip unzipped the black gym bag at her feet. The faint, discordant hum Zonk hadn’t even consciously registered grew louder, a dissonant chord played on a broken instrument. It vibrated in his molars. Pip pulled out the pink whoopee cushion set—Laughter. She laid it on the low table in front of the divan. The vinyl was garish against the dark wood.

Maud stared at it. Her expression didn’t change, but the air in the room got heavier, denser. Like the atmosphere before a lightning strike. “Where are the others?”

“In the bag,” Pip whispered.

“All of them.”

One by one, Pip laid them out. The silver tongue depressor. The red cuffs. The purple flogger. The jar of yellow salve. Finally, the tiara. It glinted under the lamplight, the star at its center holding the light in a cold, hard point.

Maud didn’t touch them. She circled the table, her gaze moving over each object with a clinical detachment that felt more dangerous than any awe or greed. She stopped behind Zonk. He could feel her there, a pressure against his back. The hair on his neck prickled.

“You took them from the Sun Vault,” she said, not a question. “During the alignment. The game quest.”

“How do you know about the game?” Blaze asked, his voice strained from inside the fur.

“It’s a sieve,” Maud said. She came back around stood facing them. “A pretty net Celestia casts into your world. Catches interesting fish. You two must have been very interesting. The perfect profile. Lore-deep, connection-averse, legally high. Prime material.” She tilted her head. “But you weren’t supposed to catch the net itself. You were supposed to be gently hauled in, dazzled offered contracts. Not this. This is a tear in the canvas.”

Zonk’s mind snagged on an irrelevant detail: a water stain on the ceiling above Maud’s head, shaped exactly like the state of Florida. He wondered if it had ever rained in this room. The thought was a life raft. He clung to it.

“We want to give them back,” he said. The sentence sounded absurd, like a child offering to return a stolen fire truck to a burning station. “We just… we don’t know how. And we need to get her,” he nodded at Pip, “somewhere safe. And then go home.”

“Safe,” Maud repeated. She looked at Pip. “You’re Sun Court bred. I can see the conditioning in the way you hold your shoulders. The brand under that leotard is a sun, yes?”

Pip’s hand flew to her flank, a useless, protective gesture. She said nothing.

“She was with the stuff when we… logged out,” Blaze said. “She got pulled through.”

“No,” Maud said. “She was placed. A potential reward. A behavioral anchor. A living, breathing leash.” She took a step closer to Pip, leaned down. Pip froze. “What’s your designation, little one? Companion submissive? Contractual familiar-in-training?”

Pip’s breath hitched. “I… I was in the tertiary cohort for Celestial Companionship. My mentor was Dame Shimmer Gleam. I was to be presented for consideration at the next gala.” The words were rote, a catechism. Her eyes were glassy, fixed on a point past Maud’s shoulder.

Zonk felt something hot and sour rise in his throat. Not anger, not yet. Something uglier, a shameful cousin of anger—the realization that he’d been thinking of her as a pet, a cute side effect. Not a person who’d been groomed for a specific, gilded cage.

Maud straightened. “So. You stole the Crown’s power supply and its intended new pet. And you want to return both, with a polite ‘sorry for the inconvenience,’ and skip back home.” She finally picked up the river stone from the sideboard. She turned it over in her hand, her thumb rubbing its smooth surface. “The return is theoretically possible. The Sun Vault has a reciprocal portal. It’s how the ‘game’ feeds data back and forth. But it’s guarded. Not by Sentinels. By the Contract itself. To open it requires a binding agreement. A signature.”

“We’ll sign a fucking permission slip,” Blaze said. “Let’s go.”

“It’s not a slip,” Maud said, her eyes still on the stone. “It’s a test. Of unity. Of intent. The Vault won’t accept the Elements back from thieves unless the thieves are in… Harmony.” She said the word like it tasted of spoiled milk. “A unified statement of purpose. A single voice. Proven.”

“Proven how?” Zonk asked. His mouth was dry.

Maud looked at him. Then at Blaze. Then at Pip. “You’re three distinct entities. Two humans, one pony. Two thieves, one asset. You have no contract between you. No formalized dynamic. The Vault will see that as discord. It will reject you. Or worse, it will try to… correct you. Forge a contract on the spot. One you might not survive.”

She placed the stone back on the sideboard with a soft, definitive click. “To pass as a unit, you need to present as one. A bonded trio. Even a crude, temporary facsimile might fool the door long enough to toss the loot inside and run.”

“A facsimile,” Zonk echoed.

“A performance,” Maud said. “But this world runs on performance made real. So you’ll need to feel it. Just a little. Enough to spark.” She walked to the curtain, pulled a braided silk cord hanging beside it. A small section of the wall beside the sideboard slid back with a whisper, revealing a shallow niche. Inside, on a velvet-lined tray were three simple items: a slim black collar, a matching bracelet, and a plain silver ring. “Symbols. Anchors. Put them on. Then you’re going to tell me, together, why you’re doing this. And you’re going to mean it. All three of you. One voice.”

Zonk stared at the objects. The collar looked soft, but the buckle was heavy steel. This was veering into territory his brain, even stoned had flow-charted as ‘Very Bad Ideas.’ “That’s it? We put on jewelry and give a group speech?”

“No,” Maud said. She picked up the collar. “You put on the jewelry. Then I ask questions. You answer. Truthfully. And if your answers lack conviction, or contradict, or taste like lies… the symbols have a mild feedback mechanism. Encouragement.” She held the collar out to Zonk. “You’re the apparent point of cohesion. You wear the primary.”

The plastic helmet was suddenly suffocating. Zonk pulled it off. His hair was plastered to his scalp, cold air biting the sweat on his forehead. He looked at Blaze. His friend’s face was pale under the grime and glitter, eyes wide. He gave a tiny, helpless shrug. Your call.

He looked at Pip. She was staring at the collar in Maud’s hand, her expression unreadable. Terror was that longing? Terror? He couldn’t tell.

The gym bag of world-breaking kink toys sat on the floor. The sounds of a hostile city were muffled beyond the velvet. The water-stain Florida loomed overhead. There was no good choice. There was only the next stupid, desperate move.

Zonk reached out and took the collar. The silk was cool. The metal of the buckle was colder.

“Okay,” he said. The word was a dry leaf, crumbling in his mouth. “Let’s get harmonious.”
