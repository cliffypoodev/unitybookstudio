The warehouse smelled of fermented apples and rust. It wasn’t a bad smell, Zonk decided, if you ignored the part where it meant they were hiding in a glorified, magical fruit cellar at the ass-end of a pastel dystopia. The light through the grimy, high windows was the color of weak tea. Dust motes swam in the beams, thick as plankton. He watched them, his back pressed against the cold iron curve of a massive, dormant cider press. His heart was doing a weird, syncopated rhythm against his ribs, like a drummer who’d lost the sheet music.

Across the aisle, wedged between two empty oak barrels, Pip sat with her knees drawn up. She hadn’t spoken since they’d stumbled in here, gasping, ten minutes ago. Or was it an hour? Time had that stretched, gummy quality he usually associated with a strong sativa. She just stared at the floor, at a dark, sticky patch where some long-ago batch had spilled and never been cleaned. Her fluffy blue tail was absolutely still.

Blaze was gone. Captured. The thought landed in his gut with a cold, physical thud.

He’d said they should double back, try something stupid. Luna had looked at him like he’d suggested juggling live grenades. “They have him in a Sky Wagon bound for the Spire. You would be a second gift for my sister. A matching set.” Her voice had been flat. “You have the Elements. The plan remains. You hide. I will… inquire.

So they’d run. Luna’s people had shoved them into a service cart that smelled of wet hay and panic, dumped them in this district with a hissed instruction: “The old Acres press. Wait.

Wait. Right.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: his mother’s kitchen, the year before she left. A bowl of apples on the counter, going soft. That same sweet, faintly alcoholic scent. He’d been nine. He’d poked one with a finger and the skin had given way with a sigh.

“They’re going to hurt him, aren’t they?”

Pip’s voice was small. It didn’t echo. The smell and the dust ate sound.

Zonk’s tongue felt too big for his mouth. “I don’t know. Maybe. Probably.” The lie—the comforting one—wouldn’t form. It felt like trying to shape smoke.

“Because of me. Because I’m with you.

“No.” The word came out too fast. “Because of the shiny crap we stole. Not you.

She lifted her head. Her eyes were huge in the dim light. The careful, hopeful gloss she usually wore was gone, scraped off by the last hour. A matched accessory.” She said it like she was reciting a product description. “That’s what the nice attendant was saying, before. Wasn’t she? That I was placed.

The air left his lungs. It just went, like he’d been punched.

Fluttershy’s gentle, probing voice in the green chamber. The heart grows attached to strays.

He’d shoved it aside, a weird moment in a string of weird moments. But Pip had been chewing on it. Of course she had. Her whole world was protocols and placements. She’d been trained to look for her position in the order of things.

The cold knot in his stomach tightened. Planted in the path of the heist. Luna’s twist, now blooming in Pip’s own mind. A high-value trainee. Cultivated naivete.

He almost believed it.

A sound cut through the fermented silence. Not from outside. From the sack.

A low, resonant hum. Like a tuning fork struck against a tooth.

The main warehouse door, twenty yards away, groaned.

Not a casual sound. The long, protesting shriek of hinges that hadn’t moved in years, forced.

Zonk’s hand froze. Pip went perfectly still, one hand flying to her mouth.

No voices. No shouted commands. Just the slow, inevitable grind of metal on metal.

The door groaned again, louder. A sliver of harsh, white daylight cut across the floor, illuminating a million dancing dust motes.

No time to argue.

They were pressed together in the dark, his chin on the top of her head, her breath hot against his collarbone. The sack of Elements was a lumpy, warm barrier between their chests. Laughter’s hum had quieted to a barely perceptible thrum.

Through the forest of machinery, he saw them.

Two figures. Tall. Armor that wasn’t cheap plastic like the street Guards. This was ornate, seamless, polished to a mirror shine that reflected the dusty beams in painful starbursts. Solar Sentinels. Their helmets were crested, stylized suns radiating from the brows. They moved with a silent, synchronized precision that made his skin prickle.

One stopped, head tilting. A sensor sweep. The other moved down the central aisle, hoof-falls—were those actual hooves?—clicking on the stone floor.

Pip trembled against him. A fine, constant vibration. He willed her to be still. Willed himself to be still. His lungs burned. He needed to breathe. He took a silent, shallow sip of air that tasted of iron and fear.

The Sentinel in the aisle paused. It was standing right by the sticky patch Pip had been staring at. It knelt, one articulated gauntlet touching the stain. It brought its fingers to the seam of its helmet. Testing the smell.

It’s old, Zonk screamed in his head. Years old. It doesn’t mean anything.

The Sentinel’s helmet fixed on it.

Trapped.

Not a shadow. A shape. Broad-shouldered, topped with the unmistakable, absurd silhouette of a horse’s head helmet, the eye-slits glowing with a soft, golden light. A Solar Sentinel. It stood framed in the doorway to the main floor, not moving, just… occupying the space.

Zonk’s mind went blank. Not the useful, focused kind of blank. The white-noise static kind. The kind that usually preceded him saying something incredibly stupid.

He grabbed her arm, hauling her to her feet. She stumbled, light as a bundle of sticks. Behind them, one of the functioning Sentinels barked a command, the words metallic and distorted. The spell of confusion was breaking. The sparking guard’s head gave another jerky twitch.

They had maybe five seconds.

The street offered nothing. High, smooth walls of candy-colored plaster. No doors. No alleys they hadn’t already been chased out of. The transport wagon sat twenty feet away, its crystal harnesses glittering with a malevolent cheer. A dead end with better lighting.

Just this awful, yawning vacuum where a plan should be, and the crushing certainty that he was about to watch her be hurt again.

Something hot and formless boiled up from his gut. It wasn’t bravery. It was the opposite—the sheer, stupid inability to stand there and do nothing while the world closed in. It was the same impulse that made him mouth off to teachers, the same one that made him pick a fight with a bigger kid in seventh grade just to feel the impact. A destructive, frantic need to connect, to prove he was still there, even if it was by setting himself on fire.

He pulled her to him.

It wasn’t graceful. He was off-balance, she was leaning away, and he nearly headbutted her. His hand found the side of her face, his thumb brushing the sticky warmth below her nose. Her skin was shockingly cool.

Then his mouth was on hers.

It was a collision. A mess of fear and salt and the coppery tang of her blood. Her lips were chapped, parted in surprise. She made a small, muffled sound against his mouth—not a protest, just a gasp of air. For a second, she was rigid, a statue of confusion. Then her free hand came up, fingers tangling in the front of his shirt, clutching the fabric like a lifeline. She kissed him back, a desperate, clumsy mirror of his own panic. It was all pressure and no rhythm, teeth and shaky breath and the frantic, wordless communication of two people who were about to lose everything.

The world narrowed to the points of contact: her mouth, the grip of her hand, the cool curve of her ear under his palm. The street, the guards, the hum of the broken Sentinel—it all faded into a distant buzz. This was it. This was the last coherent thing. Not a declaration. Not romance. It was a hand grenade of feeling tossed into the void, a way to scream we were here without making a sound. A final, stupid, human act of defiance against a world that wanted them to be quiet, compliant things.

It lasted three seconds. Maybe four.

A shadow fell over them. The smell of ozone returned, stronger, mixed with a scent like hot leather.

The kiss broke. Pip’s eyes flew open, looking past his shoulder. Her grip on his shirt tightened, then went slack.

A gauntleted hand closed around the back of Zonk’s neck. The grip was impersonal, cold, and utterly immovable. It yanked him backward, separating them with a brutal, effortless force. His feet scrabbled for purchase on the cobbles, finding none.

He was lifted, then slammed face-down onto the street. The impact drove the air from his lungs in a painful whoosh. Gravel bit into his cheek. A heavy knee planted itself in the small of his back, pinning him. He could turn his head just enough to see.

The sparking Sentinel was fully operational again. He had Pip by the upper arm, holding her a foot off the ground. Her legs dangled. She wasn’t struggling. She just looked at Zonk, her expression wiped clean, empty. A single tear traced a clean line through the dust on her cheek.

The other two guards stood over him. One leaned down, his helmet filling Zonk’s vision. The glowing eye-slits pulsed with a steady, righteous gold.

“Protocol violation,” the helmet intoned, the voice devoid of all inflection. “Resistance. Contamination of asset.

The knee in his back ground down. Zonk grunted, his vision sparking at the edges.

“You will be pacified.”

A different guard produced a slender crystal rod from his belt. It hummed to life with a sickly violet light. He pressed it against the side of Zonk’s neck.

The world dissolved into a silent, white-hot wire of pain. It didn’t spread. It just was, a single, perfect line of agony that erased every other thought, every other sense. His body arched against the knee, a rigid bow, then went limp. The cobblestones rushed up to meet his face again, but he didn’t feel the impact. A distant, ringing silence filled his head. He could see the guard’s polished gold greaves, could see a single, perfect dandelion growing from a crack in the stone, but he couldn’t connect the images to anything called a body.

Vaguely, as if from another room, he heard the clatter of hooves on stone, the creak of harnesses. Hands—or were they hooves?—gripped him under the arms. He was dragged, his boots scraping twin lines in the dirt. The world tilted, then went dark as he was hauled into the enclosed bed of the transport wagon.

The last thing he saw before the doors shut was Pip, being handed up into a separate, brighter compartment at the front. She didn’t look back. The door closed with a soft, final click.

Darkness. The smell of old hay and anxiety sweat. The wagon lurched into motion.

The pain in his neck subsided to a deep, throbbing ache, a lighthouse pulse in the fog. He lay where they’d dropped him, trying to remember how to breathe. His lips felt swollen. He could still taste her blood.

A laugh bubbled up in his chest, a raw, silent thing that hurt. He’d kissed her. In the middle of a hostile takeover, with monsters in gold armor, he’d stopped to kiss the girl. It was the most profoundly idiotic thing he had ever done. The punchline to a joke nobody had told.

The pink glow from the burlap sack across from him pulsed once, softly, in the dark.

Laughter, indeed.
