The back office of the Gilded Saddle smelled of old leather, cheap polish, and something sweetly chemical that made Zonk’s sinuses prickle. Brass Tacks, the unicorn bartender, leaned against a desk made from a repurposed cider barrel. He wasn’t polishing a glass. He was sharpening a hoof pick with a slow, grating scrape of stone on metal. The sound was the exact opposite of soothing.

“Directions,” Brass said, not looking up. His horn, a twisted brass spike caught the sickly yellow light from a glow-crystal chandelier made of bent spoons. “That’s the favor. Simple.”

“Nothing’s simple here,” Zonk said. His voice felt thin in the close, cluttered space. Shelves lined the walls, crammed with junk that looked like it fell off a toy truck and got cursed: a music box with a chipped ceramic pony figurine, a set of mismatched teacups, a bundle of rainbow-colored ribbons that seemed to twitch on their own. His eyes fixed on a crack in the plaster behind Brass’s head. It branched like a lightning bolt, or a river on a map leading nowhere.

“It is,” Brass insisted. The scraping stopped. He held the pick up, examined the point. “You take a box. You carry it three blocks. You give it to a mare named Cirrus at the entrance to the Cloud District. She gives you a token. You bring me the token. I give you a location. A safehouse. Where you might find… softer ears.”

Blaze shifted his weight, making the floorboards groan. “A box. That’s it?”

“That’s it.”

“What’s in the box?” Zonk asked.

Brass’s eyes, the color of tarnished pennies, finally lifted. “The less you muse on that, the longer your thoughts remain your own. The Cloud District is a pleasure dungeon. The box is a delivery. The contents are a transaction. You are the couriers. Not the auditors.”

Pip, standing slightly behind Zonk made a small, involuntary sound. A quick inhale caught. Her training, probably. Protocol for acknowledging dangerous information without contesting it.

“Why us?” Zonk said. “You’ve got runners. You’ve got… Pony-people.”

“You,” Brass said setting the pick down with a definitive click, “are nobodies. No clan marks. No contract scars. Your aesthetic is… an intriguing disaster.” He gestured a hoof at their stolen guard gear, which was too big for Blaze and hung off Zonk like a deflated parade float. “You look like you’re in costume, which is a violation, but you also look like you might be powerful eccentrics, which is a privilege. It makes you unpredictable. Unreliable, too. But for a one-time drop? Useful. If you get stopped, you’re confusing. Confusion buys seconds. Sometimes that’s enough.”

It wasn’t a compliment. It was an assessment of tactical garbage.

“And if we say no?” Blaze asked, crossing his arms.

Brass smiled. It didn’t touch his eyes. “Then you leave my establishment. And the two Solar Sentinels currently enjoying a surprisingly affordable ale at my bar will be informed that the three aesthetic deviants they’re hunting for Grand Theft Harmony just walked out my back door.” He tilted his head. “The posters are quite good. The artist captured your friend’s…” He waved a hoof at Zonk. “… perpetual state of bewildered dismay.”

A cold knot tightened under Zonk’s ribs. Not fear, not yet. The prelude to it. The chemical smell in the air was definitely getting stronger. It reminded him of the spray his dad used to clean the shower, a sharp, fake lemon scent that never quite covered the mildew underneath.

“We’ll do it,” Zonk said.

“Zonk—” Blaze started.

“We’re doing it.” The words came out flat. Final. He was so tired of running. Tired of the dumpster smell in his clothes, tired of Pip’s wide eyes, tired of the low-grade panic humming in his veins like a bad electrical current. A concrete task. A box. Three blocks. He could hold onto that.

Brass nodded once. He bent behind the desk and heaved a small chest onto the barrel-top. It was made of dark wood, banded with iron that looked stained, not rusted. There was no visible lock. Just a simple brass latch.

“Don’t open it,” Brass said.

“Wasn’t planning to,” Zonk muttered.

“The latch is spell-sealed. It reads intent. Curiosity feels like a key to it. Try, and your fingers will learn a new definition of ‘tingling.’ Possibly permanently.” Brass pushed it toward them. “Carry it together. It’s heavier than it looks.”

Blaze reached for it first. His biceps flexed. A vein stood out in his neck. “Holy shit,” he grunted, heaving it off the desk. It thumped against his chest. “What’s in here, a cannonball?”

“Or several,” Brass said, his tone implying that was a distinct possibility. “The Cloud District. The main entrance is a pink pavilion with silver streamers. Can’t miss it. Ask for Cirrus. She’ll be expecting a box. Don’t chat. Don’t admire the architecture. In and out.”

Zonk moved to help Blaze, getting a grip on the opposite side of the chest. The wood was unnaturally smooth, cold. The weight was deep, a dense, sinking pull that seemed to want to drag them through the floor. It felt wrong. Not just heavy. Inert in a way nothing else in this vibrating, pastel hellscape did. Like a hole in the world.

As they maneuvered it toward the door, Pip scurried ahead to open it.

Brass’s voice stopped them at the threshold. “One more thing.”

Zonk looked back, his shoulders already aching.

The unicorn’s gaze was on Pip. “The little one. She should carry something. A distraction. Here.” He tossed a small felted bag from the shelf. It landed at Pip’s hooves with a soft thud. “Sparkly hair baubles. Trendy with the court submissives this season. If you’re stopped, she’s making a delivery to the District’s dressing rooms. You two are her escorts. Plausible deniability with a side of triviality.”

Pip bent and picked up the bag with her mouth, holding it delicately. She looked like a very confused, very pastel retriever.

“Thanks,” Blaze said, the word strained by the weight.

“Don’t thank me,” Brass said, picking up his hoof pick again. The scraping resumed. “Just don’t die on my doorstep. It’s terrible for business.”

The door swung shut behind them cutting off the sound and the chemical lemon stink. The hallway was narrow, dark, and smelled of spilled beer and wet straw. The chest between them was an anchor.

“Three blocks,” Zonk said softly.

“Yeah,” Blaze grunted. “Which way is fucking north?”

Pip adjusted the bag in her mouth, then pointed a hoof down the hall toward a faint rectangle of garish pink and purple light. The distant thump of music, a saccharine synth-pop beat, drifted toward them. It sounded like happiness being processed through a woodchipper.

Great. The party was that way. And they were the uninvited guests, bringing the world’s heaviest plus-one.

The Cloud District’s main entrance was exactly as advertised: a pink pavilion with silver streamers. It was also, Zonk thought, the single most aggressive display of whimsy he had ever seen. The streamers weren’t just hanging; they fluttered in a non-existent breeze, propelled by tiny hidden enchantments that gave off a high-pitched whine just at the edge of hearing. The pink wasn’t a color so much as an assault. It was the pink of bubblegum and poisoned candy and a very specific shade of Pepto-Bismol his grandmother had sworn by. It made his teeth ache.

A unicorn stallion in a white waistcoat stood by the arched opening. His coat was a bland beige, his mane slicked back with something that smelled like coconuts and regret. He held a crystal clipboard.

“Membership?” he asked, not looking up.

Blaze shifted the chest between them. The wood groaned. “We’re, uh, expected. Delivery for Cirrus.”

The stallion’s eyes, a watery blue, flicked over them. He took in their ill-fitting guard barding, the sweat beading on Blaze’s temple, the way Pip was trying to make herself smaller behind Zonk’s shoulder. His nostrils flared. “Deliveries use the service entrance. Around the back. Past the midden heap.”

“Brass Tacks said main entrance,” Zonk said. His throat was dry. The chemical lemon smell from the office was still in his sinuses, layered now with the pavilion’s cloying floral perfume. He focused on a chip in the stallion’s left hoof polish. A perfect little crescent moon of beige against the white. “Said to ask for Cirrus. Said she’d be expecting a box.”

“Cirrus is in auditions.” The stallion made a note on his clipboard. The crystal pen made a sound like ice cracking. “You are not on the audition roster. You are, in fact, violating three separate aesthetic bylaws just by standing there. The barding is a historical inaccuracy—Solar Sentinels haven’t used that cuirass design in a decade. The fit is an abomination. And your…” He waved the pen at their faces. “… lack of appropriate equine prosthetics is frankly disturbing. Service entrance. Now.”

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was the same feeling he got when a cop asked him for ID he knew was in his pocket but couldn’t find. A bureaucratic freefall.

“Protocol 12-B,” Pip’s voice piped up, small but clear.

Everyone looked at her. She flinched under the attention but didn’t look down. Her ears were flat against her skull.

“What?” the stallion said.

“The bylaws. Section Twelve. ‘Admittance of Non-Conformant Entities for the Purpose of Contract Fulfillment.’ Subsection B.” Pip’s words came out in a rush, like she was reciting something drilled into her. “If a bonded third party—that’s Brass Tacks, he’s got a vendor bond with the District, I saw the seal in his office—sends a delivery with specific ingress instructions, and the recipient is expecting said delivery, aesthetic violations are temporarily waived pending completion of the transaction. Provided the entities present no immediate threat and the delivery is not a biohazard.” She took a quick breath. “We’re not a biohazard. Probably.”

The stallion stared. So did Blaze. Zonk felt a weird, inappropriate spark of pride, immediately drowned by the awareness that they were now arguing pony law with a glorified bouncer.

“You’re citing administrative code,” the stallion said, his voice flat.

“I was trained in contract facilitation,” Pip said. “Before.”

The stallion’s eyes narrowed. He looked at his clipboard. He looked back at Pip. Something in her earnest, terrified face must have checked out, because he let out a long, suffering sigh that smelled of mint and contempt. “Fine. You get a provisional pass. But you stick to the Lavender Run. Straight to the audition chamber. You do not linger. You do not gawk. You make your delivery and you exit via the service corridor. Deviate, and the aesthetic waiver is revoked. Understood?”

“Yes,” Zonk said.

“Crystal,” Blaze added.

The stallion stepped aside. The silver streamers parted without being touched.

Inside, the Lavender Run was a hallway that felt like being swallowed by a very expensive, very anxious throat. The walls were a deep purple velvet, the carpet a plush lavender that swallowed the sound of their steps. Glow-crystals shaped like lilies floated at intervals, casting a light that was somehow both dim and painfully bright.

Zonk’s eyes fixed on a painting. It was a pastoral scene—ponies in a meadow—but the brushstrokes were weirdly violent, slashes of paint that made the smiling faces look like they were screaming. He looked away. The carpet had a pattern woven into it, a repeating motif of intertwined locks and keys. His brain, unhelpfully started trying to count them.

They passed open archways. To the left, a room full of silken swings and ponies in diaphanous robes being fanned by others with giant feathers. To the right, a chamber where a unicorn was slowly carefully painting elaborate designs onto a bound earth pony’s back with a glowing brush. The earth pony was weeping silent, happy tears. Zonk sped up.

“You knew the rules,” Blaze muttered to Pip as they lumbered along, the chest a lead weight between them.

“I know a lot of rules,” she said, her voice barely audible. “It is mostly what I know. It does not usually help.”

“It helped just now.”

“It helped just now,” she repeated, as if testing the truth of it.

The Lavender Run ended at a pair of double doors upholstered in the same purple velvet, with silver hoof-plates for handles. Music leaked out around the edges—Something with too many glockenspiels.

Zonk pushed one door open.

The audition chamber was a circular room with a sunken floor. Tiered seating rose around it, empty except for a few scattered clusters of ponies who looked bored and rich. The central space was lit by a single, harsh spotlight. In it, a pegasus mare with a sky-blue coat was contorted into a shape that defied spinal integrity, one hoof touching the back of her own head. She trembled with the effort. A thin unicorn in a severe black dress watched from the shadows, a crystal clipboard identical to the bouncer’s floating beside her.

“Cirrus?” Zonk called out. He spoke, the sound in the vast, quiet room.

The unicorn in black didn’t turn. “Quiet. She’s finding her center.”

The pegasus in the spotlight whimpered.

“We have a delivery from Brass Tacks,” Blaze tried, hefting the chest slightly. The grunt of effort was loud.

That got her attention. The unicorn—Cirrus, presumably—turned her head. Her face was all sharp angles and cold, violet eyes. Her mane was pulled into a tight, silver bun. She looked like a math teacher who’d discovered dungeon management. “You’re early.”

“Traffic was light,” Zonk said.

Cirrus ignored him, her eyes on the chest. She floated her clipboard over and made a note. “Set it there.” She pointed a hoof to a small, bare pedestal near the wall. “You’ll need to hold it until the binding recognizes me. Don’t let it touch the stone.”

Blaze and Zonk shuffled the chest over. The pedestal was white marble, veined with grey. As they got closer, the brass latch on the chest began to glow with a faint, sullen orange light.

“Recognition protocol,” Cirrus said, gliding over. She placed her hoof directly on the latch. The orange light flared, then shifted to a calm, steady blue. A soft click echoed in the chamber. “There. You may release it.”

They let go. The chest settled on the pedestal without a sound.

“Your token,” Cirrus said. She didn’t reach for anything. Just stared at them.

Zonk’s stomach did a slow, unpleasant roll. “Brass said you’d give us a token. For him.”

“I did.” A faint, cruel smile touched her lips. “The token is your safe passage out of my district. The alternative is me calling security to discuss your blatant violation of aesthetic codes. Which would you prefer?”

The pegasus in the spotlight collapsed with a soft thud. No one looked over.

“That wasn’t the deal,” Blaze said, his voice low.

“The deal was between you and Brass. My deal with him is the box. You are the couriers. Your payment is not getting arrested.” Cirrus’s smile widened. “It’s a very valuable payment. I suggest you take it and go. The service corridor is through that arch. Don’t dawdle.”

The hot thing in Zonk’s chest solidified into a hard, cold lump. He’d been played. Of course they’d been played. They were nobodies. Tactical garbage. He looked at Pip. Her ears were drooping, her eyes fixed on the pattern of the carpet—tiny, repeating horseshoes.

He wanted to argue. To pick up the damn chest and walk out with it. But the latch was blue now. Bound to her. And the bouncer was outside. And the posters with their faces were probably in a tube on his desk.

“Fine,” Zonk spat the word out. It tasted bitter.

“Wise,” Cirrus said. She was already turning back to the spotlight, where the pegasus was struggling to stand. “The arch. Now.”

They went.

The service corridor was concrete. Grey, cold, smelling of damp and industrial cleaner. It was a relief after the lavender throat. A single, flickering glow-strip ran along the ceiling. Their footsteps echoed.

No one spoke for a long time. The only sound was the hum of the light and the distant, muffled thump of music from somewhere deep in the District.

Blaze finally broke the silence. “We just got scammed.”

“We didn’t get eaten by guards,” Zonk said. He spoke, quieter now, hollow in the concrete tunnel. “Call it a win.”

“It’s not a win. We did the job and we got nothing. Brass isn’t gonna give us a safehouse location out of the goodness of his heart.”

“He might,” Pip said quietly.

They both looked at her. She was staring straight ahead, walking with that careful, precise step she used when she was thinking. “He said the token was for the location. He did not say the token was a physical object. Cirrus said our token was safe passage. He will ask if we delivered the box. We will say yes. He will ask if we got a token. We can say yes. It is… technically true.”

Zonk blinked. A loophole. A stupid, semantic, desperate loophole. It was exactly the kind of thing this world ran on. His brain, tired of counting carpet keys, latched onto it. “She said the token was our safe passage. That’s a statement. That’s a thing we got.”

“He might ask what the token was,” Blaze said.

“Then we tell him. We say ‘safe passage.’ It’s a token of safe passage. It’s a… metaphysical token.” Zonk ran a hand over his face. He could feel the grit from the dumpster alley still under his nails. “God, I sound like I’m arguing about D&D rule semantics.”

“It might work,” Pip insisted. “He is a business pony. He values completion. We completed the task. The terms were ambiguous. He might… Respect the hustle.”

Blaze let out a short, sharp laugh. “Respect the hustle. In Ponyland.”

They reached a heavy metal door at the end of the corridor. A sign on it read, in cheerful cursive, “Egress! Thank you for your discretion!” Zonk pushed it open.

They spilled out into a narrow, filthy alley behind the Cloud District. The midden heap Brass’s bouncer had mentioned was a reeking mound of glittering refuse: broken crystal glasses, wilted flower arrangements, shreds of expensive fabric. The pink pavilion was a garish blot against the grey sky above the rooftops.

Zonk leaned against the damp brick wall, the cold seeping through his stolen barding. The chemical lemon smell was finally gone, replaced by garbage and his own sweat. He watched a single, iridescent streamer from the pavilion drift down and land on the midden heap. It settled on a moldy cupcake, its silver catching the dull light for a second before going dark.

They had nothing. They had less than nothing. They had a technicality.

Pip was watching him, waiting for his next move. Blaze was cracking his knuckles, staring back at the door as if he might kick it in.

The weight of the chest was gone from his arms, but a new weight had settled in its place. Heavier. Familiar. This was the game now. Not a VR simulation. A brutal, petty, legalistic scramble where the only currency was loopholes and bluff.

“Okay,” Zonk said, pushing off the wall. The word felt like gravel in his mouth. “Let’s go lie to a bartender.”

The corridor to the private suite was a throat. Pink velvet walls swallowed sound and light, the air thick with the scent of overripe peaches and ozone. Their footsteps on the deep carpet made no sound. Zonk’s shoulder ached from the chest’s impossible weight. It wasn’t just heavy. It felt reluctant, like it was fighting them every step.

A door at the end stood ajar. Faint, amber light spilled out cut by the sharp silhouette of a pony leaning against the frame.

“Cirrus?” Blaze grunted, shifting his grip. The chest thumped against his thigh.

The silhouette moved. A pegasus mare stepped into the light. Her coat was the color of a winter sky just before snow, her mane a messy cascade of silver and slate. She wore a harness of soft, grey leather, utilitarian, not decorative. Her eyes were the flat grey of a spent bullet casing.

“The box.” The way she said it was a low rasp, like she’d been shouting.

“From Brass,” Zonk said.

She nodded once, jerking her head toward the room behind her. “In here. Set it on the table. Quietly.”

The suite was a study in muffled opulence. The four-poster bed dominated the space, its posts carved into spirals of clouds and tiny, smiling ponies. The whole room was sound-dampened. The air felt dead. In the far corner, a unicorn stallion in an elaborate, embroidered vest stood with his back to them, horn glowing a soft, persistent gold. He was focused on a pony kneeling at his hooves—an earth mare with a butter-yellow coat, her head bowed, a delicate silver chain running from a collar to the stallion’s fetlock.

The tension in the room wasn’t the hot, sharp kind. It was cold. Congealed. The stallion’s voice was a clipped, precise monotone.

“Again. The third clause of the Sunset Aria. The inflection for subservience, not supplication. You are a vassal, not a beggar.”

The kneeling mare trembled. Her voice, when it came, was a threadbare whisper.

“Wrong.” The stallion didn’t raise his voice. The chain gave a tiny, vicious jerk. The mare flinched, a full-body spasm. “Your cadence is pathetic. You sound like a foal. Again.”

Zonk’s skin prickled. This wasn’t play. This wasn’t kink. This was something else, something mean and small and desperately sad. He dragged his eyes away, fixing on a painting on the wall—a pastoral scene of ponies picnicking. One of them had a smear of blue paint across its flank where the artist had messed up. He focused on that smear. It looked like a clumsy bird.

Cirrus pointed a wing toward a low, polished table near the door. “There.”

They shuffled over, the chest a leaden secret between them. As they bent to lower it, the wood touched the table’s surface with a soft thud that seemed deafening in the quiet.

The stallion’s horn-glow flickered. He half-turned, a look of significant irritation etching his narrow features. “Cirrus. I contracted for privacy.”

“Couriers,” Cirrus rasped, not looking at him. She was studying the chest’s latch, her wings tense. “They’re leaving.”

The stallion’s gaze swept over them—Their ill-fitting guard gear, their human awkwardness. His nostrils flared. “Aesthetic deviants. Delivering contraband to a pleasure attendant. How… pragmatic.” He turned back to the mare on the floor. “You see? Even the gutter has a function. Now. The clause. From the beginning.”

The yellow mare took a shuddering breath. She began again, the words a rote, hollow chant.

Blaze straightened up, rolling his shoulder. He caught Zonk’s eye gave a tiny shake of his head. Let’s go.

Cirrus produced a small, iron token from her harness and held it out. “For Brass. Now get out.”

Zonk reached for it. His fingers were an inch from the metal when the kneeling mare’s voice broke.

“I can’t.” It was a sob, ripped out of her. She crumpled forward, forelegs over her head. “Please. I can’t remember it. I’m sorry, I’m so sorry, I just can’t.”

The silence that followed was absolute. The dead air absorbed the sob, leaving only the visual echo of her collapse.

The stallion stared down at her. His horn’s glow didn’t waver. His expression didn’t change. It was the placid look of a man discovering a bug on his dinner plate. “A breach,” he said, conversationally. “Section eight, paragraph two of your indenture. Willful non-compliance during sanctioned refinement.” He gave the chain another little tug. “You understand the penalty adjustment.”

The mare just wept, soft, hopeless hitches of breath.

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite anger, not quite the old, familiar urge to disengage and light up. It was a sour, curdling revulsion. It tasted like the fake lemon spray in the back office. His dad used that spray. He’d use it on the mold in the corner of the shower, scrubbing with a kind of grim, silent fury that never fixed the leak, just made the bathroom smell like a chemical lie for a week.

The stallion’s horn brightened, shifting from gold to a hard, magnesium white. He raised it, aiming the tip at the mare’s exposed flank.

“A reminder,” he murmured.

“Hey.”

The word was out before Zonk knew he’d spoken. It hung in the dead air, stupid and flat.

The stallion froze. He turned his head, slow. “Excuse me?”

“You heard him,” Blaze said. He took a half-step forward, his body a solid, broad barrier between the stallion and the couriers. His knuckles were white where he’d clenched his fists. “She’s done. Pack it in.”

Cirrus made a sharp, hissing sound. “Don’t. He’s Day Court. A scribe. You have your token. Leave.”

The stallion—the scribe—smiled. It was a thin, paper-cut of a smile. “The deviants have opinions. How novel.” His eyes, a watery blue, fixed on Zonk. “This is a legally binding contractual correction. Your interruption is a violation of Solar Accord 12-B. Which carries its own penalties.”

Zonk’s mouth was dry. The prelude to fear was over. This was the real thing, a cold wire drawn tight around his lungs. He saw the scribe’s horn, that focused, white light. He saw the weeping mare. He saw the stupid, smiling ponies carved into the bedpost. The sheer, petty cruelty of it all clicked into place, a gear finding its groove. It wasn’t grand evil. It was this. This miserable little power trip in a soundproofed room.

He heard his own voice, distant and strangely calm. “Legally binding. Right.” He nodded toward the mare. “She consented to ‘penalty adjustments’?”

“Her consent is embedded in her signature on the indenture. She consented to the structure. The structure dictates the correction.”

“Yeah. See, I’m not from around here.” Zonk took a step forward, now shoulder-to-shoulder with Blaze. The action felt alien, like watching someone else move his limbs. “Where I’m from, if someone’s crying on the floor and saying they can’t, you stop. That’s the structure. The structure is: you stop.”

The scribe’s smile vanished. His horn flared. “You are ignorant. And you are in breach.”

The white light lanced out—not at them, but at the chest on the table.

The spell-sealed latch glowed red-hot for a split second. Then it blew apart with a sound like a gunshot in a pillow.

The lid flew open.

No cannonballs. No contraband. The chest was filled with a swirling, luminescent grey smoke that smelled of thunderstorms and burnt sugar. It billowed out, fast, coalescing in the center of the room into a gleaming, amorphous shape that hummed at a frequency that made Zonk’s fillings ache.

The scribe’s eyes went wide with pure, undiluted horror. “A memory wraith. You brought a wraith into a secured chamber?”

Cirrus backed toward the door, her grey face ashen. “The deal was for the sealed box. You broke the seal.”

“You provoked a breach!” the scribe shrieked, his monotone shattered.

The wraith pulsed. The room’s temperature dropped twenty degrees. Frost spiderwebbed across the pastoral painting. The thing had no face, but Zonk felt its attention sweep the room, a physical pressure against his skin.

It floated toward the scribe.

“No! No, I am a servant of the Sun! My memories are protected!” The scribe stumbled back, his horn sparking erratically. He fumbled at his vest, pulling a small scroll sealed with a sun-shaped wax blob. He thrust it toward the wraith like a crucifix.

The wraith hesitated. Its humming pitch changed, questioning.

Then it slowly turned.

It drifted toward the yellow mare, still huddled and weeping on the floor.

Her head lifted. She saw the swirling grey mass hovering toward her. She made a small, choked sound of terror, scrambling backward until her shoulders hit the wall.

The wraith extended a tendril of smoke toward her forehead.

“Hey!” Blaze roared. He didn’t think. He moved. He snatched the heavy, now-empty wooden chest from the table and, with a grunt of effort, hurled it sideways like a discus.

It spun through the wraith’s form. The smoke parted, then re-knit, unharmed. But the distraction worked. The wraith retracted its tendril, humming in agitation.

The scribe saw his chance. He bolted for the door, shoving past Cirrus, his scroll clutched to his chest. He was gone, the velvet door swinging shut behind him.

Cirrus stared at the wraith, at the sobbing mare, at them. Her professional detachment had cracked, revealing something raw and furious underneath. “You idiots. You glorious, catastrophic idiots.” She didn’t run. She lifted a wing, pointing at the wraith. “It’s a psychic leech. It was supposed to go to a client who wanted a painful memory removed. It’s confused. It’s just trying to feed.”

The wraith, agitated by the movement, drifted toward Pip.

Pip, who had been silent and still by the door, let out a tiny gasp. She didn’t run. She stood there, her eyes wide, her body rigid with a fear that looked like recognition. “It is… hollow,” she murmured.

The wraith loomed over her.

Zonk’s body moved before the cold wire around his lungs could tighten any further. He didn’t charge. He stepped in front of Pip, putting himself between her and the smoke. He had no plan. No weapon. Nothing.

He just spread his hands, a useless, human gesture. “No,” he said. The word wasn’t a command. It was a fact. A statement of a new, unnegotiable boundary.

The wraith paused.

It hummed. The sound was different now. Less hungry. Curious.

From the bedpost, one of the carved, smiling pony heads chose that moment to break free with a dry crack. It hit the carpet with a soft thump. The sound was absurdly loud.

The wraith’s form shivered. It seemed to look at Zonk, at the broken carving, at the weeping mare in the corner. Its swirling slowed. The grey smoke softened, fading from the color of a storm cloud to the color of morning fog.

With a sound like a sigh, it dissolved. The smoky essence poured back into the open chest settling into an inert, pearlescent pool. The lid lay on the floor beside it, the latch a melted ruin.

The room was silent. The cold began to recede.

Cirrus let out a long, shaky breath. She looked from the chest to Zonk, her bullet-casing eyes unreadable. “It lost cohesion. Conflicting stimuli. Too many targets, no clear… pain.” She shook her head, a quick, sharp motion. “You should be a puddle of forgotten birthdays right now.”

Blaze helped the yellow mare to her hooves. She leaned against him, trembling, her tears slowing to hiccups. She couldn’t look at any of them.

Pip touched Zonk’s arm. Her hoof was light, a whisper of pressure. “You stood,” she said quietly. “Protocol does not cover wraiths. There was no rule.”

Zonk looked down at his hands. They were steady. He felt empty. Clean. The revulsion was gone, burned away. What was left was a simple, crystalline certainty.

He turned to Cirrus. “The safehouse. The directions.”

Cirrus studied him for another long moment. Then she nodded. She plucked the iron token from where it had fallen on the carpet and tossed it to him. “For Brass. Tell him the delivery was… eventful.” She walked to a writing desk, scrawled something on a slip of parchment with a quill clutched in her wingtips, and handed it to Zonk. “The Silken Glove. Down by the wharves. Ask for Plush. She’s Old Herd. She’ll listen.” Her gaze flicked to the yellow mare. “I’ll handle the scribe’s breach complaint. He ran. That’s a forfeit.” She said it like she was stating the weather.

They left her in the quiet, rich room with the broken carving and the pool of dormant wraith. The peach-and-ozone air of the corridor felt light, almost breathable.

Outside the Cloud District, under the garish pink pavilion, Blaze let out a low whistle. “A memory-eating smoke monster. Of course. Why not.” He punched Zonk’s shoulder, gently. “You’re a crazy person.”

Zonk looked at the parchment in his hand. The address was written in a looping, elegant script. He thought of the scribe’s terrified face, the precise cruelty evaporating in an instant. He thought of the wraith, dissolving in the face of something it couldn’t categorize.

“Yeah,” he said. The word felt right. It felt true. He didn’t feel like a crazy person. He felt, for the first time since the headset came off, like he was exactly where he was supposed to be. It was a terrifying feeling. It was a relief.

He tucked the parchment into his stolen guard tunic, next to the cold, hard weight of the Element of Magic’s tiara. Two directions. One to a safehouse. One to a crown. Both led deeper in.

The alley behind the Cloud District smelled of wet trash and the ghost of old perfume. The pink pavilion’s back door had been a simple push-bar exit, but the sudden plunge into the greasy twilight of the city felt like falling out of a dream and into a cold puddle. Zonk’s hands were still buzzing, not from any spell, just from the leftover adrenaline of walking through a dungeon with a box that probably contained someone’s severed head.

Cirrus, a pegasus with feathers the color of a storm cloud had taken the chest without a word. She’d dropped a single, cold metal token into his palm—a disc stamped with a crescent moon—and pointed a wing toward the door. The transaction had taken less than ten seconds. It left him feeling hollow, like he’d just passed a kidney stone he didn’t know he had.

Blaze leaned against a brick wall streaked with something iridescent. “Well. That was… efficient.”

“It was something,” Zonk said. He rolled the token between his fingers. It was smoother than it should be, worn down by countless other nervous hands. He thought of all the other idiots who’d done favors for Brass Tacks. Where were they now? Probably in a box of their own.

Pip stood a few feet away, her nose wrinkled. She was staring at a puddle that reflected the neon sign of a club across the way. The sign flickered, spelling out “The Prancing Pony” in a garish, seizure-inducing magenta. “The water,” she said softly. “It smells of sugar and… disappointment.”

That was it exactly. Zonk filed the observation away. Her training might have made her fragile, but it had also sharpened her senses to a razor’s edge. She noticed things. He just noticed the problems.

“Let’s get this over with,” he muttered, pushing off the wall. The directions to the safehouse were back at the Gilded Saddle. Three blocks. They felt longer on the return trip, every shadow in a doorway a potential guard, every clopping hoofbeat on the cobblestones a threat. He kept seeing the chest in his mind’s eye. The stained iron bands. The way the weight had seemed to pulse.

They cut through a narrower alley to avoid a pair of laughing unicorns in matching sequined vests. The wall here was plastered with posters. Playbills for underground fight clubs, advertisements for “Authentic Pony-Play Experience Sessions,” missing pet notices for creatures called “Parasprites.” His gaze slid over them, a blur of color and desperation.

Then he stopped.

There, wheat-pasted crookedly next to a peeling ad for hoof polish was a drawing of a face. It was crude done in thick black ink. The eyes were two circles of bewildered dismay, the mouth a slack line of confusion. The hair was a scribbled nest. Underneath, in blocky, official-looking script: “WANTED FOR GRAND THEFT HARMONY. AESTHETIC DEVIANTS. CONSIDER ARMED AND UNPREDICTABLE.”

It was him.

Not a good likeness. But it was clear. The artist had captured the permanent state of low-grade shock that lived on his face. He looked like a man who’d just been told the sky was actually green.

“Oh, you have got to be kidding me,” Blaze said from beside him. He was staring at the poster next to Zonk’s. This one showed a face with a square jaw and wide, earnest eyes, rendered with slightly more sympathy. “Armed and unpredictable? Dude, I’m the predictable one. I try to punch things. That’s my whole thing.”

Pip made a small, choked sound. She was looking at a third poster. This one was smaller, tucked in the corner. The drawing was simpler, almost gentle. A soft face, big eyes, a stylized curl of mane. No mention of being armed. The text just read: “ACCOMPLICE. PROPERTY OF THE SUN COURT. RETURN FOR REWARD.”

“Property,” she said quietly. The word didn’t sound like a word coming from her. It sounded like a bone breaking.

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It sat behind his sternum like a burning stone. They’d reduced her to a line item. A missing piece of inventory. And they’d made him look like a constipated owl.

“Okay,” Blaze said, his voice dropping low. He reached up and tore his poster down in one rough motion. The paper ripped, leaving a jagged white scar on the wall. “We’re leaving. Now.”

“Wait.” Zonk’s hand shot out, stopping Blaze from reaching for Pip’s poster. “Leave it.”

“What? Why?”

“If we tear hers down, it looks like we were here. Like we care about that one specifically.” His mind was moving fast, a frantic, skittering thing. “We leave it. We take ours. It’s arrogant. It’s what unpredictable aesthetic deviants would do. They’d care about their own faces, not their… not their friend.”

He couldn’t look at Pip. He heard her breath catch, then even out. A protocol breath. In, hold, release.

Blaze stared at him for a second, then gave a sharp nod. He tore down Zonk’s poster, crumpling both into a tight ball. He shoved them into a pocket of his stolen guard tunic. The fabric strained. “Let’s go.”

The walk back to the Gilded Saddle was a silent, sweaty parade. Every glance felt like a spotlight. Zonk noticed a chip in the tooth of a passing earth pony merchant. He fixated on it, the tiny, dark triangle against the yellow enamel. It was the only thing that kept him from scanning the crowd for guards.

Brass Tacks was behind the bar again, polishing a glass that already gleamed. The two Solar Sentinels were gone. The bar was empty save for a lone, snoring pegasus in the corner, his head cradled in his wings.

Without a word, Zonk slid the crescent token across the polished wood. It made a faint scraping sound, like a needle on a record.

Brass looked at it. He didn’t pick it up. “Cirrus took the box?”

“Yes.”

“She say anything?”

“No.”

A slow smile spread across the unicorn’s face. This one almost reached his eyes. “Good. Means it wasn’t a trap for you. Just business.” He put the glass down and pulled a thin slip of parchment from under the bar. He placed it next to the token. “The Shivering Mare. It’s a boarding house in the lower Spire District. Ask for Mistress Moondrop. Show her the token. Don’t mention my name.”

Zonk took the parchment. The address was written in a spidery, elegant script. “Mistress Moondrop.”

“She’s… sympathetic to certain points of view. She’ll give you a room. No questions. For a price.”

“Of course,” Zonk said. The prelude to fear in his gut had solidified into the real thing, a cold, hard lump. “What price?”

“The same price everyone pays for safety here.” Brass picked up the token, finally, and tucked it away. “Information. She’ll want to know what you’ve seen. Who’s hunting you. What you stole. She’s Night Court. Not an agent, just a… listener. A collector. You tell her stories. She gives you a roof. Fair trade.”

Telling stories to the Night Court. It sounded like the beginning of every bad spy novel he’d ever skimmed. But the posters were a ball of crumpled heat in Blaze’s pocket. Pip was a small, quiet shadow at his elbow, her eyes on the floor.

They had no other doors to knock on.

“Thank you,” Zonk said, the words ash in his mouth.

“Don’t,” Brass said, turning back to his glasses. “Favors are transactions. This one is concluded. Now get out of my bar before the next shift of Sentinels comes in for their after-work disappointment.”

The street outside was darker, the glow-crystals in the lampposts beginning to shimmer to life with a soft, blue-white radiance. It was a pretty effect. It made the garish colors of the storefronts look almost magical. Zonk hated it.

He looked down at the parchment in his hand. The Shivering Mare. It was a name. A destination. It wasn’t safety, but it was the next step. For now, that was all he could hold onto.

Blaze clapped a heavy hand on his shoulder. “Lead the way, oh fearless planner of alleyway poster strategy.”

Pip’s hoof brushed his other arm, a tentative touch. She was looking up at him, her eyes reflecting the false starlight of the lamps. She didn’t say anything. She didn’t need to.

The three of them turned and melted into the deepening twilight, heading for the Spire District, a direction at last.
