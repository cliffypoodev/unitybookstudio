The air in the grotto tasted like wet pennies and the ghost of last autumn’s leaves. It wasn’t a smell Zonk knew how to process, so his brain kept circling back to it, discarding the more pressing issue of interdimensional collapse in favor of cataloging the scent. Moss grew in thick, spongy patches on the curved stone walls, glowing with a faint, internal light that had nothing to do with the weak dawn filtering through the canopy above. The light was the color of a bruise. It made everyone look sick.

“Original magic,” said the old mare. Her name was Sorrel, and she looked like someone had been carved from a gnarled root and forgotten to paint. She sat on a flat rock, her knees jutting out at sharp angles. “Is not a transaction. It is not a contract. It is not a lever for power.”

“Great,” Zonk muttered. His shoelace was still untied. The frayed end tickled his ankle. “So it’s, like, vibes.”

Sorrel’s ear twitched. It was a long, expressive ear, tufted with gray hair. “It is vibration. A harmony that arises from genuine connection. Joy freely given. Support without obligation.”

“Sounds like a cult,” Blaze said. He was leaning against the wall, arms crossed, but his posture was all wrong—too stiff, like he was trying to prove he wasn’t intimidated by a room full of geriatric pony-kin. A drop of water from the ceiling plinked onto his shoulder. He flinched.

“It is the opposite of a cult,” said another of the Old Herd, a stocky earth-kin with a beard that seemed to be made of actual lichen. The words came out a low rumble. “Cults demand conformity. This requires individuality. The unique note you bring to the chord.”

Pip was perched on a smaller rock near Sorrel, leaning forward. Her eyes were wide, absorbing every word. The cut on her cheek from the Sentinel’s backhand had been cleaned and sealed with a paste that smelled of honey and thyme. It left a shiny, dark patch on her skin. She looked like a kid who’d found the secret answer to everything.

Zonk’s stomach was a hard, cold knot. Not fear, exactly. More like the feeling you got when you realized you’d signed up for a group project with overachievers, and you were the one who hadn’t done the reading. He picked at the skin around his thumbnail. The raw spot from yesterday had scabbed over. He worried the edge of the scab.

“The Elements you carry,” Sorrel continued, her gaze moving between the three of them. “They are tools. Amplifiers. For centuries, the Court has used them to force a harmony—to make the song match their tune, by binding the singers. But their original function was to clarify. To help distinct voices find a beautiful alignment, without anyone losing their… key.”

“So we just, what, hold hands and think happy thoughts?” Blaze pushed off the wall, cracking his knuckles. The sound was too loud in the quiet space. “And that’ll, like, reboot the universe?”

“No.”

The voice came from the entrance. Luna stood there, a silhouette against the bruised glow. She had cleaned the blood from her temple. In this light, the regal severity of her features was softened, making her look merely exhausted. “The ritual at the Gala is a focusing lens. A ceremony observed by the entire power structure. If you perform it there, with the Elements attuned to this ‘original frequency,’ the effect will propagate through every linked contract in the Court. It will not destroy the magic. It will… unshackle it.”

“And that’s good?” Zonk asked.

“It is unpredictable.” Luna stepped into the grotto. The moss-light seemed to shy away from her. “It will break Celestia’s control. It will nullify the coercive bonds. What fills the void is the question. Chaos, perhaps. Or something new.”

“Or the whole damn wall comes down,” Blaze said. “You said a full collapse merges the dimensions. That’s the ‘true danger’ your spy crap found out. That’s not just us getting stuck here. That’s… what? Equestrian kink law taking over Cleveland?”

A beat of silence. The water plinked again.

“Essentially,” Luna said, her voice flat. “The barriers are maintained by the stable, forced harmony of the Court’s contracts. Break the control without providing a new stability, and the dimensional membranes… tear. The rules of this reality would bleed into yours. Permanently.”

The image bloomed in Zonk’s head, unbidden and stupidly specific: his local DMV, but the clerks all had tails and cutie marks, and you had to negotiate a service contract involving a flogger just to renew your license. A giggle bubbled up in his throat, hot and sharp and entirely inappropriate. He choked it back. It came out as a wet snort.

Everyone looked at him.

“Sorry,” he managed. “Just. The thought of the guy at the Taco Bell drive-thru making me safeword for a Cheesy Gordita Crunch. It got me.”

Blaze stared at him, then a slow grin spread across his face. “Dude. The Supreme Court.”

“Don’t,” Zonk said, the hysterical laughter threatening to spill over. “Just don’t.”

Pip looked between them, confused but smiling at their smiles. Sorrel watched them, her head tilted. The lichen-bearded kin let out a grunt that might have been amusement.

Luna did not smile. “The trivialization is a defense. I understand. But the stakes are not trivial.”

“We know,” Zonk said, the laughter dying as suddenly as it came. The cold knot was back. “We literally have front-row seats to the shitshow. So how do we… attune the things?”

“You must resonate with them,” Sorrel said, as if it were obvious. “Not as a wielder. As a participant. The magic you three share—it is unformed, chaotic, but it is genuine. It is not a contract. It is a choice. You keep choosing each other.”

Zonk looked at Blaze. His friend’s grin was gone, replaced by a familiar, stubborn set of his jaw. He looked at Pip. She gave him a small, earnest nod, as if to say, you can do this.

He felt profoundly unqualified.

“Okay,” he sighed. “How do we start?”

The practice was absurd.

Sorrel had them sit in a triangle on the damp floor, the six Elements placed in the center. The Loyalty cuffs, the Laughter gag ball, the Kindness salve, the Generosity flogger, Honesty’s tongue depressor, Magic’s tiara. They looked like junk from a very specific garage sale.

“Close your eyes,” Sorrel instructed. “Steady. Do not try to do anything. Simply… be present. Notice the space between you.”

Zonk closed his eyes. He heard Blaze shift, his jeans scraping on stone. He heard Pip’s soft, steady stillness between them. He noticed the space between them. It was filled with the smell of wet pennies. And his own racing thoughts. And the persistent, itchy awareness of his untied shoe.

This was useless.

He peeked. Blaze’s brow was furrowed in intense concentration, like he was trying to lift a car with his mind. Pip’s expression was serene, trusting. Zonk shut his eyes again.

His mind wandered. He thought about the bakery, the taste of the weird cocktail. He thought about the aglet on his shoelace. It had been blue. He’d bought those laces because they were the cheapest at the convenience store. He wondered if the aglet was lying in the alley, a tiny piece of his world slowly getting covered in other-dimensional grime.

A warmth brushed against his awareness. Not a physical heat. A… presence. It felt like the hum of a transformer on a quiet street felt more in your teeth than your ears. It was coming from the center of the circle.

He opened his eyes.

A soft, buttery light was emanating from the tiara. Not the harsh, glittering shine it had in Celestia’s vault. This was gentle. Warm. As he watched, the red of the Loyalty cuffs deepened, like a heart’s-blood ruby. The pink of the gag ball softened to the color of cotton candy at dusk.

“You’re doing it,” Pip said quietly, her eyes wide with wonder.

“I’m not doing anything,” Zonk said.

“That,” Sorrel said, a note of satisfaction in her dry voice, “is the point.”

Blaze opened his eyes. “Whoa.” He looked at the glowing objects, then at Zonk. A slow, real smile spread across his face. “Dude. You’re a natural.”

“I was thinking about my shoelace,” Zonk confessed.

“Even better,” the lichen-bearded kin rumbled. “The magic does not require your full attention. It requires your authentic self. Even the distracted, petty parts.”

They tried for what felt like hours. Sometimes the glow would flare, synchronized with a burst of shared laughter when Blaze made a terrible pun, or when Pip tried to explain a point of pony etiquette with utter seriousness. Sometimes it would dim when Zonk’s cynicism crept back in, a muttered “this is so stupid” causing the light to flicker.

It was frustrating. It was boring. It was the least epic training montage imaginable.

But by the end, they could sit in silence, and the Elements would hum with that steady, warm light, a low chord only they could feel vibrating in their chests. It felt like recognition.

During a break, while Pip chatted with Sorrel about herb lore, Zonk found Blaze by the grotto’s tiny seep spring, cupping water to his mouth.

“Hey,” Zonk said.

“Hey.”

Zonk leaned against the cool stone. A rivulet of water traced a path through the moss next to his head. He watched it. “I’m scared I’m gonna fuck this up.”

Blaze wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. “Which part? The magic friendship beam, or the infiltrating-the-evil-princess’s-party part?”

“The part where you or Pip get hurt because I… I overthink it. Or I freeze. Like in the alley.”

Blaze was quiet for a moment. He picked up a pebble, tossed it into the shallow pool. It made a soft plonk. “You didn’t freeze forever. You got us out. You came for me.”

“That was desperation. That was… I didn’t have a choice.”

“You always have a choice, Zonk.” Blaze looked at him, his expression uncharacteristically grave. “You chose to care. That’s the scary part, right? Not the monsters. The caring.”

Zonk’s throat tightened. He looked away, back at the water. “It’s a terrible system. Makes you vulnerable to shit.”

“Yeah,” Blaze agreed. He clapped a hand on Zonk’s shoulder, the grip firm, familiar. “But it’s the only system we’ve got. So we’ll do it like we do everything else.”

“Half-assed and stoned?”

“Together,” Blaze said, and grinned. “The half-assed-and-stoned part is a given.”

From across the grotto, Luna observed them. She did not approach. Or maybe that was just a trick of the bruise-colored moss.

The grotto smelled different with the Elements lit up. The wet-penny scent was still there, but underneath it now was something warmer, like sun on old wood, or the way a record sleeve smelled when you pulled it out after years in a crate. The six objects sat in a rough circle on the moss, each giving off a soft, steady pulse. The pink whoopee-cushion thing—Laughter—wheezed gently every few seconds. The yellow jar of Kindness ointment gave off a scent of chamomile. The tiara’s star-points glowed with a light that didn’t cast shadows, just filled the air like fog.

“Stand between them,” Sorrel instructed. She hadn’t moved from her rock. “Not as handlers. Not as bearers. As… guests. Listen.”

Zonk’s untied shoelace was a lost cause. He stepped over the red cuffs—Loyalty—and into the center of the circle. The air thickened. It wasn’t pressure, exactly. More like the moment before a thunderstorm, when the world held its pause and everything felt charged. His skin prickled. A memory flashed, useless and sharp: his mother’s perfume, something floral and cheap, the smell of her coat as she hugged him goodbye. He hadn’t thought of that in years. He pushed it down.

Blaze stepped in beside him, cracking his neck. “Okay. Listening. Hearing a whole lot of nothing, dude.”

“Shut up,” Zonk said, but there was no heat in it.

Pip took her place, completing a rough triangle. She looked small between them, her eyes fixed on the tiara. Her silence between them was quick and shallow.

“Now,” Sorrel said. “Do nothing.”

They stood there. The moss-light bruised the edges of everything. A drip from the ceiling landed on the silver tongue depressor—Honesty—and sizzled away into a tiny puff of steam. Zonk counted his breaths. In. Out. His thumb found the raw spot near his nail and worried it. The scab came loose. A bead of blood welled up, bright and stupid.

This was ridiculous. They were standing in a glowing mushroom circle waiting for a vibe check. The fate of two dimensions rested on their ability to… what? Feel feelings? He was bad at feelings on a good day, and this was the worst day of a very bad week.

Blaze shifted his weight. The movement was loud in the quiet.

The light from the cuffs flared. Not brightly. A single pulse, deep crimson, that washed over Blaze’s boots and faded. He froze. “What was that?”

“A response,” Sorrel said dryly. “It heard your impatience. It is not a machine to be activated. It is a mirror.”

“Great,” Zonk muttered. “So we’re in therapy with a set of magic handcuffs.”

Another pulse. This one came from the tiara. A wash of violet light that made the inside of Zonk’s skull hum. It carried a sensation—not an image, not a sound. A shape. It felt like the hollow ache of being alone in a crowded room. It felt like his own.

He flinched. “Okay. No jokes. Got it.”

They stood in silence again. The seconds stretched. Zonk’s mind, untethered began to drift. He noticed the pattern of lichen on the wall behind Sorrel. It looked like a map of a country that didn’t exist. He wondered if the Old Herd ever got bored down here. If they played cards. What pony-kin cards would even look like.

Pip’s silence hitched. A soft sound, almost a whimper.

He glanced at her. She was trembling, her hands clenched at her sides. Tears tracked clean lines through the dust on her cheeks. The light from the Kindness jar—a soft, buttery yellow—had wrapped around her like a shawl. It pulsed in time with her shaking.

“Pip?” Blaze’s voice was uncharacteristically gentle.

“It’s… it’s too much,” she said, low. “It’s all the loneliness. Everyone’s. It’s in the walls.”

The jar’s glow intensified. The smell of chamomile grew stronger, mixed now with the salt tang of her tears. Zonk understood, suddenly and without words. The Element wasn’t reading her. It was resonating. Her loneliness was a tuning fork, and the jar was the same note, vibrating in sympathy. It wasn’t an attack. It was an echo. A recognition.

Something hot moved through his chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. He wanted to look away. This was private. This was the raw, stupid nerve of her, exposed in the glow of a magic salve container. He wasn’t built for this.

But Blaze was already moving. Not a big gesture. He just uncrossed his arms and let his hand drop, so the back of his knuckles brushed against Pip’s. A point of contact. Silent. Simple.

The yellow light softened. The violent trembling in Pip’s shoulders eased. She didn’t look at Blaze, but she leaned into the touch, just a fraction of an inch.

The red cuffs pulsed again, a warm, steady beat this time. Loyalty. Not a demand. An observation.

Zonk’s throat felt tight. He looked at his friends—Really looked. Blaze, solid and scared and trying so hard to be the rock. Pip, shattered and brave and putting herself back together in real time. And him. The one who was good at running. The one who knew all the exits.

This time, the hollow feeling was joined by another shape. It was the solid, stubborn weight of Blaze’s shoulder-check against a door. It was the earnest confusion in Pip’s voice when she asked about Star Wars. It was the two of them, waiting for him to catch up.

He didn’t choose the words. They just fell out, quiet and ragged. “I don’t know how to do this.”

Silence.

Then Blaze let out a short, sharp air. It was almost a laugh. “Join the club, man.”

“I’m scared I’m gonna fuck it up.” The admission, ugly and plain. He’d never said it out loud. Not about anything that mattered. “I’m not… I don’t know how to be responsible for this. For you guys.”

Pip turned her head. Her eyes were huge, the tears still clinging to her lashes. “You already are.”

The six lights in the circle brightened. Not in flashes, but in a slow, coordinated swell. The colors didn’t mix. They braided. Crimson from the cuffs wove with violet from the tiara. Yellow from the jar threaded with the pink glow of the whoopee cushion. The silver of Honesty gleamed; the deep purple of the diamond flogger—Generosity—shimmered. The light climbed, filling the grotto, pushing back the bruise-colored moss-glow. It was warm. It didn’t feel like power. It felt like being seen.

The hum was inside his bones now. It was the same frequency as the vibration in his chest when Blaze made a truly terrible pun. The same as the quiet click of things making sense when Pip explained some obscure pony custom. It was their specific, messy, chaotic chord.

Sorrel was standing. He hadn’t noticed her get up. She looked at the three of them, her gnarled face unreadable. Then she gave a single, slow nod. “The mirror does not lie. You are not in harmony. You are harmony. It is a noisy, discordant, fragile thing. But it is real.”

The lights began to fade, sinking back into the objects. The charged air dissipated. The grotto was just a damp cave again, smelling of pennies and chamomile.

Zonk’s legs felt weak. He realized he was sweating. Blaze rubbed a hand over his face, leaving a smear of dirt on his forehead. Pip wiped her cheeks with the back of her hand, sniffling once.

“So,” Blaze said, his voice rough. “We passed?”

“You are the key,” Sorrel said. She sounded tired. “And the lock. And the door. The ritual at the Gala will focus what you already are. It will broadcast it. For good or ill.”

Luna spoke from the shadows near the entrance. She had watched the whole thing in silence. “Then we have our weapon. And our window. The Gala begins at moonrise. You will enter as the entertainment—a trio of traveling players. The Old Herd has secured the disguises and the placement.” She stepped forward, her eyes on Zonk. “The hard part is not the performance. It is carrying that…” She gestured at the air where the light had been. “… that truth, into the heart of the lie. Without flinching.”

Zonk looked at the tiara. Its glow was gone, just a piece of weird jewelry now. He thought of the hollow feeling. He thought of the map of lichen on the wall. He thought of his untied shoe.

“Yeah,” he said. “No pressure.”

The air backstage was a physical thing—a soup of sweat, floral perfume, and the sharp, chemical tang of stage fog. It clung to the back of Zonk’s throat. He adjusted the ridiculous feathered collar of his costume, a garish purple number that was part jester, part performing poodle. The sequins itched. Across the crowded, shadowy wing, Blaze was trying to fit his broad shoulders into a matching outfit two sizes too small. He looked like a sausage straining its casing.

“This is a hate crime against fabric,” Blaze muttered, tugging at a sleeve.

“Quiet,” Luna bit out. She was a silhouette against the glowing curtain, dressed in the severe black of a stage manager. Her cutie mark, a crescent moon was temporarily obscured by a clip-on badge that read “PRODUCTION.” “The overture begins in three minutes. You know your positions. You know your cues. Do not improvise.”

“We never improvise,” Zonk said.

Luna’s look could have frozen lava. “The Element of Laughter is a whoopee cushion. Your entire plan is improvisation.”

She melted into the darkness, leaving them in the chaotic swirl of performers. A trio of unicorn-kin juggled glowing orbs with their prehensile tails. A pegasus-kin acrobat stretched her wings, the feathers rustling like pages of a book. The noise was a solid wall—chattering, last-minute tuning of instruments, the thump of props being moved. Zonk’s heart was doing a frantic, irregular tap-dance against his ribs. He noticed a single sequined shoe abandoned in a corner. It was silver, scuffed at the toe. Someone had lost it. He wondered if the owner was now performing with one bare foot.

Pip appeared from behind a rolling rack of costumes, her own disguise somehow convincing. She wore a simple harlequin pattern, the diamonds in soft pastels, and a cap with tiny bells that jingled softly when she moved. The shiny patch of healing paste on her cheek looked like part of the makeup. Her eyes, though were huge.

“I have the placement scroll,” she said, patting a pouch at her hip. “The focal points are marked. When the Royal Procession reaches the dais, the attention of the Court will be… Optimal.”

“Optimal for what?” Blaze asked, finally giving up on the sleeve and rolling it to his elbow. “World-altering magic or, like, a really embarrassing flash mob?”

“Both,” Zonk said. The word came out dry. “Probably both.”

A sudden swell of music from beyond the curtain made the floor vibrate. The overture. The crowd noise dimmed to an expectant murmur, a sound like distant ocean waves. This was it. The plan, Sorrel’s teachings, the weird, humming warmth they’d managed to coax from the Elements during practice in the grotto—it all funneled down to this moment, this backstage panic, and the persistent itch of a sequined collar.

Blaze bumped his shoulder against Zonk’s. “You good?”

“Peachy. I’ve always wanted to commit metaphysical treason while dressed as a rejected Mardi Gras float.”

“Hey.” Blaze’s voice dropped, losing its jokey edge. He was looking at Zonk, really looking, and Zonk had to fight the urge to look away. “We got this. You and me. We’ve bullshitted our way through worse.”

“Name one time.”

“Senior year. Mrs. Henderson’s final. The essay you ‘wrote’ about the symbolic weight of lawn gnomes in postmodern literature.”

“I got a B-minus.”

“Exactly. We’re geniuses.” Blaze grinned, but it didn’t reach his eyes. They were tight at the corners. He was scared, too. The realization landed in Zonk’s gut, a small, cold stone. Blaze was never scared. Annoyed, frustrated ready to charge in, but never this quiet, watchful dread. It was wrong. It made the world feel even more off its axis.

The stone in Zonk’s gut grew hooks. Something hot and sour moved up his throat—not quite fear, not quite the old, familiar urge to run, but a messy, unnamed sludge of both. He picked at his thumb. The scab came off. A bead of blood welled, bright and startling against his skin.

“If this goes sideways,” Zonk said, the words coming out before he could stop them, “like, full-on ‘Equestrian bylaws at the DMV’ sideways… I just. I need you to get her out. Pip. However you can.”

Blaze stopped smiling. “What about you?”

“I’ll be… improvising.” Zonk tried for a smirk. It felt like a crack in clay.

“No.” Blaze said it flatly, a door slamming shut. “We all go, or we all stay. That’s the deal. That’s the whole fucking point of this friendship magic crap, right? No one gets left behind to be a hero. It’s a package deal.”

“That’s a terrible business model.”

“It’s the only one we’ve got.” Blaze’s hand closed around Zonk’s wrist, just for a second. His grip was warm, solid. It grounded the spinning in Zonk’s head. “You’re not failing us, man. We’re a team. A messed-up, stupid team in glittery outfits, but a team. So shut up and get ready to make some magic.”

The stage manager—a different one, a harried earth-kin with a clipboard—waved frantically at their group. “Jester trio! You’re on after the aerial silk act! Places!”

The hooking feeling in Zonk’s chest didn’t vanish, but it shifted. It wasn’t lighter. Just different. A weight shared, not a weight carried. He took a pause. The air still tasted of sweat and fog, but underneath it he caught a thread of something else—the smell of the greasepaint, waxy and nostalgic. It reminded him of a school play in fourth grade. He’d been a tree. He’d stood perfectly still the whole time, and his mom had been in the front row. She’d waved.

He blinked the memory away. Irrelevant. Useless. His heart was still pounding.

Pip slipped between them, her bell-cap jingling. “It is time,” she said softly. Her hand found Zonk’s gave a quick, hard squeeze, then did the same to Blaze. Her palm was damp. “For the real magic.”

They moved into position behind a heavy velvet curtain, their view of the stage a slim slice of glaring light. The Gala was a kaleidoscope nightmare of color and opulence. Ponies and pony-kin in extravagant gowns and tailored suits milled below a crystal dome. Chandeliers dripped light. On a raised dais at the far end sat an empty, sun-shaped throne. Celestia’s seat.

The aerial silk act was ending, two pegasus-kin twirling down from the rafters in a swirl of magenta fabric. The applause was polite, a patter like rain.

“Go, go, go!” the stage manager quietly yelled.

And then they were stumbling into the light.

The heat of the spotlights was a physical shock, a blanket of dry warmth. The crowd was a sea of blurred faces and glittering eyes. Zonk’s first step was too hard, his shoe slipping slightly on the polished floor. He caught himself, his body moving into the ridiculous, bouncy gait they’d practiced. Blaze and Pip fell in beside him, their movements synchronized, a parody of cheer.

Their act was simple, stupid: clumsy jugglers who kept dropping their props. Blaze fumbled a set of glowing clubs immediately, sending one rolling toward the footlights. A titter of laughter rippled through the audience. Good. They were watching the clowns, not the three figures moving with deliberate, subtle purpose toward the pre-marked points of a hidden triangle around the royal dais.

Zonk’s pouch hidden beneath his garish jacket felt like it was vibrating. Or maybe that was him. The Element of Magic—the tiara—was a cold circle against his chest. He could feel the others, too, a faint, sympathetic hum from the objects Blaze and Pip carried. Laughter. Loyalty. It was like holding a chord no one else could hear.

He reached his mark, a specific tile in the mosaic floor that was a slightly darker shade of gold. He dropped to one knee, pretending to retrieve a “lost” juggling ball, and palmed the small, cool vial from his sleeve. Sorrel’s mixture. A concentrate of memory, she’d said. Of joy that asked for nothing.

As he straightened, his eyes met Blaze’s across the twenty feet of open floor. Blaze gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod. He was in position. Pip, on the third point was adjusting her cap, the signal.

The music swelled, a fanfare of trumpets. The main doors at the back of the hall swung open.

Princess Celestia entered.

She was taller than Zonk remembered, and the air in the room changed. The polite chatter died. The temperature seemed to drop. She moved with a slow, inevitable grace, her white coat seeming to generate its own light, her mane a flowing rush of aurora colors. Her gown was sunlight woven into silk. The crowd bowed as she passed, a wave of submission.

She didn’t look at the performers. She looked straight ahead, at her throne. Her expression was serene, absolute. A goddess surveying her creation.

This was it. The procession reached the dais. All eyes were on her. No one was looking at the three jesters frozen in their positions.

Zonk’s mouth was dust. He uncorked the vial with his thumb.

The plan was to pour it on his tile, to let it mix with the drops from Blaze and Pip’s vials, to complete the circuit. A simple physical action.

His hand wouldn’t move.

Celestia ascended the steps of the dais. She turned, surveying her Court. Her gaze swept over them—and for a fraction of a second, it paused on Zonk. Her eyes were violet, deep, and utterly empty. They held no malice, no curiosity. Just the absolute certainty of a mountain regarding a speck of dust.

The hooking fear in his chest became a cage. She knows, a voice in his head screamed. This is a trap. It’s always been a trap.

He was ten years old again, waiting for a mother who never came back. He was useless. He was going to get them all killed.

Then he saw Pip. She was looking at him, not at the Princess. Her eyes were wide, but not with fear. With trust. With a stupid, earnest, terrifying belief that he could do this.

And Blaze. Blaze, who was already moving, who couldn’t wait for the signal. He gave a theatrical shrug for the crowd, pretended to trip over his own feet, and as he fell, his hand slapped the floor. The clear liquid from his vial splashed onto the dark-gold tile.

It was the wrong order. It didn’t matter.

Zonk poured his out. The liquid was cool, smelling of rain on dry earth and, inexplicably, of grape bubblegum. Pip did the same.

For a second, nothing.

Then the tiles under their feet began to glow. Not with the bruise-light of the grotto, but with a gentle, gold warmth. It spread in three quick lines, connecting their points, forming a triangle that enclosed the sun throne.

A hum filled the air, a low note that vibrated in Zonk’s teeth.

Celestia’s serene expression finally cracked. Her head snapped down. She looked at the glowing lines on her floor. Her eyes widened, just a fraction.

“Now,” Zonk said softly.

He reached into his jacket, his fingers closing around the cold metal of the tiara. He didn’t put it on. He just held it, focusing on the hum, on the warmth under his feet, on the stupid, trusting face of a pony-girl who believed in magic, and on his best friend who believed in him.

The tiara grew warm in his hand. Then hot.

On the dais, Princess Celestia took a step back.

The dressing room smelled of stale talcum powder and the ghost of a thousand floral perfumes. A single bare bulb hung from a wire, casting a harsh, unforgiving light that turned the sequins scattered across the floor into a field of dead stars. Pip stood in front of a smudged, full-length mirror, her back to Zonk. She was trying to tie the sash of her costume, a ridiculous, frilled number meant to make her look like a backup singer for a pastel-themed lounge act. Her fingers fumbled with the silky fabric.

The roar of the Gala crowd was a constant, low-grade pressure on the other side of the thin door, like a distant ocean trapped in a box.

Zonk leaned against a rack of feathered headdresses. One of the feathers tickled his ear. He didn’t brush it away. He was watching the tight line of Pip’s shoulders, the way her hands shook just enough to make the bow she was attempting come out lopsided. She saw it in the mirror. She let out a tiny, frustrated huff and started again.

“You don’t have to do this,” he said, quieter now, too loud in the cramped space.

“I do.” She didn’t turn around. Her reflection met his eyes in the glass. “It’s the only part of the plan that requires… me. Specifically. Not the Elements. Not your knowledge. Me. My voice.”

“Yeah, but the ‘me specifically’ part involves walking onto a stage in front of Princess Terrifying and her entire kink-cabal and singing a friendship hymn that’s supposed to, I don’t know, spiritually defibrillate the universe.” He picked at his thumbnail. The scab was gone, leaving pink, tender skin. “That’s a lot of ‘specifically.’”

“It’s not a hymn. It’s an invocation of original hum.” She finally got the bow tied. It was still lopsided. She stared at it, then shrugged, a small surrender. “Sorrel said my training, all the vocal control is perfect for it. I can hit the tonal key they need. I’m just… the amplifier. You and Blaze are the signal.”

“We’re a pretty shitty signal.”

“You’re not.” She turned then. The costume was absurd—puffy sleeves, a skirt that stuck out like a bell—but her face was utterly serious. The shiny paste on her cheek caught the light. “You’re chaotic. And real. That’s what they need. The old magic isn’t about perfect harmony. Sorrel said it’s about distinct voices choosing to align. You’re very distinct.”

A laugh escaped him, short and dry. “Thanks.”

From beyond the door, a burst of orchestral music swelled and then cut off, followed by a wave of polite applause. Someone’s number was up. Theirs was next.

The doorknob rattled. It opened a crack, and Blaze squeezed in, bringing with him a wave of crowd noise and the smell of spiced wine and sweat. He shut the door quickly, muffling the sound back to its dull roar. He was wearing a matching ridiculous outfit, a velvet jacket that strained across his shoulders. He had a smudge of what looked like glitter on his jaw.

“Showtime in five,” he said, his voice tight. “Luna’s guy is at the stage-left curtain. He’ll get us to the prop table once Pip starts her bit.” He looked at Pip, then at Zonk. “You good?”

“Peachy,” Zonk said.

“I’m ready,” Pip said at the same time.

Blaze nodded, but he was looking at Zonk. Really looking. His eyes did that thing, the thing they’d done since they were kids, where all the easygoing bullshit drained away and left something solid and worried underneath. “Your shoelace is untied.”

“I know.”

“You’re gonna trip on it.”

“I won’t.”

“You will. You always do.” Blaze walked over, crouched down in front of him. The velvet jacket made a soft shush sound. He grabbed the frayed lace and started tying it with quick, efficient motions. His head was bowed. Zonk stared at the part in his hair, at a single gray strand he’d never noticed before.

“I’m scared I’m gonna fuck this up,” Zonk said. The words were out before he could shape them into something less pathetic. They just fell into the quiet between the distant applause.

Blaze finished the knot double-tied it. He didn’t look up. “Which part? The part where we sneak past the guards, or the part where we touch the magic BDSM artifacts during a sacred ritual and try not to get our souls contractually bound to a mob boss?”

“The part where I get you two killed.” His throat felt thick. “The part where I… where I don’t know what I’m doing. I never know what I’m doing. I just make sarcastic comments and hope it works out.”

Blaze sat back on his heels and looked up. “Yeah. And?”

“And?” Zonk’s voice cracked. “And that’s not a plan! That’s not a thing! Pip’s over here talking about harmony and distinct voices, and I’m the guy who once got us lost for three hours in a Walmart because I was too high to read the exit signs. I’m not a distinct voice. I’m a broken GPS.”

“You’re my GPS,” Blaze said, simple as stating a fact. He stood up, his knees popping. “Broken or not. You’re the one who remembers the lore. You’re the one who figured out the safeword tap on the cuffs. You’re the one who knew Luna was playing us before I did. You think I’d be here without you? I’d have charged a Solar Sentinel head-on week one. I’d be a nice rug in somepony’s foyer by now.”

“That’s not—”

“It is.” Blaze put a hand on his shoulder. The grip was firm, warm through the thin fabric of his own stupid costume. “We’re a team, Z. It’s not you leading and me following. It’s not me protecting and you thinking. It’s just… us. Messing it up together. Like always.”

Pip was watching them, her eyes soft. She didn’t say anything. She just smoothed the front of her frilly skirt, her small hand pressing out a wrinkle that wasn’t there.

A sharp knock rattled the door three times. The signal.

The air in the room changed. It got heavier, like the atmosphere before a lightning strike. Zonk’s stomach clenched, a hard, involuntary spasm. Not fear, not panic. Anticipation. The kind that made the hair on your arms stand up.

Blaze’s hand fell from his shoulder. “Okay. Distinct voices. Let’s go make some noise.”

Pip took a deep, shuddering beat. She closed her eyes for a second, her lips moving silently. When she opened them, she looked different. Not older, but focused. The nervous energy was gone, folded away somewhere. She walked to the door, her ridiculous skirt swishing, and opened it.

The wall of sound hit them—music, laughter, the clink of glasses, a thousand conversations swirling into one meaningless roar. The corridor outside was dim, lit by flickering sconces shaped like smiling suns. A stagehand with a headset and a clipboard nodded urgently down the hall toward the swirling brightness of the stage wings.

Pip went first. She didn’t look back. She walked with her shoulders straight, head high, into the noise and the light.

Zonk watched her go, that small figure in the frilly dress, and something hot moved through his chest—not quite pride, not quite terror, something between the two that didn’t have a name. He followed Blaze into the din, the untied shoelace of his mind finally, for once, securely knotted.
