The storage locker smelled of dust, defeat, and the ghost of someone else’s mothballed sweaters. Zonk’s back ached from where he’d wedged himself between a collapsed cardboard box labeled “Xmas” and a rolled-up rug that might have once been beige. The single bare bulb overhead buzzed like an angry insect trapped in a jar, casting long, jumpy shadows that made everything look like it was twitching.

Blaze had the door propped open a crack with his foot, one eye pressed to the slit. His breathing was too loud. “Clear. I think. Nobody followed us into the alley. Just a dumpster and a cat that looks like it’s judging my life choices.”

“It’s probably right,” Zonk muttered. His hands wouldn’t stop trembling. He shoved them under his thighs. The concrete floor was cold through his jeans, a gritty, specific cold that seeped right into the bone. He focused on that. Better than focusing on the way his heart was trying to punch its way out of his ribcage.

Pip sat cross-legged in the only clear patch of floor, her pastel bodysuit already smudged with grime. She held the yellow jar of Kindness ointment in both hands, turning it over and over as if reading invisible instructions. The motion was hypnotic, and Zonk found himself staring at the way the weak light caught the curve of the glass. It looked like a prop from a bad fantasy movie. It had sealed a cut on Blaze’s knuckles in under a minute, the skin knitting together without a scar.

“So,” Zonk said. The word hung in the dusty air. “Psychotic party-store mannequins. Magical first-aid cream. A talking pony-girl.” He gestured vaguely at Pip. “No offense.”

“None taken,” she whispered, not looking up.

“Right. So we’ve ruled out shared psychosis. The weed isn’t that good. Which leaves us with the option I really, really didn’t want to be the right one.” He took a breath that didn’t seem to reach his lungs. “This is real.”

Blaze let the door click shut and slumped against it. The lock engaged with a cheap, tinny sound. “Real. Okay. Real is a word. What does ‘real’ mean here, exactly? Like, alternate-dimension real? Secret-society-under-Cleveland real? Because my brain keeps offering ‘elaborate prank’ as a comfort blanket and I’m about to freeze to death.”

Pip finally lifted her head. Her eyes were huge in the dim light. “You took the Sacred Elements of Harmony from the Sun Court Vault. You broke the seals. You pulled me through the. The shimmer. The barrier.” She said the words like they were stones she was forced to carry. “The Royal Guard of her Solar Majesty will not stop. The aesthetic violation of your dwelling was. Profound. And the theft. ” She trailed off, a small shudder running through her.

“Aesthetic violation,” Zonk repeated. He remembered the Guards, their armor that looked like cheap plastic but had moved with a terrifying, jointless grace. Their blank, polished faceplates. “You mean our apartment wasn’t pony enough for them?”

“It was a screaming void,” Pip said, utterly earnest. “A chaos of angles and colors that obey no harmony. It hurt to look upon. It is why they were so. Forceful.”

Blaze barked a laugh that had no humor in it. “Forceful. Yeah. That’s one word for trying to turn my skull into modern art with a rainbow baton.” He rubbed the now-smooth skin on his knuckles.

Zonk’s mind, traitorous and trained on a thousand hours of lore started connecting dots. It was a stupid, ridiculous picture. He wished he was too stoned for this. “The VR booth. At the Starlight Arcade. That’s not a game console is it?”

Pip blinked. “Game?”

“The thing we used. The headset. The. Portal.”

Her expression cleared, replaced by a kind of polite confusion. “Oh. The Node. The permitted aperture. Yes. The Sun Court maintains several stable Nodes in your. Realm. For trade. For observation. For. ” She hesitated, her fingers tightening on the jar. “For acquiring new consultants. Those with an affinity for the forms.”

A cold knot tightened in Zonk’s gut. It had nothing to do with the dusty floor. “Acquiring.”

“The game’s a recruiting tool,” Blaze said, his voice flat. He got it too. “They fish for guys like us. Bronies with a side of kink. Who know the lore. Who wouldn’t freak out at the. The aesthetic.” He looked sick. “We didn’t beat a game. We aced an entrance exam.”

The buzzing of the bulb seemed to get louder. Zonk noticed a dead fly lying in a web of dust on the shelf to his left, one wing slightly raised as if in a final, futile salute. His throat was dry. “And the heist quest? The Elements?”

Pip looked down at the jar again. “That was not a test. That was an error. A catastrophic system failure. The vault’s wards should have been impenetrable. Your method of bypassing them. The game did not recognize it as a possibility. You were not supposed to be able to touch them. Ever.”

A weird, hysterical pride flickered in Zonk’s chest and died just as fast. He’d used a sequence he’d found on a meme forum, a glitch-speedrun trick involving pixel-perfect jumps and equipping a useless “whoopee cushion” item at a precise frame. He’d done it for the laugh. To see the legendary loot render in his inventory for a screenshot.

He’d broken reality for a screenshot.

“Okay,” he said, his voice sounding very far away. “Okay. So we broke their world. Stole their foundation stones. And kidnapped a. ” He waved at Pip.

“Companion-in-training,” she supplied softly. “I was in the vault antechamber. Practicing my. Receptivity meditations. The pull of the Elements as they crossed the barrier was. Magnetic.”

“Great. So we need to put it all back. The stuff. You. We need to go back to the Node and shove it all through the hole we ripped.” He said it like it was simple. Like explaining a plan made it exist.

Blaze shook his head. “The arcade’s gonna be crawling with those Guard freaks. Or worse. They know we came from there. It’s the first place they’ll lock down.”

“Then we need another Node,” Zonk said.

Pip was already shaking her head, a quick, nervous motion. “There are only three stable Nodes in this city. The Sun Court controls two. The Night Court controls the third.”

“Night Court.” The words felt strange in his mouth. “Princess Luna.”

Pip flinched at the name, a full-body recoil that made the dust around her stir. “You should not speak of the Lunar Majesty so. Casually. Her agents are everywhere when the sun falls. Their ways are not the ways of the Sun Court. They trade in different currencies.”

“What currencies?” Blaze asked.

“Secrets. Favors. Memories. Aesthetic. Deviations.” She swallowed. “The Night Markets. Where the rules are bendier, she says. But the price is often sharper.”

A market. A place where things were bought and sold, where people with problems went to find solutions. Zonk’s mind, always looking for the cheat code, latched onto it. “Can they get us to a Node? Can they get this stuff back where it belongs?”

“Perhaps,” Pip whispered. “But to seek the Night Court is to step from one kind of danger into another. The Solar Sentinels will crush you for breaking the law. The Lunar Agents will. Reshape you for their own purposes. They are not kinder. They are simply. Colder.”

Outside, in the alley, the cat Blaze had mentioned let out a long, yowling cry. It was a lonely hungry sound. The bulb above them flickered once, plunging them into a heartbeat of total blackness before sputtering back to life.

In the dark, Zonk hadn’t been able to see his own hands. He’d just felt the cold of the floor and the frantic drumbeat of his pulse in his ears. Two kinds of danger. A crushing fist or a cold, reshaping hand.

Blaze pushed off from the door. He rolled his shoulders, the motion cracking the silence. “We’re sitting ducks here. We need info. We need to know what we’re dealing with, not just the pony-primer version.” He looked at Zonk. “You’re the lore master. What’s the move?”

Zonk looked at Pip, small and scared in the dust. He looked at the jar in her hands, the one that mended. He thought of the cuffs in his backpack that wouldn’t unlock without a safeword, the tiara that hummed with a low, electric vibration. He thought of the apartment door splintering inward.

“We find the Night Market,” he said, the words leaving his mouth before he could talk himself out of them. “We get the lay of the land. The real land. And we find someone who can make us a deal.”

“A deal for what?” Blaze asked.

“For going home.” Zonk stood up, his joints protesting. A cloud of dust motes swirled around him caught in the buzzing light like tiny, frantic stars. “Everything else is just negotiation.”

The buzzing bulb was the only sound for a long minute. Zonk watched the dust motes dance in its weak, angry light. They moved like tiny doomed stars in a miniature universe contained by four cinderblock walls and someone else’s forgotten junk. His thumb found the ragged skin beside his nail and picked. A tiny sting, bright and specific.

“So we’re illegal immigrants,” Blaze said finally. He wasn’t looking at Pip. He was staring at a stain on the concrete floor that looked vaguely like Australia. “In a dimension run by a pony domme mob. And we stole her crown jewels, which also happen to be her legal system.”

“The Elements are not jewels,” Pip corrected softly, automatically. She placed the jar of Kindness on the floor with a gentle tock. “They are covenants. Physical anchors for the conceptual agreements that maintain harmony. The tiara is the master key. The cuffs are for binding oaths. The flogger allocates resources. The gag ball enforces levity and. Compliance. The scales measure truth. The salve heals breaches.” She recited it like a catechism. “Without them, the contracts lose potency. The Sun Court’s authority becomes. Theoretical. Based on fear alone, instead of fear backed by magic.”

“That sounds worse,” Zonk said. He lowered the words to thin in the dusty space.

“It is unstable,” Pip agreed. “Which is why they will tear your realm apart to find you. The aesthetic violation of your dwelling was a transgression. This theft is an existential threat.”

Blaze pushed off the door, pacing the three steps the cramped space allowed. He turned, paced back. “Okay. Okay. So we give them back. We walk into the Starlight Arcade, drop the bag of magic sex toys on the counter, say ‘sorry for the trouble,’ and go home. Pip goes home. Problem solved.”

Pip’s ears—Real ears, under the blue fabric of her hood, Zonk was sure of it now—flattened. “The Node will be guarded. The Sun Court will have sealed it. And even if you could reach it. Returning the Elements is not a transaction. It is a surrender. It requires a formal offering, a contrition ritual before the Solar Throne.”

“A what now?” Blaze stopped pacing.

“You would have to kneel,” Pip said, her voice dropping. “You would have to state your violation. You would have to accept the binding punishment for your crime, to restore the balance your theft created.” She looked from Blaze to Zonk, her eyes wide and dark. “The punishment for stealing a Covenant is. Absorption. You become part of the Court’s energy matrix. A living battery. A consultant whose will is no longer your own.”

The cold knot in Zonk’s gut turned to lead. A living battery. He saw it instantly, the aesthetic: a human form, polished and still, wired into a wall of crystalline circuitry in some pastel dungeon, forced to spit out lore-accurate plot points for eternity. A permanent game guide. His mouth went dry. “Hard pass.”

“So we can’t give them back,” Blaze said. He ran a hand over his face. “Great. We’re stuck with the magical nuclear codes.”

“We are not stuck,” Pip said. She drew her knees up to her chest. “There are. Other courts. Other ways to manipulate the contracts. The Night Court deals in information. In loopholes. Princess Luna’s agents operate in the margins. They might know a way to nullify the theft. To return the Elements without the. The absorption.”

Zonk’s mind, always tracking two steps ahead into worst-case scenarios, latched onto the pronoun. “Might know.”

“The Night Court is not benevolent. It is opportunistic. But it is an alternative to the Sun.” Pip picked at a thread on her sleeve. “They frequent the lower markets. Where the aesthetic rules are. Interpreted more flexibly. Where things from your world sometimes appear.”

“Black markets,” Zonk translated. He leaned his head back against the cardboard box. The edge of a forgotten ornament dug into his scalp. “You want us to go to the pony underworld to find the pony mob’s rival, to ask for help fixing the mistake we made stealing from the first pony mob.”

“When you say it like that,” Blaze muttered.

“How else is there to say it?” Something hot and brittle rose in Zonk’s throat. It wasn’t laughter. It was the precursor to either screaming or crying, and he choked it down. “This is insane. This is a fucking Saturday morning cartoon gone wrong. We should be. I don’t know. Calling the police. The army. Somebody.”

“And tell them what?” Blaze’s voice was quiet. “That we downloaded a pony game and now pastel fascists want to turn us into Duracells? They’d lock us up. They’d take Pip.” He glanced at her, a quick, protective flick of his eyes. “They’d take the stuff. And then where are we?”

He was right. Zonk knew he was right. The knowledge settled in his bones, heavier than the concrete chill. There was no authority to appeal to. The world they’d left—the real world—had no frame for this. They were on their own. The realization was a vacuum, sucking the air out of the storage locker.

Pip broke the silence. “The lower market is called the Twilight Bazaar. It moves. But there is a fixed entrance in your city. A place where the barrier is. Thin. From your side, it looks like a utility door. In an alley behind a restaurant that serves grilled cheese sandwiches.”

Zonk blinked. “The Melty Mare.”

“You know it?” Pip asked, a spark of hope in her voice.

“It’s a brony hangout,” Blaze said, a ghost of his old grin touching his mouth. “Best ‘Hay Fries’ in the state. They’ve got a full-size Derpy Hooves mural in the bathroom.”

“The door is in that alley. It is marked with a crescent moon worn almost smooth. You must knock with a specific rhythm. And you must be carrying an object of transactional value from this world. Something with. Emotional weight. A trade.”

“A trade for what?” Zonk asked.

“For passage. For information.” Pip hugged her knees tighter. “For a chance.”

Zonk looked at Blaze. His friend’s face was all hard angles in the ugly light, but his eyes were steady. He gave a slight, almost imperceptible nod. I’m in. Of course he was in. Blaze was always in. It was Zonk who got stuck on the ledge, calculating the drop.

He thought of his apartment. The shattered door. The rainbow batons. The blank, polished faces. Running. Always running. He thought of the jar of salve healing Blaze’s hand. He thought of Pip, sitting in his laundry basket, trying to explain magic with the earnestness of a kindergarten teacher.

The alternative was waiting for the Guards to find them. Or living in storage lockers until the money ran out. Or turning into a fucking cosmic AA battery.

“Emotional weight,” Zonk said, his voice rough. He patted his pockets. Wallet, phone, a half-crushed pack of gum, his glass pipe. His fingers closed around the pipe. It was a beautiful piece, cobalt blue with a silver spiral. He’d saved for three months for it. It was his ritual. His comfort. The little clink of the lighter, the first smooth pull. It was the only thing that ever made the world’s sharp edges soften.

He pulled it out. The light caught the cobalt, making it glow like a deep, private jewel.

“Will this work?”

Pip looked at it. She didn’t understand its purpose, but she sensed its significance. She nodded. “Yes. I believe so.”

Blaze was already moving, slinging the duffel bag with the Elements over his shoulder. “Then let’s go buy a grilled cheese and knock on a magic door.” He put a hand on the latch. “You ready?”

Zonk looked at the pipe in his palm. He thought about leaving it in some alien pawn shop. He thought about the dust motes, still dancing. He shoved the pipe back into his pocket, the weight of it suddenly all wrong.

“No,” he said, and stood up. “But let’s go anyway.”

The smell of old french fries and defeat had settled into the storage locker, a greasy film over the dust. Zonk picked at the corner of a burger wrapper, the paper waxy under his thumb. His stomach was a tight, unhappy fist, but he’d forced the food down anyway. Fuel. That’s what Blaze kept calling it. Fuel for the next stupid, terrifying thing.

Pip sat with her back against a stack of yellowed phone books, the Kindness jar cradled in her lap like a sleeping animal. She’d stopped turning it. Now she just stared at the closed locker door, her eyes wide and unblinking in the bad light.

“Okay,” Blaze said, crushing his own wrapper into a tight ball. He bounced it off the wall. It hit the rolled-up rug with a soft thud. “Okay. We know what it is. We know why they’re pissed. Next question: how do we give it back?”

“We can’t,” Pip whispered.

“We stole it. We un-steal it. Walk up to the vault, say ‘our bad,’ drop the stuff, run like hell.”

“The Node will be guarded,” Zonk said. He spoke, quieter now, scraped raw. “The arcade. If it’s a. A permitted aperture. After a breach? They’ll have it locked down tighter than a drum. Probably with those blank-faced assholes in plastic armor.” He could see it: the grimy Starlight Arcade, the flickering neon, and between the racing game and the claw machine, a phalanx of pastel guards waiting for them to be that dumb.

“So we need a distraction. Or a back door. Or. ” Blaze trailed off, cracking his knuckles one by one. The sound was like little gunshots in the quiet. “Intel. We need to know what we’re walking into. We need a map. A schedule. Something.”

Pip shifted. The phone books rustled. “There are places,” she said, so quietly Zonk almost didn’t hear her. “Where the Sun Court’s light doesn’t reach so directly. Where the rules are. Bendier.”

Zonk looked at her. “Bendier.”

“The Night Markets.” She said it like a secret. “They operate in the interstitial spaces. The alleys that don’t quite belong to any district. The rooms behind rooms. Information is traded there. As well as other things.”

“Black markets,” Blaze said, leaning forward. His eyes had that gleam, the one that usually preceded him trying to jump a bike over something. “Shady deals. The underworld. Perfect.”

A cold, slick feeling dripped down Zonk’s spine. It wasn’t fear. It was the déjà vu of a thousand bad heist movies. The plucky crew diving into the underworld for the one guy who could help. The one guy who always, always sold them out. “And we just walk in? Two humans and a. You. With the literal crown jewels of your dimension in a backpack. We’ll stand out.”

“We would,” Pip agreed. She finally looked away from the door, her gaze settling on the grimy floor between them. “But you would not go as yourselves. You would go as consultants. New acquisitions. It is what the Node is for. It would explain your. Aesthetic dissonance. Your strangeness.”

The word “acquisition” landed in the pit of Zonk’s stomach and stuck there, cold and hard. He saw the logic of it, the brutal, elegant cover. Of course the weird new humans would be in the Night Markets. They were merchandise.

“And you?” he asked.

Her shoulders lifted in a small, helpless shrug. “I am a trainee. An unassigned submissive. It is not uncommon for us to be taken to the Markets by our handlers. For. Seasoning.”

The locker felt suddenly smaller, the air thicker. Zonk noticed a single, fat spiderweb in the far corner, strung between a broken lamp and the wall. It quivered in a draft he couldn’t feel.

“So the play is,” Blaze said, ticking points off on his fingers, “we pretend to be fresh meat from the human side. You pretend to be our. Trainee. We act like we’re shopping for info, or work, or whatever. We listen. We find out how to get to the Node without getting turned into a fine paste.” He nodded, as if agreeing with himself. “It’s a plan. It’s got steps.”

“It’s a plan that relies on us successfully impersonating people who belong in a BDSM pony crime dimension,” Zonk said flatly. “A dimension we learned about from a video game. We’re going to bluff a bunch of actual criminals with lore we got from memes.”

Blaze grinned, a quick, wild flash. “Dude. We aced the entrance exam. We’re basically experts.”

“We broke the system! We’re not experts, we’re glitches!” The volume of his own voice surprised him. It echoed off the metal walls. Pip flinched.

Silence pooled back in, heavier than before. The bulb buzzed. Zonk focused on the spiderweb, the complex, pointless architecture of it.

“Do you have a better idea?” Blaze’s question wasn’t a challenge. It was tired.

Zonk didn’t. He had a swirling vortex of panic and a deep, cellular need to be anywhere else. Neither was useful. He shook his head.

“Then it’s the plan.” Blaze pushed himself to his feet, groaning as his knees popped. “We need to move before we fossilize here. And we need to look the part.” He started rooting through his backpack, pulling out the few items they’d grabbed in their panicked flight: a black hoodie, a pair of aviator sunglasses, a length of nylon rope from a camping phase he’d never actually had.

Pip watched him, her head tilted. “Your garments are all wrong,” she said, polite but firm. “They are. Aggressive. And drab. The aesthetic is harmony. Even in the Night Markets, there is an expected palette. A presentation.”

“Great,” Zonk muttered. “So we need a makeover from the pony mafia.”

“We need not look native. Only plausible. New acquisitions are often disoriented. Their clothing is. Transitional.” She stood up, brushing dust from her flank. She approached Zonk, her steps hesitant. She stopped just outside his personal space, studying him with an unsettling intensity. “May I?”

He had no idea what she was asking. He gave a short, jerky nod.

Her hands came up, small and delicate. She didn’t touch him. Instead, she gestured, tracing shapes in the air an inch from his chest, his shoulders. “The hood is too shrouding. It suggests concealment, not submission. The sunglasses. They are a barrier. Eyes must be visible. To show intention. Or fear.” Her gaze flicked to his face. “You have very human eyes.”

Something about the way she said it—not as an insult, just an observation, like noting the weather—made his skin prickle. “Yeah. Sorry.”

“Do not be.” She finally reached out, her fingers just brushing the front of his worn gray T-shirt. “This color is acceptable. Neutral. But it is loose. It does not. Define.”

Before he could process what was happening, she had gathered a handful of fabric at his side, pulling it taut against his ribs. Her other hand went to the nylon rope in Blaze’s grasp. “May I use this?”

Blaze handed it over, eyebrows raised.

With a few efficient movements, Pip looped the rope around Zonk’s waist, over the gathered fabric, and tied it off at his hip in a knot that looked both simple and impossibly complex. She stepped back. The shirt was now cinched, the shape of his torso outlined. It felt strange. Exposed. The knot was a hard, definite pressure against his hipbone.

“Better,” she said, tilting her head. “It suggests. Readiness. Acknowledgment of form.”

Zonk looked down at himself. He looked like an idiot who’d been tied up by a very earnest scout. “I feel like a sack of potatoes someone gift-wrapped.”

“It is a start.” She turned to Blaze, her professional demeanor returning. “For you, the hoodie must go. The shirt beneath is acceptable. The sleeves should be rolled. To show the forearms. It is a sign of having nothing to hide.”

Blaze, to Zonk’s utter lack of surprise, looked delighted. He shucked the hoodie, rolled his sleeves with theatrical care. “How’s that? Do I look bendy?”

“You look like a bouncer at a vegan restaurant,” Zonk said.

“You look like a hostage.”

Pip ignored them, circling Blaze. “The posture is wrong. You stand like a sentry. Like you are claiming space. You must soften the shoulders. Lower the chin. Just slightly.” She demonstrated, her own posture shifting from attentive to something else—a gentle yielding, an awareness of the space around her that wasn’t passive, but. Receptive.

Blaze tried to mimic it. He looked like he had a back spasm.

“It is not a physical adjustment alone,” Pip said, her voice softening. “It is an intention. You are not entering to conquer. You are entering to. Observe. To learn. To be assessed.”

The locker was too warm. Zonk’s mouth was dry. He watched Pip, this small, dirty creature in a pastel bodysuit, giving them a crash course in how to not get killed in a world she found magical. Her earnestness was a physical ache in his chest. It wasn’t kindness. It was something more terrifying—a faith that things had rules, that if you just learned them, you’d be safe.

He realized, with a lurch, that he wanted to protect that faith. Not because it was true, but because the world where it could be true was a better one than this metal box smelling of fries.

“Pip,” he said. The words shifted, out rough.

She turned to him, her big eyes questioning.

“The brand on your flank. The sun. That’s from your school?”

A faint, instinctive shift, a half-turn to hide her flank. Then she stopped, forcing herself still. “Yes. The Solar Academy. All companion trainees bear it.”

“Will that get us in trouble? In the Night Markets? If we’re supposed to be with you, but you’re marked as Celestia’s?”

She was quiet for a long moment. The buzzing bulb filled the space. “It may,” she admitted. “Some will see it and avoid us. Some will see it as a challenge. It is a risk.”

“But it’s also our cover,” Blaze said. “If she’s a legit trainee, and we’re the new consultants, it makes sense we’d have her. Right?”

“It makes a story,” Zonk corrected. “Stories can be questioned.” He looked at Pip, at the clean, elegant lines of the sun symbol against her blue fur. It looked like a corporate logo. He remembered the blank faceplates of the Guards. “Can we hide it?”

Pip’s ears drooped. “To alter a mark is. A profound dishonesty.”

“Pip.” He didn’t mean to sound so tired. “They tried to kill us. Honesty might be a luxury.”

She stared at him, a war playing out behind her eyes. Then, slowly, she nodded. She looked around the locker, her gaze landing on the grimy, beige rug. She walked over, knelt, and rubbed her fingers hard against a particularly dirty patch. She came back with her fingertips coated in a dark, granular grime.

“This will not hold,” she whispered, more to herself than to them. She turned, presenting her flank. With a careful, precise motion, she smudged the dirt over the sun brand, blurring its edges, dulling its gold into a muddy brown. It wasn’t gone. It was just harder to see, a secret hiding in plain sight.

When she turned back, there was a smudge of dirt on her hand and a strange, hollow look on her face. She had done something unthinkable.

Zonk’s throat tightened. He didn’t have words. He reached out, not thinking, and wiped a stray streak of grime from her wrist with his thumb. Her skin was warm. The gesture was clumsy, too rough.

She didn’t pull away. She just looked at the spot he’d cleaned, then up at him.

“It’s just dirt,” he said, the words stupid and inadequate. “It’ll wash off.”

She gave a single, slow nod. The hollow look remained.

Blaze cleared his throat. The sound was like a door slamming. “Okay. We look the part. We’ve got a crap plan. We’re running out of time sitting here.” He hefted the backpack containing the Elements. The cuffs inside clinked softly. “Where’s the nearest Night Market?”

Pip took a deep breath, squaring her shoulders. The movement was different now—not the soft yielding, but a resolve. A choice. “There is a Node leakage point three blocks from here. An alley behind a bakery that always smells of burnt sugar. The glamour is thin there. We can slip through.”

“A bakery,” Zonk repeated. Of course. The portal to a kink dimension was behind a donut shop.

“We go at twilight,” Pip said. “When the light is uncertain. It is easier to cross.”

Twilight was maybe an hour away. The air in the locker seemed to shift, charged with a new, terrible momentum. The plan was in motion. There was no more debate.

Zonk looked at the spiderweb. The spider was gone, long gone. The web was just a relic, a beautiful, empty trap waiting for nothing at all.

The discount store’s back door opened into a puddle that smelled of stale rainwater and something vaguely chemical. Zonk’s shoes were already soaked. The alley was a canyon of brick and graffiti, lit at one end by the sickly orange glow of a sodium streetlight. At the other end, darkness pooled, thick and complete.

“Okay,” Blaze said, hefting the plastic shopping bag. It rustled, absurdly loud. “Disguises. Let’s see what fifty bucks of clearance-bin pony-play gets us.”

He dumped the bag’s contents onto a relatively dry patch of asphalt. The items looked even more pathetic under the alley’s grim light. A pair of vinyl horse ears on a headband, the fake fur matted. A tail plug with a cheap, synthetic blue tail attached. A rainbow-colored bandana. A tube of glitter body gel. And a set of fuzzy, purple hoof-covers meant to slip over hands and feet.

Pip picked up the hoof-covers with two fingers, her nose wrinkled. “These are. Festive.”

“They were on sale,” Blaze muttered.

Zonk stared at the pile. His brain kept trying to frame this as a joke, a bit for a con, but the cold knot in his stomach wouldn’t let him. This was their camouflage. Their survival depended on looking like they belonged in a world where this was normal streetwear. The sheer, towering absurdity of it pressed down on him, a physical weight. He noticed a single, fat drop of water gather on the rusted fire escape above them, catch the orange light for a second, and fall, vanishing into the puddle with a perfect, quiet plink.

“Right,” he said, his voice sounding detached, like it was coming from another room. “So. We’re going to walk into a criminal underworld dressed like the losing team at a fetish convention.”

“The aesthetic is law,” Pip recited, her tone solemn. “If you do not make an effort, the gaze will find you. The guard will find you. They will. Correct you.”

“With the rainbow batons,” Blaze added, rubbing his knuckles again.

“Yes.”

Zonk picked up the vinyl ears. One of them was bent. He tried to straighten it, but the wire inside had kinked. It listed to the side, giving the impression of a horse that had suffered a minor stroke. Perfect.

He put the headband on. The plastic band dug into his temples. The world didn’t end. No dimensional police descended. He just looked like an idiot standing in a wet alley.

Blaze let out a snort that was halfway to a sob. “Dude. You look like you asked a five-year-old to draw a horse from memory.”

“Your turn, fucko.”

Blaze picked up the tail. He turned it over in his hands, his expression unreadable. Then, with a shrug that seemed to cost him something, he undid his belt, loosened his jeans just enough, and fitted the plug. He winced. “Cold. And. Cheap. Really emphasizes the ‘budget’ in ‘budget dungeon.’”

The blue tail hung limply against his thigh. He fastened the rainbow bandana around his neck. The glitter gel he smeared a stripe of across one cheekbone. It looked like a sad metallic scar.

Pip approached him. Without a word, she reached out and adjusted the bandana, her small fingers deft. She fluffed the synthetic tail a little. Her touch was clinical, professional. “It is a. Start. The attempt matters. It signals you are trying to obey the forms. It may grant you a moment’s hesitation before a challenge.”

“A moment’s hesitation,” Zonk repeated. He felt like he was floating a foot above his own body, watching this happen to someone else. “Great. So we get to hear the philosophy behind our beating. Progress.”

He handed Blaze the lopsided ears. Blaze put them on. They were too small, perched high on his head. With the glitter stripe, he looked like a deranged, interspecies pirate.

Pip looked at them both. Her earnest face performed a complicated series of micro-expressions—appraisal, dismay, a fierce, suppressed pity. She finally settled on a strained smile. “You are. Very brave.”

“We’re very something,” Blaze said. He picked up the purple hoof-covers. “Do I have to?”

“Your hands are wrong,” Pip said gently. “The shape is all angles. These will soften the silhouette.”

Blaze pulled them on. They were basically mittens with a thumb hole, the purple fleece already pilling. He flexed his fingers. “I can’t feel anything.”

“You are not meant to feel. You are meant to be seen.”

Zonk’s own hands felt naked, exposed. He had no hoof-covers. He shoved them into his jacket pockets. The vinyl ear on his head chose that moment to finally give up, flopping forward to dangle in front of his eye. He left it. Maybe it added to the look. The look of a man who had given up on everything, including basic motor function.

Pip smoothed down her own bodysuit, which was at least the right general aesthetic, even if it was now alley-filthy. She was their benchmark of normalcy. A terrifying thought.

“The Night Market,” she said, turning toward the pool of darkness at the end of the alley. “It moves. But there is a stable entrance not far. A place where the barrier is. Thin. Where the rules of your world and mine blur. It will feel wrong. Do not react.”

“What’s it disguised as?” Zonk asked.

“A karaoke bar. It is called ‘The Gilded Pony.’”

Of course it was.

They moved down the alley, their footsteps making wet, shuffling sounds. The hoof-covers made Blaze’s walk into an awkward shuffle. The dangling vinyl ear bobbed in Zonk’s vision with every step. He focused on the rhythm of it, the stupid, pendulous swing, because focusing on the fact that they were walking toward a magic karaoke portal to a BDSM pony underworld was a one-way ticket to a screaming fit.

The alley turned, narrowing. The brick walls closed in, smelling of moss and urine. The orange light faded, replaced by a deep, velvet-blue darkness that didn’t feel entirely natural. It was the blue of a deep bruise, of the sky just after the sun had vanished but before the stars dared appear.

A door appeared in the wall. It was unmarked, painted a chipped, cheerful pink. A single, flickering neon sign above it depicted a cartoon pony holding a microphone. The “G” in “Gilded” was dark.

Music leaked from behind the door—a tinny, synth-heavy pop song from at least three decades ago, the vocals muffled.

“Here,” Pip whispered. She took a deep breath, her small chest rising and falling. “Remember. You belong here. You chose to be here. Confidence is a posture. Assume it.”

Zonk’s mouth was a desert. He wanted a hit off his pipe so badly his teeth ached. He wanted to be back on his couch, with the dust and the bad pizza and the safe, pixelated unreality of a game. He looked at Blaze. His friend’s face was set in a grimace under the glitter, his eyes wide and white in the gloom. He gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod.

Ride or die, Zonk thought. The phrase had always been a joke before. Now it felt like the only law left that made any sense.

Pip reached for the door handle. It was shaped like a horseshoe.

She pushed.

The music swelled, a wave of saccharine sound and cheap beer smell. The light from inside was a riot of spinning colors—pinks, purples, electric blues—strobing across a small, crowded room. Silhouettes moved, some with obvious animal-ear outlines, others with tails that swished. A pony-themed mural on the far wall seemed to shimmer, the painted grass looking a little too much like real, shifting blades.

It was a karaoke bar. And it was something else entirely. The air tasted like ozone and candy.

Pip stepped through. She didn’t look back.

Blaze shuffled forward next, his purple hoof-hands held out slightly from his body like he was afraid to touch anything.

Zonk followed. The dangling ear brushed his cheek.

The door swung shut behind them with a final, soft click, sealing them in the neon-lit hum. The pop song ended. A new one began, a power ballad. Nobody was singing. The crowd just swayed, a sea of pastel and vinyl and watchful, glittering eyes that turned, slowly, toward the three new, poorly disguised arrivals at the door.
