The training chamber smelled of ozone and old leather, with a faint, unsettling undercurrent of cotton candy. The walls were polished obsidian, but someone had stuck glow-in-the-dark stars on them in childish constellations. Zonk stood in the middle of it all, holding a practice flogger that was an appalling shade of pink. The tails were tipped with tiny, jingling bells.

“This is a weapon of psychological warfare,” he announced to the empty room. The words back, thin and pathetic.

A door hissed open on the far side. A figure entered, moving with a liquid grace that made Zonk’s shoulders tense. She was tall, a pegasus with a coat the color of tarnished silver and a mane cut in severe, geometric lines. Her cutie mark was a pair of crossed needles. She wore a fitted black harness, utilitarian, not decorative. She didn’t look at him. She walked to a rack of implements and selected a simple, unadorned crop.

“You are Zonk.” It wasn’t a question. The words came out flat, bored.

“Last I checked.”

“I am Needlepoint. You will refer to me as Needle. Your partner and the little one are with other instructors. Princess Luna believes in specialized training.” She finally turned her head. Her eyes were the gray of a dead screen. “Your file says you have theoretical knowledge. Lore. Protocol. This is useless if your body is stupid.”

“My body’s got a PhD in being profoundly uncomfortable right now.”

Needle didn’t smile. She gestured with the crop toward a series of pastel-colored posts arranged in the center of the room. Each had a leather pad attached at roughly chest height, painted with a smiling cartoon pony face. “The Gala is a dance. A very specific, very dangerous dance. Every movement is a statement. A challenge. A submission. You will learn the steps. You will not speak unless given a cue. You will not meet the gaze of a superior unless invited. You will hold the Element you are assigned as if it were a part of your own nervous system. We begin with posture.”

Zonk looked at the smiling dummy faces. One of them had a chipped paint smile, giving it a deranged, lopsided leer. He focused on that one. It was easier than looking at Needle.

“Posture,” she repeated. “You are a noble of the Night Court. You do not slouch. You do not fidget. You are a controlled silhouette.” She moved behind him. He felt the air shift, then the sharp tap of the crop against the back of his knees. “Locked. But not rigid. There is a difference.”

He adjusted his stance. The suit Luna had provided was a masterpiece of tailoring, the midnight blue fabric whispering against his skin, but it felt like a costume from a play he’d never auditioned for. A thread of silver in the weave caught the light every time he murmured. He hated it.

“Your hands,” Needle said.

He looked down at them. They were holding the stupid pink flogger. His left thumb was already seeking out the familiar rough patch of skin near the nail.

“Stop that.” The crop tapped his wrist, not hard, but the surprise of it jolted up his arm. “Your hands are either in service to your implement, or they are still. They do not pick. They do not clutch. They do not reveal your anxiety to the entire Sun Court. Again.”

They drilled for what felt like hours. Posture. The angle of his chin. The way to hold the Element of Magic—the tiara—on a velvet cushion without looking like a waiter. The specific, rolling gait of a Night Court noble that was somehow both a swagger and a retreat. Needle was a merciless, silent critic. A tap here. A sharper correction there. She offered no praise, only a cessation of correction when he stumbled into something approximating correct.

His mind wandered, a desperate escape. The ozone smell reminded him of the old tube TV his dad had left on when he walked out, the static hiss filling the empty house for three days before the neighbors complained. The leather of the dummy pads was cracked in places, showing white foam underneath like exposed bone. He counted the glow-in-the-dark stars on the west wall. Twenty-seven. One was peeling at a corner.

“You are thinking,” Needle said, her voice cutting through his mental tally. “Stop. Your body must know these things without your mind’s permission. Your mind will be occupied with not dying.”

“Helpful.”

The crop whistled past his ear. He flinched, a full-body spasm he couldn’t control.

“That,” Needle said, the crop now pointing at his chest, “is what happens when you think. Your body betrays you. At the Gala, a flinch is a confession. A twitch is a provocation. You will be watched by creatures for whom micro-expressions are a language. You must be a closed book written in a forgotten alphabet.”

“I’m more of an open browser tab with thirty unread Wikipedia articles.”

“Silence.”

They moved on to the obstacle course. It was less of a course and more of a surrealist’s interpretation of a BDSM dungeon built by a colorblind clown. There were balance beams coated in something slick and glittery. Swinging padded bars meant to simulate the jostle of a crowd. A section where confetti fell from the ceiling while a recorded track of polite applause and tinkling music played, and he was supposed to navigate it without getting any on his suit or losing his hold on the practice tiara.

He failed. Repeatedly.

The confetti stuck to the sweat on his neck. He slipped on the glitter-beam, his ankle twisting in a way that sent a bright, familiar pain up his leg—the same pain from a skateboarding wipeout at fourteen. He dropped the cushion twice. The second time, Needle simply stared at the tiara where it lay on the polished floor, its star-shaped jewel winking under the lights.

“Your attachment to the outcome is a weakness,” she said, not moving to pick it up. “You are afraid of dropping it. So you clutch. So you drop it. You must be willing to let it shatter.”

“That’s the opposite of the mission,” Zonk grunted, bending to retrieve it. His knee popped.

“The mission is to use it. A tool held in a fist of fear is a brick. It is useless.” For the first time, she seemed to actually see him, her gray eyes scanning him from head to toe. “You are afraid of the commitment. The suit. The role. You think if you perform it badly, you have not truly agreed to it. This is a child’s logic.”

A hot flush crept up his neck. It wasn’t anger. It was the embarrassment of being seen so clearly by someone who didn’t care enough to look away. “I didn’t ask for any of this.”

“No one does.” She turned back to the rack. “Again. From the entrance simulation.”

As he trudged back to the starting mark, the door hissed open again. Blaze stumbled in, followed by a massive earth pony stallion with a brick-red coat and a cutie mark of a coiled whip. Blaze’s tunic was torn at the shoulder. A fresh, livid bruise was flowering on his jaw. His eyes were bright, not with pain, but with a frustrated, boiling energy.

“You good?” Zonk called over, the informality of the words a relief.

Blaze waved a hand, shaking his head. “Dude. You have no idea. My guy’s all about ‘controlled force application’ and ‘structural leverage.’ I tried to show him a basic hip throw.” He gestured to the bruise. “He showed me the floor. A lot.”

The red stallion grunted, a sound like stones grinding together. “The pup has enthusiasm. And the structural integrity of a noodle.” He nodded once at Needle. “We break for refueling.”

Needle gave a curt nod back. “One hour. Do not be late.” She left without another word, the door sealing behind her with a soft thump.

The stallion lumbered out a different door, leaving them alone in the candy-colored nightmare gym.

Blaze walked over, rolling his shoulder with a wince. He picked up the pink practice flogger Zonk had discarded and gave it an experimental swing. The bells jingled feebly. “So. Posture lessons?”

“And existential dread. They’re a package deal.” Zonk sank onto a low bench, the elegant suit protesting the movement. He plucked a piece of purple confetti from his sleeve. “This is insane. We’re learning to waltz while Celestia’s probably turning the city upside down looking for us.”

“Pip’s with some mare learning ‘courtly diction and decorative standing,’” Blaze said, sitting heavily beside him. He smelled of sweat and leather and that sharp, clean odor of the healing salve they used here. “She seemed kinda into it. Got that look she gets when someone’s explaining proper tea service.”

Zonk watched the dust motes swirl in a beam of light from a fake stained-glass window. The window depicted Luna raising the moon, but the artist had given her a disturbingly cheesy wink. “We’re training her for this. We’re putting her in a lavender dress and teaching her how to stand still and look pretty in the lion’s den.”

“We’re keeping her alive,” Blaze said, his voice losing its usual easy tone. It was low, simple. A statement of fact.

“By making her a better target?” The words came out sharper than he meant. The hot, nameless thing in his chest—the one that wasn’t quite panic wasn’t quite anger—squeezed tight. “We’re polishing the bait, man. Luna’s using us to poke her sister, and we’re using Pip as… what? Our mascot? Our alibi? I can’t tell what’s the con anymore.”

Blaze was quiet for a long moment. He spun the flogger by its handle, the bells making a sad, circular jingle. “You remember that peer counseling course I took? Back in community college?”

The non sequitur was so perfectly Blaze that Zonk almost laughed. “The one you said was just to meet girls?”

“Yeah. Well. First thing they teach you. You can’t make someone’s choices for them. Not if they’re grown. You can give ’em the info. You can show ’em the door. But you can’t… you can’t install safety. It’s not a software patch.” He stopped spinning the flogger. “Pip chose to come with us. She’s choosing to do this. Our job ain’t to wrap her in bubble wrap. It’s to make sure when she makes her choice, she’s got the best damn chance we can give her. Even if that chance is a stupid dance in a stupid suit.”

Zonk stared at his friend. The bruise on Blaze’s jaw was darkening. His knuckles were scraped raw. He looked like a brawler who’d lost a fight, but his eyes were clear, certain in a way Zonk’s thoughts never were.

“Since when are you the wise one?” Zonk muttered, looking away.

“Since you started thinking with your fear instead of your big, weird brain.” Blaze stood up, groaning as he stretched. “C’mon. They said something about food in the commissary. I need to eat my body weight in whatever passes for carbs here before Needle Pointy comes back to murder your posture.”

Zonk didn’t move. He looked at his hands, resting on the fine blue fabric of his thighs. No picking. No clutching. Just there. He willed them to be still. For a second, they obeyed.

The confetti on the floor looked like a toxic bloom. The deranged, smiling dummy watched him from across the room. Somewhere, Pip was learning how to stand. Somewhere, Celestia was hunting them. And here he was, in a suit that cost more than his first car, afraid of a piece of plastic jewelry.

He pushed himself to his feet. The suit said its disapproval. His ankle throbbed.

“Yeah,” he said. “Let’s get carbs.”

The alley smelled of stale sugar and wet cardboard. A pink, viscous liquid dripped from a broken pipe overhead, splattering into a puddle that reflected the neon sign of a club called “The Prancing Mare” in wobbly, nauseating letters. Zonk leaned against a wall that was sticky with something he didn’t want to identify, the fine fabric of his borrowed suit a stark, stupid contrast to the grime. Pip stood a few feet away, her nose wrinkled. She was holding a small paper sack containing the “supplies” Luna had sent them for: a tin of polishing wax for ceremonial bits, a vial of “essence of nightshade” that was probably just cheap perfume, and three blank scrolls of vellum that cost more than Zonk’s last electric bill.

“It’s quieter here,” Pip said, not looking at him. She was staring at a poster plastered to the opposite wall, peeling at the corners. It advertised a “Friendship Drive” at the local community center. The cartoon ponies on it had aggressively cheerful smiles. One of them had a speech bubble that read, “Your Contract is your Shield!”

“Quiet’s worse,” Zonk muttered. The silence in the training chamber had been a pressurized thing, full of Needle’s disapproval. This silence was different. It was the hollow, waiting kind. It reminded him of the empty minutes after his dad’s car had pulled away for the last time, the engine noise fading down the street until there was nothing left but the hum of the refrigerator and the certainty that he’d said the wrong thing. He picked at his thumb. The rough patch of skin was raw.

Blaze was at the mouth of the alley, a broad silhouette against the garish street. He’d been pacing for five minutes, a restless energy coming off him in waves. They’d passed a group of rough-looking pegasi on the way back, their flight jackets patched and their wings preened to sharp, aggressive points. Blaze had tensed up, his shoulders squaring, his hands curling into loose fists at his sides. Zonk had seen the look in his eyes—a familiar, brittle challenge. Prove it.

“We should go,” Zonk called, his voice too loud in the narrow space.

“In a sec,” Blaze said, not turning around. “Just… scouting.”

“We’re not scouts. We’re errand boys in fancy dress. The scouting is done.” The words came out sharper than he meant. The suit itched. The memory of Needle’s crop tapping the back of his knees was a ghost sensation on his skin.

Blaze finally turned. The neon lit half his face in electric blue, the other half in shadow. “You hear that?”

Zonk listened. Distant music from the club. The drip-drip of the pink goo. A low, rhythmic thumping from somewhere above, like someone beating a rug. Or a body. “Hear what?”

“Exactly.” Blaze cracked his knuckles, one after the other. A loud, deliberate pop in the quiet. “It’s too clear. Like they’ve cleared the street.”

“Maybe they’re all at the Friendship Drive.” Pip’s voice was small. She was still looking at the poster, her head tilted. “The happy pony on the left. His smile is wrong. The lines don’t meet at the corners. It’s a practiced smile.”

Zonk stared at her. That was the kind of thing he noticed. The useless, obsessive detail. He felt a weird, sideways lurch in his chest—not quite recognition, not quite pity. Something between the two that felt like a stone in his shoe.

“It’s a trap,” Blaze said, with the grim satisfaction of someone whose paranoia was about to be validated. “They’re herding us. Luna’s people, Celestia’s goons, one of the stupid syndicates. They’re all out there, and they’re funneling us somewhere.”

“So we stay in the alley that smells like diabetic vomit? Solid plan.”

“We don’t stay.” Blaze took a step back toward them, his eyes scanning the rooftops. “We move fast. We use the service cuts Luna’s guy marked. We get back to the bolt-hole. Simple.”

“Nothing’s simple.” Zonk pushed off the wall. The sticky residue pulled at the fine wool of his sleeve with a faint tearing sound. “You’re itching for a fight, dude. I can smell it on you. It’s mixing with the alley stink and it’s a bad perfume.”

Blaze’s jaw tightened. “I’m not itching. I’m ready. There’s a difference. You’re the one who’s all…” He made a vague, fluttering gesture with his hand. “Twitchy. Overthinking.”

“Someone has to!”

“And someone has to do something!”

Their voices bounced off the close walls. Pip flinched, taking a small step back, the paper sack crinkling in her grip. The movement was subtle, but Zonk caught it. A retreat. He saw the exact moment Blaze saw it too—a flicker of something like shame in his eyes, quickly buried under a fresh layer of frustration.

“Look,” Blaze said, forcing his voice lower. “I’m just saying. We can’t let them push us around. We gotta show we’re not just… prizes in a box. We got the Elements. We got skills.”

“You have theoretical knowledge of pony lore, and I can tie a decent single-column tie,” Zonk said flatly. “We’re a threat to nobody except the structural integrity of a couch.”

Silence.

Then Blaze said, “I took a peer counseling course.”

Zonk blinked. “You what?”

Blaze looked away, his neck flushing a dark red above his collar. “Back in community college. Before I dropped out. It was a thing. Conflict resolution. Active listening. I got a certificate.”

The absurdity of it hung in the air, a glittering, fragile bubble. Peer counseling. Here, in a candy-colored hell-alley. Zonk felt a laugh clawing at his throat, but it got stuck behind something else—a sudden, sharp ache. Blaze had never mentioned it. All these years, and he’d never mentioned it.

“Okay,” Zonk said, the word coming out soft. “Okay. So you’re a certified listener. Listen to me now. Let’s just get the wax and the night-essence and the stupid expensive paper back to the scary princess and live another hour.”

All three of them froze. The alley was suddenly, profoundly silent. Even the drip from the pipe seemed to pause.

Then a voice, smooth as oiled leather, drifted down from the rooftop. “Aw. And here I was hoping for a more spirited debate.”

A pegasus dropped into the alley, landing with a soft pat that didn’t match the bulk of him. He was big, his coat a dull, stormy gray, his cropped mane the color of dried blood. His flight jacket was open, showing a harness strapped over a muscular chest. His cutie mark was a cloud with three lightning bolts slashing through it. Two more pegasi landed behind him, blocking the alley’s far end. They moved in unison, a practiced pincer.

“Rainbow Dash’s crew, I presume?” Zonk said, his mouth dry. He shifted, putting himself slightly in front of Pip. The motion felt theatrical, useless.

“Dash’s retrieval specialists,” the lead pegasus corrected. He smiled. It didn’t reach his eyes, which were the flat yellow of old streetlights. “You have items that don’t belong to you. You’re causing price fluctuations in the contractual magic market. It’s bad for business.”

“We’re on a diplomatic errand for the Night Court,” Zonk tried, his voice straining for a haughtiness he didn’t feel.

“Princess Luna’s credit is… shaky at the moment.” The pegasus took a step forward. His wings gave a single, powerful snap, stirring the foul air. “We’ll take the package. And the little trainee. Dash likes to collect interesting things.”

Blaze moved before Zonk could process the words. He didn’t shout didn’t posture. He just charged, low and fast, aiming to tackle the lead pegasus around the middle. It was a classic, earthbound, dumb-as-rocks move. It should have worked.

The pegasus didn’t dodge. He dropped into a crouch and his wings snapped out again, not to fly, but to beat the air in a short, violent burst. The air in the alley thickened. It was like trying to run through wet cement. Blaze’s charge turned into a stumble, his momentum stolen. One of the other pegasi darted in, a blur of motion, and struck Blaze across the back of the knees with a telescoping baton. It made a sound like a branch snapping.

Blaze went down hard, gasping.

“No magic!” the lead pegasus sang, his voice cheerful now. “Just loyalty. My guys? They’re loyal. Means they move as one. You’re just one guy. One guy is easy.”

Zonk’s body went cold. He saw Blaze try to push himself up, his arms trembling. Saw the second pegasus plant a hoof on his back, shoving him down into the filth. The paper sack fell from Pip’s hands, the tin of wax rolling away with a hollow clatter.

“Stop,” Zonk heard himself say. The word was a whisper.

The lead pegasus ignored him, advancing on Pip. “Come on, filly. Time to go back to school.”

Something hot moved through Zonk’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was a useless, boiling feeling. He had a pink flogger with bells back in the training room. He had a suit. He had a brain full of trivia and a thumb he’d picked bloody.

He took a step forward anyway.

A shadow fell across the alley mouth. Not a pegasus. Tall, slender, a silhouette in a dress of lavender and silver that seemed to gather what little light there was and hold it close. Pip’s dress. The one from the box.

Pip stood there, her small frame trembling, but her chin was lifted. She wasn’t looking at the pegasi. She was looking at their leader’s harness. Specifically, at the central buckle, which was shaped like a stylized cloud.

“Your fastening is incorrect,” she said, her voice clear and precise cutting through the tense air like glass.

The pegasus stopped. He blinked. “What?”

“The central buckle. On a Storm Squadron harness, the third strap is to be fed under the second, not over. It’s a safety protocol. For high-altitude dives.” She took a tiny, shaking step forward. “Yours is over. It’s sloppy. Rainbow Dash would be very disappointed.”

The pegasus looked down, genuine confusion on his face. It was a second. Less than a second.

Blaze moved. He didn’t try to get up. He twisted, grabbing the ankle of the pegasus standing on him, and yanked with all his weight. The pegasus squawked, off-balance, and Blaze surged upward, driving a shoulder into his gut.

The lead pegasus snarled, turning from Pip back to the threat. “Enough!”

Zonk didn’t think. He bent, snatched the rolling tin of wax, and threw it. It was a pathetic projectile. It hit the lead pegasus in the side of the head with a dull bonk and fell to the ground.

It was enough. The pegasus flinched, his wing-beat faltering for an instant. The thickened air wavered.

Blaze was on his feet, breathing raggedly. He didn’t charge again. He backed up, putting himself between the pegasi and Zonk and Pip, his hands up in a guard position he must have learned somewhere Zonk didn’t know about. “Run,” he grunted.

The lead pegasus rubbed his head, his yellow eyes gleaming with cold fury. “Cute.” He gestured to his companions. “Take the big one. The other two can scatter. We’ll find them.”

The two flanking pegasi shot forward. This time, there was no fancy air magic. This was just speed and numbers. One went high, the other low. Blaze tried to block the first strike took a baton hit to the ribs that made him cough, and the second pegasus swept his legs out from under him again. They were on him in a heartbeat, pinning his arms, hauling him upright. He struggled, a raw, wordless sound tearing from his throat.

Zonk took a step toward them. The lead pegasus turned that flat yellow gaze on him. “Don’t,” he said softly.

Zonk stopped. His heart hammered against his ribs, a frantic bird in a too-small cage. He looked at Blaze held tight, his face a mask of pain and fury. He looked at Pip, standing frozen in her ridiculous, beautiful dress.

“Zonk!” Blaze shouted, and it wasn’t a plea, it was an order. An old, familiar order. Get out.

The pegasi lifted off, their wings beating hard, dragging Blaze up with them into the neon-streaked dark. He kicked once, a final, futile protest, and then they were gone over the rooftops, the sound of their flight swallowed by the distant thump of club music.

The alley was quiet again. Just the drip. Just the sticky wall. Just the paper sack lying torn on the ground.

Pip let out a small, choked sound. She sank to her knees, the layers of her dress pooling around her in the pink puddle.

Zonk didn’t move. He stared at the spot where Blaze had been. The cold in his body had turned to a solid, heavy thing, a lump of ice in his gut. He’d watched. He’d thrown a tin of wax.

Overhead, a single, glowing star—one of the fake ones from the training room maybe—peeled free from a rooftop and drifted down, spinning lazily, before it went dark and landed in the filth with a silent, final plop.

The corridor back to Luna’s solarum was too quiet. Zonk’s boots—stupid, silver-fastened things—made no sound on the deep violet carpet. Pip’s lavender dress rustled with every step, a sound like leaves in a gentle wind, which was insane because they were underground and the air was dead still. He kept waiting for her to say something, to ask a question he couldn’t answer, but she just walked beside him, one hand lightly touching the wall as if for balance. Her other hand clutched the velvet pouch containing her assigned Element, the yellow jar of salve. She held it like a baby bird.

His own cushion with the tiara felt like it weighed forty pounds. The silver threads in his suit itched. A single, pointless thought circled his brain: the carpet pile was exactly the height of the grass in the front yard of the house he’d lived in when he was nine. He could remember the specific resistance under his sneakers. That was the summer before his mom left. The memory had no business being here. He shoved it down. It bobbed right back up.

The door to the solarum was already open. Inside, the false stars glimmered in the domed ceiling. Luna stood before a large, dark table that was definitely a repurposed spanking bench, maps and star charts pinned down by what looked like leather cuffs. She didn’t turn.

“Close the door.”

Zonk nudged it shut with his elbow. The click was final.

“Your training was cut short.” Luna’s voice was flat. She finally turned, her eyes moving from Zonk’s face to Pip’s, then to the empty space beside them. “Where is your compatriot?”

“That’s the thing.” Zonk’s mouth was dry. He set the cushion down on a nearby stool shaped like a kneeling pony. “We don’t know. Needlepoint said he was with another instructor. We finished early. He wasn’t at the rendezvous point.”

“He is not in the Night Quarter.” Luna picked up a slender data-crystal from the table. It glowed a soft, poisonous green in her hand. “My scouts report a disturbance near the Aerie Markets. A physical altercation involving a large human male and several members of the Dash Syndicate’s courier wing.”

A cold knot tightened under Zonk’s ribs. Not fear—Something else. A hot, sour feeling that made his scalp prickle. “Blaze.”

“He engaged them.” Luna’s tone suggested this was a uniquely stupid form of suicide, like juggling lit dynamite in a gunpowder factory. “Witnesses described him attempting a ‘citizen’s arrest’ on a pegasus accused of pickpocketing. A noble, if misguided, impulse. The Dash enforcers operate on pack loyalty magic. He was outnumbered five to one in the air. They dropped him with tactical bolas weighted with loyalty-binding charms. He ceased struggling almost immediately.”

“Is he dead?” The question came out of Zonk’s mouth before he could stop it, blunt and ugly.

“The Dash syndicate does not waste useful assets. They take trophies. A human, particularly one linked to the missing Elements is a significant trophy. He is alive. For now.” She placed the crystal down. “They will attempt to leverage him. To draw you out. Or to sell him to Celestia for a favor. The bidding has likely already begun.”

Pip made a small, choked sound. Zonk didn’t look at her. He stared at a whorl in the wood of the spanking-bench table. It looked like a screaming face. He focused on that.

“We have to get him back.” He heard himself say it. The words felt like someone else’s.

“‘We’?” Luna arched one perfect eyebrow. “Your assignment was to learn the Gala protocols. To infiltrate. The plan did not include a mid-act rescue mission for a captured comrade. It complicates the narrative.”

“Fuck the narrative.” The heat in his chest had a voice now, sharp and brittle. “He’s my friend.”

“A sentiment.” Luna waved a hoof, dismissing it like a gnat. “Sentiment is a luxury you cannot afford. The Gala is in forty-eight hours. The window to return the Elements and re-stabilize the barrier is closing. Your attachment endangers the primary objective.”

Zonk’s hands were fists at his sides. The fine fabric of the suit sleeves pulled tight across his knuckles. He forced them to relax. A useless, theatrical gesture. She saw it all anyway. “What’s the plan, then? We just let Rainbow Dash have him? Go to the party and hope they serve canapés while my best friend gets turned into someone’s loyal footstool?”

“The plan,” Luna said, moving around the table toward him, “adapts. The Dash syndicate’s stronghold is the Cloudstone Vault. A floating platform anchored by loyalty-bound weather magic. Impregnable to a frontal assault. Fortunately, I do not deal in frontal assaults.” She stopped a few feet away. She smelled of cold stone and night-blooming jasmine. “They will be distracted. The entire underworld is distracted by the hunt for you. The Gala is the greatest distraction of all. While Celestia’s court is occupied with pageantry, a small, skilled team can infiltrate the Vault.”

“A team.” Zonk’s brain, always three steps ahead in the worst direction was already connecting dots. “You mean us. You mean me.”

“The little one stays here. She is not trained for infiltration. You, however have just spent hours learning to move like Night Court nobility. To hold your silence. To observe.” Her moonlit gaze was constant. “The Cloudstone Vault will be lightly guarded during the Gala. Most of Dash’s elite will be in attendance, currying favor. You will use the credentials I provide to access the service entrance. You will retrieve your friend. You will return before the final toast. Then you will fulfill your original purpose at the Gala.”

It was insane. It was a suicide run wrapped in a diplomatic nightmare. It was the kind of plan Blaze would have cheered for. Zonk felt a hysterical laugh bubbling in his throat. He swallowed it. It tasted like metal and regret. “And if I get caught?”

“Then you will have failed. The Elements will be lost. The barrier will collapse. Your friend will remain a trophy. And I will deny any knowledge of you.” She said it without malice. A simple statement of fact. “The risk is considerable. The alternative is to abandon him. The choice is yours.”

There was no choice. That was the hell of it. The realization didn’t arrive with a bang, just a quiet, sickening click in the back of his mind, like a lock sliding home. He couldn’t leave Blaze. The thought of trying—of sitting through a fancy party eating tiny food while Blaze was somewhere being broken—made the sour heat in his chest curdle into something solid and cold. He was stuck. He was attached. The very thing he’d spent his life avoiding had snuck up and clamped down on him, and now it was going to get him killed.

He looked at Pip. She was staring at him, her eyes wide, the jar of salve clutched to her chest. She gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod.

“Fine,” Zonk said. The word was ash in his mouth. “What’s the play?”

A faint, almost-smile touched Luna’s lips. It didn’t reach her eyes. “First, you change. Midnight blue is for court. For this, you need to disappear.” She gestured to a curtained alcove. “Black. Leather. Something with tactical give. It’s in the wardrobe. Second, you listen. Every detail matters. The loyalty magic that binds the Dash enforcers is contagious in close quarters. Prolonged eye contact can trigger a compulsion to join the pack. You must avoid their gaze. Third, the extraction point is a cargo gondola on the western anchor chain. It makes one run an hour. You have a fifteen-minute window.”

She began outlining routes, passcodes, guard rotations. Zonk tried to listen, but his mind kept snagging on irrelevant details. The way a stray beam of artificial starlight caught a dust mote floating near the ceiling. The precise, citrusy scent of the oil Luna used on her hoof polish. The faint, rhythmic squeak of a pulley somewhere in the distant walls, like a mouse trapped in the machinery.

His body knew what was coming. His stomach was a hard, cold ball. His fingers were numb. This was responsibility. This was caring. It wasn’t a warm feeling. It was a trapdoor opening under his feet.

“You will go alone,” Luna finished. “The little one remains under my protection. A guarantee of your return.”

Pip’s breath hitched. “But I can help, I—”

“You are a liability.” Luna’s voice cut through the protest like a blade. “Your training is in submission and service, not stealth and subterfuge. You will stay. You will be safe.”

Zonk looked from Luna’s implacable face to Pip’s stricken one. The hot-cold mess in his chest twisted again. He wasn’t a hero. He was a guy who was good at running away. Now he was volunteering to walk into a floating fortress because his friend was an idiot who tried to arrest a magical pony thief. The irony was so perfect it was almost funny.

Almost.

“Get the clothes,” he said, not to anyone in particular.

He walked to the alcove, pulling the curtain shut behind him. The space was small, lined with hanging garments. The black leather outfit was there, sleek and menacing. He started to unbutton the beautiful, useless midnight blue jacket. His hands didn’t shake. He wished they would. Shaking would feel like something. This was just a hollow, electric calm, the quiet before the storm you know is going to drown you.

Outside the curtain, he heard Luna speaking softly to Pip. A low, reassuring murmur. He didn’t catch the words. He didn’t want to.

He changed. The leather was cool against his skin. It fit perfectly, of course. It always did here. The costume was always perfect. The role was always waiting.

He took a deep breath. It didn’t help.

Then he pushed the curtain aside and stepped back out ready to be someone he didn’t know how to be.

The hallway outside the training chamber was too quiet, the kind of silence that felt like a held breath. Zonk leaned against the cool obsidian wall, the pink flogger dangling from his hand. His shoulders ached. A phantom sting lingered on the back of his knees where Needle’s crop had tapped, a correction burned into muscle memory. The suit, the ridiculous, beautiful suit was damp under the arms.

Blaze emerged from a door down the hall. He moved differently. Stiff. Purposeful. His gray tunic was rumpled, and a fresh, livid bruise bloomed across his right cheekbone, the color of a stormy twilight. He walked with a slight hitch in his step, like he was favoring his left side.

“You look like shit,” Zonk said.

“You look like a midnight-themed party favor.” Blaze stopped, cracked his neck. The sound was a dry pop in the silent hall. “My instructor was an earth pony mare who could bench-press a car. We did ‘close-quarters negotiation.’ Her negotiation technique involved a lot of walls. And my face.”

“Charming.”

“She kept calling it ‘applied kinetics.’” Blaze prodded the bruise and winced. “Where’s Pip?”

As if summoned, a third door opened it. Pip stepped out, followed by a willowy unicorn mare with a mane the color of mint leaves. Pip was wearing the lavender dress. It suited her in a way that made Zonk’s stomach twist. The layers of tulle floated around her, the silver thread catching the low light. She held herself differently too—spine straight, chin level, hands clasped loosely in front of her. The transformation was unsettling. She looked like a perfect, fragile doll. The unicorn gave a tiny, approving nod and glided away without a word.

Pip’s composed expression fractured when she saw them. Her eyes went wide. “Blaze! You’re hurt!”

“It’s nothing. A learning experience.” He tried for a grin. It looked more like a grimace. “You clean up good, Pip.”

She smoothed the dress, pleasure crossing her face before it was hesitated by anxiety. “Mistress Chamomile said I have a natural aptitude for passive presentation. I don’t… I don’t think that’s a compliment here.”

A door at the end of the hall swung inward. Luna stood there, a silhouette against the softer light of her antechamber. “The carriage awaits. We depart for Serenity Springs. Do not speak unless I cue you. Do not touch anything you are not explicitly permitted to touch. The proprietor, Fluttershy is… particular. Her domain is the Element of Kindness. You will understand the irony momentarily.”

The carriage was a closed black coach drawn by two bat-winged ponies whose coats blended into the shadows. The interior smelled of old velvet and cold stone. Zonk sat across from Luna, the tiara—the Element of Magic—resting on a black cushion in his lap. The cushion was softer than anything he’d ever touched. He focused on that, the nap of the fabric under his thumb, to avoid looking at Luna’s impassive face.

Blaze sat beside him, a solid, bruised presence. Pip was next to Luna, a splash of lavender in the dark. She stared out the window at the passing city, her reflection a pale ghost over the speeding streaks of light.

“Fluttershy’s syndicate controls wellness,” Luna said, her voice a low hum in the confined space. “Spas. Rehabilitation clinics. Herbal remedials. Information flows through places where the powerful go to be soothed. Her methods of extraction are non-violent. And therefore, entirely inescapable. You will be offered a treatment. You will accept. It is part of the dance.”

“What kind of treatment?” Blaze asked.

“The kind that makes you talk.” Luna didn’t elaborate.

Serenity Springs looked like a birthday cake that had been left out in the rain. The building was a confection of pale pink and butter yellow, with swooping, organic curves. A sign in looping script promised “Restorative Harmony for Body and Soul.” The air, even from the carriage steps was a thick blanket of lavender and something sharper beneath it, a chemical tang like hospital-grade antiseptic.

The lobby was a masterpiece of tranquil horror. The floors were honey-colored wood, polished to a mirror shine. The walls were soundproofed with pale fabric, studded with abstract art that, after a moment, resolved into stylized, weeping ponies. A waterfall feature trickled down one wall, the water making a sound that was almost, but not quite, a human whisper. The temperature was exactly skin temperature, which made it feel like the room was breathing on them.

A pegasus mare stood behind a crescent-shaped desk. Her coat was a soft butter yellow. Her mane, a long, silken pink cascade fell over one eye. She wore a simple smock the color of a spring leaf. She looked up as they entered, and her smile was so gentle it felt like a physical pressure.

“Princess Luna,” she said, her voice a feather-light murmur. “Welcome. Your suite is prepared. And these must be your guests from the… Distant court.” Her large, blue eyes swept over them. They lingered on Blaze’s bruise. A tiny, sympathetic frown touched her lips. “Oh, dear. We have just the poultice for that.”

“Our gratitude, Fluttershy,” Luna said, her tone formal, distant. “The standard preparatory package, if you please. We have a long evening ahead.”

“Of course. Always a pleasure to serve the Court.” Fluttershy’s gaze settled on Pip. “What a delicate bloom. The Gala will be quite a shock for one so tender. A calming infusion before would be wise. To steady the nerves.”

Pip took a half-step closer to Blaze.

Fluttershy glided from behind the desk. She moved without a sound. “Right this way. The Quiet Room is ready.”

She led them down a softly lit corridor. Doors lined the hall, each marked with a different, soothing symbol: a drifting cloud, a falling leaf, a closed eye. The air grew warmer, heavier with the scent of lavender and steam. From behind one door, Zonk heard a low, rhythmic hum, like a giant purring cat. From another, a soft, hiccupping sob that was cut off abruptly as they passed.

The Quiet Room was neither quiet nor a single room. It was a series of interconnected chambers, each bathed in a different colored light. The first was a pale green, filled with hanging ferns that brushed their shoulders as they passed. The next was a deep blue, with a pool of still, black water in the center. The third was amber, warm and dry, the floor covered in a fine, fragrant sand.

In the center of the amber chamber, three low tables awaited, each with a neatly folded white towel.

“Please, disrobe and lie down,” Fluttershy murmured. “Your garments will be cared for. The attendants will be in shortly. Remember, surrender is the first step toward true serenity.”

She melted back into the foliage, disappearing as if she’d never been there.

Luna, without a hint of self-consciousness began to unfasten her cape. “Do as she says. Refusal is not an option they entertain politely.”

Zonk’s fingers felt thick and stupid on the silver fastenings of his beautiful jacket. He shrugged it off, folded it over the back of a bamboo chair that looked like it would collapse under the weight. The shirt came next. The air in the room was dry, and it raised goosebumps on his skin. He kept his boxers on, lying face down on the padded table. The leather was warm, unnervingly body temperature.

To his left, Blaze grunted as he lowered himself onto his table. To his right, he heard the whisper of Pip’s dress falling, the soft, terrified intake of breath.

They lay there in the amber gloom. The sand on the floor smelled like cinnamon and dust. A tiny, iridescent beetle trudged a determined path near Zonk’s table leg. He watched it, counting its steps, following its futile, zigzag journey. Fourteen steps toward the wall. A pause. A change of direction. Twenty-three steps toward the pool room. His mind was a blank, buzzing static. The ozone-and-cotton-candy smell of the training chamber was gone, replaced by this cloying, herbal tomb.

The attendants did not enter through the door.

They emerged from the hanging ferns and the shadows behind the pool. Three of them. Earth ponies, their coats muted browns and grays, their eyes downcast. They moved with a silent, fluid efficiency that was more unnerving than Needlepoint’s military precision. One approached each table.

Zonk’s attendant was a stocky mare with a blunt-cut mane. Her hooves were wrapped in soft, white cloth. She placed her hands—strong, broad hands—on his shoulders. Her touch was warm. Heavy. It pushed him deeper into the table.

She began to knead the muscles of his back. Her thumbs found a knot between his shoulder blades he didn’t know he had and pressed. Hard. A sharp, bright pain lanced through him, followed by a deep, unwelcome wave of relief. He bit back a groan.

“You carry much tension,” the attendant murmured, her voice a monotone echo of Fluttershy’s whisper. “The weight of stolen things is heavy. It settles in the trapezius.”

He froze. Her hands didn’t stop their work, digging, pressing, releasing.

“I don’t know what you’re talking about,” he muttered into the face cradle.

“Of course not.” Her fingers traced the line of his spine. “The spine is the pillar. Yours is misaligned. A slight rotational defect. From looking over your shoulder, perhaps.”

Her touch moved lower, pressing into the small of his back. A different kind of tension lived there, coiled tight. Her pressure was inexorable. Something in his lower back gave a soft, internal pop. A flood of warmth spread through his hips. His thoughts, for a second turned to liquid, formless.

“There,” she hummed. “Better. The body does not lie. It holds every secret. Every fear. We simply… Listen.”

From Blaze’s table, there was a sharp hiss of breath. “Hey. Easy.”

“The tissue is bruised,” a second attendant’s voice soothed. “The trauma must be expressed. Breathe into the pain. Let it speak.”

Zonk turned his head, just enough to see. Blaze’s attendant, a lean stallion was working on the bruise on his cheek with two fingers, applying a circular pressure that made Blaze’s jaw clench. A thin, yellow ointment glistened on the skin.

Pip made no sound at all.

Zonk strained to see her. Her attendant, a mare with a face like a kind grandmother was gently stroking Pip’s mane, her fingers combing through the pink strands. The other hand rested, palm flat, on the center of Pip’s back, right over the brand. The sun symbol.

“Such a pretty mark,” the grandmother attendant crooned. “A school of high repute. Tell me, little one did they teach you about the old contracts? The ones before the sun? Or only the new harmonies?”

Pip’s voice was a thread. “I… I learned my protocols.”

“Protocols. Yes.” The hand on her back stroked in a slow circle. “But what of the heart? The heart is not a protocol. It flutters. It fears. It grows attached to strays.” The attendant’s eyes, soft and brown, lifted and met Zonk’s across the room. She smiled. It was the gentlest, most terrifying thing he had ever seen. “Doesn’t it?”

Luna’s voice cut through the amber haze, sharp as a shard of ice. “Our time is short, Fluttershy. The Gala waits.”

The attendants’ hands stilled. As one, they withdrew, fading back into the ferns and the shadows.

Fluttershy stood in the doorway to the green chamber, her head tilted. “So it does. A pity. The deep work takes time.” Her eyes rested on Pip, who was trembling, small and pale on the white table. “The little bloom seems chilled. The heart is so… exposed. Protect it, or the frost will claim it.”

She was gone.

Zonk pushed himself up, his body feeling loose, unstrung, betrayingly good. He fumbled for his clothes. His hands shook. It was just a massage. Just words. But the room felt like a throat, and they had just been stopped.

Luna was already dressed, her expression unreadable. “We go. Now.”

Blaze scrubbed the yellow ointment from his face. The bruise was already fading, the skin just a faint, sickly yellow. He looked spooked. “They knew things.”

“They know nothing,” Luna snapped, but the edge in She said it, new. A hairline crack in the porcelain. “They guess. They fish. It is what they do. Dress the child.”

Zonk helped Pip up. Her skin was cold. He handed her the dress turned his back while she slipped it on. His fingers brushed the silver fastenings of his own jacket, and he remembered the attendant’s words. The weight of stolen things.

From the deep blue chamber, near the black pool, a sound drifted out. Not a whisper from the water this time. A voice, familiar, strained, speaking in short, gasped phrases.

“… not a game… the node at Starlight… booth seven…”

Zonk’s blood turned to ice in his veins. He knew that voice. It was Blaze’s.

But Blaze was right beside him, buckling his tunic, his face pale under the fading bruise.

The voice from the blue room came again, weaker. “Zonk… get the tiara out…”

Luna’s hand clamped on Zonk’s arm. Her grip was iron. “Do not look. Do not acknowledge. It is not for you.”

She pulled him toward the exit, her pace leaving no room for argument. Blaze, confused, followed, pulling Pip along. Zonk glanced back once, into the dark mouth of the blue chamber. He saw nothing but the still, black water, and the faint, glinting outline of a figure suspended within it, a bubbling stream of silver escaping from its lips.
