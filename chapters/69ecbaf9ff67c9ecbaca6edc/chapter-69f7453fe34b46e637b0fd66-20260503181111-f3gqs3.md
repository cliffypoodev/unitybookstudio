The silence after their plan felt like a held breath in the apartment, thick and fragile. Zonk stared at the pile of junk on the coffee table. A silver tiara, a purple flogger with glittery tassels, a tongue depressor that looked like it belonged in a doctor’s office from a porno. Absurd. They were going to hide absurdity around the city. His thumb found the ragged skin beside his nail and worried it.

Across the room, Pip was trying to fold a blanket from the couch. She handled it with a solemn precision that suggested she’d been trained in linen care as a sacred art. The afternoon light cut through the dusty window and caught the fine, almost invisible down on her forearms. It made her look fuzzy, like a peach. That detail, the fuzz, lodged in his brain for no reason at all.

“Okay,” Blaze said, cracking his knuckles. The sound was a gunshot in the quiet. “I’ll take the east side. You got west. Meet back at the bridge in two hours if we don’t get murdered by pastel fascists.”

“We stole the foundation of reality, Zonk.”

“Allegedly.”

A thump from the hallway outside.

Not a knock. A thump. The solid, meaty sound of a shoulder testing a door.

All three of them froze. Pip’s hands stopped mid-fold, clutching the blanket to her chest. The fuzz on her arms stood up. Zonk’s stomach did a slow, cold roll, like something heavy and unpleasant had settled there.

Another thump. This one had more weight behind it. The cheap hollow-core door of their apartment vibrated in its frame. A flake of white paint drifted to the floor.

“Uh,” Blaze said. He was already moving, his body shifting from relaxed to coiled without a single wasted motion. He didn’t look scared. He looked like he’d been waiting for this. “Pip. Get behind the couch.”

“But the proper greeting protocol requires—”

“Now, Pip.”

His voice wasn’t loud. It was low, flat, a command that brooked zero debate. Pip scurried, blanket and all, ducking behind the stained corduroy sofa. Zonk was still holding the tiara. He looked at it, then at the door, then at Blaze, who was positioning himself to the side of the entrance, back to the wall. The analytical part of his brain, the part that had memorized guard patrol patterns in a hundred games, kicked in. They wouldn’t knock. They’d breach. Standard door-breaching protocol was two hits: one to test, one to break. The next hit would be the one.

The next hit was the one.

The door didn’t so much open as it ceased to be a door. It exploded inward in a shower of splintered wood and shattered deadbolt, the frame tearing loose from the drywall with a shriek of tortured nails. Two figures filled the doorway.

They were huge. Not just tall, but broad shoulders straining against plastic-looking armor painted in garish candied colors—sunset orange and lemon yellow. The armor was stylized with crude sunbursts and curlicues, the kind of thing a kindergarten class might design for a play. It looked cheap. Flimsy. The effect was profoundly wrong. A child’s toy come to life with the physical presence of a wrecking ball.

The one in front took a step into the apartment. His head swiveled, the painted eyes scanning the room. They landed on Zonk, on the tiara in his hand.

A voice issued from the helmet, distorted and buzzing, like a bad PA system at a county fair. “Aesthetic deviant. In possession of a Stolen Harmony. Compliance is mandatory.”

“Hey fellas,” Zonk heard himself say. The way he said it was too high, tinged with a laugh he didn’t feel. “Wrong apartment. We ordered the pizza.”

The guard didn’t react. He took another step, his partner moving in behind him to flank. Their movements were synchronized, practiced. Zonk’s gaming brain supplied the pattern: Tank and Controller. Tank advances, Controller covers angles and prepares crowd control. He’d seen this a thousand times. Knowing it didn’t make the fear any less liquid in his veins.

“Surrender the Harmony and the deviant,” the Tank buzzed. “Submission will be noted. Resistance will be met with corrective friendship.”

From behind the couch, a small voice piped up. “Honored Solar Sentinels! I am Pip Squeak, companion-in-training under the Sun Court’s auspices!”

The Tank’s head snapped toward the sound of her voice. The painted smile seemed to tighten. “Unauthorized vocalization. Additional correction required.”

He lunged, not for Zonk, but toward the couch. He was fast. Too fast for something that size.

Blaze moved faster.

He didn’t shout. He just came off the wall like a sprung trap, driving his shoulder into the Tank’s side as he passed. It wasn’t a clean hit—the armor took the brunt—but it knocked the guard off his line sent him stumbling into the television stand. The old CRT TV rocked, the glass bowl Zonk used as an ashtray tipping over and scattering gray flakes across the carpet.

The Controller turned, a baton extending from his forearm with a sharp shink sound. It was rainbow-colored. Of course it was.

“Pattern Delta!” Zonk yelled, the old gaming call flashing into his mind.

Blaze didn’t question it. As the Controller advanced on Zonk, baton raised, Blaze grabbed the floor lamp next to the ruined door. It was a cheap, rickety thing. He swung it like a baseball bat.

The Controller, programmed to focus on the primary threat—the one with the Harmony—didn’t adjust in time. The lamp base connected with the back of his knee, the one spot the stylized armor didn’t cover. The guard grunted, a human sound of surprise and pain, and went down on one knee.

“Oh, come on,” Zonk whispered.

“Window!” Blaze shouted.

The fire escape. It was behind Zonk, past the kitchenette. A rusted metal skeleton clinging to the building’s brick face. Getting to it meant getting past both guards.

His foot caught the Tank’s overextended ankle. The guard’s momentum did the rest. He crashed forward, smashing face-first into the edge of the coffee table. The plastic helmet cracked. The cheerful smile split down the middle.

She scrambled out from behind the couch, eyes wide. Blaze was already at the window, wrestling with the rusted latch. It shrieked in protest, then gave. Cold afternoon air flooded the room, smelling of exhaust and distant rain.

No one was in the apartment. The Elements were scattered, one kept. The plan was in motion.

They were also homeless, weaponless, and being hunted by smiling men with rainbow batons.

The Tank, still gripping Blaze, backhanded her.

Something hot and sour flooded Zonk’s throat. Not anger. Not fear. It was the taste of the copper penny he’d swallowed on a dare in fourth grade, sitting heavy and wrong in his gut.

Blaze saw Pip fall. A sound came out of him—not a word, a raw exhale of pure fury. He stopped trying to pull his arm free. He stepped into the Tank’s space brought his forehead down on the smiling pony snout of the helmet.

The plastic crunched. The Tank reeled back, his grip loosening. Blaze yanked his arm free, skin already purpling with the imprint of segmented fingers.

The Controller moved. Finally. He didn’t rush. He gave it a sharp snap. The air cracked, and the rope stiffened into a glowing, rigid bar.

Crowd-control weapon. Energy lash. Minimal range. Telegraphed wind-up.

“Blaze, duck left!” Zonk yelled.

Blaze ducked left. The rainbow bar whistled through the space where his head had been and connected with the standing lamp. The lamp exploded in a shower of sparks and cheap porcelain. The room stank of ozone and burnt dust.

The Tank shook his head, the dented helmet making his movements lopsided. He focused on Blaze again, advancing.

Zonk’s eyes darted. The apartment was a death trap. No room to maneuver. The Controller was blocking the only door. The fire escape was out the bedroom window, past the wrecked doorway and both guards. A plan, they needed a plan. Plans were his job. His thumb was bleeding where he’d ripped the cuticle clean off.

Pip pushed herself up on her elbows. Her lip was split, a bright bead of blood welling against her pale skin. She looked from the advancing Tank to the Controller readying his lash, to Zonk standing frozen with a tiara. Her eyes, wide with pain and protocol, cleared for a second. She met Zonk’s gaze and gave a tiny, sharp shake of her head. No.

Then she did the last thing he expected.

She curled into a tight ball on the floor, hands over her head, and screamed. Not a scream of terror. It was a high, piercing, official-sounding shriek. “BREACH OF CONTRACT! UNAUTHORIZED CORRECTION ON A SUN COURT AFFILIATE! I INVOKE AUDIT PROTOCOL DELTA! AUDIT PROTOCOL DELTA!”

The two Sentinels froze.

The Tank halted his advance. The Controller lowered his glowing bar a fraction. Their painted faces turned toward the small, balled-up figure on the floor.

“What did she say?” the Tank’s distorted voice asked.

“Protocol Delta,” the Controller buzzed. There was a new note in the static—uncertainty. “An audit invocation. By a trainee.”

“She’s bluffing. Trainees can’t invoke.”

“But if she can. The paperwork. ”

They stood there for three full seconds. Three seconds of bureaucratic hesitation, born in a system so obsessed with rules it forgot how to break them efficiently.

It was all the opening Blaze needed. He didn’t look at Zonk. He just moved. He grabbed the edge of the overturned coffee table—a solid slab of cheap particle board and laminate—and charged the Controller.

The Controller saw him coming started to bring his lash up. Too late. Blaze used the table like a battering ram, driving it straight into the Sentinel’s chest. The Controller crashed back into the shattered doorframe with a crunch of armor and splintering wood. He slid down, the rainbow bar flickering and going limp in his hand.

The Tank roared—a real, human sound of rage bleeding through the helmet’s filter. He lunged for Blaze, who was off-balance from the charge.

“The window!” Zonk shouted. He didn’t think. He threw the tiara.

It wasn’t a good throw. It spun end over end and clanged off the Tank’s shoulder plate. But it made him flinch, turn his head for a half-second. Blaze dropped the table and scrambled toward the bedroom.

Zonk didn’t wait. He dove for Pip, hooked his hands under her arms, and hauled. She was lighter than he expected, all bone and tense muscle. “Can you run?”

She nodded, her breath hitching. The blood from her lip smeared across her chin.

The Tank was coming. Zonk half-dragged, half-pushed Pip toward the bedroom. Blaze was already at the window, wrestling with the rusty latch. The ancient paint screamed in protest.

A hand closed on the back of Zonk’s shirt. Fabric tore. Cold air hit his skin. He stumbled, Pip falling against him. He turned and saw the Tank, close enough to smell the plasticky scent of his armor and something underneath—sweat and old leather.

The Tank drew back a fist.

Zonk did the only thing he could think of. He reached into the pocket of his hoodie, pulled out the first thing his fingers touched—the silver tongue depressor, the Element of Honesty. He held it out like a tiny, pathetic sword.

“You forgot your receipt!” he yelled, his voice cracking.

The Tank hesitated. His helmet tilted. The painted eyes stared at the mundane medical implement.

The bedroom window screeched open. “Zonk! Now!”

Zonk shoved Pip ahead of him into the bedroom. He backed away, keeping the tongue depressor pointed at the Tank, who took one slow, confused step forward.

Then Zonk turned and ran. He vaulted the unmade bed, following Pip out onto the rusty grating of the fire escape. The cold afternoon air hit him like a slap. Blaze was already clattering down the metal stairs, two at a time. Pip went after him, her movements clumsy with shock.

Zonk glanced back through the window. The Tank stood in the bedroom doorway, watching them go. He made no move to follow. He just stood there, a huge, colorful statue of violence, his dented smile seeming almost thoughtful.

Then Zonk was moving, his boots slipping on the icy iron. The world narrowed to the rhythm of his pounding heart, the jarring impact of each step, and the distant, rising wail of a siren that didn’t sound like any police car he’d ever heard. It sounded like a chorus of off-key trumpets.

The impact was a wet, crunching sound that made Zonk’s teeth ache. Blaze’s shoulder met the Solar Sentinel’s side, right where the orange plastic armor flared over the ribs. The guard grunted, a human sound of surprise beneath the cheerful pony helmet, and stumbled into the coffee table. The table collapsed. The remaining Elements—the flogger, the tongue depressor—clattered to the floor.

Zonk didn’t think. He dropped the tiara, grabbed the flogger by its handle, and swung. It wasn’t a weapon. It was a silly thing with purple tassels. The weighted handle connected with the back of the second guard’s knee. The guard buckled, one knee hitting the carpet with a thud that shook a dust bunny loose from under the sofa. It skittered like a gray tumbleweed.

“Run!” Blaze roared. He wasn’t asking. He’d already hooked an arm around Pip, who was frozen in a crouch, her eyes wide and unblinking. He hauled her toward the shattered doorway. Zonk scooped up the tiara, his fingers slipping on the cool metal, and followed.

The hallway was a narrow, dim tunnel of peeling floral wallpaper. It smelled of old cabbage and cat piss, a smell so familiar Zonk usually filtered it out. Now it was sharp, acrid. Behind them, a roar of distorted fury. A crash as something heavy—the remains of their couch, probably—was thrown aside.

“Fire escape!” Zonk gasped. The words tasted like copper. His lungs were already burning.

Blaze was ahead, yanking open the grime-caked window at the hall’s end. The rusty latch screamed. Cold, damp air flooded in, carrying the distant wail of a siren that had nothing to do with them. Blaze shoved Pip through the opening onto the iron landing. She went without a sound, a pastel blue ghost swallowed by the gloom.

Zonk stumbled after her. His foot caught on the lip of the windowsill. He pitched forward, the tiara flying from his hand. It clanged against the iron grating and spun, coming to rest against the railing. The purple star stared up at the dirty sky.

A hand closed on the back of his hoodie. Blaze. He dragged Zonk the rest of the way out, then turned and slammed the window shut. It wouldn’t latch. The frame was warped. He braced his back against it, his broad shoulders blocking the rectangle of light from their destroyed apartment. His face was sheened with sweat, his breathing ragged but controlled.

“Down,” Blaze hissed.

The fire escape was a skeletal, trembling thing. Rust had eaten through the bolts in paisley patterns. Pip was one flight down, pressed against the brick wall, her arms wrapped around herself. She was shivering. Or vibrating. Zonk couldn’t tell.

He grabbed the tiara and scrambled down after her, the metal stairs groaning under his weight. His hand closed on a railing. A flake of rust came off in his palm, gritty and orange. He wiped it on his jeans.

Blaze followed, his descent slower, heavier. The entire structure swayed. When he reached their landing, he pointed to a dark alcove beneath the stairs, shielded from above by a giant, wheezing air-conditioning unit. Condensation dripped from its belly in a steady, cold plink-plink-plink onto a mound of blackened cigarette butts.

“In there. Don’t move.”

They crammed themselves into the space. It was a tight fit. Zonk’s back was against the cold brick. Pip was wedged between him and the AC unit, her shoulder digging into his ribs. Blaze crouched at the opening, a human barricade. The smell of mildew and stale water was overwhelming. Zonk could taste it.

From above, through the broken window came the sounds of systematic destruction. The smash of glass. The dry crack of splitting wood. A drawer being upended, its contents spilling. They were tearing the apartment apart. Looking. The thumps were methodical, brutal. Not rage. Procedure.

Pip made a small sound. A whimper, choked back. Zonk looked down. In the jagged shadow, her face was pale. She had her eyes squeezed shut, but a single tear had tracked through the dust on her cheek. It left a clean, shiny line. He stared at that line. It seemed important. A detail that meant nothing.

His own heart was a frantic bird trapped behind his sternum. His mouth was so dry his tongue stuck to the roof. Somatic, not cerebral. A fact. A dry mouth.

“They will find the absence of the other Harmonies,” Pip said, low, her voice frayed. “The dissonance will. It will anger them further. Corrective friendship protocols escalate with non-compliance.”

A heavier crash shook the building. Something big hit the floor. Their refrigerator maybe. Zonk had a sudden, vivid memory of the magnet on it—a stupid, smiling cartoon taco from a food truck that had long since gone out of business. He wondered if it was now shattered on the linoleum.

The noises stopped.

The silence was worse. It was a listening silence. Zonk held his breath. The plink-plink of the AC drip was suddenly deafening. He wanted to reach up and catch the drops, swallow them.

A shadow fell across the landing above. The cheerful, smiling muzzle of a guard helmet appeared at the railing, looking down. The painted eyes seemed to scan the zigzag of stairs. Zonk pressed himself deeper into the brick. Pip’s whole body went rigid against him. He could feel the fine tremor running through her. Or maybe that was him.

The guard leaned out further. Zonk saw his own reflection, distorted and tiny, in one blank plastic eye.

Then, from inside the apartment, the other guard’s buzzed voice: “No secondary egress signature. The deviants have fled the node. Contamination risk is elevated. Report to the Sun Court for tracking authorization.”

The helmet tilted. For a second, Zonk was certain they’d been seen. That the guard would simply step off the landing and drop down on them, a candy-colored avalanche.

The shadow pulled back. Heavy footsteps receded. The window scraped shut. A few moments later, the distant, muffled sound of their own apartment door being pulled closed—a useless, polite gesture on a frame that was now just splinters.

They didn’t move. A minute passed. Another. The city sounds seeped back in: a car horn, a shouted argument from a street over, the constant plink-plink.

Blaze let out a long, slow breath. It fogged in the cold air. “Okay,” he said, the word barely audible. “Okay. They’re gone.”

Pip slumped. The tension left her in a rush, and she sagged against Zonk. He had no choice but to put an arm around her shoulders to keep them both from tipping over. She was shockingly light. Her bodysuit was soft, but the material was cold. She buried her face against his chest, and her breathing hitched. She wasn’t crying. It was something quieter, more defeated. The shivers took her in waves.

Blaze looked at his hand as if noticing it for the first time. He flexed the fingers. “It’s nothing.” He nodded at the tiara, still clutched in Zonk’s other hand. “That’s the one we keep?”

“Yeah.”

“Gone.”

“My pipe. The good glass one.”

“I know.”

They hit the alley at a sprint. The ground was slick with something unidentifiable. Zonk’s foot nearly went out from under him. The alley was a tunnel of shadows, lit at the far end by the sickly yellow glow of a streetlight on the main road. Their apartment was on the third floor. He heard the heavy, rhythmic clang of armored boots on the fire escape behind them. They weren’t giving up.

“Left!” Blaze barked, and they peeled out of the alley onto the sidewalk.

The main street was a Tuesday-night ghost town. A few cars snapped by on wet asphalt. A guy in a hoodie was vomiting in a doorway. Nobody looked twice at three people running for their lives. This was that kind of neighborhood.

Zonk’s lungs burned. He wasn’t built for this. He was built for sitting. His brain, however was clicking, scanning the familiar blocks with a new, terrible overlay. Guard AI. Pursuit protocols. In the game sentinels had search patterns.

“They’ll cut us off at the bridge,” he gasped, the words tearing out of him. “Standard perimeter sweep. We can’t go straight.”

“Where then?” Blaze’s voice was steady, but his breath was coming hard too.

Pip was between them, keeping up, her face a mask of focused terror. She pointed a slender finger down a side street cluttered with dumpsters. “There! A place of many humming machines and steam! It smells of clean linen and static!”

The 24-hour Sudsy Heaven laundromat. It was a tomb of fluorescent lights and ancient, rumbling dryers. Zonk had never been inside. He’d just seen the sign, flickering and sad.

“It’s a box,” Blaze argued. “One way in, same way out.”

“It’s off their pattern!” Zonk shot back. The clanging from the fire escape had stopped. That was worse. It meant they were on the ground now, plotting their intercept. “They’ll expect us to keep moving. To head for open ground or a transit point. They won’t expect us to stop and do our fucking laundry!”

It was the stupidest logic he’d ever voiced. It made a terrible, game-theory kind of sense.

Blaze didn’t answer. He just changed direction, veering into the side street. They pounded past overflowing dumpsters, the stench of rotting food and wet cardboard thick in the air. The Sudsy Heaven glowed at the end of the block like a beacon of mundane safety.

The door was a heavy slab of glass and aluminum. Blaze hit it first, bursting into a wall of humid, perfumed air. The place was empty save for an old woman folding towels with monastic slowness in the far corner. She didn’t look up.

They stumbled into the aisle between two rows of churning washers. The sound was immense, a thunder of spinning drums and sloshing water. Zonk’s knees gave out and he leaned against a machine, the vibrations traveling up his spine. His hands were shaking. He stared at them, fascinated by the tremors. A drop of sweat fell from his nose and landed on the speckled linoleum floor, right on a piece of hardened pink gum.

Pip crouched behind a rolling laundry cart, peering back through the glass door. Her ears—were they actually twitching? He must have hit his head.

Blaze was at the other window, his back to the wall, chest heaving. “See anything?”

“No movement,” Pip murmured. “The street is quiet.”

The silence in here, under the mechanical thunder was deep. Zonk became aware of a dozen irrelevant details. A single, lonely sock was trapped in the gap between a dryer and the wall. The fluorescent light above them buzzed at a frequency that made his fillings ache. A faded poster on the wall advertised a fabric softener from 2003 with a grinning cartoon bear. The bear’s smile was chipped and peeling.

“Okay,” Blaze said, after a minute that felt like an hour. He slid down the wall to sit on the floor. “Okay. We’re not dead.”

“Yet,” Zonk added. He spoke quieter now, raw. “They’ll expand the search grid. They have our scent, or our. Aesthetic deviation signature, or whatever.”

“We need a plan that lasts longer than five minutes,” Blaze said. He pulled the yellow jar of salve from his hoodie pocket, stared at it, then shoved it back. “We can’t go home. They know where we live. They’ll be waiting.”

The reality of it landed on Zonk’s shoulders, a physical weight. His apartment. His shitty, messy, safe apartment. The headset cables snaked across the floor. The dust on the TV. Gone. Contaminated. A cold knot tightened in his gut, something sharper than fear. It was grief. For a couch. For a crappy apartment. For the before-time.

“The other Elements,” Pip said quietly. She had turned from the window, her big eyes moving between them. “You hid them. That was clever. Chaotic, but clever. It will confuse the tracking spells for a time.”

“Tracking spells,” Zonk repeated flatly.

“The Harmonies are bound to the Crown. The tiara. It calls to them. Spreading them creates. Dissonance.” She wrung her hands. “But they will find them. The Sun Court’s scribes are very thorough.”

“So we need to get rid of the tiara,” Blaze said. “The source.”

“We can’t,” Zonk said. The thought was a sudden, clear pool in the chaos of his mind. “It’s the master key, right? The. Control element. If we’re ever going to fix this, if we’re ever going to put this all back and get you home, we probably need it. Throwing it in the river just makes us clueless fugitives instead of fugitives with a potential bargaining chip.”

Blaze looked at him. Really looked at him. “You’re thinking about fixing it.”

“I’m thinking about not being chased by psychotic party-store mannequins for the rest of my life.”

“The rest of my life might be measured in hours, Zonk.”

A dryer finished its cycle with a loud metallic thunk. The old woman in the corner shuffled over, unloaded her clothes into a basket, and left without a glance in their direction. The bell on the door jingled. The silence after felt heavier.

Pip stood up slowly. She walked over to Zonk, her steps silent on the linoleum. She stopped in front of him, her head tilted. “You kept the Kindness,” she said, not looking at Blaze’s pocket. “You chose the one that heals. Not the one that commands, or binds, or reveals truth. The one that mends.”

Zonk couldn’t meet her gaze. He focused on the peeling cartoon bear over her shoulder. “Seemed practical.”

“It was,” she said. And then, quieter: “It was kind.”

Something hot moved through his chest—not quite shame, not quite panic, a squirmy, uncomfortable feeling that didn’t have a name. He deflected. “Don’t get a fucking cavity over it. We’re still screwed.”

“We are,” Blaze agreed, pushing himself to his feet. He cracked his neck. “But we’re screwed together. And we’re not in that apartment. So. Next move. We can’t stay here.”

Zonk knew he was right. The laundromat was a pause, not a destination. He pushed off from the vibrating washer. His body felt old, used up. “We need information. We need to understand what we’re in. Not Pip’s sunshine-and-lollipops version. The real version.”

Pip flinched.

“Where do we get that?” Blaze asked.

Zonk’s gaming brain, his lore-nerd brain offered the only answer it had. When you were lost in a hostile world, when the main quest had blown up in your face, you went to the seedy underbelly. You went to the place the guards didn’t control.

“The Night Court,” he said. The words felt dangerous just saying them. “Luna’s side. If Celestia’s guards are after us, her competition might. Not be.”

“Or they might hand us over for a reward,” Blaze countered.

“Or they might know how to send a pony-girl back where she came from.” Zonk looked at Pip. “You know where to find them? The. Shadowy places?”

Pip hugged herself. She looked small again, the brief moment of insight gone. “Such places are not for companions-in-training. They are spoken of in whispers. But. The whispers say there is a club. A place of loud music and flashing lights, deep in the iron canyons where the sun does not reach. The ponies there wear different colors. Darker colors.”

A club. Of course it was a club. Zonk almost laughed. It was always a goddamn club.

“Can you get us there?” Blaze asked.

Pip took a deep, shuddering breath. She nodded once. “I can try. The way is not safe.”

“Nothing is,” Zonk said, and the truth of it was a cold stone in his belly. “Lead on.”
