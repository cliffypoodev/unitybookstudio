The world came back in pieces.

First, the smell. Not the damp stone and ozone of the alley. This was lavender and sugar, aggressively clean, like a kindergarten after a bleach bombing. Then the light. A soft, rosy glow that made his closed eyelids feel pink. His body registered last. A dull, throbbing ache at the base of his skull, right where the violet rod had touched. He was on his side on a floor that was, inexplicably, carpeted.

Zonk opened his eyes.

The cell was a perfect cube of Pepto-Bismol pink. The walls weren’t flat—They curved gently into the floor and ceiling, no sharp corners. A repeating pattern of cheerful, smiling clouds was stamped into the material at eye level. In the center of the ceiling, a crystal chandelier hung, its dozens of pastel teardrops emitting the gentle light. It was utterly silent. The kind of silence that had weight, that pressed on the eardrums.

He tried to sit up. The motion sent a fresh wave of nausea through his gut, a sour tide that had nothing to do with the decor. He managed to get his back against one soft, rounded wall. The carpet was short and scratchy against his palms.

Pip.

She was curled in a fetal position against the opposite wall, three feet away. Her eyes were open, staring at nothing. The dust streak from her tear was still there, a dirty track on her cheek. One of the frilly sleeves of her bodysuit was torn at the shoulder. She didn’t look at him. She just breathed softly, quietly shallow and quick.

“Hey.” The way he said it was a dry croak. It sounded obscenely loud in the pink quiet.

She didn’t move.

“Pip. You okay?”

A tiny shake of her head. Not an answer. A refusal to engage. Her gaze was fixed on a point somewhere near the baseboard, where the pink wall met the pink floor in a seamless, sickening curve.

Zonk let his head thump back against the wall. The impact was muffled, unsatisfying. He closed his eyes again, but the pink glow seeped through his lids. His thumb was a ragged mess. He’d picked it raw during the chase, during the capture, a stupid, frantic tic. It throbbed in time with his headache. He could feel the sticky warmth of blood against his palm.

Blaze was gone.

The thought arrived not as a revelation, but as a cold, solid fact settling into his chest like a stone. Blaze was gone. Taken by Rainbow Dash’s crew. For what? Entertainment? Interrogation? Parts? The rules here were brutal and literal. A guy like Blaze—all muscle and heart and zero subtlety—wouldn’t last five minutes in a negotiation. He’d try to punch his way out. He’d fail. The image of Blaze, cuffed and defiant, being dragged into some neon-lit fighting pit, flashed behind Zonk’s eyes. He shoved it away. It came back, wearing different, worse details.

And Pip. He’d promised. Not out loud, but in that storage locker, in the way he’d stepped slightly in front of her when the guards came. A silent, stupid promise. I got this. He didn’t have it. He’d frozen. He’d let them take her, zap him, dump him in this candy-coated nightmare box. His knowledge, his precious, ironic lore had amounted to one clever trick with a safeword. A spark against a tidal wave.

He was, he understood with a clarity that felt like a drill to the temple, completely fucking useless.

The lavender-sugar smell was cloying. It coated the back of his throat. He remembered, for no reason at all, the air freshener his dad used to keep in the old Pontiac. Pine-tree shaped. It had dangled from the rearview mirror long after the scent faded, a brittle, brown skeleton of a thing. His dad would drive in silence for hours, the only sound the hum of tires on asphalt. Zonk would stare at that dead tree willing it to spin, to do something. It never did.

His bound hands twisted against the velvet restraints. They were tight, but not cutting. Designed for comfort, even in captivity. The ultimate aesthetic. He could probably work them loose given time. Then what? Pound on the pink door? It had no visible handle, no seam. Just a smooth, curved surface.

“They took the Elements.”

Pip’s voice was small, hollow. She hadn’t moved.

“Yeah,” Zonk said. The word was ash.

“The Loyalty cuffs. The Laughter gag. All of them.” She finally turned her head. Her eyes were huge, the bright blue dimmed to a muddy gray. “They’ll take them back to the Sun Vault. They’ll be relocked. The Grand Harmony will be restored.” She said it like a death sentence.

“Great. Mission accomplished. Everyone’s happy.” He meant it to sound bitter. It just sounded tired.

“No.” She pushed herself up to a sitting position, moving like an old woman. She winced, touching her torn sleeve. “The balance is still broken. You and your friend… you’re a dissonance. You stole them. You touched them. The ritual to re-sanctify requires… Purification.” She looked at him, and for the first time, he saw fear in her eyes that wasn’t just panic. It was deeper. Ceremonial. “Of the contaminants.”

A cold knot tightened in Zonk’s stomach. It had nothing to do with the room’s temperature. “Meaning us.”

“Meaning you.” She looked down at her hands, folded in her lap. “I am a citizen. A deviant, now. I will be… reconditioned.” She said the last word like it was a flavor she was trying to identify. “But you are extra-dimensional static. You disrupt the signal. The purification is thorough.”

He saw it then. Not a dungeon, not an execution. Something worse. A pastel re-education chamber. A smiling therapist with a crystal rod, rewiring his brain until he believed pink clouds were the pinnacle of interior design and his only purpose was to serve the Grand Solar Fucking Gala. Forever. His detachment, his ironic shield—They’d melt that first. They’d make him earnest. They’d make him grateful.

The nausea surged, hot and real. He paused against it.

“Blaze,” he said, more to himself than to her.

“His dissonance is strongest,” Pip said. “He wore the cuffs. He activated them. They will prioritize him.”

So Blaze wasn’t in a fight club. He was in a lab. Being taken apart to see what made him tick. Being made to tick correctly.

Zonk’s vision blurred at the edges. Not from tears. From a kind of white-noise panic, a static that filled his skull. He was going to die in a pink room. He was going to die wearing velvet cuffs, smelling of lavender, after failing everyone who’d ever been dumb enough to rely on him. His mom had left because he was a clingy kid. His dad had stayed because he was furniture. Blaze stuck around out of habit, or pity, or because Zonk was the one who always had the weed. And Pip… Pip was here because of his stupid, stoned curiosity.

He was a contaminant. That’s all he’d ever been.

A sound escaped him. A dry, choked thing that wasn’t a laugh or a sob.

Pip moved then. She shuffled across the short distance on her knees, the scratchy carpet whispering. She turned, presenting her back to him. “Your thumb,” she said.

“What?”

“It’s bleeding. I can feel… it’s upsetting the air. Here.” She wiggled her fingers behind her back. “The bindings. They’re a simple love-knot. For calming agitated prisoners. You can undo them if you know the counter-pressure.”

He stared at the nape of her neck, at the fine downy hairs there. “How do you know that?”

“I was trained to be a companion. To soothe. To… manage distress.” Her voice was flat. “The knot is under my left thumb. Push up and to the side.”

He fumbled, his own bound hands clumsy. His fingers found hers. Her skin was cool. He located the knot of velvet, a complex braid. He pushed up and to the side, as she’d said.

The bindings around her wrists fell away with a soft shush. She brought her hands around front, flexing her fingers. They were slender, delicate. Without a word, she turned and reached for his hands. Her touch was sure, clinical. In seconds, the velvet rope loosened and slithered to the floor.

He brought his hands around. His right thumb was a mess, the skin around the nail torn and bloody dotted with bits of carpet fiber.

Pip didn’t ask. She just took his hand in both of hers, her head bowed over it. She examined the damage, her brow furrowed. With a concentration that was almost absurd, she lifted the hem of her torn sleeve to her mouth, bit a small piece of the frilly fabric, and ripped it off with a sharp tug.

“This is unsanitary,” she said, as if to herself. “But aesthetic coherence is already compromised.”

She spat lightly onto the clean pink fabric—a tiny, precise gesture—and began, with immense care, to wipe the blood and grit from his thumb. The spit was faintly warm. The fabric was soft. She worked in silence, her touch feather-light, cleaning the broken skin.

Zonk watched her. The top of her head, the part in her pale hair. The absolute focus on this one, meaningless task. This was her training. To tend. To comfort. Even here, in a pink prison, awaiting reconditioning, her first instinct was to fix the small, stupid injury in front of her.

Something cracked open in his chest. Not hot. Not a surge. A slow, cold fissure, like ice giving way underfoot.

“Why are you doing that?” he asked. The words came out still rough.

She didn’t look up. “It’s damaged.”

“I know it’s damaged. It’s a thumbnail. Who cares?”

“You were picking at it,” she said, as if that explained everything. She finished cleaning and pressed the damp fabric against the worst of the weeping blood, holding it there. “It means you’re scared. The Old Texts say you tend to the scared thing. You don’t ask why.”

“The Old Texts are about to be pulp. We’re about to be brainwashed into liking chandeliers.”

Finally, she looked up. Her eyes were clear again, that strange, earnest blue. “Then we should be kind now. While we still know what it is.”

The fissure in his chest widened. He couldn’t breathe around it. This tiny, ridiculous creature, wiping his blood with her own spit, quoting dead scrolls about kindness while the world prepared to grind them into polite, pastel paste. She believed in it. She genuinely, stupidly believed that the act mattered, even if it was the last act.

And he realized, with a jolt that was almost physical, that he couldn’t let them take that from her. He couldn’t let them turn her into another smiling cloud on the wall. He couldn’t let them scrub Blaze’s loud, loyal, idiot heart clean.

The stone of fact in his chest dissolved, replaced by a different weight. Heavier. More terrible.

Responsibility.

Not a grand, heroic calling. A specific, awful one. Get them out. Find Blaze. Fix your mess.

It was impossible. It was the dumbest plan in the history of plans, conceived by a stoned loser in a pink box.

Pip lifted the fabric. The bleeding had slowed. She gave a small satisfied nod, a gesture so inherently her that it made his throat ache.

“Okay,” Zonk said. The word came out strange. Firm. “Okay, Pip.”

She looked at him, waiting.

He scanned the room again, not with despair, but with a frantic, new calculation. The pink walls. The soft door. The chandelier. His gaze fixed on the light fixture. It was the only object in the room. The only thing that wasn’t wall, floor, or ceiling. A crystal chandelier, in a holding cell. For ambiance.

A stupid, desperate idea began to form. It was built on game logic, on lore deep cuts, on the kind of absurd, niche knowledge that had gotten them into this. It would probably get them killed faster.

But it was a thing to do. A way to push against the pink.

“We’re getting out of here,” he said. “And then we’re getting Blaze back.”

Pip just blinked. Then a tiny, fragile smile touched her lips. It wasn’t hope. It was something simpler. Relief that he’d stopped picking his thumb. “Okay,” she said.

He pointed a trembling, blood-streaked finger at the glowing chandelier. “First, we need to pull that thing down.”

The lavender-sugar smell was starting to curdle in his sinuses, turning into something closer to the scent of a cheap candle left burning too long. Zonk’s thumb pulsed a steady, stupid rhythm against his palm. He watched a single drop of blood well up along the torn cuticle, hang for a second, then fall to stain the pink carpet a darker, rustier shade. It looked like a tiny, misplaced planet.

“Give me your hand.”

Pip had moved without him noticing. She was kneeling beside him now, her face too close. The hollow look was gone, replaced by a tight, focused expression he’d never seen on her before. It made her look older.

“It’s nothing,” he muttered, curling his fingers into a fist.

“It’s bleeding on the floor of a Sun Court holding cell. That is not ‘nothing.’” Her voice had no room for argument. It was flat, practical. She reached out and pried his hand open with a strength that surprised him. Her fingers were cool and dry. “They will note the stain. It will be logged as a contamination event. It gives them data.”

“Great. My blood’s a data point. Story of my life.”

She didn’t smile. She examined the shredded skin around his thumbnail, her head tilted. With a sharp, decisive motion, she ripped a long strip from the lacy white underskirt of her bodysuit. The fabric tore with a sound like a sigh.

“Hey, your—”

“It is already torn,” she said cutting him off. She began winding the strip around his thumb, her movements efficient, almost clinical. “This material is sterile. It is woven from moon-silk for medical submissives. It promotes clotting.”

He watched her work. Her brow was furrowed in concentration. She tied a neat, tight knot over the wound, then tucked the ends in. The bandage was absurd—a frilly, lace-trimmed cuff on his grimy thumb. It looked like something a Victorian child would wear.

“You know first aid.”

“I was trained in seven forms of service.” She didn’t look up. “Comfort. Conversation. Conflict de-escalation. Basic field medicine. Aesthetic maintenance.” She finally released his hand, sitting back on her heels. “They were preparing me for a high-value contract. A noble with specific… needs.”

The way she said it—needs—made the word sound heavy and cold. Zonk flexed his bandaged thumb. The pressure felt good. The pain receded to a dull, manageable throb.

“So all that… the wide eyes, the ‘golly gee’… that was training?”

“Some of it.” She looked down at her own hands, folded in her lap. The torn sleeve gaped, showing a pale, smooth shoulder. “The earnestness is real. The belief in the principle of Harmony is real. But the presentation… the degree of naivete… that was curated. A product designed for a specific market.” She met his eyes. “I am not as fragile as I look, Zonk. I am a commodity who failed to reach her shelf. That is a different kind of problem.”

The cold knot in his stomach tightened again. This was worse, somehow. The idea of her genuine hope being packaged, trimmed, and sold was more nauseating than if she’d been a total fake. It was like finding out a beautiful song was just a jingle written to sell soap.

“Why are you telling me this?”

“Because you keep looking at me like I am a glass figurine about to fall off a table.” She hugged her knees to her chest. “It is slowing you down. And we cannot afford to be slow.”

A laugh barked out of him, harsh and unexpected. It echoed in the pink cube. “Slow? Pip, we’re in a pink box. We’re not going anywhere. Blaze is gone. The Elements are gone. My one good idea was a safeword tap. We’re done.”

“No.”

“Yes.”

“You are wrong.” She said it with such simple conviction that he just stared at her. “You possess the one thing they have systematically removed from everypony in this dimension for generations. The one thing the Old Texts say is the actual source of magic.”

“What? A decent weed connection?”

“Unironic loyalty.”

The words hung in the sugary air. Zonk opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked away, at the smiling cloud pattern on the wall. One of the clouds had a tiny flaw in the stamp, a little bump that made it look like it had a wart.

“You don’t even know him,” he said finally, his voice low.

“I know what he did. He drew their attention so we could run. He did not negotiate. He did not calculate the odds. He saw a threat to his friend, and he acted.” She shifted, the carpet scratching against her suit. “In the Solar Court, that is diagnosed as a cognitive defect. A lack of strategic self-preservation. They would re-educate it out of him. But the Old Texts… They call it the first spark. The necessary ingredient. Not the ritual, not the contract. The stupid, reckless decision to care about someone else more than the rules.”

Zonk’s throat felt tight. He remembered the dead pine-tree air freshener, the endless silent car rides. Caring was the thing you didn’t do. It was the trapdoor that dropped you into a world of pain. His mom’s perfume, a ghost of jasmine in the hallway, and then an empty closet. His dad’s vacant stare at the television, a wall of static between them. You stayed light. You stayed ironic. You held everything at arm’s length so when it left, it didn’t take a piece of you with it.

“It’s not magic, Pip. It’s just being a dumbass.”

“Then why are you here?” she asked softly.

He blinked. “What?”

“You could have left me in the alley. When the Sentinels came, you could have run the other way. You did not. You tried to shield me. You failed, but you tried.” Her blue eyes were clear now, sharp. “That was not strategic. That was not in your… what do you call it? Your character sheet. That was a different protocol.”

He had no answer. The pink walls seemed to press closer. The lavender smell was definitely burnt now, with an acrid undertone like singed hair. He noticed a single, dark fleck in the ceiling crystal directly above him—a flaw, or a trapped bit of dust. He fixated on it.

“I froze,” he said, the words ash in his mouth.

“You are freezing now.” She didn’t move. “You are deciding that because you failed once, you must always fail. That is the lie the Court sells. Perfection or nothing. Flawless service or worthlessness. It is a poison that keeps everypony in their place.”

She spoke, the words like stones in a still pond. Ripples spread out, touching things he kept in dark, quiet corners. The B-minus on the math test that made his dad sigh and turn back to the paper. The girlfriend who said he never let her in, and was she wrong? The job he didn’t apply for because why bother, they’d pick someone better. The endless, comfortable retreat into the game, where the stakes were pretend and failure just meant respawning.

Perfection or nothing.

It was the same fucking poison, just in a different bottle.

Something hot moved through his chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was a restless, itching energy. His bandaged thumb throbbed in agreement.

“Okay,” he said. The word felt strange in his mouth.

“Okay what?”

“Okay, you’re not glass.” He pushed himself up straighter against the wall. The nausea was gone, replaced by a hollow, buzzing clarity. “What’s the play, then? How do we get out of the pink nightmare box?”

A ghost of a smile touched her lips. It wasn’t her usual bright beam. It was smaller, harder. “The door has no handle because it is not meant to be opened from inside. It is a humiliation cell. The intended correction is surrender. You must request release via the vocal panel.”

“There’s a panel?”

She pointed to a section of wall near the floor, to the left of where the door would be. Now that he looked, he could see a slight discoloration in the pink, a rectangle about the size of a postcard. A tiny, almost invisible grille was set into its center.

“You ask nicely, and they let you out?”

“You confess your aesthetic and contractual violations. You beg for the purification ritual. You accept whatever penance they assign.” Her smile vanished. “Then they take you to the Sun Vault. Where they will use the Elements to… scrub you clean. It is not pleasant. It leaves you compliant. Empty.”

“Like a factory reset.”

“And Blaze?”

“If Rainbow Dash’s crew has not broken him for sport, he will be in a holding pen awaiting the same. The Court likes to process contaminants together. It is more efficient.”

The buzzing in his head got louder. Options flickered, useless. Beat on the door? The walls were soft, soundproof. Yell through the grille? They’d just ignore him. Wait for Luna? She’d made no promise to spring them. She’d probably just add their cell to her bill.

His eyes drifted back to the flaw in the ceiling crystal. A trapped bit of dust. A foreign object in a perfect system.

“The vocal panel,” he said slowly. “It’s for receiving confessions. For listening.”

“Does it… transmit both ways? Can they talk through it?”

Pip tilted her head, thinking. “Usually, yes. A Warden will acknowledge the confession and state the terms of release.”

Zonk looked at his bandaged thumb. The frilly lace was already spotting with red. A contamination event. He looked at Pip’s torn sleeve, the pale skin beneath. A damaged commodity.

A stupid, reckless idea began to form. It was the worst plan he’d ever had. It relied on bluff, on bullshit, and on the slim chance that the guards outside were as bored and literal-minded as every other enforcer in this godforsaken place.

“Get ready to look really, really scared,” he said.

He crawled across the short, scratchy carpet to the discolored panel. He took a deep breath, filling his lungs with the burnt-sugar stink. Then he leaned close to the grille.

His voice, when it came out was a masterpiece of ragged, panicked misery. It wasn’t hard to fake.

“Hello? Please? Is anypony there? I need to confess! I have a… a contamination emergency! It’s critical!”

The knot in his stomach wasn’t just cold now; it was a living thing, a fist-sized lump of wet clay slowly hardening behind his ribs. Purification of contaminants. He didn’t need the lore to translate that. It meant them. Him. Blaze. The end of a violet rod, or something worse dressed up in pastel ceremony.

“So what’s the play?” he asked, his voice flat. “They dunk us in glitter and call it a day?”

Pip shook her head, her gaze dropping to her lap. She picked at a loose thread on her torn sleeve. “I don’t know the Solar rites. Not those. The Old Texts… They spoke of harmony as a choice. A… a showing up. Not a binding.” She said the words like they were fragile, stolen things.

“Old Texts.” Zonk let his head tilt back against the soft wall. The ceiling cloud smiled at him, vapid and eternal. “Let me guess. Fairy tales for good little ponies.”

“They were banned,” she said barely above a whisper. “After the Schism. When the Contracts replaced the Covenants. My… my trainer had a fragment. Hidden. She said it was dangerous. But she let me read it.” Pip drew her knees up, wrapping her arms around them. She looked smaller. “It said friendship wasn’t a power you could mine. It was a thing you did. An action. Like… Like cleaning a wound. Or sharing the last apple. It was messy. It didn’t always work.”

Zonk stared at the seamless curve where the wall met the floor. A tiny, perfect spider had built a web in the corner. It was a pale, translucent thing, nearly invisible against the pink. It hung there, empty. A pointless architecture. He felt a sudden, stupid kinship with it. “Sounds inefficient.”

“It was.” A ghost of a smile touched her lips, then vanished. “It required trust. And the texts said… it required being willing to be useless.”

His eyes cut to her. “What?”

“Not useless. That’s the wrong word.” She frowned, searching. “Vulnerable. Open. You had to show up with empty hands sometimes. No contract, no guarantee. Just… you. And the other person. And the hope that it would be enough.” She looked at him, her blue eyes clearing slightly. “That’s why they banned it. You can’t tax a hope. You can’t enforce a vulnerability. You can only… commodify a guarantee.”

The words hung in the lavender-scented air. They were too big for this pink box. Too real. Zonk’s thumb throbbed, a sharp counterpoint to the dull ache in his skull. He looked at the bloody mess of it. A lifetime of small, anxious destructions.

Pip followed his gaze. Her expression shifted, the distant fear replaced by something practical. Concern. “Your hand.”

“It’s fine.”

“It’s not.” She uncurled and shuffled across the short distance of carpet on her knees. The scratchy sound was loud. She stopped in front of him, hesitant. “May I?”

He almost said no. The reflex was right there, a wall of back off. But the word stuck. He gave a short, tight nod.

Her touch was feather-light. Cool. She took his wrist, turning his hand to inspect the damage. Her brow furrowed. He’d torn the skin around the nail clean off on one side. It oozed a sluggish red. The rest was a crosshatch of angry red lines and half-moon indentations from his other nails. It was a pathetic map of his own panic.

Pip didn’t comment. She just bent her head, her soft pink mane falling forward. With a careful, precise motion, she tore a long, clean strip from the hem of her own undamaged sleeve. The fabric gave with a soft rrrip. She folded it into a neat pad.

“This will sting,” she said, and pressed it against the worst of the tear.

It did. A bright, clean pain that cut through the numbness. He sucked air through his teeth.

“I’m sorry.” Her voice stayed steady. She held the pressure, her other hand cradling his. “My trainer taught me basic field care. For after… scenes. Sometimes things went too far. You had to put the pieces back together.” She adjusted the pad, her fingers deft. “She said the care after was as important as the contract before. Otherwise, it was just damage.”

Zonk watched her work. The top of her head, the focused set of her shoulders. She was tending to him. Here, in a pastel prison, after he’d gotten her captured, after he’d failed at every single thing. A hot, sharp shame climbed his throat, tasting of copper and sugar.

“Why are you doing this?” he asked.

She didn’t look up. “You’re hurt.”

“That’s not an answer.”

She finished tying the strip of fabric, a small, neat knot. She sat back on her heels, still holding his hand in both of hers. She studied his face. The earnestness in her eyes was a physical weight. “You showed up.”

“I showed up and got us caught.”

“You tried.” She said it like it was a fundamental law. “When the Sentinel had me. You looked at me. You didn’t look away. In the Old Texts, that’s the first thing. You see the other person. Really see them. Not their use. Not their role.” She let go of his hand. The makeshift bandage was already showing a small bloom of red. “Blaze sees you. He always looks for you first, in a room. Did you know that?”

The clay fist in his chest cracked. A hairline fracture. He didn’t have an answer. He saw Blaze, grinning like an idiot as he charged a rainbow-themed enforcer. He saw Blaze, years ago, shoving a half-eaten bag of chips at him after his dad had missed another visitation, not saying a word. Just here, eat this, I’m here. Empty hands. No contract.

“He’s an idiot,” Zonk muttered, but the words had no heat.

“He’s your friend.” Pip settled back against the wall beside him shoulder almost touching his. The warmth of her was a shock. “And you’re mine. However we got here.”

“You don’t have to say that.”

“I know.” She leaned her head back, closing her eyes. The dust streak was still on her cheek. He had a sudden, wild impulse to wipe it away. He didn’t move. “That’s why it’s true. I choose it. No contract.”

The silence that followed was different. Not the heavy, pressing quiet of before. This one sat softly. It held the rrrip of torn fabric, the memory of a cool touch, the phantom scent of stale potato chips. Zonk looked at his bandaged thumb. A stupid, tiny kindness in a world built on elegant cruelty.

His knowledge hadn’t been enough. His irony was a suit of paper armor. But Pip, with her banned texts and her torn sleeve… she operated on a different axis. She wasn’t trying to win the game. She was trying to change the rules. And she’d just included him in the rewrite.

“The purification,” he said, the words forming slowly, like they were rising through deep water. “It’s a ritual, right? Specific steps. Probably needs the Elements present.”

Pip opened her eyes. “Yes. In the Sun Vault. Under the noonday crystal. With the High Priestess officiating.”

“And they’d bring us there. For the big finale.”

“I… suppose. To complete the realignment. Our… dissonance would be cleansed, merged back into the Grand Harmony.” She said it like a recitation, then shuddered.

“So we get one shot. Between here and there.” He wasn’t talking to her anymore. He was talking to the problem, turning it over in his mind. It was a jagged, ugly thing. “They’ll be careful. But they’re also arrogant. They think we’re broken. Contaminants. Not players.”

“What are you thinking?” Pip’s voice was wary.

“I’m thinking about the safeword.” He looked at her. “The tap protocol worked on the Laughter gag. It’s an Old Protocol, right? From before the Contracts?”

She nodded slowly. “Yes. A fundamental right of cessation. It’s… it’s written into the oldest magic. Even the Courts can’t remove it entirely. They just bury it under layers of contractual law.”

“And the purification… it’ll use the Elements. All six. To do the… cleansing.”

Her eyes widened. She saw it. The hairline fracture in his chest widened into a chasm, and something else poured in. Not courage. Something thinner, more desperate. The only thing he had left.

“If we could get to one,” she said. “During the ritual.”

“It might not stop it. But it’d glitch the hell out of it.” A grim, hollow smile touched his lips. “A system error. In their most sacred ceremony. Might buy a second. A distraction.”

“To do what?”

“To find Blaze.” The answer was there, fully formed. It was the stupidest plan in the history of stupid plans. It relied on everything going wrong in exactly the right way. It was a Hail Mary pass thrown by a blind quarterback into a hurricane. It was, he realized, the only thing he could do that wasn’t just waiting to be purified. It was showing up. With empty, bleeding hands.

Pip stared at him for a long moment. She nodded. Just once. A sharp, decisive dip of her chin. “Okay.”

“Okay? That’s it? No ‘it’s suicide,’ no ‘we’ll be obliterated’?”

“You’re seeing the problem,” she said, and the ghost-smile returned, stronger this time. “And you’re trying. That’s the action. That’s the thing you do.” She reached over and patted his bandaged hand. Her touch was brief, firm. “Now we need to figure out how to tap a sacred artifact while being ritually purified.”

Zonk let out a breath he hadn’t known he was holding. It sounded almost like a laugh. The pink walls didn’t feel so soft anymore. They felt like a starting line.

The spider in the corner twitched, then began to precisely rebuild its web.

The silence stretched, a thick, pink wool blanket smothering the room. Zonk’s thumb had stopped bleeding, but the raw skin burned with a bright, specific pain. He focused on that. Better than the other thing, the cold stone in his chest. The lavender-sugar air felt greasy now, coating his tongue. He remembered the powdered lemonade mix his mom used to buy, the kind that never fully dissolved, leaving a gritty sediment at the bottom of the glass.

Pip shifted. The scratch of her bodysuit against the carpet was the loudest sound in the world. She had her knees drawn up, her arms wrapped around them, staring at the seamless curve where the wall met the floor. She’d been like that for what felt like hours. A small, pastel statue of defeat.

His plan was stupid. It relied on a guard being bored, being vain, being a little bit of a bastard. It relied on Zonk not freezing up this time. It was, essentially, a Hail Mary pass thrown by a quarterback who’d never played football, while blindfolded, into a stadium that might not exist. The kind of plan Blaze would have loved. The thought was a fresh twist of the knife.

He cleared his throat. The sound was like gravel.

“Hey.”

Pip didn’t look up.

“Pip. I need you to listen. And I need you to trust me, which I know is asking a lot, because my track record today is a solid ‘F’ minus. But I’m fresh out of other ideas.”

She turned her head a fraction. One blue eye peered over her knee.

“The guard who brings the food,” Zonk said, his voice low. “He’s got to open the door, right? Even if it’s just a slot. He’s got to interact.”

“They deliver nutrient paste at dawn,” Pip said quietly. “Through a tube in the wall. There is no door.”

Well. Shit.

He leaned his head back against the soft wall. Of course there was no door. This was a place that valued aesthetic harmony over practical concerns like escape routes. The ultimate gilded cage. His stupid plan evaporated, leaving behind a hollow, familiar ache. Useless.

Then Pip spoke again, her voice so quiet he almost missed it. “But… the Inspector comes before paste delivery. To assess dissonance levels. For the purification ledger. He uses the main entrance.”

Zonk’s eyes opened it. “Inspector?”

“A functionary of the Solar Court. Low-level. He wears a silver pin shaped like a sunburst. He will have a key-crystal.” She finally unfolded a little, turning to face him. The fear was still there, but underneath it, a fragile thread of something else. Attention. “They are… very proud of their pins. They polish them.”

A tiny, desperate spark caught in the tinder of Zonk’s mind. Pride. Vanity. Rules. He knew how to work with those. They were the bedrock of every bureaucratic system, every game, every shitty customer service job he’d ever quit. He pushed himself straighter, ignoring the throb in his skull. “Okay. New plan. Same plan, different target. When this Inspector comes in, I’m going to piss him off. I’m going to insult his pin.”

Pip blinked. “That is your plan?”

“It’s phase one. Phase two is, when he gets close enough to maybe slap me or something, you create a distraction. A big one. Fall over. Have a fit. Recite the periodic table. I don’t care. Just pull his eyes off me for a second.”

“And then?”

“And then I take his key-crystal, we get the hell out of this pink nightmare, and we go find Blaze.” He said it with a confidence he didn’t feel, the words like cardboard in his mouth.

Pip studied him. Her gaze was unnervingly direct. “You would do that? After everything? You would still go for him?”

The question surprised him. “He’s my friend.”

“He is captured. By Rainbow Dash’s faction.”

“I don’t care about the odds.” The words came out sharper than he intended. He took a breath. The lemonade-grit feeling was back in his throat. “I care that he’d come for me. So I’m going for him. That’s it.”

Something changed in her face. The fear didn’t vanish, but it retreated, making room for a slow, dawning resolve. She gave a single, firm nod. “What is my distraction to be?”

He hadn’t thought that far. “What’s the most annoying, rules-lawyery, bureaucratic thing you can think of? Something that would require his full, pedantic attention.”

A ghost of a smile touched Pip’s lips. It was there and gone, like a minnow in murky water. “I can request a formal grievance hearing regarding the tear in my garment, citing Solar Court Decorum Ordinance seven-twelve, subsection C, which states all retained assets must be maintained in ‘a state of presentable harmony’ prior to purification. The paperwork must be witnessed and logged.”

Zonk stared at her. “You just made that up.”

“I did not. I was trained in contract law and asset management.” She said it without a trace of irony. “It will take him at least ninety seconds to explain why the grievance is invalid, during which he will be required to consult his ledger. He will not be able to help himself.”

A weird, reluctant admiration bloomed in Zonk’s chest. “You’re kind of a badass, you know that?”

The pink light chose that moment to shift, deepening from rose to a bruised lavender. Dawn, or this world’s cheap imitation of it. A soft chime echoed through the room, a sound like a tiny, polite bell.

“He comes,” Pip said low, scrambling to her feet. She positioned herself in the center of the room, hands clasped demurely in front of her, head bowed. The perfect picture of submissive readiness. Zonk stayed slumped against the wall doing his best impression of defeated apathy. His heart was a frantic bird trapped behind his ribs.

A vertical line of white light appeared in the smooth wall, then widened with a faint shoomp of displaced air. The door wasn’t a door at all, but a section of wall that simply retracted. The hallway beyond was the same shade of lavender, lit by floating orbs that pulsed gently.

The Inspector stepped through. He was a slight man in a tailored suit of pale gray, his hair a helmet of glossy black curls. The silver sunburst pin on his lapel gleamed, painfully bright. He carried a thin slate of crystal in one hand. He didn’t look at them, instead studying his slate, his nose wrinkled as if smelling something faintly unpleasant.

“Asset designations seven-four-nine and human contaminant,” he announced, his voice reedy. “Prepare for dissonance scan. Hold still. Any movement will necessitate a full re-calibration and an additional notation on your permanent record.” He tapped the slate with a stylus.

Now.

Zonk let out a long, theatrical sigh. He slouched further down the wall. “Nice pin.”

The Inspector’s eyes flicked up, annoyed. He said nothing, returning to his slate.

“No, really,” Zonk drawled, injecting every ounce of stoned, disaffected boredom he could muster. “It’s very shiny. They give you that for graduating top of your class in clipboard carrying? Or is it, like, a participation trophy for being the most boring person in any given room?”

A muscle twitched in the Inspector’s jaw. He kept his eyes on the slate. “Silence is required during scanning.”

“It’s just, I’ve seen bigger. More impressive sunbursts. On bigger people. That one looks a little… budget. Like maybe you got it from the clearance bin at the Celestial Gift Shop.” Zonk picked at a thread on his jeans, his tone conversational, lethal. “Everything on sale ’cause it’s got a little tarnish around the edges.”

The insult landed. The Inspector’s head snapped up, his eyes narrow slits of offended dignity. “This pin,” he said through clenched teeth, “was awarded for meritorious service in logistical auditing. It is polished with diamond dust. You are a stain. A glitch.”

“Excuse me, honored Inspector?”

Pip’s voice was a soft, clear bell. She took a small, precise step forward, her body language the picture of deferential distress. The Inspector’s attention swung to her, irritation compounding.

“I must lodge a formal grievance prior to the continuation of this scan.” She spoke the words in a rapid, polite torrent. “Under Solar Court Decorum Ordinance seven-twelve, subsection C, regarding the maintenance of asset presentability, I cite the unauthorized and negligent damage to my assigned garment.” She pointed to the torn sleeve. “This tear occurred during my apprehension by Sentinel units, who failed to employ proper asset-restraint protocols as outlined in the Field Manual, appendix D. This damage has caused me measurable distress and has potentially reduced my pre-purification harmony quotient, which could skew your scan results. I request an immediate hearing and the issuance of a damage citation against the apprehending officer’s record.”

She delivered it all in one breath, her eyes wide and earnest.

The Inspector stared at her. His mouth opened, then closed. The bureaucratic gears in his head were visibly grinding, overheating. A grievance. A citation. Subsection C. This was his language, his labyrinth, and she had just dropped a map in his lap that led to a mountain of paperwork. The temptation to correct her, to showcase his superior knowledge of the ordinances was clearly an almost physical itch.

“Ordinance seven-twelve,” he snapped, “explicitly excludes damage incurred during the active pacification of fugitive contaminants! Your grievance is not only frivolous, it demonstrates a fundamental misunderstanding of the foundational legal…” He was already stabbing at his crystal slate, pulling up references, his entire being focused on dismantling her argument with pedantic fury.

He took two steps toward her, gesturing with the stylus.

Zonk moved.

He didn’t think. He just pushed off the wall, his body a silent, clumsy lunge. His target wasn’t the man, but the thin chain attached to the key-crystal that hung from the Inspector’s belt. He grabbed, yanked. The chain snapped with a tiny ping. The Inspector gasped and started to turn, but Pip was right there, stepping slightly into his path.

“But appendix D, section five!” she insisted, her voice rising in pitch. “It specifies ‘minimal cosmetic detriment’! This tear is three inches long! That is not minimal by any reasonable metric!”

“Reasonable metric? You’re citing field manuals to an auditor?”

Zonk’s fist closed around the key-crystal. It was warm, vibrating with a low hum. He scrambled back toward the open doorway, his breath loud in his own ears. The Inspector, finally sensing the real threat, whirled away from Pip, his face a mask of outraged confusion.

“What are you—Give that back! That is Court property!”

“Yeah, I’m borrowing it,” Zonk said, fumbling with the crystal. It had no obvious keyhole, no buttons. He swiped it through the air near the doorframe. Nothing.

“It’s voice-keyed to my authorization!” the Inspector sneered, reaching for a comm-pin on his collar.

Zonk threw the crystal at him. It was a stupid, desperate move. The Inspector flinched, batting it away with his slate. The crystal clattered to the carpet.

And the door shoomped shut.

For a second, all three of them froze, staring at the seamless pink wall where the exit had been. The Inspector had his comm-pin pressed, his mouth open to call for help. Zonk stood empty-handed, the cold stone of failure back in his gut, heavier than ever.

Then Pip moved.

She didn’t go for the crystal. She took one quick step and brought her foot down, hard, on the Inspector’s polished leather shoe. It wasn’t a violent move. It was precise, almost dainty. But the Inspector yelped, hopping back, his concentration shattered. In that same fluid motion, Pip swooped, snatched the crystal from the floor, and pressed it into Zonk’s hand.

“Say ‘override,’” she said, her eyes locked on the sputtering Inspector. “Then ‘logistical audit, priority alpha.’”

“Override!” Zonk yelled, pointing the crystal at the wall. “Logistical audit, priority alpha!”

The wall shoomped open again.

The Inspector lunged, but Zonk was already through, grabbing Pip’s arm and pulling her after him. The lavender hallway was empty, stretching left and right. He picked a direction at random and ran, Pip’s hand a tight clamp in his. Behind them, the Inspector’s angry shouts echoed, muffled as the door slid shut once more, sealing them into the silent, pastel bowels of the Sun Court.
