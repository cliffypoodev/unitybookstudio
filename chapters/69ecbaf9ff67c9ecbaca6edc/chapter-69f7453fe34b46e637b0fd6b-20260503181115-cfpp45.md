“You want collateral,” Zonk said. The pieces kept clicking. “You want insurance we won’t fuck off or sell her a different story. You want something personal. Something that ties us to you, so we do the ritual your way.”

Luna’s expression didn’t change, but something in the room did. The temperature dropped a degree. The guard by the wall became, for a second, a person again. “Perceptive.”

“What is it?”

“A memory,” Luna said softly. “Not just any memory. The one you use to remind yourself why you never get attached. The one that made you.” She leaned forward, her eyes holding his. “You will tell it to me. You will let me see it. And I will keep it. If you betray our agreement, I will not hunt you. I will simply… let that memory slip back into the world from which you came. To the people who feature in it. Anonymously, of course.”

The floor seemed to tilt. It wasn’t a physical threat. It was worse. It was emotional surgery. The one scar he kept covered, the one story he never told, not even to Blaze on their darkest, most chemically facilitated nights. The abandoned ten-year-old in a silent house, waiting for a car that never came back. The specific, citrus scent of his mother’s perfume on the empty pillow. The way the digital clock on the microwave had blinked 12:00 for a week because his father couldn’t figure out how to set it.

Blaze was looking at him, confused. “Dude, what’s she talking about?”

Zonk’s mouth was desert. He could refuse. He could walk away. They could take their chances with the Solar Sentinels and the guilds and the collapsing barrier.

He looked at Pip, who needed a home. He looked at Blaze, who would follow him into a fire but deserved a door out. He looked at the bag of sacred sex toys that had started it all.

“Just me?” he asked. The way he said it was a whisper.

“The leader pays the toll,” Luna said. “That is the way of things.”

He nodded. A short, jerky motion. “Okay.”

“Zonk?” Blaze’s hand was on his arm.

“It’s fine.” He shook it off. He took a step toward the dais. The cold in his gut was absolute now, a frozen river. “How does this work?”

Luna lifted a hand. From the shadows beside her throne, a small, obsidian box floated into view. It had no latch. “You speak. The box listens. It is a one-time vessel. After our deal is complete, I will shatter it. The memory goes to oblivion. Not to me. Just… away.”

He stood before her. He could see the fine texture of the stone throne, the almost invisible hairline cracks like spiderwebs. He could smell her scent—Night air, ozone, and something bitter, like crushed juniper berries.

He thought of a thousand deflections. A joke. A sarcastic remark. He opened his mouth.

And he told her.

He didn’t give her the broad strokes. He gave her the splinter. The exact, irrelevant, stupid detail. The way the linoleum in the kitchen had a chip near the fridge, a little crescent-shaped flaw he’d trace with his socked toe while he waited. The way the silence wasn’t empty, but full of the hum of the refrigerator and the distant, tinny sound of a game show from the neighbor’s apartment. The specific, crushing weight of realizing the neighbor’s laugh was louder than anything in his own house.

He spoke in a monotone. He didn’t look at Blaze. He didn’t look at Pip. He watched the obsidian box. It drank his words. When he finished, the silence rushed back in, thicker than before.

Luna closed her fingers. The box vanished.

“It is done,” she said. There was no triumph in her voice. No pity. It was a statement of fact. “You have your deal. Moondrop will see you to your rooms. You will have tutors. You will learn the ritual. In three nights, you will be the guests of honor at the Gala.”

She waved a hand in dismissal. The guard by the wall stepped forward, becoming a person again. The audience was over.

As they turned to go, Luna’s voice stopped them, softer now. “A word of advice, little thief. My sister’s cruelty is a performance. A show. Do not mistake my… transactions… for kindness. But do not mistake them for cruelty, either. I am simply thorough. Remember that, when you stand in her light.”

They followed the guard back into the dark corridor. The fake stars overhead seemed to mock him. Blaze was silent, radiating confused concern. Pip was crying, quiet little hiccups she tried to stifle in her fluffy tail.

Zonk felt hollow. Clean. The cold lump was gone. In its place was a simple, chilling clarity. They had a path. They had a powerful ally. They had a way home.

And a piece of him was now a commodity in a star-flecked box held as collateral in a war between pony princesses.

The door to the mundane hallway closed behind them with a soft, final click. Somewhere, a pipe groaned in the walls, a long, low sound like a tired beast settling in for the night.

The door clicked shut behind Moondrop. The sound was final, like the lid of a jewelry box closing. The hum in the air settled into Zonk’s bones. It was the sound of a fridge in another room, a constant, low-grade annoyance you could almost ignore.

Blaze exhaled, a long, noisy rush of air. He dropped the bag on the bed. The Elements inside clanked together, a discordant jangle. “Okay. So. We have a fancy room. With a princess who wants us to be her secret weapons. This is good, right? This is the plan working.”

Pip drifted to the window. She didn’t touch it, just peered out at the obsidian wall. Her reflection was a pale smudge on the glass. “The Moon Princess’s chambers are said to be woven from shadows and lost memories,” she said. “This is just a room.”

“It’s a trap,” Zonk said. The words were out before he could shape them into something less blunt. He walked to the bed sat on the edge. The dark blue coverlet was stiff, embroidered with silver thread in patterns that hurt his eyes if he looked directly at them. Swirls. Constellations. A lot of crescent moons. He picked at a loose thread on his jeans, right near the knee. The denim was fraying. He’d have a hole soon. He focused on that. The impending hole. “We didn’t find her. She collected us. There’s a difference.”

“Dude. She’s gonna get us home. She gave us a room that doesn’t have Solar stormtroopers kicking the door in. I’m calling that a win.” Blaze started pacing, three steps one way, three steps back. The floorboards creaked in a different rhythm than his steps. “We do the thing at the party, we embarrass the sun-bitch, Luna turns on the portal, we drop the magic kink toys and run. Pip stays if she wants, we go. It’s clean.”

“Nothing here is clean.” Zonk lay back on the bed. The fake stars on the ceiling were arranged in no pattern he knew. One was flickering, a tiny, arrhythmic pulse of blue-white light. It was probably a faulty LED. He wondered if anyone would ever fix it. “She’s Night Court. Brass said she collects stories. Moondrop wanted a splinter. Luna wants a public spectacle. We’re not allies. We’re anecdotes. We’re a funny thing that happened on a Tuesday she can use to twist the knife.”

“So what?” Blaze stopped pacing. His shadow fell across Zonk’s face. “We use her back. That’s how this works. Everybody’s using everybody. It’s… transactional. You said it yourself.”

Zonk closed his eyes. The inside of his eyelids was a map of red and purple lines. “The game was a trap, Blaze.”

Silence. The fridge-hum. The creak of Blaze shifting his weight.

“What?”

“Luna. She said it. The ‘Equestria: Bonds of Harmony’ experience. It’s not a game. It’s a… a net. A fishing net. They put out the shiny quests, they watch who bites, they see who’s good at it. Who knows the lore. Who understands the… the rules.” He kept his eyes shut. It was easier to talk to the darkness. “We didn’t find a weird game. They found us. We were on a list. The heist was a test. We just… passed in the wrong fucking way.”

Blaze was quiet for a long time. “That’s messed up.”

“So we were, like… being groomed? For this?”

Pip turned from the window. Her eyes were wide.

“She was in the vault,” Zonk said, pushing himself up on his elbows. The embroidery scratched his skin through his T-shirt. “The one place in the whole dimension, according to her, that’s got the most sacred, guarded objects. And she’s just there. A trainee. She said she got lost. You think the Solar creche lets its prize pupils just wander into the main vault? She was placed. A reward. Or a leash. A cute, friendly face to make the new recruits feel safe.”

Pip’s mouth opened it. No sound came out. She looked down at the brand on her flank, the little sun symbol. Her hand went to it, fingers pressing against the fabric of her suit as if she could feel the mark through it.

“No,” she said quietly.

“It’s not your fault,” Zonk said. The words tasted like ash. “You were a prop. We all were. The game, the quest, you… it was all set dressing. We just broke the set.”

She shook her head, a frantic little motion. “The Princess of the Sun… she cares for us. She teaches us. The marks are for… for belonging.”

“Yeah. Belonging to her.” Zonk sat all the way up. A headache was gathering behind his eyes, a tight, hot band. “Luna’s no better. She’s just offering a different brand of belonging. With more stars and better interior design.”

Blaze sank into a high-backed chair by a small, empty fireplace. The wood groaned under him. “So we’re screwed. If we’re screwed with a plan, that’s better than being screwed without one.”

“Is it?” Zonk looked at the bag on the bed. The hum seemed louder now. It was in the tiara, the cuffs, the stupid whoopee cushion. A dissonant chord waiting to be played. “We follow Luna’s plan, we become her weapons. We publicly humiliate Celestia. Then what? You think she just lets us go? ‘Thanks for the coup, here’s your ticket home, tell your friends’? We’ll know too much. We’ll have done too much. We’ll be a story she can’t risk letting get out.”

“Then we run,” Blaze said. He spoke stubbornly, a rock in a stream. “We do the thing, and in the chaos, we bolt for the portal. We don’t wait for her permission.”

“With every guard in both Courts looking for us.”

“We’ve done okay so far.”

“We’ve been lucky!” The band around his head tightened. Zonk pressed the heels of his hands against his temples. The flickering star pulsed in the corner of his vision. “We got lucky with a bartender who hates the cops. We got lucky with a creepy landlady who likes gossip. Luck runs out. That’s what it does.”

A soft knock at the door.

It wasn’t a loud sound. It was three gentle raps, knuckles on polished wood. It froze them all.

Blaze looked at Zonk. Zonk looked at the door. Pip shrank back against the window.

The knock came again. Polite. Patient.

Zonk stood. His legs felt unsteady. He walked to the door, put his hand on the cold, brass handle. He didn’t open it. “Who is it?”

“A friend.” The voice from the other side was muffled, genderless. “Mistress Moondrop sent me. I have refreshments. And a message.”

Zonk glanced back. Blaze had risen from the chair, his body coiled. Pip was a statue of fear.

He turned the handle and opened the door a crack.

The corridor was empty except for a small, wheeled cart. On it sat a carafe of something dark, three crystal glasses, and a plate of small, perfect pastries that sparkled with sugar. No one was pushing it. As Zonk watched, the cart gave a slight, self-propelled shudder and rolled forward an inch, nudging against the door.

Taped to the carafe was a slip of parchment.

Zonk snatched it, pulled the cart inside, and shut the door. He leaned against it, his heart doing a clumsy, heavy beat against his ribs. He looked at the parchment.

The script was the same spidery elegance as the address. It read:

The Gala commences at moon-high. You will wear what is provided. A guide will come. Speak of this audience to no one. The walls have ears, but the stars have eyes.

P. S. The tea is safe. The pastries are excellent. Try the one with the silver dust. It tastes like regret.

Blaze took the note from his trembling fingers read it, and snorted. “Dramatic.”

“They’re all dramatic.” Zonk pushed off the door and went to the cart. The carafe held a liquid so purple it was almost black. It smelled of plums and anise. His stomach turned over. He picked up a pastry. It was light as air, shaped like a tiny crescent moon, dusted with a silvery powder that stuck to his fingers. Regret. What did regret taste like? Metallic, probably. Sour.

He put it down. His fingers left smudges on the pristine white plate.

“What’s provided?” Pip asked, her voice small. She was looking at a large, flat box that had been placed on the lower shelf of the cart. None of them had noticed it.

Blaze bent and lifted it. He set it on the bed and opened the lid.

Inside, folded with military precision were three outfits.

One was a suit of deep midnight blue, shot through with subtle silver threads that caught the light like distant galaxies. It came with a half-cape of a material so dark it seemed to drink the light from the room.

The second was a more practical, but still ornate, tunic and trousers in a shade of charcoal grey, with silver fastenings and boots that looked like they could kick through a wall.

The third was a dress. A confection of lavender and silver, layers of tulle and silk, with sleeves that would drape to the floor. It was delicate. Insane. It looked like something a child’s doll would wear to a funeral.

They stared into the box.

“Oh, hell no,” Blaze said.

Pip reached out a tentative finger and touched the dress’s fabric. “It’s beautiful,” she said quietly, then flinched as if caught doing something wrong.

“It’s a costume,” Zonk said flatly. “We’re playing dress-up for the court. We’re the entertainment.” He looked at the suit meant for him. It was beautiful. It was the most beautiful thing he’d ever been offered. It felt like a sentence.

“I’m not wearing that dress,” Pip said, low, but her eyes were still on it, wide with a kind of horrified fascination.

“You have to,” Zonk said. He felt tired down to the marrow. “We all have to. We put on the clothes, we go to the party, we do the stupid, dangerous thing. Because the alternative is sitting in this room until the Solar Sentinels finally figure out where we are and kick the door in. At least this way we’re moving.”

“Toward what?” Blaze asked. He wasn’t arguing. He was just asking.

Zonk looked at the flickering star on the ceiling, at the chipped pot on the windowsill, at the perfect, inedible pastries. “I don’t know. But it’s a different kind of screwed. Maybe that’s all we get to choose.” He picked up the silver-dusted crescent again. This time, he took a bite.

It was sweet. Crumbling. The silver dust had no taste at all, just a faint, gritty texture. The filling was a tart berry jam. It tasted nothing like regret. It tasted like a very expensive jam tart.

He finished it, licked the dust from his fingers. It was just sugar.

“Okay,” he said to the room, to the clothes, to the humming bag on the bed. “Let’s get dressed.”
