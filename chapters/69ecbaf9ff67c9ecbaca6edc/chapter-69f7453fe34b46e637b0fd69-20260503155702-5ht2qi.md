The air in the storage room tasted like dust and industrial lemon cleaner, the kind that doesn’t clean anything, just announces its failure with chemical enthusiasm. Zonk’s back ached from the concrete floor. A single, caged bulb buzzed overhead, its light the color of old teeth. He’d been staring at a water stain on the ceiling for twenty minutes. It looked like a cartoon duck smoking a pipe. Completely irrelevant. He couldn’t stop looking at it.

Pip was crying. Not the loud, dramatic kind. The quiet, hopeless kind that was just a steady leak of sound, a soft, hitching breath every thirty seconds. She sat against a stack of cardboard boxes labeled ‘Seasonal Novelties – Do not Crush,’ her knees drawn to her chest, her fluffy blue tail wrapped around her ankles like a security blanket. She’d been like that since they’d scrambled up the rickety back stairs and barricaded the door with a broken pallet.

Blaze paced. Five steps to the rust-stained sink, pivot, five steps back to the door. The rhythm was starting to drill into Zonk’s skull. Each footfall sent a tiny puff of dust from the grimy floor.

“We need a plan,” Blaze said, for the third time in ten minutes.

“The plan is to not get murdered by pastel fascists,” Zonk said, his voice flat. He finally looked away from the duck. “That’s a solid plan. I’m executing it right now.”

“That’s not a plan, dude. That’s a vibe. A bad one.”

Pip made another small sound, a wet sniffle. Zonk felt something in his gut tighten. Not fear, not exactly. More like the feeling you get when you realize you’ve agreed to water a neighbor’s plant and it’s already half-dead. A low-grade, irritating responsibility.

“Hey, Pip,” he said, not moving from his spot on the floor. “You good?”

She shook her head, the motion making the bell on her collar give a pathetic, muffled jingle. “I am… I am not ‘good,’ Master Zonk. I have failed. I brought the Contract-Wardens to your dwelling. I have no patron, I am without purpose, and I am… I am very scared.” The last part came out in a whisper.

“Don’t call me master,” Zonk sighed. “It’s just Zonk. And it’s not your fault. We stole the shiny things. We’re the idiots here.”

“But I am the asset! The liability!” She lowered the words to, trembling. “A proper companion would have negotiated terms! Would have… Would have offered suitable compensation to avert violence! I just hid behind the sofa! I know the seven hundred protocols for formal tea service, but I don’t know how to not get people smashed through doors!” A fresh wave of tears welled up. She furiously wiped her nose with the back of her hand, a gesture so childlike it made Zonk’s chest ache in a weird, unfamiliar way.

Blaze stopped pacing. He crouched in front of her, his big frame blocking the harsh light. “Hey. No. Look at me.” His voice lost its frantic edge went soft. “You didn’t get us into this. We did. You’re just stuck with us. And we’re not gonna let those glittery assholes touch you, okay?”

Pip looked up, her large eyes glassy. “You would continue your custodianship? Even though I am… defective?”

“We’re not your custodians, Pip. We’re your… we’re just the guys you’re with.” Blaze glanced back at Zonk, a silent plea for backup.

Zonk pushed himself up. His joints popped. He walked over, the sour lemon smell getting stronger near the sink. He leaned against the wall, looking down at the two of them. Pip’s little sun-brand on her flank peeked out from her bodysuit, neat and precise. A school mark. He remembered getting a vaccination once, the nurse’s perfect, circular sticker. This was like that, but permanent. Institutional.

“Blaze is right, for once in his life,” Zonk said. “We’re in it. So crying’s fine, but it’s not a strategy. We need to not be here when they inevitably check every flophouse and storage room in this district. Which means we need to know where to go.”

Pip took a shuddering breath, composing herself with visible effort. She sat up straighter. “Information is a traded commodity. The… the less reputable exchanges happen in the crepuscular markets. After the official solar curfew, but before the lunar court fully asserts its watch.”

“The night markets,” Zonk translated.

“Yes. There are rumor-brokers. Gossip-mongers. They trade in secrets and favors, not bits.” She bit her lip. “But they are not safe. The protocols are… Fluid. Unwritten.”

“Fluid we can handle,” Blaze said, standing up. “Better than solid guards with solid clubs.”

“Where?” Zonk asked.

Pip hesitated. “There is a place. A public house called the Gilded Saddle. It sits on a jurisdictional seam between the Day District and the… less savory lanes. The proprietor is said to be… pragmatic.”

“A bar,” Blaze said, a grin spreading across his face. “Dude. We can do a bar.”

Zonk wasn’t so sure. A bar in this world wasn’t just a place to get a drink. It was a stage. A theater with real consequences. His knowledge was all lore, game mechanics, forum speculation. This was different. This was Pip’s whole life, and she was terrified of it. The weight of that settled in his stomach, a cold, dense lump. He wasn’t just trying to get home anymore. He was trying to get her home, through a minefield she understood better than he ever could, and that was somehow infinitely worse.

“We need a cover,” Zonk said, his mind clicking into a familiar, analytical gear. It was a comfort, like cleaning his pipe. A problem to solve. “We can’t just walk in as us. The ‘wanted thieves and their runaway pony’ look isn’t a good one.”

“I could be a… a pleasure-escort?” Pip suggested, then immediately flinched. “No. That requires paperwork. Brand verification.”

“We’re outsiders,” Blaze said. “From… Somewhere else. Travelers. Traders.”

“Traders in what?” Zonk ran a hand through his hair. It was greasy. He needed a shower. He needed about six fewer problems. “We have six pieces of world-breaking BDSM gear we can’t show anyone.”

“Novelties,” Pip said softly. They both looked at her. She pointed a trembling finger at the boxes she was leaning against. “Seasonal novelties. From a distant… franchise. You are… scouts. For a new mercantile venture. Assessing local markets for compatible product lines.” She said it like she was reciting from a manual, which she probably was. “It is a common enough cover for spies and fugitives. So common it is often overlooked.”

Zonk stared at her. A laugh bubbled up in his throat, harsh and dry. “You’re saying we should pretend to be… MLP corporate espionage agents?”

“It has statistical plausibility,” she said, utterly earnest.

Blaze started chuckling. Then he was full-on laughing, a deep, relieved sound that filled the dusty room. “I love it. It’s the dumbest thing I’ve ever heard. Let’s do it.”

The tightness in Zonk’s gut unclenched a fraction. Not much. Just enough to breathe. He looked at Pip, who was watching them with a fragile, hopeful confusion. She’d stopped crying. Her idea. A stupid, brilliant, perfectly-pony idea.

“Okay,” Zonk said. He pushed off the wall. “Corporate scouts. We need a story. A product. Something ridiculous but just believable enough.” His eyes landed on the junk scattered in the corner of the room: a broken mop, some tangled fairy lights, a roll of glitter-coated packing tape. “I’m thinking… hygienic accessories. For the modern equine. A line of ergonomic, self-cleaning hoof-picks.”

Blaze’s laughter redoubled. Pip’s nose wrinkled. “Self-cleaning?”

“That’s the innovation, Pip,” Zonk said, a real smile tugging at his mouth for the first time in hours. “We’re selling convenience. We’re selling the future. And we’re going to use it to find the one person in this candy-colored hellscape who can tell us how to give back the goddamn jewelry.”

He walked over to the pallet barricading the door. It was heavier than it looked. The wood was splintered and rough against his palms. He glanced back. Blaze was helping Pip to her feet, his hand under her elbow. She stood, smoothing down her rumpled bodysuit, a tiny figure trying to summon dignity from nothing.

The plan was terrible. It was a house of cards built on a foundation of pop culture and desperation.

But it was a plan. And for now, that was all they had.

The Gilded Saddle squatted between a boarded-up confectionery and a shop that sold nothing but ornamental horseshoes. Its sign was a peeling, gilt-painted saddle, one of the dangling stirrups squeaking on a rusted chain with every gust of wind. The sound was a tired metallic sigh. Squeak. Thud. Zonk counted seven cycles before Blaze nudged him.

“You casing the joint or having a stroke?”

“It’s a process,” Zonk muttered.

The building was the same aggressively cheerful pastel as everything else—a vomitous shade of lavender—but the windows were smoked so dark they looked black. Music leaked out, a jaunty, tinny tune played on something that might have been an accordion with a drinking problem. Underneath it, the low rumble of a crowded room. The smell hit him next: cheap, sweet cider, the sharp tang of leather polish, and underneath it all, the sour note of spilled beer and unwashed bodies. A familiar bar smell, just wrapped in a nightmarish candy coating.

Pip stood between them, her small hands clasped tightly in front of her. She’d stopped crying, but her eyes were puffy, her ears flat against her head. She stared at the door like it was the gate to a minefield.

“Protocols?” Zonk asked her, keeping his voice low.

She took a shaky breath. “Establish presence but do not demand. Acknowledge the gatekeeper. State a simple, honest desire. Do not… do not make eye contact for more than three seconds. It can be construed as a challenge.”

“Gatekeeper,” Blaze said, nodding toward the shadowy recess beside the door.

Zonk hadn’t even seen him. A massive earth pony—or a guy in a suit so convincing the muscles seemed to ripple under the chestnut-brown latex—leaned against the wall. He had a leather vest over his broad chest, no shirt beneath it, and a coiled whip hung from his belt like a sidearm. He was polishing a glass mug with a rag, his movements slow, deliberate. He didn’t look up.

“Right,” Zonk said. His heart was doing a weird, skippy thing against his ribs. “Simple. Honest. We just want a drink and a chat.”

“We do?” Blaze whispered.

“We do now.”

They approached. The squeaking stirrup marked the time. Squeak. Thud. Seven steps. The bouncer kept polishing. Up close, Zonk could see the mug was already spotless.

Pip took the lead. She stopped a respectful four feet away, dipped her head in a shallow bow. “Good crepuscule, sir. We seek the comfort of your house.”

The bouncer looked up. His eyes were a flat, uninterested brown. He looked at Pip, at her brand, at her trembling tail. His gaze slid to Blaze, taking in his height, his tension, then to Zonk, lingering on his too-human jeans and worn sneakers. A flicker of something—contempt? Amusement? —passed over his face.

“House is full,” he said, his voice a gravelly rumble. He went back to his mug.

“We can wait,” Blaze said, too quickly, too loud.

The bouncer’s polishing stopped. He didn’t look up. “Can you.”

It wasn’t a question. Zonk’s mouth went dry. This was the part in the game where you’d have three dialogue options, each with a clear alignment shift. Here, there was just the dusty street and the squeaking chain and the feeling he was about to get his head smashed in over a point of etiquette he didn’t understand.

Pip flinched. Her training was for tea services and formal introductions, not for seedy bar bouncers. She was locking up.

Zonk found his voice. “We’ve had a long day,” he said, aiming for a tone of weary commiseration. “Chasing a contract that blew out on us. Just need a quiet corner to regroup. Maybe a word with someone who knows the currents.” He parroted a phrase he’d read once on a forum, about information brokers in MMOs. Knows the currents. It sounded stupid the moment it left his lips.

The bouncer’s eyes lifted again. He studied Zonk. “You’re not from around here.”

“Is anyone, really?” Zonk said, and immediately wanted to bite his own tongue off. Shut up, shut up, you ironic piece of shit.

But the bouncer’s mouth twitched. Not a smile. A tic. “Brass is in the back. He don’t like interruptions.”

“We’ll be discreet,” Zonk said.

“You’ll be gone in twenty minutes,” the bouncer corrected. He shifted his weight, and the doorway was clear. He went back to his mug. “Or you’ll be carried out.”

The inside of the Gilded Saddle was a temple of controlled chaos. The music was louder, the same manic polka, but now competing with the roar of a dozen conversations. The lighting was a dim, pinkish glow from stained-glass lanterns shaped like cutie marks. It made everyone look vaguely ill. The bar itself was a long, curved slab of polished something that shone like a wet tongue. Patrons crowded it—ponies of all types, a few griffons in the corner hunched over a game of dice, and here and there, a human. Or something human-shaped. They looked wrong, their edges too soft, their movements a half-second out of sync with the room, like badly rendered NPCs.

Zonk’s skin prickled. He kept his head down, following Blaze’s broad back as he carved a path toward the rear. He noticed a crack in the plaster wall, zigzagging from the ceiling to a picture of a smiling sunflower. It looked like a lightning bolt frozen mid-strike. Completely useless detail. He couldn’t stop staring at it.

They found a small, sticky table wedged between a support pillar and a wall. It gave them a view of the room and a dark, curtained archway labeled ‘PRIVATE’ in looping, elegant script. That had to be the back.

“Okay,” Blaze breathed, sinking onto a stool that groaned in protest. “We’re in. Now what? We just go knock?”

“We observe,” Pip whispered, her eyes wide as she took in the room. “We identify the power dynamics. See who approaches the curtain, and how.”

A server appeared, a pegasus with a tired expression and a tray balanced on one wing. “Cider? Ale? Sparkling water with a twist of despair?”

“Three ciders,” Blaze said, pulling a few of the odd, hexagonal coins they’d scavenged from their apartment wreckage. The server scooped them up without comment and fluttered off.

Zonk watched the curtain. For five minutes, nothing. Then a unicorn in a sharp, pinstriped vest emerged, adjusting his cuffs. He looked pleased with himself. A minute later, a hulking earth pony with a split lip went in shoulders hunched. He came out three minutes after that, face pale, moving fast toward the exit.

“Not everyone gets good news,” Zonk observed.

Their ciders arrived. Zonk took a sip. It tasted like fermented apples and regret, with an aftertaste of nail polish remover. He set it down.

“We need a play,” Blaze said. “We can’t just sit here all night. That bouncer’s gonna count.”

He was right. The clock was ticking in Zonk’s head, a loud, stupid metronome. Squeak. Thud. He scanned the room again. His gaze landed on a group at the bar: three unicorns, laughing too loudly, their horns glowing faintly with intoxication magic. One of them, a mare with a silver streak in her mane kept glancing toward their table. No, not at their table. At Pip.

She said something to her friends, tossed back her drink, and slid off her stool. She weaved through the crowd with practiced ease, coming to a stop right beside Pip. She smelled of expensive perfume and ozone.

“Well, hello,” the mare purred, her voice like silk over gravel. “Aren’t you a little far from the sunlit gardens?”

Pip froze. Her ears went flat again. “I am… accompanying my associates.”

“Associates.” The unicorn’s eyes flicked to Zonk and Blaze, her smile turning sharp. “How… eclectic. I don’t recognize your brand, darling. New stable?”

“It’s a private mark,” Pip whispered, shrinking in on herself.

“Everything’s private until it’s not.” The mare reached out, a well-manicured finger heading for Pip’s chin.

Blaze’s hand shot out, catching the mare’s wrist before it made contact. He didn’t squeeze, just held it, his grip firm. “She’s with us.”

The unicorn’s smile didn’t falter. Her horn glowed, a faint silver light. “I see. And do you often speak for your assets?”

A hot, prickling feeling spread across Zonk’s scalp. Not quite anger, not quite panic. It was the feeling of a social script he hadn’t read, with consequences written in blood. He stood up. The motion felt jerky, unnatural.

“We’re here to see Brass Tacks,” he said, his voice cutting through the bar noise better than he’d expected. “About a mutual problem. He’ll want to hear it from the source.” He nodded toward Pip. “So hands off the merchandise.”

The unicorn’s eyes narrowed. She pulled her wrist from Blaze’s grip, smoothing her vest. “Brass sees who he chooses.”

“He’ll choose this,” Zonk said, gambling everything on a line he’d heard in a noir film once. “Tell him it’s about solar overreach and misplaced property. He’ll know.”

For a long second, she just stared at him. The bar seemed to grow quieter, though the music played on. Then she snorted, a derisive sound. “Wait here.”

She stalked back to her friends, whispered something. One of them glanced their way, then got up and slipped through the curtained arch.

“What the hell was that?” Blaze hissed, pulling Zonk back down. “‘Misplaced property’? Dude, you might as well have waved the tiara.”

“She was going to cause a scene,” Zonk said, his hands trembling slightly. He shoved them under the table. “We needed a bigger scene. A redirect.”

Pip was staring at him, her expression a mix of terror and awe. “That was… incredibly reckless.”

“It’s a specialty.”

The friend returned, nodded to the silver-maned unicorn. She pointed a deliberate hoof at their table.

The curtain parted. Not the friend, but a new figure. A unicorn stallion, but unlike any they’d seen. He was older, his coat a dull, steel grey, his mane cropped short and practical. He wore simple trousers and a leather apron, like a blacksmith. He held a polishing cloth in one hand. He didn’t look at the unicorn mare didn’t look at anyone. His eyes, a cool, assessing blue found Zonk’s across the crowded room. He jerked his head toward the archway.

Then he disappeared back through the curtain.

“Go,” the silver-maned unicorn said from the bar, her voice no longer playful. It was cold. “And don’t linger.”

They went. Zonk’s legs felt like they were made of wood. Blaze put a steadying hand on Pip’s back, guiding her. The chatter at the bar dipped as they passed, eyes following them. Zonk focused on the lightning-bolt crack in the wall, tracing its path until they reached the dark fabric.

He pushed the curtain aside.

The room beyond was small, warm, and smelled of oiled metal and old paper. Shelves lined the walls, crammed with curios: strange tools, sealed scrolls, jars of unidentifiable substances. A heavy desk dominated the space, its surface scarred and stained. The grey unicorn—Brass Tacks—was already behind it, rubbing a tarnished bit of metal with his cloth. He didn’t look up.

“Solar overreach,” he said, his voice a dry rasp, like pages turning. “Misplaced property. Cute. Now say something that isn’t a line from a bad street-play.”

Zonk’s mind went blank. The prepared speech evaporated. All he had was the truth, stripped of its clever packaging. “We stole the Elements of Harmony. The real ones. And now the Sun Court wants to turn us into a cautionary tale smeared across the cobblestones. We need to get rid of them. We were told you might know a way.”

Brass Tacks stopped polishing. He set the metal bit down with a soft, precise click. He looked up, his blue eyes moving from Zonk to Blaze to Pip, who was trying to make herself invisible behind Blaze’s leg.

“Told by who?”

“A… a rock farmer. Named Maud,” Blaze offered.

A flicker in those cool eyes. Recognition maybe. Or pity. “Maud. Of course.” Brass leaned back in his chair, the leather creaking. “So you’re the idiots who cracked the Sun Vault. I wondered when you’d surface.” He picked up his cloth again, resumed polishing. “There’s no ‘getting rid’ of them. Not through any door you can open. The Vault won’t take them back from thieves. Not without harmony. And you three…” He gestured with the cloth. “You’re a discordant mess.”

“We know,” Zonk said, defeat tasting bitter in his throat. “Maud told us. She said we’d need to fake it. A performance.”

“A performance.” Brass chuckled, a sound like stones grinding. “This whole world’s a performance. The trick is making the audience believe it.” He studied them again, longer this time. “You can’t go to the Sun. You can’t go to the Mane guilds—They’d sell you for a shiny bit. There’s only one power that has an interest in seeing Celestia’s foundation shaken, and the means to maybe, get you close enough to the Vault to try your little play.”

“Who?” Blaze asked.

Brass Tacks smiled. It wasn’t a nice smile. “Her sister. The Princess of the Night. Luna. She runs the Court that operates when the sun goes down. She’s the only one with the reach and the reason to bypass Celestia’s wards.” He leaned forward, elbows on the desk. “But she doesn’t do favors. She trades. And the price for an audience with her… Well. That’s the real question isn’t it?”

He let the silence hang. Somewhere in the bar, a glass shattered, followed by raucous laughter.

“What’s the price?” Zonk asked, already dreading the answer.

“I’ll let her tell you,” Brass said. “My price is simpler. You get your audience. And when she asks you to do something for her—and she will—you say yes. No matter what it is. You owe me that ‘yes.’ A favor, to be named later.”

Zonk looked at Blaze, who gave a minute, helpless shrug. He looked at Pip, who just stared at the floor, her fate once again in the hands of reckless strangers.

They were out of options. Out of time. Out of clever lines.

“Okay,” Zonk said, the word tasting like ash. “We agree.”

Brass Tacks nodded, as if he’d never expected anything else. He pulled a blank slip of parchment from a drawer, dipped a quill in an inkwell, and scrawled a single line. He blew on it, folded it, and handed it across the desk.

“Take this to the Lunar Spire. West district, where the streetlights are always broken. Give it to the gate shadow. Don’t be late. Night Court protocol is… less forgiving than a bouncer’s whim.”

Zonk took the paper. It was warm, almost humming with a faint, cold energy.

“One more thing,” Brass said as they turned to leave. He was looking at Pip again, his gaze lingering on her brand. “She’s not just a runaway is she?”

Pip flinched.

“She’s with us,” Blaze repeated, his voice hard.

“I’m sure she is,” Brass said softly. “Just remember. In this world, everypony belongs to somepony. Even the ones who think they’ve gotten away.”

He went back to his polishing, dismissing them. The audience was over.

Outside, the street was darker, the air colder. The stirrup on the sign squeaked on, a lonely metronomic beat in the gathering night. Zonk clutched the folded parchment, the promise of a favor he couldn’t imagine, a debt he already knew would be too heavy to pay.

Brass Tacks led them to a booth at the back of the bar, a nest of worn burgundy velvet and dark wood that smelled of spilled cider and old polish. The seat was still warm from its last occupants. Zonk slid in first, the leather creaking under him. Pip followed, tucking herself into the corner, her movements small and precise. Blaze took the outside edge, a human wall between them and the rest of the room. His knee bounced under the table, a steady, silent tremor.

The unicorn slid in across from them, his bulk making the whole booth shift. He set a fresh tankard down—this one held a dark, oily liquid that smelled of licorice and regret. He didn’t drink. Just looked at them, one scarred hoof resting on the tabletop. His cutie mark, the balancing scale seemed to glare in the low light.

“So,” Brass Tacks said. The words came out a gravel road. “You stole from the Sun Court. You’re harboring a branded asset without papers. And you want to find the one entity in this city who might, on her best day, tell Celestia to go fuck herself with a candelabra.” He took a slow sip. “Am I missing a detail, or is that the full portrait of your catastrophic life choices?”

“That’s the gist,” Zonk said. His own voice sounded thin in comparison. He cleared his throat. “We need to give the stuff back. To the Vault.”

“I know what the rock whisperer said.” Brass Tacks’s eyes—a flat, gunmetal grey—didn’t blink. “A bonded trio. A unified voice. Cute. You’ve got the aesthetic cohesion of a dog’s breakfast. The pony’s trembling, the big one’s leaking nervous energy like a cheap radiator, and you…” He leaned forward slightly. “You’re trying to calculate the angles. Always a bad sign.”

Zonk’s jaw tightened. He forced it to relax. Don’t pick at your thumb. Don’t.

“We can do unified,” Blaze said, his voice too loud for the booth. “We’re a team.”

“A team.” Brass Tacks snorted. A puff of vapor, smelling faintly of ozone, escaped his nostrils. “This isn’t a hoofball league. The Vault doesn’t want teamwork. It wants ownership. A clear, clean line of authority. A single will. You three look about as single-willed as a bucket of eels.”

Pip made a tiny, distressed sound. Brass Tacks’s ear twitched toward her, but his gaze stayed on Zonk.

“The location of Luna’s current court,” Zonk said, ignoring the commentary. “What’s the price?”

“Ah. Down to it.” The unicorn leaned back, the velvet sighing. “Favors are the currency of the desperate. I have a problem. A… delivery problem. A certain consignment of mood-stabilizing crystals meant for a client in the Pegasus Enclave, failed to arrive. The courier vanished. The client is unhappy. My reputation is, let’s say, tarnished.”

“You want us to find your drugs,” Blaze said.

“I want you to find my courier. Or the wreckage. Or a credible story. The crystals are secondary. The flow of information is primary. A broken link in the chain makes all the other links nervous.” He tapped his hoof on the wood. Tap. “You find out what happened. You bring me proof. Then we talk about Luna.”

“That’s a wild fucking goose chase,” Zonk said. The duck stain from the storage room flickered in his mind. “We’d be walking blind into some gang dispute or… or whatever eats couriers out there. We need a location, not an errand.”

“Then you have nothing to trade.” Brass Tacks started to slide out of the booth.

“Wait.”

It was Pip. She said it, small, but it didn’t shake. Both Zonk and Blaze stared at her. She was sitting up straight, her hands clasped in her lap, her eyes fixed on a point just over Brass Tacks’s left shoulder. A formal posture. “The courier. Would they have been using the high-altitude thermal drafts above the Smokestack District? Or the older, sanctioned routes through the Commerce Spires?”

Brass Tacks went very still. “The Spires. Sanctioned. Why?”

“Because the Smokestack drafts are contested by the Wonderbolt Syndicate after sundown. A sanctioned courier on a sanctioned route doesn’t just vanish.” She finally looked at him, her gaze direct. “They are intercepted. By someone with the authority to override the route protocols. Or the appearance of it.”

The air in the booth changed. The background murmur of the bar seemed to recede. Brass Tacks’s expression didn’t shift, but his eyes narrowed the smallest fraction. “Go on.”

Pip’s nostrils flared. She was breathing quickly. “Your reputation isn’t tarnished by a lost courier, Master Brass. It’s being tested. By a rival broker. Or by a client making a power play. They wanted to see if you could be made to look weak. Sending two confused humans and a… a defective companion,” she said the word without a flinch, “to chase the wreckage would be the response of someone who is weak. And desperate.”

Silence.

A waitress passed by with a tray of clinking glasses. Someone laughed, a sharp bark near the dartboard. The lemon-cleaner smell from the storage room was gone, replaced by the thick, sweet scent of the unicorn’s drink and the oil from the booth’s leather.

Zonk looked at Pip. Really looked. Her face was pale, but set. That institutional brand on her flank seemed to pulse in his peripheral vision. Textbook-perfect knowledge. Zero street-level understanding. But this… this was something else. This was the cold, clean logic of a system she’d been bred to understand. The politics of reputation. The chess moves of power.

It was fucking terrifying.

Brass Tacks let out a long, slow breath. “You’re not defective, little one. You’re dangerously well-trained.” He turned his head toward Zonk. “She’s right. Sending you would be a confession of weakness. So. New price.”

“We’re listening,” Zonk managed. His mouth was dust-dry.

“You will be my weakness. Publicly. You will sit here, in my bar, for the next cycle. You will look scared and out of your depth. You will let it be known you came to me for help and I turned you away with a pointless task. The rumor will spread: Brass Tacks is so scared of Celestia’s sunburned thugs that he won’t touch the biggest scandal to trot through the city in a decade.”

“I find my courier. I reassert my strength. Quietly. Meanwhile, the world sees me as cautious. Prudent. Not worth testing again.” He smiled. It didn’t reach his eyes. “And for playing the part of convincing fools, I give you a name. A place. A back door into the Night Court.”

“What’s to stop you from just taking the information after we’ve done your theater?” Zonk asked.

“The same thing that stops you from walking out of here and trying your luck in the alleys.” Brass Tacks gestured vaguely toward the bar’s entrance. “Mutually assured annihilation. You need me. I need you to be visibly useless. A simple transaction.”

Pip was shaking again, fine tremors running through her shoulders. The burst of analytical clarity had drained her, leaving the fear behind. Zonk felt that irritating responsibility knot in his gut again, tighter this time.

“We stay here,” he said. “In the open. Where the Contract-Wardens could walk in any minute.”

“The Wardens don’t come to the Gilded Saddle,” Brass Tacks said, a hint of pride in his rumble. “My licenses are in order. My taxes are paid. I am a respectable businessman. They have no cause. And every other cutthroat in this district will see you here, under my roof, and assume you’re already my problem. Or my property.” His gaze lingered on Pip. “That will keep the wolves at bay better than any back-alley hidey-hole.”

It was a choice between a visible cage and an invisible hunt. Zonk hated both. He looked at Blaze, who gave a minute, helpless shrug. Your call.

“The name,” Zonk said. “Now. Not after.”

Brass Tacks studied him for a long moment. Then he dipped a hoof into his tankard, lifted it, and drew a symbol on the sticky tabletop with the dark liquid. It was a crescent moon, cradling a single, stylized star. The smell of licorice grew stronger. “The Silver Crescent. A bathhouse. Three blocks east, in the Gleaming District. Ask for the ‘midnight soak.’ The attendant will be a thestral named Murk. Tell him Brass sent you with the moon’s blessing. He’ll get you to a facilitator.”

“And the facilitator gets us to Luna?” Blaze asked.

“The facilitator decides if you’re worth the dust on her shoes. That’s not my department.” He wiped the symbol away with a swipe of his hoof, leaving a dark, wet smear. “Your part starts now. Look scared. Drink something. Try not to cause a scene.”

He slid out of the booth and was gone, melting back into the shadowy crowd near the bar.

For a full minute, none of them spoke. The noise of the bar rushed back in—a wave of clattering and conversation that felt like a physical pressure. Zonk became acutely aware of the grain of the wood under his fingers, the specific pattern of water rings and knife scars on the table’s surface. One of them looked like a little house. A stupid, simple house.

“Well,” Blaze finally said, slumping back against the velvet. “We’re sitting ducks.”

“Publicly useless ones,” Zonk corrected. His heart was doing a strange, uneven thump against his ribs. Not quite panic. Not quite anger. The nameless hot thing in his chest. “We just have to sit here and look like we’ve given up.”

Pip curled a little tighter into her corner. “I am sorry,” she said, low. “I spoke out of turn.”

“You were brilliant,” Zonk cut her off. The words surprised him. They were true. “You saved us from a suicide mission. Now we just have to… sit.”

So they sat. Blaze ordered a pitcher of something that smelled like fermented apples and regret. They pretended to drink. Zonk watched the door, every flash of color in the entrance making his breath hitch. Pip stared at her clasped hands. Blaze tried to make a game out of guessing patrons’ jobs by their cutie marks, but his jokes fell flat, swallowed by the general din.

Time stretched. The caged light above a nearby gambling table flickered once, twice, casting jagged shadows. Zonk noticed a fly trapped between the windowpane and the grimy velvet curtain next to their booth. It buzzed, a frantic, tiny sound, beating itself against the glass. Going nowhere.

The alley behind the Gilded Saddle smelled of rotten vegetables and ozone, the slick cobblestones catching the sickly purple glow of a buzzing sign for a defunct enchantment parlor. Zonk’s shoulder throbbed where it had connected with the doorframe on the way out. Brass Tacks’s final words—The Night Court’s eyes are everywhere. Run fast, little thieves—still hummed in his ears, a low, unpleasant frequency.

Blaze took point, his bulk a moving shadow ahead. Pip pressed close to Zonk’s side, her small hand clutching a fistful of his hoodie. Her breathing was a rapid shallow patter against the alley’s deeper sounds: a distant clatter of trash cans, the hum of something magical and unseen in the walls.

“Left,” Blaze hissed, veering into a narrower cut between two taller buildings. The space was tight, forcing them single-file. Dumpsters lined the walls, overflowing with glittering refuse—broken crystal goblets, torn scrolls, a single, oversized satin glove.

Zonk’s foot slipped on something wet. He caught himself against a damp brick wall, his palm coming away sticky. The smell here was different. Not just garbage. Metallic. Coppery. His stomach did a slow, cold roll. He didn’t mention it.

“This is a dead end,” Pip said, her voice small.

Blaze had stopped at a chain-link fence that sealed the passage. Beyond it, a wider, better-lit street pulsed with neon. Freedom maybe. The fence was ten feet high, crowned with spirals of razor wire that glinted with a faint, menacing enchantment.

“Can we climb it?” Blaze asked, already gripping the links.

“The wire,” Zonk said. “It’s spelled. Look at the shimmer. That’s not just sharp, it’s… hungry.”

Blaze dropped his hand. “Great. Magical barbed wire. Of course.” He turned, his back to the fence, scanning the alley they’d just left. “Okay. Back the way we came. Find another turn.”

A door slammed, loud in the confined space. The sound came from the mouth of the alley they’d entered. Heavy footsteps, not trying to be quiet. The clop-clop of hooves on stone, but mixed with the jangle of harness and gear.

“Contract-Wardens,” Pip breathed. The terror in She spoke, the sound a live wire. Her grip on Zonk’s hoodie tightened, twisting the fabric against his neck.

Blaze cursed, a low, creative string of words. He looked at the fence, then back at the approaching sounds. The dumpsters. He pointed to the largest one, a hulking metal thing crusted with unidentifiable grime. “In. Now.”

“In the trash?” Zonk’s voice cracked.

“You got a better portal to Narnia in your pocket? Move!”

Blaze lifted the heavy lid. The smell that billowed out was breathtaking—a fungal, sweet-rot stench that made Zonk’s eyes water. Without ceremony, Blaze hooked his hands under Pip’s arms and lifted her. She gave a tiny squeak of protest, but didn’t fight, her legs disappearing over the rim. Blaze vaulted in after her, the dumpster groaning under his weight.

The footsteps were closer. A voice, gruff and bored. “Check the recesses. She said they’d bolt for the seams.”

Zonk stopped thinking. He grabbed the cold edge, hauled himself up, and tumbled into the darkness. The lid came down behind him with a soft, definitive thud, plunging them into absolute blackness and overwhelming stink.

He landed on something soft and lumpy. Fabric. Maybe ruined costumes. Pip was a warm, trembling shape to his left. Blaze was a solid wall on his right. The dumpster was deeper than it looked. They were buried.

The footsteps stopped right outside.

Zonk held his breath. The air in the dumpster was hot, thick with decay and his own panic. He could feel every beat of his heart against his ribs, a frantic bird in a cage too small. Something dug into his hip. A broken handle. He didn’t move.

Outside, a snort. “Nothing. Just vermin.”

“The fence is clean. They didn’t go this way.”

“Check the next artery. Move out.”

The footsteps receded. The silence they left behind was worse. It had a texture, like wool stuffed in Zonk’s ears. He waited, counting to thirty in the dark. The only sounds were Pip’s shaky attempts to breathe quietly and the distant, eternal hum of the city.

“Okay,” Blaze murmured finally, his voice muffled by the confines. “I think they’re gone.”

“Don’t lift the lid yet,” Zonk said. His own voice sounded strange. Thin. “Give it another minute.”

They sat in the foul dark. Zonk became aware of a thousand tiny details. The scratch of cheap velvet against his wrist. The faint, sugary smell of spoiled cordial clinging to one particular lump of trash. A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: his mother’s perfume, something cloying and floral she’d worn before she left. It had smelled nothing like this, but the association was there, a neural misfire in the stress. He pushed it away.

Pip shifted. A small, choked sound escaped her.

“You okay?” Blaze asked, his whisper gentler now.

“I am… adhering to protocol,” she said quietly back, her voice thick. “Quiet in the face of adversity. It is… it is very dark.”

“It’s just a dumpster,” Zonk said, but the words felt inadequate. Hollow. It wasn’t just a dumpster. It was a metal coffin hiding them from glittering batons and contractual oblivion. It was the logical endpoint of his entire philosophy: nothing matters, so why not steal from a VR game? And now he was hiding in literal garbage with a talking pony and his best friend, all of them terrified.

Something hot and sharp moved behind his sternum. Not quite anger. Not quite fear. It was the feeling of a joke that had stopped being funny, the punchline lingering in a silent room where everyone was just waiting for someone to speak.

“I’m sorry,” Pip said, low into the blackness.

“For what?” Blaze said. “You didn’t do anything.”

“For being a burden. For requiring protection. A proper… a proper companion would have a skill. Would be an asset in evasion. I can recite the lineage of the Canterlot noble houses. I know seventeen approved methods for polishing ceremonial bits. This…” She trailed off. A small, wet sniff. “This is not in my training.”

Zonk felt the words land. He saw her in his mind’s eye, curled in the trash, her perfect manners a useless shield. Her brand was a mark of that training. An institution that taught her how to serve and shine, but not how to run or fight or hide. They’d made her fragile on purpose. Something in him curdled, a cold, slow anger that had nothing to do with the guards outside.

“Your training’s bullshit,” he said, the words coming out rougher than he intended.

Pip went very still.

“Zonk,” Blaze warned.

“No. It is.” He couldn’t stop. The pressure in his chest needed a valve. “They taught you how to be a good ornament. How to be manageable. That’s not a skill. That’s a cage. Knowing how to run when someone’s trying to smash you? That’s a skill. Knowing when to hide in a fucking dumpster? You’re learning it right now. So stop apologizing for not already knowing the things they never wanted you to know.”

The silence after he finished was absolute. He could hear Blaze breathing. He could hear the faint drip-drip of some leaky enchantment outside.

Then, a very small voice. “You are angry.”

“Yes,” Zonk said. He wasn’t sure who he was angry at. The world. Celestia. The game. Himself. All of it.

“I… I do not know what to do with anger,” Pip admitted. “Protocol advises soothing tones and submissive postures to de-escalate.”

“Forget the protocol.”

“I do not know how.”

The simple honesty of it gutted him. His detached, analytical calm shattered. He didn’t feel chill. He felt raw. The dumpster walls were too close. The smell was in his hair, his clothes, his lungs. This was real. She was real. Her fear was a tangible thing in the dark between them, and his own fear was answering it, a silent, screaming chorus.

“Then just be scared,” he said, the fight gone from his voice. “Just be scared with us. That’s the protocol. That’s the only one that works here.”

He felt her move. Then, a slight, tentative pressure against his arm. She was leaning into him, her head resting against his shoulder. She was still trembling. He didn’t shrug her off. He sat in the reeking dark, feeling the weight of her, the solid, real presence of Blaze beside him, and let the pretense of detachment fall away. It was a relief, like dropping a heavy bag he hadn’t known he was carrying.

After a long moment, Blaze let out a slow breath. “Alright. I’m gonna peek. Stay low.”

The lid lifted an inch. A sliver of sickly purple light cut across the trash, illuminating motes of dust and the terrified, wide-eyed look on Pip’s upturned face. Zonk looked away, his throat tight.

“Clear,” Blaze murmured. “Let’s go. Slowly.”

They emerged like ghosts from a tomb, blinking in the neon glow. The alley was empty. The street beyond the fence still pulsed, indifferent. They were free, for another few minutes. Zonk wiped his sticky hand on his jeans, the metallic smell still clinging to him. He didn’t look at Pip. He just started walking, following Blaze’s lead, the three of them moving as one unit through the garish, dangerous night.
