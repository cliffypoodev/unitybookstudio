The alley smelled of wet cardboard and the ghost of piss. Zonk leaned against the cold brick, his lungs burning. The bass from the club was a fading tremor in his teeth. Blaze stood a few feet away, hands on his knees, breathing in ragged gulps. A dark bruise bloomed under his left eye, and his knuckles were scraped raw.

Pip stood between them, shivering. Her pastel bodysuit was smudged with alley grime, and one of her fake ears had come partially unstitched, flopping at a sad angle. She looked from Blaze to Zonk and back, her eyes wide and liquid in the dim light. “That was… Suboptimal.”

“Suboptimal.” Zonk pushed off the wall. A laugh escaped him, thin and airless. “Yeah. That’s one word for it.” His thumb was bleeding again. He’d picked the scab off during the cage-latch fumbling. He stuck it in his mouth, the copper taste familiar. “You okay?”

Blaze straightened up, wincing as he rolled his shoulder. “Peachy. Just a friendly discussion about workplace safety standards. They were very passionate.” He cracked his neck. “You get the stuff?”

The stuff. The Elements. Zonk’s stomach, which had been a tight knot of adrenaline, dropped into his shoes. He looked at Pip. Pip looked at the empty space where her little satchel should have been. Her hands patted her sides, a frantic, hopeless gesture.

“The… the bag,” she said quietly. “It was on my shoulder. In the conga line. I felt the strap. And then I didn’t.”

Silence, thick and heavy. A dumpster lid creaked in a faint breeze. Somewhere, a pipe dripped.

“Fuck,” Blaze said. It wasn’t angry. It was final. A stone dropped down a well.

They’d lost them. After everything. The panic in the apartment, the chase, the stupid dungeon delivery, the audience with Luna, Blaze’s capture, the whole ridiculous conga-line rescue—all of it, for nothing. The Sacred Elements of Harmony were gone, probably scooped up by a bouncer or some party-goer who thought they were fancy party favors. Zonk’s brain, usually so quick with a sarcastic loop or a pop-culture reference, just offered static. A blank screen. He noticed a faded graffiti tag on the wall opposite—a smiling sun with X’s for eyes. The irony was so perfect it was boring.

“Luna’s gonna be pissed,” Blaze said quietly.

That was the understatement of the century. Their only move was the safehouse. The directions were a tangled mess in Zonk’s head—left at the fountain shaped like a rearing stallion down the service alley behind the bakery with the pink awning, third cellar grate on the right. It felt like walking to their own execution.

The city at night was different. The candy colors turned to muted pastels under the faux-moonlight, the streets mostly empty except for the occasional cloaked figure or a patrolling guard duo whose polished armor clicked in the quiet. They stuck to the shadows, a trio of failures. Zonk kept waiting for Pip to cry, or to launch into some earnest speech about how it wasn’t their fault. She just walked, her head down, one hand occasionally reaching up to touch her drooping ear. The silence was worse.

The bakery, Sugar Cube Corner was dark. They slipped down the service alley, the scent of yesterday’s frosting clinging to the air. The third cellar grate was heavy iron. Blaze grabbed the handle, muscles straining, and lifted it with a groan of rust. A set of narrow stone steps led down into darkness that smelled of damp earth and yeast.

A single glowing crystal set in a sconce at the bottom of the stairs provided the only light. It illuminated a small, circular cellar. Stone walls. A rough wooden table. And Princess Luna, standing perfectly still beside it, waiting.

She looked like a statue carved from moonlight and shadow. Her dark blue ensemble was simpler than her court robes, but no less imposing. She watched them descend, her expression unreadable. Her gaze swept over Blaze’s bruised face, Pip’s grimy dishevelment, Zonk’s general aura of defeat. It lingered on Pip’s empty hands.

“You are unharmed,” Luna said. It wasn’t a question.

“Mostly,” Blaze grunted, lowering the grate back into place with a solid clang that echoed in the small space.

“The Elements,” Luna said, her voice cool.

“Gone,” Zonk said. The word tasted like ash. “Lost in the club. During the… extraction.”

Luna was silent for a long moment. The only sound was the drip of water somewhere in the stones. Zonk found himself staring at the wood grain of the table. It swirled like a frozen whirlpool, one knot looking exactly like a frowning face. He wanted to point it out. He didn’t.

“They are not lost,” Luna said finally.

Zonk’s head snapped up. “What?”

“Solar Sentinels raided the Party Cannon approximately twenty minutes after your departure. They recovered a satchel containing six items matching the description of the Sacred Elements.” A faint, icy smile touched her lips. “My sources within the Sun Court are prompt. The Elements are now secured in the central vault beneath the Grand Ballroom. For the Gala.”

The relief that washed through Zonk was immediately chased by a colder, sharper dread. “So… They’re safe? We’re off the hook?”

Luna’s smile vanished. “You misunderstand. Their placement in the vault is not for safekeeping. It is for display. For use. Celestia intends to perform the Ascension Ritual at the height of the Gala, using the Elements—recovered from treacherous thieves—to renew her contracts with the major syndicates in a grand, public spectacle. Her power will be cemented for another century.” She stepped closer, the light from the crystal casting deep shadows under her eyes. “Your original mission, to use them to embarrass her is now impossible. The vault is impregnable.”

Blaze let out a long, slow breath. “So we’re back to square one. Actually, square negative five. Great.”

“No.” Luna turned and walked to the far wall, where a heavy weave hung—a scene of a starry night sky over a peaceful meadow. With a sharp tug on a concealed cord, the pattern swung aside, revealing a low, arched doorway. “We are at square zero. A new board. Come.”

The room beyond was smaller, warmer. A fire crackled in a tiny hearth, and the air held the scent of old paper and lavender. An ancient pony sat in a high-backed chair, a blanket over her lap. Her coat was a faded gray, her mane white and wispy. Her eyes, though were sharp and clear as she watched them enter.

“This,” Luna said, her voice softening a fraction, “is Mistmane. One of the last of the Old Herd.”

Mistmane inclined her head. She spoke—a dry rustle of leaves. “You carry the stink of chaos, children. And fear. Sit.”

There were no other chairs. They remained standing, clustered near the fire. Pip, for the first time seemed to forget her distress. She stared at the old pony with something like reverence.

“The Elements,” Luna began, pacing slowly before the hearth. “You know them as tools of control. Contracts. The foundation of my sister’s power.”

“Yeah, the magical BDSM kit,” Zonk said, the old defense mechanism kicking in. “We got the memo.”

Luna didn’t react to the flippancy. “That is what they are. It is not what they were.” She stopped, looking at Mistmane. “Tell them.”

Mistmane’s gnarled hands smoothed the blanket. “In the beginning, before the Courts, before the contracts, there was only connection. The Elements were… facilitators. Not enforcers.” She closed her eyes, as if remembering a taste. “Honesty was the mirror that helped one see oneself truly, without judgment. Laughter was the release of shared joy, not a gag. Loyalty was the promise that held fast because it was chosen, not locked.” She opened her eyes, pinning Zonk with her gaze. “They were tuned to a different frequency. A frequency of genuine harmony, not imposed order.”

Zonk’s mind, always chasing connections made one. “You’re saying they’re like… a radio. And Celestia’s broadcasting on a shitty AM station, but they can still get FM.”

A faint smile touched Mistmane’s lips. “A crude analogy. But not incorrect. The hardware remains. The software has been corrupted.”

Blaze crossed his arms. “Okay. So they used to be nice. History lesson. How does that help us now? They’re in a vault, tuned to the evil station.”

Luna turned, her eyes alight. “Because the ritual at the Gala requires the Elements to be active. To be played, like instruments.”

“The broadcast jams,” Zonk finished, the idea unfolding in his head, terrible and brilliant. “All her contracts, the power source—it glitches. It doesn’t renew.”

“Revert,” Mistmane said, low. “To their true function. A wave of genuine connection, recalibrating every bond it touches to its base state: consent.”

The weight of it settled in the room. This wasn’t about returning stolen property anymore. This was about throwing a wrench into the engine of a whole reality. Zonk’s mouth went dry. He thought of the guard back in his apartment, the casual violence. He thought of Pip getting backhanded. He thought of the broken man in Pinkie’s club. A hot, sour feeling moved through his chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that wanted to be heroic but mostly just wanted to hide.

“We can’t get into the vault,” Blaze said, practical as ever. “You said it yourself.”

“You will not need to,” Luna replied. “The Elements will be brought out for the ritual. On display. You will be there.”

“As what? Guests? I don’t think we’re on the list.”

“As staff.” Luna’s smile returned, edged with cunning. “The Gala employs hundreds of temporary servants. My operatives have already secured three positions: a waiter, a coat-check attendant, and a junior assistant to the floral arranger. You will be inside. When the Elements are presented, you will be close.”

Pip found her voice, small but clear. “But… how do we change the frequency? We are not technicians of harmony.”

Mistmane shifted in her chair, reaching for a small wooden box on the table beside her. She opened it. Inside, on a bed of faded velvet, lay six small, smooth stones. Each was a different, muted color. They hummed with a faint, warm light that pulsed in time with the fire.

“Tuning stones,” Mistmane said. “From the old quarries, before they were sealed. They resonate with the original song.” She took out a pale pink stone—Laughter. “You must make physical contact between the stone and its corresponding Element at the moment of the ritual’s peak. The hum will do the rest.”

Blaze picked up a deep red stone—Loyalty. He rolled it in his palm. “So we just… touch them? That’s it?”

“The ritual chamber will be shielded, guarded, and watched by every power in this city,” Luna said, her voice dropping. “Celestia will be at her most powerful, channeling the focused will of the entire Court. Getting close enough to touch will be… a significant challenge.”

Zonk looked at the stone in Blaze’s hand, then at the fire, then at the worried crease between Pip’s fake ears. The plan was insane. It was a heist-movie climax directed by a lunatic. It required precision, timing, and a level of bravery he was pretty sure he’d left in a baggie back in his apartment. The old detachment tried to rise, to scoff, to make a joke about the odds.

It didn’t come.

All he could think about was the frowning knot in the table in the other room. And the fact that he never wanted to see Pip look that scared again.

“Okay,” he heard himself say. The word hung in the lavender-scented air. “So we’re going to a party.”

The cellar was a cold stone fist holding them. Luna’s words hung in the damp air: not lost, just relocated to the most heavily guarded vault in the city. The relief that washed over Zonk was brief and tepid, a warm trickle that cooled instantly when it hit the reality of what came next. His thumb found the raw spot again. The taste of blood was a grounding note in the yeasty gloom.

“So we break into a vault,” Blaze said, rolling his sore shoulder. “During a party. Swell.”

“The Gala is not merely a party,” Luna corrected, her voice the soft scrape of velvet over ice. “It is the apex of Celestia’s social control. Every contract, every alliance is renewed in its shadow. The vault will be at its most secure, and its most… accessible. For those with the correct invitations.”

She turned and walked toward the rear wall of the cellar, which wasn’t a wall at all but a heavy, moth-eaten cloth depicting a night sky choked with stars. She drew it aside. Behind it was an archway, choked with the creeping tendrils of some dark, flowering vine. The scent from the garden beyond was overwhelming—cloying sweetness undercut by the rot of fallen petals. It smelled like a funeral home’s idea of a garden.

“The plan has changed,” Luna said, and stepped through.

They followed, single file. The garden was a small, walled rectangle open to the faux-night sky, a pocket of neglected wildness. Moonlight-blooming flowers hung heavy on their stalks, their white petals bruised brown at the edges. A stone bench was nearly stopped by ivy. And in the center, standing very still among the weeds were three figures.

They were ponies, but unlike any Zonk had seen. No sleek bodysuits, no corporate-cute colors. Their fur—real fur, he realized with a jolt—was muted. A dusty gray, a faded roan, a soft dun. They wore simple cloth wraps, not armor. They looked old. Not ancient, but worn, like stones smoothed by a river. Their eyes held none of Pip’s eager brightness or the guards’ aggressive gleam. They just watched.

One of them, the dun-colored one with a straggly mane the color of dishwater took a step forward. Her hooves made no sound on the mossy flagstones.

“Luna,” the dun pony said. The words came out like dry leaves. “These are the ones from the other side?”

“They are,” Luna said. She didn’t move to stand with them. She remained by the archway, a mediator, a dealer. Not one of them. “They carry the dissonance. They can touch the Elements without… conditioning.”

The dun pony’s gaze settled on Zonk. It wasn’t hostile. It was assessing, the way you’d look at a strange tool whose purpose you couldn’t quite guess. “I am Meadow Brook. This is Silverfish.” She gestured a hoof to the gray one. “And Cinder.” The roan. “We are what remains of the Old Herd. We remember the song before it was made a chain.”

Pip made a small, choked sound. She was staring, her good ear twitching. “The Old Herd? But… you’re a myth. A foal’s tale. They said you were broken. That your magic was silence.”

“Silence is not broken,” said the one called Silverfish. He said it low, a rumble. “It is waiting.”

Blaze shifted his weight, the gravel crunching under his boot. “Okay. Hi. Great to meet you. What’s the play? You got a map of the vault? Schematics?”

Meadow Brook blinked slowly. “The vault is a dead end. The Elements within are inert. Locked into Celestia’s frequency. To use them as keys to re-tune the great contracts, they must first be… Reminded.”

“Reminded of what?” Zonk asked. The word felt stupid in his mouth.

“Of what they are,” Cinder said. She was the smallest, her voice a rasp. “They are not control. They were never control. They are… alignment. A ring. A tuning fork for the heart.” She looked at Pip, and for the first time, something like pity touched her weathered face. “You were taught they compel harmony. They do not. They reveal it. They make visible the bonds that are already there, genuine and freely given. Celestia perverted them. She turned a mirror into a hammer.”

Zonk’s brain, still scrambling for purchase, latched onto the metaphor. A mirror. Not a hammer. He thought of the tiara, the way it had felt on his brow in the game—not like power, but like a sudden, uncomfortable clarity. Like seeing a high-definition replay of your own dumb mistake.

“So we can’t just grab them and do a ritual,” he said, piecing it together. “We have to… what, wake them up? With a heartfelt speech?”

“With a connection,” Meadow Brook said. “The original ritual required no incantation. No blood-signing. It required six beings, their hearts in true alignment, each embodying one principle not as a duty, but as a nature. The Elements would amplify that. Make it tangible. Make it magic.” She sighed, a weary sound. “Celestia’s ritual forces alignment through pain and contract. It is a lie that produces a functional spark. Enough to maintain her system. We seek the true flame.”

The garden was quiet. Somewhere, a cricket chirped, a lonely, mechanical sound. Zonk noticed a spiderweb strung between two drooping flower heads, dewdrops caught along its threads like a string of tiny, clear lights. It was beautiful and pointless.

“So we’re screwed,” Blaze said, flat. “Because we’re not six. And we’re definitely not in true alignment of heart or whatever. I barely know what I had for breakfast.”

“You are three,” Silverfish rumbled. “And you have carried the dissonance. You have touched the Elements without being broken by them. This is more than any in this world have done for generations. You are a crack in the lie.”

“And we are three,” Meadow Brook said, nodding to her companions. “We remember the song. We hold the old frequency, though we cannot manifest it alone. Our hearts are aligned—in silence, in memory, in yearning.”

“Six,” Luna said from the shadows of the archway. She said it pure business. “A quorum. You will perform the ritual at the Gala. Not to empower Celestia, but to recalibrate the Elements to their original function. When the master control key—the tiara—is activated in that state, it will not renew the coercive contracts. It will dissolve them. A system reboot to factory settings, as it were.”

Zonk stared at her. “Factory settings. You want us to walk into the most secure event on the planet, surrounded by people who want to kill us, and perform a group therapy session with magical artifacts to reboot your society.”

“Yes.”

“And you’ll be…”

“Providing the invitation. And the distraction. My Night Court operatives will ensure the ritual space is clear at the appointed moment. Our interests are aligned.” That icy smile again. It never touched her eyes.

Pip took a step toward the Old Herd ponies. “But… if the Elements become a mirror again,” she said, “what will they show? About us?”

Meadow Brook looked at her, and then at Zonk and Blaze. Her old eyes were deep, still pools. “The truth. They will show the bonds between you for what they are. Strong or weak. True or false. There is no hiding from it. That was always the point. To see, and to choose.”

A cold knot tightened in Zonk’s gut, different from the fear of guards or clubs. This was a quieter, more intimate dread. The tiara had shown him something before, a flash of connection in the game that had felt too real, too sharp. He didn’t want to see what it would show now, after everything. He didn’t want Blaze to see it. He didn’t want to know what Pip’s earnest heart reflected back at his own cynical mess.

Blaze cracked his knuckles, the sound loud in the quiet garden. “So we gotta be emotionally honest. Got it.” He said it like he was agreeing to a difficult but straightforward repair job. “How do we do the… Tuning?”

Cinder moved forward. From a fold in her simple wrap, she drew out a small, cloth-wrapped bundle. She unfolded it on the mossy ground. Inside lay six ordinary-looking stones, each about the size of a walnut, each a different, dull color. A murky red, a cloudy blue, a lackluster pink.

“These are focus stones,” she rasped. “They hold an echo of the Elements’ true nature, unattuned. You must each take one. Hold it. And you must, together, find a moment of genuine weight. A moment where the principle of your stone is alive between you, without force, without contract. The stone will warm. It will clear. It will teach you the feeling. That feeling is what you must bring to the true Element at the ritual.”

She nudged the cloth toward them with a hoof. The stones looked like driveway gravel.

Pip knelt first, her movements reverent. She picked up the pink stone. It sat in her palm, inert. Blaze shrugged, scooped up the red one. Zonk was left with the blue. It was cool and smooth, with a faint white vein running through it like a crack. He closed his fingers around it. Nothing.

“Now what?” Blaze asked, rolling the red stone in his palm.

“Now,” Meadow Brook said softly, “you talk. You remember. You fight. You forgive. You are friends are you not? Find the thread. Pull it.”

They stood there in the dying garden, holding their stupid rocks. The cricket chirped again. Luna watched, a statue of moonlight. The Old Herd waited. And Zonk held the cool, silent stone in his hand and had absolutely no idea what to say.

The cellar’s damp, yeasty smell was giving Zonk a headache. Or maybe that was the aftermath of adrenaline, the hollowed-out feeling of having lost and then, impossibly, not lost the only things that mattered in this pastel hellscape. Luna’s words, a cold fact. The Elements were safe. Safe in the most heavily guarded vault in the city, under the nose of the terrifying sun-queen who wanted to turn them into decorative furniture. Great.

Elder Bloom, the Old Herd survivor Luna had produced from some deeper, darker hole, looked like a dried root someone had dressed in a burlap sack. His eyes were milky, but they didn’t wander. They fixed on Zonk, then Blaze, then Pip, with an intensity that felt like a physical poke.

“The ritual is not a switch,” the old pony said, his voice a rasp of stone on stone. “It is a tuning fork. You cannot force the note. You must be the note.”

“Right,” Zonk said. He rubbed his thumb over the fresh scab. “So we just, what, hum?”

Bloom didn’t smile. He gestured to the center of the cellar floor, where a shallow stone basin had been filled with water. The surface was perfectly still, reflecting the single crystal’s glow in a wavering coin of light. “You will look. You will speak. You will not lie. The water rejects artifice. It shows only what is.”

Blaze shifted his weight, his shoulder giving a soft pop. “And this does what, exactly?”

“It begins the recalibration,” Luna answered from the shadows near the wall. She hadn’t moved. She was a piece of the darkness, watching. “The Elements respond to genuine harmonic echo. Your bond—such as it is—must be made manifest. The water will reflect its quality. If it is true, the Elements in the vault will… stir. A sympathetic vibration. It is the first step.”

Pip took a small step toward the basin. She’d fixed her ear, stitching it back in place with a thread she’d produced from somewhere. The grime was still on her suit. “What do we say?”

“Whatever truth weighs on you most,” Bloom said. “The water pulls it out. Start with the simple cords. The fears.”

A silence followed, thick and uncomfortable. Zonk’s mind skittered away from the idea of truth, of fears. He noticed a hairline crack in the stone rim of the basin, a tiny fork like a lightning bolt frozen in gray. He focused on that. Anything was better than the pressure of those milky eyes.

Blaze cleared his throat. “Fine. I’ll go.” He walked to the basin, his movements stiff. He stared down into the water. His reflection looked back, bruised and tired. “I’m afraid I’m gonna get them killed.” The words came out flat, like he was reading a grocery list. “Zonk and Pip. Because I charge in. Because I think I can punch the magic away. And I can’t.”

The water didn’t change. It just sat there, holding his battered face.

Bloom gave a slow nod. “A true fear. But spoken to the air. You must speak to them. They are the cord.”

Blaze’s jaw worked. He didn’t turn around. His shoulders were rigid. He spoke to the water, but the sound came out a fraction softer. “Zonk. I’m afraid I’m gonna get you killed. Pip. I’m… I’m sorry I got grabbed. It put you both in the fire.”

Something happened. Not to the water, but to the air. A subtle charge, like the moment before a thunderstorm. Zonk felt the hair on his arms prickle. He hadn’t known Blaze could sound like that—stripped of all the bravado, just raw, ugly honesty. It was worse than seeing him in the cage.

Pip was next. She drifted to the basin’s edge, her movements quiet. She looked so small. She didn’t hesitate. “I’m afraid I’m useless.” The way she said it was a clear, quiet bell in the damp cellar. “I was made for a cushion in a solar parlor. I know protocols and poetry and how to arrange fruit. I don’t know how to fight. Or be brave. I’m just… a thing that needs protecting. And I’m afraid that’s all I’ll ever be. To either of you.”

The water shimmered. Not a lot. Just a ripple, as if a single drop had fallen from the ceiling, though none had. Zonk saw Pip’s reflection distort, then settle. What she said hit him in the quiet, and that old, familiar pressure built behind his ribs—the need to deflect, to make a joke, to call her a dumbass for thinking that. He held the thought in. It tasted bitter.

Then it was his turn. His feet felt like they were bolted to the stone floor. Everyone was looking at him. Even Luna, from the shadows. The crack in the basin rim seemed to taunt him. Just a little fracture. Nothing important.

He walked over. The water showed him a pale guy with messy hair and eyes that looked like they hadn’t seen a good night’s sleep since the Bush administration. First or second, who could remember.

Bloom said, “The truth.”

“I don’t want this,” Zonk said. The words were out before he could shape them into something clever. They just fell into the cellar. “Any of it. I don’t want to be responsible. For the fate of dimensions. For… for you two.” He nodded toward the reflections that weren’t his. “I liked it when it was a game. I liked it when the only thing I had to keep alive was my character sheet and my buzz.” His throat was tight. This was stupid. This was a bowl of water. “I’m scared that if I actually try, if I actually care, I’ll fuck it up worse than if I just didn’t give a shit at all. Because that’s what happens. Things leave. Or they break.”

He stopped. He’d run out of words. The water remained still for a long second. Then it didn’t ripple. It clouded. A faint, gray haze bloomed from the center, swirling through his reflection, obscuring it. Not anger. Not panic. A sick, gray shame.

No one spoke.

Bloom let out a long, slow pause. “Good.”

Blaze moved first. Not toward the basin. Toward Pip. He didn’t say anything. He just put a big, heavy hand on her shoulder. It wasn’t a pat. It was an anchor. She leaned into the touch, her head dipping. Her good ear brushed his wrist. She didn’t cry. She just stood there, accepting the weight of his hand, and for a second she didn’t look like a lost kid or a living prop. She looked like someone being steadied.

Zonk watched them. That hot, nameless something churned in his chest. It wasn’t jealousy. It was more like loneliness, but sharper, like a splinter under a nail. He was the one who’d just vomited his fear into a magic bowl, and Blaze was over there doing the one useful thing he always knew how to do: be solid. Be there.

“The cord is weak,” Luna observed, her voice cutting through the moment. “But it is genuine. It is a start.”

“The water reacted,” Bloom said, pointing a gnarled hoof at the cloudy basin. “The connection is acknowledged. The Elements will have felt that. A faint pulse. It is enough to proceed with the Gala plan.”

The Gala plan. Right. Infiltrate the biggest party of the year, break into the vault, and perform a ritual they barely understood in front of a domme-mob boss and all her armed-to-the-teeth guests. With a weak, genuine cord.

Blaze’s hand slid from Pip’s shoulder. He flexed his fingers, like he was surprised by his own action. “So what’s next?”

“Next,” Luna said, stepping from the shadows, “we teach you to hold the note. And you,” she said, her starry eyes landing on Zonk, “will learn that caring is not a flaw in your armor. It is the armor.”

Zonk looked away, back to the cloudy water. His reflection was gone, paused in gray. He could still see the crack in the stone. A tiny, meaningless fracture. He had the sudden, absurd thought that he wanted a cup of coffee. Not the good stuff. The bitter, burnt gas-station kind that tasted like regret and asphalt. Something real from a world that made sense.

Pip’s voice, soft but clear, broke his stare. “Thank you, Blaze.”

“For what?”

“For not saying it wasn’t true.”

Blaze just nodded, a short, sharp motion. “Yeah. Well.”

The cellar felt different. The air was still damp, still smelled of yeast and earth, but the silence had changed. It wasn’t empty anymore. It was full of the things they’d just said, hanging there like cobwebs they’d all have to brush through from now on. Zonk finally pulled his gaze from the water. He looked at his friends—one bruised, one smudged, both waiting for him to say something smart.

He didn’t. He just shrugged, a movement that felt too light for his body. “So. Tuning forks. Do we get actual forks, or is it more of a metaphorical thing?”

Elder Bloom’s mouth twitched. It might have been the beginning of a smile. Or a grimace. “Come,” he rasped. “We begin with beat.”

The lesson, it turned out was mostly about not hyperventilating.

The cellar door above them exploded.

It wasn’t a sound so much as a pressure change, a sudden vacuum that sucked the air from Zonk’s lungs and replaced it with splintering wood and the smell of burnt sugar. The iron grate blew off its hinges, spinning like a deadly coin before it slammed into the far wall with a deafening clang. Daylight—harsh, artificial sunlight from the bakery above—flooded down the stairs, filled with dancing dust and the silhouettes of armored figures.

“Solar Sentinels!” Luna’s voice cut through the ringing in Zonk’s ears. She was already moving, a blur of dark blue. She didn’t go for the stairs. She shoved the rough wooden table on its side, sending it screeching across the stone floor to crash against the bottom step, creating a pathetic barricade. “They have traced the dissonance! To me, all of you!”

Chaos, then order, then chaos again, all in the space of a heartbeat. The two Old Herd survivors—a gaunt unicorn-kin with a chipped horn named Murmur and a stout earth-pony type called Brick, who smelled of wet clay—flinched back against the wall. Pip froze, a rabbit in a spotlight. Blaze was already in motion, grabbing Pip by the arm and yanking her behind the overturned table. Zonk’s body moved before his brain caught up. He dove behind the table next to Blaze, the impact jolting his teeth. His thumb found the ragged scab again. He pressed down hard. The sharp, specific pain was an anchor.

Boots on the stairs. Not the clatter of hooves, but the heavy, purposeful thud of armored humans. The first Sentinel appeared at the base of the steps, his rainbow-hued plastron looking absurdly cheerful. He leveled a weapon that looked like a cross between a cattle prod and a kid’s light-up wand.

Luna moved.

She didn’t cast a spell. She didn’t summon shadows. She stepped into the man’s guard, grabbed the wrist holding the prod, and twisted. There was a wet pop. The man screamed, high and shocked. The prod clattered to the floor. Luna drove her elbow into his throat, dropped him choking, and kicked the prod skittering into the darkness under the stairs. It was vicious. It was efficient. It was nothing like the flowing, magical combat Zonk had half-expected. This was a street fight in a cellar.

Two more Sentinels pushed past their fallen comrade. One fired his prod. A crackling bolt of pink energy lanced out, missing Luna by inches and striking the stone wall where Murmur had been a second before. The stone didn’t scorch. It glazed over, turning into a smooth, shiny pane of candy pink. The smell of cotton candy and ozone filled the cellar.

“Do not let the harmony bolts strike you!” Luna barked, ducking under a wild swing from the second guard. “The transformation is temporary but profoundly disorienting!”

Blaze was up. He didn’t have a weapon. He had the iron grate. He grabbed the heavy, twisted metal where it lay against the wall, hefted it with a grunt of effort, and charged. He didn’t throw it. He used it like a giant, clumsy shield, ramming the edge into the midsection of the guard fighting Luna. The man oofed doubling over. Luna chopped down on the back of his neck, and he went still.

The guard with the prod fired again. Zonk saw the bolt coming, a streaking line of neon yellow. It wasn’t aimed at him. It was aimed at Brick, who was trying to wedge himself into a crack in the stonework. Zonk did the only thing he could think of. He kicked the leg of the overturned table. It rocked. The yellow bolt hit the tabletop square.

The wood didn’t turn to candy. It bloomed. Dozens of tiny, perfect buttercups erupted from the grain, their petals unfolding with a sound like rustling tissue paper. The table leg sprouted leaves. The absurdity of it—the violent, beautiful wrongness—stole the moment from Zonk’s lungs.

“The Elements!” Pip cried out, not in fear, but in a kind of desperate realization. She was crouched, staring at the flower-covered table. “Their original function! It’s not destruction, it’s transformation! Life where it shouldn’t be!”

“Great! Party later!” Blaze roared, dropping the grate and lunging for the disarmed prod. His fingers closed around the handle. The remaining guard, seeing his weapon taken, drew a baton. Not a rainbow one. This was a simple, polished nightstick. He advanced on Blaze, his expression grim behind his helmet’s visor.

Luna was at the stairs, holding the bottleneck against two more figures trying to push down. She fought with a cold, brutal grace, every movement economic. But she was one person. The cellar was a trap.

Murmur the unicorn-kin was chanting something, his voice a thin, reedy thread under the clash of combat. His chipped horn glowed with a faint, sickly light. Nothing happened. He sagged, a look of deep shame on his face. “The old words… They’re just words now,” he said.

Zonk’s brain, finally kicking into gear offered no pop-culture references. It offered the layout of the bakery above. He’d seen it for two seconds when they slipped down the alley. The pink awning. The display window full of fake cakes. The door to the street. And the other door. The one behind the counter. The employees-only door that probably led to a back alley.

“There’s another way out!” he shouted. “Up through the bakery! There’s a back door!”

Luna didn’t look back. “The way is blocked!”

“Then we unblock it!” Blaze yelled. He swung the prod like a club, meeting the Sentinel’s baton with a shower of pink sparks. The vibration visibly traveled up Blaze’s arms, making his teeth clack together. He held on.

Zonk looked at Pip, at the terrified Old Herd survivors, at the flower-covered table. The plan. The Gala. The recalibration. It was all theoretical dust if they got turned into a decorative shrub or a sentient gumdrop here in a cellar. Something hot and sour rose in his throat—not quite anger, not quite panic, a fizzy cocktail of both. He had to do something. Not think. Do.

He scrabbled on the floor, his hands finding nothing but grit and stone chips. Then his fingers brushed against something cool and metallic. The fallen guard’s helmet. It was light, cheap plastic painted to look like armor. A prop.

He picked it up. He didn’t throw it at the guard fighting Blaze. He threw it at the wall of the cellar, as hard as he could, away from the stairs.

It hit the stones with a loud, hollow crack and clattered noisily into the dark corner.

The guard fighting Blaze flinched, his head turning instinctively toward the sound for a fraction of a second.

Blaze didn’t need a cue. He dropped the prod, stepped inside the man’s guard, and headbutted him square in the visor. There was a crunch. The man staggered back, dazed. Blaze ripped the baton from his limp hand, reversed it, and brought the pommel down on the man’s collarbone. He dropped.

The bottleneck at the stairs was breaking. Luna was giving ground, a step back, then another. A Sentinel’s boot came down on the first step inside the cellar.

“Go!” Luna commanded, her voice strained. “Through the bakery! Now!”

Zonk grabbed Pip. Brick grabbed Murmur. They scrambled from behind the flowering table and bolted for the stairs. They had to pass right by the fight. Zonk kept his head down, expecting a blow that never came. Luna was a whirlwind, holding the space, creating a gap.

They poured up the stairs into the wreckage of Sugar Cube Corner.

The sunlight was blinding. The front display window was shattered, glass glittering like ice across a floor dusted with flour and pink frosting. A three-tiered fake wedding cake lay on its side, plastic bride and groom staring blankly at the ceiling. The door to the street was barred from the outside, a heavy timber slammed across it. Sentinels were out there, waiting.

“Back door!” Zonk gasped, pointing.

They ran behind the counter, through the swinging door into a tiny, hot kitchen. Mixing bowls were overturned. Something was burning—the acrid smell of scorched sugar. The back door was a plain, gray metal thing. It had a deadbolt.

Blaze hit it first shoulder-checking it with all his weight. The door shuddered but held. He cursed, rubbing his shoulder.

“Move!” Brick growled. The earth-pony kin lowered his head, a gesture that looked instinctual, archaic. He charged. It wasn’t graceful. It was pure, stubborn mass. He hit the door like a battering ram.

The metal shrieked. The frame splintered. Daylight—real, gray, alleyway daylight—spilled in.

They tumbled out into the narrow service alley, back where they started. The air was cold. The dumpster was still there.

Luna erupted from the bakery door a second later, slamming it shut behind her. She was stillness hard, a thin line of blood tracing from her temple down her cheek. She looked at them, her eyes scanning the alley exits. “They will flank. This way.”

She didn’t wait for agreement. She took off down the alley, away from the main street, moving with a predator’s swift, silent gait. They followed, a ragged, panting line. Zonk’s lungs burned. The taste in his mouth was still that fizzy cocktail, but now it was mixed with the ghost of burnt pastry and the iron tang of blood. He noticed, stupidly, that one of his shoelaces was untied. The aglet was gone. The plastic tip was missing, and the lace was fraying. He’d have to fix that. If he lived.

They rounded a corner, then another, plunging into a maze of service corridors behind the pastel-colored facades. The sounds of pursuit faded, then disappeared. Luna finally stopped in the lee of a massive industrial trash compactor that smelled of rotting fruit. She leaned against it, closing her eyes for a single second. When she opened them, they were cold and clear again.

“They know we have Old Herd knowledge,” she said. She spoke flat. “They will secure the vault beyond all reach. The Gala is in two nights. Our window is now a slit.”

Murmur was weeping silently, his shoulders shaking. Brick put a heavy hand on his back.

Pip was looking at Zonk, her good ear twitching. “You created a distraction,” she said. It wasn’t a question.

Zonk looked at his hands. They were shaking. He stuffed them in his pockets. “It was a helmet.”

“It worked.”

Blaze leaned against the wall, tilting his head back. The bruise under his eye was a masterpiece of purple and green. “So. We’ve got the team. What’s left of it. We’ve got the big secret about the sparkly mind-control toys.” He cracked his neck. “Now we just gotta break into the most secure place in the city, during a party for the rich and powerful, and perform a magic ritual nobody’s done right in centuries. With guards who turn things into dessert.”

He looked at Luna, then at Zonk. A slow, tired grin spread across his face. It was the kind of grin that had gotten them into this whole mess.

“Standard Tuesday, right?”
