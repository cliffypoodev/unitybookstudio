The air in the throne room tasted like hot metal and myrrh. Zonk’s side throbbed where Luna had kicked him, a bright, insistent pain that made his breath hitch on every other step. The polished marble floor stretched out before them, a vast, blinding sea of white veined with gold, leading to a dais so far away the figure upon it was just a smudge of platinum and ivory.

Blaze walked on his left, a solid, silent presence. Pip was on his right, her small hand gripping his sleeve. Her fingers trembled. He could feel the fine vibration through the cheap fabric of his stolen guard tunic. The silence wasn’t empty. It was a held charge, thick and heavy, pressing against his eardrums. Somewhere high above, sunlight poured through a colossal stained-glass window depicting a benevolent sun, but the light that reached the floor was hard, surgical, picking out every scuff on their boots, every fleck of dust.

They passed between two rows of Solar Sentinels. Not the cheap plastic-armored grunts from the apartment. These were different. Their armor was filigreed gold, molded into perfect equine musculature, and they stood so still they might have been statues. Zonk caught the glint of a visor tracking them. No one moved to stop them. The invitation, delivered by a trembling page in a velvet doublet, had been clear. She was waiting.

The dais grew larger. The smell shifted—less incense, more the clean, astringent scent of antiseptic wipes. It reminded him of the clinic where he’d gotten his last tetanus shot. The figure resolved.

Princess Celestia sat upon a throne that was less a chair and more a stylized explosion of gilded sunbeams. She wasn’t what he’d expected. Taller, yes. Imposing, absolutely. But her face was sharp, angles where the propaganda posters showed soft curves, and her eyes were the pale, flat blue of a winter sky. She wore a gown of white silk so stark it hurt to look at, and her mane—not hair, but a cascading wave of ethereal light in shades of pink and green—moved in a non-existent breeze. It was the most ridiculous, terrifying thing he’d ever seen.

At the base of the dais, arranged on velvet cushions like sacred relics, were the other five Elements. The pink whoopee cushion of Laughter. The purple diamond flogger of Generosity. The yellow jar of Kindness. The red cuffs of Loyalty, still locked from Blaze’s earlier mishap. His tiara, the Element of Magic, lay off to the side, as if discarded. Her gaze locked with his. The pale blue seemed to deepen, to churn. He saw it then, not in her face, but in the sudden, minute tremor in the sunlight on the floor. A vibration. A flicker. The hum in his bones from when they’d linked hands in the chaos returned, softer now, a question.

She was afraid. Not of them. Of the offer.

Slowly, with a rustle of silk, Celestia rose from her throne. She towered over them. The light from the window haloed her, but from this angle, Zonk could see the dust motes swirling in the beam, the imperfections in the colored glass. A tiny, hairline crack in a pane of blue.

“Perform your ritual, aesthetic deviant,” she said, her voice a whisper that filled the room. “Show me this ‘third way.’ And when it fails, as it must, you will learn the true price of wasting my time.

She didn’t sit back down. She stood, a statue of white and gold and cold light, waiting to be proven right.

The dam was listening.

Zonk’s body moved before his brain caught up. He lunged, not toward Celestia, but toward the velvet cushions. His hand closed around the cold metal of the tiara. The Element of Magic. A master control key, Luna had said. For a system built on coercion.

Celestia watched from her throne, her expression one of detached, clinical interest. The siphon of light pulsed, brightening. Pip shuddered. A strand of her mane turned gray, then white, falling loose against the marble.

Something hot and sour flooded the back of Zonk’s throat. Not anger. Not fear. Something older. The feeling of watching his mother’s taillights disappear down the wet street when he was ten. Helpless. Useless. Just a kid standing in an open doorway.

No.

He slammed the tiara onto his own head. It was too small, a biting pressure against his temples. The world didn’t explode with power. It just got louder. He could hear the hum of the Sentinels’ armor, the whisper of Celestia’s gown, the frantic, galloping rhythm of Pip’s heart, slowing.

“What is your intention?” Celestia’s voice cut through the noise. “The Key cannot create. It only directs existing flows. My flow is established. Hers is… available.

“We’re not directing shit,” Zonk spat. He pointed at Blaze. “Give me your hand.

Blaze stared. “What?

Blaze thrust his free hand out. Zonk grabbed his wrist, hard. Blaze’s skin was warm, calloused. Zonk dragged him toward Pip, dropping to his knees on the hard marble. He grabbed Pip’s limp hand. It was cold. He pressed her palm against Blaze’s, then slapped his own hand on top, a messy stack. The tiara dug into his skull.

“Think about the apartment,” Zonk hissed, his eyes locked on Blaze’s. “The stupid pizza. The couch. Her trying to fold the blankets.

“What?”

“Just do it!”

He focused. Not on magic, or rituals, or power. He thought about the smell of burnt popcorn from two nights ago. The way Pip had arranged Blaze’s bandana on the coffee table like it was a sacred artifact. The sound of Blaze’s snoring from the other room, a chainsaw of comfort. The three of them, a weird, terrified little triangle in a shitty apartment, waiting for the world to end.

The cuffs in Blaze’s other hand began to vibrate. A low, resonant thrum, like a bass string plucked deep underground.

Celestia leaned forward, a fraction of an inch. “A sentimental echo. It changes nothing.

The vibration traveled up Blaze’s arm, into the hand Zonk was clutching. It jumped to Zonk, a jolt that made his teeth click together. Then it flowed into Pip.

Pip gasped. The golden siphon flickered.

It wasn’t a shield. It wasn’t a wall. It was a loop. A circuit. Zonk felt it—a dizzying, three-way feedback of sensation. The ache in Blaze’s shoulder from a old weightlifting injury. The dry, papery fear in Pip’s chest. The throbbing in his own ribs. They bled together, indistinct, a shared pool of hurt.

And underneath that, something else. Blaze’s stubborn, granite certainty. I got you. Pip’s fragile, tenacious hope. It’ll be okay. His own cynical, battered, but fiercely protective grip on both of them. You are mine to lose.

The cuffs glowed. Not with a fiery light, but with a warm, steady, red pulse. Like a heartbeat.

The golden siphon wavered, fraying at the edges.

Celestia’s placid mask cracked. A line appeared between her perfect eyebrows. “This is inefficient. Chaotic. You are diluting the source.

“We’re not a source,” Zonk ground out. The shared sensation was overwhelming—Blaze’s confusion, Pip’s returning panic, his own vertigo. He felt like he might vomit. “We’re a… a knot.

He focused the feeling, not at Celestia, but through the tiara. He didn’t try to command. He offered. An image, clumsy and raw: the three of them, hands linked, a closed circle. No siphon. No drain. Just the circuit, holding.

The other Elements on the cushions stirred. The pink whoopee cushion of Laughter gave a feeble, wet squeak. The yellow jar of Kindness popped its lid, releasing a scent of chamomile and honey. The purple flogger of Generosity trembled, its tails coiling like affectionate cats.

Celestia recoiled. It was a tiny motion, just a shift of her shoulders, but it was the first unplanned movement Zonk had seen from her. The siphon snapped.

Pip collapsed forward, breathing in ragged, wet gulps. The gray streak in her mane remained.

The silence that followed was different. Not charged, but… waiting. The throne room felt smaller. The stained-glass sun seemed garish, fake.

Celestia rose from her throne. She seemed taller, the light of her mane casting long, wavering shadows. “A circle,” she said, her voice still cool, but with a new, brittle edge. “A closed system. It cannot scale. It cannot govern. It is a child’s solution to a cosmic problem.

She descended the dais, one slow step at a time. The Sentinels remained frozen. “You speak of a third way. You offer me… sentiment. A feedback loop of attachment.” She stopped a few feet away, looking down at their huddled group. Her winter-blue eyes held no malice. Only a terrible, convinced certainty. “I have seen the true alternative. The chaos that comes when connection is not managed, when power is not directed. It is not freedom. It is a fire that consumes everything.

This was the fear. Not of losing power. Of losing control. The ancient catastrophe. Zonk saw it in her face, not as a memory, but as a living thing, a ghost that sat on her shoulder every second of every day. Her rigid contracts, her brutal order—it was a cage she’d built not just for her subjects, but for reality itself. To keep the fire in the hearth.

For a second, he felt sorry for her. It was a sharp, unexpected pang, right in the center of the shared ache.

“You don’t have to choke it,” Blaze said, his voice rough. He was still holding the cuffs, the red pulse fading to a soft glow. “You can just… hold it.

“And when your hand tires?” Celestia asked. It wasn’t a challenge. It was a genuine question. “When your circle breaks? Someone always leaves. Someone always falls. The system must endure beyond the fragile hearts of its components.

Pip pushed herself up on one elbow. Her voice was a scratch. “But if the system hurts the hearts… what’s left to endure?

Celestia looked at her. Really looked. At the gray streak. At the trembling limbs. At the earnest, broken hope on her face. Something in the Princess’s gaze shifted, hardened. The moment of vulnerability vanished, sealed over like ice on a pond.

“Sentiment,” she repeated, the word final. She raised her hand. Not toward them. Toward the other Elements. “Is a luxury for those who do not bear the weight of the sky.

The tiara on Zonk’s head turned ice-cold. A new pressure built, not from their little circuit, but from outside. Celestia was calling the other Elements. Not to use them. To reset them. To purge the chaotic, emotional resonance they’d just been touched with. The air tightened, a vise of pure, impersonal order.

Their circle wouldn’t hold against that. It was a campfire facing a glacier.

Zonk met Blaze’s eyes. Blaze gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod. Okay. So this is it.

Then Blaze’s gaze dropped to the cuffs in his hand. The locked cuffs of Loyalty. A slow, stupid, beautiful grin spread across his face.

“Hey Zonk,” he said. “Remember the safe word tap?

Before Zonk could answer, Blaze slammed the locked metal cuffs—not onto their wrists—but onto the base of the tiara encircling Zonk’s head.

Tap-tap-tap.

The sound was a clear, metallic chime in the silent room.

Loyalty’s cuffs were unbreakable.

The system, for one flawless, catastrophic instant, tried to follow two contradictory commands. Celestia’s call to reset. And the cuffs’ immutable, locked-on, physical bond to the Key.

There was no explosion. No blast of light.

There was a sound like a universe sighing. A deep, resonant unclenching.

The stained-glass sun window went dark. Not shattered. Just… inert. The golden filigree on the Sentinels’ armor dulled to plain, brushed steel. The pastel colors leaching back into Pip’s coat seemed to brighten, then settle into softer, more natural hues.

Celestia staggered. Not much. A slight sway, as if the floor had tilted. The  light of her mane didn’t go out, but it stilled, hanging limp and heavy around her shoulders, just hair now. She looked at her hands, then at the now-quiet Elements on the cushions.

The coercive contracts upholding her world hadn’t broken.

They had simply… stopped listening.

Her control hadn’t been overthrown. It had been rendered obsolete. The spark plugs were still there, but the engine they were supposed to turn over was gone.

The throne room was just a room. A very large, very quiet, very empty room.

Zonk pulled the tiara off his head. The cuffs came with it, locked stubbornly onto the band. He looked at the tangled mess in his hands, then at Celestia’s frozen, uncomprehending face.

“Well,” he said, his voice echoing slightly in the new silence. “That’s structured.

The shield held for three more heartbeats before it dissolved, not with a bang but a sigh, the light bleeding away into the charged air. Zonk’s arm dropped, heavy as stone. The silence that followed was brittle, like thin ice over deep water. He could smell his own sweat, sharp and sour, and underneath it, the lingering sweetness of spent magic—like burnt marshmallows and ozone.

Celestia hadn’t moved. She watched them from her throne, a statue of cold appraisal. The annoyance was gone, replaced by something worse: clinical interest. The kind a scientist shows a promising mold.

“A voluntary resonance.” Her voice cut the quiet. “An interesting anomaly. It proves my point. The connection exists. It is a tool. You simply refuse to wield it correctly.

Blaze was breathing hard beside him. “We’re not a tool, you pastel psycho.

“You are a resource,” she corrected, utterly calm. “One that has now demonstrated a unique, if unstable, functionality. The solution is not to discard you. It is to recalibrate the binding parameters.” Her gaze shifted to the Elements on their cushions. “The ritual can still be completed. The power source is… agitated. Optimal for harvesting.

A cold knot tightened in Zonk’s gut. She wasn’t going to kill them. She was going to strap them into her machine and mine whatever this was between them, siphon it off to keep the lights on in her creepy pastel dictatorship. Forever. The old panic rose, the one that screamed to run, to detach, to be anywhere but here.

Pip’s hand found his. Her fingers were ice-cold. “She cannot,” Pip whispered, but it lacked conviction. It was a hope, not a fact.

He looked at the Elements. The stupid, gaudy artifacts they’d treated like loot. The cuffs, the flogger, the jar, the gag, the tiara. They weren’t just symbols. They were the channels. The ritual needed a connection to flow through, and Celestia’s way was to force it, to hook electrodes into a soul and crank the voltage until it burned out. Their little circle had just shown her a better battery.

And she wanted to steal it.

The thought landed with a dull, final thud. There was no third way to show her. You couldn’t offer a choice to someone who only understood ownership.

Blaze cracked his neck. “So what’s the play, Z? We gonna hug it out again? ‘Cause my shoulder’s kinda killing me.

The play. Always the play. The angle, the exploit, the glitch in the system. Zonk’s eyes scanned the throne room, the Sentinels, the distant doors. His mind, that cynical, pattern-seeking machine, spun its wheels. Find the flaw. Find the exit.

It found nothing.

Just the five Elements. And the missing sixth. The scales of Honesty, back in Luna’s clutches. The pieces of a broken system.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless. His father, the one time he’d tried to explain why he worked so much. The world runs on contracts, Zeke. Unspoken ones. You do your job, you get paid. You break the deal, you get nothing. It’s not personal. It’s just how it works. He’d been eight. He hadn’t understood. He’d just hated the tired, final sound of it.

It’s just how it works.

Celestia’s whole world was a contract. A million coercive deals, signed in fear or ignorance, all stacked up to keep the sun in the sky. Their connection—his, Blaze’s, Pip’s—wasn’t a contract. It was the opposite. It was the thing you got when you stopped keeping score.

To break her system, you couldn’t just smash it. You had to replace it. Not with another contract. With something that didn’t have a name.

His mouth was full of cotton. He swallowed, the click loud in his own head.

“The ritual needs all six,” he said, his voice rough.

“Obviously,” Celestia replied. “The scales will be retrieved. Your comrade in the Night Court has outlived her usefulness.

Celestia’s head tilted a fraction. “You propose a substitute? There is no substitute. The archetype is fixed.

He turned to Blaze and Pip. Blaze’s expression was pure, unadulterated confusion, with a side order of impending heartburn. Pip just looked at him, her big eyes wide, waiting for the plan.

Across the room, Princess Celestia, former domme-mob boss of all she surveyed, slowly sat on the sunbeam steps of her throne. She folded her hands in her lap. She looked at them, then at her own plain hands, then at the now-dull Elements.

She said nothing.

The contracts, every single one built on coercion, were null and void. The system was broken.

And the only sound was the distant, irrelevant drip of a leak from a decorative fountain in the corner, a sound that had probably been there for years, unnoticed by anyone until this moment, when everything else had finally stopped.

The light from their joined hands wasn’t a beam. It was a spill. It welled up from the space between their fingers, warm and gold and pink and a dozen other colors that had no business mixing, and it didn’t shoot toward Celestia. It just spread, slow as syrup, filling the throne room from the floor up.

It touched the gilded Sentinel first. The one who’d taken a step. The light climbed his armor, and for a second he was a statue dipped in honey. Then he shuddered. A full-body tremor, like a horse shaking off flies. His perfect, rigid posture slumped a fraction of an inch. He didn’t move, but the menace leaking from him just… evaporated. It left behind the faint, sad smell of old brass polish.

The light kept spreading. It pooled around the velvet cushions holding the Elements. The pink whoopee cushion of Laughter gave a soft, pathetic pffft. The purple flogger of Generosity unwound itself, the falls settling into a loose pile that looked, for all the world, like a sleeping cat. The yellow jar of Kindness glowed from within, a gentle candle-flame warmth.

Zonk’s teeth were vibrating. Or maybe the air was. The warmth in his hand where he gripped Blaze’s and Pip’s was a solid thing, a plug of live wood. It didn’t hurt. It hummed. A low, resonant note that bypassed his ears and went straight into the marrow of his spine. He could feel Blaze’s pulse through his palm, a frantic rabbit-thump against his own steady, deep drum. Pip’s was a bird’s heartbeat, fast and light. Three rhythms, clashing, then finding a weird, syncopated groove.

The light reached the dais.

It climbed the sunbeam steps, lazy, inevitable. Celestia watched it come. Her winter-blue eyes tracked its progress up the first step, then the second. Her expression didn’t change. That sharp, impatient annoyance. But her hands, resting on the arms of her throne, tightened. The knuckles stood out, pale as bone against the white silk of her gown.

“Sentiment,” she said again, but the word lacked its earlier venom. It was flat. A statement of fact.

The light touched the hem of her gown.

It soaked in, turning the stark white to a soft cream. It traveled up, over the intricate embroidery of suns and stars, warming the threads. It reached her hands. For a heartbeat, nothing happened. Then Zonk saw it—a tremor in her index finger. A tiny, almost invisible shake. She pressed the finger hard against the throne arm to stop it.

Her eyes widened. Just a fraction. The pale blue irises seemed to deepen, to take in the multicolored glow reflecting back at her. Her lips, pressed into a thin line, parted. A small, soundless breath escaped.

And Zonk saw it. Not in her face, but in the air around her. A flicker. A distortion, like heat haze off asphalt. For a second, the tall, sharp woman in the throne was gone. In her place was someone else. Younger. Terrified. Her hair was not a magical cascade but a tangled mess of pale pink, and her eyes were huge with a panic so profound it was a physical weight in the room. The ghost-image held a shattered crystal in her hands, and she was weeping, and the sound was the raw, ugly scrape of absolute loss.

Then it was gone. Celestia sat rigid once more, her face a mask. But her breath was coming faster now. Short, sharp inhales through her nose.

“You feel it,” Zonk said. His voice sounded strange to his own ears—calm, detached, like he was commenting on the weather. “That’s what it’s supposed to be. Not a contract. Not a lever. Just… that.

“It is chaos,” Celestia whispered. The regal projection was gone from her voice. It was thin. Haunted. “It is the unbound resonance. The Great Schism. You have no idea what you are invoking.

“We’re not invoking anything,” Blaze said. His voice was tight with strain, but clear. “We’re just… letting it be. For a minute.

“You let it be,” Pip said, her small voice cutting through the hum. “You just have to let it.

The ghost-image flickered again. The terrified girl, the shattered crystal. This time, Zonk heard the echo of a scream—high, despairing, the sound of a world coming apart at the seams. The smell changed. Not myrrh or antiseptic. Ozone. Burnt hair. The copper-tang of old blood.

Celestia flinched. A full-body recoil, as if struck.

The warmth in Zonk’s hand surged. It wasn’t an attack. It was an offer. A hand held out in a dark room. He felt it pour through him, a river of stupid, reckless, undeniable care—for Blaze’s dumb loyalty, for Pip’s fragile hope, for the dumb world that had thrown them together. It was messy and inconvenient and it hurt his ribs and it was the most real thing he’d ever touched.

It flowed up the dais and wrapped around Celestia.

She went very still. The multicolored light soaked into her, outlining her in a soft corona. For three long seconds, her face did something complicated. The sharp angles softened. The icy disdain melted into something like confusion, then a raw, aching wonder. Her eyes glistened. A single tear traced a clean path through the perfect powder on her cheek. It was the most human thing Zonk had ever seen.

She felt it.

Her mouth moved. No sound came out.

Then her eyes focused. Not on them, but on something in the middle distance. The ghost of the shattered crystal. The memory of the scream. The fear won. It slammed back into her features, hardening them into something worse than anger—certainty. The terrible, unshakable certainty of someone who has chosen their prison and now calls it a throne.

“No.” The word was a crack of ice. She wrenched her gaze down to them. The tear still gleamed on her cheek, a betrayal. “This is dissolution. This is the end of order. You offer me anarchy wrapped in a pretty feeling.” She straightened, pushing back against the warm light as if it were a physical pressure. “My way is clean. My way is safe. My way works.

She raised a hand. Not the graceful gesture from before. This was a claw, tendons standing out. She made a sharp, twisting motion, like snapping a thread.

A sound ripped through the throne room. Not a bang. A pop. The deep, resonant hum in Zonk’s bones cut off like a switched wire. The warm light flickered, dimmed.

From the cushions, the Elements reacted. The red cuffs of Loyalty snapped open with a sound like a gunshot. The lock fell to the marble with a dull clink. The purple flogger of Generosity lashed out once, wildly, as if trying to strike something, then went limp. The yellow jar of Kindness shattered, not violently, but like a soap bubble, releasing a scent of chamomile and lemon balm that fought briefly with the ozone. The pink whoopee cushion deflated with a long, mournful sigh.

The tiara at Zonk’s feet, the Element of Magic, went dark. The six-pointed star at its center turned to dull, leaden gray.

Celestia lowered her hand. She was breathing hard. A strand of her magical mane had come loose and hung lank against her neck. She looked exhausted. Triumphant. “You see? Control is the only true magic. The only safety. Your connection is broken. Your ritual is ash.

Zonk waited for the despair to hit. For the cold failure to sink into his throbbing ribs. It didn’t come. He looked at their joined hands. Blaze’s grip was still iron-tight. Pip’s fingers were still curled around his sleeve. The physical connection was there. The warmth was gone from the air, but the memory of it was a phantom limb, a ghost of heat in his palm.

He looked at Celestia, at the tear-track on her cheek, at the desperate triumph in her eyes.

“Yeah,” he said, his voice quiet in the sudden, dead silence. “You broke the magic. You broke the contract.” He shrugged, a small movement that made his side twinge. “We weren’t using that kind.

He let go of Blaze’s hand.

For a terrible second, nothing happened. Then Blaze let go of Pip’s. The three of them stood separate, a broken circle.

A breeze drifted through the throne room. It came from nowhere, stirring the dust on the floor. It touched the shattered pieces of the yellow jar, lifting a few flecks of ceramic. It rustled the limp falls of the purple flogger. It brushed past the unlocked red cuffs.

From each broken, dormant Element, a tiny wisp of light drifted upward. Not the coordinated beam of a ritual. Just… loose change. Spare energy. The last exhale of a system that had just been switched off at the mains.

The wisp from the cuffs was red. The one from the flogger, purple. From the jar’s pieces, yellow. From the whoopee cushion, pink. They rose, lazy as dust motes in a sunbeam.

The tiara at Zonk’s feet gave one last, feeble pulse. A gray wisp, the color of old ash, peeled away from the dead star.

Six little lights. Six colors. They drifted toward the center of the room, above the spot where Zonk, Blaze, and Pip had been standing.

They met.

There was no flash. No bang. No power.

They just merged. And for a single, perfect, silent second, they formed a small, six-pointed star of pure white light. It hung in the air, beautiful and utterly harmless. It cast no shadow. It made no sound.

Then it dissolved. The white light didn’t fade—it simply came apart, like sugar in water, into a million tiny, glittering particles that filled the air. They caught in Celestia’s lank mane. They settled on the shoulders of the Sentinels. They dusted the gold veins in the marble floor like the first frost.

A piece of stained glass in the vast window high above—a tiny, blue fragment in the sun’s corona—tinked softly and fell out. It shattered on the floor far below, a sound like dropped cutlery.

Somewhere in the city beyond the palace walls, a deep, foundational thrum that had been part of the background noise for centuries… stopped.

Celestia stared at the space where the star had been. Her triumph was gone. In its place was a hollow, dawning comprehension. She looked at her hands. She flexed her fingers. Nothing happened. No answering glow. No surge of power. She tried to make her mane move. It stayed where it was, a static, slightly frizzy mass of pastel light.

She was just a tall woman on a complicated chair.

“What,” she said, her voice barely a breath, “have you done?”

Zonk reached down, wincing at the pull in his side, and picked up his tiara. It was just a piece of cheap metal and plastic now. Cool to the touch. He turned it over in his hands.

“We didn’t do anything,” he said. He looked at Blaze, who was staring at his own empty palms with a look of profound confusion. He looked at Pip, who was watching the glittering dust settle on her hooves with wide, awestruck eyes. “We just stopped playing your game.

He put the tiara on his head. It sat there, crooked and stupid.

A slow, goofy grin spread across Blaze’s face. “Dude,” he said. “You look like a kid at a bad birthday party.

“Shut up,” Zonk said, and didn’t mean it at all.
