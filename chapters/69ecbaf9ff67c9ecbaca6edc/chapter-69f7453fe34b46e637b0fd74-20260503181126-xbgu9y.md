Chapter 18

The throne room held its beat. Not literally—air still moved, stale and thick with the scent of ozone and burnt velvet—but the silence had a texture now, a woolen heaviness that pressed against Zonk’s eardrums. Glittering particles turned the shafts of light from the broken window into murky soup. He stared at his hands. There was a thin, white line across his left palm where the binding oath had flared. It didn’t hurt. It just was. A receipt.

To his right, Blaze cracked his neck. The sound was a gunshot in the quiet. “Okay,” Blaze said, voice gravelly. “So. That happened.”

A Sentinel by the far wall shifted his weight. The motion was hesitant, unsure. His rainbow-plumed helmet was askew. He didn’t move to stop them. He just stood there, a pastel statue in a room full of broken toys.

Zonk’s eyes tracked across the carnage. The purple flogger lay in a sad heap, its falls limp. The red cuffs were open, their lock mechanism dark. The tiara at his feet looked like cheap plastic now, the star a dull gray. He had a sudden, stupid memory of a carnival when he was nine, winning a similar crown for knocking over milk bottles. His dad had been there. Hadn’t he? The memory was blurry at the edges, the smell of cotton candy and diesel fuel overwritten by this room’s weird, sweet decay.

“Is she…” Pip’s voice was small. She was staring at the dais.

Princess Celestia hadn’t moved. She knelt in the same spot, her magnificent gown pooled around her like a melted sundae. Her head was bowed. The glitter caught in her hair, turning the white strands into a cheap, sparkly wig. She wasn’t defeated. That was the wrong word. She was… switched off. The terrible, humming power that had vibrated in Zonk’s teeth was just gone. What remained was a woman in an elaborate costume shoulders slumped. One of her golden shoes had come off. Her foot, in a sheer stocking was turned at an awkward angle. The nail polish on her big toe was chipped. Zonk fixated on that. The chipped red polish. A domme-mob goddess who did her own pedicures. Or had someone else done it? Did she have a submissive just for that? The thought looped, pointless.

“She’s done,” Zonk heard himself say. The words felt thick. “The contracts are toast. The whole… power grid’s down.”

“So what powers the lights?” Blaze asked, gesturing to the flickering crystal chandeliers overhead. One of them sputtered, casting jagged shadows.

“No clue. Backup generators? Magic batteries? Who fucking knows.”

“Language,” Pip said, automatic. Then she blinked, as if hearing herself. A faint, wobbly smile touched her lips. “Sorry. Force of habit.”

The smile did something to Zonk’s chest. Not a bad thing. A warm, uncomfortable pressure, like a muscle he’d never used before was trying to flex. He looked away, toward the great window. The missing piece of blue glass left a jagged hole in the sun’s corona. Through it, he could see the city. No explosions. No panic in the streets. Just the usual pastel sprawl under a sky that was, for the first time, just a sky. No magical shimmer. No visible ley lines. It looked almost boring.

“We should go,” Blaze said. “Before anyone gets their shit together and decides to blame us for… all this.” He swept a hand around the room.

“Where?” The question came out flat.

“The node. The portal. Home, Zonk. Our apartment. The one with the broken door and, presumably, several very confused Solar Sentinels still looting our snack cupboard.”

Home. The word had no shape. It was a sound. Zonk pictured the stained carpet, the TV with the dead pixel, the lingering smell of burnt popcorn and regret. It felt like a story someone had told him about a place he used to live.

“Pip,” he said, not looking at her. “You coming?”

The silence that followed was different. It was the kind of silence that had weight and corners. He finally turned his head.

Pip was looking at the scattered Elements. She knelt, not in submission, but carefully, and picked up the pieces of the shattered yellow jar. She fit two shards together. They didn’t magically fuse. They were just broken pottery. She held them in her cupped hands, her brow furrowed. The glitter dusted her eyelashes.

“I don’t think I can,” she said, so softly he almost didn’t hear.

Blaze went still. “What do you mean? The door’s open. We did the thing. You can go anywhere.”

“I know.” She looked up, and her eyes were clear. Earnest. That hadn’t changed. “But this is my anywhere. It’s… broken. But it’s not a game here anymore. It’s real. And there are so many who only know the rules. The old rules. The bad ones.” She gestured toward the Sentinels, toward the distant city. “Someone has to help them learn the new ones. The real ones. The ones you showed me.”

Zonk’s throat tightened. “You don’t owe them anything.”

“I know that, too.” She smiled again, stronger this time. “It’s not a debt. It’s a… it’s a choice. My first real one, I think. I want to stay. I want to help the Old Herd. I want to see what grows when you don’t force it.”

The warm pressure in Zonk’s chest became a sharp, specific ache. He recognized it. It was the feeling of something being taken away. It was the feeling of his mom’s suitcase by the door when he was ten. A different kind of magic failing.

“You’ll get eaten alive,” he said, his voice rough.

“Probably,” Pip agreed, standing. She still held the pottery shards. “But I won’t be alone. And I won’t be naive. Not anymore. I know what a safeword is for. I know friends aren’t just in contracts.” She looked at him, then at Blaze. “I learned from the best.”

Blaze let out a long, shaky beat. He scrubbed a hand over his face. “Damn it, Pip.”

“I’m sorry.”

“Don’t be sorry. Just… be careful. Kick the tires on any new friendship contracts. Get a second opinion. Our rates are reasonable.”

She laughed, a bright, clean sound that seemed to push back the woolly silence. “I will.”

Zonk couldn’t speak. The ache was a solid thing, a knot behind his breastbone. He’d signed up for this. The binding oath on his palm itched. He’d promised to protect her. So was this protecting her? Letting her walk into a broken world because she chose to? The math didn’t work. His old math, the nothing-matters ledger was smoking wreckage. This was a new equation, and he didn’t have the formula.

He walked over to the discarded tiara. He picked it up. It was light. Insubstantial. He turned it over in his hands, then held it out to Pip. “Here. Souvenir. Might be worth something for parts.”

She took it, her fingers brushing his. The contact was electric, a static shock of pure feeling. She didn’t put it on. She just held it. “Thank you. For everything. For showing me the door.”

“You’re the one who showed us it was a door,” Zonk muttered. He jammed his hands into his pockets. The white scar on his palm pressed against the seam of the fabric. “So. How does this work? We just walk out?”

“The node in the Starlight Arcade will be stable now,” Pip said. “The barrier should be… calm. You should be able to pass through cleanly. I’ll make sure you get to the market gate unseen.”

It was efficient. It was a plan. It was an ending.

The walk out of the Sun Palace was a blur of cracked pastel marble and avoiding eyes. Sentinels and courtiers stood in clumps, whispering. Some were crying. No one stopped them. They were ghosts already, slipping between the folds of a reality that was busy re-stitching itself without them.

The familiar, garish glow of the Starlight Arcade sign was the most beautiful thing Zonk had ever seen. The booth was there door ajar, the inside dark.

Pip stopped at the mouth of the alley. “This is far enough.”

Blaze didn’t hesitate. He pulled her into a hug that lifted her off her feet. She squeaked, then hugged him back fiercely, her face buried in his shoulder. “You take care, little pony,” Blaze mumbled. “You need us, you… I don’t know. Send a singing telegram. We’ll come back.”

“I know,” she said, her voice muffled.

He set her down, clapped her on the shoulder, and turned away, walking quickly toward the arcade. He didn’t look back. Zonk knew he couldn’t.

It was just Zonk and Pip then, in the alley that smelled of garbage and fake candy. A single, waxy leaf from a plastic plant brushed his arm.

“Zonk,” she said.

“Don’t.” The words slipped out.

“Okay.”

He looked at her. Really looked. The dust in her hair, the resolve in her eyes, the chipped yellow pottery and the stupid tiara clutched to her chest. She was terrified. She was brave. She was staying.

He didn’t hug her. He just reached out and tapped her forehead once, gently, with two fingers. A benediction. An inside joke. A safeword.

Her eyes filled, but she smiled. “Go home.”

He went.

The booth was cold. The headsets dangled from their hooks. Blaze was already sitting on the stained floor, his back against the machine. He didn’t speak.

Zonk slid down the wall opposite. He fished in his pocket found his pipe, his lighter. His hands were steady. He packed the bowl, lit it, inhaled. The smoke burned, familiar and sweet. He held it, then passed it to Blaze.

Blaze took it. Took a hit. Passed it back.

Outside the booth door, the neon buzzed. A car honked. A real car. Their world, waiting.

They finished the bowl in silence, sharing the smoke, sharing the quiet. The high was a soft blanket, a buffer against the raw, scraped-clean feeling inside him. It didn’t erase it. It just let him look at it from a slight distance.

“We’re not heroes,” Blaze said finally, staring at the ceiling.

“We trashed our apartment and lost a friend,” Zonk agreed, his words slow. “We’re basically the worst heroes ever.”

“She’s not lost.”

“No.” Zonk turned the pipe over in his hands, watching the light play on the glass. “She’s not.”

Blaze nodded. He leaned his head back. “My back is killing me. That marble floor was no joke.”

“My palm itches.”

“We should get a pizza.”

“We should fix the door.”

Another silence. Comfortable, this time. The kind that doesn’t need filling.

Zonk put the pipe away. He pushed himself to his feet, knees popping. He held out a hand. Blaze looked at it, then took it, letting Zonk haul him up.

They stood there for a second in the dark booth, two guys in weird clothes that smelled of another world.

“Together?” Blaze asked.

“Duh,” Zonk said.

They pushed the booth door open and stepped out into the fluorescent glare of the arcade. The air smelled of stale popcorn and floor cleaner. It smelled like home.

Their sneakers squeaked on the linoleum as they walked toward the exit, side by side. The doorbell over the arcade entrance jingled as they pushed out into the night.

The arcade air tasted like stale electricity and the ghost of a thousand spilled sodas. Zonk’s sneakers squeaked on the linoleum, a sound so aggressively normal it felt like a personal insult. They’d walked here from the palace through streets that were too quiet, past pastel storefronts where ponies stood in clumps, whispering, casting glances at the sky as if expecting it to fall. It hadn’t. It just hung there, a bland, cheerful blue.

The node was Booth Seven. A flickering “Out of Order” sign was taped to the curtain. Blaze ripped it off. “Classic misdirection,” he muttered, and yanked the curtain aside.

The booth was a coffin. A single, grimy seat, a VR headset dangling from a hook, a screen dark except for a single green pixel in the lower left corner. On the seat was a folded piece of lavender paper. Pip picked it up, her hands careful.

“It’s from Luna,” she said, scanning the elegant script. “She says the path is clear. The node is… calibrated to your unique dissonance signature. It will only open for the two of you.” She looked up, her face pale in the sickly fluorescent light. “One-way trip.”

“Good,” Zonk said. The word came out too fast. He leaned against the booth’s frame, the metal cool through his threadbare hoodie. A poster on the opposite wall advertised a bowling league. The date was from two years ago. One corner had peeled away, showing a layer of older poster beneath—a clown’s smiling mouth, just a curve of red. He couldn’t stop looking at it.

Blaze cleared his throat. “So. This is it.”

“This is it,” Pip echoed.

Nobody moved. The arcade’s ambient soundtrack—a tinny, endless loop of 8-bit carnival music—played a jaunty tune that felt like a mockery. Zonk’s chest ached. Not a sharp pain. A dull, persistent throb, like a bruise you keep pressing.

“You sure about this?” Blaze asked Pip. He wasn’t looking at her. He was studying the headset as if it might explode. “I mean, we could… I dunno. Figure something out. You could visit. Maybe there’s a weekend pass. A… dimensional timeshare.”

Pip’s smile was small, real. “I don’t think it works like that, Blaze.”

“Yeah. Probably not.” He scrubbed a hand over his face. “Shit.”

The silence pooled. Zonk focused on the physical details, the irrelevant anchors. A cigarette burn on the edge of the seat cushion. The way Pip’s borrowed cloak—a dark blue thing from one of Luna’s people—was too big at the shoulders. The smell of his own sweat, sharp and human under the candy-sweet arcade fog. His brain kept trying to loop back to the palace, to the chipped red polish on Celestia’s toe. He shut it down. That was over. This was now. This was a dirty booth in a dying strip mall in a dimension that was technically free but smelled like feet and disinfectant.

A laugh barked out of him, harsh and unexpected. “Thanks.”

“But you tried. Even when you were scared. Even when you didn’t want to. You tried for me. For him.” She gestured to Blaze, who was now very intently studying a hole in the toe of his boot. “That’s the magic. The trying.”

Zonk’s throat closed. He wanted a hit off his pipe so badly his teeth hurt. He wanted the fuzzy edges, the comfortable distance. He wouldn’t take it. Not here. That was the whole fucking point wasn’t it? To be here, in this awful, raw clarity.

“I don’t do goodbyes,” he managed.

“I know,” she said.

“They’re stupid. Sentimental bullshit.”

“Yes.”

“So let’s just… not.”

Pip took a step forward. Then another. She stopped in front of him, close enough that he could see the faint dusting of freckles across the bridge of her nose, a detail he’d never noticed. She smelled like lavender and the peculiar, clean scent of her own world’s rain.

“Okay,” she replied softly.

Then she hugged him.

It was awkward. His arms were stuck at his sides for a second, pinned by surprise and a lifetime of practiced detachment. Her head tucked under his chin. She was warm. Solid. Real. A tremor went through her—not a sob, just a fine, wire-tight vibration. Something hot and nameless clawed up behind his sternum. It wasn’t sadness. It was worse. It was the absence of the wall he’d spent years building, and the terrifying, wide-open space left behind.

His arms came up. He hugged her back. One hand cupped the back of her head, fingers tangling in the soft, fuzzy hair at the base of her skull. He held on. He didn’t say anything. He just held on, and for a moment, the arcade, the booth, the whole ridiculous nightmare of it all fell away. There was just this. This warmth. This anchor.

She let go first. Stepped back. Her eyes were glistening, but she wasn’t crying. She smiled, a wobbly, brave thing that shattered something inside him.

She turned to Blaze. “Your turn.”

Blaze didn’t hesitate. He swept her up in a bear hug that lifted her off her feet. “You take care of yourself, you hear?” he mumbled into her shoulder, his voice thick. “You eat. You sleep. You find some big, scary earth-pony dude to watch your back. Or lady. Whatever. Just… Someone.”

“I will,” she squeaked, undone.

He set her down gently, hands on her shoulders. “And if anyone gives you shit, you tell ’em you’ve got friends in low places. Really, really low places. Like, another-dimension low.”

She laughed, a wet, hiccuping sound. “I’ll tell them.”

Blaze nodded, jaw tight. He blinked fast, looking at the ceiling. “Damn arcade’s dusty.”

Zonk’s turn. He had nothing. No wise words. No grand pronouncement. The silence stretched, thin and fragile.

“The Old Herd,” he said finally. “They’re idealists. Probably incompetent. Don’t let them talk you into anything without a clear exit strategy.”

Pip nodded, solemn. “I won’t.”

“And the… the new rules. The real ones. Go easy on them. It’s gonna be messy.”

“I know.”

He took a deep moment. The ache in his chest was a living thing now. “Good.”

That was it. That was all he had.

Pip looked from him to Blaze and back again. She squared her shoulders. The gesture was so her, so earnest and determined, that Zonk felt another piece of his interior armor quietly crumble to dust.

“Thank you,” she said. “For everything. For the adventure. For the friendship.” She took another step back, toward the curtain, toward her world. “Now go home.”

Blaze reached out and tapped the headset. The screen flickered to life. Not with a game logo, but with a familiar, static-laced view of their own living room. The couch was overturned. A Solar Sentinel’s rainbow-plumed helmet sat on the coffee table like a bizarre trophy. The door to the hallway was hanging off its hinges.

“Home sweet home,” Blaze replied softly.

Zonk didn’t look at the screen. He looked at Pip. He memorized the line of her jaw, the set of her mouth, the stupid, hopeful light in her eyes that they hadn’t managed to extinguish. He gave her a single, slow nod.

Then he turned sat in the grimy seat, and pulled the headset down over his eyes.

The world dissolved into light and a low, resonant hum. The last thing he heard, fading like a radio signal lost to distance was her voice.

“Bye.”

Then nothing. Then the smell of burnt popcorn and the feel of cheap foam against his ears. He pushed the headset up.

He was sitting in Booth Seven. Alone. Blaze was in the seat beside him, staring blankly at the dark screen.

The arcade music jingled on.

Zonk stood up. His legs felt unsteady. He pushed the curtain aside. The strip mall was exactly as they’d left it. Dusk was falling, painting the asphalt parking lot in shades of orange and gray. A kid on a skateboard clattered by. A car honked.

Blaze emerged behind him. They stood there for a minute, two guys in a doorway, saying nothing.

“Well,” Blaze said. “Fuck.”

“Yeah.”

They walked to the bus stop in silence. They got on the nearly empty bus. They rode. They got off. They climbed the three flights of stairs to their apartment. The door was, as predicted, broken. It leaned against the frame held in place by a single bent hinge.

They stepped inside.

The place was trashed. Cushions slashed, drawers pulled out, their meager stash of snacks gone. The Sentinel helmet was still on the table. Someone had put a beer can in it.

Zonk righted the couch. It sighed a cloud of dust. He sat. Blaze sat next to him.

The quiet was different here. It was their quiet. It had texture. The hum of the faulty refrigerator. The drip of the kitchen faucet. The distant bass thump from the apartment below.

Blaze pulled out his pipe and his weed pouch. He packed it with a ritualistic slowness. He lit it took a long drag held it. He passed it to Zonk.

Zonk took it. He put it to his lips. He inhaled. The familiar, earthy smoke filled his lungs. But for the first time in as long as he could remember, it didn’t feel like an escape. It felt like a pause. A comma.

He passed it back.

“She’s gonna be okay,” Blaze said, smoke curling from his lips.

“We’re gonna be okay?”

Zonk looked around the wreck of their apartment. The broken door. The slashed cushions. The helmet on the table. He thought of a palace made of crystal and lies, now just a building. He thought of a chipped red toenail. He thought of a hug in a dirty booth.

“Yeah,” he said. “I think we are.”

They sat in the gathering dark, passing the pipe, not saying much. Not needing to. The fear was still there, a low background hum. The uncertainty. The world was just as broken and strange as it had always been.

But the silence between them wasn’t empty anymore. It was full. It was a choice. It was a thing they were building, word by word, pause by beat, in the wreckage. Together.

The apartment door hung open. It wasn’t kicked in anymore, just leaning on its broken hinges like a drunk against a lamppost. Zonk stared at the splintered frame, at the familiar, faded sticker of a cartoon pony someone had slapped there years ago. One of its eyes was scratched off. He’d never noticed that before.

Inside, the silence was a physical thing. It wasn’t the throne room’s heavy wool. This was the silence of a stopped clock. A place where life had been interrupted and hadn’t bothered to start again.

Blaze shouldered past him, his movement stirring the air. It smelled of cold pepperoni, spilled soda, and underneath it all, that faint, sweet ozone that clung to their clothes now. A ghost of Equestria in a shitty one-bedroom.

“Home sweet fucking home,” Blaze muttered. He didn’t sound happy. He sounded tired.

The living room was a museum of their panic. The couch was overturned, cushions slashed, white foam guts spilling onto the carpet. The TV was a dark, blank eye. Books and game cases were fanned across the floor like a deck of cards dropped by a giant. Someone had emptied the snack cupboard onto the kitchen linoleum; a single, sad bag of barbecue chips lay burst open, its contents ground into colorful dust.

Zonk stepped over the threshold. His sneaker crunched on a piece of a Solar Sentinel’s rainbow-plumed helmet. Just plastic. He toed it aside. It skittered under the wrecked coffee table.

Blaze righted the couch with a grunt. It landed with a thud that shook the floor. Dust motes, stirred from their weeks-long slumber, danced in the thin light from the streetlamp outside. One landed on Zonk’s wrist. He didn’t brush it away.

“They didn’t take the good pipe,” Blaze announced from the kitchen. He spoke, the sound too loud.

“Small miracles.”

“It’s under the sink. With the drain cleaner. Guess even magical fascist enforcers draw the line at rummaging under the sink.”

Zonk didn’t move. He was looking at the crack in the ceiling above the TV. The one that looked like a lopsided smile. It was still there. Of course it was. The world could end, dimensions could reset, but the crack in the ceiling would remain. The thought was weirdly comforting. An anchor.

Blaze came back, the small glass pipe and a lighter in one hand, a half-crushed box of cereal in the other. He sat on the floor, his back against the overturned couch. He didn’t sit on the couch itself. Just on the floor. Zonk joined him. The carpet was gritty under his palms.

The ritual was familiar. Blaze packed the bowl, his big hands surprisingly deft. The lighter flicked. A cherry glow bloomed in the glass, painting his face in brief, warm tones. He held it, inhaled, the sound a soft, dry rustle. He passed it.

Zonk took it. The glass was warm. He put it to his lips, lit it, pulled. The smoke hit his lungs—harsh, earthy, a taste he knew better than his own reflection. He held it. Let it burn. Exhaled slowly. A gray plume dissipated into the dusty air.

It didn’t feel like escape. That was the thing. It felt like… punctuation. A period at the end of a very long, very stupid sentence.

They passed it back and forth in silence. The streetlight outside flickered once, then steadied. A car alarm went off two blocks over, wailed for fifteen seconds, and choked off.

“She’ll be okay,” Blaze said finally. He wasn’t looking at Zonk. He was staring at the busted chip bag. “Pip. She’s tougher than she looks.”

“She had that look in her eye. At the end. Not the scared one. The other one.”

“I saw it.”

Zonk took the pipe. “We didn’t teach her shit. She already had it. We just stopped… I don’t know. We stopped standing in front of it.”

“Deep.”

“Shut up.”

Another pass. The smoke was making the room softer at the edges. The chaos receded became set dressing. The tight coil in Zonk’s chest, the one that had been there since the throne room—since before that, since the first guard came through the door—began to loosen. Not unwind. Just… loosen. Like a fist unclenching finger by stiff finger.

He became aware of the space between them. The eighteen inches of stained carpet. It felt like a canyon. It felt like nothing at all. He could feel the heat coming off Blaze’s arm. He was always warm. Like a furnace. Zonk had joked about it for years. Dude, you’re like a space heater with bad jokes.

Blaze shifted, his shoulder brushing Zonk’s. A point of contact. Solid. Real.

“You’re thinking too loud,” Blaze mumbled.

“My brain’s just echoing. It’s empty in there.”

“Liar.”

Zonk looked at him then. Blaze’s profile was lit by the streetlight, his jaw tight, a smudge of dirt or ash on his temple. He looked older. Not in a bad way. Just… present. Like he’d finally grown into the size of his own body.

Without really deciding to, Zonk reached out. He touched the smudge on Blaze’s temple with his thumb. The skin was warm. Blaze went perfectly still. Zonk rubbed gently. The mark came away.

Blaze turned his head. Their eyes met. Zonk’s hand stayed there, cupping the side of his face. The silence changed again. It wasn’t empty anymore. It was full of the things they hadn’t said for years. The loyalty that wasn’t a contract. The friendship that didn’t need a magic tiara.

Blaze’s hand came up, covered Zonk’s, pressed it harder against his cheek. His eyes closed. A moment felt it move through him, out of him.

Then he leaned in.

It wasn’t a dramatic kiss. It was a collision of exhaustion and need. Chapped lips, the shared taste of smoke, a little clumsy. Blaze’s hand slid from Zonk’s to the back of his neck, pulling him closer. Zonk dropped the pipe. It thudded on the carpet, rolling away. He didn’t care.

It was grounding. That was the word. An anchor line thrown in a storm. Blaze’s mouth was hot, his stubble rough. Zonk grabbed a fistful of his shirt, fabric bunching in his hand. He could feel Blaze’s heartbeat, a frantic drum against his own chest. Or maybe that was his. They were pressed so close the rhythm could have been either of them, both of them.

They broke apart, gasping. Foreheads touching.

“Okay,” Blaze said quietly.

“That’s… that’s a thing now.”

Zonk kissed him again. Slower this time. A discovery. The fear in his chest wasn’t gone, but it had melted, reformed into a different kind of urgency. This wasn’t about escaping a dimension. This was about mapping a new one. The territory of Blaze’s shoulder under his palm. The soft sound he made when Zonk’s teeth caught his lower lip. The way his hands, always so sure, shook just a little as they pushed under Zonk’s shirt.

Clothes became obstacles. They didn’t leave the floor. They just pushed fabric out of the way, a tangle of jeans and shirts around their ankles. The air was cool on Zonk’s skin. Then Blaze was against him, skin to skin, and the warmth was everywhere.

It wasn’t graceful. An elbow knocked against the leg of the coffee table. Zonk’s head bumped the couch. They were both breathing hard like they’d run a mile, laughing into each other’s mouths at the awkwardness of it. The laughter felt better than the kissing. It was the same thing, anyway.

Blaze looked down at him, his face shadowed. “You sure?”

Zonk answered by hooking a leg around his hips, pulling him down. “Just… don’t get metaphysical about it.”

“Wouldn’t dream of it.”

It hurt for a second. A sharp, bright point of otherness that made Zonk gasp. Blaze froze, his whole body tense. “Okay?”

“Yeah. Keep going. Just… Keep going.”

It wasn’t about pleasure, not really. It was about presence. Each slow, deliberate movement was a confirmation. You’re here. I’m here. This is us. This is now. The pain faded, replaced by a feeling so dense it had no name. It was a knot in his stomach, a pressure behind his eyes, the solid reality of Blaze above him, inside him, around him. Zonk clung to him, fingers digging into the muscles of his back. He buried his face in Blaze’s neck, rhythm in the scent of sweat and smoke and him.

He didn’t know when he started crying. Silent, helpless tears that leaked out and soaked into Blaze’s skin. Blaze felt them. He didn’t say anything. He just lowered his head, pressed his lips to Zonk’s temple, and kept moving. His own pause was ragged, hitching.

It didn’t last long. It couldn’t. The tension of weeks, of a lifetime was a coiled spring. When the release came, it was quiet. A shudder, a choked-off sound, a collapse. Blaze went heavy on top of him, then quickly rolled, pulling Zonk with him, keeping them connected. They lay on their sides, facing the ruined room, a mess of limbs and shared beat.

The streetlight flickered again.

Zonk’s heart hammered against his ribs. The tears were drying on his face, itchy tracks. He felt hollowed out. Clean.

Blaze’s arm was around him, holding him close. His fingers traced idle patterns on Zonk’s hip.

“So,” Blaze said, his voice a rumble in his chest. “That happened, too.”

Zonk snorted. It turned into a weak laugh. “Yeah.”

“Good?”

“That’s a yes.”

They lay there. The digital clock on the microwave, somehow still plugged in, blinked 12:00. Over and over. A silent, persistent zero.

Eventually, Blaze shifted. “We should move. The floor’s disgusting.”

“You first.”

“You’re on my arm.”

“Your problem.”

Blaze sighed, a contented, heavy sound. He didn’t move his arm. His silence between them between them evened out, deepened. Zonk listened to it. He watched the dust motes, stirred by their chaos, finally begin to settle.

The floor of the apartment was cold. The single lamp they’d managed to salvage cast a weak, yellow puddle of light that didn’t reach the corners. The room was a disaster zone of overturned furniture, emptied drawers, and the sad, scattered remnants of their lives. A single, lonely corn chip lay near Zonk’s knee. He stared at it. Some Sentinel had probably dropped it. The thought was so stupid it made his eyes ache.

Blaze passed him the pipe. It was Zonk’s good one, the glass precisely cleaned with isopropyl and salt until it shone. The ritual felt different. Not a desperate escape hatch, but a decompression stop. A way to let the pressure bleed out slowly so their heads didn’t pop.

“They took my lucky bandana,” Blaze said. He wasn’t looking at Zonk. He was tracing a crack in the floorboard with his thumb. “The purple one from the Shpongle show. It was in my top drawer.”

“Bastards.”

“You think she’s okay?” Blaze asked. He didn’t have to say who.

Zonk saw Pip’s face in the glitter-fall, earnest and terrified and determined. “No. Probably not. But she will be.” The words surprised him. He believed them.

Blaze nodded took his turn with the pipe. The lighter flickered, casting his face in sharp, orange relief. “We left her there.”

“She stayed.”

“Same difference from where I’m sitting.” Blaze exhaled. “Feels shitty.”

Another pause. The refrigerator in the kitchen kicked on with a rattle and a hum. A normal sound. A sound from before. It felt alien.

“My dad had a toolbox,” Zonk said. The words were out before he knew they were coming. He never talked about this. Not sober, not high, not ever. “A red metal one. He kept it on a shelf in the garage. I wasn’t allowed to touch it.” He picked at the skin around his thumbnail. The familiar sting. “One day, after my mom left, he forgot to lock it. I just… opened it. All these perfect, shiny tools. Everything in its place. Wrenches lined up by size. Screwdrivers standing at attention. I took one. A little flathead. Just to hold it. He found it in my room a week later.”

He stopped. His throat was tight. He hadn’t thought about that in years. The memory had the crisp, irrelevant detail of a forgotten photograph: the smell of oil and concrete, the feel of the cold metal in his small hand, the way his father had just looked at him, not angry, just… tired. So tired.

“What’d he do?” Blaze’s voice was quiet.

“Nothing. He just took it back. Put it in the box. Locked it again.” Zonk paused. The tightness wouldn’t go away. “I think I thought… if I had the right tool, I could fix it. Whatever it was. Make it so she’d come back. Make it so he’d look at me.”

The admission hung in the smoky air. It felt dangerous, like leaving a loaded gun on the floor between them.

Blaze didn’t offer platitudes. He just nodded, slow. “I failed geometry. Twice. Mr. Hendricks’s class. The numbers, they just… They’d swim on the page. I’d look at an equation and my brain would just white out. Like static.” He rubbed a hand over his face. “My old man, he’d say, ‘Just focus. It’s not rocket science.’ But it was. To me, it was. I started lifting weights because at least that was a number I could understand. More weight, more reps. Progress you could feel in your hands.”

He handed the pipe back. Zonk took it, but didn’t light it. He just held the warm glass.

“I thought we were just grabbing shiny stuff,” Zonk said. “A game. A quest. A thing to do so we didn’t have to think about anything else.”

“We were.”

“But it wasn’t.”

“No.”

The simplicity of it was brutal. They sat with it. The ghost-shaped stain on the ceiling seemed to pulse in the lamplight.

“I was scared the whole time,” Blaze said, looking straight ahead at the blank, dark TV screen. “Not just, like, ‘oh shit’ scared. I was scared I’d freeze up. That when it mattered, my brain would white out again and I’d get you both killed. That I’d be useless.”

Zonk looked at him. His big, loyal, stupid friend who’d charged past rainbow-colored batons and negotiated with mob princesses and held a broken pony-girl while she cried. “You weren’t.”

“I know that now.” Blaze finally met his eyes. A faint, wry smile touched his mouth. “Turns out the test wasn’t about shape.”

A laugh punched out of Zonk. It was a rough, startled sound. It hurt his throat. “No shit.”

Blaze grinned, then the grin faded. “We’re not going back to normal are we?”

Zonk thought about the before-normal. The endless, circular days. The comfortable, numb grind. The world reduced to a screen and a bag of weed and the reliable presence of the one person who didn’t expect anything from him. It felt like a story about two other guys. “Do you want to?”

“Fuck no.” The answer was immediate. Blaze shrugged, a massive rolling of shoulders. “But I don’t know what comes next.”

“Nobody does.” Zonk finally lit the pipe. The flame caught, glowed. “That’s the thing they don’t put in the brochures.”

They passed it back and forth in silence for a while. The shared ritual. The smoke weaving a fragile bridge over the wreckage of their living room. Zonk noticed a blue feather stuck to the underside of the coffee table—a bit of pegasus down from some Sentinel’s armor. A piece of a different world, trapped in theirs. He left it there.

“We’ll check on her,” Blaze said. It wasn’t a question.

“Yeah. Figure out how the node works now. Bring her some… I don’t know. Cheetos. A flashlight. Something useful.”

“A flashlight. In the magic pony dimension.”

“You got a better idea?”

“No.” Blaze’s smile was real this time. “Flashlights are good.”

The pipe was cashed. Zonk set it carefully on the floor between them, a tiny glass monument. The high was a soft buzz at the base of his skull, taking the sharpest edges off the fatigue. He leaned his head back against the overturned couch. The ceiling ghost seemed to be waving.

The doorbell rang.

The sound was so ordinary, so aggressively mundane, it was shocking. A two-tone chime. Ding-dong.

Zonk and Blaze looked at each other. No one ever rang their bell. The pizza guy left it on the mat. Their one friend, Chad, just texted “here” from the curb.

The bell rang again. Insistent.

“You think the Sentinels are back?” Blaze said, low, already shifting his weight ready to spring up. “With, like, a bill for property damage?”

“They don’t ring.”

Ding-dong.

A third time. Whoever it was wasn’t going away.

Zonk pushed himself to his feet. His knees popped. Every muscle protested. He felt a thousand years old. He looked at Blaze, who was also standing, rolling his shoulders in that familiar pre-conflict way.

They didn’t say anything. They just moved together, stepping over the debris, toward the front door. A team. Not a hero and a sidekick. Just two guys in a trashed apartment, answering a bell.

Zonk put his hand on the knob. The metal was cool. Through the cheap wood, he could feel a presence on the other side. A waiting.

He glanced back at Blaze, who gave a single, sharp nod.

Zonk turned the knob and pulled the door open.
