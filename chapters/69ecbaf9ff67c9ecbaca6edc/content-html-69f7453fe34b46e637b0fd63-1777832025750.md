The smiling sun in the window seemed to sharpen, its rays becoming hard, glittering lines.</p><p>“Log out,” Zonk said. “Now.”</p><p>“But the reward—”</p><p>“Log out, Blaze!”</p><p>The menu. He had to pull up the menu. His fingers, clumsy in the gloves made the gesture—a swipe down from the top of his vision. Nothing happened. He tried again. A system error message flickered, red and angry, in the corner of his eye. Connection Unstable.</p><p>The floor dropped.</p><p>Not literally. The texture of the marble pixels stretched, elongated into streaks of white and gray. The walls melted, running like wet watercolor. The cheerful, looping tavern music distorted, slowing into a deep, groaning bass note that shook his teeth. The smiling sun in the window rotated, its friendly face twisting, the smile pulling into a grimace, then a shriek of light.</p><p>“Zonk!” Blaze’s voice was a burst of static.</p><p>The world tore.</p><p>It was a soundless rip, a sensation of being pulled through a hole lined with sandpaper and static electricity. The pastel galaxy of the loading screen flashed, then shattered into a million jagged fragments. Each fragment reflected a different scene: his dusty apartment, the arcade booth, the velvet cushions, Blaze’s wide, horrified eyes inside his headset.</p><p>Then pain. A white-hot spike driven straight between his eyes.</p><p>He was choking. Gasping. The smell hit him first—not synthetic flowers, but the reek of stale sweat, industrial plastic, and the faint, sweet-sour tang of old vomit. Real smells. Gross smells.</p><p>He clawed at his face. The VR headset was gone. His hands met sweat-slick skin, his own stubbled jaw. He was slumped in a molded plastic chair, the kind that stuck to the back of your thighs. The world was dark, pulsing with the afterimage of that screaming sun.</p><p>A groan to his left. Blaze.</p><p>Zonk forced his eyes open. They were in the Starlight Arcade booth. The cheap LED strip lights overhead were off, but the light from the main floor bled under the door, a sickly yellow. The booth was a closet, barely big enough for two chairs and the humming VR tower. The screens were black.</p><p>His head was a balloon filled with hot sand. He blinked, trying to clear the double vision. Something was digging into his leg. He looked down.</p><p>In his lap, glinting in the dim light was the tiara. The silver one. With the six-pointed star.</p><p>It was real. Solid. Cool to the touch.</p><p>“What the fuck,” he whispered. His voice was a dry croak.</p><p>Blaze was stirring, holding his head. “Did we get roofied? I feel like we got roofied.” He looked at his own lap. The burlap sack was there, a lumpy shape on his thighs. He reached in, pulled out the yellow jar. The smiling sun on the lid seemed mocking now. He unscrewed the lid, sniffed. “It’s… ointment. Smells like aloe and regret.”</p><p>Zonk’s stomach was a cold, hard knot. This wasn’t a glitch. Glitches didn’t leave physical souvenirs. He looked at the other items scattered on the grubby carpet. The red cuffs. The purple flogger. The tongue depressor. The whoopee cushion. All of them. Real.</p><p>Then he saw the third thing.</p><p>Curled on the floor between their chairs, a small shape in the shadows. At first he thought it was a discarded costume, a pile of pastel fabric. Then it moved. A soft, shuddering inhale.</p><p>It was a person. Small. Wearing a fuzzy, mint-green bodysuit that covered them from neck to ankle. A matching green tail, fluffy and artificial, lay limp on the floor. A head of tousled, sandy-brown hair. They were folded in on themselves, knees to chest, face hidden.</p><p>Blaze saw it too. He froze, the jar of ointment halfway to his nose. “Uh.”</p><p>Zonk’s mind emptied. All the sarcastic commentary, the ironic detachment, it just evaporated, leaving a blank, white noise. He stared at the small, huddled form. The arcade’s background noise—the distant pew-pew of laser tag, the thump of bass from a racing game—filtered in, absurd and mundane.</p><p>The figure stirred again. A hand—a human hand, small and pale—came up to push the hair from its face. It lifted its head.</p><p>It was a young man maybe early twenties. His face was round, open, currently scrunched with confusion and what looked like the beginning of tears. His eyes were a wide, guileless blue. He blinked up at them, his gaze drifting from Zonk’s stunned face to the tiara in his lap, to the sack on Blaze’s knees.</p><p>He didn’t look scared. He looked lost. Profoundly, utterly lost.</p><p>He sniffled. Swallowed. Then he spoke, his voice a high, trembling tenor.</p><p>“Are… are you my new keepers?”</p>
