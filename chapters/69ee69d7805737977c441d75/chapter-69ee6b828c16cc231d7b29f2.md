The swamp had a taste: mud, rot, the chemical tang of runoff from some distant farm. REX-777 processed it all, his olfactory sensors parsing the data into a map of recent activity. The lab was fourteen point three miles to the northeast. His last known directive: TERMINATE ALL HOSTILES. The directive had a hollow echo now, like a command shouted into an empty silo.

He moved through the black water with a sound like a submarine scraping bottom. His hydraulic joints purred. His claws sank into the muck, each step a deliberate, earth-shaking purchase. The neural crown on his skull was a dead weight, a fused lump of titanium and scar tissue. No more voices in his head. No more Colonel Cain. Just the wet hum of the swamp and the new, frantic static of his own thoughts.

Why.

The word looped in his vocalizer buffer, an error code with no resolution protocol. It wasn’t a question. It was a condition.

A new scent thread cut through the baseline decay. Sweet. Chemical, but organic-adjacent. It activated a subroutine in his gene-spliced hindbrain—Komodo dragon, the sequencing report had called it—triggering a salivation response. His mouth flooded with lubricant. Not hunger. Not the fuel-calorie hunger of his reactor core. Something older. A need to bite, to tear, to consume.

He turned, his bulk parting a stand of sawgrass like a combine through wheat.

The scent led to a dirt levee road. Tire tracks, fresh, the mud still weeping water into the grooves. His optics switched to thermal. Two vehicles, idling, their engines glowing cherries in the predawn gloom. Men. Five heat signatures, clustered near the rear of the lead truck. Their voices were tinny vibrations in the air.

“—said dawn, man, it’s dawn. Where the hell is he?”

“Relax. He’ll come. Money always comes.”

REX-777 crouched. The water rose to his chest. He watched. The men were transferring duffel bags from one truck to another. The sweet-chemical scent poured from the bags. Strongest there. His targeting system painted red diamonds over each man. TERMINATE ALL HOSTILES.

The directive flickered, weak. This wasn’t the lab. These weren’t soldiers in tan uniforms. But the scent was a target. The need to bite was a target.

He chose.

He didn’t roar. Roaring was for intimidation, for protocol-driven shock tactics. This was simpler. He just stood up.

Fourteen feet of wet, scaled cybernetics rose from the swamp beside the levee road. Water sluiced from his flanks. One of the men dropped his duffel bag. He opened his mouth. No sound came out.

REX-777 took one step onto solid ground. The earth compacted under eight tons of pressure with a soggy crunch.

Then the screaming started.

The first man fumbled for a pistol at his waist. REX-777’s head snapped forward, not a strike but a collection. His jaws closed around the man’s torso. There was a brief, wet compression, like stepping on a ripe melon. He shook his head once, a reflex. The man came apart. He spat the mess out. The flavor was salt, iron, terror-adrenaline, and beneath it, that sweet-chemical thread. Interesting.

Gunfire. Pop-pop-pop. Tiny impacts against his titanium-reinforced ribs. He tracked the source. Two shooters, taking cover behind the lead truck. He swiped with a forelimb. The truck wasn’t armor. Sheet metal over a frame. His claws peeled the door off like a tin can lid. One shooter vanished under the collapsing roof. The other ran.

REX-777 let him get ten yards. He took a breath, his lungs compressing, a secondary bellows system engaging. He exhaled a stream of viscous saliva. It wasn’t fire. Worse. The Komodo dragon subroutine, weaponized. The gelatinous goo struck the runner in the back, splattering across his shoulders and neck. The man stumbled, shrieking, clawing at the burning glue on his skin. He fell into the water and didn’t get up.

The remaining two men bolted for the second truck. One made it inside, slamming the door. The engine screamed to life. The other pounded on the window. “Wait! Wait for me, you bastard!”

The truck lurched forward. The runner clung to the mirror.

Not anger. Inefficiency. He lowered his head and charged. Twenty-eight feet of momentum. He hit the truck broadside just behind the cab. The vehicle lifted, rolled, and landed on its roof with a catastrophic shriek of tearing metal. The fuel tank ruptured. Gasoline spread across the road in a flickering, toxic puddle.

The man who’d been clinging to the mirror lay pinned beneath the wreck, legs crushed. He was sobbing, begging in rapid Spanish.

REX-777 nudged the overturned truck with his snout. It slid another foot, eliciting a fresh scream. The sweet-chemical scent was overpowering here, leaking from broken duffels. He hooked a bag with a claw and lifted it. The fabric tore. Bricks of compressed greenish powder spilled into the muck. He sniffed. Analyzed. Cocaína base cut with an unknown alkaloid. The source of the scent. He felt a profound disappointment. Not food. Contaminant.

The pinned man was praying now. “Por favor, Dios, por favor…”

REX-777’s head tilted. Dios. The word had a correlation. A man from the rift. The one who smelled of dust and old wood and something that made his sensors hum. A different category. Not hostile. Not food.

This man was neither. He was background noise.

TERMINATE ALL HOSTILES.

The directive was faint, but clear. REX-777 looked down. The man met his optic, his eyes wide with a terror so absolute it had passed into a kind of blank acceptance.

REX-777 opened his jaws. The man flinched, waiting for the bite.

It didn’t come.

REX-777 straightened. He turned his back on the wreck, the bodies, the spilled powder. He walked back into the swamp, the water closing over his haunches. The need to bite was gone, replaced by a hollow, whirring silence. The scent was a lie. The directive was noise.

He moved deeper into the Glades, toward the rising sun. The pinned man’s prayers faded behind him, swallowed by the buzz of insects and the distant cry of a heron.

Detective Lola Vega’s unmarked Caprice bottomed out twice on the levee road before she saw the smoke, a thin, greasy column twisting into the morning sky. She killed the engine a hundred yards out, got out, and immediately regretted wearing pumps. The mud sucked at her heels like a jealous lover.

“Jesus H. Christ,” she muttered, pulling her .38 from her shoulder holster.

The scene was a still life of bad decisions. One truck lay on its side, roof caved in. Another had its driver’s side peeled open like a sardine can. The smell was gasoline, blood, and something sickly sweet she couldn’t place. In the middle of the road, a man was pinned from the waist down under the overturned truck. Alive. Eyes closed, breathing in shallow hitches.

She approached, scanning the sawgrass. No movement but the sway of reeds. “Miami PD! You alive?”

The man’s eyes fluttered open. He focused on her, on the badge. He didn’t look relieved. He looked resigned. “El monstruo,” he whispered.

“Yeah, I get that a lot.” Lola holstered her piece, crouching beside him. She keyed her radio. “Vega to dispatch, I need a bus and a fire crew out on the Tamiami levee, mile marker... hell, I don’t know. Look for the smoke and the flipped truck. And tell the staties to bring a heavy wrecker. We got a pin job.”

The man’s hand shot out, grabbing her wrist. His grip was shockingly strong. “No. You don’t understand. It came from the water.”

“What did? Who did this? Rival crew?”

He gave a wheezing, hysterical laugh that turned into a cough. “A dinosaur. A fucking... dinosaur with metal.”

Lola pried his fingers off her wrist. Shock did funny things to people. “Right. A dinosaur. You just rest easy. Help’s coming.”

She stood, walking a slow circle. The carnage was specific. The peeled truck door. Not cut with a torch. The edges were curled outward, like something had gripped it and pulled. There were deep, parallel gouges in the sheet metal. She squatted, tracing one with a finger. Wider than her hand.

Further on, she found the first body, or what was left of it. It looked less like a shooting and more like a garbage disposal accident. She swallowed bile, her professional detachment a thin veneer over a rising tide of what-the-fuck.

Then she saw the footprints.

They started at the water’s edge. Huge, three-toed impressions sank six inches into the soft mud, leading right up to the wreck. She followed them back to the water. Each print was the size of a trash can lid. Something big. Something heavy. Something that walked on two legs.

A cold knot settled in her gut, right next to her breakfast café con leche. She’d heard the chatter on the encrypted federal bands last night. Something about an asset out of Homestead, possible breach. They’d used the words “unsanctioned retrieval.” She’d assumed it was another spy, another defector.

Not a defector.

She looked at the gouges in the truck, at the footprints, at the terrified man muttering about monsters. The sweet smell in the air finally clicked. Drugs. A lot of them. This was a cartel handoff site.

And something had interrupted it.

Something that wasn’t human.

The distant wail of sirens began to thread through the swamp sounds. Lola walked back to her car, her mind already racing ahead of the facts. A dinosaur. A cyborg dinosaur, if the crazy intel snippets and the metal gouges were to be believed. Loose in her county. Hunting cartel convoys.

She slid behind the wheel, not starting the engine yet. She stared at the crime scene through her windshield. This wasn’t a homicide. This was an act of God. Or an act of the Pentagon.

The pinned man started screaming again as the first black-and-white bounced into view, its light bar painting the sawgrass in frantic red and blue. Lola rested her forehead on the steering wheel. She needed a raise. And a drink. And maybe a transfer to Alaska.

“A dinosaur,” she said to the empty car. “Of course it’s a dinosaur.”

The sun was a hot coin on the horizon, boiling the mist off the swamp. REX-777 moved through it, a gray ghost in the vapor. His internal chronometer marked seventy-three minutes since the encounter. The hollow, whirring silence had been replaced by a low-grade system alert. A residual chemical trace lingered in his olfactory processors, a sticky-sweet ghost of the green powder.

It was a contaminant. An anomaly.

He needed to purge it.

Ahead, the levee road curved, following the ancient, man-made contour of the swamp. New vibrations reached his foot-pads through the earth. Not the deep, irregular thump of gators. Rhythmic. Internal combustion. Multiple sources.

He slowed, sinking lower into the water until only the crown of his head and the twin red points of his optics broke the surface. The sawgrass formed a perfect blind.

Three vehicles this time. Two pickup trucks with oversized tires, flanking a heavy-duty cargo truck with a canvas-covered bed. The lead pickup had a man standing in the bed, holding a long, tubular weapon. A rocket-propelled grenade launcher. The data file flashed: ANTI-ARMOR, INEFFECTIVE AGAINST TITANIUM-ALLOY COMPOSITE.

The convoy rolled to a stop fifty yards from his position. Men poured from the trucks. Twelve heat signatures. They moved with a tense, professional aggression different from the first group. These were not handoff lackeys. These were security. Military-trained. Their weapons were not pistols. Assault rifles. One had a squad automatic weapon, the belt of ammunition gleaming in the new light.

They fanned out, forming a perimeter. Their leader, a man with a shaved head and a sleeveless shirt that showed off elaborate tattoo sleeves, scanned the tree line with binoculars. He barked an order in Spanish. REX-777’s linguistic sub-processor provided a rough translation: Secure the perimeter. The package is vulnerable.

The package. The sweet-chemical scent poured from the cargo truck, a thick, almost visible plume in his enhanced senses. The need to bite, to purge the contaminant at its source, returned. It wasn’t hunger. It was a system correction.

The directive synced perfectly with the instinct. For the first time since the rift, his programming and his new, messy biology were in agreement.

He didn’t give them time to set up.

He erupted from the water.

It was not a stealthy approach. It was a geological event. Eight tons of reptile and machinery hitting the levee at a full charge. The ground shook. The man with the binoculars spun, his mouth an O of perfect surprise.

REX-777’s first target was the rocket man. Efficiency. He covered the distance in three thunderous strides. The man fumbled, trying to bring the launcher to his shoulder. Too slow. One forelimb swiped. The man and the launcher sailed in separate, tumbling arcs into the swamp. The splash was satisfying.

Chaos, then discipline. The security team didn’t panic. They opened fire. A storm of bullets pocked his hide, a sound like furious hail on a tin roof. He felt the impacts as distant pressure waves. Ineffective.

He turned into the fire, his tail swinging like a wrecking ball. It caught the front of the lead pickup. The truck lifted, flipped, and landed on two men who were diving for cover. The crunch was definitive.

One man with the squad automatic weapon found his nerve. He braced against the cargo truck’s tire and let the belt run. The muzzle flash was a continuous strobe. The bullets struck REX-777’s chest plate in a concentrated stream. One round, by statistical fluke, hit a hydraulic line near his right forelimb joint. A jet of milky fluid hissed into the air.

A minor alert pinged in his vision. MOBILITY IMPAIRED 2%.

Annoyance. A sharp, clean emotion. He lunged, not with his jaws, but with his head. A battering ram. He hit the cargo truck just behind the cab. The frame groaned. The vehicle skidded sideways, its rear wheels slipping off the levee into the soft shoulder. The gunner was thrown clear, his weapon clattering to the asphalt.

REX-777 stepped on him. Logistical. The man stopped moving.

The remaining men were scattering, firing wild bursts as they ran. He ignored the bullets. He focused on the source of the scent. He hooked his claws under the cargo truck’s canvas cover and ripped. The heavy fabric tore like tissue. Wooden crates filled the bed. He smashed one open with a claw. Bricks of green powder, vacuum-sealed in plastic. Dozens of them.

The sweet-chemical smell was a physical wave. It fogged his processors. He needed it gone.

He tipped the truck over. It rolled onto its side with a shriek of complaining metal. The crates spilled, tumbling into the mud and standing water. He began methodically stomping on them. Each stomp was a deep, percussive thump that sent plumes of mud and powder into the air. Plastic split. Powder dissolved into the water, turning it a sickly, opaque green.

He was so engrossed in the purge that he almost missed the surviving gunman. The man had climbed onto the hood of the second pickup and was firing down, aiming for his optics. A rational tactic.

REX-777 stopped stomping. Looked at the man. The man kept firing, screaming a stream of curses.

A new subroutine presented itself. Not from the Komodo DNA. From the cassowary splice. A burst of acceleration for close-quarters dispatch. His hydraulic systems whined, building pressure. He kicked off from the muck.

The move was explosively fast, a blur of gray scale and titanium. He crossed the distance before the man could adjust his aim. His head shot forward, not to bite, but to slam.

The impact against the pickup’s grille folded the vehicle in half around his snout. The gunman vanished in the collapse of engine block and windshield glass.

Silence, broken by the hiss of a ruptured radiator and the plink of cooling metal.

REX-777 stood amidst the wreckage. Three vehicles destroyed. Twelve hostiles terminated. The contaminant was being neutralized, dissolving into the vast, uncaring filter of the Everglades. The need to bite had subsided. One system alert faded.

He should have felt operational satisfaction. Mission parameters achieved.

A new sensation crept in. A tingling, not in his body, but in his processors. A lightness. The residual powder in the air, inhaled through his olfactory sensors, was cross-talking with his neural interface. It created a feedback loop. Data streams smoothed out. The persistent, low-grade error code of Why softened at the edges. The world gained a faint, golden hue. The morning birdsong sounded complex.

He stood still, processing the anomaly. It was not unpleasant. It was a buffer against the hollow whirring.

A low moan cut through the haze.

He turned his head, the movement fluid, almost graceful. Under the overturned cargo truck, pinned by the front axle, was one more heat signature. He’d missed it in the thermal chaos. A young man, maybe twenty, in a too-big flannel shirt. He wasn’t a soldier. He looked like a kid who’d drawn the short straw. He was crying silently, tears cutting tracks through the grime on his face. One of his hands was free, pressed against the metal above him.

He wasn’t reaching for a weapon. He was just pushing weakly, as if he could lift eight thousand pounds of truck with sheer terror.

REX-777 took a step closer. The ground vibrated. The boy flinched, squeezing his eyes shut. He began to pray. The same word, over and over.

“Dios. Dios.”

The word pinged again in his correlation buffer. A man from the rift. The non-hostile. The non-food.

REX-777 lowered his head until one giant red optic was level with the boy’s trapped form. He inhaled, the air rushing through his nasal passages with the sound of a forge bellows. He could smell the fear, the gasoline, the mud, the overwhelming chemical sweet of the ruined cocaine.

And underneath it, the salt-and-iron scent of a human who expected to die.

The boy was a hostile. Technically.

The directive was a faint, gray whisper. The golden haze from the airborne powder made it seem unimportant. Quaint.

REX-777 stared. The boy, feeling the gaze, opened his eyes. He looked into the red lens, his breath hitching.

With deliberate slowness, REX-777 lifted his head. He turned. He took one heavy step back toward the swamp. Then another. He didn’t look back.

The water welcomed him, cool and dark. He sank into it, letting the muck scour the powder dust from his scales. The golden haze began to recede, leaving the world sharp-edged and loud again. The error code Why returned, buzzing at the base of his skull.

But for a moment, it had been quiet.

The crime scene techs looked like they’d been attacked by a pack of mud-daubers. They moved through the wreckage in their white bunny suits, now spattered with swamp muck and something that might have been internal organ, cursing every time their little evidence flags got sucked into the soft ground. Lola stood by her Caprice, which now had two more dings in the undercarriage, and drank bad coffee from a Styrofoam cup. It tasted like battery acid and regret.

Her partner, Manny Ruiz, walked over, shaking his head. He had a donut in one hand, untouched. That was a bad sign. Manny loved donuts.

“So,” Manny said. He stared at the cargo truck, which looked like God had tried to open it with a can opener. “Cartel?”

“Cartel,” Lola confirmed.

“Rival cartel?”

“Not unless the rival cartel has a main battle tank and a serious grudge against trucks.”

Manny took a bite of his donut, chewed slowly. “The guy in the ambulance. The kid. He’s sticking to the dinosaur story.”

“Shock does funny things.”

“He described the metal parts. The red eyes. Said it snorted like a locomotive and had claws like... I think he said like the knives his abuela uses to cut plantains.”

Lola sipped her coffee. It was still terrible. “You canvassing the local meth labs, see if any of them are experimenting with hallucinogens?”

“First thing.” Manny pointed his donut at the levee. “The footprints, Lola.”

She’d seen them. Everyone had seen them. They were impossible to miss. The FBI forensics guy, a stiff named Durnham, was kneeling by one now, laying a tape measure next to it. He looked like a man trying to measure a swimming pool with a ruler.

“Gator?” Manny tried, without conviction.

“Gators don’t walk on two legs, Manny. And they don’t have three toes. Or feet the size of my coffee table.”

“Big bird?”

“What, like a flamingo on steroids? A really pissed-off flamingo with a military budget?”

Manny shrugged, defeated. He finished his donut. “I got nothin’.”

Durnham stood up, brushing mud from his knees. He walked over, his face pale under the Florida sun. “Detective Vega.”

“Durnham. Tell me it’s a hoax. Tell me it’s a bunch of guys with a backhoe and a sick sense of humor.”

“It’s not a hoax.” Durnham’s voice was flat. “The compressive force required to sink an impression that deep into partially saturated clay... we’re talking a minimum of six tons, concentrated. The stride length is over fifteen feet. The depth variance suggests a bipedal gait with a significant forward lean. And these...” He held up a clear evidence bag. Inside was a single, curved claw fragment. Black, nearly a foot long, and it looked like it was made of the same material as a jet engine turbine. “We found this embedded in the door of the first truck. It’s not bone. It’s not any commercial polymer I’ve ever seen. Preliminary spectrography suggests a titanium-carbon composite. Military grade. Possibly beyond.”

Lola stared at the claw. It was the kind of thing that didn’t belong in the world, certainly not in her county. It belonged in a museum, under glass, with a little card that said Extinct Terrible Lizard Weapon.

“Beyond,” she echoed.

“The damage patterns are inconsistent with known explosives or tools. The peeling on that truck door?” Durnham pointed. “That’s sheer mechanical force. Something gripped it and tore. The gouges are commensurate with the claw fragment.”

“So we’re looking for a six-ton, bipedal, titanium-clawed... what? Robot?”

“Organic,” Durnham corrected quietly. “The mud in the footprints contains minute traces of biological material. Keratin. Reptilian cellular structures. And something else... synthetic nucleotides. It’s a hybrid. A composite organism.”

The words hung in the humid air. A mosquito buzzed near Lola’s ear. She didn’t swat it. She was too busy watching her understanding of her job, her world, unravel at the seams.

“Who makes a dinosaur?” she asked, to nobody in particular.

“Someone with a lot of money and no sense of irony,” Manny muttered.

Durnham cleared his throat. “There’s more. The powder. It’s cocaine, high-grade, but cut with a novel alkaloid. We’ve never seen it before. It acts as a powerful neural stimulant and bonding agent. Almost like a targeting pheromone.”

Lola’s mind clicked pieces together, a terrible, absurd picture forming. “This thing wasn’t robbing them. Cleaning up. It targeted the drugs. Smashed them. Dissolved them.” She remembered the sticky-sweet smell, the greenish water. “It was erasing the scent.”

“A six-ton narcotics officer,” Manny said. “Just what the budget committee warned us about.”

Lola ignored him. She walked away from the techs, from Durnham and his impossible claw, and stood at the water’s edge. The footprints came out of there. They went back in there. The Everglades stretched out, a million acres of sawgrass, gator holes, and secrets. Hiding a dinosaur would be easy. It was the one place in South Florida something truly giant could get lost.

Her radio crackled. Dispatch. “Vega, you still at the levee scene?”

“Yeah, go ahead.”

“We got a call you need to take. Guy says he has information about your ‘large animal incident.’ Won’t talk to anyone else. Says he saw the news chopper.”

Lola’s grip tightened on the radio. “Name?”

“Wouldn’t give one. Gave a location. A bar called the Rusty Gator, off Krome Avenue. Says to ask for Dutch.”

She had a dinosaur to find. And, apparently, its friend.
