The badge didn't impress the cartel guys. It didn't impress Rex either. The lead SUV revved again, its driver clearly weighing the pros and cons of running over a cop versus getting eaten by a cybernetic dinosaur. Lola kept her Glock leveled. “Miami PD! Everyone freeze!”

Fridge Guy spat on the pavement. “No eres policía. Eres basura.”

Dutch leaned out the window. “Hey, hey, let's not get racist—”

The SUV lurched forward. Rex moved faster.

Lola had seen gators strike in the Everglades—that terrifying burst of speed where biology became blur. Rex made gators look like seniors doing water aerobics. One second he was standing there, the next his tail whipped sideways and took out the SUV's front axle like a Louisville Slugger hitting a beer can. The vehicle flipped. It rolled twice before smashing into a ditch, its horn blaring one long, dying note.

The other SUVs froze.

So did Lola.

Rex turned his glowing red optics on her. His text-to-speech voice crackled. “BADGE. INEFFECTIVE.”

Dutch clapped his hands. “That's my boy! Tactical genius!”

Jesus sighed. “Perhaps we could try diplomacy first next time?”

The kid in the backseat made a sound like a deflating balloon. “They're gonna kill us.”

Lola holstered her gun. “Not today.” She jerked her chin at the remaining cartel guys. “You! Help your friends out of that wreck before it catches fire. Then get the hell out of my county.”

Fridge Guy's men exchanged glances. One muttered, “Está loca.”

Rex took a step toward them. Hydraulics hissed.

They moved.

Lola turned to Jesus. “You. In the van.”

He raised an eyebrow. “That wasn't a request, was it?”

“Damn right it wasn't.” She shoved him toward the passenger seat, then rounded on Dutch. “Keys.”

He clutched them to his chest. “My van!”

“Your van's got twelve bullet holes and a missing rear bumper. I'm driving.” She snatched the keys. The engine coughed to life with a sound like a tuberculosis patient. Rex tilted his head. “VEHICLE. UNRELIABLE.”

Dutch patted the dashboard. “She's got character.”

Lola threw it in gear. “Where's this shelter?”

Jesus pointed south. “Overtown. Sister Mercy's place.”

The kid perked up. “She's got tamales.”

They left the cartel to their wrecked SUVs. Rex kept pace beside the van, his footfalls shaking the asphalt. Every few steps, he glanced back, scanning for pursuers. Lola caught herself checking the mirrors too. Old habit.

Dutch lit a cigarette with shaking hands. “So, Detective Vega. How's your first day going?”

She white-knuckled the wheel. “I had a stack of parking tickets to process this morning.”

“Better than getting shot at, right?”

“Debatable.”

The kid leaned forward. “Are you gonna arrest us?”

Lola didn't answer. The truth was, she didn't know. Her career had just nosedived into a dumpster fire. But the alternative—pretending she hadn't seen a talking dinosaur and a guy who dodged bullets like they were bad Tinder matches—wasn't an option either.

Jesus studied her profile. “You're wondering if this is worth it.”

She shot him a look. “Don't do that.”

“Do what?”

“The mind-reading messiah crap.”

He smiled. “It's not mind-reading. You've got a tell.”

“What tell?”

“Your left eyelid twitches when you're weighing moral dilemmas.”

Dutch snorted. “Mine does that when I forget to pay my cable bill.”

The van hit a pothole. Something clanged loose underneath. Rex's voice crackled from outside. “RECOMMEND. MECHANICAL. ASSISTANCE.”

Lola ignored him. “This Sister Mercy. She's cool with harboring fugitives?”

Jesus folded his hands. “She's cool with feeding the hungry. The rest is semantics.”

Overtown smelled like fried plantains and diesel exhaust. Sister Mercy's shelter occupied a converted storefront between a pawn shop and a laundromat. The sign out front read: ALL ARE WELCOME (YES, EVEN YOU).

Rex crouched in the alley, optics dimmed to avoid drawing attention. “I'LL. STAND. GUARD.”

Dutch gave him a thumbs-up. “Attaboy.”

Inside, the shelter was all folding chairs and crockpots. A dozen people looked up as they entered. A woman in a flour-dusted apron wiped her hands on her hips. “Well. If it isn't the Second Coming and his entourage.”

Jesus smiled. “Hello, Mercy.”

Sister Mercy took one look at Lola's torn blazer and the kid's shell-shocked expression, then jerked her thumb toward the back room. “Kitchen. Now.”

The tamales were, as advertised, incredible.

Lola ate three before she realized how hungry she'd been. The kid inhaled his like he was storing for winter. Dutch talked with his mouth full.

Mercy smacked his hand with a wooden spoon. “Swallow first.”

A small TV in the corner played the noon news. The volume was low, but the caption grabbed Lola's attention: TELEVANGELIST DECLARES “MIAMI MESSIAH” A FRAUD.

Reverend Bobby Lux's face filled the screen, his tan too perfect, his teeth too white. “This so-called miracle worker is a dangerous impostor! A blasphemer! And let me tell you, friends, his association with that... that monster proves he's in league with dark forces!”

The camera cut to footage of Rex flipping a dumpster. The chyron read: CHURCH OF THE HOLY CHOMP?

Dutch choked on his tamale. “They named a religion after him?”

On screen, a young woman with neon-blue hair waved a sign that read REX 4EVER. The reporter asked why she'd joined the movement.

She grinned. “Duh. Giant robot dinosaur.”

Rex's voice crackled from the alley. “I. AM. NOT. A. ROBOT.”

Mercy muted the TV. “You boys brought trouble to my doorstep.”

Jesus wiped his hands. “We'll leave—”

“No.” She pointed the spoon at him. “You'll stay. Eat. Then we figure out how to keep you alive.”

Lola's phone buzzed. Morales again. She silenced it.

Outside, a car slowed in front of the shelter. Tires crunched gravel.

Rex's optics flared red. “COMPANY.”

Lola drew her Glock. “How many?”

“ONE. VEHICLE. NON-MILITARY.”

The doorbell rang.

Mercy sighed. “Lord, give me strength.”

A teenager in a REX 4EVER T-shirt stood there, holding a clipboard. “Uh. Hi. We're, like, collecting signatures to get our guy on the ballot?”

Dutch blinked. “What ballot?”

“Mayor of Miami.”

Jesus put his head in his hands.

Lola holstered her gun. “I need a drink.”

Mercy handed her a tamale instead. “Eat first. Then we'll discuss your life choices.”

Outside, Rex made a sound that might've been a chuckle. Or a hydraulic leak. Hard to tell.

The kitchen smelled of cumin and lard, a scent that reminded Jesus of market days in Capernaum. He rolled up his sleeves and plunged his hands into the sink, scrubbing tamale pots with the same focused attention he'd once given to fishermen's nets. The soap bubbles caught the light, iridescent for a moment before popping into nothing.

Sister Mercy hip-checked him aside to grab a dish towel. “You don't have to do that.”

“Neither do you.” He handed her a dripping plate.

She snorted. “Touché.”

Through the serving window, he watched the chaos unfolding in the common room. Dutch held court with the Rex fan club, sketching merch ideas on napkins—apparently the Holy Chomp logo needed more “bite appeal.” Miguel demonstrated his best dinosaur roar to impressed onlookers. Lola sat stiffly in a folding chair, her fingers drumming against her holstered Glock like it was the only sane object left in the room.

Miguel bounced on his toes. “We're gonna be on TV?”

“Is it true you can walk on water?”

Lola grabbed Miguel's collar. “Stick close.”

Lola gritted her teeth. “Faster, Dutch.”
