Detective Lola Vega's coffee had gone cold three witness statements ago. The fluorescent lights buzzed like angry hornets trapped in the precinct's drop ceiling. Across the desk, Captain Morales massaged his temples with fingers that smelled like cigar smoke and regret.

“Let me get this straight,” he said. “You want me to authorize overtime and a forensics team to investigate...” He squinted at her report. “A quote-unquote ‘cyborg dinosaur’ that massacred a cartel convoy, then allegedly saved a hitchhiking messiah from a drug deal gone wrong.”

Lola didn't blink. “The bite radius matches no known predator. The claw marks are consistent with titanium alloy. And the survivor swears it spoke.”

Morales flipped a page. “Says here he also claims the dinosaur winked at him.”

“Eyewitnesses are unreliable under stress.”

“So is your judgment, apparently.” He tossed the folder aside. “Vega, you're my best detective, but this? This reads like someone snorted their evidence locker.”

Lola's pen tapped against her thigh. Three distinct rhythms—cop, cop, criminal, a habit from the academy she'd never shaken. “Sir, we have twelve dead cartel operatives with wounds inconsistent with any firearm or tool in our database. We have a missing Pentagon black-site project called LAZARUS REX.”

“We have a city that averages three gangland executions a day.” Morales leaned forward, his chair groaning. “You know what I don't have? Budget for Jurassic Park fan fiction.”

Outside, a patrolman laughed at something. The sound grated like sand in a wound. Lola's gaze drifted to the crime scene photos pinned to her corkboard, tire tracks bisected by something massive, something that left prints deeper than a gator's in Everglades mud.

Morales followed her stare. “Christ, Vega. You're not even listening.”

“I'm building a case.”

“You're building a conspiracy board.” He sighed. “Look, file your report. But unless your dinosaur starts paying taxes, this ends here.”

Lola stood. Her badge caught the light, throwing a jagged reflection across the ceiling. “One more thing. The hitchhiker.”

“The messiah.” Morales rolled his eyes.

“Witnesses say he healed a gunshot wound with his bare hands.”

“Witnesses also say Elvis works at their laundromat.”

She pulled a plastic evidence bag from her jacket. Inside, a single crushed cigarette bore the faintest shimmer of gold along its filter. “Lab says this isn't tobacco. Or pot. Or anything they've seen before.”

Morales didn't touch it. “And?”

“And I've got a CI who swears Dutch Donovan's been peddling ‘miracle weed’ that makes people tell the truth.” She leaned in. “The same Dutch Donovan whose van was spotted at Eden World the night the Pentagon lost their pet project.”

For the first time, the captain hesitated. His pinky ring tapped the desk, once, twice. Then he waved her off. “Coincidences. Go write up the cartel shooting. Standard drug-war crap.”

Lola's jaw tightened. She'd expected this and had prepared for it. From her pocket, she slid a second folder across the desk.

Morales opened it. His eyebrows climbed. “This is a classified military incident report.”

“Leaked by a source at Homestead Air Reserve Base. Note the timestamp.” She tapped the page. “03:47 AM, June 15. Same night our ‘coincidences’ started.”

The captain's face did something complicated. “Where the hell did you—”

“Sir, respectfully?” Lola smiled, all teeth. “I'm not the one who needs to answer that question.”

Morales stared at her. Then at the file. Then at the ceiling, as if praying for patience. The fluorescent light flickered. Somewhere, a phone rang unanswered.

“Goddammit, Vega.” He slammed the folder shut. “One week. No badge, no backup, no paper trail. You bring me something I can actually take to IA without getting us both committed, or you drop this insanity forever.”

Lola was already moving. “You'll want to see this next part.”

“What next part?”

She paused at the door. “The part where I tell you Dutch's van was just spotted at a Publix on 8th Street.”

Morales groaned. “Of course it was.”

* * *

The parking lot smelled of spilled soda and bad decisions. Lola crouched behind a minivan with a “My Child Is an Honor Student” sticker peeling off its bumper. Across the asphalt, Dutch Donovan's van—a rusted Dodge with a wizard dolphin airbrushed on the side—sat conspicuously empty.

Her radio crackled. “Vega, you sure about this?”

She adjusted her earpiece. “Positive. Stay back unless I signal.”

A pause. “Signal for what, exactly? Rabid dinosaur or second coming?”

Lola didn't answer. The supermarket's automatic doors hissed open, and Dutch emerged, arms laden with grocery bags. Behind him shuffled the kid from the warehouse, bandaged, pale, gripping a Slurpee like it was the only thing tethering him to reality.

No messiah. No dinosaur.

Lola exhaled. Maybe Morales was right.

The kid dropped his Slurpee. It hit the pavement with a wet splat, neon red spreading like a bloodstain. Dutch spun, groceries forgotten.

From the alley beside the store, a shadow moved.

Not a shadow.

A shape.

A hulking, impossible silhouette stepped into the light with the deliberate grace of something that knew exactly how terrifying it was.

Lola's hand went to her sidearm. Her pulse roared in her ears.

The cyborg T-Rex, because that's what it was, Jesus Christ, that's what it actually was, tilted its head, scanning the parking lot with a single glowing eye. Its metal claws clicked against the asphalt.

Dutch said something Lola couldn't hear. The dinosaur rumbled in response. Then, with terrifying casualness, it bent its massive head and plucked a shopping bag from Dutch's grip, holding it delicately in its teeth like a dog carrying a kitten.

The kid fainted.

Lola's radio crackled again. “Uh, Vega? You seeing this?”

She swallowed. “Negative. Maintain position.”

“Bullshit. That's a—”

“Maintain. Position.” She thumbed the volume down, heart hammering.

Across the lot, Dutch dragged the kid toward the van. The dinosaur followed, its tail sweeping a parked Cadillac's mirror clean off. No one screamed. No one ran. Just another Miami Tuesday.

Then the gunfire started.

Lola ducked as bullets pinged off the van's hood. A black Suburban screeched into the lot, windows down, muzzles flashing. Cartel. Had to be.

The dinosaur roared—a sound like a garbage compactor eating a jet engine—and lunged.

Lola's training warred with her sanity. Her hand hovered over her badge.

Morales's voice echoed in her head: No badge. No backup. No paper trail.

She made a decision.

“All units, disregard. False alarm.” She keyed the radio off, then drew her gun.

First, she had some questions for Dutch.

Lola moved low and fast between parked cars, her Glock leading the way. The cartel shooters had better firepower, but she had the element of surprise, assuming anyone could be surprised by anything after a cyborg T-Rex just went grocery shopping.

Dutch yanked the kid behind the van's open door. The dinosaur—Rex, the kid had called it—was gone, vanished into the urban sprawl with terrifying efficiency. Bullets chewed through the wizard dolphin's tailfin.

“Federal agent!” Lola barked, though she wasn't, not today.

Dutch's head snapped up. “Oh great. Cops.”

The Suburban's doors flew open. Four men in designer sunglasses and cheap suits spilled out, AKs blazing. A bag of oranges exploded near Lola's feet. Pulp splattered her shoes.

She returned fire. One shooter dropped, clutching his thigh. The others scrambled for cover behind a pickup truck with a bumper sticker that read “My Other Ride is Your Mom.”

Dutch grabbed the kid's collar. “Move!”

They bolted for the store. Lola cursed and followed, her badge bouncing against her chest. The automatic doors wheezed open just as another volley shattered the glass.

Inside, chaos. Shoppers screamed. A stock boy dropped a crate of avocados. The manager hit the deck behind the register, his name tag reading “STEVE—ASK ME ABOUT OUR WEEKLY SPECIALS.”

Dutch skidded around a pyramid of canned beans. “This way!”

Lola tackled him into the dairy aisle. Milk gallons toppled like dominoes. “You're not going anywhere, Donovan.”

“Lady, I don't know who you think—”

“Save it.” She wrenched his arm behind his back. “Where's the dinosaur?”

The kid groaned from the floor. “He doesn't like that word.”

A bullet punched through the egg display. Yolks rained down.

Lola dragged Dutch behind a stack of pallets. “Fine. Where's Rex?”

Dutch blinked. “You know his name?”

“I read case files.” She peeked over the pallets. The shooters were fanning out, checking aisles. “Now tell me why a Pentagon black-site experiment is buying Frosted Flakes with you.”

The kid sat up, rubbing his head. “They're for Rex. He likes the tiger.”

Lola stared at him.

Dutch sighed. “Long story short—”

Gunfire cut him off. The cereal boxes above them erupted in a colorful storm of puffed rice and marshmallows.

Lola ejected her spent mag and slapped in a fresh one. “Short version. Now.”

“Okay, okay!” Dutch flinched as a Lucky Charms leprechaun took a bullet to the face. “Rex escaped from Eden World. I helped him. Now the cartel, the feds, and a televangelist with a private army want us dead.”

The kid nodded. “Also, Jesus is back.”

Lola's finger slipped off the trigger. “What?”

A voice spoke behind her, calm as a Sunday morning. “Technically, I never left.”

She spun.

The man from the warehouse stood there, barefoot in a stolen Publix apron, holding a banana like it was a microphone. His beard had crumbs in it.

Lola's training short-circuited. “You.”

He smiled. “Detective.”

The shooters rounded the corner.

Jesus peeled the banana. “We should go.”

Dutch groaned. “Oh, now you wanna lead?”

The first shooter raised his rifle.

Jesus took a bite of the banana. Chewed. Swallowed. Then he lobbed the peel onto the linoleum with the precision of a Vegas blackjack dealer.

The lead shooter hit it at full sprint. His feet went skyward. His AK discharged into the ceiling. Sprinklers kicked on, drenching everyone in lukewarm water.

Lola blinked. “Did you just—”

“Rule number one in food service.” Jesus wiped his hands on the apron. “Always clean up your slips.”

The remaining shooters hesitated. One crossed himself.

Dutch grabbed Lola's sleeve. “You coming or what?”

She hesitated. Morales's warning echoed in her head. Then a bullet took out the light fixture above her.

“Goddammit.” She shoved Dutch toward the stockroom. “Move!”

The kid scrambled after them. Jesus brought up the rear, humming something that sounded suspiciously like “Day-O.”

The stockroom was a maze of pallets and shrink-wrapped toilet paper. Lola kicked open the emergency exit. Sunlight and car horns poured in.

Dutch bolted for the van. “Shotgun!”

The kid tripped over a pothole. “He means the seat, right?”

A bullet answered him.

Lola returned fire, herding them toward the van. The engine roared to life. Dutch leaned out the passenger window. “Get in or get left!”

She glanced back. Jesus stood in the alley, watching the sky like he expected divine intervention.

“Hey!” Lola snapped. “Messiah or not, I'm not dragging your ass!”

He sighed. “Fair.”

They piled in just as the Suburban screeched around the corner. Lola slammed the door. “Go!”

Dutch floored it. The van lurched forward, its wizard dolphin grinning madly as they peeled out onto 8th Street.

Lola twisted in her seat. The cartel guys were gaining.

Then the dumpster launched. Six hundred pounds of steel and garbage flipped through the air like a Frisbee and landed square on the Suburban's hood. The impact sounded like the world's worst gong.

Rex stepped out from behind a Taco Bell, wiping his claws on the pavement.

Dutch whooped. “That's my boy!”

Lola's brain stuttered. “Did he just—”

“Yep.” The kid buckled his seatbelt. “He does that.”

Rex gave them a thumbs-up with his tiny arms. Then he turned and sauntered into an alley, tail swinging like a metronome set to “don't give a damn.”

The van swerved onto I-95. Lola gripped the dashboard. “Pull over.”

Dutch laughed. “Not a chance.”

“I said pull—”

Jesus touched her shoulder. “Detective Vega.”

She recoiled. “Don't.”

His hand was warm, dry, nothing like the stained-glass saints of her childhood. “You've seen too much to walk away.”

The kid nodded. “Also, they'll definitely kill you now.”

Lola's phone buzzed. Morales. She let it go to voicemail.

The van merged into traffic, its wizard dolphin peeling under the Miami sun. Somewhere behind them, a dumpster fire raged.

She holstered her Glock. “One condition.”

Dutch glanced at her. “Yeah?”

“You buy me a new pair of shoes.”

Jesus smiled. “That seems fair.”

The kid popped the glove box. Inside, a single crushed pack of miracle weed glimmered gold.

Lola closed her eyes. Just another Miami Tuesday.

The van fishtailed onto a side street, its rear bumper dragging sparks. Lola braced against the dashboard as Dutch took a hard right without slowing. The kid in the backseat yelped and slid into Jesus, who caught him with the serene patience of a man used to handling falling objects, loaves, fishes, wayward teenagers.

“Where the hell are we going?” Lola shouted over the engine's roar.

“Someplace with less bullets!” Dutch swerved around a pothole the size of a kiddie pool.

A black SUV rounded the corner behind them, its tinted windows bristling with gun barrels. Lola counted three muzzles before the first volley punched through the van's rear doors. The kid screamed. Jesus sighed.

“Down!” Lola shoved Dutch's head below the dash as glass exploded inward. The rearview mirror disintegrated, showering them with shards.

Dutch white-knuckled the wheel. “Okay, new plan—”

The windshield spiderwebbed. A second SUV pulled alongside, its passenger leaning out with a sawed-off. Lola drew her Glock and fired through her own window. The shooter jerked back, his shotgun discharging skyward. A streetlight exploded in a shower of sparks.

Jesus picked glass from his beard. “Perhaps a less linear path?”

Dutch groaned. “Oh, now you're a tactician?” But he cranked the wheel hard left, sending the van careening into an alley barely wider than the vehicle itself. Metal screeched against brick. The wizard dolphin lost its smile, and most of its snout.

The lead SUV tried to follow. It didn't fit. The sound of crumpling quarter panels was almost musical.

Lola ejected her spent mag. “How many of these guys are there?”

The kid peeked over the seat. “All of them?”

A third SUV appeared ahead, blocking the alley's exit. Its doors flew open. Four men in matching linen suits stepped out, their sunglasses reflecting the van's headlights like insect eyes.

Dutch slammed the brakes. The van skidded to a stop inches from the newcomers. The one in front—a mountain of a man with a neck thicker than Lola's thigh—adjusted his cuffs and smiled.

“Fridge Guy,” Dutch whispered.

Lola blinked. “What?”

“La Reina's personal meat freezer. They say he once crushed a DEA informant with his—”

Fridge Guy drew a Desert Eagle the size of a small dog.

“—with his hands,” Dutch finished.

Lola's mind raced. No backup. No exit. Three against... she did a quick count... twelve, minimum. The math was bleak.

Jesus unbuckled his seatbelt. “I'll talk to them.”

Dutch grabbed his sleeve. “Dude, they literally just shot at us.”

“And yet we persist.” Jesus opened the door.

Lola lunged across Dutch to stop him, but the kid got there first. “Don't! They're not here for the weed. They're here for you.”

A beat.

Jesus frowned. “Why?”

Fridge Guy racked the Desert Eagle's slide. The sound echoed like a coffin slamming shut.

The kid hesitated. “Because La Reina thinks you're the new courier. The one who stole her product.”

Lola's stomach dropped. “The miracle weed.”

Dutch groaned. “Oh, come on! That was one time!”

Outside, Fridge Guy's patience expired. He raised the pistol.

Jesus stepped out of the van.

Lola's training screamed at her to take cover. Curiosity won. She watched as Jesus walked toward the cartel enforcers, his stolen Publix apron flapping in the humid breeze.

Fridge Guy hesitated. His men exchanged glances.

Jesus stopped an arm's length away. “You seem upset.”

The Desert Eagle wavered.

Lola held her breath. This was either the dumbest or bravest thing she'd ever seen. Maybe both.

Fridge Guy recovered first. He pressed the barrel to Jesus's forehead. “Where's the package?”

Jesus didn't flinch. “Which one?”

“The one you stole from La Reina's men at the docks!”

“Oh.” Jesus nodded. “That would be the frankincense.”

Silence.

Dutch facepalmed. The kid whimpered. Lola's finger hovered over her trigger.

Fridge Guy blinked. “The what?”

“Frankincense. And myrrh.”

The Desert Eagle fired.

Lola's heart stopped.

Jesus staggered back, not from the bullet, but from the shell casing that bounced off his nose. The round itself was nowhere to be seen. Fridge Guy stared at his weapon, then at Jesus's unmarked forehead.

The kid gasped. “He missed?”

Lola knew better. At that range, you didn't miss. You couldn't.

Fridge Guy knew it too. His face darkened. “Putas.” He raised the gun again.

Lola made her decision. She kicked open her door and fired three shots into the nearest SUV's grille. The radiator exploded in a geyser of steam and antifreeze.

“Go!” she yelled.

Dutch didn't need telling. The van lurched forward, plowing through the cloud of steam. Fridge Guy dove aside, bellowing in rage. His men scattered like bowling pins.

Lola grabbed Jesus's arm and yanked him into the moving van. He came willingly, his expression almost apologetic.

The kid hyperventilated in the back. “They shot him!”

“Not now!” Dutch swerved onto a main road, nearly clipping a tour bus painted like a flamingo.

Lola checked the mirror. The SUVs were regrouping. “They'll have roadblocks.”

Dutch nodded. “Everglades?”

“Everglades.”

The kid whimpered. “There's gators out there.”

Jesus patted his knee. “And mosquitoes. And snakes.”

A bullet took out the remaining taillight.

“—and slightly worse problems,” Jesus finished.

The van accelerated, its engine howling in protest. Behind them, the cartel convoy closed ranks. Ahead, the city limits loomed, and beyond them, the wild, untamed sprawl of the Glades.

Lola reloaded. “Dutch, tell me you have a plan.”

He grinned. “I always have a plan.”

The kid groaned. “That's a lie. He never has a plan.”

Dutch ignored him. “Step one: lose these guys in the swamp. Step two: find Rex. Step three...” He trailed off.

Lola waited. “Step three?”

“Profit?”

Jesus sighed. “I believe the term is ‘divine intervention.’”

Another volley slammed into the van. The rear doors flew open, revealing their pursuers and the growing distance between them. Dutch whooped.

Then the engine coughed.

Lola's stomach sank. “Tell me that's not what I think it is.”

Dutch patted the dashboard. “Come on, baby, just a little farther—”

The van sputtered. Died.

Silence, except for the whine of approaching engines.

The kid buried his face in his hands. “We're gonna die.”

Lola ejected her mag, counted rounds, slammed it home. Seven left. Not enough.

Jesus studied the approaching headlights. “There's another way.”

Dutch groaned. “If you say ‘faith,’ I swear to—”

A metallic screech cut him off.

From the mangroves to their left, something moved. Something big. Something that made the cartel's SUVs skid to a sudden, uncertain stop.

Lola's pulse pounded in her ears. She knew that sound.

Rex stepped onto the road, his optics glowing in the twilight. His tail swept a slow, deliberate arc across the asphalt. When he opened his jaws, the sound that emerged wasn't a roar. It was something between a challenge and a greeting, low and resonant and vibrating deep in Lola's bones.

Fridge Guy's men froze.

Dutch exhaled. “Oh thank God.”

Jesus smiled. “You're welcome.”

Lola tightened her grip on the Glock. Outnumbered. Outgunned. But not, it seemed, outmatched.

The lead SUV's engine revved. Rex's head swiveled toward it. His eye lenses narrowed.

Lola made a choice.

She stepped out of the van.

And raised her badge.
