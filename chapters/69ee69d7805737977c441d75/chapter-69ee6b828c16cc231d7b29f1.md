The van hit a pothole hard enough to rattle Dutch’s teeth. The shotgun slid across the floor. Rex’s talons flexed, punching fresh holes in the rusted metal. Jesus—because that’s who the database said he was, and Dutch had seen enough X-Files episodes to know you didn’t argue with a government database—braced himself against a stack of VHS tapes labeled PROOF: BIGFOOT IS REAL (PLEASE RETURN).

Dutch white-knuckled the wheel. “Okay. New rule: nobody pukes in the van. I just had the seats steam-cleaned.”

Jesus blinked at the duct-taped upholstery. “You have an interesting definition of ‘steam-cleaned.’”

“Shut up, messiah.” Dutch swerved onto the Tamiami Trail. The Everglades loomed on either side, black water glinting under the headlights. A gator watched them pass, eyes glowing like cigarette coals.

Rex’s vocalizer crackled. “Query: destination.”

Dutch’s grip tightened. “First, we ditch the heat. Second, we figure out why the Pentagon turned a T-Rex into a philosopher.” He shot a glance at Jesus. “Third, we get you some damn shoes.”

Jesus wiggled his toes. “I had sandals.”

“Yeah, well, now you’ve got tetanus.” Dutch jerked the wheel to avoid a possum. The van fishtailed. Rex’s tail slammed into the side panel, leaving a dent shaped like Australia.

Jesus studied the dent. “You drive like my cousin.”

“Which one?”

“The one who stole chariots.”

Dutch groaned. “Great. A messiah with jokes.”

Headlights flared in the rearview. Not cops—too dark, no light bar. Military. Dutch stomped the gas. The van shuddered but obeyed.

Rex’s optics tracked the pursuing vehicle. “Threat assessment: probable.”

“No shit, Sherlock.” Dutch cranked the wheel, skidding onto a dirt road. Mangroves slapped the windshield. Jesus grabbed a roll of duct tape from the dashboard and held it out.

“For the messiah’s wounds?”

“For the damn mirror.”

Jesus peeled off a strip and slapped it over the dangling side mirror. The duct tape held. The van did not.

The engine coughed. Smoke billowed from the hood. Dutch smacked the dashboard.

The van died with a whimper. Silence, except for the distant hum of the pursuing vehicle.

Rex’s head swiveled. “Optimal strategy: ambush.”

Dutch yanked the shotgun from the floor. “Optimal strategy: shut up.” He turned to Jesus. “Can you, like, miracle us out of this?”

Jesus frowned. “Technically, yes.”

“Save the sermon.” Dutch racked the shotgun. “We’re about to have company.”

The headlights grew brighter. The vehicle—a black SUV with no plates—rolled to a stop twenty yards back. Doors opened. Shadows moved.

Dutch exhaled. “Okay. New plan: you two stay here. I’ll do the talking.”

Jesus raised an eyebrow. “With the shotgun?”

“It’s persuasive.”

Rex’s claws flexed. “Alternative: I eat them.”

Dutch hesitated. “Tempting. But let’s try words first.” He stepped out, shotgun leveled. “Evening, fellas. Lost?”

The figures didn’t answer. One raised a hand—something glinted. Dutch ducked. The tranquilizer dart whizzed past his ear and embedded itself in the van’s door.

“Okay,” Dutch muttered. “Plan B.”

Rex burst through the van’s roof like a scaly Kool-Aid Man. The SUV’s windshield shattered under his tail. Men shouted. Something heavy hit the dirt.

Jesus sighed. “I really wish violence wasn’t always the default.”

Dutch peered around the van. Rex stood over three unconscious men in tactical gear. A fourth was pinned under the SUV’s bumper.

Rex’s optics flickered. “Query: consume?”

“No.” Dutch grabbed a radio from one of the men. Static hissed. A voice crackled: “Team Two, report.”

Jesus picked up a dart. “They’re not here to kill us.”

Dutch snorted. “Yeah, just kidnap us. Much better.” He tossed the radio into the swamp. “We need wheels.”

The pinned man groaned. Rex leaned down. “Why?”

The man’s eyes rolled back. He fainted.

Jesus folded his arms. “You’re overusing that word.”

Rex’s head tilted. “Why?”

Dutch rubbed his temples. “I need aspirin.” He rifled through the SUV, coming up with a key fob, a wallet, and a half-eaten Slim Jim. He tossed the Slim Jim to Rex, who swallowed it whole.

Jesus frowned. “You shouldn’t eat processed meat.”

Rex’s vocalizer emitted a sound like a dial-up modem. “Tasty.”

Dutch climbed into the SUV. “Get in. We’re borrowing this.”

Jesus hesitated. “Stealing is—”

“—better than dying.” Dutch revved the engine. “Priorities, messiah.”

Rex clambered into the back, crushing the seats flat. Jesus slid into the passenger side, eyeing the bloodstain on the dashboard.

Dutch hit the gas. The SUV lurched forward. Behind them, the van sat smoking, duct tape flapping in the breeze.

Jesus glanced at Dutch. “Where are we going?”

“Somewhere with fewer government hit squads.”

“That narrows it down.”

Rex’s optics dimmed. “Query: purpose.”

Dutch sighed. “Buddy, you’re asking the wrong guy.”

Jesus leaned back, watching the stars through the cracked windshield. “I think that’s the first honest thing you’ve said.”

The road stretched ahead, dark and endless. Somewhere, an owl hooted. The SUV’s engine purred.

Dutch tightened his grip on the wheel. “All right, messiah. Start talking. What do you remember?”

Jesus closed his eyes. “Light. Pain. Then...” He trailed off. “You.”

Dutch groaned. “Fantastic.”

Nobody answered.

The SUV sped on.

Dutch pulled the SUV behind a 7-Eleven that looked like it had lost a fight with a hurricane. The neon sign flickered, buzzing like an angry wasp. One of the letters was gone, so it read “7-LEV N.” Jesus stared at it like it held the secrets of the universe.

“All right,” Dutch said, killing the engine. “We need ground rules. One: no more existential questions from the dinosaur. Two: no turning water into wine in the backseat. Three: if you’re gonna pull any messiah crap, give me a heads-up so I can monetize it properly.”

Jesus touched the dashboard, tracing a crack in the plastic. “Your world is... loud.”

“Yeah, well, welcome to the future. It’s got neon and cholesterol.” Dutch dug through the glove compartment, coming up with a half-smoked joint and a lighter. He lit it, took a drag, and exhaled toward the cracked sunroof. “Now start talking. What’s the last thing you remember before waking up in Dino Disneyland?”

Jesus frowned. “The sky. Then... nothing. Then you.”

“Helpful.” Dutch passed him the joint. “Try again.”

Jesus took it gingerly, holding it between thumb and forefinger like a dead insect. He inhaled, coughed, and blinked rapidly. “This is... different.”

“Yeah, that’s the THC talking. Focus.”

Rex’s head appeared between the seats, nostrils flaring. “Query: purpose of combustion ritual?”

Dutch snatched the joint back. “It’s not a ritual, it’s stress management.” He took another drag. “Look, messiah, I need answers. The feds are after us, we’re driving a stolen SUV with a bloodstain that looks like Florida, and I’ve got a cybernetic T-Rex in the backseat asking why the sky’s blue. So either start making sense, or start making miracles.”

Jesus studied his hands. “I don’t... remember how.”

Dutch groaned. “Fantastic. A messiah with amnesia.”

A truck rumbled past on the highway, shaking the SUV. The 7-Eleven’s door chimed as a guy in flip-flops walked out, clutching a Big Gulp and a lottery ticket. He glanced at the SUV, did a double take at the massive dent in the roof, and hurried to his pickup.

Rex’s optics tracked him. “Threat assessment: minimal.”

“Yeah, no kidding.” Dutch rubbed his temples. “Okay. Let’s try this another way. What do you know?”

Jesus tilted his head. “The earth is the Lord’s. The meek shall inherit it. Love thy neighbor.”

“Great. Useless, but great.” Dutch flicked ash out the window. “You know what a microwave is?”

“No.”

“Television?”

“Credit score?”

Jesus blinked.

Dutch sighed. “You’re gonna get us all killed.”

A drop of water hit the windshield. Then another. Within seconds, rain sheeted down, turning the parking lot into a shallow lake. The 7-Eleven’s awning flapped like a wounded bird.

Jesus watched the rain, transfixed. “It’s beautiful.”

“Yeah, until your sandals grow mold.” Dutch slumped in his seat. “We need a plan. We can’t stay here. We can’t go back.”

Rex’s head snapped up. “Alert: approaching vehicle.”

Dutch twisted in his seat. A black sedan rolled into the lot, creeping toward them like a shark. No plates. Tinted windows.

“Son of a—” Dutch reached for the ignition. The engine sputtered. Died. “Oh, come on!”

The sedan stopped ten feet away. The passenger window rolled down. A hand emerged, holding a sleek black handgun.

Dutch’s stomach dropped. “Okay. New plan: everybody out. Run different directions.”

Jesus opened his door.

“Wait, you idiot—!”

But Jesus was already walking toward the sedan, bare feet slapping through puddles. The gun tracked him. Dutch cursed, fumbling for the shotgun under his seat.

Then Jesus raised his hand. Not in surrender. Like he was greeting an old friend.

The rain stopped.

Not gradually. Instantly. Like someone hit pause. Water droplets hung in midair, glittering under the rising sun. The sedan’s windshield wipers froze mid-swipe.

Dutch’s jaw dropped. “What the hell?”

Jesus touched one of the floating droplets. It shivered, then transformed, not into wine, but into something that looked suspiciously like high-grade cannabis oil. The scent hit Dutch like a freight train: earthy, sweet, with a kick that made his eyes water.

The gun wavered. The sedan’s door opened. A man in a black suit stepped out, gun still raised, but his expression was pure confusion.

Jesus smiled. “Peace be with you.”

The man’s knees buckled. He collapsed into a puddle, snoring.

Dutch stared. “Did you just... drug him with holy rain?”

Jesus examined his palm. “I think it’s more of a... spiritual vaporizer.”

Rex’s vocalizer emitted a sound like a malfunctioning kazoo. “Query: theological implications?”

Dutch grabbed Jesus by the arm, hauling him back to the SUV. “Forget theology. We need to go. Now.”

The engine roared to life on the first try. Dutch didn’t question it. He peeled out of the lot, the sedan and its unconscious occupant shrinking in the rearview.

Jesus settled into his seat, studying his hands like they belonged to someone else. “That was... unexpected.”

Dutch white-knuckled the wheel. “You think?”

Rex’s head tilted. “Conclusion: messiah functions as mobile dispensary.”

Dutch took a deep breath. The SUV smelled like a Grateful Dead concert. His head swam. His fingers tingled. For the first time since this nightmare began, he felt something dangerously close to hope.

He glanced at Jesus. “Can you do that again?”

Jesus frowned. “I’m not sure.”

Dutch grinned. “Well, messiah, you just became the most valuable hitchhiker in Florida.”

Dutch took a left toward the interstate. “Somewhere we can monetize this.”

Jesus sighed. “That’s not how miracles work.”

“Welcome to America,” Dutch said. “Everything’s for sale.”

The SUV merged onto the highway, trailing the faint, blessed scent of divine intervention.

Dutch kept one eye on the rearview and the other on Jesus, who was sniffing his own fingertips like a confused sommelier. The SUV reeked of holy hydroponics. Rex’s nostrils flared in the backseat, his optics cycling through what Dutch could only assume were dinosaur emotions.

“So,” Dutch said, rolling down the window to clear the haze, “let me get this straight. You can turn water into... what, exactly?”

Jesus examined a droplet on his wrist. “It’s not wine.”

“No kidding. Wine doesn’t make you want to hug strangers and listen to Phish.” Dutch tapped the wheel. “How much can you make?”

Rex’s head tilted. “Query: intent?”

“Business opportunity, scaly. Keep up.” Dutch glanced at Jesus. “Well?”

Jesus frowned. “It’s not a faucet.”

“Could’ve fooled me.” Dutch swerved around a slow-moving Cadillac. The driver, an old man in a Panama hat, gave them the finger. Dutch returned it with enthusiasm. “All right, messiah. Here’s the deal. We get to Miami, we find my guy, we test the market. You handle production. I handle distribution. Rex here handles... intimidation, I guess.”

Rex’s claws flexed. “Clarification: I do not ‘handle’ anything. I choose.”

Dutch waved a hand. “Sure, sure. Point is, we’re sitting on a goldmine. Holy weed, man. Do you have any idea what rich hippies will pay for this?”

Jesus rubbed his temples. “This feels... wrong.”

“Yeah, well, so does getting shot at by government goons, but here we are.” Dutch spotted a roadblock ahead, two cop cars parked diagonally across the highway, lights flashing. His stomach dropped. “Shit. Play it cool.”

Jesus straightened. “We should tell them the truth.”

Dutch choked. “The truth? ‘Hey, officer, this is Jesus and his pet cyborg dinosaur, we’re fleeing a black-ops lab’? That truth?”

Rex’s vocalizer crackled. “Alternative: I eat them.”

“No. No eating cops.” Dutch slowed the SUV, scanning for an exit. None. Just swamp on either side. The cop ahead waved him forward.

Jesus rolled down his window. Dutch grabbed his arm. “What are you doing?”

“Being honest.”

“Christ almighty—”

The cop leaned in, flashlight sweeping the interior. His gaze landed on Rex. Paused. Blinked.

Dutch forced a grin. “Service animal. PTSD.”

The cop’s mouth opened. Closed. He took a step back, sniffed the air, and his eyes widened. “Son, you got any drugs in this vehicle?”

Jesus smiled. “Not yet.”

The cop’s radio crackled. “Unit 12, we got a 10-78 at mile marker 53. Possible armed suspect.”

Dutch held his breath. The cop stared at Rex. Rex stared back.

Finally, the cop shook his head. “Move along.”

Dutch didn’t need telling twice. He hit the gas, heart hammering. As they passed the roadblock, Jesus waved.

Rex’s optics dimmed. “Analysis: law enforcement is inefficient.”

Dutch wiped sweat from his brow. “You don’t say.”

Jesus folded his hands. “That was close.”

“Yeah, and whose fault was that?” Dutch shot him a look. “No more honesty. From now on, we lie like rugs.”

The highway stretched ahead, Miami’s skyline a jagged silhouette in the distance. The sun climbed higher, baking the asphalt. Dutch’s shirt stuck to his back.

Rex shifted in the back, crushing a duffel bag. “Query: destination specifics.”

“Little Havana. My guy Pepe runs a bodega. Front for the best black-market pharmacy this side of Havana.” Dutch adjusted the mirror. “We get there, we lay low, we figure out our next move.”

Jesus frowned. “You have many criminal associates?”

Dutch snorted. “In Miami? Everyone’s a criminal. Difference is, some of us admit it.”

A sign flashed by: MIAMI CITY LIMITS. The air thickened with humidity and exhaust. Billboards advertised lawyers, strip clubs, and all-you-can-eat buffets. Jesus watched them like they were alien artifacts.

Rex’s head swiveled toward a passing ice cream truck. “Query: purpose of audible propaganda vehicle?”

Dutch sighed. “It’s not propaganda, it’s—you know what, never mind.”

He took a left onto Calle Ocho, the heart of Little Havana. The streets pulsed with salsa music, the scent of cafecito thick in the air. Old men played dominoes in the park. A rooster strutted across the sidewalk like it owned the place.

Dutch parallel-parked the SUV half a block from Pepe’s Bodega. He killed the engine, then turned to Jesus. “All right. Ground rules. You let me do the talking. You don’t do any... messiah stuff.”

Rex’s optics gleamed. “I do not take orders.”

“Fine. Just don’t eat anyone.”

Jesus hesitated. “This feels like a mistake.”

Dutch opened his door. “Welcome to my life.”

The bodega’s bell jingled as they entered. Pepe—a wiry man with a salt-and-pepper mustache—looked up from his racing form. His gaze flicked from Dutch to Jesus to Rex.

He put down the paper. “No.”

Dutch spread his hands. “Hear me out—”

“No.” Pepe pointed at Rex. “That is a dinosaur.”

Rex’s head tilted. “Affirmative.”

Pepe crossed himself. “Dios mío.”

He grabbed a baseball bat from under the counter. “Out.”

Dutch sighed. “Pepe, come on. It’s me.”

“You bring a dinosaur and a homeless man into my store, and you say, ‘it’s me’?” Pepe shook the bat. “I have a family!”

Jesus raised his hands. “We mean no harm.”

Pepe’s grip tightened. “The last time Dutch said that, I had to explain to my wife why there was a flamingo in our bathtub.”

Dutch winced. “That was one time.”

Rex’s claws clicked on the linoleum. “Query: is violence imminent?”

Pepe paled. The bat trembled.

Jesus sighed. He reached into the cooler, pulled out a bottle of water, and uncapped it. The liquid shimmered. The scent of Burning Bush OG filled the bodega.

Pepe froze. Sniffed. Lowered the bat. “What is that?”

Dutch grinned. “Opportunity.”

Pepe took a cautious step forward. “Is that...?”

“The good stuff,” Dutch said. “The best. And we’ve got the messiah here making it.”

Pepe stared at Jesus. “You’re joking.”

Jesus shrugged. “Apparently not.”

Pepe rubbed his temples. “I need a drink.”

Dutch clapped him on the back. “First one’s free.”

The bell jingled again. All four turned.

A woman stood in the doorway, hand on her hip, badge glinting in the fluorescent light. Detective Lola Vega.

She took in the scene—Dutch, Jesus, Rex, Pepe, the open bottle of holy weed—and sighed. “Donovan. Of course.”

Dutch’s smile faded. “Lola. Fancy meeting you here.”

She stepped inside, shutting the door behind her. The lock clicked.

“Everyone,” she said, “put your hands where I can see them.”

Rex’s optics brightened. “Query: eat?”

Dutch closed his eyes. “No, Rex. We do not eat the detective.”

Lola’s gaze landed on him. Paused. “Oh, you’ve got to be kidding me.”

Dutch breathed out. “Lola, meet Jesus. Jesus, meet the woman who’s probably gonna arrest us.”

Lola holstered her gun and pinched the bridge of her nose. “I need a raise.”

Outside, a patrol car rolled to a stop, lights flashing.

Dutch glanced at Jesus. “Got any more miracles up your sleeve?”

Jesus frowned. “I’m fresh out.”

Pepe grabbed the baseball bat again. “Dios nos ayude.”

Lola pulled out her cuffs. “All right, boys. Let’s take this downtown.”

Rex’s tail twitched. “Clarification: I am not a ‘boy.’”

Dutch groaned. “This day just keeps getting better.”

The cuffs clicked. The patrol car waited. Somewhere in the distance, a rooster crowed, as if laughing at them all.
