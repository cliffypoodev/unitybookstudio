The baker’s oven had cooled for the night, leaving the air in the alley behind the shop thick with the ghost of charred wheat and honey. Leah pressed her cheek against the rough plaster wall, the grit catching in the fine hairs of her temple. Her father stood three paces ahead, a silhouette cut from the deep blue of dusk. He didn’t look back. He never did on these nights. He simply waited, his hand resting on the wooden hatch set into the stone foundation, until the last clatter of a cart faded from the Vicus Judaeorum.

He lifted the hatch. A breath of air, cool and tasting of wet clay and something older, rose to meet the alley’s warmth. It was not a bad smell. It was the smell of under-the-world. Leah liked it. She’d once tried to describe it to her brother, who was eight and thought himself wise. He’d said it was just dirt. But it was not. Dirt was sun-baked and simple. This smell had layers, like the onions their mother peeled, each skin thinner and sharper than the last.

“Leah.” Her father’s voice was low, a stone dropped into a deep well. A command, not a name.

She pushed off the wall, a flake of whitewash sticking to her cheek. She didn’t brush it away. Her sandals whispered over the worn stones. He descended first, his shoulders blocking the faint, guttering light of the oil lamp he carried. She followed, her own smaller lamp clutched in a hand that was just beginning to forget its baby-roundness. The wooden rungs of the ladder were smooth, worn into shallow dips by many hands.

Her world became a circle of wavering light and the sound of her own breathing, too loud in the close space. Then her feet found packed earth. The ceiling was just above her father’s head. The walls were not walls, but a pressing absence beyond the lamp’s reach. The tunnel sloped downward, a gentle insistence.

They walked. The only sounds were the scuff of leather on earth and the liquid sigh of the lamp flames. Her father was a silent column ahead, his shadow a giant that lunged and retreated on the wall. Leah watched her own shadow, a smaller, jerking puppet. She made her hand into a bird, its beak opening and closing against the ochre stone. Her father didn’t see. He was listening to the silence.

A branch in the tunnel. Her father turned left without hesitation. The right-hand passage smelled different—a hint of rot, sweet and cloying. She glanced back at it, a dark mouth yawning, until the turn took it from view. Her lamp guttered, painting a long, dancing crack in the wall that looked exactly like the crooked staff old Amos used to walk with. She named it Amos-crack in her mind and promised to look for it next time.

They were not alone down here. She knew that. Sometimes a scuffle echoed from a side passage, a cough smothered into fabric. Once, a child had cried, a thin, tired sound that was cut off abruptly. Her father’s back had stiffened, but he had not stopped. Leah had dreamed of that cry for a week, mixing it with the mewling of the cats that fought in the alley.

The tunnel opened into a chamber. Her father stopped at the edge of the lamplight. Leah peered around him.

This room was a hollowed-out belly in the earth. Niches were carved into the walls, long rectangular shelves stacked three high. Some were empty, dark mouths. Others were sealed with slabs of marble or simple brick, etched with strange, beautiful letters she could not read. This was a place of bones. She knew that, too. But the knowledge was distant, academic. The awe was immediate. The ceiling was higher here, lost in a dome of shadow. Their lamplight could not touch it. It was like standing under a night sky with no stars.

Men stood in small clusters. Ten, maybe twelve. Their voices were a low murmur, a sound like bees in a jar. They were all shapes—a burly man with forearms thick as Leah’s thighs, a stooped elder whose beard was a cloud of white, a younger man who tapped his fingers against his leg in a restless, silent rhythm. They fell quiet as Leah’s father entered. Nods were exchanged. No smiles.

Her father moved to a central space where the earth was worn smooth. He set his lamp on a flat stone that seemed placed there for the purpose. Leah hovered near the tunnel entrance, a spectator. She found a spot where the wall curved and sat, drawing her knees to her chest. The earth here was cool through her thin tunic. A fragment of pottery, an edge of blue glaze, was half-embedded in the floor near her foot. She picked at it with her thumbnail.

The prayer began. It was not the soaring, communal recitation of the synagogue. This was different. A threadbare fabric of whispers, woven by men who kept their eyes on the ground or the dark niches. The words were familiar—blessings, the Shema—but the tone was all wrong. Hungry. Desperate. It sounded like men holding onto a rope in the dark, telling each other it was still there.

Leah stopped picking at the pottery. She listened to the spaces between the words. A fear was there. A new flavor in the under-the-world air, metallic and sour.

The prayers ended. The murmuring resumed, sharper now.

“—three families from the Trans-Tiber. Questioned by the frumentarii.”

“On what pretext?”

“None, they would say. A ‘survey of loyalties.’ They took young Marcus bar Jacob. He hasn’t returned.”

A beat of silence. Leah saw the stooped elder close his eyes, his lips moving soundlessly.

Her father’s voice, steady and low, cut through. “We cannot meet in the house of Priscus again. It is watched.”

“Where, then?” the burly man asked, his voice gravelly. “The catacombs cannot hold us all, not for a proper gathering. The air grows bad. The children cough.”

“There is a cellar near the Porta Capena. A wool merchant. He is sympathetic.”

“Sympathetic costs,” the young man with the restless fingers said. His taps increased their tempo. “He will want more than prayers. He will want a percentage. Or favor.”

“We have no favors left to grant,” her father said. The finality in his voice made Leah hug her knees tighter. She focused on the blue pottery shard, the exact color of a jay’s feather she’d found once in the market. She wondered where the rest of the bowl was, whether it was broken or whole somewhere in the dark.

The talk moved to money, to safe passage for a teacher from the east, to the decoding of a letter. Leah’s mind drifted. She traced the Amos-crack on the far wall with her eyes. She listened to the drip of water somewhere deep in the earth, a slow, patient clock.

Then a new voice, thin and reedy, from the elder: “The letter from Philippi... it confirms the growth of the Way there. Even among Greeks. It is... unsettling.”

Her father shifted his weight. The lamplight caught the hard line of his jaw. “It is the path. One door is opened. Peter himself has seen it.”

“And where is Peter?” the burly man grumbled. “Hiding in Antioch? While we here breathe Roman air and count Roman spies.”

“We keep the door open here,” her father said, his tone leaving no room. “That is our task. In silence. In secret. We are the roots, not the branches.”

The words meant little to Leah. Doors. Roots. But the next phrase, spoken by the restless man, dropped into the chamber like a stone.

“Roman eyes are on the roots now. They have a list.”

A cold stillness settled over the men. Not a movement, not a rustle of cloth. Leah held her breath without meaning to. The jay-blue pottery shard blurred before her eyes.

Her father turned his head, just slightly, and his gaze found her in the shadows by the wall. His eyes, in the lamplight, were not the eyes that helped her with her sums or brushed flour from her cheek. They were the eyes of a man measuring a distance across a chasm. They held her for a second that stretched too long. Then he looked back to the circle of men, his voice dropping even lower, so that Leah had to strain to catch the edges of the words.

“Then the list must be lost,” he said.

The burly man let out a short, sharp breath that was not a laugh. “Lost. And how do we lose a Roman list? Ask nicely?”

“The scribe who copies the watch-reports for the Cohortes Urbanae,” her father said, the words deliberate. “He has a daughter. A rash on her neck that will not heal. The physicians ask too much. His pay is... insufficient.”

Leah blinked. This man was talking about a sick girl. About money. It had nothing to do with lists or roots. But the men around him leaned in, their faces grim with understanding.

“A risk,” the elder whispered.

“Everything is a risk,” her father said. “Even sitting here tonight.”

The discussion coiled inward, becoming specific, hushed. Names Leah did not know. Amounts of denarii. A planned meeting at a bathhouse. The fear in the air tightened, became a wire drawn taut.

Leah looked down at her own hands. Small. Clean. She thought of the sick girl with the rash, a girl she would never meet. She thought of her father’s eyes when he’d looked at her. That measuring distance. A shiver started at the base of her spine, a tiny, living thing. It had nothing to do with the cool air of the catacombs.

The blue pottery shard, she decided, was not a piece of a bowl. A piece of a sky buried long ago. A sky someone had broken and forgotten. She closed her fingers around it. The edge bit into her palm, a sharp, clean pain. She did not let go.

* * *

The meeting dissolved into a series of hushed, paired conversations. The men broke from their circle like beads of mercury, drifting into the darker edges of the burial chamber where the lamplight frayed into gloom. Leah watched her father speak with the stooped elder, their heads close together. The elder’s hands trembled as he gestured, a faint, constant tremor that made the shadows around his fingers quiver. Her father listened, his own hands still at his sides. He nodded once. A slow, heavy motion, like a gate closing.

Leah uncurled her legs. The pottery shard was a hard, familiar pressure in her fist. She stood, her joints stiff from the cool earth, and drifted away from the tunnel entrance. The adults were occupied. She was a ghost here, unseen.

She moved along the wall of niches, her small lamp held aloft. Light licked over the sealed fronts. Some were plain brick, the mortar crumbling like old cheese. Others were marble, gone cloudy with time, carved with letters that curled and hooked. She stopped before one. The inscription was clearer than the others, the Latin characters deep-cut and stark.

AVRELIA PRISCA
DVLCIS FEMINA
VIXIT ANNIS XXVIII

Twenty-eight years. Leah counted on her fingers. That was older than her mother. She tried to imagine a woman named Aurelia Prisca, but all she could conjure was a face like the marble—smooth, cold, featureless. Did she like honey cakes? Was she afraid of spiders? A stone gave no answers. A single, dried sprig of rosemary, brittle as a bird’s skeleton, lay in a shallow depression at the niche’s base. An offering forgotten by time. Leah touched it with her toe. It crumbled into dust without a sound.

A low murmur drew her attention. The restless man and the burly one stood near an archway that led into a further chamber. Their voices were a tense undercurrent.

“—not a question of faith. It’s a question of coin. The wool merchant’s ‘sympathy’ lasts only as long as the silver does.”

“And our sympathy should last longer?” the burly man countered, his voice a gravelly rasp. “My children eat once a day. You speak of percentages. I speak of empty bowls.”

“Then perhaps we pray harder. Perhaps the empty bowls are a test.”

The burly man made a sound in his throat, wet and dismissive. He turned and saw Leah standing there. His eyes, deep-set in a face weathered by a sun she rarely saw, fixed on her. There was no kindness in them, no annoyance either. Just an assessment, like a man checking the weight of a tool. He looked through her, then back to his companion. “We are discussing grown things, girl. Go.”

She did not move. Not out of defiance, but because her feet felt rooted to the spot. One man’s dismissal was a door slammed in a corridor she had not known she was walking down. Her face grew warm. She took a step back, her sandal scuffing the earth.

“Leave her.” Her father’s voice came from behind her, calm but final. He did not raise it. He did not need to. The burly man grunted, waved a thick hand, and turned his back.

Her father did not touch her. He stood beside her, looking at the niche of Aurelia Prisca. “The dead are good listeners,” he said, his voice dropping to a tone meant only for her, that voice he used when explaining why the moon changed shape. “They keep secrets forever.”

“What is their secret?” Leah said, low.

“That they were once afraid, too.” He placed a hand on the cold marble. A simple gesture, almost tender. “Come. We go a little further tonight. There is something to collect.”

He took his lamp and moved toward the arch. Leah followed, the exchange with the burly man already fading, replaced by the pull of a new, deeper dark. The archway led to a narrower tunnel, its ceiling so low her father had to duck his head. The air changed. The wet-clay smell sharpened, mixed with a dry, papery scent she could not name. The smell of very old things left alone.

The tunnel wound, a slow corkscrew into the earth. The walls here were not lined with niches, but rough-hewn, marked by the pickaxes that had bitten them out of the stone. The shadows thrown by their lamps were misshapen, leaping across the pitted surface. Leah saw a horse, a twisted tree, a man with three heads. She did not name them. She kept her eyes on the back of her father’s tunic, the familiar weave of the cloth.

They reached a small, circular room. No niches here. Just a single, low stone bench carved from the wall, and a recess in the floor covered by a flat slate. Her father set his lamp on the bench. He knelt before the slate, his fingers feeling around its edges. With a soft grunt of effort, he lifted it. A cavity yawned beneath, black and depthless.

From within, he withdrew a bundle wrapped in oiled leather. Long and narrow. He laid it on the floor and unfolded the leather with precise, reverent movements. Inside were scrolls. Three of them, their ends capped with plain wood, their parchment creamy and tight. Next to them lay a small pottery jar, sealed with wax.

“The letters from the brothers in the east,” he said, more to himself than to her. He touched one scroll, his fingertips brushing the surface as if checking for fever. “And the chrism. From James’s own store.”

Leah crouched opposite him, her own lamp casting a second pool of light that merged with his. “What is chrism?”

“Oil. For blessing. For marking.” He looked at her, his face all planes and hollows in the dual light. “It is a promise. A small piece of light kept in the dark.”

He rewrapped the scrolls with the same care, leaving the jar out. He pressed it into her hands. Warm from the leather, smooth and heavy. “Carry this. Do not drop it. If we are separated, hide. Wait for me. Guard this.”

The weight of the jar was immense. It was not the physical weight, which was only that of a cup of olives, but the weight of his words. If we are separated. The concept was a dark, cold draft in the little room. She nodded, her throat tight. She cradled the jar against her chest with one arm, her lamp in the other hand.

A sound trickled down the tunnel. Not a scuffle or a cough. A voice. Muffled, distorted by stone and distance, but unmistakably a voice calling out. A single word, repeated. A Latin word.

“Hodie!”

Today.

The effect was instantaneous. Her father froze, his head cocked like a hound. His eyes met hers. The measured distance was gone, replaced by a flash of pure, animal alert. He shoved the leather-wrapped bundle back into the cavity, dropped the slate over it with a gritty thud, and swept dirt over the edges with his palm. He snatched up his lamp.

“Quickly. No light.”

He pinched the wick of his lamp between his fingers. Darkness swallowed half the room. Leah stared at her own flame, a tiny, brave eye of orange. Her father’s hand covered hers, his calloused fingers guiding hers to the wick. The heat was a brief sting. Then nothing.

The blackness was absolute. A thing. It pressed against her eyes, filled her mouth, pooled in her ears. She could not see her own hand before her face. She could hear only her own heart, a frantic drum in the cage of her ribs, and her father’s chest rising shallow and controlled beside her.

From the main chamber, far behind them now, came a rustle of movement. A stifled curse. The scrape of sandals on stone, moving fast. Then a new sound—a different scrape, from above. From the world. The sound of the wooden hatch being dragged open. A heavy wooden groan that traveled down the tunnel like a poison.

Her father’s hand found her shoulder. His grip was iron. He pulled her, not back the way they came, but around the stone bench, into a fold of the wall she had not seen. A crack, not much wider than her body. He pushed her into it. The stone was cold and rough against her cheek. He pressed in behind her, his body shielding hers from the opening. The smell of his sweat, sharp and familiar, cut through the ancient odors of the crypt.

They waited. The darkness let the words out around them. Leah clutched the jar of chrism so tightly her fingers ached. She thought of the promise, the piece of light in the dark. She thought of the sick girl with the rash, and the Roman list, and the roots that were being watched. The cold from the stone seeped through her tunic and into her bones.

From the main chamber, the sounds were muffled, indistinct. A low voice, Latin, too smooth and rounded for the guttural Aramaic whispers of the meeting. A reply. Then a boot, heavy-soled, striking the earth floor. A sound of ownership. It did not shuffle or scuff; it planted.

The bootsteps moved, deliberate, exploring. They paused near the tunnel entrance. Leah heard the rasp of a hand brushing against the wall. Her mind painted the picture: a Roman soldier, his helmet crest brushing the low ceiling, peering into the black mouth that led to where they hid. Did he have a torch? Would its light reach around the winding corridor to their crack?

Her father’s chest between them had all but stopped. She mimicked him, holding the air in her lungs until they burned. The jar of chrism was a hard, smooth orb between her palm and her chest. A piece of light in the dark. She closed her eyes. It made no difference.

A grunt from the chamber. The Latin voice again, closer now, laced with a boredom that was more terrifying than anger. “Nihil. Rats and old bones.”

The bootsteps receded. The groan of the wooden hatch came again, a long, weary complaint. A sliver of gray, dusty light filtered down the main tunnel for a single, breathtaking moment—the world above, a memory. Then it was cut off. The thud of the hatch settling back into place was final.

Silence rushed back in, thicker than before.

They did not move. Minutes piled upon them in the dark. Leah’s legs began to cramp. The cold of the stone had seeped into her marrow. Her father waited, a statue of patience. He was listening for the trick, the pretended departure, listening for the sigh of a man who had remained behind, holding his own silence in the black.

Finally, a release. A long, slow exhalation warmed the top of her head. His grip on her shoulder loosened, became a pat. “Wait,” he said, the word barely a stir of air.

He slid out of the crack. She heard the soft strike of flint, the tiny, scratching fury of it. A spark died. Another. Then a small, blessed flower of flame bloomed on the wick of his lamp. Light, after the absolute void, was a physical shock. She blinked against the sting of it. The world reassembled itself in ochre and umber: the rough walls, the stone bench, the slate on the floor still smudged with his palm prints.

His face was grim in the low light. He did not look at her. His eyes were fixed on the tunnel, his head tilted, listening. He held up a finger.

From somewhere above, through layers of earth and stone, came the faint, rhythmic crunch of hobnailed sandals on paving stones. A patrol, moving away. The sound faded into the general night murmur of the city.

Only then did he turn. He looked at her, crouched in the fissure, the jar still clutched like a second heart. His expression softened, but it was a weary softness, the softness of a cloth worn thin. He knelt, bringing his eyes level with hers.

“You did well,” he said. His voice was hoarse.

She uncurled her fingers from the jar. Her hand was stiff. “Were they looking for us?”

“For anyone not sleeping in his bed. For whispers. For roots.” He took the jar, his fingers brushing hers. They were ice-cold.

He stood, offering his free hand to pull her from the crack. Her legs were numb, prickling with returning fire as she stumbled out. He kept hold of her hand for a moment, his thumb rubbing over her knuckles. An absent gesture, one he’d done a thousand times, but now it felt like a message in a code she was just learning.

“We go home a different way,” he said.

He did not relight her lamp. They moved single-file, his light the only beacon. The return journey through the catacombs was a ghostly retreat. The chambers were empty now, the echoes of the meeting and the Roman intrusion lingering in the stale air like different kinds of smoke. Leah saw the Amos-crack on the wall as they passed. It looked smaller. Just a crack.

They did not climb the ladder by the bakery. Her father led her through a branch she had not noticed, a tight, wet corridor that ended at a rough-hewn staircase. At the top was another hatch, this one hidden beneath a pile of discarded, rotten reed matting in the basement of a potter’s workshop. The potter, a man with clay ingrained in the wrinkles of his hands, nodded once to her father from his wheel, his face a blank mask. He said nothing.

Then they were in the street. The night air was a slap—cool, smelling of dung and woodsmoke and the distant Tiber. It felt impossibly vast after the tunnels. The moon was a shard of bone behind a haze of cloud. Rome was asleep, or pretending to be.

They walked not as father and daughter, but as two shadows. He took alleys so narrow she could touch both walls. He paused at every intersection, a silhouette melting into a doorway, listening. Once, he pulled her into the recess of a fountain as another patrol clattered past on the main street two lanes over. Leah pressed her face against the cold, damp stone of the fountain’s basin. A stray cat, sleek and silent, watched them from atop a wall with unblinking green eyes before vanishing.

This was her city. She knew its market squares in the sun, the route to the well, the best step to sit on and watch the street performers. She did not know this city. This city of whispers and cracks and measured distances. This city where her father’s eyes turned to flint.

They reached their own street, a narrow climb on the Aventine. Their door was a patch of deeper shadow. Her father produced the key, the iron scraping loudly in the quiet. Inside, the familiar smell of lentils, wool, and her mother’s lavender sachets wrapped around her. A single oil lamp glowed on the table, trimmed low. Her mother was asleep on a pallet by the hearth, a half-mended tunic in her lap. She stirred as the door shut, her eyes opening.

She looked at her husband’s face, then at Leah’s. She asked no questions. She simply rose, came to Leah, and placed a hand on her cheek. Her palm was warm and dry. She looked again at her husband, a silent conversation passing between them. Then she nodded, took the lamp, and went to the back room, leaving them in the near-dark.

Her father bolted the door. He placed the jar of chrism on the shelf beside the household gods, the small, carved figures of wood and stone. It sat among them, a strange, smooth foreigner. He stood there for a long moment, his back to her, his shoulders slumped under an invisible weight.

Leah stood in the middle of the room, unmoored. The fear was gone, drained away during the long, careful walk. What remained was a hollow, buzzing space. She looked at her father—really looked. Not at Abba, who told stories, but at the man who had hidden a bundle of scrolls and bargained for a scribe’s loyalty with medicine for a rash. The man who had measured her with his eyes in a tomb.

He turned. He saw her looking. For a flicker, his guard was down. She saw the exhaustion, the worry cut in lines she’d never noticed, the quiet dread that came from carrying a secret that could bury him. It was not the face of a hero from a story. It was the face of a man who was tired.

He held out his hand.

She went to him. She did not take his hand. She wrapped her arms around his waist and pressed her ear against his tunic. She could hear his heart, a slow, solid drum. He hesitated, then his arms came around her, heavy and real. He rested his chin on the top of her head.

No words passed. None were needed. The hug was not comfort; it was an acknowledgment. Him saying, you saw. And her saying, I am here.

In that silent exchange, the last of her childhood’s simple world dissolved. The catacombs were not an adventure. The meetings were not just prayers. The jar on the shelf was not just oil. It was a promise that required hiding in cracks in the earth. A piece of light that men in boots would stamp out.

She clutched the rough wool of his tunic. She was not afraid for herself. The new feeling was colder, clearer. It was the understanding of a cost. The Way, not as a story, but as a truth her father carried in his weary bones, a truth he had just silently passed to her. She felt its shape now. It was as heavy as the jar, and as fragile, and it fit perfectly in the hollow space the fear had left behind.
