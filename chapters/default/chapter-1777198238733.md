The rain came sideways today. It always did when the wind turned east, driving drops like pebbles against the shutter hinges until they whined like hungry dogs. Elara pressed her thumb into the wax seal of the letter—brown, not red, already cracked from the journey—and tried to remember her sister’s face. Five years. Judea might as well have been the moon.

The first line was a blade: I am forgiven.

Elara set the papyrus on the table, where the wood bowed from years of scrubbing. The ink blurred at the edges, smudged by sea spray or sweat. She traced the word forgiven with her littlest finger, the nail bitten to the quick. The grain of the paper snagged at her skin.

Her husband’s boots thudded on the step outside. She slid the letter beneath the wool carding comb before the door swung open.

“Still raining,” Gaius said, shaking water from his cloak. A droplet landed on her wrist. Cold. She wiped it against her thigh without looking up.

“The ditch by the north gate’s flooded again.” He unbuckled his belt, the leather sighing as it loosened.

“Mm.” She pushed a loose thread back into the seam of her sleeve.

He paused. The hearth-fire popped. Then he shrugged and reached for the wine jug.

She waited until his back was turned to retrieve the letter. The second line was worse: There is a living hope now, one that does not end with the grave.

Elara exhaled through her nose. Miriam had always been prone to fits of enthusiasm—chanting with the temple women, fasting until her lips cracked—but this was different. This was not poetry. It was a statement, flat as a Roman road.

A living hope.

She crumpled the edge of the papyrus between her fingers. Outside, the rain slowed to a drizzle. The garrison’s trumpets sounded the hour, their brass voices muffled by the mist.

Gaius drained his cup and reached for his cloak again. “Watch duty.” His thumb brushed her shoulder as he passed, a habit left over from their first year, when he still bothered to kiss her goodbye.

The door closed. The latch clicked.

Elara unfolded the letter fully.

They say he walked out of his own tomb. They say he ate fish with his friends afterward, that the holes in his hands still bled a little. I touched one of the women who touched him. Her fingers were warm.

The words wriggled in her gut like eels. She pressed a hand to her stomach. The fire spat.

Miriam had never lied. Not even as a child, not even to save her own skin.

Elara stood abruptly. The bench scraped the floor. She paced to the window, where the last raindrops clung to the shutters like translucent beetles. Beyond them, the garrison’s torches flickered in the dusk.

She imagined her sister’s hands, ink-stained and trembling, pressing these words into the page. The sister who had once held her hair back when she vomited from eating green plums. The sister who had not written in five years.

The letter ended with a plea: Come home.

Elara laughed. A short, sharp sound, like a dog’s bark. Home. As if Judea were still home. As if the dirt there remembered her feet.

She folded the letter along its creases, then unfolded it. Folded it again. The papyrus was softening at the edges from her sweat.

The fire burned low. She should stir it. She should card the wool.

A knock at the door.

Elara startled, the letter crinkling in her fist. No one visited at this hour.

“Who’s there?”

Silence. Then a child’s voice, high and thin: “My mother says you have yarrow.”

Elara tucked the letter into her girdle. The wax seal cracked further as she bent. “A moment.”

She opened the door to a girl of maybe eight, her tunic damp at the hem. One of the legion’s bastards, probably. They always needed something.

The girl held out a chipped cup. “For the fever.”

Elara took it. The clay was warm from the child’s hands. “Wait here.”

She turned to the shelf where she kept the herbs. Behind her, the girl shuffled her feet.

“My mother says you’re from the east,” the girl said suddenly.

Elara’s fingers hesitated over the yarrow. “Yes.”

“Is it true they bury their dead in caves there?”

The question hung in the air, innocent as a dropped spindle. Elara pinched the dried flowers too hard. A petal fluttered to the floor.

“Sometimes.” She tipped the yarrow into the cup. The scent rose between them, green and bitter.

The girl took the cup with both hands. “My mother says the dead stay dead.”

Elara’s thumb found the edge of the letter through her girdle. They say he walked out.

“Usually,” she said.

The girl nodded, satisfied, and scampered into the gloom.

Elara barred the door. The fire had dwindled to coals. She did not build it up again. Instead, she sat at the table and spread the letter flat, smoothing the creases with her palm.

Come home.

She touched the word living.

Outside, the wind turned. The rain began again.

The market square stank of fish and wet wool. Elara adjusted the basket on her hip, the weight of turnips and barley flour pressing into her side. Around her, the legion’s wives haggled with merchants in clipped Latin, their voices sharp as the knives at the butcher’s stall. A child darted past, trailing a string of giggles and the sour tang of goat’s milk.

She paused at the spice seller’s booth. The man—a Gaul with one milky eye—grinned as she approached. “Juniper berries fresh from the ships,” he said, shaking a pouch so the dried berries rattled like teeth. “Good for cleansing ill humors.”

Elara reached for the pouch. The scent of pine and earth rose from it, so unlike the flat, salted air of Britannia. For a breath, she was eight again, watching her mother crush juniper into oil for the lampstand. The memory slipped away before she could catch it.

“How much?”

The Gaul named a price. She countered. They settled. As she counted coins into his palm, a phrase from Miriam’s letter floated up unbidden: The holes in his hands still bled a little.

Her fingers twitched. A coin clattered to the ground.

The Gaul scooped it up, still grinning. “Roman silver always finds its way home.”

She tucked the juniper into her basket and moved on. The crowd thickened near the blacksmith’s, where two legionaries argued over a dagger’s balance. One of them—a thick-necked man with a scar across his chin—gestured wildly, his voice rising above the din. “—like holding a fucking brick!”

Elara edged past them. The letter burned against her skin where she’d sewn it into the hem of her tunic. Foolish. She should have burned it.

A hand caught her elbow.

“You’re late.” Tullia, the centurion’s wife, stood too close, her breath sour with yesterday’s wine. “The barley’s half-gone already.”

Elara stepped back. “I had to—”

“Save it.” Tullia’s gaze dropped to Elara’s basket. “Juniper? Planning a feast?”

“For the cough.”

Tullia sniffed. “Gaius never coughs.”

The unspoken unlike my husband hung between them. Elara tightened her grip on the basket. “Prevention.”

Tullia’s eyes—pale as lard—narrowed. “You eastern girls and your potions.” She leaned in. “They’re saying the Judean grain shipment’s delayed again. Some nonsense about riots.”

Elara’s pulse jumped. “Riots?”

“Rebels. Always rebels.” Tullia waved a hand, her rings glinting. “Something about a dead man walking. As if the empire hasn’t enough problems without ghost stories.”

The words struck like a slap. Elara’s mouth went dry.

Tullia didn’t notice. She was already turning away, calling to another wife. “Marcella! Did you hear about the Judean tax collector?”

Elara didn’t stay to listen. She walked, her sandals slapping the wet stones. The crowd blurred around her. A merchant shouted. A donkey brayed. The smells of roasting meat and dung and wet leather swirled together, thick as soup.

Dead man walking.

She passed the barracks, where recruits drilled in the mud. Their shouts rose in unison, a rhythm as familiar as her own breath. Gaius would be among them, his shield steady, his strikes precise. A good soldier. A Roman.

She stopped at the edge of the training yard. The recruits formed a testudo, their shields overlapping like scales. Gaius stood at the front, his jaw set. The centurion barked an order. The formation shifted, seamless as water.

Order. Discipline. Strength.

Miriam’s words whispered in her ear: A living hope.

Elara’s fingers found the hidden letter. The recruits charged, their boots churning the mud. The sound was thunder, inevitable. She imagined the dead man walking into that wall of shields, into the spears, into the empire’s unyielding might.

What chance did hope have against that?

A hand touched her shoulder. She flinched.

“You’re staring.” Gaius wiped sweat from his brow, his forearm streaked with dirt. “Tullia send you to spy?”

Elara shook her head. The recruits were dispersing, their laughter loud and young. One of them—a boy with barely a beard—caught her eye and grinned.

Gaius followed her gaze. “New batch from Italia. Useless as tits on a bull.” He reached for her basket. “Get the juniper?”

She handed it to him. His fingers brushed hers, calloused and warm. For a heartbeat, she wanted to tell him. About the letter. About Miriam. About the dead man who bled.

He sniffed the juniper. “Smells like home.”

“Not my home,” she said before she could stop herself.

Gaius frowned. “Britannia’s home now.”

The recruits were singing as they marched away, their voices rough but merry. A marching song. A soldier’s song.

Elara watched them go. The letter prickled against her thigh.

“Come on.” Gaius nudged her. “I’m starving.”

She followed him toward their quarters. The sun broke through the clouds, painting the puddles gold. A bird—some small, brown thing—darted across their path, its wings a blur.

Gaius didn’t notice. He was already talking about the day’s drills, about the new recruits, about the centurion’s temper. His voice was steady, familiar.

Elara nodded at the right places. The bird vanished into the trees.

That night, she dreamed of caves.

Dark mouths in the earth, breathing cold. Miriam stood at the entrance, her arms outstretched. Behind her, shadows moved.

“You don’t have to be afraid,” Miriam said.

Elara reached for her. Her fingers passed through Miriam’s like smoke.

The shadows stepped forward. They had holes in their hands.

She woke gasping. The hearth was dead. Gaius snored beside her, one arm flung over his face.

Outside, the wind moaned through the barracks. Somewhere, a dog howled.

Elara sat up. Her hands shook. She pressed them to her mouth, as if she could push the dream back inside.

The letter rustled as she moved. She hadn’t burned it.

She lay back down. Gaius muttered in his sleep and rolled toward her, his breath hot on her neck.

The dog howled again. Closer this time.

Elara closed her eyes. The caves waited behind her lids.

Dawn came gray and slow. Gaius rose with the watch-horn, his movements brisk in the cold. Elara pretended to sleep until the door clicked shut behind him.

She rose then, her bones aching. The market would be opening soon. The wives would be gathering. Life would continue, orderly as the legion’s ranks.

She dressed mechanically. The letter whispered against her skin.

At the door, she hesitated. The wind tugged at her cloak, insistent.

Somewhere, a bird sang.

Elara stepped outside. The sky hung low and heavy, the color of old bruises. The recruits were already drilling, their shouts punctuated by the centurion’s bark.

She turned away from the noise. Toward the trees. Toward the bird.

The first step was the hardest. The second, less so.

By the third, she was running.

The oil lamp guttered, its flame bending low as if to listen. Elara sat at the edge of the bed, her fingers tracing the creases in Miriam’s letter for the hundredth time. The parchment had grown soft as old linen, the ink blurring at the folds. She pressed her thumb against the word living until the skin turned white.

Gaius’s cloak hung on the peg by the door, still damp from the day’s patrol. The wool smelled of salt and sweat, familiar as her own breath. She imagined packing it away, folding it neatly beside his spare tunic, leaving it behind. The thought lodged in her throat like a fishbone.

Outside, a cart creaked past, its wheels spattering mud against the wall. Voices followed—two legionaries arguing about dice. Their laughter was sharp, careless. The sound of men who belonged.

Elara stood abruptly. The letter trembled in her hand. She crossed to the small writing desk Gaius had carved for her last winter, its surface scarred from her stylus. A single sheet of fresh papyrus lay there, untouched for weeks. She smoothed it with her palm, then hesitated.

What would she even write? Dear husband, I am leaving to chase a ghost?

The lamp flickered. Shadows leapt across the wall, twisting into shapes—a bent tree, a reaching hand, the curve of a woman’s back. Elara exhaled slowly. She set Miriam’s letter down beside the blank papyrus, aligning the edges precisely. The wax seal, now brittle, flaked onto the desk like dried blood.

A knock at the door. Elara’s head snapped up. Too early for Gaius. Too late for visitors.

Silence. Then, a child’s whisper: “My mother says thank you.”

The fever girl. Elara unbarred the door. The child stood hunched in the twilight, clutching a woven reed doll. Its face was smudged with charcoal, the eyes two uneven dots.

“The yarrow worked.” The girl thrust the doll forward. “For you.”

Elara took it. The reeds were damp, the binding twine frayed. “You shouldn’t be out after dark.”

The girl shrugged. “Mother’s asleep.” She peered past Elara into the cottage, her gaze lingering on the writing desk. “Are you going away?”

Elara stiffened. “Why do you ask?”

“Your cloak’s on the bed. And your good shoes.” The girl pointed to the corner where Elara had laid them out without realizing. “That’s what my aunt did before she left.”

The doll’s rough edges pressed into Elara’s palm. “Go home.”

The girl skipped backward, unoffended. “The dead man walks at night, my mother says.” She grinned, revealing a missing tooth. “But you’re not afraid.”

Elara shut the door before the child could see her hands shake.

The doll left wet streaks on the desk. Elara turned it over. Someone had stitched a tiny red mark on its left hand—a clumsy X in fraying thread. Her breath caught.

She dropped the doll as if burned. The reed limbs clattered against the wood. Outside, the wind picked up, rattling the shutters. Somewhere in the barracks, a trumpet blew two short notes—the signal for all clear.

Gaius would be home soon.

Elara stared at the two letters on the desk: one worn soft with longing, the other blank with possibility. She reached for the stylus, then stopped. No words would bridge this. No explanation would suffice.

She lifted the oil lamp and carried it to the hearth. The fire had died hours ago, but the ashes still held a faint warmth. Kneeling, she stirred them with an iron poker. Embers winked red in the gloom.

Miriam’s letter would burn quick. One spark, and the words living hope would curl into smoke. She could still choose the life she’d built—the steady rhythm of the fort, Gaius’s familiar weight in the bed beside her, the certainty of tomorrow.

The lamp trembled, casting wild shadows.

Elara set it down. She returned to the desk and folded Miriam’s letter once more, this time pressing the creases with the edge of her hand until they lay sharp as knife blades. She placed it deliberately in the center of the desk, where the lamplight pooled thickest.

Then she turned to the chest at the foot of the bed. Her winter cloak lay folded beneath Gaius’s spare sandals. She shook it out, the wool heavy in her hands. The dye had faded along the seams, the blue leaching into gray. Judean blue.

The door latch clicked.

Elara froze, the cloak half-folded. Gaius stepped inside, his helmet tucked under one arm. A fresh cut marked his cheekbone, the blood already drying.

“You’re up late.” He unbuckled his sword belt, the scabbard thudding against the wall. His gaze skipped over the open chest, the cloak in her hands, the doll on the desk. “What’s that?”

“A gift.” She nodded to the reed doll. “From the fever girl.”

Gaius grunted. He peeled off his tunic, revealing the old scar across his ribs—a souvenir from his first campaign. “Tullia’s been asking after you.”

Elara laid the cloak back in the chest. “Oh?”

“Said you ran off like a spooked hare in the market.” He splashed water on his face from the basin, the droplets darkening the floorboards. “You sick?”

She watched him wipe his face, the motion brisk, efficient. A soldier’s habit. “No.”

“Good.” He tossed the towel aside and reached for her, his palm rough against her cheek. “You’d tell me, right?”

His thumb brushed the corner of her mouth. A question. A habit.

Elara leaned into the touch, just enough to feel the calluses. “Always.”

Gaius nodded, satisfied, and turned to bank the hearth. Elara studied the slope of his shoulders, the way his hair curled damp at his neck. Memorizing.

The letter waited on the desk.

Gaius didn’t glance at it as he passed. He kicked off his boots and collapsed onto the bed, his exhale loud in the quiet. “Big drill tomorrow. New recruits from Gaul.” He yawned. “Like herding cats.”

Elara blew out the lamp. Darkness swallowed the room, but the image of the letter lingered behind her eyelids—bright as a wound.

She lay beside Gaius, careful not to touch him. His breathing slowed into sleep within minutes. Outside, the wind gnawed at the thatch.

Elara closed her eyes. She imagined the caves from her dream, their dark mouths yawning. The shadows with holes in their hands. Miriam’s voice, steady as a drumbeat: You don’t have to be afraid.

Dawn was hours away. The fort would sleep until the watch-horn sounded.

Elara rose silently. She dressed by touch—her sturdiest sandals, the cloak she’d woven last winter, the knife Gaius had given her for butchering hares. The letter she slipped inside her tunic, against her ribs.

At the door, she hesitated. Gaius stirred, muttering in his sleep. A name—maybe hers, maybe a battlefield’s. She couldn’t tell.

The wind gusted, rattling the latch. Elara stepped into the night.

The sky was clear for once, the stars sharp as nailheads. To the east, beyond the fort’s walls, the road to the port unspooled like a ribbon.

She took the first step. Then the second.

By the time the watch-horn sounded, she was already beyond the tree line, her shadow stretching long and thin ahead of her.

The oil lamp guttered out. On the desk, the reed doll lay where Elara had dropped it, its stitched wound facing upward. The blank papyrus beside it remained untouched.

Outside, the wind turned. The first drops of rain struck the shutters like thrown pebbles.

Gaius slept on.
