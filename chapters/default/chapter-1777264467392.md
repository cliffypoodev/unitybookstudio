The notebook lay open on the desk, the photograph of his burned face a stark white rectangle against the wood grain. Cassius closed it. He’d documented the event. That was the ritual now. A log of impossible things, a ledger for a bank that accepted only the counterfeit currency of his own decaying reality.

Two days passed. The sunburn faded to a tight, pink itch, then to peeling flakes of skin that littered his shoulders like ash. He vacuumed them up. He made coffee. The routine was a cage, but he knew the dimensions of the bars. Tuesday meant eggs.

He cracked two into a bowl, the yolks wobbling like jaundiced eyes. He whisked them with a fork, the tink-tink-tink against the ceramic the only sound in the kitchen. The gas stove hissed to life with a blue flower of flame. Butter sizzled in the pan, releasing its warm, nutty smell. He poured the eggs in. They bubbled at the edges, solidifying.

He’d eaten scrambled eggs every Tuesday for a decade. Maybe longer. He couldn’t remember deciding on it. It was just what he did. A fixed point. He slid them onto a plate, the pale yellow mound steaming. He carried the plate to the small table by the window. The sky outside was the color of dirty chalk.

He took the first bite. The texture was familiar—soft, a little rubbery. The taste was salt and fat and nothing else. He chewed.

A prickling started at the back of his throat. Not a tickle. A sharp, specific sting, like he’d swallowed a speck of fiberglass. He swallowed the mouthful, took a sip of water. The water didn’t help. The sting spread, becoming a hot, swelling tightness.

He set the fork down. His tongue felt thick. He ran it over the roof of his mouth. The flesh there was puffing up, ridges swelling against the smooth palate. He breathed in. The air had a thin, whistling quality.

This was wrong. He wasn’t allergic to eggs. He ate eggs. Every Tuesday.

He stood. The movement was too fast. A wave of dizziness tilted the room. He gripped the edge of the table. His skin was crawling now, a thousand invisible ants marching up his arms, across his chest. He looked down. Red welts were rising on his forearms, angry and defined, tracing the paths of veins. Hives.

His heart was a frantic animal kicking against his ribs. Not fear, not yet—this was mechanical, a runaway engine. His breath hitched. He tried to draw a full lungful and couldn’t. The tightness in his throat was a fist, squeezing shut.

Panic arrived then, cold and clear. He stumbled toward the bathroom, one hand clawing at his collar. The medicine cabinet. He needed Benadryl. Did he have Benadryl? He yanked the mirrored door open. Bottles clattered. Aspirin, antacids, the half-used tube of burn cream. No pink tablets. No relief.

His reflection. His lips were bluish, swelling. His eyes were wide, the whites veined with red. The hives had reached his neck, a lurid crimson map.

Call.

The thought was a single, urgent pulse. He lurched back into the living room, toward the phone on the desk. His legs were heavy, uncoordinated. The distance stretched. He fumbled the receiver, dropped it. A dial tone buzzed from the floor. He crouched, a groan escaping his swollen throat. The world grayed at the edges, tunneling.

He punched 9-1-1. His voice was a strangled croak. “Allergic. Can’t… breathe.”

The dispatcher’s questions were calm, distant. Address. What was the allergen? He tried to say eggs. The word came out a wet gurgle. He dropped the phone. It clattered on the hardwood. He slumped against the desk leg, his back to the wood. The air was syrup. He fought to pull it in, each attempt a shallow, desperate sip.

This was how it happened. Not with a ghostly whisper or a phantom sunburn. With eggs. A Tuesday morning and his own body turning against him with a quiet, efficient violence.

The itching was everywhere now, a deep, maddening fire under his skin. He scratched at his arms, his nails leaving white trails across the red welts. It didn’t help. The sensation was inside, in a layer he couldn’t reach. His lungs burned. They were flat, useless sacks.

A different sensation cut through the panic. A coolness, starting in his gut. It spread outward, a slow diffusion like ink in water. It wasn’t relief. It was a separate presence, a calm, biological counterpoint to the chaos. His racing heart stuttered, skipped, then settled into a slower, harder rhythm. Thud. Like a fist on a door.

The swelling in his throat didn’t recede, but the terror blurred. A strange detachment descended. He was a spectator. He watched his own blue-tinged hand splayed on the floorboards. He noticed a scratch in the varnish, a long, pale line near the baseboard. He’d never seen it before.

From the static in his head, a voice. Not the cold presence. Not memory.

Cassius.

Lilith. It was her. Not a memory of her, not a sensory spike. Her voice, spoken now, into the narrowing dark of his oxygen-starved brain. It held no fear. It was a command. A summoning.

Breathe.

He couldn’t. The fist was clenched. Black spots bloomed in his vision, merging into a pool.

Then, underneath the dispatcher’s tinny voice from the fallen phone, underneath the ragged whistle of his own struggle, he heard it. A new internal sound. A wet, tearing pop, deep in his chest. A release. Not in his throat, but lower, in the branching pathways of his lungs. A biological switch, thrown.

A shudder racked him. He drew a sudden, ragged gasp. The air was thin, insufficient, but it came. Then another. The tightness remained, a crushing band around his ribs, but a thread of oxygen got through. The gray tunnel receded a fraction.

Sirens wailed in the distance, growing louder. Tires screeched outside. Heavy footsteps pounded up the stairwell. A fist hammered on his door.

“Emergency services!”

He tried to call out. A weak exhale was all he produced. He kicked his foot, a feeble thump against the floor.

The door crashed open. Two paramedics filled the doorway, a blur of dark uniforms and equipment. Their eyes scanned the room, found him slumped against the desk. They moved with a swift, practiced economy.

“Sir? Can you hear me? What did you ingest?”

He opened his mouth. A sound like rustling paper came out. One of the paramedics was already beside him, fingers on his wrist, tilting his head back to look at his throat. The man’s face was all business, but his eyes flickered—a quick, professional assessment of the swelling, the hives, the cyanosis.

“Severe anaphylaxis. Let’s get him on the gurney. Epi ready.”

Hands lifted him. The world tilted, became the ceiling of his apartment, a water stain shaped like a continent he’d never identified. The stretcher straps were tight across his chest. They wheeled him out. He saw the ceiling of the hallway, then the harsh white sky.

In the ambulance, the siren a wall of noise, one paramedic drove. The other worked over him, swabbing his arm, prepping a needle. “This is epinephrine. It’s going to help.”

The needle bit. A cold rush flooded his vein, a chemical tide. His heart, that slow, heavy fist, exploded into a frantic gallop. The paramedic watched the monitor, nodded. “Pressure’s coming up. Oxygen sat improving.”

Cassius stared at the fluorescent light tube above him, vibrating with the vehicle’s motion. The itching began to recede, the swelling in his throat loosening its grip by millimeters. The medical intervention was real, a solid anchor in the physical world. But underneath the drug’s effects, the other knowledge sat, cold and certain.

The paramedic hadn’t asked the right question. He’d asked what. Not who.

The reaction wasn’t to the eggs. It was to the version of himself that could eat them. His body was a contested territory, and a new claimant had just lodged a violent protest. The epinephrine was fighting the symptom. The cause was still in residence, a silent, biological squatter with different rules, different allergies, a different history written in flesh and blood.

Lilith’s voice had been a lifeline thrown from a different shore. He knew, with a certainty that dwarfed the fading panic, that the next time his body betrayed him, she might not be there to call his name. The war was no longer about memories. It was about biology. And he was the battlefield.

The ambulance hit a pothole. The jolt traveled up the gurney’s frame and into his teeth. The fluorescent light above him streaked, leaving a green afterimage on his retina. The paramedic’s face swam into view again, a young man with a close-cropped beard and eyes that missed nothing.

“You’re improving. Throat’s opening. Just stay with me, okay? Breathe with me.” His voice was a steady metronome under the siren’s scream.

Cassius tried. His lungs obeyed, but they felt borrowed. The air had a chemical tang—oxygen, plastic, antiseptic. The epinephrine buzz was a wild, electric thrumming in his limbs, like he’d been plugged into a faulty socket. His heart hammered against his sternum, a frantic prisoner. He stared at the light.

The water stain on his ceiling. The shape of a continent. He’d meant to look it up once, see which one it resembled. He never had. That was the story of everything in that apartment. Intentions that dissolved into the daily static of being Cassius.

A different pressure built behind his eyes. Not pain. A fullness, like a dam straining.

The paramedic was talking to the driver, words about protocols and alerting the ER. His voice became a low hum. The siren warped, stretching into a long, atonal drone. The vibration of the ambulance floor softened, the hard edges of the world blurring like wet clay.

Then, smell.

Not a memory of a smell. The thing itself.

Lilac and gasoline.

It flooded his sinuses, rich and overpowering. The sterile ambulance air vanished. He was somewhere else. The light changed—not the cool fluorescence, but a honeyed, late-afternoon sun, thick with dust motes. He felt rough wood grain under his fingertips, sun-warmed. He heard the distant, rhythmic thwack of a screen door swinging in a breeze, a loose hinge.

A laugh.

It was low, unselfconscious, caught in the throat. Lilith’s laugh. It wasn’t directed at him. She was laughing at something she’d read, her head bent over a book, a strand of dark hair stuck to her damp temple. He could see the fine down on her forearm, golden in the light. He could feel the humid, heavy air of a Midwestern summer, the kind that made your shirt stick to your back.

He was on a porch. A wraparound porch. Their porch.

The certainty was absolute. It was a physical knowing in his bones, a rightness of posture—his shoulders relaxed, one ankle hooked over the opposite knee. This was his place. This was his life.

He watched her turn a page. Her finger traced a line of text. She had a scar on the knuckle of her index finger, a tiny white crescent from a long-ago cut. He’d kissed it once. The memory of that kiss arrived, not as a thought, but as a ghost-sensation on his lips.

“Listen to this,” she said, her voice real enough to make his stopped heart ache. She didn’t look up. “It’s so perfectly stupid.”

He tried to speak. To say what? To play his part in this scene he knew by heart. His mouth wouldn’t move. He was a passenger here, too.

The ambulance hit another bump.

The porch shuddered. The image flickered, a film reel jumping its sprockets. The warm light bleached into the cold ambulance glow. The smell of lilac and gasoline twisted, becoming the odor of his own sweat and industrial cleaner. The thwack-thwack of the screen door fragmented into the siren’s pulse.

“—stay with me, sir. Eyes open.”

The paramedic’s hand was on his shoulder, a firm, real pressure. Cassius forced his eyes to focus. The young man’s brow was furrowed. He’d seen the lapse.

“You faded out on me. You’re okay. Just breathe. We’re two minutes out.”

Cassius gasped. The breath was a sob. The loss was immediate and visceral, a physical amputation. The porch, the sun, the her-ness of her—ripped away. It left a vacuum more terrifying than the anaphylaxis. The allergic reaction had attacked his body. This attacked his home.

He wasn’t remembering. He was visiting.

And he’d been evicted.

Tears welled, hot and shameful. They spilled over, tracking through the sweat on his temples. The paramedic mistook them for fear, for pain. “Almost there. You’re doing great.”

The ambulance slowed, turned sharply. Tires crunched over gravel. The siren cut off mid-wail, leaving a ringing silence that felt louder than the noise. The back doors flew open. Cool, institutional air washed in. New voices, clipped and efficient. The gurney unlocked, rattled, was pulled smoothly from the ambulance bay into the bright, chaotic maw of the emergency room.

Ceiling tiles streaked past. Harsh lights. A nurse calling out stats. They transferred him to a bed in a curtained bay. More hands. A blood pressure cuff tightened on his arm. A clip on his finger. Questions came in a rapid-fire staccato.

“Any known allergies? Any history of anaphylaxis? What did you eat? Just eggs? Any new medications?”

He answered in monosyllabic croaks. No. Eggs.

A doctor, an older woman with tired eyes and a sharp gaze, examined his throat with a penlight. “Swelling is significantly reduced. Hives are fading. You’re one lucky man. Another few minutes without that epi…” She didn’t finish. She made a note on a chart. “We’ll observe you for four to six hours. Monitor for a biphasic reaction.”

They left him then, the curtain swishing shut. He was alone in the buzzing, beeping semi-dark. The adrenaline was metabolizing, leaving a profound, hollow exhaustion. His body felt like a wrung-out rag, trembling with aftershocks. But his mind was a riot.

It was more vivid than the hospital room. More real than the itchy sheet under his fingers. That other life had texture, weight. It had the mundane, precious detail his own life lacked. He had no porch. He had a fire escape that groaned. He had no Lilith. He had a silence so deep it echoed.

The epinephrine had saved his life, but it had also, somehow, thinned the membrane. The other Cassius—the one who lived in a house with a wraparound porch, who knew the sound of Lilith’s laugh as she read—that man was allergic to eggs. And when this Cassius, the one in the apartment with the water-stain continent, had eaten them, that other man’s body had screamed in protest through his own.

It wasn’t possession. It was overlap. A catastrophic failure of the soul’s insulation.

A shudder racked him, deep and cold. He pulled the thin hospital blanket up to his chin. The shaking wouldn’t stop. It was in his bones.

He closed his eyes, seeking the dark, quiet blankness of oblivion.

Instead, he got sound.

Not a voice. Not a memory. Music. A few bars of a song, tinny and distant, played on a cheap radio. A man’s voice singing about a broken wheel. It was there, then gone.

Then, the smell of turned earth and rain.

Then, a flash of a blue front door, paint peeling.

Fragments. Shards of other lives, other days, pushing through the cracks. The static was resolving into signals. Weak, corrupted, but undeniable.

The curtain rustled. A nurse entered, bright and brisk. “How’s the breathing? Any tightness returning?”

He opened his mouth to say fine. To be the compliant patient. He shook his head.

She checked his monitors, noted the numbers. “You’re stable. Try to rest.” She patted his blanket-covered foot, a gesture of impersonal kindness, and left.

Rest. The word was a joke.

He let his head fall back against the pillow. The ceiling here was clean, white, and blank. No continents. No maps. He focused on it, trying to anchor himself in this sterile, real-time now. He counted the holes in the acoustic tiles. He followed the path of a fluorescent light’s flicker.

The exhaustion was a tidal pull. His eyelids grew heavy. The tremors in his limbs finally began to quiet. The sounds of the ER—the distant page, a cart rolling by, a murmured conversation—faded into a soft roar.

As he slid toward the dark, he heard it one last time. Not a memory. An echo, bleeding through from a porch a thousand miles and a whole life away.

Lilith’s laugh. Clear, intimate, a sound meant for a shared private joke.

It curled in his ear, warm and alive.

Then the world, both of them, went dark.

Consciousness returned in layers, each one heavier than the last. First came the smell: antiseptic and stale sweat under a floral air freshener. Then the sound: the low beep-beep-beep of a heart monitor, a steady, mocking reminder. Then the light, a diffused gray through his eyelids. Last was the feeling: a deep, cellular ache, as if every part of him had been stretched thin and snapped back.

Cassius opened his eyes. The ceiling tiles were still there, a grid of perforated white. The curtain around his bed was a faded mint green. He was in a different bay, quieter. Observation. The word from the doctor surfaced in his mind like a buoy in oil-slick water.

He tried to sit up. A wave of nausea and dizziness pinned him back to the pillow. His throat was raw, a scraped-tube feeling. He touched his neck. The skin was tender, swollen. The hives on his arms had faded to faint pink blotches, like the ghost of a rash.

He was alive. The fact was neutral. A biological outcome.

The memory of the porch was not neutral. It sat in the center of his mind, not as a recollection but as a physical object, dense and radiant. He could still feel the sun-warmed wood grain under his fingertips. He could still see the exact way the light had caught the fine hairs on Lilith’s forearm. That scar. The white crescent.

His own left hand lay on the blanket. He turned it, examining the knuckles. Unmarked. No scar. The skin was pale, a little dry. A stranger’s hand.

A different thought, cold and surgical, cut through the haze. The allergy wasn’t to eggs. It was to being this Cassius while eating eggs that belonged to that one. His body had rejected the act as a foreign protocol. Like transplant rejection. The host body attacking the donor organ. Only he was both.

The curtain rings scraped along the rod. The older doctor stepped through, holding a clipboard. She had reading glasses perched on her nose now. She looked at him, then at the monitor.

“Mr. Cassius. You’ve been out for a while. How are you feeling?”

He worked his tongue, found a shred of voice. “Tired.”

“That’s the epinephrine crash. And the trauma.” She moved closer, shone a penlight into his eyes. He flinched. “Pupils are reactive. Throat?”

“Sore.”

“Let’s see.” She had him open his mouth, say ‘ah.’ The light probed. “Swelling is almost gone. You’re very fortunate. No biphasic reaction.” She clicked off the light. “We’ll keep you a bit longer, but you should be able to go home this evening. I’m prescribing an EpiPen. You’ll need to carry it at all times.”

He stared at her. “I’m not allergic to eggs.”

She gave him a look, the kind reserved for patients who were confused post-crisis. “Your body today suggests otherwise. Sometimes adult-onset allergies develop. It’s uncommon, but not impossible. Have you eaten eggs recently without issue?”

“Every Tuesday. For years.”

“Well.” She made a note. “Not anymore. Your immune system has decided eggs are a threat. You’ll need to avoid them completely. Read labels. Ask at restaurants. This is serious.”

He wanted to argue. To explain it wasn’t the eggs, it was the context. It was the Tuesday ritual of one man trespassing on the biology of another. The words piled up in his raw throat and died there. He nodded.

She seemed satisfied. “A nurse will bring your discharge papers and the prescription. Rest.”

She left. The curtain swayed in her wake.

Alone again, he focused on the monitor’s steady beep. It was supposed to be reassuring. It felt like a countdown. He looked at his hands again. They were trembling. A fine, constant vibration, like a tuning fork struck too hard. He willed them to stop. They shook.

He remembered the wet, tearing pop in his chest. The biological switch. That hadn’t been the epinephrine. That had come before the paramedics, in the moment Lilith’s voice had cut through the static. Breathe.

Had she done that? Had some version of her, in some other reality, reached across the bleed and thrown a switch in a body she knew? Or was it just his own desperate brain, personifying a last-ditch autonomic function?

It didn’t matter. The effect was the same. She was in the system. A variable in his code.

The trembling spread from his hands to his arms, a deep shiver that had nothing to do with temperature. He felt like a shell that had been cracked and hastily glued back together. Everything inside was settling wrong.

A cart rattled by outside his curtain. He heard two nurses talking, their voices a low murmur.

“—just got admitted, third floor. Fell off a ladder cleaning gutters. Broken hip, maybe a concussion. His wife’s a mess.”

“Old Mr. Hennessy?”

“No, the other one. Jenkins.”

Their voices faded. A mundane tragedy. A broken hip. A wife who was a mess. A real problem, with real causes and real consequences. He envied them with a sudden, desperate ache. He would trade all his phantom porches for a broken hip. For a problem that existed here, that everyone could see and agree upon.

He closed his eyes. Big mistake.

The darkness behind his lids wasn’t empty. It was a field of static, black and white snow. And in the snow, images flickered, weak and brief.

A child’s red mitten, lost in gray slush. The taste of spearmint gum and diesel fumes. A wallpaper pattern of tiny, repeating sailboats. A doorknob that was always cold.

Fragments. Detritus from other lives, washing up on the shore of his consciousness. They had no narrative. They were sensory litter. His mind tried to grab one, to follow the thread of the cold doorknob, but it dissolved into the noise.

He opened his eyes, gasping. The hospital room was a relief, its sterility a bulwark. He focused on the IV stand, the chrome gleam, the plastic bag of clear fluid. He counted the drops falling into the chamber. One. Two. Three.

He became aware of a new sensation, a faint, prickling dissonance in his own skin. It was like wearing a sweater that was slightly the wrong size, the seams not sitting right. This body, this collection of tissues and nerves he’d called his own for forty-odd years, felt… ill-fitted. As if he’d put on a coat from a rack and only now noticed the shoulders were too narrow, the sleeves too short.

The epiphany was quiet, and it carried no heat. It was a simple, dreadful acceptance.

He was not being haunted. He was being crowded.

Other versions of Cassius were not just sending memories. They were broadcasting their physicality. Their allergies. Their scars. Their postures on sun-warmed porches. His body was the receiver, and it was tuning into multiple stations at once. The signal for “Cassius-who-lives-with-Lilith” was strong today. Its carrier wave was anaphylaxis.

The war was over. He had lost. He was no longer a man with a problem. He was the problem itself. A glitch in a system, awaiting correction. The only question left was which version of him would the system finally decide to keep, and what would it delete to make room.
