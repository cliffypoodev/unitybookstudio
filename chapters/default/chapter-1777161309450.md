The final, formal act of erasure took place on the morning of December 10, 1810. That day was unseasonably warm. A column of U.S. Troops from Fort Adams, commanded by General Wade Hampton had arrived days earlier, joining the militia Governor William C. C. Claiborne had brought from New Orleans. Their combined force outnumbered the entire former rebel militia of West Florida. There was to be no ceremony of transfer, no recognition of sovereignty. The process, as Claiborne’s official correspondence to Secretary of State Robert Smith makes clear was one of simple substitution. He was there to execute President Madison’s proclamation, a document that contained not a single mention of the Republic of West Florida.

The officers assembled before the gatehouse of Fort San Carlos. The same weathered pole from which the Spanish colors had been hauled down eleven weeks prior now held the lone star flag of the defunct republic. The blue field was faded, the single white star fraying at the points. A U.S. Army sergeant and two privates stood ready. According to a later account by a planter’s wife published in the Louisiana Gazette, a small crowd of townspeople and former militiamen watched from a respectful distance. They were quiet. The only sound was the snap of the two flags in the river breeze—the star above, the Stars and Stripes held ready below.

Fulwar Skipwith was not present. He had submitted his final protest to Claiborne three days earlier and had withdrawn to his plantation, Springfield, in East Feliciana. His brief presidency was over. One man who waited as the closest official witness was Philemon Thomas. The military commander of the rebellion was now a civilian, his authority dissolved. He watched, arms crossed, his expression unreadable. In an interview decades later, Thomas said only that he remembered the ropes being stiff, the pulleys rasping.

The sergeant gave a quiet order. A private began to lower the halyard. The lone star flag descended in jerks, not a smooth glide. It did not fall with the dramatic finality of a defeated standard; it came down like a spent signal. When it reached the ground, a soldier gathered it into a bundle, treating it not with reverence but with the brisk efficiency of a man folding a ground cloth. There was no salute from the former rebels, no spoken word. The sergeant then attached the new flag. The Stars and Stripes ran up the pole and broke open at the top, catching the wind.

The fort was now a U.S. Army post. The town was part of the Territory of Orleans. The rebellion was complete, and in its completion, it had abolished itself.

Claiborne’s administrative machinery began turning before the rope had stopped swaying. His clerks set up tables in the fort’s former guardroom. Their task, outlined in his orders from Washington was to inventory all public property and records of the “late insurrectionary government” and to administer an oath of allegiance to the United States to all inhabitants. The process was methodical and thorough. Men who had, weeks before, styled themselves senators and cabinet officers now queued to swear loyalty to the power that had erased their titles. They signed their names in a ledger. In return, they received Claiborne’s assurance, printed and disseminated, that their land claims would be honored and their rights protected. The transaction was clinical. The rebellion had been a gamble for security; here was the payout.

The physical archives of the seventy-four-day nation did not fare well. Account books, minutes of the convention, Skipwith’s official correspondence—all were packed into wooden crates and shipped under guard to New Orleans. They were not treated as state papers for preservation, but as evidence to be reviewed and filed. In a report to Secretary Smith, Claiborne referred to them as “the papers of the late convention,” dismissing their constitutional pretensions with a bureaucratic adjective. These crates were eventually deposited in the territorial capital’s administrative offices. Over the subsequent decades, through fires, floods, and simple neglect, the majority of these documents were lost. The paper trail of the republic, like its flag was folded away and forgotten.

For the Anglo planters, the lowering of the flag was the closing of a necessary but awkward chapter. The editor of the Mississippi Messenger, a paper that had cautiously supported the rebellion, captured the prevailing mood in an edition published the following week. “The object is attained,” the editorial stated. “Our brethren are now under the protection of the American Constitution, their property secure, their commerce free. The temporary expedient of a separate government has answered its purpose.” There was no nostalgia, no lament for the lost republic. Its purpose had always been instrumental: to force a change of sovereignty. Once that was achieved, the instrument could be discarded.

Only Philemon Thomas seemed to carry a lingering grain of something else. He remained for a long time after the crowd dispersed, looking at the new flag. He had gotten everything he had fought for: secure title to his land, American jurisdiction, peace. Yet, as he told his family later, it stuck in his craw. The rebellion had been a real thing—blood, risk, the sharp smell of gunpowder in the morning fog. Its ending felt like paperwork.

Fulwar Skipwith lived another thirty-four years, but the presidency of the Republic of West Florida proved to be the zenith of his public life. He retired to Springfield, his plantation, and recovered the fortune he had lost in France through shrewd land speculation and agriculture. He served briefly as a postmaster and as a parish judge, local offices that were a quiet comedown from chief magistrate of a nation. In his later years, he was known more for his library and his gardens than for his political adventures. When he died in 1839, his obituary in the Baton Rouge Gazette devoted a single, vague clause to the events of 1810: “…in earlier life, he was connected with the political movements of West Florida.” The man who had drafted a constitution and protested the “outrage” against the Lone Star flag was buried under a simple headstone. His personal papers, including his memoir of the republic were lost after his death, sealing his retreat into the background of history.

John Rhea, the constitutional architect who had presided over the convention and proclaimed independence, also receded from view. He served his term as a senator in the republic’s short-lived legislature, then faded into the legal and agricultural life of the Felicianas. He never sought higher office under the American government. His later correspondence, fragments of which survive in family collections, reveals a man preoccupied with cotton prices and land disputes, not the grand experiment he had helped engineer. The proceduralist who had crafted the mechanisms of a new state seemed content to let those mechanisms be dismantled. He died in 1832, his role in the birth of the republic unmarked by any public monument or dedicated historical record. His obscurity was so complete that later local historians would occasionally confuse him with his more famous contemporary, Congressman John Rhea of Tennessee.

Philemon Thomas, the rebellion’s military spark had the most publicly active later life, yet even his story was ultimately absorbed into other narratives. He commanded a regiment under Andrew Jackson at the Battle of New Orleans, a service that fully rehabilitated him as an American patriot and earned him a seat in the U.S. House of Representatives. He served two terms, but his congressional career was undistinguished. In Washington, he was a backbencher, never once, according to the Annals of Congress, rising to speak about the West Florida revolution. He seemed to wear his past not as a point of pride but as a private relic. When he died in 1847, his funeral was well-attended by old comrades from 1812, but the assault on Fort San Carlos was a secondary note in his eulogy. The act that had created a nation was remembered, if at all, as a prelude to his service under Jackson.

This collective fade was not an accident of poor record-keeping. It was a function of the rebellion’s success. The republic had been a bridge, not a destination. Its leaders had sought not to found a lasting nation but to cross over into the United States. Once they arrived, the bridge could be burned behind them. To dwell on their seventy-four-day sovereignty was to highlight the awkward, extralegal means of their annexation—a topic the Madison administration and the new territorial government had no interest in promoting. Their revolutionary credentials were inconvenient, even mildly embarrassing, in the orderly narrative of American expansion. So they were not celebrated as founding fathers. They became planters, postmasters, and quiet congressmen, their moment of defiance softened into a local curiosity, a story for grandchildren that sounded, even to them, increasingly like a tall tale.

President James Madison’s public pronouncements on West Florida, from the first rumblings of rebellion through the final annexation, formed a masterclass in strategic omission. The republic’s sovereignty was a fact his administration could not acknowledge without undermining its own legal and diplomatic position. As his correspondence and proclamations show, he crafted a narrative of restoration, not acquisition.

The clearest evidence of this intentional framing lies in the contrast between his administration’s response to Reuben Kemper’s 1804 filibuster and its handling of the 1810 rebellion. When Kemper and his brothers had launched their private raid on Spanish outposts, President Jefferson’s Secretary of State, James Madison had been unequivocal. He denounced the Kempers as “adventurers” and “lawless men” whose actions violated U.S. Neutrality laws. American sovereignty, Madison insisted could not be advanced by such rogue violence. The official position was one of stern disapproval, distancing the government from the embarrassing aggression of its citizens.

Six years later, when a far larger body of those same citizens overthrew Spanish authority, declared independence, and petitioned for annexation, Madison’s calculus shifted entirely. His private letters, particularly those to Governor Claiborne and Secretary of State Robert Smith, reveal a keen focus on the opportunity, not the illegality. Publicly, however, he could not celebrate a revolution he had once condemned. His solution was to erase the revolutionaries from the story.

The October 27, 1810, proclamation authorizing the occupation of West Florida is the definitive document of this erasure. It is a dry, legalistic text, grounded in the disputed claim that the territory was part of the Louisiana Purchase. Madison cited the “actual possession” by Spanish authorities and the “urgency of the occasion” as justification for sending in troops. The proclamation does not mention a convention at St. John’s Plains. It does not reference a declaration of independence. It ignores Philemon Thomas, Fulwar Skipwith, and the Lone Star flag. In Madison’s telling, the United States was merely stepping in to secure its own property from a Spanish administration that had collapsed, filling a vacuum. The armed settlers who created that vacuum were rendered invisible, their government a non-event.

This narrative was enforced down the chain of command. Claiborne’s instructions were to take possession of the territories “lying west of the river Perdido, and claimed as part of the Louisiana Purchase.” His reports back to Washington scrupulously adopted this language. He spoke of pacifying the inhabitants and securing public order, never of negotiating with a sovereign entity. When he referenced the rebel leaders, it was as “influential inhabitants” or “the late insurrectionary leaders,” never as the president or senators of a republic. The administrative paperwork of annexation—the oaths of allegiance, the property inventories—was designed for a population transitioning from Spanish rule, not from self-rule.

The effectiveness of this institutional forgetting is demonstrated by the immediate historical accounts that followed. American newspapers, taking their cue from the official narrative, reported the annexation as a straightforward reclamation. A typical item in the National Intelligencer in November 1810 stated that the president had ordered the occupation of “that portion of the Louisiana purchase which has remained in the hands of Spain.” The ninety-day life of an independent republic went unmentioned. By the time the first histories of American expansion were written, the episode was already compressed into a footnote about the enforcement of the Louisiana Purchase’s ambiguous boundaries.

Madison’s construction was politically brilliant. It allowed the United States to absorb 5,000 square miles of territory and several thousand new citizens without ever admitting to having annexed a neighboring republic. It avoided the diplomatic thorn of recognizing a revolution against Spain, a neutral power. And it transformed the rebels from founders into petitioners, their bold gamble reduced to a quiet administrative process. The republic did not fall; it was disappeared by a paperwork coup. The dead air was not an oversight, but policy.

The lone star on a blue field did not vanish with the bundled flag at Baton Rouge. It migrated. Its journey from a specific emblem of frontier realpolitik to a generalized symbol of secession and independence is a case study in how symbols outlive their creators and are stripped of their original, inconvenient context.

That path leads directly to Texas. In the chaotic years of the Texas Revolution against Mexico (1835-1836), a variety of flags flew over rebel forces. One of the most prominent was a simple white star on a blue ground. Its first documented use in Texas, according to vexillologist Charles A. Spain, Jr. was by a group of volunteers from Georgia in late 1835. These men, many of them from the plantation culture of the southeastern United States would have been familiar with the "Bonnie Blue" that had recently gained notoriety in a different context. months before, in 1835, a popular song called "The Bonnie Blue Flag" had been published in New Orleans, celebrating the West Florida revolt. The song's composer, often credited to a performer known as "Mr. Walsh," used a tune borrowed from a Scottish ballad. Its lyrics were explicit: "We are a band of brothers, and native to the soil, / Fighting for the property we gained by honest toil; / And when our rights were threatened, the cry rose near and far, / Hurrah for the Bonnie Blue Flag that bears a Single Star!"

The song was a hit in the taverns and music halls of the Gulf Coast. When the Georgia Battalion marched to Texas, they carried the symbol they knew from song and, perhaps, from vague family legend. The connection was more than musical. Several key figures in the Anglo settlement of Texas had direct ties to the West Florida parishes. Dr. John S. Roberts, a signer of the Texas Declaration of Independence was a former resident of the Felicianas. The political and cultural currents that had produced the West Florida rebellion—Anglo-American immigration into technically Spanish territory, disputes over land titles and legal rights, and a final, violent assertion of control—bed reenacted on a grander scale in Texas. The lone star was a fitting, pre-made emblem.

The Republic of Texas formally adopted a national standard with a single gold star on a blue vertical bar next to broad white and red bars in 1839. While this "Burnet Flag" was more complex, the lone star element was its central, defining feature. The symbol became synonymous with Texan sovereignty. When Texas joined the Union in 1845, the star was incorporated into the state flag and seal, cementing its legacy. In the process, its older, Floridian origin story was largely overwritten. To most Texans, and to the world, the lone star was a Texan invention, born on the plains of San Jacinto.

The symbol completed a circular journey back to secession in 1861. On January 9, 1861, the Mississippi convention voted to secede from the United States. As delegates spilled into the streets of Jackson, a blue flag bearing a single white star was raised over the capitol building. The act was spontaneous, but the choice of flag was not. A memory of the West Florida banner had been kept alive in the region, and the old song "The Bonnie Blue Flag" was revived with new, secessionist lyrics by the Irish comedian Harry Macarthy. His version, performed to rapturous applause across the nascent Confederacy began: "We are a band of brothers, and native to the soil, / Fighting for the property we gained by honest toil; / And when our rights were threatened, the cry rose near and far, / Hurrah for the Bonnie Blue Flag that bears a Single Star!" The lyrics now celebrated Mississippi's secession as the first step in "forming a Southern Confederacy."

Macarthy’s song, an instant anthem, explicitly linked the flag to a lineage of resistance. One verse proclaimed: "Then here's to our Confederacy, strong we are and brave, / Like patriots of old we'll fight, our heritage to save; / And rather than submit to shame, to die we would prefer, / So cheer for the Bonnie Blue Flag that bears a Single Star!" The "patriots of old" were left unnamed, but the geographic and cultural echo was clear. The Confederate Bonnie Blue Flag was never an official national standard, but it became the most popular unofficial flag of the early Confederacy, flown from Virginia to Texas. It served as the canton for the first official Confederate national flag, the "Stars and Bars."

In this final transformation, the symbol born of a calculated, land-driven coup against a weakened Spain was purified into an abstraction: a stand-alone emblem of Southern independence and state sovereignty. The complicated, self-interested founders of West Florida—men like Skipwith who were gratified by annexation—were replaced in the popular imagination by romanticized patriots fighting for a principle. The flag’s ninety-day life as a tool for securing American citizenship was forgotten; its new life as a symbol of rejecting that citizenship was embraced. The paperwork coup that erased the republic was so complete that its most enduring creation could be appropriated without irony by a movement that saw itself as the spiritual opposite of Madison’s union. The lone star endured not despite its origins being forgotten, but because they were forgotten. Unmoored from history, it could mean anything anyone needed it to mean.

The pattern pioneered in West Florida—settler infiltration, local rebellion, and then annexation by the United States under a thin legal cover—did not remain an isolated case. It became a template, a recurring script in the drama of American continental expansion. The actors and the scenery changed, but the essential plot points remained the same. The Republic of West Florida was not a historical curiosity; it was a prototype.

The most direct and consequential reenactment occurred in Texas. As historian David J. Weber and others have documented, the parallels were structural, not coincidental. Beginning in the 1820s, the Mexican government, like Spain before it, invited Anglo-American settlers into its northern province of Tejas as a buffer against Comanche raids and to develop the land. Stephen F. Austin and other empresarios brought thousands of families, who agreed to become Mexican citizens, convert to Catholicism, and obey Mexican law. Within a decade, the demographic and cultural imbalance was stark. By 1835, the Anglo-American population outnumbered the Tejano population by nearly four to one. Tensions over slavery, local autonomy, and legal rights escalated, mirroring the grievances in West Florida. When the Mexican central government moved to tighten control in 1835, the Anglo settlers, joined by some Tejanos, rebelled. They formed a provisional government, declared independence in March 1836, and fought a war for sovereignty. The Republic of Texas was born.

The West Florida model held. The Texas rebels did not seek to create a permanent, standalone nation. Their declared goal, as articulated in their early consultations was annexation by the United States. Their declaration of independence was, like West Florida’s, a necessary intermediate step to break from Mexico and clear the legal path for American absorption. The United States government, under Presidents Jackson and Van Buren, initially held back, wary of war with Mexico and the explosive political issue of adding another slave state. But the underlying dynamic was identical: American settlers created a revolution on foreign soil, then petitioned their mother country to adopt them. After nine years of Texan independence, the U.S. Congress approved annexation in 1845. The legal justification was the new republic’s request, not the Louisiana Purchase, but the principle of exploiting a settler-created fait accompli was the same. The diplomatic fallout—war with Mexico—was simply a larger, bloodier consequence of the tactic.

The template appeared again, in a more compressed and blatant form, during the California Bear Flag Revolt of 1846. Here, the settler population was smaller, but the context was ripe. Following the American victory in the Mexican-American War, U.S. Forces were already operating in California. A group of American immigrants, fearing preemptive action by Mexican authorities, seized the town of Sonoma on June 14, 1846. They proclaimed the “California Republic” and raised a crude flag featuring a grizzly bear and a star. Their rebellion lasted twenty-six days—shorter even than West Florida’s—before U.S. Navy Commodore John D. Sloat arrived and claimed California for the United States, citing the ongoing war with Mexico. The Bear Flag was hastily lowered, the “republic” forgotten. As in West Florida, the brief sovereignty was a tactical bridge to American rule led by settlers on the ground and validated by the arrival of federal power. The legal cover here was military conquest, but the local uprising served to muddy the waters and provide a veneer of internal consent.

What linked these episodes was a consistent federal strategy, one first refined by Madison in 1810. The U.S. Government would not openly instigate rebellion—that would violate international law and risk war. It relied on its own migratory citizens to serve as the vanguard. They would move into contested or foreign territory, swell in number, chafe under the existing authority, and eventually overthrow it. The federal government would then observe from a distance, offering tacit encouragement or benign neglect. Once the settlers had declared independence and created a “vacuum of authority,” the United States could step in. The justification could be a dubious clause in an old treaty (Louisiana Purchase), a request from the new republic (Texas), or the umbrella of ongoing war (California). The method was always annexation, never colonization. The fiction maintained was that the United States responded to events, not directing them; absorbing willing brethren, not conquering territory.

This settler-rebellion template offered distinct advantages. It was deniable. It was cheap, outsourcing the initial risk and violence to private citizens. And it was politically palatable domestically, framed as the liberation of American brethren and the natural extension of republican principles. The West Florida experiment proved it could work quickly, cleanly, and with minimal international backlash. Madison’s paperwork coup showed how the messy reality of revolution could be administratively erased.

The precedent set on the banks of the Mississippi in 1810 echoed across the continent. It demonstrated that expansion could be achieved not by treaty or war, but by a calculated, three-act play performed by settlers with the federal government waiting in the wings for its cue. The republic’s true legacy was not its forgotten constitution or its quiet founders, but this durable, replicable blueprint for making land American.

The land itself, however, refused to forget. The “Florida Parishes” of Louisiana—that curious geopolitical appendage east of the Mississippi and north of Lake Pontchartrain—remained a distinct cultural and political entity, a quiet to the republic’s physical reality. Their borders were those first claimed by the convention at St. John’s Plains: from the Pearl River to the Perdido, between the 31st parallel and the Gulf. When Louisiana entered the Union in 1812, this territory, still bearing the name of its former Spanish overlord was incorporated as a set of parishes separate from the lands of the Louisiana Purchase proper. The administrative legacy of the seventy-four-day nation was, paradoxically, more enduring than its political one. The region developed its own economic rhythms, tied to the piney woods and small-scale farming as much as to the grand cotton plantations of the Felicianas. Its politics often ran counter to the rest of the state. During the Civil War, the Florida Parishes were a stronghold of Confederate loyalty, but in the decades that followed, they became a bastion of populist and Republican sentiment in an otherwise solidly Democratic Louisiana. This stubborn distinctiveness, historians like Samuel C. Hyde, Jr. have argued can be traced to the region’s foundational experience: its birth in a rebellion that was neither fully sanctioned nor fully celebrated, its early isolation from New Orleans, and its persistent sense of being a place apart. The ghost of the republic shaped local identity long after its name vanished from textbooks.

The rebellion also left a more sinister and indelible mark on the land: the entrenchment of a rigid racial hierarchy. The West Florida revolution was, at its core, a revolt by slaveholders. The convention delegates and the armed men who stormed Baton Rouge were, almost without exception, men of property, and their property was primarily in enslaved people. The Spanish colonial system, while no egalitarian paradise had offered certain pathways to freedom and a degree of legal recognition for free people of color, the gens de couleur libres. The republic’s constitution made no mention of slavery, implicitly protecting it as a foundational economic institution. The American annexation cemented this. Under the U.S. Regime, the Black Codes of the territorial and later state government severely restricted the rights of free people of color and brutally policed the enslaved population. The rebellion’s success ensured that the Florida Parishes would enter the American union not as a frontier of fluid possibilities, but as a hardened slave society. The “property” defended by the Bonnie Blue Flag, in both its 1810 and 1861 lyrics was, in concrete terms, enslaved human beings. The republic’s forgotten story is thus also a story about the early-nineteenth-century closing of the Gulf Coast frontier to anything but a white, planter-dominated order. Its quiet annexation was a victory for that order.

To study the Republic of West Florida, then is to study the mechanics of history’s back room. It is to watch the uncelebrated, often inelegant process by which nations are actually made, before the story is sanded smooth for public consumption. Most national histories are written as biographies of the survivor—the enduring state, the victorious cause. They follow a clear arc from struggle to triumph, populated by heroic founders whose motives are purified by hindsight. The lost causes and short-lived states are relegated to footnotes or curiosities, their stories dismissed as irrelevant to the main narrative. This is a failure of historical imagination. The “failures” and the forgotten often reveal more about the underlying forces at play than the successes do. They show the paths not taken, the contingencies that could have swung another way, the raw, unvarnished motives that in a triumphant story are later cloaked in grand principle.

The West Florida episode lays these mechanics bare. Here was no grand ideological crusade for liberty against tyranny. The Spanish governor, Delassus was not a brutal despot but an understaffed administrator hosting peace dinners. The rebels’ grievances were specific and material: insecure land titles, commercial restriction, a distant and collapsing imperial authority. Their goal was not eternal independence but expedient annexation. Their founding document was a pragmatic petition to James Madison as much as a declaration against Spain. Their revolution succeeded because a larger power found it useful, and was forgotten because that same power found its memory inconvenient. A revolution of realpolitik, executed by planters and lawyers who calculated the odds and seized a window of opportunity that clossed. There is a total honesty in that. It strips away the mythologizing to show nation-building as it frequently is: a messy, calculated, and often cynical business of land, power, and demography.

This case study forces a reconsideration of the standard narrative of American expansion. The clean, map-colored progression westward—from treaty to treaty, war to war—obscures the murkier, ground-level processes that made those treaties and wars possible. Before the diplomats and generals arrived, the settlers had already moved, rebelled, and created facts. The federal government did not always plan this, but it learned, with Madison, to exploit it. The Republic of West Florida stands as the first clear, successful prototype of this method on American soil. It proved that a relatively small group of determined settlers, acting at a moment of imperial weakness could detach a valuable piece of territory and that the United States could then absorb it with minimal diplomatic cost. A proof of concept. The fact that it is missing from most national narratives is precisely what makes it so instructive. Its omission is part of the lesson.

The historical silence surrounding the republic is, therefore, not a void but an artifact in itself. It is a monument to the power of institutional memory-making. Madison’s paperwork coup was more than an administrative maneuver; it was an act of narrative control. By refusing to acknowledge the republic’s sovereignty, he controlled how the event would be recorded and remembered. The archives were shaped to tell a story of rightful reclamation, not opportunistic annexation. The rebels, complicit in their own erasure for the prize of citizenship did not contest it. Later historians, working from those official archives and the sparse private papers that survived, naturally reproduced the quiet. The forgetting was systemic.

Recovering stories like that of the Republic of West Florida is an act of historical restoration. It is not about claiming a lost greatness or inserting a new set of heroes into a patriotic pantheon. It is about complexity. It is about restoring the wrinkles to a story that has been pressed too flat. In these wrinkles—in the ambivalent figure of Fulwar Skipwith, in the procedural diligence of John Rhea, in the calculated violence of Philemon Thomas, in the bewildered captivity of Carlos de Lassus—we see the human dimensions of large historical forces. We see empire not as an abstract concept but as a few dozen underpaid soldiers in a dilapidated fort. We see revolution not as a pure uprising of the spirit but as a risky property dispute. We see annexation not as a destined merger but as a clandestine political deal.

The final image of this history is not a flag or a fort, but a document. It is the single sheet of paper that constituted President Madison’s proclamation of October 27, 1810. A dry text, grounded in a disputed clause of an old treaty, authorizing the occupation of territory that was already in the hands of American settlers who had governed themselves for ninety days. It made no mention of them. It did not need to. Their work was done. The proclamation was the official stamp on a transaction that had already occurred on the ground, in the pine forests and along the muddy rivers of the Gulf Coast. The republic’s significance lies in that gap—between the lived reality of those seventy-four days and the blank silence of the document that erased them. In that gap, the true nature of the nation’s growth is revealed: not as a smooth, inevitable tide, but as a series of calculated gambles, local rebellions, and quiet administrative fictions, each one adding another piece to the map, each one leaving a story behind that was too messy to tell, and so, for a long time was not told at all. The Republic of West Florida is that story.
