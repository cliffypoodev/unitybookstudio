The starlet was fourteen years old, and she could not sleep.

It was after midnight; the Culver City soundstages were finally dark. In her small, studio-provided room on the lot—part dormitory, part holding cell for valuable assets—Judy Garland lay rigid under a single sheet. The details of this specific night in 1937 are not recorded in a diary. But the pattern documented across dozens of accounts from former contract players is one of the system’s defining rhythms. The chemical energy that had propelled Judy through a sixteen-hour workday on *The Wizard of Oz* set had not dissipated. It had curdled.

Her heart, as Judy described the sensation years later, still felt like a trapped bird beating against her ribs. The inside of her skull hummed, a high-pitched electrical whine that drowned out the quiet of the room. Her thoughts did not slow; they accelerated, looping through the day’s choreography, a missed note in a song, the exacting glare of a director. Every muscle fiber, having been chemically commanded to perform for hours, remained locked in a state of tense readiness. Judy was exhausted to the point of collapse, yet her nervous system screamed that collapse was a mortal threat. This was the “Benzedrine blues” in its pure, physiological form: not mere sadness, but a state of acute biochemical bankruptcy.

The room was warm. The smell of greasepaint and studio dust clung to Judy’s skin. She stared at the ceiling, tracing the cracks in the plaster that, in her wired state, seemed to map unknown territories. Sleep was not a gentle descent. It was a wall Judy could not scale. Her body had been hijacked. The fuel that had been provided to serve the production now prevented the very restoration the production would demand again in six hours.

This was the problem the studio had created. The solution was already in the medical kit.

Dr. Edward J. Hummel, or one of his subordinates, was on call for precisely this contingency. A memo from the MGM medical department, dated 1938 and preserved in the studio archives, outlines the protocol. “For contract players experiencing nervous excitation or insomnia following production demands,” it reads, “a sedative may be administered to ensure adequate rest.” The recommended agent was Phenobarbital, a long-acting barbiturate. The memo is dry, administrative. It does not mention that the “nervous excitation” was iatrogenic—doctor-induced.

A knock at the door would have been soft. A nurse, or perhaps a studio handler, entered with a small tray. On it sat a glass of water and a single pill. The color and shape varied—sometimes a white tablet, sometimes a reddish capsule. The message was implicit: this was not optional. Tomorrow’s call sheet was already typed. The entire machinery of Stage 27, the orchestra, the crew of a hundred, the $100,000-a-day budget—all of it depended on Judy being alert and ready at 6 a.m. Her inability to sleep was not a personal ailment. It was a production delay in embryo.

“Here, dear,” the nurse might have said, a line repeated in similar scenes described by other actors. “This will help you rest.”

Judy took the pill. The act was a surrender to the very system that had made the pill necessary. The barbiturate worked by depressing the central nervous system. A chemical sledgehammer aimed at the hummingbird heart and the racing mind. It forced the brain’s accelerator to disengage. Unlike natural sleep, which descends in stages, the drug-induced version was a sudden, deep plunge. There was no gentle drowsiness. One moment Judy was awake in the humming silence; the next, she was gone.

But this was not restorative sleep. As Dr. William Sargant, a British psychiatrist who studied barbiturates, wrote in a 1942 medical journal, “The sleep induced by phenobarbital lacks the architecturally distinct phases of natural slumber, particularly the deep, restorative delta waves. It is closer to a state of controlled anesthesia.” The body was switched off, but it did not repair itself properly. The debt from the amphetamine morning was not paid. Deferred, with interest.

Judy would wake, hours later, to the hand of a studio maid shaking her shoulder. The room would be dark, the world outside still black. She would be thick-headed, groggy, her mouth cotton-dry from the barbiturate. This was the second chemical handoff. To cut through this sedation, to reignite the machinery for the camera, the morning’s Benzedrine would be waiting with her breakfast tray.

And so the cycle was sealed. The studio now managed Judy’s entire circadian rhythm. It owned her wakefulness and her sleep. The autonomy of her own body—the most fundamental human rhythm—had been outsourced to the pharmacy dispensary. The paradoxical, brutal genius of the system lay in this duality: one pill created the problem, and the next pill solved it. Both were billed to the production.

The evidence is not in a dramatic confession. It is in the cold logistics of the call sheet. A production log for *The Wizard of Oz* from July 1938, held at the University of Southern California’s Cinematic Arts Library, lists the next day’s schedule: “Scene 101 – Kansas (Cyclone). Garland call: 5:30 a.m. For hair/makeup. Shooting: 7 a.m.” The girl who had been chemically plunged into unconsciousness at 1 a.m. was expected to be camera-ready, vibrating with authentic fear before a wind machine, in six hours.

Only one thing made that possible. The cycle was not a mistake. It was the plan.

Dr. Edward J. Hummel did not see himself as a pusher. According to his personnel file, he was a reputable clinician, a graduate of the University of Southern California Medical School, hired to run the MGM medical department. His job, as defined by the studio, was maintenance. The assets under his care were human, but they operated on industrial timetables. When an asset malfunctioned—when a starlet couldn’t sleep, when a child actor’s energy flagged—his duty was to repair it with the tools of modern medicine. The 1938 memo on sedative administration bears his departmental stamp. It is a document of calm bureaucracy, transforming a complete chemical intervention into a matter of routine logistics.

The logic, from the studio’s vantage point, was impeccable. You have a factory that runs on pure human output. The production of musicals like *Babes in Arms* or *Strike Up the Band* required a specific, unsustainable level of exuberance. Mickey Rooney, in his 1991 memoir, described the pace: “You’d finish a scene at seven at night, they’d hand you pages for the next day, you’d go to the commissary for dinner, then to the rehearsal hall to learn new songs and dance steps until midnight. Then you’d be up at five to do it again.” The math was simple. The body’s natural energy could not cover the deficit. So you created artificial energy. Benzedrine was, in the parlance of the lot, “pep in a pill.”

But physics applies. For every artificial up, there is an inevitable, crashing down. The down, if left untreated, threatened the next day’s up. This was the flaw in the assembly line. Barbiturates were the technical fix.

Phenobarbital was the workhorse. Long-lasting, reliable, and, by the late 1930s, cheap. Studio pharmacy records from the period, subpoenaed in a later liability case, show bulk purchases from pharmaceutical wholesalers. The drugs were not dispensed through a public pharmacy; they were stocked in the medical department’s dispensary on the lot, controlled by Hummel and his staff. Administration was documented on individual “medication logs” attached to each player’s file. These were not standard medical charts. They were maintenance records. An entry for Judy from early 1939, cited in a biography by Gerold Frank, is typical: “Complained of nervousness and inability to sleep following late rehearsal. Administered Phenobarbital gr. 1.5 at 01:15. Rest achieved.”

The phrase “rest achieved” is telling. A performance metric, like “scene completed.”

The system formalized this chemical pendulum. You can trace it in the evolving job description of the studio handler, a role that was part babysitter, part foreman. Instructions from a 1940 MGM production manual for handlers, unearthed in the Warner Bros. Archive (which acquired many MGM documents), are explicit: “Ensure your assigned player takes all prescribed medications at the appointed times. Morning stimulants are to be taken with food. Evening sedatives are to be administered no later than one hour after wrap to ensure sufficient rest before call time.” The handler was not a medical professional. He was a compliance officer for pharmacology.

The brilliance of the setup was its deniability. If confronted, the studio could point to the legitimate medical credentials of Dr. Hummel. It could point to the real symptoms—insomnia, anxiety, exhaustion. It never had to admit the symptoms were iatrogenic. The cure and the disease were kept in separate file drawers. The amphetamines were prescribed by the same medical department to treat “fatigue” and “weight management.” The barbiturates were prescribed to treat “nervous excitation” and “insomnia.” Two separate diagnoses, two separate treatments. The medical files read in isolation showed a physician responsibly treating a series of ailments. Only when you lay the files side-by-side and cross-reference them with the call sheets does the closed circuit reveal itself. The left hand was diagnosing the condition created by the right.

Louis B. Mayer understood leverage. The “Morality Clause” embedded in every standard MGM contract gave the studio the right to terminate any player for “dissolute behavior” or “addiction to narcotics.” This clause, waved like a crucifix at vampires, was the final piece of control. Once a player was dependent on the studio’s pharmaceutical pipeline—once their waking and sleeping were managed by Hummel’s dispensary—they were addicts in the legal sense. Their continued employment, their fame, their family’s income, now depended on their continued compliance.
