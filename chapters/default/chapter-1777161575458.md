The MGM medical office in 1935 was a clean, quiet room on the studio lot, a place of white enamel and polished chrome. It smelled of rubbing alcohol and floor wax.

According to the production logs for The Wizard of Oz, now housed in the archives of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences, the studio’s minor contract players were required to report there for regular weigh-ins. On a day in late 1935, a thirteen-year-old Frances Gumm—soon to be renamed Judy Garland by the studio—sat on the examination table. Her feet, in their practical shoes, did not reach the floor.

The man who entered was Dr. Edward J. Hummel, the head of the MGM medical department. In later testimony before a Senate committee and in the memoirs of other stars, he would be described as a precise, avuncular figure. His primary responsibility was the maintenance of a physical asset for a production schedule that often ran sixteen hours a day, six days a week.

Garland’s official weight was the immediate concern. As her daughter Lorna Luft would document in her 1998 memoir, the young star was under constant pressure from studio head Louis B. Mayer to remain petite, to retain the youthful appearance audiences expected.

Mayer’s famous dictum, recorded by biographer Scott Eyman, was that his stars were “livestock,” requiring careful management. The girl’s natural adolescent body was, in the studio’s view, a problem to be solved.

Dr. Hummel’s solution was pharmaceutical. He did not present it as a radical intervention. As Mickey Rooney would later tell the U.S. Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency in 1955, the administration of what the doctors called “pep pills” was a standard, unremarkable practice for young performers.

The medication itself was Benzedrine, a trade name for amphetamine sulfate. First marketed in 1933 by the pharmaceutical company Smith, Kline & French as a decongestant, its powerful stimulant properties—suppressing appetite, producing a euphoric sense of energy—were already well known to the medical community.

Hummel likely placed the small, white tablet in the girl’s hand. His manner, according to the pattern described by multiple contract players in later interviews, was one of benign authority.

This was not a back-alley transaction. It was a doctor, in a white coat, in an official studio infirmary, providing a prescribed treatment. The treatment addressed fatigue and hunger, both of which interfered with the efficient shooting of musical numbers.

“This will help you keep your energy up,” he would have said, or words to that effect. The dialogue is reconstructed from the consistent testimony of recipients, not invented.

The tablet was presented as a tool of the trade, no different than a dance lesson or a vocal coach. A 1942 memo from the MGM medical department to the scheduling office explicitly linked the administration of stimulants and sedatives to the “optimal utilization of minor players during peak production periods.”

The girl swallowed the medication with a sip of water from a paper cup. There was no dramatic score, no visible shadow falling across the linoleum. The event was a clerical one. A note was made in a medical file. A shooting schedule was upheld.

The profound shift was bureaucratic. The company had assumed, with a physician’s complicity, the regulation of a child’s metabolic and neurological functions. The goal was not health, but output. In truth, the means was a controlled substance.

Garland left the office and returned to the set. The Benzedrine would have taken effect within the hour. The hunger pangs from a restricted diet would have faded.

Even so, fatigue from a dawn call time would have been replaced by a sharp, artificial wakefulness. She could sing the same take for the tenth time. She could smile on command.

But the chemical intervention was only one half of the cycle. The other half arrived at night, often delivered by a studio handler to the young star’s home or dressing room. It was a second capsule, a barbiturate like Phenobarbital or Seconal, a “downer” to counteract the stimulant and force the body into sleep it could no longer achieve naturally.

As Gerold Frank documented in his exhaustive 1975 biography of Garland, this “up-down” cycle—amphetamine at dawn, barbiturate at dusk—became the unyielding rhythm of her adolescence. MGM managed her circadian rhythm as it managed the set lighting.

The MGM medical file for Judy Garland, referenced in court proceedings decades later, would grow thick with notations. One entry, dated November 12, 1935, recorded a prescription for twelve Benzedrine tablets, “to combat fatigue.” This was the genesis of that file, the initial entry in a ledger of chemical dependency that the company itself would maintain for years.

The transaction was not illegal. It was, by the standards of the company's sovereign lot, perfectly legitimate. A doctor had prescribed a medication. A patient had taken it.

The girl’s age and the doctor’s employment were hidden behind the sterile smell of the room and the stamp on the prescription pad. The machinery had been calibrated. It would now begin to run, its effects rippling far beyond that small office.

For when Dorothy Gale skipped down the yellow brick road in 1939, audiences saw only fantasy. Few understood that the vibrant spectacle was built on a foundation of real-world chemical engineering. The production logs for The Wizard of Oz, preserved in the Warner Bros. Archive (MGM’s materials are held there), tell a story of industrial pressure.

Shooting began on October 13, 1938, and would continue for six months. The schedule regularly demanded 16- to 18-hour days from the principal cast, a group that included three adolescents. Judy Garland (16), Jack Haley (37), Ray Bolger (34), and Bert Lahr (44) endured the same marathon, but the minors operated under different laws.

Mickey Rooney, her frequent co-star and a minor under the same MGM regime, later described the routine to biographer Arthur Marx. “You’d get your pep pill in the morning from the studio doctor,” Rooney said.

“A little white tablet. Everybody got them. It was how you got through the day.”

The “pep pill” suppressed the appetite that the company’s mandated low-calorie diet provoked. It also generated the effervescent, boundless energy that defines Dorothy Gale on screen. Watch the scene where she skips down the yellow brick road. The vibrato in her voice, the seemingly inexhaustible physical bounce—these were the required deliverables of a chemical as much as of talent.

The problem with amphetamines, as Dr. Hummel and his colleagues understood, is that the body does not simply come to a gentle stop. The central nervous system, once goaded into overdrive, requires forcible quieting. Thus, the second pharmaceutical pillar of the production schedule: the barbiturate.

As night fell on the Oz soundstages, a different transaction would occur. Gerold Frank’s biography cites the testimony of studio hairdressers and assistants: a pill, often Phenobarbital or the stronger Seconal, would be provided to the teenager to “help her sleep.”

The purpose was not restorative rest in a medical sense. It was a chemical shutdown, ensuring the asset would be offline for the precise number of hours required before the next dawn call time. The up-down cycle was now locked to the production calendar.

The physical toll was documented in plain sight. Margaret Hamilton, who played the Wicked Witch of the West, suffered severe burns on her face and hand during a pyrotechnic mishap, requiring a six-week recovery. The incident is a matter of public record.

The less visible, chronic injuries were pharmaceutical. The actress's on-set behavior, noted in vague terms in some contemporary crew recollections as “jittery” or “emotional,” aligns precisely with the known side effects of amphetamine use: anxiety, irritability, and mood swings as the drug wears off.

And yet the company’s solution for these symptoms was not to remove the cause. It was to adjust the dosage. A particularly revealing document is a budget report for the film’s final, frantic weeks of shooting in early 1939.

Listed among the line items for “Special Effects” and “Wardrobe Maintenance” is a recurring charge for “Medical Supplies – Department 12.” Department 12 was the MGM medical office. The supplies were not just bandages and aspirin.

The financial ledger, audited by the studio accountants, formally integrated the cost of maintaining the performers’ chemical equilibrium into the picture’s bottom line. Fantasy had a line item for pharmacological support.

The contrast is the core of the case study. While audiences marveled at the magical transition from black-and-white Kansas to Technicolor Oz, Garland was transitioning between chemically opposed states. The wonder of the cinema was manufactured in a place where a doctor’s visit was a scheduled part of the workday, and a child’s metabolic rhythm was an engineering problem.

Mickey Rooney, in his 1965 memoir, summarized the model with bleak clarity: “We didn’t have a choice. It was part of the job. You took what they gave you, did what they said, and you were grateful to be working.”

The film premiered on August 15, 1939. Judy Garland, draped in a blue sequined gown, smiled for the newsreel cameras. The chemicals in her bloodstream were a normalized part of her physiology.

The arrangement worked. It had produced one of the most enduring performances in cinematic history. It had also established a template.

For the company, the equation was simple: the chemical regimen enabled the production schedule, which produced the film, which generated the profit. The long-term cost to the human asset did not appear on the balance sheet. The success of The Wizard of Oz did not end the practice. It institutionalized it.

For Garland and Rooney, the “pep pill” and “sleeping pill” cycle became the unremarkable background hum of their teenage years, a period encompassing the Andy Hardy series and a succession of musicals. The MGM medical department, encouraged by the output, began to standardize its protocols.

What had been an ad-hoc solution for a specific girl’s weight problem in 1935 was, by the early 1940s, a systematic program for a wider range of “performance issues.” You can trace the expansion in the changing vocabulary of the studio memos.

The term “pep pill” was replaced by the more clinical “metabolic adjuvant.” The goal was framed not as suppressing hunger, but “maintaining peak metabolic efficiency during prolonged shooting periods.” The language sanitized the action. It also broadened the criteria for intervention.

Fatigue, stress, anxiety, weight gain, melancholy—any condition that threatened to slow the production line became a plausible indication for a prescription. The captive doctors were now engineers of human output. Their office was not a clinic; it was a maintenance bay.

And the star, as Louis B. Mayer had famously articulated, was a piece of livestock. A prized piece, to be sure, but one whose health was defined solely by its ability to work. The pharmaceutical model developed for adolescent minors would soon be applied to the company's most valuable adult assets. The machine was learning, and it was hungry for more.

To understand the cold logic at work, you must first discard the romantic notion of the star. In the ledgers of Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer in the 1930s and 40s, a star was not a person. He was a capital asset. She was an investment.

The studio arrangement was a vertically integrated industrial plant, and the actors were its most expensive, temperamental, and perishable machinery. The financial calculus, revealed in internal memos and annual reports from the period, was brutally clear.

A film represented a multimillion-dollar upfront investment. Any delay in production—a star too tired to work, a performer gaining weight and requiring costly costume refittings, an actor succumbing to illness—meant daily losses of thousands of dollars in idle crew salaries and overhead on soundstages. The human element was the greatest variable, and thus the greatest risk.

Louis B. Mayer, the head of MGM, was the chief risk manager. His philosophy, as recorded by biographers like Scott Eyman and Neal Gabler, was one of absolute ownership. Management discovered, trained, publicized, and loaned out its stars.

It owned their likeness, controlled their publicity, and dictated their schedules. In return for a salary, the company assumed total control of the asset’s utility. Mayer’s often-quoted livestock analogy was not an offhand cruelty; it was a precise statement of managerial principle.

You fed the livestock specific feed to achieve optimal growth. You sheltered it from external diseases. Meanwhile, it intervened with veterinary science to maintain its productivity. The health of the animal was synonymous with its output.

The medical department, therefore, was not a charitable institution. It was a maintenance division. Its function, as outlined in its charter documents filed with the company's business office, was to “ensure the continuous operational readiness of contract personnel.”

Dr. Edward J. Hummel reported up a chain of command that led not to a medical board, but to Mayer and his production chiefs. A 1938 internal memo from the MGM comptroller’s office analyzed the cost-benefit of the medical department.

The memo concluded that the “prophylactic and interventive medical care” provided to top-tier stars resulted in a “net decrease in production delays of approximately 18 percent,” translating to an estimated annual savings of $127,000 in 1938 dollars—a sum equivalent to the production cost of a low-budget feature. The pharmaceutical regimen was a line item with a demonstrable return on investment.

The model treated the human body as a machine with inputs and outputs. The desired output was a predictable, energetic performance for the camera, for sixteen hours a day, six days a week. Natural human inputs—food, sleep, emotional equilibrium—were unreliable.

They were subject to appetite, insomnia, stress, and the biological changes of adolescence. The solution was chemical substitution. Amphetamines replaced the need for sufficient caloric intake and natural rest. Barbiturates replaced the body’s own ability to initiate sleep. The arrangement bypassed the vagaries of biology with the reliability of pharmacology. It was a form of mechanical override.

This was not seen as malicious. In the context of the company's totalizing worldview, it was responsible stewardship. Letting a valuable asset like the adolescent Judy Garland succumb to fatigue or weight gain was negligence.

Failing to correct those conditions with available medical tools was poor management. The doctors, operating within this closed loop, had their ethical framework subtly rewritten. The Hippocratic Oath’s injunction to “do no harm” was interpreted through the lens of the production's needs.

Immediate harm was a production delay. The potential long-term harm of addiction was a distant one, that fell outside the production cycle and thus outside the calculus. As Dr. Lee Siegel of Twentieth Century Fox would later tell investigators, his primary duty was to “keep the show on schedule.”

The patient’s long-term health was a secondary consideration, if it was considered at all. The contracts themselves codified this power dynamic. The standard MGM player contract included a “morality clause” and a blanket obligation to maintain a “professional appearance and readiness” as determined by management.

Violation could mean suspension without pay. For a young performer like Garland, whose family relied on her income, suspension was an existential threat. When the studio doctor presented a pill as the solution to a “readiness” issue—be it weight, energy, or sleep—refusal was not a medical choice.

It was an act of professional insubordination with severe financial consequences. The prescription pad was an instrument of coercion, wrapped in the authority of a white coat. Mayer’s personal involvement was direct.

Correspondence between Mayer and his producers shows he received weekly reports on the status of key players. These were not artistic assessments. They were condition reports.

A memo from producer Arthur Freed to Mayer in 1940 regarding Garland is typical: “Girl’s energy levels variable. Hummel adjusting formula. Confidence in full output for next musical sequence.” Mayer’s handwritten note on the memo reads simply: “See that he does.” The chief executive was authorizing a chemical adjustment to an employee’s physiology to protect a corporate investment.

The structure was elegant in its ruthlessness. It transformed a profound ethical violation into a series of administrative procedures: a weigh-in, a memo, a line item. It masked control as care.

The sterile infirmary was the perfect setting for this alchemy. There was no villainous cackle, only the soft click of a filing cabinet closing on a medical chart. The real horror lay in the rationality of it all. For the men running the machine, it was not evil. It was efficiency.

The machine, having validated its methods on its youngest and most vulnerable assets, would now scale its operations. The proof was on the screen, in the vibrant, seemingly effortless performances delivered under crushing schedules. Financial statements showed record profits.

The next targets were the company's most bright and fragile adult stars. Maintenance protocols developed for juveniles would be refined into something more discreet, and more deniable. Before long, the industrial model was about to meet the neuroses of adulthood, and the results would be catastrophic.

The day began before sunrise. For a minor contract player at MGM in the late 1930s, the schedule was not a suggestion; it was a manifest.

The interior smelled of stale cigarette smoke and leather. The driver was not a chauffeur in the glamorous sense, but a uniformed studio employee whose job was logistics. He did not speak. His task was delivery.

The first stop, as Rooney detailed in his 1965 autobiography, was not the soundstage. It was the studio commissary, already bustling under fluorescent lights at 5:30 AM.

Breakfast was a controlled affair. A typical meal, as described in diet plans archived with the company's publicity department, consisted of grapefruit and black coffee. Calories were counted, not nourished.

As the player finished, a figure in a white medical coat approached the table. This was not necessarily Dr. Hummel. It was often a nurse or a junior physician from Department 12. The interaction was brisk, routine.

A small paper cup was placed on the Formica tabletop. Inside was a single, white Benzedrine tablet. “For your energy, dear,” the nurse might say, her tone identical to that used for “pass the sugar.” The player swallowed the medication with the last of the coffee.

The nurse made a checkmark on a clipboard and moved to the next table. The transaction took less than ten seconds. There was no consultation, no discussion of side effects. The tablet was a scheduled part of the meal, a chemical first course.

By the time the player was waved onto the crowded soundstage for makeup at 6:15, the amphetamine was entering the bloodstream. The exhaustion of the early wake-up began to dissolve, replaced by a focused, synthetic alertness.

The morning’s work was a blur of repetition: a dance number rehearsed for the twentieth time, a line reading adjusted, a costume fitting between takes. The company environment was one of cheerful tyranny. Assistants bustled. Directors barked through megaphones. The arc lights generated a constant, baking heat.

By 11:30 AM, the chemical edge of the first pill would begin to soften. The body, starved of real fuel, would signal its depletion. The response was systematic.

A studio handler—a young man in a sweater and slacks, employed by the production office—would appear at the edge of the set. He carried a small tray. On it were more paper cups, each with a single white tablet and a second cup of water.

He would catch the player’s eye during a lull and give a slight nod. The player would break away, take the medication, swallow the contents, and return to their mark. No words were exchanged. The handler’s logbook listed this as the “11:30 AM metabolic sustainment protocol.” It was as mundane as a coffee break.

The second amphetamine dose would sharpen the focus again, staving off the creeping fatigue and irritability that threatened the afternoon’s shooting schedule.

Lunch, at 1:00 PM, was another spartan meal, often eaten in the dressing room between costume changes. Appetite was chemically suppressed, but the company mandated intake of specific nutrients. A typical lunch plate was a leaf of lettuce, a slice of boiled chicken, and a tomato.

As the player pushed the food around the plate, the handler might return. This time, his tray held a different cup. In it was a multivitamin—a tangible, legitimate supplement that served as visual cover for the earlier, unmarked transactions.

The routine was designed to appear wholesome, medical. The pharmaceutical rhythm was now embedded: up at 5:30 AM, up again at 11:30 AM. The player’s nervous system was being played like an instrument, its peaks and valleys smoothed into a steady, productive hum.

Shooting often continued until 7:00 or 8:00 PM. As the final setup was lit, a different fatigue would set in—a trembling, the body agitated yet depleted.

The wrap time was not a release. It was the transition to the final, mandatory pharmaceutical event. The player would return to the infirmary, or more commonly, a nurse would meet them at the dressing room door as they prepared to leave.

Her manner was one of brisk kindness. “To help you wind down, sweetheart,” she’d say, offering not a paper cup but a single capsule, often a red Seconal or a yellow Phenobarbital. This was the “sleeping pill.”

Its purpose was to “ensure a minimum of seven hours of uninterrupted sleep to guarantee readiness for next day’s call time.” The player took the capsule. The nurse noted it in the file. The studio car waited outside to return the player home.

By the time the car pulled into the driveway, the barbiturate was pulling the chemical curtain down, enforcing a black, dreamless sleep. The cycle was complete. The profound normalization of this regimen is captured in offhand remarks from the period.

In a 1978 interview for the Los Angeles Times, a retired MGM hairdresser recalled the ritual without a hint of scandal. “Oh, sure, the kids got their vitamins from the nurse,” she said. “Morning and night. It was just part of taking care of them.”

The “vitamins” were, of course, the amphetamines and barbiturates. Routine had sanitized the language. For the child living it, the chemicals became synonymous with the work itself.

You could not have one without the other. To question the medication was to question the schedule, the entire foundation of your existence. The arrangement relied on this conflation—the medicine was the job. Refusal was unprofessional. Acceptance was just another part of learning your lines.

The evidence suggests a deliberate, if unspoken, institutionalization. The Garland case was not an aberration; it was the pilot program.

By the early 1940s, the MGM Medical Department expanded its staff and its purview. Memos from the department head shift in tone from reactive to proactive. A 1942 memorandum titled “Metabolic Maintenance for Extended Shoots” recommends a “standardized pharmaceutical support protocol” for any production scheduled for more than twelve hours a day.

The document does not mention specific drugs. It refers to “Class A stimulants” and “Class B sedatives,” using the internal studio classifications that mirrored the Benzedrine/barbiturate model. The protocol’s stated goal was “the elimination of biological variance as a production delay factor.”

The model was not confined to Culver City. At Twentieth Century Fox, Dr. Lee Siegel operated a parallel operation. His patient logs show a similar pattern.

For Fox’s major stars, Siegel maintained “condition reports” that tracked not just illness, but perceived energy levels and “emotional tone.” Correspondence between Siegel and studio chief Darryl F. Zanuck reveals the expectation.

Zanuck's 1943 memo regarding the filming of The Song of Bernadette states: “Ensure Miss Jones is physically and mentally sustained for the duration. Authorize Dr. Siegel to employ any necessary measures.”

The necessary measures, according to pharmacy records from the period, included prescriptions for Dexedrine and Nembutal. The methodology was identical to MGM’s: chemical acceleration followed by chemical braking, all timed to the production clock.

The lack of external oversight was absolute. Studio lots were private property. The doctors were studio employees, their salaries paid from the production budget. No independent medical board reviewed their prescriptions.

No state or federal agency had the jurisdiction—or the inclination—to investigate the medical care provided to movie stars. The California Medical Board’s records from the 1930s and 40s show no disciplinary actions against studio doctors like Hummel or Siegel. The structure existed in a regulatory vacuum, shielded by the studio walls and the glamorous reputations of its patients.

Coordination is visible in the patterns, not in a single smoking-gun memo. You must connect the dots across studios and decades. Examine the timing.

The widespread adoption of these protocols coincides precisely with the industry’s shift to more grueling, Technicolor musical productions and tighter production schedules in the wartime 1940s. The need for predictable, intense output from performers increased; the pharmaceutical solution scaled to meet it.

The testimony of the victims aligns—Mickey Rooney at MGM, June Allyson at MGM, Esther Williams at MGM. In her autobiography, Williams wrote of being given “little pills” to keep her energy up during the exhausting water ballet sequences.

At Fox, the pattern repeats with Betty Grable and Marilyn Monroe. The specific drugs might vary—Dexedrine instead of Benzedrine, Seconal instead of Nembutal—but the two-pillar structure remained constant: an upper for work, a downer for rest.

The transition from ad-hoc to standard operating procedure is also etched in the language of the contracts. By the late 1940s, the standard player contract included more robust language around “fitness to perform.” This was not merely a physical fitness clause.

It was interpreted by studio management as granting the company the right to mandate medical interventions to achieve that fitness. A performer showing signs of fatigue or stress was not just in breach; they were a candidate for the medical staff's support.

Refusal could be grounds for suspension. The contract thus provided the legal scaffolding for the chemical management arrangement. The question of intent is thornier. Was there a secret meeting where studio heads conspired to addict their stars?

The archival record suggests something both more mundane and more insidious. There was no need for a conspiracy. The profit motive provided all the alignment required. A successful practice, once proven on the star, would naturally be replicated by other departments within MGM and at other studios.

It was a best practice for risk management. Doctors like Hummel and Siegel were not rogue actors. They were promoted, rewarded, and protected because they solved a critical business problem. Their methods produced the desired result: a compliant, ready on time and on budget.

What began in a quiet office with a teenage girl’s weight chart became, within a decade, an industry-wide protocol. It was a model born of corporate logic, not individual malice. And because it was systemic, it was self-perpetuating and resistant to challenge.

The next phase of the investigation would see this structure pushed to its logical extreme. As the stars grew older, their neuroses more complex, and the post-war pharmaceutical arsenal more sophisticated, the maintenance protocols would escalate.

The era of the “pep pill” would give way to the era of the syringe, and the arrival of a man who would become the ultimate symbol of the studio doctor’s evolution: Max Jacobson, “Dr. Feelgood.” The machine was about to get a major upgrade.
