Harold’s left knee predicted rain by noon. It was a dull, familiar ache, like a tooth that remembered every cavity. He leaned against the chipped cinderblock wall of his office and watched the dust devils twist across the empty parking lot. Not a single car had pulled into Heavenly Suds in three days. The sign out front, which once boasted a smiling cloud with soap bubbles for cheeks, now just showed a sad, sun-bleached smear.

The strip mall baked. To his left, Pawn Stars & Stripes displayed a pyramid of outdated VCRs in the window. To his right, The Last Rites Chapel had its neon cross flickering on the fritz, buzzing like a trapped hornet. Harold’s own place sat in the middle, smelling of stale citrus cleaner and the faint, metallic tang of regret. He’d mixed the last batch of suds himself that morning, dumping in the generic concentrate and then, because his knuckles were cracked and weeping from the eczema, a generous glob of his prescription cream. The tube said for external use only. Harold figured a car wash was external enough.

A minivan turned in. It was a miracle.

Harold pushed off the wall, his knee giving a sharp twinge that meant the rain would arrive early. The van was beige, dust-coated, and held a family of four. The father rolled down the window, his face a mask of road-weary exasperation.

“The deluxe,” the man said, thrusting a crumpled ten-dollar bill through the window.

Harold took the money. “Deluxe it is. Pull forward ‘til the light turns green.”

He trudged to the control booth, a hotbox of peeling laminate and a single buzzing fan. He started the cycle. The machinery groaned to life—the rumble of the track, the hiss of the pre-soak spray. Harold watched through the streaked window as the van crept into the dark maw of the wash bay.

The soap cycle began. Thick, pearlescent suds cascaded over the van’s roof. It was his new formula, the one with the cream. It looked richer than usual, almost creamy. Harold made a mental note to write down the ratio. Then the van stopped moving.

The track was still running. The rollers should have been pushing it forward. But the van just sat there, halfway through the arch of soap nozzles.

“Hell,” Harold muttered. Another mechanical failure. He was about to hit the emergency stop when he saw the driver’s side door swing open.

The father got out. He didn’t slip on the wet floor. He didn’t curse. He stepped into the downpour of suds, tilted his head back, and spread his arms. His mouth opened in a soundless O. Then he began to cry. Not sad crying. This was the weepy, joyous kind, the kind Harold hadn’t seen since his wife packed her suitcase and announced she was leaving to become one with a cumulonimbus formation over Sedona.

Harold froze, his finger hovering over the big red button.

From the van’s sliding door, the mother emerged. Then a teenage boy. Then a little girl in pigtails. They all stood in the soapfall, embracing, laughing through their tears. The father shouted something lost to the machinery’s roar. He pointed upward, into the foam-speckled gloom of the wash bay ceiling.

Then his feet left the ground.

Not a lot. Just an inch. Maybe two.

He hovered. The soap rinsed, switching to the spot-free cycle. The man drifted back down, his socks making a soft plop on the wet concrete. His family clustered around him, touching his arms as if to make sure he was real. They were all drenched, beaming, transformed. They got back into the van, silent now, and the track carried them through the final blower. When they emerged into the blistering sun, the van glistened. The father rolled down his window. He looked at Harold in the booth. His eyes were red, his face serene.

“Brother,” he called out, his voice thick. “That was… transcendent.”

He drove off, leaving a trail of clean water on the asphalt.

Harold stared at the empty bay. The machinery clanked to a standstill. The only sound was the fan’s whine and the distant, erratic buzz of the chapel’s cross.

His knee throbbed. Rain, definitely.

The next customer arrived twenty minutes later. A college kid in a rusty hatchback. Harold took his five dollars for the basic wash and sent him through, heart thumping a weird rhythm against his ribs. He watched, knuckles pressed to the glass.

The same thing happened. The kid got out during the soap cycle. He didn’t float, but he sank to his knees, sobbing about the interconnected beauty of all living things. He tried to hug one of the rotating brushes.

Harold killed the cycle early. The kid drove away, damp and quoting Rumi.

By the time the third car pulled in—a real estate agent’s Lexus—Harold had a theory. It was the soap. It had to be. Something in his eczema cream had reacted with the chemicals. He’d used that cream for years. It was just cortisone and aloe and a bunch of unpronounceable moisturizers. But he’d also, last week, knocked over a gallon jug behind the chapel. The jug had been labeled “Holy Water (For Topical Use Only).” He’d mopped most of it up, but some had sloshed into the drain that fed his recycling tank. He’d meant to flush the system. He’d forgotten.

Holy water, cortisone, aloe, and a dash of industrial surfactant.

He ran the Lexus through. The agent, a woman in a linen blazer, emerged from her car at the exact midpoint of the soap arch. She didn’t cry. She removed her jacket, then her shoes, and began waltzing with the spray arm. She had a beautiful, practiced grace. When the rinse cycle hit, she came back to herself, blinking. She gathered her things, got in her car, and paid Harold double on her way out.

“I’ve been searching for that my whole life,” she said, her voice hushed. “What do you call it?”

“The… Deluxe,” Harold said.

Word spread. Not like wildfire—like a slow, steady seep. By the end of the week, Harold had a line. Cars idled in the lot, spilling onto the access road. People waited for hours in the Arizona heat. He had to raise his prices just to manage the demand. He mixed new batches of soap, carefully stirring in a dollop of his cream. The effect was consistent: a sixty-second window during the soap cycle where people experienced a profound, blissful connection to the universe. Some saw lights. Some heard music. One man claimed to have finally understood the plot of Twin Peaks.

Harold was too busy to be baffled. Money, real money, was piling up in the cash box. He bought a new fan for the booth. He fixed the smiling cloud sign. His knee still ached, but the pain felt different now—like an old friend reminding him of the forecast, not a jailer.

Then the pamphlets started appearing.

They were slick, professional jobs. The Church of the Cosmic Cleanse: Find Your Rapture in the Ritual. They featured a stock photo of a glowing sunrise that looked suspiciously like a soap bubble. Harold found them tucked under windshield wipers in his lot. He found them stuffed in his office door. They talked about “the sacred suds” and “the baptism of the brushless.”

On Friday, a van pulled in that wasn’t a customer. It was matte black, with no windows. A man and a woman got out. They wore matching khaki vests covered in pockets. The man had a clipboard. The woman had a grim, set mouth and carried a metal case.

They bypassed the line, walking straight to Harold’s booth. The woman knocked on the doorframe. Her nametag said “EPA.”

“Harold Jenkins?” she asked, though it wasn’t really a question. “We’ve had reports concerning your runoff water. Unusual chemical readings from the county treatment plant. Mind if we take a sample of your cleaning solution?”

Harold’s mouth went dry. He glanced at the line of cars, at a woman in a station wagon who was meditating in her driver’s seat, preparing for her wash. He thought of the holy water, the prescription cream. He thought of the money.

“It’s just soap,” he said.

“We’ll be the judge of that,” the man with the clipboard said, already heading toward the storage tanks.

The woman stayed, her eyes scanning the operation. “Also, we’ve been contacted by a… religious group. They’re filing for a cease and desist. Claiming you’ve commercialized a sacrament.” A faint, humorless smile touched her lips. “First time for everything.”

Harold watched the EPA man unscrew the cap on his main soap drum. The twist in his gut wasn’t fear, not exactly. It was the feeling of watching something simple—a way to clean cars, a way to stop his skin from itching—slide out of his hands and into a world of clipboards and lawsuits and cosmic copyright.

A sleek, sensible sedan pulled into the lot then, not joining the line but parking neatly in front of the chapel. A woman got out. She wore a severe, grey dress and clutched one of the glossy pamphlets to her chest. She didn’t look at the cars waiting. She looked directly at Harold, her gaze piercing even across the hot asphalt. She began walking toward his booth, each step precise and deliberate.

The EPA woman followed his look. “Friend of yours?”

Harold’s knee gave a sudden, vicious pang. It wasn’t rain this time. It was a deep, bone-deep throb that spoke of turning points, of systems in collision, of a storm with no clouds. He reached down and rubbed the joint, his fingers finding the familiar, scaly patches through his pants.

“No,” he said, watching the stern woman advance. “I don’t think she’s here for a wash.”

The woman in grey reached the booth just as the EPA man called out, “Got a sample!” He held up a glass vial filled with Harold’s pearlescent goop.

“Harold Jenkins,” the grey woman stated. Her voice was a dry, crisp thing, like pages turning in a legal binder. “I am Sister Constance Prudence of the Church of the Cosmic Cleanse. You are in violation of divine copyright.”

Harold blinked. “Divine what?”

“The experience you are selling,” she said, tapping the pamphlet against her palm. “The sixty-second epiphany. The somatic unburdening. The… foamy grace. It is a registered sacrament of our faith. Our lawyers have determined your operation constitutes willful infringement.” She produced a sheaf of papers from a fold in her dress. “This is a cease and desist. You will halt all commercial use of the ritual immediately and surrender any proprietary liturgical materials.”

The EPA woman, whose nametag read ‘L. GARRETT’, looked from the vial to Sister Constance. “You’ve got a trademark on a religious experience?”

“A very specific one. The combination of pressurized aqueous solution, sonic vibration from the machinery, and a proprietary blend of spiritually resonant compounds.” Sister Constance’s eyes narrowed at the vial. “Which you appear to be removing from the premises without the Church’s consent.”

The EPA man, ‘R. DAVIS’, tucked the vial into his case. “Ma’am, this is a federal environmental investigation. Step aside.”

“The sanctity of the suds is a matter of celestial jurisdiction.”

Harold’s head swam. The line of cars had begun to honk. A man in a pickup leaned out his window and yelled, “I drove from Tucson! Is this the place with the God-soap?”

“See?” Harold said, gesturing weakly at the lot. “It’s just a car wash. With a… special additive. For shine.”

Sister Constance peered past him into the booth, her gaze landing on the half-empty tube of prescription cream on the counter, next to a grease-stained invoice. Her breath hitched. “Is that… the chrism?”

“It’s for my skin,” Harold said, moving to block her view. His knee screamed.

“Unbelievable,” Davis muttered, scribbling on his clipboard. “We’ve got a 304-B violation here for sure. Unpermitted discharge of… whatever this is.” He snapped the case shut. “We’re going to need to shut you down, Mr. Jenkins. Until we can run a full analysis.”

“Shut him down?” Sister Constance’s composure cracked for a second, revealing a flash of pure, avaricious panic. “You can’t! The source must be preserved! It must be studied, replicated in a controlled, devotional setting!”

“Controlled is the key word, sister. This stuff is going into the watershed.”

They began arguing over his head, their voices climbing over the honks and the rumble of a Civic going through the wash. Harold felt a familiar tightening in his throat, the same one he got when his ex-wife explained how her new life as a “vapor-form empath” required all the equity in the house. This was too many people. This was too much talking.

He did the only thing he could think of. He hit the big green button on the console.

The machinery roared back to life for the next car in line—a Volvo station wagon covered in kid-sized soccer stickers. The track clanked. The spray arches hissed.

“What are you doing?” Garrett demanded.

“Customer’s waiting,” Harold said, his voice flat. “Paid for the deluxe.”

He watched, all of them watched, as the Volvo crept into the soap bay. The pre-soak sprayed. Then the thick, cream-infused suds began to fall. The driver, a harried-looking woman with a messy bun, had her window rolled down. A glob of foam landed on her forearm.

Her head snapped toward it. She stared at the bubble as it slid down her skin.

Then she opened the car door.

“Oh, not again,” Harold muttered.

She didn’t get out. She simply sat there, one foot on the wet concrete, and began to laugh. It started as a giggle, then built into a full, shoulder-shaking howl of delight. She pointed at the rotating brush. “It’s a sunflower!” she shouted to no one. “A giant, happy sunflower!”

Her kids in the backseat started crying. Not the sad kind. The joyous, hiccuping kind.

Sister Constance gasped, her hand flying to her chest. “Witness. Pure, unmediated witness!”

Davis just looked baffled. “Is she… is she high?”

“She’s elevated,” Sister Constance corrected, her eyes gleaming. “She has achieved foam-consciousness.”

The woman in the Volvo tried to high-five the brush. The track carried her forward, her arm still outstretched, into the rinse cycle. The water hit her. She blinked, shook her head like a dog, looked at her dripping hand with utter confusion, and pulled her foot back inside just before the car entered the blower.

Garrett turned to Harold. “What is in that soap?”

“It’s a trade secret,” Sister Constance declared, stepping between them. “A holy trade secret.”

“It’s cortisone and holy water!” Harold snapped, the words bursting out of him. The silence that followed was immediate, broken only by the distant thump-thump-thump of the blower drying the Volvo.

All three of them stared at him.

Davis opened his case and looked at the vial. “Holy water. You mean, like, from a church?”

“From the chapel next door. It spilled. And my eczema cream.” Harold rubbed his forehead. “The tube says for external use only. Cars are external.”

Sister Constance’s face underwent a rapid transformation. The legal haughtiness melted away, replaced by the fervent calculation of a miner who’d just found the mother lode. “A sacrament born of dermatological necessity and liturgical runoff. It’s… it’s perfect. It’s accessible. It’s affordable grace.” She grabbed Harold’s arm. “You must come with me. We need to document the exact ratios. The specific strain of your eczema is crucial!”

Garrett held up a hand. “Nobody is going anywhere. We’re impounding the tank.” She nodded to Davis, who started toward the large soap drum with a roll of bright yellow “CONDEMNED” tape.

“You will not desecrate the font!” Sister Constance yelled, and to Harold’s horror, she marched after Davis, her grey dress swishing.

The line of cars was now a solid block of idling metal and impatient humanity. A man in a convertible stood on his seat. “Hey! Is this the line for the enlightenment?”

Harold’s knee gave a final, catastrophic throb. He grabbed the edge of the console to steady himself. The world narrowed to the feeling of brittle bone, the smell of hot electronics and wet asphalt, the sight of the EPA agent and the cult leader squabbling next to a vat of miracle soap. His miracle soap. His stupid, accidental, bankrupting miracle.

He limped out of the booth. The sun hit him like a physical weight. He ignored Garrett calling his name, ignored the honking, and walked straight toward the storage tanks.

Davis was trying to wrap the tape around the drum’s valve. Sister Constance was batting at his hands. “You philistine! That is not a contaminant, it is a consecrated compound!”

“It’s an unregulated chemical discharge!”

“It’s the answer to existential dread!”

Harold reached them. He didn’t say a word. He bent down—his knee popping like a firecracker—and picked up the length of loose hose connected to the recycling tank drain. He pointed the nozzle at the open top of the soap drum.

“What are you doing?” Davis asked.

“Cleaning up,” Harold said.

He opened the valve.

A jet of filthy, recycled water, the dregs from a hundred washes, shot into the drum. It mixed with the holy suds, diluting the creamy mixture into a murky, grey-brown slurry. He kept it going, hosing it down, swirling the contents until the pearlescent sheen was gone, replaced by the color of dishwater and regret.

“No!” Sister Constance shrieked, lunging for the hose. Harold sidestepped, his bad leg buckling slightly. She stumbled, her hand coming down on the wet rim of the drum. A splash of the diluted goop landed on her wrist.

She froze.

Davis, trying to pull her back, got a spatter across his khaki vest.

They both stopped. Their arguing died in their throats.

A strange calm settled over Sister Constance’s face. The frantic zeal drained away. She looked at the grey water on her skin, then at the buzzing neon cross of the chapel next door. “Oh,” she said softly. “It’s just… water.”

Davis blinked, looking at his vest. He touched the wet spot. His stern, bureaucratic expression softened into something like wonder. “My… my daughter has eczema. On her elbows. It must be terrible.”

They looked at each other. Not with anger, but with a profound, shared confusion.

Harold shut off the valve. The sudden silence was heavy. The honking had stopped. The line of cars was watching, a silent audience to the anticlimax.

“It’s all just… stuff,” Harold said, dropping the hose. “Mixed together wrong. Or right. I don’t know.” He rubbed his knee. “The magic’s gone. Diluted. You can test it all you want now. It’ll just be soap and water and a little cream.”

Garrett walked over, her eyes on her partner’s dazed face. “Davis? You okay?”

“I think I should call my daughter,” he said, his voice distant.

Sister Constance sat down on an overturned bucket, her grey dress soaking up a puddle. She looked deflated, empty. The pamphlets in her hand were ruined, the glossy paper bleeding ink. “We had a newsletter ready,” she murmured. “A subscription model.”

One by one, the cars in line began to pull away. The man from Tucson shouted, “Rip-off!” before peeling out. The lot emptied, leaving only oil stains and the shimmer of heat on asphalt.

Harold limped back to his booth. He took the cash box, heavy with the week’s absurd profits, and placed it on the counter. He looked at the tube of cream. He picked it up, squeezed a dot onto his cracked knuckle, and rubbed it in. It felt cool. It felt ordinary.

Garrett appeared at the door. “We’ll still need to file a report. And you’ll need a permit for any future… special additives.”

“Just the standard stuff from now on,” Harold said. “Promise.”

She gave a short nod, then went to collect her partner, who was now on his phone, saying, “No, honey, it’s okay. I just wanted to hear your voice.”

Sister Constance remained on the bucket, staring at the condemned drum. Harold walked over and handed her a clean shop rag. She took it without looking at him, dabbed at her wrist.

“What do I do now?” she asked, not really asking him.

“I hear the chapel’s looking for volunteers to fix their neon,” Harold said.

He left her there and went back inside. The booth was hot, the new fan whirring pointlessly. He sat on his stool. Through the streaked window, he watched Garrett and Davis drive away in their sensible sedan. He watched Sister Constance finally stand, wring the rag, and walk slowly toward the flickering cross next door.

His knee still ached. The forecast was unchanged. Rain by evening, maybe.

A single car turned into the lot. A dusty sedan. It pulled up to the pay station. Harold didn’t move. The driver, an old man with a face like a dried apple, got out and shuffled to the booth window. He peered in.

“Basic wash still five dollars?” he asked, his voice raspy.

Harold looked at him. No expectation of rapture. No hope for transcendence. Just a dirty car.

“Yeah,” Harold said. “Five dollars.”

He took the bill, a worn Lincoln folded twice. He pointed to the green light. “Pull forward.”

The machinery groaned the same familiar groan. Harold watched the old sedan creep into the wash. Soap, rinse, wax. No one got out. No one floated. The car emerged, dripping, marginally cleaner than before. It drove off.

Harold leaned back. The empty lot stretched before him, baking in the sun. The sign with the smiling cloud seemed to grin its stupid, sun-bleached grin. He put the five-dollar bill in the now-light cash box. He picked up his tube of cream, considered it, and tossed it into the trash.

It landed with a soft, final plop. Just stuff. Mixed together wrong. Or right.

Outside, the first drop of rain hit the dusty asphalt. It made a dark, perfect circle. Then another. Then a thousand more, washing it all away.
