The financial collapse of Spanish West Florida was not a slow leak, but a severed artery. The situado—the annual subsidy from the royal treasury in Mexico City meant to fund the colony—stopped arriving in 1808. The lifeline was cut. Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus’s dispatches to his superiors in Pensacola and Havana, preserved in the Spanish colonial archives, shifted in tone from administrative formality to desperate pleading. He itemized the consequences: soldiers unpaid for over a year, their uniforms in tatters; the fort’s wooden palisades rotting and unrepaired; the powder in the magazine dangerously caked with humidity. His garrison, he reported, numbered fewer than fifty effectives. These men were not the proud defenders of an empire, but stranded, destitute conscripts whose loyalty frayed with each missed meal. Delassus had no money for provisions, for repairs, or even for the couriers carrying his own increasingly frantic letters.

Fort San Carlos, perched on the Mississippi bluffs was less a military installation than a monument to imperial overreach. A visitor in the summer of 1810, as described in later accounts by American settlers would have seen a small, square stockade of hewn timber. The blockhouse was a simple two-story affair, its upper loopholes offering a sweeping view of the river it could no longer control. The dry moat was choked with weeds. A air smelled of damp wood, mildew, and the slow decay of neglected authority. The Spanish flag hung limp in the humid air, a symbol whose power had bled away peso by absent peso.

The fiscal paralysis reached every function of government. The colonial court in Baton Rouge, which had once moved with a leisurely, bureaucratic pace, now ground to a halt. Without funds for clerks or judges, cases languished. Land grant applications from the swelling population of Anglo settlers piled up unanswered, creating a backlog of grievance and uncertainty. The Spanish legal system, opaque and corrupt at the best of times became utterly inert. For planters whose wealth was tied up in claims to thousands of acres, this was more than an inconvenience. It was an existential threat. A man’s title was his livelihood, and the government that was supposed to guarantee it had ceased to operate.

Delassus understood his position with a clarity that bordered on despair. His correspondence reveals a man performing the rituals of power long after the substance had evaporated. He hosted formal dinners for the leading planters at the Government House—elaborate affairs with silver and fine linens, as recalled in later memoirs—using his personal resources to maintain a facade of normalcy. These “peace dinners” were acts of political theater. He toasted the health of King Ferdinand VII, a monarch held captive by Napoleon, while his guests, men like John Rhea and Fulwar Skipwith, smiled and drank his wine. They discussed crop yields and river traffic, but the unspoken subject was the vacuum in the room. Delassus playyed host in a house that was already, in every meaningful sense, theirs.

The governor’s strategy, as he confided to his superior in Pensacola, Vicente Folch was one of calculated concession. He would grant every minor request, avoid any provocation, and hope the storm of rebellion would pass or that reinforcements would arrive from Havana. It was a policy of delay, rooted in the knowledge that he commanded a phantom garrison. His soldiers, when they could be mustered were a pitiable sight. An American militiaman who saw them that summer noted their “motley and wretched appearance,” their coats patched, their shoes worn through. They were farmers’ sons from Cuba or Mexico, stranded thousands of miles from home in a land whose language they did not speak, surrounded by a population that viewed them with contempt or pity. Their morale was not broken; it had never been built.

This material decay had a direct, physical consequence for security. The fort’s magazine held a dwindling supply of black powder, compromised by the humid Gulf air. Its cannons, a handful of small field pieces were undermanned and rarely drilled. The palisade walls, their logs softened by rot would not stop a determined rush. Delassus knew this. His intelligence network, such as it was brought him warnings. James Mather, a prominent settler came to him that summer with a blunt report: a convention bed organized, and its purpose was to overthrow his government. Delassus thanked Mather for his loyalty. He then doubled the guard—a gesture as symbolic as it was militarily useless. He had no other moves to make.

The boiling point in Baton Rouge was not reached in a dramatic confrontation, but in a slow, simmer. It was the sound of a planter’s fist hitting a table in frustration over a land title that would never be signed. The sight of a Spanish soldier bartering his brass buttons for a loaf of bread. The stillness from Havana, where colonial administrators were preoccupied with their own survival. By the time the delegates gathered at St. John’s Plains in July of 1810, Spanish authority had already evaporated. All that remained was the ceremony of its burial, and the question of who would inherit the keys to the empty fort.

For the Anglo-American planters of the Feliciana parishes, the Spanish system was not foreign. It was, in their view, economically irrational. Their primary grievance, repeated in their petitions and later in their convention resolutions was the land title. Under Spanish law, a settler could receive a grant, but the process was Byzantine. It required multiple approvals from distant officials in Pensacola or Havana, each layer an opportunity for delay or for a palm to be greased. The final title, or título was often conditional, subject to vague obligations and vulnerable to challenge. This created a cloud over every acre of improved land. As John Rhea and his neighbors saw it, a man’s property was not truly his if a bureaucrat in Cuba could, in theory, revoke it.

The legal framework itself was a source of constant friction. Spanish civil law, based on the Napoleonic Code operated on principles alien to men raised on English common law. Inheritance, debt collection, and commercial contracts all followed different rules. The court in Baton Rouge conducted its business in Spanish, a language few of the planters bothered to learn. They depended on interpreters, an arrangement ripe for misunderstanding and corruption. A lawsuit became a gamble, its outcome determined less by precedent or merit than by the mood of a Spanish judge or the influence of a well-connected interpreter. For a community whose wealth was increasingly measured in acres, slaves, and cotton bales, this legal uncertainty was intolerable. Risk was one thing; capriciousness was another.

The administrative collapse detailed in Delassus’s dispatches translated directly into economic paralysis for the planters. With the situado cut off, the local government could no longer perform even basic functions. Roads between plantations fell into disrepair. The rudimentary postal service faltered. There was no capital for public improvements—no clearing of river snags that impeded cotton shipments, no support for the levees that protected their fields. The planters builded an agrarian capitalist empire in the rich bottomlands, but they doed it atop a colonial state that provided no services and, in their eyes, actively obstructed progress. They taxxed themselves through informal levies to fund local needs, a practice that only underscored the redundancy of Spanish rule.

Beneath these practical complaints lay a deeper, cultural conviction. The Anglo settlers, as documented in their own letters and later memoirs, viewed the Spanish colonial model as fundamentally decadent and static. They saw a system built on patronage, hierarchy, and personal loyalty, not on industry, contract, and individual enterprise. The Spanish ideal of an ordered, Catholic society held no appeal for these largely Protestant, fiercely independent migrants from Georgia, the Carolinas, and Tennessee. Were not subjects seeking inclusion; they were conquerors awaiting their moment. The term “filibuster,” which would later define their more aggressive neighbors, hovered at the edges of their self-conception. Were men of action governing themselves in all but name, chafing under the ceremonial authority of a dying regime.

This cultural and economic alienation found a precise political expression in the summer of 1810. The convention at St. John’s Plains was not, at its inception, a revolutionary body. Its initial stated purpose, recorded in its early proceedings was to “assist” Governor Delassus in maintaining order, given his weakened state. This was a polite fiction, a tactical nod to legal form. The planters’ real aim, as their subsequent actions proved was to create a parallel, de facto government that could do what the Spanish could not: provide clear land titles, enforce predictable laws, and secure their property against external threat. They wanted a government that operated in English, respected common law traditions, and served their commercial interests.

The key figures who emerged—John Rhea, Fulwar Skipwith, Philemon Thomas—were not landless adventurers. Were established men with significant capital at risk. Rhea was a major landholder and a practiced political operator. Skipwith, a Virginia-born diplomat who had lost a fortune in France rebuilded his life on a Feliciana plantation. Thomas was a Kentucky-born veteran and planter. Their stake was not in chaos, but in stability—a stability of their own making. For them, Spanish rule was not merely incompetent. The single remaining barrier between their volatile, prosperous community and the sovereign control of its own destiny. The decaying fort on the bluffs was not a protection. A lock on a door they were ready to break down.

The final, catalytic grievance was security. With Spain engulfed by war in Europe, the colony was defenseless. The planters feared, with justification, that West Florida could be seized by Britain, still a rival in the Gulf, or even by France. Their slaves, who outnumbered the white population in the Feliciana district represented both their wealth and their deepest anxiety. A powerless Spanish garrison offered no protection against external invasion or internal uprising. The planters had formed their own militia companies for this very reason. Were already providing their own defense. The logical next step, as their correspondence shows they debated was to provide their own sovereignty, thereby gaining the legitimacy to treat with the United States for permanent protection.

The boiling point, then was not a sudden surge of revolutionary fervor. The culmination of a cold calculation. Spanish authority had failed them legally, administratively, and militarily. It offered no path to greater prosperity or security. Every day it persisted was a day their assets—their land, their crops, their human property—remained under a cloud. The rebellion, when it came would be dressed in the language of natural rights and liberty, as articulated in their September declaration. But its engine, as the economic records and land deeds of the participants make clear was the practical ambition of a propertied class. Were not throwing off a yoke. removed a failed business partner.

Carlos Dehault Delassus arrived in Baton Rouge in the spring of 1810, a man stepping into a role that had already been hollowed out. He replaced Don Carlos de Grand-Pré, a governor who had, according to the archival record of their correspondence, grown weary and ineffective. Delassus was a career officer, a lieutenant colonel in the Spanish army. His personal papers, fragments of which survive, suggest a man of routine and formality, accustomed to the hierarchies of a functioning empire. What he found was a ghost of one.

His garrison, as he reported in his first dispatch to Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, consisted of twenty-eight to forty troops. The exact number seemed to shift with each report, as sickness and desertion took their toll. These were not front-line infantry. Were a mix of aging veterans and young conscripts from Cuba, posted to the furthest, wettest edge of New Spain. A muster roll from the period, preserved in the Spanish military archives, lists their names and origins: Manuel García, carpenter, from Havana; José Martínez, farmer, from Puebla. Were tradesmen and peasants, not soldiers. Their weapons were obsolete, their powder damp, their pay years in arrears. Delassus commanded a phantom army.

The governor’s residence, the so-called Government House was a modest structure compared to the great plantation homes rising in the Felicianas. An administrative shell. Delassus’s authority extended no further than the sound of his own voice. Beyond the stockade, the population was overwhelmingly Anglo-American. Spanish subjects numbered perhaps 1,400 in the entire district, while American settlers and their slaves totaled over 6,000. The demographic reality was a reverse siege. Delassus was the one surrounded.

His strategy, as revealed in his letters to Folch was one of appeasement and theater. He understood that any show of force would be not only futile but provocative. He invested his energy in maintaining the illusion of control. This is where the “peace dinners” originated. Delassus used his personal funds to host elaborate meals for the leading planters. He served fine wine brought upriver from New Orleans. A table was set with silver. The conversation, as recalled by attendees like Fulwar Skipwith in later accounts was cordial, even jovial. They discussed cotton prices and river pilots. They toasted the health of the captive King Ferdinand VII. The performance was one of mutual respect and normalcy.

But the subtext was the vacuum. Delassus knew his guests were the men organizing the convention at St. John’s Plains. He knew, because some of them told him. James Mather, a planter with ties to both sides came to him that summer with an explicit warning. A convention formed, Mather said, and its intent was to take control of the district. Delassus thanked him for his loyalty. He then doubled the guard at the fort—a gesture as symbolic as a raised eyebrow. He had no other cards to play. His dispatches to Pensacola and Havana became a chronicle of impotence. He requested troops, money, and instructions. The replies, when they came offered only vague assurances and excuses. The empire had turned its back.

Delassus was not a tyrant. The historical record shows no acts of cruelty or repression attributed to him. He was, instead, a bureaucrat stranded by history. His personal predicament mirrored Spain’s. His king was a prisoner in France. His funding had evaporated. His orders were to hold a territory that his government could no longer afford to keep. He administered a bankruptcy.

His relationship with the convention delegates was a delicate minuet. When the convention sent its initial resolution in July, offering to “assist” him in governing, Delassus accepted it with public gratitude. He recognized the language for what it was: a polite ultimatum. He played along, hoping to buy time for reinforcements that would never come. In private, he confided to Folch that he was merely “accommodating the storm.” He was a man in a small boat, trying to steer through a hurricane by adjusting the sails.

The governor’s physical isolation was compounded by a professional one. He was a French-born officer serving a Spanish crown, ruling over English-speaking planters in an American landscape. Was a man of three worlds, and at home in none. His command of English was likely poor; his correspondence is all in Spanish. He relied on interpreters, placing him at a constant disadvantage. Every conversation was filtered, every nuance potentially lost. He governed through a veil.

By September, the pretense of the peace dinners wearred thin. The convention had declared its intent to meet continuously. Armed militia companies drilled openly in the settlements. Delassus received a second, more urgent warning about an imminent attack on his fort. Again, he could only double the guard. He did not arrest the conspirators. He did not call out his garrison. He lacked the strength for confrontation. His only remaining power was the power to surrender gracefully.

On the night of September 22, 1810, Delassus retired to his quarters in the blockhouse. His few dozen soldiers were at their posts. The flag of Spain still flew over the palisade. But the governor reading the latest reports by candlelight, must have known the performance was nearly over. Was the last actor on a stage where the audience had already rewritten the play. His role was to await his cue for exit. Outside, in the damp darkness, the armed planters of West Florida movved toward the fort, not as subjects, but as authors of his final scene.

The governor's dispatches from Baton Rouge did not vanish into a void. They traveled east, by courier and ship, to the desk of Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola. Folch commanded the remainder of Spanish West Florida, a stretch of coast and pine forest from the Pearl River to the Perdido. His fortress was stronger, his garrison larger, his situation less immediately desperate. The archival chain of correspondence between the two men, preserved in the Spanish colonial records, forms a case study in imperial paralysis.

Folch received Delassus’s first urgent request for reinforcements in early summer. The letters, as filed in the Legajo de Florida in Seville are methodical. Delassus listed his troop strength, his deficits in powder and shot, the swelling numbers and brazen confidence of the Anglo settlers. He described the convention at St. John’s Plains not as a nuisance, but as a rival government in embryo. Folch’s initial replies, dated weeks later were not dismissive. Were bureaucratic. He acknowledged the gravity of the situation. He promised to forward the requests to his own superiors in Havana. He advised patience and prudence.

But Folch had his own crisis. Pensacola was also starved of funds, its situado interrupted. His effective force was perhaps two hundred men, enough to hold his own fort but insufficient to split for a rescue mission two hundred miles to the west. More importantly, Folch’s strategic calculus differed from Delassus’s tactical plea. As he wrote to the Captain-General in Cuba in August, a relief expedition to Baton Rouge would be a profound risk. The overland route was a trackless wilderness, vulnerable to ambush by the militias Delassus feared. A sea route would require shipping he did not have, and would expose any vessel to the British warships that prowled the Gulf, ever-ready to snap up a Spanish prize. To send his limited troops into that gauntlet, for what he began to suspect was a lost cause seemed not difficult, but foolish.

The appeals from Baton Rouge grew more frantic through August and into September. Delassus reported the peace dinners, the open drilling of militia, the second warning of attack. Folch’s responses grew shorter, their tone shifting from reassurance to a grim realism. Had written to Havana, he said. Had asked for men and money. No answer had come. The empire’s lines of supply and command were not stretched; they were severed. The war in Spain consumed all attention, all resources. West Florida was a ledger entry marked for closure.

In mid-September, Folch made a decision. He would not send troops. He would not sail to Baton Rouge. He would, as he recorded in a lengthy dispatch to Havana, “await developments.” His reasoning, laid out in cold prose was that any expedition would arrive too late to prevent a rebellion that was already a fait accompli. The American settlers, he wrote were too numerous, too organized, and too convinced of their own right to the land. Spanish authority there had already collapsed in everything but name. To pour scarce soldiers into that maw would be to sacrifice Pensacola for a corpse. A relief expedition, he concluded was “useless.”

This word, inútil, appears in his final assessment. It was not a judgment on Delassus’s efforts. A strategic verdict. The empire could not hold every outpost. Baton Rouge was expendable.

Folch did not abandon his colleague entirely. He sent letters of his own to the convention delegates, urging moderation and reminding them of their duties as Spanish subjects. These letters, received in St. John’s Plains in late September were met with polite disregard. Were artifacts from a power whose will to enforce its words had demonstrably expired.

The chain of inaction extended beyond Pensacola to Havana and ultimately to Madrid. The Captain-General of Cuba, the Marqués de Someruelos, faced a cascade of crises: slave revolts, British threats, and the political chaos of the Peninsular War. The file on West Florida was one of many. His replies to Folch, when they came, authorized him to use his discretion. They offered no resources. The message was one of tacit delegation of failure. Madrid, under the shadow of Napoleon had no instructions to give to a governor on the Mississippi bluffs. The hush from the center was absolute.

Thus, Baton Rouge was not merely neglected. Strategically written off. Delassus’s pleas were processed through a hierarchy that had already accepted the loss. Each layer of administration—Folch in Pensacola, Someruelos in Havana, the paralyzed junta in Spain—passed the responsibility upward until it dissipated into the fog of a global war. One result was a perfect isolation. Delassus was not unsupported. Was formally, if quietly, sacrificed to preserve the remnants of Spanish power elsewhere.

When the armed planters moved on Fort San Carlos in the predawn dark of September 23, they were not attacking the Spanish Empire. seizzed an asset that empire had already abandoned. The shots fired at the gate were not the opening volley of a war, but the final administrative act in a long, silent withdrawal. Folch, in Pensacola, learned of the fort’s fall days later. His reaction, as recorded in his subsequent report was one of resignation, not surprise. Had made his calculation. Delassus had lived with its consequences. The flag that was lowered that morning had been lowered in the mind of the Spanish bureaucracy weeks before.

The collapse in Baton Rouge was not observed with indifference from the north. In Washington, President James Madison and his cabinet monitored the disintegration of Spanish West Florida with a focused, strategic interest. The United States had an existing claim to the territory, one asserted by Thomas Jefferson at the time of the Louisiana Purchase in 1803 and maintained by Madison. The American position, articulated in diplomatic notes to Spain and Britain held that West Florida, as far east as the Perdido River had been part of the French colony of Louisiana and was therefore included in the sale. Spain vigorously rejected this interpretation, and for seven years the issue remained a simmering diplomatic dispute. The rebellion of 1810 presented Madison not with a problem, but with a long-awaited opportunity.

The administration’s posture, however was one of official disapproval toward the methods of frontier revolution. This was a lesson learned from the Kemper brothers. In 1804, Reuben, Nathan, and Samuel Kemper had led a small, abortive filibuster into Spanish territory, attempting to stir a revolt. The effort failed, but it provoked an international incident. Jefferson’s administration, as State Department records show, publicly denounced the Kempers as outlaws and disavowed any connection to their plot. The policy was one of strict neutrality, at least in public pronouncements. The message to ambitious settlers was clear: do not expect the United States Army to rescue your private invasion.

Yet the archival trail reveals a more nuanced reality. While disavowing the Kempers, the American government never relinquished its legal claim to the territory. Madison, first as Secretary of State and then as President kept the West Florida issue active in diplomatic channels. His instructions to American agents in New Orleans, preserved in the National Archives, consistently emphasized the Perdido River boundary as non-negotiable. The administration’s public denunciations of filibustering served a dual purpose: they maintained a veneer of international legality while quietly allowing demographic and political pressure to build against Spanish rule. A strategy of patient erosion.

The news of the convention at St. John’s Plains reached Washington by late summer of 1810. Madison’s cabinet, particularly Secretary of State Robert Smith began to analyze the reports not as a border disturbance, but as a potential geopolitical windfall. The key question, debated in meetings whose substance was recorded in private memoranda was one of timing and pretext. How could the United States acquire the territory without appearing to instigate the overthrow of a neutral power—a power whose European parent was currently an ally against Napoleon? The ideal scenario, from Madison’s perspective was for the Anglo settlers to complete the revolution themselves, establish a de facto independent government, and then petition for annexation. This would provide a legal fig leaf of invitation, transforming a land grab into a rescue of American brethren.

Madison therefore pursued a policy of watchful waiting. He issued no public statements supporting the convention. He sent no arms or overt encouragement. But he also took no action to prevent American citizens from crossing the border to join the movement. The flow of men and information continued unimpeded. When Governor Claiborne in New Orleans sent worried reports about the growing unrest, Madison’s replies found in his presidential papers, urged calm observation. The administration gathered intelligence, not preparing to intervene—yet.

This calculated pause was rooted in a cold assessment of power. Madison knew Spain could not respond. He knew Britain, though a rival was overstretched by the Napoleonic Wars. The risk of European retaliation for annexing West Florida was minimal. The greater risk was inaction: if the republic collapsed or, worse, appealed to Britain for protection, a strategic wedge could be driven between the United States and the Gulf Coast. The territory’s value was not merely in its land. Control of the rivers flowing into Lake Pontchartrain and the Gulf ports at Biloxi and Mobile Bay would secure the southern flank of the Louisiana Purchase and complete American dominance of the Mississippi trade. The planters’ rebellion doed the hard work of clearing out the old regime. Madison’s task was to ensure the United States was the sole beneficiary.

By September, as the convention shifted from talk of assisting Delassus to planning his overthrow, Madison’s cabinet began drafting contingency orders. The documents held in the War Department files are dry and procedural. They outline the movement of troops from New Orleans, the authority of Governor Claiborne, and the procedures for raising the American flag over Spanish installations. They are administrative blueprints for an annexation that had not yet been announced, for a rebellion that had not yet succeeded. The planning was precise because the outcome was considered inevitable. The only unknown was the date.

When Philemon Thomas’s men stormed Fort San Carlos on the morning of September 23, they believed they were acting as independent revolutionaries. In Washington, their action was the final piece of a puzzle Madison had been assembling for years. The rebellion provided the pretext. The republic’s declaration of independence provided the invitation. The administration’s pre-written orders provided the mechanism. The planters saw themselves as founders of a new nation. Madison saw them as the unwitting agents of a long-planned territorial expansion. Their Lone Star flag would fly for seventy-four days. From the moment it was raised, the clock ticked on an American takeover that was not a reaction, but the culmination of a covert strategy. The republic’s birth was, for the United States government, a signal to begin the final phase of a diplomatic and military operation that had been in development since 1803.

The atmosphere in the Feliciana districts that summer was one of open preparation. As John Rhea later recounted to the Mississippi Messenger, the convention’s initial meetings at St. John’s Plains were procedural, focused on drafting resolutions and establishing committees. But by August, the tone shifted. The talk in the clearings and at the crossroads stores, as documented in the letters of planters like William Wikoff and Benjamin O. Williams turned from grievance to mobilization. Men who had once debated land titles now discussed powder supplies and muster rolls. The Spanish flag still flew, but the authority it represented had receded into a distant abstraction. The real power coalesced around the convention’s table and in the militia camps.

John Rhea provided the political architecture. A former congressman from Tennessee, he brought a proceduralist’s mind to the chaos of frontier discontent. His letters, preserved in the Tennessee State Archives, show a man methodically translating raw ambition into the language of legitimate revolution. He understood the need for a document—a declaration—that would frame their economic and security interests as a principled struggle for natural rights. Under his guidance, the convention ceased to be a protest group and began to act as a provisional government. It established a postal system. It appointed tax collectors. It issued orders, signed by Rhea as presiding officer, that bypassed Governor Delassus entirely. Each act was a quiet rehearsal for sovereignty.

The fort’s gates would not hold. The flag would come down. A new one, a lone star on a blue field would go up. And in Washington, a clerk would pull a pre-written proclamation from a file ready for the president’s signature. The revolution, when it came would feel like a beginning to those who fought it. To everyone else, it was an ending long foreseen.
