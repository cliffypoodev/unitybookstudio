The hymnal hit the stained-glass Jesus square in the face with a satisfying thwack. Rex's tail twitched. "Again."

Sister Mercy tossed him another. "Elbow in. Wrist loose. Like you're throwing a curveball, not ripping someone's spine out."

Rex adjusted his grip. The book's gold-embossed title—Songs of Salvation—gleamed under the flickering fluorescents. He wound up. The hymnal sailed past the plywood cutout of a televangelist, smacked into a rack of votive candles, and sent them clattering across the concrete floor.

Jesus pinched the bridge of his nose. "We're supposed to be training for stealth infiltration. Not desecrating a house of worship."

"House of what now?" Sister Mercy kicked a stack of old bulletins aside. Dust plumed. "This place got condemned when the roof caved in during a Benny Hinn rerun. Last sacrament performed here was a meth deal." She lobbed another hymnal at Rex. "And we're not desecrating. We're repurposing."

Rex caught it midair. His claws punctured the cover. "Define 'grenade' again."

"Explosive device. Hurts people. Makes noise." Sister Mercy patted the duffel bag at her feet. "These just skip step three."

Jesus crouched to examine the bag's contents. His fingers emerged holding a clump of wires and what looked like a harmonica glued to a smoke detector. "These are the holy hand grenades?"

"Patent pending." Sister Mercy grinned. "Hymnal covers are lined with UHF-reactive foil. Tear out the pages, pack in some static scraped off Eden's broadcast towers, add a prayer wheel for a fuse—boom. Spiritual shrapnel."

Rex frowned. "Boom literally or boom metaphorically?"

"Depends how close you're standing."

A moth batted against the lone working lightbulb. Somewhere above them, a pipe dripped in rhythm with the distant thump of bass from a passing car. Rex studied the hymnal in his claws. "So I throw this. Then what?"

"Then ideally, the televangelist's earpiece explodes and he starts speaking in actual tongues." Sister Mercy demonstrated with an underhand toss. The book arced neatly through the plywood cutout's open mouth. "But we'll settle for distraction and chaos."

Jesus stood. "This is the worst plan we've ever had."

"Correction." Sister Mercy pulled a rosary from her belt and started clicking the beads like a speedometer. "This is the only plan where nobody gets eaten. Unless Rex gets hungry, in which case, priorities."

Rex's stomach growled.

Jesus closed his eyes. "We're doomed."

"Focus, scaly." Sister Mercy chucked another hymnal. Rex caught it left-handed. "You're thinking like a dinosaur. Start thinking like a delinquent altar boy."

Rex's nostrils flared. The scent of mildew and old wiring mixed with something sharper—Sister Mercy's sweat, the metallic tang of the jury-rigged explosives. His tail flicked, adjusting his balance.

The hymnal spiraled through the air and nailed the plywood televangelist right between the eyes. The cutout wobbled, then toppled backward into a pile of moldy choir robes.

Sister Mercy whistled. "Hallelujah."

Rex blinked. "I did it?"

"You did it." She slapped his flank. "Now do it fifty more times until you can hit a moving target while being shot at."

Jesus groaned. "We're not actually getting shot at during this, right?"

Sister Mercy's grin was all teeth. "Oh, honey. You don't know Mercy's Ministry of Mayhem very well, do you?"

A siren wailed in the distance. Close enough to raise the hairs on Jesus's arms.

Rex picked up another hymnal. His claws flexed. "Fifty, you said?"

The lightbulb flickered. Somewhere above them, a floorboard creaked.

Sister Mercy cracked her knuckles. "Better make it sixty."

The neon clown’s grin flickered above them, its bulbous nose casting a sickly green glow across the mini-golf windmill. Rex’s claws clicked against the Astroturf as he adjusted his stance. "Define ‘moving target.’"

Sister Mercy lobbed a hymnal grenade underhand. It bounced off the windmill’s blades with a hollow thunk. "Anything that makes you say ‘oh shit’ in a church voice."

A twig snapped in the mangroves.

Rex’s tail went rigid. His nostrils flared—synthetic motor oil and ozone. "We’re being audited."

The first drone emerged from the foliage, its chrome plating scuffed with algae. Its mono-eye swiveled, scanning the scene: Rex mid-windup, Sister Mercy crouched behind a fiberglass dinosaur, Jesus conspicuously absent.

Sister Mercy sighed. "You couldn’t have waited five minutes?"

The drone’s speaker crackled. "Unauthorized miracle replication detected. Penalty: immediate soul garnishment."

Rex threw.

The hymnal grenade sailed wide, smacked a water hazard, and sank with a gurgle.

Sister Mercy facepalmed. "Elbow in—"

The drone fired.

Rex ducked. The laser seared a hole through the clown’s forehead. Its neon grin dimmed, then reignited in violent violet.

"Okay." Sister Mercy rolled behind a giant golf ball. "New rule: if it shoots first, all bets are off." She chucked a grenade. It arced beautifully—then bonked off the drone’s dome and landed in the sand trap.

Rex blinked. "That was worse than mine."

"Shut up and throw."

The second drone emerged, this one armed with what looked like a taser grafted onto a collection plate. Rex’s muscles coiled. He remembered Sister Mercy’s advice—elbow in, wrist loose—but mostly he remembered the scent of Eden’s labs, the way his old restraints had smelled like antiseptic and fear.

He hurled the grenade like he was aiming for the sun.

It nailed the drone’s taser. A spark leapt. The collection plate shuddered, then began vibrating at a frequency that made Rex’s teeth ache.

The drone’s eye crossed. "Unauthorized… donation…" It shorted out in a puff of sanctimonious smoke.

Sister Mercy whooped. "That’s my boy!"

The remaining drone backed up, its gears whirring. Rex could practically hear its processors overheating. "Containment protocol initiated."

A hatch slid open on its underside. Dozens of tiny, cruciform probes rained down, each one buzzing like an angry wasp.

Sister Mercy’s grin faltered. "Oh. That’s new."

Rex grabbed the fiberglass dinosaur’s head and wrenched it free. "Swing or shield?"

"Swing. Always swing."

The first probe dive-bombed. Rex batted it into the water hazard. It fizzed. The second got a backhand.

Sister Mercy’s nunchucks blurred. She moved like someone who’d spent years deflecting collection plates mid-sermon. A probe shattered against her rosary beads. Another got a faceful of hymnal shrapnel.

Rex’s arms burned. His vision narrowed to the rhythm of strike-dodge-pivot. The world smelled of singed plastic and Sister Mercy’s sweat, sharp as gunpowder.

The last probe zigged when it should’ve zagged. Rex’s tail speared it mid-air.

Silence.

The remaining drone hovered, its mono-eye darting between them. Calculating.

Sister Mercy wiped her brow. "Think it’s gonna call for backup?"

Rex hefted the dinosaur head. "Hope so."

The drone’s speaker hissed static. Then, in a voice like a Sunday school teacher mid-nervous breakdown: "This… displeases the Lord."

It self-destructed.

The blast sent Rex skidding backward. His claws gouged trenches in the Astroturf. When the smoke cleared, the drone was gone, leaving only a smoldering hole in the shape of a tithe envelope.

Sister Mercy coughed. "Well. That escalated."

Rex’s adrenaline ebbed, leaving his limbs shaky. He stared at the wreckage. "We won?"

"Define ‘won.’" She nudged a probe carcass with her boot. "We’re not dead. That’s a start."

A muffled chirp came from her belt. She unhooked the nunchuck-phone—a repurposed Nintendo controller grafted to a walkie-talkie—and frowned at the screen.

Rex leaned in. "More drones?"

The neon clown’s light pulsed erratically, casting her face in garish stripes. Her thumb hovered over the message. "Worse."

MIRACLE AUDIT INBOUND: 12 HRS. TARGET: FUNLAND’S ‘RESURRECTION’ TUNNEL. BRING CASH.

Rex’s stomach dropped. "That’s not a drone thing."

Sister Mercy’s jaw tightened. "Nope." She pocketed the phone. "That’s a ‘someone’s been skimming miracles’ thing."

The windmill creaked. Somewhere beyond the mangroves, the first birds of dawn began their screechy, half-hearted chorus.

Rex flexed his claws. The fight had been messy, chaotic, absurd—but this? This smelled like betrayal. And betrayal always left a trail.

Sister Mercy cracked her neck. "Guess we’re skipping breakfast."

The neon clown’s grin flickered out as the sun rose, its dead bulbs reflecting in Rex’s eyes like unanswered questions.

Rex blinked. The reflection vanished. Just glass and dead bulbs.

He turned from the window. The apartment smelled of old coffee and the ozone from his rig. He didn't brew more. He pulled on his jacket, the leather stiff across his shoulders.

The street outside was a different animal in the morning. The night’s glitter was just grime under flat gray light. Delivery drones hummed at head-level. A sanitation bot whirred past his ankles, brushing glitter and broken glass into its maw.

His comm buzzed. Once. A location ping, no signature. Coordinates for a docking bay in the mid-levels. He deleted it. He’d walk.

The elevator to the mid-levels stank of disinfectant and wet metal. A man in a stained courier uniform stared at the floor numbers, jaw working on nothing. Rex watched the man’s hands. They were clean. Too clean for the uniform.

“Level forty-seven,” the man said, not looking up.

“You asking or telling?”

“Just making conversation.”

The doors opened. The courier didn’t move. Rex stepped out. The doors slid shut behind him, trapping the man inside.

Docking Bay C-12 was a cavern of noise and cold wind. Freight loaders clanked. A ship was venting atmosphere in a sharp, frosty gasp. The coordinates led to a service alcove behind a stack of polymer crates.

Silas was there. He leaned against the wall, picking at a thread on his sleeve. He didn’t look up as Rex approached.

“You’re late.”

“I walked.”

“Sentimental.” Silas finally glanced at him. His eyes were red-rimmed. “The clown’s dead. For real this time. City maintenance logged a full circuit burn at oh-four-hundred.”

“I saw.”

“You see who did it?”

“No.”

Silas pushed off the wall. He pulled a small data-tab from his pocket. It was cheap, the kind sold in blister packs at kiosks. “Found this. Wedged in the joint of the clown’s left arm. Not city issue. Not maintenance.”

Rex took it. The plastic was warm. “You scanned it.”

“Of course I scanned it. It’s clean. Too clean. No fingerprints, no DNA trace, no serial. Just data. Encrypted.”

“And?”

“And I know a guy who knows a guy who works for a guy in data forensics. On the municipal payroll. He ran a passive decrypt. Took two minutes.” Silas lit a cigarette. The smoke was snatched by the bay’s wind. “It’s a key.”

“For what?”

“For a door. A very specific, very expensive door in the Aurora Spire. Penthouse level.”

Rex turned the tab over. It was blank. “Why leave it there?”

“Why do you think?” Silas exhaled. “It’s an invitation. A sloppy one. They wanted it found, but not by just anyone. By someone who’d look. You were there last night. Someone saw you.”

“The caretaker.”

“Maybe. Or someone else watching the clown.” Silas dropped the cigarette and ground it under his heel. “The key’s active for a forty-eight hour window. Started at the burn-out. You’ve got until tomorrow morning.”

“You’re not coming.”

“I’m not stupid.” Silas looked past him, at the bustling bay. “Aurora’s Vanguard territory. Corporate security with shoot-on-sight protocols for unregistered entries. That key might open the door, but it won’t stop a plasma bolt.”

“You have a better idea?”

“Yeah. Toss it into the nearest recycler and go home. The clown’s dead. Let it lie.”

Rex pocketed the tab. The plastic felt heavy.

Silas watched the motion. His shoulders slumped. “You’re going.”

“I’m looking.”

“It’s a trap.”

“Probably.”

A loader roared past, its warning siren blaring. Silas waited for the noise to fade. “Why? What’s in your head, Rex? It was a machine. A broken one.”

Rex thought of the reflection in the glass. The unanswered questions. He said nothing.

Silas nodded, a short, sharp motion. “Fine. I’ll do a perimeter scan on the spire. See if I can find a blind spot in their external sensors. But if I smell Vanguard, I’m gone. I’m not dying for your nostalgia.”

“I’m not asking you to.”

“You never ask.” Silas turned to leave. He stopped after two steps. “The caretaker. Her name’s Lira. She logs her shifts. She’s off at eight tonight. If you want to talk before you do something suicidal.”

Then he was gone, swallowed by the flow of dockworkers and cargo.

Rex stood in the alcove. The wind cut through his jacket. He pulled the tab back out and held it up. The morning light caught the cheap plastic, showing nothing at all.

The caretaker’s building was a stack of prefab units near the edge of the neon district. The lobby door was propped open with a brick. Rex took the stairs. The air smelled of fried food and mildew.

He knocked on unit 3B.

The door opened a crack. A chain lock gleamed. Lira’s eye peered out. It widened. The door closed. He heard the chain rattle. It opened again.

She wore a faded sweatshirt. Her hair was down. She looked younger.

“You.”

“Me.”

“I told you everything last night.”

“You told me about the clown.” Rex kept his hands visible, at his sides. “You didn’t tell me who else was watching it.”

Her gaze flickered past him, down the empty hallway. “Come in.”

The apartment was one room. A bed folded into the wall. A small table with two chairs. A kitchenette. It was neat. Austere. A single photograph on the wall showed a younger Lira with an older man, both smiling in front of a different, brighter clown sign.

“My father,” she said, following his look. “He maintained it. Before the city contract. Before it… stopped talking.”

Rex didn’t sit. “Someone burned out its circuits last night. After I left.”

“I know. I got the alert.” She leaned against the counter. “I’m glad. It was a mercy.”

“Was it?”

She crossed her arms. “What do you want?”

He didn’t turn. “They can try.”
