The Rusty Barnacle didn’t so much float as sulk in the water, a permanent scowl of wood and barnacles. Captain Salty stood at the prow, a man whose face had forgotten how to make any expression other than profound annoyance. Three hundred and seven years of salt and sun will do that. He inhaled the morning air—brine, wet rope, and the faint, fizzy scent of his holy water cologne. Underneath it all, a sour tang. A tinge of betrayal. A whiff of cowardice from the port-side watchman who’d pocketed an extra biscuit. Moral decay smelled like old lemons and damp socks.

“Right, you bilge rats!” he bellowed, turning to face his crew.

They were a collection of men who looked less like pirates and more like men who’d been shipwrecked on a pirate ship. Gummy Pete, the quartermaster, was trying to pick a fight with a seagull. One-Eyed Wally had his one eye squeezed shut in concentration as he attempted to mend a sock. Barnaby, the cabin boy, was just staring at his own hand, flexing his fingers as if he’d never seen them before.

Salty’s heart, an organ that hadn’t bothered beating with any real enthusiasm since the late 1600s, gave a weary thud. He wanted to die. Not in a poetic, dramatic way. In a specific, dignified, horizontal-in-a-soft-bed way. A way that required first obtaining the Divine Chalice of Unmaking, currently guarded by a parrot on the Temple Isle of Perpetual Dusk.

“We’re going on a raid,” he announced.

Gummy Pete paused his avian negotiations. “Again? We just raided Tuesday.”

“That was a bakery, Pete.”

“They had a very aggressive muffin defense. I’ve got a scar.” Pete pointed to a faint smudge of flour on his forearm.

“This is different,” Salty said, leaning forward. The holy water in the tiny vial around his neck sloshed, releasing another puff of sanctified citrus. It was supposed to ward off evil spirits. Mostly it just attracted bees. “This is a holy raid. A sacred heist. We’re stealing from a god.”

One-Eyed Wally opened his eye. “Which one?”

“The one with the treasure.”

“They all have treasure, Cap’n,” Wally said, reasonably. “That’s the problem with gods. No fiscal restraint.”

Salty pinched the bridge of his nose. He could smell Wally’s moral ambiguity from here—a scent like burnt toast and indecision. “This treasure is the Chalice of Unmaking. It can unmake things. Like curses. Like, say, a curse of immortality bestowed upon a dashing pirate captain for the grievous sin of stabbing his beloved while attempting to pilfer a different, slightly less holy cup.”

A heavy silence fell, broken only by the Barnacle’s timbers groaning and Barnaby whispering to his hand, “How do you do that?”

“Point is,” Salty continued, voice dropping to a conspiratorial rasp that was mostly genuine phlegm, “this is my one shot. My ticket to a nice, finite end. A sunset on a mortal life. You’ll be doing me a solid. A final captain’s order.”

Gummy Pete scratched his chin. “What’s in it for us?”

“The satisfaction of a job well done.”

The crew stared.

“Fine. There’s probably also rubies. Or incense. The temple’s lousy with incense.” The smell of it, a cloying sandalwood, already tickled his nostrils from leagues away, mixing unpleasantly with the lemon-sock aura of his men.

“Guarded, is it?” Wally asked.

“Moderately.”

“How moderately?”

Salty waved a hand. “A few zealots. Some slippery stairs. The main issue is the guardian.”

“Big fella?”

“Feathery.” Salty cleared his throat. “It’s a parrot.”

Gummy Pete burst out laughing. A wheezing sound that ended in a cough. “A parrot? We’re afraid of a parrot now? I’ve eaten parrot. Tastes like guilt and crackers.”

“This parrot,” Salty said, his voice flat, “predicts the exact time of your death. To the second. It tells you, and then you… well, you sort of arrange your affairs accordingly. It’s very efficient. Also, it’s got a beak like a cutthroat razor and a vocabulary that would make a longshoreman weep.”

The concept settled over the deck. Barnaby finally looked up from his hand. “It… tells you when you’ll die?”

“Yes.”

“Why would you want to know that?”

“You wouldn’t,” Salty said. “That’s the terror of it. That’s the security system. It’s not a lock, it’s a deadline. The ultimate deterrent. Who wants to hear their own expiry date from a glorified budgie?”

One-Eyed Wally’s face paled. “It just… says it? Out loud?”

“A piercing shriek, I’m told. Very specific. ‘High noon on Tuesday!’ or ‘Three past midnight when the tide is slack!’ That sort of thing.” Salty leaned in again. “But here’s the beauty. I’m immortal. Cursed. The parrot looks at me, it’s got nothing. My ticket’s punched ‘When the stars grow cold.’ It’ll be stumped. Confused. We waltz in during its existential crisis, grab the chalice, and waltz out.”

He presented the plan with a flourish. It was, he felt, airtight.

Gummy Pete squinted. “What if it predicts our deaths?”

“Don’t listen to it. Simple. Fingers in ears. La-la-la.”

“You said it was a piercing shriek.”

“Metaphorical fingers!”

The Temple Isle of Perpetual Dusk wasn’t so much an island as a jagged rock that had gotten ambitious. It rose from the mist-shrouded sea like a broken tooth, the temple itself a bizarre amalgamation of Gothic spires and what appeared to be polished brass pipes that vented steady streams of fragrant smoke. The smell was overpowering now—sacred incense and wet stone, undercut by the ever-present salt. It made Salty’s holy water cologne bubble angrily.

They anchored the Barnacle in a hidden cove that was mostly sharp coral and bad intentions. The row to shore was conducted in a silence so tense you could pluck it like a violin string. Barnaby kept checking his pulse. Wally was muttering what sounded like a will.

“Stop that,” Salty hissed as they dragged the longboat onto the black sand beach. “It’s a bird. A chatty bird. We are pirates. We fear nothing except creditors and scurvy.”

“And that thing in the water that looked like a squid with too many teeth,” Gummy Pete added helpfully.

“Except that. Now, the plan.” Salty pointed a bony finger up the steep, moss-slick path. “The temple door is around that bend, behind the waterfall that cries actual, slightly salty tears. The parrot’s perch is in the inner sanctum, right above the chalice. I’ll go in first. The parrot will see me, have its little feathered breakdown, and then you lot follow. Grab the cup. No looking at the bird. No listening. We’re in and out in five minutes. A spiritual smash-and-grab.”

He didn’t wait for agreement. He started up the path, his boots slipping on the perpetual damp. The tears from the weeping waterfall collected in his collar. They did taste salty. Also a little bitter, like regret. He pushed through the curtain of water, the cold shock a welcome distraction from the lemon-sock smell of his own crew’s mounting fear.

The inner sanctum was smaller than he’d imagined. And warmer. The air hummed. The Chalice of Unmaking sat on a simple stone pedestal in a beam of light that had no business being there, considering it was perpetually dusk. It was… underwhelming. A tarnished silver cup with a chip on the rim. It looked like something you’d find at the back of a disappointing pub.

On a perch directly above it, preening a brilliant scarlet feather, was the parrot.

It was larger than any parrot had a right to be. Its eyes were two beads of polished obsidian. It stopped preening and tilted its head at Salty.

Salty puffed out his chest. The holy water cologne fizzled. “Go on, then. Do your worst. Captain Alistair ‘Salty’ McGraw. Cursed. Immortal. Try and find an end for me, you overgrown duster.”

The parrot opened its beak. It didn’t screech. Its voice was dry, precise, and sounded exactly like Salty’s old accounting tutor.

“Captain Alistair ‘Salty’ McGraw,” it recited. “Time of death: Thirty-seven seconds from… mark.”

Salty blinked. “What?”

“Thirty-six.”

“That’s not right. I’m immortal. The curse of the Black Chalice, 1642. Eternal life until I atone.”

“Thirty-five,” the parrot said, utterly bored. “The curse wasn’t for eternal life. It was for eternal consciousness. Your body can, and will, expire. Quite messily, I’d imagine. Thirty-four.”

A cold that had nothing to do with the weeping waterfall settled in Salty’s gut. It wasn’t a knot. It was a void. “That’s… that’s a lie. A trick.”

“Thirty-three. It’s not a trick. It’s a test. Thirty-two.”

“A test of what?”

“Thirty-one. Of what you’ll do with the information. Thirty. Most people weep. Some pray. A surprising number try to bargain. Twenty-nine.”

Salty’s mind, a three-century-old stew of regrets and sea shanties, went blank. Then it filled with a single, white-hot point of rage. Not at the parrot. At the universe. At the sheer, bureaucratic pettiness of it all. A test? His entire miserable, endless existence was a pop quiz?

“Twenty-eight.”

The sanctum curtain rustled. Gummy Pete’s face appeared, etched with worry. “Cap’n?”

The parrot’s head swiveled. “Seaman ‘Gummy’ Pete. Time of death: Four minutes, twelve seconds from… mark.”

Gummy Pete’s jaw went slack. “I beg your pardon?”

“Four-eleven. Four-ten.”

Pete backed into Wally and Barnaby, who were crowding in behind him. The parrot’s obsidian eyes flicked to each of them.

“Seaman One-Eyed Wally. Time of death: Four minutes, five seconds.”
“Cabin Boy Barnaby Smith. Time of death: Four minutes, one second.”

Chaos, of a particularly quiet and horrified variety, ensued.

Wally sat down hard on the stone floor. Barnaby started counting on his fingers, tears welling. Gummy Pete drew his cutlass and pointed it shakily at the bird. “You take it back!”

“Three-fifty,” the parrot said to Pete, then continued its merciless count for the others. “Three-fifty-five for Wally. Three-fifty-one for Smith.”

Salty stared at the chalice. The chip on the rim looked like the mouth of a tiny, mocking smile. This was the twist. Not a security system. A celestial experiment. God wasn’t guarding the treasure; He was running a focus group on mortal panic. Mortality wasn’t his motivation—it was the bait.

And he had just led his men into the lab.

“Twenty-two,” the parrot said, turning back to him.

Salty McGraw, 307 years old, murderer of his true love, failure of a pirate, and connoisseur of moral stenches, made a decision. It wasn’t dignified. It wasn’t even particularly clever. It was, like most of his life, profoundly messy.

He snatched the Chalice of Unmaking from its pedestal. It was warm.

“Right!” he yelled, his voice cracking. “New plan! We’re leaving!”

“But it said I’ve got four minutes!” Gummy Pete wailed.

“Then stop wasting them staring at a bird! Move!”

He shoved Barnaby toward the curtain. He hauled Wally to his feet. He grabbed Pete by the collar and propelled him forward. The parrot, nonplussed, continued its countdowns in a serene monotone, a chorus of impending doom.

“Eighteen. Three-twenty. Three-sixteen. Three-twelve.”

They stumbled back through the weeping waterfall, a tangle of soggy, terrified humanity. Salty clutched the warm chalice to his chest. He could still hear the parrot, its voice cutting through the water’s sob.

“Ten. Three-oh-two. Two-fifty-eight. Two-fifty-four.”

They hit the black sand, legs pumping. The longboat was twenty yards away.

“Five. Two-forty. Two-thirty-six. Two-thirty-two.”

Salty’s chest burned. Not with immortality. With a stitch. A real, human, out-of-shape stitch. It was the most wonderful pain he’d ever felt.

“Two. Two-twenty. Two-sixteen. Two-twelve.”

They reached the boat. He threw the chalice in. It landed with a dull clunk. He turned to help heave the boat into the surf, his back to the temple, the parrot’s voice finally fading into the mist and the crash of waves.

He didn’t hear his zero.

He just heard Gummy Pete, gasping as he pulled on an oar. “I’m alive! I’m past four minutes! The stupid bird was wrong!”

Salty glanced back at the jagged Isle. The mist swirled around the weeping waterfall. He couldn’t see the sanctum. He could still smell the incense, though. And under it, something new. Not lemons. Not socks.

It was the scent of a loophole. A technicality. It smelled like ozone and cheap whisky.

The parrot’s predictions were lies. But the fear had been real. The running had been real. The warm, tarnished weight of the chalice in the bottom of the boat was real.

He looked at his crew, panting, bewildered, alive.

“Of course it was wrong, you idiot,” Salty said, grabbing an oar himself. A grin cracked his face, a strange, unused muscle stretching. “It’s a parrot. Now row, before it flies out here and gives us the weather forecast.”

The row back to the Barnacle was a symphony of ragged breath and the rhythmic, frantic slap of oars. No one spoke. The only sounds were the sea, the creak of wood, and Barnaby’s quiet, persistent sobbing.

Salty stared at the tarnished chalice between his feet. It was warm. Not a comforting warmth. The warmth of a stone left in the sun, or a fevered brow. He’d expected… something. A hum. A glow. This felt like a slightly used tankard.

“It lied,” Gummy Pete whispered, breaking the silence. He kept flexing his arms, patting his chest as if checking for holes. “The bird. It lied. I’m not dead.”

“Told you,” Salty grunted, not looking up. “Fingers in ears.”

“But it was so specific.”

“Lots of things are specific and wrong. My first mate’s navigation, for instance.”

Wally, who was manning the tiller, ignored him. “It said four minutes. We’ve been rowing for seven. Eight.” He blinked his one eye slowly. “Do you think… do you think it just wanted us to leave?”

Salty’s head snapped up. The idea landed in his brain like a drunk seagull. It was clumsy, obvious, and left a mess. The parrot wasn’t a guardian. It was a bouncer. A feathered, psychological bouncer for a god who couldn’t be bothered with actual security. The ultimate deterrent wasn’t death. It was a spoiler.

He looked at the chalice again. The chip on the rim winked at him.

“It doesn’t matter,” he said, his voice firmer than he felt. “We have the prize. The job is done.”

Barnaby sniffled. “What does it unmake, Captain?”

“Curses, boy. Specifically, mine.” He reached down, his fingers closing around the cup’s stem. It was smoother than he’d expected. “The curse of eternal consciousness in a body that can apparently still die on a schedule. A bureaucratic nightmare.”

He lifted the chalice. He held it up against the perpetual dusk sky. No lightning cracked. No voices boomed. The warm metal just sat in his hand, looking profoundly ordinary.

“How does it work?” Gummy Pete asked.

Salty realized he hadn’t the faintest clue. Did he drink from it? Smash it over his own head? Recite a poem? The pirate who’d told him about it three ports ago had been deeply drunk and mostly concerned with the price of lint. The instructions had been vague. ‘It unmakes, mate. It just… unmakes.’

“You have to mean it,” Salty declared, a bluff forming in the void where a plan should be. “True intent. A pure desire for unmaking.” He tried to summon a pure desire. Mostly he felt a desperate need for a nap and for his socks to stop smelling of cowardice. He brought the chalice to his lips.

He tipped it back.

Nothing happened.

Well, something happened. A single, warm drop of something that tasted of dust and faintly of lavender hit his tongue. Then the chalice was empty. He lowered it, blinking.

“Well?” Wally said.

Salty waited. He waited for a shudder, a flash, a sudden profound understanding of mortality. He felt a slight itch behind his left knee. That was it.

“It’s… processing,” he said.

A derisive squawk echoed across the water.

They all turned. On the Barnacle’s sagging mainyard, preening a wing, was the parrot. Its obsidian eyes fixed on Salty.

“Processing error,” it announced, its tutor-voice dry. “Subject ‘McGraw, A.’ exhibits insufficient existential dread. Prerequisite for unmaking not met. Please experience genuine, pants-wetting terror and try again.”

Salty’s grip tightened on the chalice. “You.”

“Me.” The parrot fluffed its feathers. “You took the prop. That’s fine. The experiment is concluded. Data recorded: ‘Subject chose flight over fight, with moderate weeping.’ Fascinating.”

“Prop?” The word came out as a croak.

“The Chalice of Unmaking,” the parrot recited, as if from a manual, “is a ceremonial focus object with no inherent thaumaturgical properties. Its perceived power is derived from the placebo effect and the user’s own desperate belief. Which you, Captain, appear to be fresh out of.” It cocked its head. “Your belief levels are currently registering at ‘mild skepticism’ trending towards ‘petulant disappointment.’”

The silence in the longboat was absolute. The only sound was the lapping of water against the hull and the distant, cheerful weeping of the waterfall.

Gummy Pete broke it. “So… we almost died for a… a fancy cup?”

“You almost thought you were going to die,” the parrot corrected. “Key difference. The temple’s security is entirely psychological. Slippery stairs induce anxiety. Weeping water induces mild depression. I provide the deadline. It’s about the narrative. The story of your end. Most beings are terrified of the last page.” It pecked at its claw. “You lot just… ran away from the book.”

Salty looked from the parrot to the chalice. The warm, empty, powerless chalice. Three hundred and seven years. A murder. A curse. A lifetime of smelling sin and seeking a dignified exit. All for a prop in a divine psychology experiment. The rage that filled him was hot, sudden, and utterly directionless. Who did he punch? The bird? God? Himself?

He settled for the closest thing. He threw the Chalice of Unmaking at the parrot.

It was a good throw. For a man whose most strenuous activity in the last century was scowling, it was a fantastic throw. The cup tumbled end over end, a silver blur against the grey sky.

The parrot didn’t flinch. It just opened its beak and caught the chalice by the rim with a precise clink. It held it there, looking at Salty with what could only be described as avian pity.

“Tantrum noted,” it said, the words slightly muffled by the metal. “Data point added: ‘Subject reacts to metaphysical disappointment with projectile-based pique.’” It dropped the chalice. The cup hit the deck of the Barnacle with a sad, hollow thud and rolled into a scupper.

Something in Salty broke. Not his heart. Something lower. His spirit, maybe. Or just his last shred of dignity. It snapped with a sound only he could hear, like a dry twig stepped on by a giant.

He started to laugh.

It began as a wheeze, a thin stream of air escaping a broken valve. Then it built. It became a cackle, then a full-bodied, knee-slapping, tear-streaming roar of laughter. He laughed so hard he dropped his oar. He laughed until he choked, until his sides ached, until the confused, horrified faces of his crew became the funniest thing he had ever seen.

“A prop!” he gasped, wiping his eyes. “A placebo! He doesn’t want my atonement! He wants my data points!”

The crew exchanged glances. This was new. The Captain was many things—morose, irritable, lemon-scented—but he was not a laugher. This was unsettling.

“Cap’n?” Wally ventured. “You alright?”

“No, Wally!” Salty howled, slapping the gunwale. “I’m not alright! I’m a joke! A three-century-long joke with a punchline delivered by a bird! My curse isn’t a tragedy, it’s… it’s a case study!” He doubled over again, his laughter turning slightly ragged at the edges.

The parrot watched, unimpressed. “Hysterical deflection. Common stage. Usually precedes bargaining or a sudden interest in abstract painting.”

Salty’s laughter died as quickly as it came. He stood up in the wobbling longboat, pointing a trembling finger at the bird. “You listen to me, you feathered bureaucrat. You go back to your temple. You tell your boss his experiment is over. I’m leaving the control group.”

He turned to his men. Their faces were a mosaic of fear, confusion, and the dawning realization that their captain might have finally, truly lost his mind. It was time for a speech. A rousing one. The kind that turned desperate men into loyal ones.

He opened his mouth.

Barnaby spoke first. “So… you’re still cursed?”

Salty deflated. “Yes, Barnaby. I am still cursed.”

“And that cup won’t fix it?”

“No.”

“And the bird can’t really tell when we die?”

“It would seem not.”

Gummy Pete scratched his head. “So we risked our necks, heard our… our expiry dates, and got chased off by a waterfall that cries, all for a fake cup we just threw away?”

“In essence.”

Another heavy silence. This one had a different quality. It was less horrified, more calculative. Salty knew that silence. It was the silence that came before a discussion of shares, or rations, or the delicate matter of a captain’s competence.

One-Eyed Wally cleared his throat. “That’s not very good pirating, Captain.”

“It’s terrible pirating!” Salty agreed, trying to steer the ship of mutiny back toward camaraderie. “It’s farcical! It’s the kind of story you tell in a tavern to get laughed out of the room! We’re in uncharted waters of embarrassment, men! Together!”

Gummy Pete wasn’t listening. He was looking at the Barnacle, then at the island, then at the sad, empty scupper where the fake chalice lay. “We followed you because you said this was your one shot. Your ticket out. You said we were doing you a solid.”

“I was misinformed!”

“You were wrong,” Pete said, and the words were flat, final. “You’re always wrong. The bakery raid was wrong. The map to the Diamond Caves was wrong. That ‘shortcut’ through the Sargasso of Screaming Eels was spectacularly wrong.” He stood up, making the boat rock. “I’m not dying for a wrong captain who smells of lemons.”

It wasn’t a shout. It was a statement. And it landed with more weight than any cutlass.

A mutiny, Salty realized, didn’t need shouting. It didn’t need swords drawn or a dramatic storm. It could happen in a damp longboat, under a dusk sky that never changed, sparked by a fake cup and a truthful bird.

Wally stood up beside Pete. “The bird made more sense than you did, Captain. And it’s a bird.”

Barnaby looked between them, his young face crumpled in distress. He didn’t stand, but he didn’t meet Salty’s eyes either. He just stared at his hands again, as if they held an answer.

Salty looked at them. His crew. The last in a long line of disappointments. He saw the resolve in Pete’s squared shoulders, the weary agreement in Wally’s slumped posture, the sheer confusion in Barnaby’s hunched form. He smelled it too—the sharp, vinegar tang of mutiny, overlaying the lemon and the sock-smell of their fear. It was a new scent. Acrid. Final.

The parrot, from its perch, observed. “Crew morale has reached critical levels. Suggested action: abdication. Or a very compelling apology.”

Salty ignored it. The laughter was gone, boiled away, leaving a cold, clear residue. He was tired. He was so tired of the smell of lemons, of the taste of salt, of the weight of years that had no end in sight. He was tired of being wrong.

He sat down. The boat rocked with his weight.

“You’re right,” he said.

The word hung in the air. The crew froze.

“I’m wrong. I have been, consistently, for centuries. My navigation is suspect. My plans are disasters. My understanding of divine curses is apparently based on tavern hearsay.” He looked at the fake chalice in the scupper. “That’s not a leader. That’s a liability.”

Gummy Pete’s belligerence faltered. “So… you’re stepping down?”

And then, just barely audible, the sound of Captain Salty’s laugh—a dry, rusty thing, but real—carried back across the waves.
