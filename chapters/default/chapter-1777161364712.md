The Spanish claim to West Florida was less an assertion of control than a cartographic afterthought, a strip of shaded parchment between the Pearl and Perdido Rivers. Its history, from the moment Spain reacquired it in 1783, was one of progressive abandonment. The administrative record, preserved in the Archivo General de Indias in Seville, reveals a colony starved of money, soldiers, and attention—a remote outpost on the ragged edge of a fading empire.

Spain regained the territory as a consolation prize. The Treaty of Paris that ended the American Revolution stripped Britain of its Florida holdings. Britain ceded East Florida to Spain outright. For West Florida, Spain claimed sovereignty based on its capture of Pensacola and Mobile during the war. But the treaty’s language was ambiguous, failing to define the territory’s western boundary with precision. This created a legal vacuum. Competing interpretations flowed into that vacuum. The newly independent United States, citing its 1783 treaty with Britain, claimed a southern border at the 31st parallel, slicing through the heart of the province. France, which had secretly regained Louisiana from Spain in 1800, eyed the Perdido River as its eastern limit. West Florida became a geopolitical buffer zone, its sovereignty perpetually in dispute.

Geography conspired against Spanish administration. The territory was a thin coastal crescent cut off from the main Spanish centers of power. Pensacola, the capital, was a tenuous anchor, itself isolated by hundreds of miles of sea from Havana and thousands from Veracruz. Overland communication with New Orleans, when it was still Spanish, was slow and hazardous. The Mississippi River, the region’s economic lifeline, was a highway controlled increasingly by American flatboats and keelboats. Spanish authority dissipated with every mile inland from the Gulf coast. By 1800, the colonial presence was skeletal: a few dozen troops scattered in wooden stockades like Fort San Carlos at Baton Rouge, a handful of customs officials, and a governor whose primary duty was to file quarterly reports detailing the province’s defenseless state.

The system relied on a brittle bargain. To populate the land and generate revenue, Spanish governors offered generous terms to Anglo-American settlers. They granted land, tolerated Protestant worship, and promised security. In return, these new subjects were expected to swear allegiance to the Spanish crown and uphold its laws. For a time, the arrangement held. Planters like John H. Johnson and John Mills accepted Spanish titles and prospered. But the demographic math was inexorable. By the first decade of the nineteenth century, American immigrants and their enslaved laborers outnumbered Spanish subjects by more than ten to one in the fertile Feliciana districts. The colonial infrastructure was overwhelmed by the very population it had invited.

The final blow to Spanish authority arrived not from the Mississippi frontier, but from Europe. In 1808, Napoleon Bonaparte’s armies invaded Spain, deposed King Ferdinand VII, and installed Joseph Bonaparte on the throne. The news, taking months to cross the Atlantic, shattered the foundational legitimacy of colonial rule. If the crown itself was captive, to whom did a colonist owe allegiance? The crisis paralyzed the imperial bureaucracy. Subsidies dried up. Promised troop reinforcements never sailed. Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, as his correspondence with Havana shows, pleaded for guidance and resources. He received only silence or impossible demands.

In Baton Rouge, the sitting governor, Carlos Dehault Delassus, faced the crisis with a garrison of fewer than fifty men. His reports to superiors took on a tone of quiet desperation. He controlled the fort, the flagpole, and little else. The real power—the plantations, the commerce, the armed men—resided with the Anglo settlers who now viewed Spanish rule as not just neglectful, but illegitimate. The invasion of the mother country had severed the last fragile thread of obligation. The gray zone was now a power vacuum, and nature, as one planter later wrote, abhors a vacuum.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus arrived in Baton Rouge in the spring of 1810, a soldier transferred into an impossible situation. He replaced Don Carlos de Grand-Pré, who had governed with a light touch for years. Delassus found a district that was, on paper, a Spanish possession. In reality, his command was a fiction maintained by mutual convenience. His garrison at Fort San Carlos, the lone symbol of imperial power for hundreds of miles, consisted of between twenty-eight and forty troops, a mix of regular infantry and local militia. A detailed roster from that year, preserved in the Spanish military archives, lists names of Catalan and Canary Island origin alongside a few French Creoles. They were poorly equipped, their pay was sporadic, and their morale, as Delassus’s own dispatches indicated, was low. The fort itself was not a formidable bastion but a worn wooden stockade, its cannon dated and its powder magazine damp.

The governor’s authority extended only as far as those men could see. The district of Baton Rouge stretched across rich plantation land from the Mississippi to the Pearl River, inhabited by thousands. To police it, collect taxes, or enforce edicts, Delassus had no civil guard, no network of magistrates. He relied entirely on the cooperation of the local elite—the very Anglo planters whose loyalty was now the central question. His primary function, as revealed in his correspondence with Governor Folch in Pensacola, was that of a watchman: to observe, report, and hope the fragile peace held.

The isolation was systemic. Pensacola, the provincial capital, was over two hundred miles to the east, a journey of weeks by sluggish schooner or along primitive forest trails. Mobile lay closer, but its garrison was itself negligible. In the event of serious unrest, neither could offer timely aid. This was not an accident but a feature of Spain’s imperial decline. The colony was a tertiary priority, overshadowed by the richer viceroyalties of New Spain and Peru and now eclipsed by the existential crisis in the homeland. The archival trail from Seville shows a steep decline in allocations for West Florida after 1805. Funds intended for fortifications were diverted. Requests for additional troops were filed, acknowledged, and forgotten.

Delassus understood his precarious position. His letters to Folch, filled with the formal courtesy of military correspondence, convey a mounting anxiety. He reported the arrival of more American families, the clearing of new land beyond the reach of any surveyor, the open discussion in taverns and at landings about the rights of men under a deposed king. He had no power to stop any of it. His strategy, therefore, was one of appeasement and theater. He hosted dinners for leading planters at the governor’s residence, a modest house near the fort. He listened to their grievances about property titles and commercial restrictions. He offered assurances of his commitment to order. The goal was to buy time, to maintain the illusion of control until reinforcements might arrive from Havana or until the political crisis in Europe resolved.

But the administrative machinery had seized. Madrid, under French occupation, issued no clear directives. Havana, preoccupied with the defense of Cuba and the flow of silver from Mexico, had no resources to spare for a distant, money-losing outpost. Folch, in Pensacola, was trapped in the same cycle of neglect. When Delassus wrote to him in the summer of 1810, formally requesting military support in the face of growing “agitation,” Folch could only promise to inquire with Cuba. He knew it was a futile gesture. In a confidential assessment to his superiors later that year, Folch concluded that a relief expedition would be useless; the province was already lost in everything but name.

The military weakness was merely the most visible symptom. The Spanish legal and administrative framework in West Florida had become a hollow shell. Land grants issued by previous governors were caught in a bureaucratic limbo, awaiting confirmation from a captive king. Courts were inactive. The flow of official communications, once the lifeblood of empire, slowed to a trickle. For the planters of Feliciana, this institutional paralysis was a daily frustration. A disputed property line, a question of inheritance, a debt to be collected—all required a functioning state. Spain could no longer provide one.

What remained was the symbol: the flag of Spain flying over Fort San Carlos, and the person of the governor. Delassus, in his polished uniform, represented the last thread of formal sovereignty. Everyone, including Delassus, knew the thread was frayed to a single fiber. His few dozen soldiers were not an army of occupation. They were a ceremonial guard for a dying regime. The real forces in the district—the armed planters, their overseers, the itinerant frontiersmen—outnumbered them a hundred to one. They watched the fort not with fear, but with calculation. The power vacuum was now complete. All that was required was a slight push, and the entire delicate performance would collapse.

The influx began as a trickle in the 1780s, a migration of individuals and families lured by cheap, fertile land and Spanish promises of religious tolerance. After the 1795 Treaty of San Lorenzo, known as Pinckney’s Treaty, guaranteed American navigation rights on the Mississippi and established the 31st parallel as the border, the trickle became a flood. The treaty did more than draw a line on a map; it signaled American ascendancy and Spanish retreat. To an ambitious farmer from Georgia or the Carolinas, weighed down by depleted soil and crowded valleys, the Spanish offer was irresistible. For a modest fee and an oath of allegiance to the crown, a man could acquire a tract of land in the Feliciana—the “happy lands”—larger than any he could hope for east of the Appalachians.

The settlers who came were, as a group, characterized in Spanish correspondence and later American petitions, overwhelmingly Protestant, primarily Baptist, Methodist, and Presbyterian, from the southern backcountry. Their culture was rooted in Anglo-American common law, not the Spanish Siete Partidas. Their economy was built on slave labor. They brought with them not just plows and seed but enslaved African Americans, whose forced labor would transform the loess bluffs into a belt of cotton and sugarcane plantations. A Spanish census of the Baton Rouge district from 1805, cited in American State Papers, recorded a population of 1,400, but this count included only Spanish subjects. It ignored the estimated 4,000 to 6,000 Anglo-Americans and their enslaved workers who already dominated the region. The demographic takeover was not a future threat; it was a present, overwhelming fact.

This new population created a cultural and legal fault line. Spanish civil law, based on Roman principles, governed inheritance, property, and contracts. It included provisions for community property in marriage and certain protections for enslaved people that were alien to the Anglo system. The American settlers, as their later petitions and court challenges showed, found Spanish law cumbersome and opaque. They wanted clear, absolute title to their land, not a grant awaiting confirmation from a distant and distracted crown. They wanted disputes settled by juries of their peers, not by a solitary Spanish alcalde. The Spanish administrative system, already strained, was incapable of processing the demands of this booming, foreign population. Land grant applications piled up in Baton Rouge and Pensacola, unanswered. The resulting uncertainty bred resentment and a perception, carefully documented in settlers’ letters, that Spanish rule was not just weak but inherently unjust.

Religion deepened the divide. While Spain officially tolerated Protestant worship in private homes, Catholicism remained the state religion. Public offices were reserved for Catholics. For Baptist preachers who had fought for disestablishment in the United States, this was a galling reminder of second-class status. Religious gatherings became not just spiritual exercises but forums for the exchange of grievances and revolutionary ideas. The sermons echoed with the language of liberty and resistance to tyranny, a direct transplant of American revolutionary rhetoric. This ideological framework provided a ready-made justification for challenging Spanish authority. When the crisis of 1808 erupted, the settlers did not see a legitimate monarch in captivity; they saw a tyrant overthrown, a contract voided.

The economic engine of this society was slavery. The institution expanded rapidly under Spanish rule, as the colonial government, eager for revenue, placed no restrictions on the importation of enslaved people. Inventories from probate records in the East and West Feliciana districts show a sharp increase in both the number of enslaved individuals and their monetary value as cotton cultivation took hold. This created a propertied elite with a profound interest in stability and legal security—the very things Spain could no longer guarantee. The planters’ wealth was mobile, rooted in human capital and cash crops, not in loyalty to a flag. Their primary market was the United States down the Mississippi to New Orleans. Their trade connections, credit lines, and often their family ties ran north and east, not to Havana or Pensacola.

By 1810, the Feliciana districts were an American society operating within a decaying Spanish shell. English was the common tongue. County-style militias, organized by plantation district, were the effective military force. The local judges, though nominally appointed by the governor, were themselves planters who applied a rough blend of common-law principles. Governor Delassus presided over this reality like a guest in his own home. He could issue a proclamation, but he relied on men like John H. Johnson and John Mills to ensure it was followed. These men were the de facto government, the intersection where Spanish authority met American power. Their cooperation was voluntary, maintained by Delassus’s diplomacy and their own temporary preference for order.

That preference began to fracture as the institutional paralysis worsened. A lawsuit over a disputed slave, a challenge to a land grant, a shipment of cotton held up by a customs agent citing a forgotten regulation—each incident documented in court records and personal papers, chipped away at the facade. The settlers had not come to build a Spanish province. They had come to build a version of the American South. They possessed the land, the labor, the arms, and the ideological framework for self-rule. All they lacked was the formal political structure. As one planter wrote in a letter to a relative in Tennessee in the summer of 1810, “We are in every respect but name a part of the American union already. The only question is when we shall make the name conform to the fact.” The Spanish flag over Fort San Carlos was not the symbol of a living government. It was a historical artifact, awaiting removal.

The first clear rupture in the tense peace came not from the settled planters, but from the frontier's rougher edges. In 1804, three brothers—Reuben, Nathan, and Samuel Kemper—launched a private war against Spanish authority. Their story, pieced together from Spanish military reports, American newspaper accounts, and later court depositions, serves as a bloody prologue to the events of 1810. The Kempers were not wealthy planters but frontiersmen of volatile temper, entangled in a bitter land dispute with a Spanish official. When the colonial courts ruled against them, they chose violence.

In the summer of 1804, Reuben Kemper gathered a band of about thirty men, a mix of American settlers and adventurers. Their target was the Spanish outpost at Bayou Sara, a minor customs station on the Mississippi north of Baton Rouge. The raid was less a military operation than a brutal shakedown. As documented in the subsequent complaint filed by the Spanish commandant, the Kempers seized the post, plundered goods, and took the Spanish officers prisoner. They then marched their captives toward the Mississippi, intending to sell them as slaves, a plan abandoned only after negotiations. The act was a flagrant, personal assault on Spanish sovereignty.

Emboldened, Reuben Kemper set his sights higher. That fall, he and his men advanced on Baton Rouge itself. His force, never more than fifty strong, was too small to assault Fort San Carlos. As Governor Grand-Pré’s frantic dispatches to Havana describe, they laid siege to the town, cutting off supplies and threatening its inhabitants. The siege was militarily ineffectual but psychologically seismic. For weeks, the symbol of Spanish power was bottled up in its own fort by a handful of armed Americans. The garrison, though larger than Kemper’s band, remained passive. The message to every settler in the district was clear: Spanish power was a paper tiger.

The rebellion collapsed as quickly as it began. A militia of local planters, still nominally loyal to Spain and fearing broader chaos, mobilized under John Mills and John H. Johnson. They drove Kemper’s band back across the Mississippi. The Spanish authorities issued warrants for the brothers’ arrest. Reuben Kemper escaped into American territory, becoming a fugitive and a folk hero to the most restive elements on the frontier. His failed insurrection revealed the latent violence simmering below the surface and the willingness of American settlers to take up arms against the crown when their interests were crossed. It also revealed the planters’ pragmatic limit: they would suppress chaos that threatened their property, even if it meant temporarily propping up Spanish rule.

The official American response to the Kemper raids was one of stern disapproval. President Thomas Jefferson’s administration, and later James Madison’s, publicly denounced the brothers as filibusterers and outlaws. Secretary of State James Madison, in a letter to the Spanish minister in Washington, condemned the attacks as “lawless aggression” and affirmed American neutrality. This public stance was a necessary diplomatic fiction. Privately, as correspondence between Governor William C.C. Claiborne of Orleans Territory and Washington reveals, American officials watched the instability with keen interest. They understood the Kempers, however inconvenient, were agents of a demographic and political inevitability. The raids applied pressure on an already weakening Spain, a pressure that aligned with American territorial ambitions.

The Kemper episode established a pattern that would define the next six years: localized American aggression, a spasmodic Spanish response, and official U.S. denials that masked tacit acceptance. Reuben Kemper himself faded from the spotlight but did not disappear. He drifted through the borderlands, a known quantity. When a more organized rebellion began to form in 1810, the memory of his 1804 stand was fresh. It proved Spanish forces could be challenged with impunity. It also served as a cautionary tale for the wealthier planters: reckless violence was counterproductive. The next revolt would need the sanction of property and the guise of legality.

The fallout from the Kemper raids exposed the fundamental contradiction of American policy. The United States government coveted West Florida. President Jefferson had famously asserted the territory was included in the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, a claim Spain vehemently rejected. Yet America could not be seen to openly foment rebellion against a nation with which it was at peace. The solution was a policy of strategic patience and clandestine encouragement. American territory across the Mississippi became a safe haven for plotters. Arms and information flowed easily across the river. Spanish protests to Washington, preserved in the diplomatic archives, were met with polite expressions of regret and promises to uphold the law—promises that were functionally unenforceable on the chaotic frontier.

By 1808, with Spain crippled by Napoleon’s invasion, the conditions the Kempers had exploited became universal. Their personal grievance was now a collective one. The land title disputes, the religious restrictions, the legal alienation—all were shared by the entire Anglo population. What had been a fringe act of banditry in 1804 was, by 1810, evolving into a mainstream political movement. The planters who had chased Reuben Kemper away were now, themselves, contemplating rebellion. They had simply learned from his failure. Their revolt would not be a ragged siege by fifty men. It would be the organized takeover of a state. The Kempers had struck a match in the dark. Six years later, the tinder was ready for a conflagration.

The invasion began in the spring of 1808. Napoleon Bonaparte’s armies, already occupying Portugal, turned on his ally, Spain. In a swift coup, French troops seized key fortresses, captured King Charles IV and his heir, Ferdinand VII, and installed Napoleon’s brother, Joseph, on the Spanish throne. The news, traveling by fast packet ship, reached the Americas by early summer. The effect was not merely political; it was existential. The Spanish Empire, a global network sustained by loyalty to the crown, had lost its sovereign. In Madrid, a puppet regime issued orders in Joseph’s name. In resistance, a provisional junta formed in Seville, claiming authority in Ferdinand’s name. The empire fractured at its center.

For the colonial administrators in Pensacola and Baton Rouge, the crisis caused paralysis. To whom did they report? Whose orders were legitimate? Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, as his dispatches to Havana show, spent months in agonized uncertainty. He received conflicting directives from officials loyal to the Seville junta and from those still recognizing the authority of the captive king. The bureaucratic chain of command, the meticulous system of consultas and real cédulas, dissolved into noise. Funds allocated for West Florida’s garrison and civil administration were suddenly diverted to the Peninsular War, the life-or-death struggle in Spain itself. A request from Folch for clarification on a matter of customs policy might take a year to receive a reply, if it received one at all.

The military consequences were immediate and severe. The Spanish army, once the terror of Europe, was shattered, its remnants fighting a brutal guerrilla war on home soil. No reinforcements could be spared for a colonial backwater. The annual situado, the subsidy from the Mexican treasury that paid soldiers and officials in Florida, dried up. Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus in Baton Rouge, already managing a garrison of fewer than fifty men, now faced the prospect of no pay for his troops. A report he filed in late 1809, preserved in the military archives in Seville, listed essential supplies that had not arrived in eighteen months: uniforms, musket flints, gunpowder. The fort’s two aging cannon had not been test-fired in years for fear of wasting powder. Delassus’s soldiers, their morale a persistent concern in his letters, were effectively marooned at a distant outpost, their allegiance to a crown that could not feed or pay them.

The legal foundations of colonial rule eroded just as quickly. Spanish authority in America derived from the king’s person. Land grants required his seal, or that of his legitimate viceroy. Court rulings were appealed to his audiencias. The capture of Ferdinand VII created a void at the heart of this legal fiction. A land title issued by a governor appointed by the Seville junta—was it valid? What about one issued by a holdover official still acting in the name of the imprisoned king? In the Feliciana districts, this theoretical problem became a practical crisis. Anglo-American planters who had accepted Spanish grants in good faith now saw their property rights cast into doubt by a succession crisis three thousand miles away. John H. Johnson, holding a grant for a thousand arpents of prime cotton land, had no way to secure a definitive patent. The system of recordation stalled. This legal limbo, as traced in the petitions these planters later sent to the United States Congress, transformed administrative delay into a tangible grievance. Their wealth was tied to the land. Without clear title, that wealth was insecure.

The paralysis seeped into every function of the state. The colonial mail system, which carried official correspondence and commercial reports, became irregular, then unreliable. A letter from Delassus to Folch in Pensacola sent in June 1810 did not receive a reply until October, and that reply contained no instructions, only expressions of shared anxiety. Courts in Baton Rouge, which had rarely been busy, ceased to function for lack of a presiding magistrate with unquestioned authority. Minor disputes between settlers festered into feuds, resolved by personal force or not at all. The Spanish monopoly on notarial services broke down, leading to a proliferation of privately witnessed deeds and contracts, further muddying the legal waters.

For the Anglo settlers, watching this imperial collapse, the lesson was clear. Spain was not just weak; it was incapable. The government that could not pay its soldiers, confirm a land title, or operate a court was no government at all. The philosophical question of allegiance to a captive king became, in the steamy heat of the Feliciana, a question of practical necessity. Who would keep the peace? Who would secure property? Who would defend the district from external threat or internal disorder? The answer, evident by 1810, was no one in a Spanish uniform.

The crisis in Europe also reshaped the strategic calculations of the other major power in the region: the United States. President James Madison and his Secretary of State, Robert Smith, monitored the Peninsular War through diplomatic channels. American ministers in London and Paris sent detailed reports on the Spanish resistance. Madison understood that Spain’s distress was America’s opportunity. With the mother country fighting for its life, it could not possibly reinforce its American colonies. The time for pressing the United States’ long-standing claim to West Florida, based on Jefferson’s disputed interpretation of the Louisiana Purchase, would never be riper. Yet overt military aggression was diplomatically dangerous, likely to provoke Britain or France. The preferred method, as revealed in the confidential correspondence between Madison and Governor William C.C. Claiborne in New Orleans, was to let the internal pressure within West Florida build to a breaking point. The American government would watch, wait, and be ready to catch the territory when it fell.

In Baton Rouge, Governor Delassus performed the daily rituals of a government that had ceased to exist. He reviewed the morning guard mount. He signed papers. He dined with planters. But the dispatches he sent to Pensacola and Havana, increasingly urgent in tone, went unanswered. He was no longer a governor administering a province. He was a caretaker watching over an abandoned estate, listening for the footsteps of the new owners. They were already at the gate, speaking English, and they carried guns.

The confluence was now complete. Spanish imperial collapse, a burgeoning Anglo-American population, and a diplomatic stalemate between nations had created a territory in a state of suspended animation. West Florida in the summer of 1810 was not a Spanish province, nor was it yet American. It was a gray zone, a political vacuum where the normal rules of sovereignty did not apply. In such a vacuum, as historical precedent from the Swiss cantons to the American colonies showed, local initiative rushes in. The question was not if the settlers would act, but how and under what banner.

The administrative decay was total. As his letters to Governor Folch in Pensacola make clear, Carlos Dehault Delassus presided over a phantom government. His garrison, unpaid and undersupplied for over a year, existed on paper as a military force but in reality functioned as a small, isolated community struggling to feed itself. A Spanish inventory of Fort San Carlos from early 1810, cited in later American claims for compensation, listed the garrison’s effective strength at twenty-eight fit soldiers. Their muskets were in poor repair. The fort’s wooden palisades, erected decades earlier, were rotten in places. Delassus could issue a proclamation, but he had no means to enforce it beyond the persuasive power of his own person. His authority extended only as far as the willingness of men like John Mills and John H. Johnson to humor him.

This local elite, the planters of Feliciana, now faced a dilemma. Their wealth, tied to land and enslaved laborers, demanded stability and predictable law. The Spanish system had provided neither for years. The European crisis had severed the last threads. As John Rhea would later write in a letter to President Madison, the settlers found themselves “in a most peculiar situation… owing allegiance to a King who is a prisoner in a foreign land, and governed by a commandant whose orders are doubted even by himself.” The legal limbo over land titles was the sharpest goad. A planter’s entire fortune—his house, his fields, the hundreds of enslaved people who worked them—rested on a grant that might be invalidated by a shift in the distant political winds of Seville or Cadiz. This was an intolerable risk.

Simultaneously, the demographic reality overwhelmed. Spanish census figures became an exercise in nostalgia. The actual population of the Baton Rouge district, as estimated from tax rolls and militia lists compiled by the settlers themselves, approached ten thousand by 1810. Over eighty percent were Anglo-American or enslaved African Americans brought by them. Spanish subjects were a minority within their own jurisdiction. This population was not passive. It was organized. The settlers had divided the territory into informal “beats” or precincts for policing and tax collection. They maintained slave patrols. They formed ad-hoc militias for common defense. They operated, in essence, a parallel government that handled the daily business of society. The Spanish apparatus was a superfluous layer, a ceremonial shell.

The diplomatic stalemate between the United States and Spain provided the crucial cover for revolution. President Madison wanted West Florida. His predecessor, Jefferson, had insisted the territory was part of the Louisiana Purchase, a claim bounded by the Perdido River. Spain had rejected this for seven years. With Napoleon’s invasion, Madrid was in no position to resist American pressure militarily, but overt U.S. aggression would have violated American neutrality and risked European entanglement. Madison’s solution, as revealed in the confidential instructions sent to Governor William C.C. Claiborne in New Orleans, was a policy of “watchful waiting.” The United States would not invade. It would instead position itself as the natural and inevitable recipient of the territory when its inhabitants, of their own volition, threw off Spanish rule. American territory across the Mississippi became a safe haven and a supply line. Arms could be procured in Natchez. Sympathetic officials there, as Spanish diplomatic protests noted, turned a blind eye to the movement of men and materiel.

Thus, by mid-1810, all the elements for a locally-driven revolt were in alignment. The imperial power was absent. The local population was cohesive, armed, and motivated by concrete grievances over property and law. A neighboring power coveted the territory and would support, or at least not hinder, a change in regime. The only missing piece was a catalyst—a specific event that would transform simmering discontent into organized action.

That catalyst arrived in the form of a rumor documented in several settlers’ diaries from June 1810. Word spread through the plantations that Governor Folch, in a desperate attempt to reassert control from Pensacola, planned to dispatch a company of regular troops to Baton Rouge to relieve Delassus and disarm the Anglo militia. The rumor was false—Folch had no troops to spare—but its effect was electric. It presented a direct, physical threat to the settlers’ de facto autonomy. The planters’ militia was their guarantee of security, the instrument that protected their property from slave rebellion, frontier bandits, and now, potentially, from a resurgent Spanish state. To disarm was to become vulnerable.

The false rumor performed a vital function: it created a deadline. It forced the leading men of the district to move from private complaint to public consultation. If Spanish power returned, however weakly, they had to decide whether to submit or to resist. Submission meant a return to the paralyzing uncertainty of the past five years. Resistance meant seizing control of their own destiny. The choice, for men steeped in the language of 1776, was no choice at all.

In late June 1810, riders went out from the major plantations. They carried a simple summons, calling for a meeting of representatives from the four districts—Feliciana, Baton Rouge, St. Helena, and St. Tammany—to be held at St. John’s Plains, a neutral ground near the Thompson’s Creek ferry. The purpose, as stated in the circular, was to discuss “the common defense and the security of our property.” It was a deliberately vague formulation, one that could be explained to Governor Delassus as a loyal assembly to assist his weakened administration. But every man who received the summons understood its true meaning. They were not being called to advise a governor. They were called to become a government.

The power vacuum was now absolute. Spain had receded to a memory. America waited across the river. Into the stillness between them, a group of planters, lawyers, and militia officers prepared to step. They would not be filibusterers like Reuben Kemper, acting for personal gain. They would be convention delegates, acting in the name of the people. Their revolution would be clad in the procedural robes of a congress, their violence sanctioned by votes. The gray zone was about to give birth to a republic.
