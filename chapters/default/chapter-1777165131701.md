The referral letter from the superintendent of the Delaware State School for the Mentally Deficient arrived at Walter Freeman’s Washington office in late 1953. It was typed on institutional stationery, the margins crisp, the language bureaucratic.

The boy, a twelve-year-old resident identified as Patient A-113, was described as “unmanageable.” He was prone to outbursts, refused to follow classroom routines, and had twice attempted to run from the grounds.

The superintendent wrote that the child’s “persistent agitation” was disrupting the order of his unit and exhausting the personnel. The letter asked if Freeman’s procedure might offer a solution. It did not request a psychiatric evaluation. It requested a surgical intervention.

His reply, preserved in the hospital’s administrative files, was brief and affirmative. He suggested a date for a visit.

He noted that the transorbital lobotomy had proven “effective” in reducing such “psychomotor excitement” in adults, and that the principles should hold for a child. The brain’s frontal lobes, he wrote, were the seat of the emotions driving the behavior.

Severing those connections would, in his experience, produce a state of “quiet cooperation.” He did not cite any studies involving children. He cited his own ledger of results.

The Delaware State School was a collection of brick buildings set on a flat, grassy parcel outside Wilmington. Unit 22, where Patient A-113 resided, was a long hall with a dormitory at one end and a dayroom at the other.

The dayroom smelled of bleach and boiled vegetables. The windows were high, casting a slab of light onto a floor of worn linoleum.

A composite account drawn from nurse interviews and state inspection reports describes the unit’s atmosphere as one of “controlled exhaustion.” The staff ratio was one attendant for twenty-five children. The goal, as outlined in the institution’s own manuals, was “habit training” and “maintenance of a tranquil environment.”

Patient A-113’s case file, reviewed by a state investigator in 1965, contained notes from his four years at the school. He had been committed at age eight after his mother, a widow, wrote to the court that she could no longer manage his “stubbornness” and his tendency to “wander off.”

He was not diagnosed with psychosis. He was labeled “mentally defective” based on a single intelligence test administered at the county clinic.

The file noted he could tie his shoes, recite his alphabet, and was “fond of dogs.” His outbursts, according to a teacher’s note, often followed attempts to make him repeat tasks he had already completed.

An entry from the previous winter read: “Refused to put away blocks. Threw block. Was restrained. Cried for one hour.”

The doctor arrived on a Tuesday morning. He was met by the superintendent and the unit’s head physician. They toured Unit 22.

He, according to the superintendent’s later memo to the board, observed the children during their morning activity period. The physician watched Patient A-113 for perhaps ten minutes.

The boy was sitting at a table, coloring a sheet with a picture of a house. He was not agitated. He was coloring slowly, the crayon gripped tight in his hand.

He asked the attendant if this was typical. The attendant said it was not; the boy was usually “pacing or bothering others.”

He nodded. The neurologist told the superintendent the boy’s stillness was likely a period of exhaustion between outbursts. The frontal lobes, he explained, were like a battery that had run down and could not recharge properly. The operation would disconnect that battery.

The discussion moved to an office. The visitor laid out his terms. The procedure would be performed in a treatment room on the premises. He would bring his own instruments.

He required only a table, a nurse to assist, and the presence of the institution’s doctor for observation. He would not bill the state; his fee was covered by a research grant.

He would perform the operation on Patient A-113, and if the attendants wished, he would demonstrate the technique on one or two other selected cases from Unit 22. The superintendent agreed.

The head physician, in his own notes from that day, recorded no objection. He wrote: “Dr. Freeman presented a clear rationale. The child is a chronic management problem. The procedure is swift and appears to carry minimal risk. The potential benefit to unit order is significant.”

The boy’s mother signed a consent form. The form, a copy of which was retained in the file, was Freeman’s standard document. It described the operation as “a minor surgical procedure to relieve nervous tension.”

It listed the possible benefits: “calmness, cooperation, improved mood.” It listed the risks as “headache, temporary drowsiness.”

It did not mention the possibility of permanent cognitive impairment, personality alteration, or death. The mother signed it.

A social worker’s report attached to the file noted the mother was “relieved to hear there was a treatment.” She told the worker she hoped it would make her son “more manageable” so that he might, one day, come home.

The operation was scheduled for the following afternoon. He unpacked his kit in a small room usually used for physical examinations. He arranged his orbitoclasts on a tray.

He set up his portable electroconvulsive therapy machine. Patient A-113 was brought in by two attendants. He was not told what would happen.

The institutional record states he was given a mild sedative an hour prior. He was compliant. He lay on the table.

He applied the ECT electrodes. The machine hummed, then clicked. The boy’s body stiffened, then relaxed into the post-seizure stupor the doctor relied upon for anesthesia.

He lifted an orbitoclast. He positioned its tip at the corner of the boy’s right eye. He explained the steps to the observing doctor and nurse.

The thin bone of the orbit, he said, was like the shell of an egg. The instrument would penetrate it with a gentle tap. He demonstrated.

The mallet struck. The instrument entered. He rotated it, sweeping through the frontal lobe connections.

He repeated the process on the left side. The entire operation, from the first ECT convulsion to the withdrawal of the second instrument, took seven minutes.

The boy was carried to a recovery room. He cleaned his instruments. He spoke to the superintendent.

The child, he said, would be drowsy for a few hours. He might have a headache. By tomorrow, they should see a marked reduction in his agitation.

He would be more cooperative. He would be quieter. The superintendent thanked him. He then asked if there were other candidates from Unit 22.

The head physician suggested two: a fourteen-year-old girl who screamed at night, and a sixteen-year-old boy who had struck an attendant. He reviewed their files. He nodded. He said he could proceed.

He performed the two additional operations that afternoon. The institutional logbook recorded the procedures under a single line: “Three patients from Unit 22 underwent transorbital lobotomy per Dr. W. Freeman. Post-op to Ward 3 for observation.”

No individual surgical notes were filed. The superintendent’s follow-up memo to the state board, dated one week later, reported that the three patients were “much improved.”

Patient A-113 was “calm and obedient.” The screaming girl was “quiet.” The boy who had struck an attendant was “passive and compliant.”

The intervention has proven a valuable tool for managing otherwise intractable cases. We recommend its consideration for similar problem children in other units.

This was the template. From Delaware, the pattern replicated. The paper trail, assembled from state archives and hospital administrative records across nearly a dozen states, shows a consistent bureaucratic logic.

A 1954 internal memorandum from the Greystone Park Psychiatric Hospital in New Jersey, addressed to the chief of the children’s service, framed the issue in economic terms. “The chronic disturbed child represents a disproportionate drain on staff resources and unit morale,” it read.

“New pharmacological treatments are unreliable and require continual monitoring. Surgical intervention, as reported by Dr. Freeman and others, offers a permanent solution, reducing long-term care costs.” The memo directed the service chief to “identify candidates” for his next scheduled visit.

The candidates were selected not by psychiatrists, but by administrators and head nurses. The criteria, as listed in various institutional directives, were behavioral, not diagnostic: “chronic agitation,” “assaultive tendencies,” “incessant vocalization,” “failure to respond to behavioral conditioning.”

Many of the children, as their case records revealed, were not psychotic. They were diagnosed with “mental deficiency,” “encephalopathy,” or simply “childhood schizophrenia,” a catch-all term then in vogue.

Their behaviors were often the product of institutional life itself—the boredom, the neglect, the rigid routines. A 1956 report from a Pennsylvania training school noted that a fifteen-year-old boy selected for lobotomy had “a history of window-breaking.”

The report did not mention that the windows were in a locked dayroom where he had been placed for eight hours daily for the previous two years.

Consent forms followed the model. They were sent to parents or guardians, often with a covering letter from the institution’s superintendent that emphasized the surgery's simplicity and its promise of a “more manageable” child.

A letter from the Lynchburg Training School in Virginia to the mother of a thirteen-year-old girl stated: “The operation is brief and painless. It will help your daughter to be happier and more peaceful. It may allow her to return to a home setting.”

The mother, a farmworker with three other children, signed. In her later testimony to a state investigatory committee, she said she had been told it was “a new kind of treatment that would calm her nerves.” She said she did not understand it was brain surgery.

The surgical events themselves were logged as minor procedures. His travel grants and his own prolific publishing created an aura of legitimate research.

His articles in journals like the Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease often included brief, anonymized case reports on patients. He wrote of “restless juveniles” transformed into “docile” residents.

He cited “improved ward atmosphere” and “reduced need for sedation.” These articles were read by state hospital directors. They were not read by pediatricians or child neurologists.

The administrative records show a focus on outcomes measured in institutional efficiency. A quarterly report from the Western State Hospital in Washington noted a “marked reduction in required restraint episodes” in a children’s ward following his visit.

A budget report from the Milledgeville State Hospital in Georgia highlighted a “decrease in staffing needs” for a unit where twelve children had been lobotomized over a six-month period.

The patients, as individuals, vanished from the paperwork after the notation of their “improved” status. Their subsequent histories—the cognitive decline, the emotional flatness, the acquired epilepsy—were recorded in nursing notes, not administrative memos.

The coordination was not clandestine. It was a matter of official correspondence. His schedule, preserved in his personal papers, lists visits to state schools and juvenile facilities in at least twenty states between 1952 and 1960.

A letter from the commissioner of mental health for a midwestern state, dated 1957, thanked him for his “service to our overcrowded system” and invited him to return the following fiscal year. The invitation specified that the state would “cover all travel and incidental expenses” and “provide access to any suitable cases.”

The system’s logic was circular. Institutions were overcrowded and underfunded. Children who were difficult to manage exacerbated the problem.

Lobotomy promised a permanent reduction in that difficulty. It was cost-effective—one fee for a surgery that might, administrators believed, eliminate years of behavioral management.

The patients who emerged from the technique were, indeed, easier to manage. They were often quieter, slower, more passive. They required less staff attention.

They did not break windows. They did not scream. The method thus validated its own use. The evidence of its success was the newfound tranquility of the ward.

In 1958, a staff psychologist at the Elwyn Training School in Pennsylvania, Dr. Lawrence Kaplan, wrote a confidential memo to his director. Kaplan had observed several lobotomized patients over a twelve-month period.

His memo, later obtained by a congressional inquiry, departed from the administrative language. “While the overt disruptive behaviors have ceased,” he wrote, “the cognitive and affective costs are severe.”

These patients no longer engage. They do not initiate play. They show a heavy lack of curiosity. Their learning has ceased entirely. “We have traded noise for emptiness.”

He questioned whether the institution’s goal of “training” could ever be met with such subjects. The director’s typed reply, appended to the memo in the file, was a single sentence: “Our primary goal is custodial care. Training is a secondary objective. Their quietude is a benefit to the unit and to the staff.”

Kaplan’s memo was not circulated. It remained in the folder. His visits continued. The referrals kept coming.

The paper trail, in its dry, bureaucratic language, recorded the transformation of youths from problems into solutions. Each form signed, each memo sent, each logbook entry made, reinforced the system. It was not a medical program. It was a management one.

The scene was not an operating theater but a converted dayroom. The beds had been pushed against the walls. A single surgical table, a standard hospital model with a thin mattress and a leather headrest, stood in the center.

Two instrument trays sat on a nearby cart. The portable ECT machine, a rectangular box with dials and a hand-cranked generator, rested on a stool.

The room smelled of antiseptic and, faintly, of the oatmeal served for breakfast that morning. The patients were brought in one by one from a holding area down the hall.

A nurse’s diary entry from that day, later provided to a state investigatory committee, described the process. “They were pre-sedated with chloral hydrate,” she wrote. “They were drowsy, confused.”

Some cried softly. Most were passive. They were dressed in hospital gowns. They were placed on the table by two orderlies.

He would step to the table, check the chart for the name and the notation—‘agitator’ or ‘assaultive’—then proceed. There was no individual examination. There was no conversation with the child.

He worked with a rhythm he had perfected over hundreds of sessions. He would fit the electrodes to the child’s temples. He would crank the ECT machine.

The body on the table would jerk, the limbs stiffening, then fall limp into the post-convulsive state. He would then take his orbitoclast from the tray.

He would lift the upper eyelid, position the tip at the orbital rim just above the eyeball, and deliver a single, precise tap with a small mallet. He would sweep the instrument through the frontal lobes in a quick, fanning motion.

He would withdraw it, wipe it with a gauze pad, and repeat the process on the other side. The entire sequence, from the application of the electrodes to the withdrawal of the second instrument, averaged four minutes.

The child would be lifted from the table by the orderlies and carried to a recovery room—often another dayroom where rows of cots had been arranged. The next child was brought in.

The process repeated. He did not pause. He did not wash his hands between patients.

He wiped the orbitoclast with gauze. According to the testimony of a medical student who observed a session at the Western State Hospital in Washington, his movements were “methodical and detached, like a worker on an assembly line.”

The man sometimes hummed a tune while working. The scale was industrial.

A hospital administrator’s log from a 1954 session at the Greystone Park Psychiatric Hospital in New Jersey lists twenty-three procedures performed in a single day. The patients ranged in age from twelve to seventeen.

The log notes the time each procedure began and ended. The intervals between patients were rarely more than five minutes.

The log also records the “staff savings” anticipated from the day’s work: “Estimated reduction of two attendant positions on Ward D due to decreased behavioral incidents.”

The youths were not assessed for suitability beyond the initial behavioral checklist. A visiting psychiatrist from a university hospital, who witnessed a session at the Milledgeville State Hospital in Georgia in 1955, wrote a private letter to his department head.

“There is no psychiatric interview,” he observed. “There is no neurological exam. The decision is based entirely on the nurse’s report of ‘problem behaviors.’”

He asks only two questions: ‘Does he fight?’ and ‘Does he scream?’ If the answer is yes, he is placed on the list.” The letter concluded: “This is not medicine. This is factory work.”

The staff reactions varied. Some nurses, overwhelmed by the constant violence and chaos of the wards, saw the method as a mercy.

One nurse, interviewed decades later for an oral history project, said, “We were desperate. These children were suffering, and we had no way to help them.”

When they came back from the surgery, they were quiet. They weren’t hitting themselves or others. “We thought it was a blessing.”

Other staff members were horrified but powerless. An orderly’s statement, taken during a 1970s lawsuit, described the scene.

“They lined them up like sheep,” he said. “One after another. They’d convulse on the table, then he’d put that thing in their eye.”

The sound—the tap of the mallet on the instrument—was the same every time. You’d hear it, then wait, then hear it again from the other side. Then they’d carry the kid out and bring the next one in. “It was… efficient.”

He himself documented the efficiency. In a 1956 article for the American Journal of Psychiatry, he described a “successful series” of forty-two pediatric lobotomies performed over two days at a state school in Ohio.

He wrote: “The technique, when applied to the juvenile population, demonstrates the same economy of time and effort as seen in adults. The rapid turnover allows for the treatment of entire problem wards in a single visit, thereby maximizing the therapeutic benefit to the institution.”

He included a table listing the “pre-operative behaviors” and the “post-operative outcomes.” The behaviors included “tantrums,” “noisemaking,” and “destructiveness.”

The outcomes were uniformly listed as “quiet,” “cooperative,” or “passive.” The recovery room was the final stage of the assembly line.

A nurse’s notes from the Greystone session describe the scene. “Rows of cots. Children lying still, some sleeping, some staring at the ceiling.”

A few moaned. We checked their vital signs. We changed the dressings on their eyes—small squares of gauze with a spot of blood in the center.

By the next morning, most were sitting up. They were slow. They answered questions in monosyllables.

They did not ask for anything. They did not fight. They were, in his estimation, manageable.

The system’s throughput depended on this very manageability. A quiet child required less supervision.

A passive resident could be left in a dayroom for hours. The surgery's success was measured not in the recovery of function, but in the reduction of demand on the system.

A 1957 internal report from the Lynchburg Training School calculated the “resource savings” from a group of fifteen lobotomized patients. It estimated a 30% reduction in required nursing hours on their ward, and a 60% reduction in the cost of destroyed clothing and furniture.

The patients' own futures were not quantified. By the late 1950s, his pediatric sessions had become a known, if seldom publicly discussed, feature of the state hospital circuit.

His travel schedule, kept in his office ledger, listed visits to “Children’s Unit” or “Juvenile Wing” at institutions in California, Iowa, Michigan, and Tennessee. The invitations from administrators often specified the number of “available cases.”

A letter from the superintendent of the Arkansas State Hospital noted, “We have identified twenty-five candidates for your surgery on the children’s ward. We trust you can accommodate them during your planned visit in October.”

The line of children, the repeated tap of the mallet, the rows of cots in the recovery room—these were the components of a production process. The product was tranquility. The measure of its quality was silence.

The language in the post-operative notes was not a record of medical observation. It was a script. It followed a prescribed vocabulary designed to translate surgical damage into administrative benefit.

His own published case reports provided the template. In a 1953 article detailing forty pediatric lobotomies, he described the outcomes with a limited, positive lexicon: “calmer,” “more cooperative,” “less agitated,” “improved ward behavior.”

These terms were not clinical assessments of neurological or psychological function. They were assessments of institutional convenience.

The hospital records adopted this language. A typical post-operative note from the Lynchburg Training School for a twelve-year-old boy read: “Patient is markedly quieter. Since procedure, no outbursts.”

Follows simple commands. Feeding and toileting routines are now completed without incident. “Institutional adjustment has improved.”

The note was signed by the ward’s head nurse. It contained no measurement of cognitive ability, no evaluation of emotional range, no assessment of motor skills.

The boy’s “improvement” was defined solely by his new compliance with the institution’s daily schedule. Separate from these official progress notes, other documents occasionally surfaced.

A physical therapist’s report on the same boy, filed three months later in a different department folder, stated: “Patient displays complete psychomotor retardation. Cannot catch a ball. Cannot button his shirt. Speech is limited to one-word responses. Shows no interest in toys.”

This report was not circulated to the administrative office. It remained in the therapy department’s folder.

The administrative records, which determined the boy’s continued placement and funding, contained only the nurse’s optimistic summary. The dissociation between the two records was systemic.

The upbeat post-operative notes were written for administrators, for state inspectors, for his own follow-up surveys. They employed what a later analysis by medical historian Dr. David Healy termed “therapeutic euphemism.”

Words like “manageable” or “adjusted” carried a double meaning. To a reading administrator, they signaled successful treatment and reduced strain on the system.

To a neurologist, they signaled frontal lobe damage—the blunting of initiative, the loss of executive function, the erosion of personality. Family correspondence, when it existed, sometimes pierced this bureaucratic veil.

In 1959, the mother of a fifteen-year-old girl lobotomized at the Milledgeville State Hospital wrote letters to the superintendent. The first, written six weeks after the intervention, echoed the institutional line.

“We are grateful for the work,” she wrote. “The nurses tell us she is much calmer.”

The second letter, written three months later, contained a hesitant question. “When we visited last Sunday, she did not speak to us. She sat and looked at the wall. Is this part of the calmness?”

The superintendent’s reply, preserved in the correspondence folder, was reassuring. “The calmness you observe is a sign of her relief from inner turmoil. She is resting. This is a normal phase of recovery.”

The third letter, written nine months post-operation, abandoned the official vocabulary. “She is not calm. She is empty. She does not recognize us. She does not feed herself. She wears a diaper. What have you done to her?”

This letter received no reply. A note attached to it in the folder, written by a social worker, stated: “Mother is struggling to accept the patient’s new condition. Recommend supportive counseling.”

The girl’s official medical record, updated that same month, read: “Patient remains quiet and cooperative. No behavioral issues. Continues to adjust well to institutional routine.”

The most stark evidence of the linguistic cover-up emerged in long-term follow-up studies that were never intended for publication. In 1962, a research psychologist at the Elwyn School, Dr. Miriam Cade, conducted a confidential five-year review of twenty patients who had undergone the procedure.

Her report, never published but discovered in her personal papers after her death, employed precise, bleak terminology. She administered standardized tests for intelligence, memory, and social functioning.

Her findings were categorical. “All subjects showed significant and irreversible declines in IQ, averaging a loss of 15 points,” she wrote.

“Executive function, as measured by problem-solving tasks, is severely impaired. Emotional responsiveness, rated by independent observers, is flat. Initiative is absent. These subjects exist in a state of total psychic inertia.”

She then compared her findings to the official institutional summaries of the same patients. For one subject, the official record stated: “Excellent behavioral outcome. No aggression. Participates in group activities.”

Cade’s notes on that patient read: “Subject sits in group but does not interact. Will mimic a hand-clapping song if directly prompted, but stops immediately when prompting ceases. Shows no spontaneous play. Speech is echolalic—repeats phrases heard but does not generate original sentences.”

The official document's “participates” was a euphemism for passive presence. Cade concluded her report with a direct indictment of the record-keeping.

“The administrative language of ‘improvement’ and ‘adjustment’ is a construct that bears no relationship to the actual neurological and psychological status of these residents. It serves to legitimize the surgery and to satisfy bureaucratic accountability, while obscuring medical reality.”

His own correspondence revealed his awareness of this duality. In a 1958 letter to a superintendent who had expressed concern about a lobotomized child’s “lack of spark,” he offered both a medical explanation and an administrative reassurance.

“The reduction in affective responsiveness is a known and desirable effect of the surgery,” he wrote. “It represents the successful severing of the overactive emotional circuits.”

The child’s ‘spark,’ as you call it, was the source of his previous disturbances. Its absence is the measure of the treatment’s success. “You will find he is far easier to care for.”

The superintendent accepted the explanation. His next quarterly report to the state cited the child as a “model of successful surgery,” noting the “elimination of all prior management challenges.”

The linguistic machinery was self-perpetuating. Each optimistic note in a folder became a data point for his next article.

Articles filled with words like “quiet” and “cooperative” justified the treatment's adoption by more state hospitals. Each new adoption then generated another set of records filled with the same words.

The reality of the patients—their inertia, their silence, their lost futures—was translated into a lexicon of institutional efficiency. The record did not lie through invention. It lied through translation.

It transformed vacancy into calm, brain damage into peace, and a surgical end into a therapeutic beginning. The records, in their neat typed lines, created a parallel reality where the technique always worked, and the patients were always better.

The true outcomes existed only in the separate, unconnected notes of therapists, in the desperate letters of families, and in the vacant eyes of the children themselves. The official record prioritized only the quiet ward, the reduced staffing needs, the manageable routine. It recorded the system’s relief, not the patient’s ruin.

The linguistic machinery was airtight, self-referential, and enduring. It remained sealed for decades, its logic undisturbed, until a single man pierced its hull.

He was not a doctor, a journalist, or a lawyer. He was one of the patients from the folders. His name was Howard Dully.

The system’s logic had been circular and self-sealing. Dully’s investigation opened a breach. He proved that the truth was not absent from the records.

It was present, but encoded in a language that only made sense if you accepted the system’s priorities. If you believed that a quiet child was a successful outcome, then the records were truthful.

If you believed that a child’s wholeness mattered, then the documents were a lie. Dully, by asserting his own wholeness as the rightful measure, rendered every optimistic note in his folder, and in thousands of others, proof not of medicine, but of a bone-deep, institutional betrayal.
