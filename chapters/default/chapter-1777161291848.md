The first meeting of the convention at St. John’s Plains on July 15, 1810 left no minutes. No official roll call survives. Its location, a stretch of open ground near the home of planter John Mills was chosen not for convenience but for discretion, away from the ears of Spanish informants in Baton Rouge. What we know of its opening moments comes from fragmentary letters and later testimony, a reconstruction of whispers in the pines. The men who gathered were not yet rebels. They were petitioners. Their immediate grievance documented in a flurry of correspondence that spring was a specific, grinding frustration: the paralysis of the Spanish land-title system.

Under Spanish law settlers who had occupied and improved tracts for years were still considered mere “squatters” until their grants received final approval from the Captain General in Havana. That approval, dependent on a sluggish transatlantic bureaucracy, often never came. A man could plant cotton on a claim for a decade, build a house and outbuildings, and still lack a clear deed. This legal limbo choked credit and clouded inheritance. As the economic value of the Feliciana cotton lands skyrocketed, the insecurity became intolerable. The second complaint was judicial. Spanish law, administered by commandants who were military officers, not jurists felt arbitrary and alien to men steeped in English common law. There was no trial by jury. Procedures were opaque. The commandant’s word was often final.

The convention’s initial purpose, as stated in a circular sent to the four districts was to draft a respectful petition to Governor Delassus, urging administrative reforms. They would request a local land office to settle titles and the introduction of jury trials. It was an appeal for the rights of British subjects, an attempt to graft Anglo-Saxon legal customs onto a crumbling Spanish trunk. The tone was loyalist. John Rhea, elected to preside over the gathering was a careful man. A former congressman from Tennessee who had moved to the region for its fertile soil, he understood the power of procedure. His first act as chair, according to later accounts by attendees was to establish a committee to draft the petition. This was the safe, sanctioned path.

But the act of convening an extra-legal assembly of armed planters, beyond the governor’s summons was itself a revolutionary gesture. Each man who rode to St. John’s Plains that day had made a calculation. He had chosen to seek redress from his peers, not from his governor. The atmosphere, recalled in a letter from delegate William Barrow was one of wary seriousness. Men stood in small knots under the loblolly pines, speaking in low tones. They did not talk of independence. They discussed crop prices, the high cost of goods from New Orleans, and the incompetence of the commandant at Bayou Sara. Yet the subtext of every complaint was a declaration of self-sufficiency. These were men who managed large estates, commanded enslaved labor, and were accustomed to order. The spectacle of a penniless garrison in a rotting fort trying to govern them was becoming an absurdity they would no longer tolerate.

The petition they produced was a model of diplomatic phrasing. It addressed Delassus as “His Excellency” and professed the “attachment and loyalty” of the inhabitants. It then listed, with forensic clarity, the “grievances which threaten ruin to this flourishing district.” The land-title crisis led the list. The lack of impartial courts came second. The document concluded with a request for the governor’s “prompt and effectual interposition.” It was signed by Rhea and the other delegates and dispatched to Baton Rouge.

Delassus received it with apparent sympathy. He promised to forward their concerns to his superiors. He did so, but the delegates knew the pipeline to Havana was clogged, possibly severed. The promise was a dead letter. The petition had served its purpose, but not the one written on its surface. It had been a test of Delassus’s authority and capacity. His powerless response—a pledge to write a letter—confirmed the delegates’ growing conviction. That system could not repair itself. Reform within the Spanish framework was a fantasy. The discussion at the next meeting, scheduled for late July would turn from appeals to logistics. The language of grievance was about to become the language of power.

John Rhea’s election as chairperson on that July day was not an accident of popularity. It was a strategic choice by men who understood that legitimacy was a currency they desperately needed to mint. Rhea, a lawyer who had served two terms in the U.S. House of Representatives before migrating to the Feliciana district brought a specific gravity to the proceedings. His was not the charisma of a frontier brawler or the fervor of an ideologue. His authority derived from parchment and precedent. In later correspondence, delegates would refer to his “calming influence” and his “methodical mind.” He was, in essence, their parliamentary shield. Under his gavel, their gathering could be framed not as a seditious cabal but as a deliberative body, a convention in the American revolutionary tradition.

This insistence on procedural legitimacy was Rhea’s primary contribution, and it marked the critical divergence from the region’s earlier violent outbursts. The ghost at St. John’s Plains was Reuben Kemper. Six years earlier, Kemper and his brothers had launched a crude, bloody filibuster, attempting to seize Baton Rouge with a handful of men motivated by personal grievance and land speculation. It was a raid, not a revolution. Easily crushed and just as easily dismissed by authorities as the work of bandits. Rhea and the planters at the convention were determined not to repeat Kemper’s mistake. Their rebellion, if it came to that would wear the garments of law. They would create a paper trail of resolutions, declarations, and constitutions that would justify their actions to themselves, to their neighbors, and, crucially, to the government in Washington.

Rhea’s first moves as chair were administrative. He appointed committees. He insisted on written resolutions. He guided the debate toward the production of documents—first the petition to Delassus, and later, more forceful instruments. A surviving fragment of a letter from delegate John H. Johnson held in the Louisiana State Archives, describes a moment of tension when a hotheaded member proposed simply marching on the fort. Rhea, Johnson wrote, listened patiently and then redirected the discussion to the question of “by what right” they could take such an action. One answer, he argued, must be founded in a collective mandate from the inhabitants, properly expressed through their elected delegates. He builded a case, not just an army.

This legalistic scaffolding served a dual purpose. Internally, it restrained impulsive action and forged consensus among a group of strong-willed, independent men whose interests were not perfectly aligned. A small planter from St. Helena had different priorities than a large slaveholder from Feliciana. The shared ritual of debate and vote, managed by Rhea’s rules of order gave each man a stake in the outcome. Externally, it was a performance of sovereignty for multiple audiences. For the Spanish, it was a demonstration of serious, organized intent that made their position untenable. For the American settlers still on the fence, it offered the reassuring spectacle of familiar political forms. And for President James Madison, it presented the rebellion not as a chaotic frontier disturbance but as a principled, constitutional movement seeking to join the American union.

Rhea’s most significant maneuver was his steady push to expand the convention’s mandate. From addressing specific grievances, he guided it toward asserting a general right to govern. The second meeting in late July, and the third in early September saw a gradual escalation in language. Resolutions began to refer to the “inhabitants” as a political body capable of making law. They discussed forming a “permanent council.” The shift was subtle but deep. They were no longer merely asking Delassus for reforms; they drafted the architecture of a government to replace him. Rhea’s hand is visible in the structure of the documents that emerged—their numbered articles, their invocation of “rights and liberties,” their careful separation of powers.

The contrast with Kemper’s revolt was now absolute. Where Kemper had relied on surprise and violence, Rhea’s convention relied on procedure and proclamation. Where Kemper’s failure was swift and total, the convention’s progress was slow, deliberate, and politically irreversible. Each resolution passed, each committee formed was a brick in a road that led away from Spanish rule. By the time armed conflict became inevitable, the rebellion had already won its most important victory: it had convinced a critical mass of the populace that its actions were rightful, necessary, and lawful. This was John Rhea’s quiet, paperwork revolution. He understood that on the frontier, the man who controls the minutes controls the narrative, and the narrative would determine whether history recorded them as patriots or pirates.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus received the convention’s petition at his residence within Fort San Carlos, a crumbling earthwork fortification overlooking the Mississippi. His situation documented in his dispatches to Havana and to his superior, Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola was militarily absurd. A March 1810 muster roll, preserved in Spanish archives, listed his effective force at thirty-four soldiers. Other accounts put the number below fifty. Were a mix of regular infantry and local milicianos, poorly paid, equipped with aging muskets, and scattered across garrison duties. The fort itself, a relic of British occupation was in disrepair. Its palisades were rotten; its few cannon were undermanned. To govern a district of nearly ten thousand inhabitants, most of them restive Americans, Delassus commanded fewer men than a single large plantation owner could field from his own enslaved workforce and white overseers.

Delassus was not a fool. A career military officer who had served in Louisiana for decades, he understood the arithmetic of power. His response to the St. John’s Plains petition, therefore was a performance of accommodating weakness. He wrote back to John Rhea, expressing his “sincere desire” to address their grievances and his intention to forward their requests to higher authority. This was the expected, hollow protocol. But his next move was more calculated. He invited the convention’s leading delegates to a series of dinners at the fort. These “peace dinners,” described in later accounts by attendees like William Barrow and in Delassus’s own reports were exercises in theatrical civility.

The governor, playing the gracious host offered food and wine. He listened with apparent sympathy to the planters’ complaints about land titles. He lamented the slow wheels of Spanish bureaucracy as if he were a fellow victim. According to Barrow’s recollection, Delassus even voiced his own frustrations with the lack of support from Pensacola and Havana. The message he conveyed was one of shared powerlessness. He was, he implied, their ally in seeking reasonable reform, hamstrung by a distant and indifferent crown. This feigned alignment was a classic delaying tactic, a mask for his strategic dilemma. By treating the convention as a legitimate advisory body, he hoped to keep its actions within the bounds of petition and dialogue. He sought to buy the one thing he needed most: time for reinforcements to arrive.

Privately, in ciphered dispatches, Delassus told a different story. To Folch in Pensacola, he described the convention as a “grave sedition” and stated flatly that he lacked the force to suppress it by arms. He requested troops—at least two hundred regulars—urgently. “Without a prompt and powerful remedy,” he warned in a July dispatch, “the consequences will be the loss of this Post and District.” His reports to Havana struck a similar tone of alarm, couched in the formal language of imperial administration. The public dinners and the private dispatches revealed a man straddling two realities, trying to placate the rebellion before him while pleading for the means to crush it from behind.

The response from Pensacola was a study in imperial paralysis. Vicente Folch received Delassus’s pleas and understood the threat. But his own position was scarcely stronger. Pensacola’s garrison was small, and the vast territory he nominally governed was itself simmering with discontent. Folch’s correspondence held in the Spanish colonial records, shows a man trapped by procedure and pessimism. He forwarded Delassus’s requests to Cuba, asking for men and money. He drafted plans for a relief expedition. But he delayed. He convened councils of war that debated risks and logistical shortfalls. An August letter from Folch to the Captain General in Havana reveals his creeping resignation. He wrote that sending a small force would be useless, and a large one was impossible to assemble. He began to conclude that the rebellion in Baton Rouge might be a fait accompli.

Back at Fort San Carlos, Delassus continued his charade. As the convention grew bolder in its late July and early September meetings, passing resolutions that effectively assumed legislative powers, Delassus feigned acceptance. He even agreed, in principle, to a convention proposal to form a joint committee to “assist” in governance. This was a breathtaking concession, an acknowledgment that his authority now depended on the rebels’ consent. He sanctioned his own usurpation. For the delegates, this was a critical signal. Delassus’s compliance proved their growing power. For Delassus, it was a desperate gambit to maintain a semblance of control and avoid provoking an attack he could not withstand.

The peace dinners grew more strained. The conversations, once focused on land laws, now circled the unspoken question of sovereignty. Delassus, according to one delegate’s later testimony reported in the Louisiana Gazette began to press subtly for assurances of the settlers’ continued loyalty to Spain. The delegates, in turn spoke vaguely of their desires for “security and prosperity.” Both sides knew they lyed. The shared meals became a ritual without substance, a hollow pantomime of goodwill where the only real exchange was the assessment of resolve. Delassus lookked for signs of division or hesitation among the rebels. The delegates gaugged the governor’s remaining will to fight.

By early September, the mask had all but fallen away. Delassus stopped receiving convention documents. His invitations to the fort ceased. The flow of polite correspondence dried up. The change was not lost on the men at St. John’s Plains. The governor’s strategy of feigned cooperation had run its course. It had bought him roughly six weeks. No reinforcements had come from Pensacola; no word of relief had arrived from Havana. The hush from his superiors was its own kind of answer. Delassus was now a garrison commander waiting for a siege, his earlier diplomacy revealed as what it always was: a stall tactic born of heavy weakness. The convention, meanwhile had used the time he gifted them to perfect their organization, spread their influence, and quietly drill their militia. The governor’s peace had been the rebellion’s perfect preparation.

The turning point arrived not with a dramatic vote, but with a grim, shared realization. Articulated in a letter from delegate John H. Johnson to a business partner in Natchez, dated early August 1810. “We have shouted into a well,” Johnson wrote, “and the only echo is the sound of our own voices.” The polite petition to Delassus, and his powerless response had completed a circuit of futility. The Spanish crown, consumed by the Peninsular War against Napoleon was incapable of action. Governor Folch in Pensacola was paralyzed. Delassus, they now understood was not a partner in reform but a hostage in a crumbling fort. The legalistic path championed by John Rhea had reached its logical end: it had proven the system was dead. The convention’s purpose now had to evolve from diagnosing the illness to performing the autopsy.

The shift in language across the convention’s resolutions, preserved in fragmentary copies, maps this evolution. The July documents speak of “grievances” and “redress.” Those drafted in late August and early September begin to invoke “inherent rights,” “self-preservation,” and the “duty” of the inhabitants to provide for their own security. This was the lexicon of sovereignty, borrowed directly from the American Declaration of Independence and the constitutions of the states from which most delegates had emigrated. A committee was formed, not to draft another appeal to Havana, but to “consider and report on the means of defense for the district.” The word “defense” was a flexible one. It could mean protection from external threats, but in the context of a district with only one tiny, Spanish garrison, it implicitly meant defense from that garrison.

The catalyst for this sharper turn was the rising influence of men whose authority derived from the muster field, not the committee room. Foremost among them was Philemon Thomas, a sixty-three-year-old veteran of the American Revolution and a substantial planter from the Baton Rouge district. Thomas, as described in later accounts by his contemporaries, possessed a temperamental opposite to Rhea’s. Was direct, physically vigorous, and impatient with endless debate. His military record gave him instant credibility on questions of action. While Rhea built the case for legitimacy, Thomas began to assess the means of enforcement. He moved through the settlements, speaking with other veterans and younger men chafing for a fight. His conversations, recalled in a deposition given years later by a participant were practical. He asked about gunpowder supplies, the number of serviceable muskets in a given parish, and who could be relied upon to ride at a moment’s notice.

Thomas’s presence at the convention introduced a new, concrete variable into the deliberations. As the talk shifted to “means of defense,” he could translate abstract resolutions into a tactical appraisal. He reportedly stood during one September meeting and, according to notes kept by delegate William Barrow gave a blunt assessment. The fort, he said was weak. Its garrison was demoralized and small. Its powder magazine was vulnerable. Taking it, he argued was a matter of will, not of overwhelming force. This was a seismic statement. It replaced a nebulous fear of Spanish power with a specific, achievable military objective. The rebellion now had a target and a man who believed he could hit it.

The convention’s response was characteristically procedural, yet profoundly radical. They did not vote to storm the fort that day. They empowered Thomas to “organize and inspect” the militia companies forming in each district. This resolution, passed under the guise of community defense, effectively sanctioned the creation of a rebel army under a unified command. It gave Thomas the authority to conduct what would, in modern terms, be called reconnaissance. Riders were dispatched not just to rally men, but to gather intelligence on the fort’s routines, the morale of its soldiers, and the layout of its gates.

Simultaneously, the convention took a step that severed its last psychological tie to Spanish authority. They began drafting a constitution. This was no longer a list of requested reforms; it was the blueprint for a new polity. The act of writing a constitution is the ultimate assertion of self-government. It declares that the people, not a distant monarch are the source of law. The committee assigned to this task led by Rhea worked in parallel with Thomas’s military preparations. One group was writing the republic into existence on paper; the other prepared to midwife it with force.

The two threads—constitutional and military—were now braided together. The lack of Spanish response was no longer a frustration; it was a precondition. It created the vacuum the convention moved to fill. The delegates understood, as Johnson’s letter put it, that “to wait is to wither.” Every day without a Spanish reinforcement made their position stronger, yet every day also risked the arrival of that reinforcement, which would transform a calculated rebellion into a desperate battle. The momentum became self-sustaining. Preparing the militia made the drafting of a constitution a practical necessity. Drafting the constitution made the seizure of the fort a political imperative, the first act of a new state.

By the third week of September, the convention at St. John’s Plains had quietly crossed its Rubicon. Its members were no longer petitioners hoping to fix a broken government. Were the provisional government, planning the violent dissolution of the old one. The language of law had paved the way for the logic of revolution. John Rhea’s parchment shield was now strapped to the arm of Philemon Thomas’s clenched fist.

Philemon Thomas did not call for a general muster. A public gathering would have alerted the fort’s sentries and shattered the crucial element of surprise. As documented in later sworn testimonies from participants, he employed a method as old as frontier insurrection: the silent call. He dispatched trusted riders on predetermined circuits. Each man carried no written orders, only verbal instructions relayed from Thomas himself. The message was simple, specific, and urgent. Were to go to known figures in each settlement—the veteran of the Revolution, the prosperous planter with a score to settle over a land title, the tough-minded overseer respected by his peers. These men, in turn would quietly alert their own networks. The call was not for a mob, but for a company. Participants were to bring their own firearm, shot, and powder. Were to be at a designated rendezvous point, a plantation landing on Bayou Sara or a crossroads in the pine woods north of Baton Rouge, before dawn on the appointed day.

The force Thomas assembled was a portrait of the district itself. Militia rolls from the period, though incomplete, list planters like Thomas himself alongside smaller farmers, merchants, and skilled craftsmen. Many were veterans of the American Revolution or the various Indian conflicts that had shaped the frontier. Their commonality was not ideology in the abstract, but a concrete investment in the land. Were men who had cleared acres built homes, and purchased enslaved laborers to work their fields. The crumbling Spanish fort represented not just political neglect, but a direct threat to that material investment. A stray spark of wider conflict, an order from a desperate governor conscripting their property, or a legal decree invalidating their hard-won titles—any of these possibilities made the garrison’s continued existence an untenable risk. Their rebellion was, in essence, a property owners’ revolt.

Thomas’s logistical challenges were formidable. Secrecy was paramount, but so was coordination. The men had to be gathered from districts scattered across fifty miles of riverfront and backcountry. They needed to be fed and, critically, prevented from dispersing or losing their nerve during the final hours of waiting. Thomas relied on the region’s geography of kinship and commerce. Rendezvous points were often the plantations of known sympathizers, where barns could shelter men and outbuildings could store provisions. The movement of small groups through the woods and along secondary trails was masked by the ordinary traffic of a working plantation district. A dozen men arriving at a remote landing could be mistaken for a timber crew or a hunting party.

The most valuable intelligence came not from spies within the fort, but from the mundane observations of the settlers who lived around it. In depositions given years later, participants recounted how Thomas’s men exploited their local knowledge. They knew the fort’s sentry schedule because they had passed it at all hours for years. They knew the condition of its gate because they had delivered firewood or produce through it. They knew the morale of the garrison from conversations in Baton Rouge’s taverns and from the weary expressions of the soldiers sent to patrol the riverbank. This intimate familiarity bred a specific, actionable contempt. The fort was not an impregnable bastion; it was a familiar, poorly maintained feature of their daily landscape, and its weaknesses were as well-known as the rotten plank in a neighbor’s fence.

Thomas’s final preparation was a deliberate act of psychological warfare, though he would not have called it that. In the days immediately before the assault, he allowed—or perhaps instructed—a subtle change in the demeanor of his recruits. As recalled by William Barrow, the settlers in Baton Rouge stopped averting their eyes from the Spanish soldiers. Their greetings grew shorter, their posture less deferential. A silent, collective withdrawal of consent. The governor’s authority had always been a fiction propped up by the inhabitants’ willingness to pretend it was real. Now, that pretense was dropped. That soldiers isolated and aware of the gathering storm could feel the change in the air. A demoralization as effective as any siege. When Philemon Thomas finally gave the order to move, he was not leading a reckless charge against a fortified enemy. guidded a coordinated action by the district itself, a community closing its hand around a foreign artifact it had decided to expel. The riders had done their work. The militia, a scattered collection of individuals with private stakes had been forged into a single instrument with a single target. All that remained was to strike.

The plan, as pieced together from later participant accounts and Thomas’s own reports was a model of frontier pragmatism. It leveraged the rebels’ intimate knowledge of the fort’s layout and routines, intelligence passively gathered over months of social and commercial interaction, and now sharpened by recent, deliberate observation. The assault was set for the pre-dawn hours of Sunday, September 23. The timing served two purposes. First, it exploited the cover of darkness and the frequent river fog that clung to the bluffs in the early morning, obscuring movement. Second, Sunday was a day of relative inactivity; sentries might be less alert, and the garrison’s small size meant guard shifts were long and wearying.

The target was Fort San Carlos itself, but the objective was not a slaughter. A swift, decisive seizure of the symbol of Spanish power—the governor, the flag, the arsenal. Thomas divided his force, which numbered between fifty and eighty armed men according to most estimates, into two groups. The first and smaller group, composed of volunteers who knew the fort’s interior was tasked with infiltration. Under cover of the darkness, they were to approach the palisade at its most degraded section, slip inside, and silently neutralize the sentries guarding the main gate. Their success would allow the second, larger group to storm the fort directly without having to breach a defended barrier.

This scheme relied entirely on the accuracy of the settlers’ intelligence. They knew which sections of the wooden wall were rotten. They knew the sentries changed at approximately four in the morning. They knew the gate’s locking mechanism was a simple bar, not a complex bolt. This was not the product of espionage, but of community memory. Every man involved had likely been inside the fort at some point on civilian business. The fort’s vulnerability was, therefore, a commonplace fact among them.

Governor Delassus was not entirely blind to the threat. The historical record shows he received at least two explicit warnings. The first came from a loyal Spanish subject, who reported seeing armed men gathering in the woods. The second, more detailed warning arrived just days before the attack, informing the governor that an assault was imminent. Delassus’s response documented in his later report to Spanish authorities telled. He did not call out the district’s militia—a force he no longer trusted. He did not attempt a preemptive arrest of Thomas or other known leaders, an action for which he lacked the men. He doubled the guard. He placed additional sentries on the walls and ordered his few dozen soldiers to sleep in their uniforms with their weapons at hand.

This doubling of the guard was the act of a commander who understood his weakness but was powerless to remedy it. A defensive gesture, not an offensive one. It acknowledged the threat but could not counter it. For the rebels monitoring the fort, the increased activity may have even served as a confirmation that their secrecy was compromised, pushing them to act before any reinforcements could materialize. Delassus’s measures treated the symptom—the possibility of a surprise attack—while the disease was the utter collapse of his political authority. More sentries on a rotten wall did not change the arithmetic.

In the final hours, Thomas’s dispersed companies converged. They met at predetermined points in the woods north and east of Baton Rouge, their movements masked by the normal Saturday night traffic of planters returning from town and enslaved people moving on errands. The men carried an assortment of weapons: Kentucky long rifles, trade muskets, pistols. Were not a uniformed army but a levy of property owners, their clothing practical for fighting—boots, heavy coats, wide-brimmed hats against the damp. Their cohesion came from shared purpose, not drill. They had been called not to serve a king or a general, but to secure their own futures.

The atmosphere in these final rendezvous, as later described by men like Isaac Johnson to local historians was tense but focused. There was little of the boisterous enthusiasm of a mob. The stakes were too concrete. Failure meant not just death or injury, but the certain confiscation of their estates and the potential destruction of everything they had built. Thomas moved among them, a stabilizing presence. His instructions were repeated quietly: follow orders, hold fire until commanded, and once inside, secure the powder magazine to prevent a catastrophic explosion. The goal was a bloodless capitulation, if possible. But every man understood that violence was not just likely, but inherent in the act.

As the men took their positions in the tree line overlooking the Mississippi, the fort below was a dark silhouette against the star-flecked river. A few lanterns glowed from within, marking the guard posts. The only sounds were the river’s sluggish flow and the distant cry of a night heron. The infiltrators, a shadow within shadows began their advance down the bluff toward the weak section of the palisade. The main force, muskets primed, waited for the signal—the opening of the fort’s gate from within. The plan was a simple sequence of cause and effect. Its success hinged on the accuracy of their local knowledge, the steadiness of their nerve, and the complete isolation of the Spanish soldiers inside, who stood watch on a wall that the country around them had already decided to tear down.
