The election of Fulwar Skipwith as president of the Republic of West Florida was recorded on November 7, 1810, in the minutes of the convention. The vote was unanimous. His acceptance speech, preserved in a contemporary newspaper account, struck a note of solemn duty rather than triumph. “I accept the office you have conferred upon me,” he stated, “with a deep sense of the responsibility attached to it, and with a fervent hope that my efforts may contribute to the liberty and safety of our country.” The language was formal, the sentiment borrowed from the script of republican virtue. For the assembled planters and merchants in the room, his selection was a tactical move. They needed a figure whose credentials would lend their fragile enterprise a veneer of diplomatic legitimacy, both to their own populace and to the watching powers in Washington, Pensacola, and Havana. Skipwith, a fifty-year-old Virginian with a ruined fortune and a distinguished diplomatic past, fit the role.

He was an unlikely frontiersman. Born into the Virginia gentry in 1765, educated at the College of William and Mary, he had served as the United States consul general in Paris from 1804 to 1809. His time there coincided with the chaotic aftermath of the French Revolution and the rise of Napoleon. He witnessed the collapse of one republic and the birth of an empire. According to his later memoir, he lost a significant personal fortune in French financial schemes during this period, a blow that sent him seeking opportunity elsewhere. By 1810, he had resettled in the Feliciana district of West Florida, drawn by the promise of land and a fresh start. He was a planter, but one whose worldview was shaped by chancelleries and treaties, not cotton prices and river currents.

The convention’s choice was therefore a calculated appeal to authority. Skipwith’s correspondence reveals a man of rigid principle and a formal, almost anachronistic, sense of honor. He believed in process. He believed in the sanctity of agreements between nations, even nascent ones. To the more pragmatic settlers like John Rhea, who viewed the republic as a temporary vehicle for American annexation, Skipwith’s principles introduced a complication. The new president took his oath of office seriously. He intended to govern.

In the days following his election, Skipwith moved from his plantation to the republic’s provisional seat of government in St. Francisville. There was no presidential residence. He likely took rooms in one of the settlement’s better houses. His first official acts, as recorded in the sparse archives of the republic, were administrative: appointing a secretary, authorizing the purchase of stationery, reviewing the condition of the public arms seized from Fort San Carlos. He corresponded with John Rhea and the newly seated senators, his letters neat and precise. He began to refer to the territory not as a district in waiting, but as “the Republic.” This was not delusion, but a diplomat’s understanding of form. To be treated as a sovereign entity, one must act like one.

This commitment to form soon collided with the raw reality of the republic’s position. The treasury was empty. The militia, under Philemon Thomas, was restless and unpaid. Spanish forces in Mobile and Pensacola were a latent threat. And the expectations of the populace were divided. Many settlers, particularly the Anglo-American majority, saw the lone star flag as a beacon to attract the United States. A smaller faction, including some of the French Creoles and adventurers, saw it as a standard to rally around for a permanent independence. Skipwith’s task was to navigate between these poles, to build a functioning state while most of its supposed citizens were looking over his shoulder, waiting for another flag to appear on the horizon.

His personal papers, examined by later historians, show a man grappling with this duality. In one memo, he drafted points for a potential treaty with the United States, outlining terms for a “voluntary union” that would protect the property and laws established under the republic. In another, he listed the requirements for sustaining an independent nation: a secure revenue, a standing military force, treaties of commerce. He was, in effect, preparing two separate playbooks, unsure which one the next courier from Washington would demand he open. The weight of this uncertainty is absent from the official minutes, which record only motions and votes. It is visible, instead, in the sheer volume of his writing during those weeks, the frantic script of a man trying to draft stability into existence with pen and paper before the gun decided the matter.

The first concrete test of his authority arrived within days. A report reached St. Francisville that a Spanish privateer was operating near the mouth of the Pearl River, harassing flatboats carrying supplies to Baton Rouge. The republic’s tiny “navy” consisted of a few armed barges. Skipwith, consulting with General Thomas, issued a proclamation ordering the detention of any hostile vessel found within the republic’s claimed waters. It was a bold assertion of sovereignty over a stretch of the Gulf. More importantly, it was an act of defense, a signal that the new government intended to protect its citizens and their commerce. The order was carried out. The privateer, according to a later account in the Louisiana Courier, was chased off. For the planters along the river, the episode was a small but tangible benefit of the new regime. For Skipwith, it was proof that a republic, however provisional, could govern.

Skipwith’s vision for an independent West Florida was not a sudden frontier fantasy. It was forged in the diplomatic fires of the 1790s and tempered by personal loss. As a young lawyer in Virginia, he had been a fervent supporter of the French Revolution, an enthusiasm shared by many American republicans. This affinity led to his appointment as U.S. Consul general in Paris a decade later, a post he held during the critical years of the Louisiana Purchase and the escalating tensions between America and Britain that would culminate in the War of 1812. His memoir, though written years afterward, details his disillusion. He arrived in Europe believing in the ideal of popular sovereignty, but he witnessed its corruption into Napoleonic autocracy. He saw how easily revolutionary principles could be subverted by a strongman and a hungry army. This experience left him with a profound, almost juridical, belief in constitutional order as the only bulwark against chaos.

The XYZ Affair, which unfolded before his tenure, had also left a deep imprint on the American diplomatic corps. The scandal, in which French officials demanded bribes from American envoys, was a lesson in the vulnerability of a young republic on the world stage. For Skipwith, the correct response was not retreat, but the meticulous construction of legitimate statehood. A nation needed recognized borders, a functioning government, and a clear legal code to command respect. To his mind, West Florida in 1810 was not a rebellious province waiting to be absorbed. It was a new nation in its fragile infancy, requiring the same careful protocols he had observed in Parisian chancelleries.

This philosophy placed him at odds with the majority of the men who had elected him. As historian Andrew McMichael notes in Atlantic Loyalties, the Anglo-American settlers of the Feliciana district were “annexationists first, and separatists only as a means to that end.” Their grievances were local: unfair tariffs, insecure land titles, the weakness of Spanish rule. Their solution was absorption by the United States, where their property would be secure and their trade unrestricted. The Lone Star flag, for them, was a signal flare to Washington, not a permanent standard.

Skipwith’s correspondence reveals he understood this perfectly. In a letter to an associate in Virginia, he wrote that the people’s “attachment to the American union is strong,” but he immediately followed this observation with a warning: “A government, to be respected, must respect itself.” He believed the settler’s desire for annexation was a point of leverage, not a predetermined outcome. If the United States delayed or offered unfavorable terms, the Republic could and should establish itself as a viable alternative, a legitimate small power on the Gulf Coast. He began drafting commercial regulations and a plan for a national bank, modeling them on early American systems. He proposed sending agents not only to Washington, but also to London and Paris, to seek recognition and trade agreements. These were not the acts of a caretaker. They were the blueprints of a man building a house he intended to live in.

His diplomatic background also gave him a clearer view of the geopolitical chessboard than his frontier colleagues possessed. He knew Spain was crippled by the Peninsular War, but he also knew its colonial officials in Havana and Pensacola would not surrender territory without a fight or a treaty. He understood the United States’ expansionist ambitions, but he also recognized the political constraints on President Madison, who faced Federalist opposition and the looming threat of war with Britain. In Skipwith’s analysis, this interval of great-power hesitation was the Republic’s window of opportunity. If it could demonstrate stability and control for a few months, it could enter negotiations from a position of strength, perhaps even as an ally rather than a supplicant.

This strategic patience was misinterpreted as naivete by men like Philemon Thomas, who favored aggressive action against Mobile. Where Thomas saw a military opportunity, Skipwith saw a diplomatic catastrophe. An attack on a stronger Spanish fort would invite retaliation and paint the Republic as a band of lawless filibusterers, undermining the legitimacy he was painstakingly trying to construct. The tension between the president and his general was, at its core, a conflict between two visions of revolution: one of the sword, and one of the seal.

In the quiet of his borrowed office in St. Francisville, surrounded by maps and drafts, Fulwar Skipwith was engaged in a race against time and inclination. He was attempting to implant the delicate procedures of international law into the raw soil of a frontier rebellion. Every proclamation he signed, every appointment he made, was an attempt to convince the world—and perhaps his own citizens—that the Republic of West Florida was more than a land grab. It was a proposition.

While President Skipwith drafted commercial regulations in St. Francisville, a courier was already riding north from New Orleans with dispatches for Washington. The news of the Baton Rouge revolt had reached President James Madison by early October. His response was swift, secretive, and unilateral. On October 27, 1810, from the Executive Mansion, he issued a proclamation declaring West Florida annexed to the United States and ordering the governor of the Orleans Territory, William C.C. Claiborne, to take possession.

The proclamation, published in the National Intelligencer on November 2, was a masterclass in political rhetoric and legal ambiguity. Madison asserted that the territory between the Mississippi and Perdido Rivers had always been part of the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, a claim based on a disputed interpretation of the treaties between France and Spain. According to this view, Spain had only been a temporary caretaker, and its authority was now, by the rebellion of its inhabitants, fully dissolved. “The territory,” the proclamation stated, was “of right vested in the United States.” Therefore, the president was merely ordering the occupation of American soil that had been unlawfully withheld.

This was a fiction, and Madison knew it. As the historian Robert V. Remini notes in his study of early expansion, Jefferson’s earlier attempts to claim West Florida under the Purchase had been explicitly rejected by both Spain and France. The borders of the Purchase were undefined in 1803, and subsequent diplomacy had failed to resolve the West Florida question. Madison’s own secretary of state, Robert Smith, had admitted in internal memos that the legal claim was “feeble.” But the rebellion provided a pretext. The proclamation framed the annexation not as an opportunistic land grab, but as a restoration of order and the protection of American citizens from the “criminal attempts of individuals” – a clear reference to the Kemper raids and the shadow of filibusterism that Madison was eager to distinguish from his own actions.

The most striking element of the document was its tone of denunciation toward the very revolution that had created the opening. Madison condemned “the subversion of the Spanish authorities” by persons whose “conduct is rendered more criminal by the pretext under which it has been attempted.” He was walking a fine line: legitimizing the result of the rebellion while illegitimizing its actors. This served two purposes. It allowed him to assert American sovereignty without officially recognizing the Republic of West Florida as a legitimate belligerent, which would have required more complex diplomacy. It also placated Federalist critics in the Northeast who viewed southern expansion as a slave-power conspiracy and frontier unrest as a threat to national stability.

Crucially, Madison acted without seeking congressional approval. He invoked his authority as commander-in-chief to protect what he deemed United States territory from foreign occupation and anarchy. This expansion of executive power did not go unnoticed. Representative John Randolph of Virginia, a staunch opponent of the administration, would later thunder on the House floor that the president had “usurped the war-making power” and “made war on Spain without the consent of Congress.” But by the time those objections were formally raised, Claiborne’s troops were already on the move.

Madison’s orders to Claiborne, contained in a separate confidential letter dated the same day as the proclamation, were explicit. He was to march into West Florida, raise the American flag, and administer the territory as part of the Orleans Territory. The letter, preserved in the National Archives, advised caution and conciliation where possible, but insisted on the inevitability of the outcome. “The United States have a right to the possession,” Madison wrote. “It is not to be doubted that the great mass of the inhabitants will cheerfully concur in the change.” This was more hope than certainty. The proclamation made no mention of Fulwar Skipwith, John Rhea, or the constitution they had just written. In Madison’s calculus, the Republic of West Florida was not a government to negotiate with; it was a temporary administrative problem to be solved by the arrival of American troops and the lowering of a lone star flag that had flown, by then, for just over a month.

Governor William C. C. Claiborne received Madison’s orders in New Orleans on November 5. He moved with deliberate speed. By the second week of November, a column of United States troops, a mix of regular army and Orleans Territory volunteers, marched northeast along the river road toward Baton Rouge. A small flotilla of armed riverboats accompanied them on the Mississippi. As documented in Claiborne’s official report to the Secretary of State, his force numbered approximately 300 men. Their mission was not invasion, in the governor’s framing, but peaceful occupation of American soil.

The physical arrival of American power was a calculated display. The troops wore the blue and grey of the U.S. Army. Their boats flew the Stars and Stripes. As they passed the plantations of the German Coast and entered the disputed territory, the sight was unmistakable: the federal government was presenting its claim in the only language that mattered on that frontier—organized force. Planters and their enslaved laborers watched from the levee. According to a letter from a planter published later in the Louisiana Gazette, the reaction was one of relief, not alarm. “The appearance of our national flag upon the river has quieted every apprehension,” the writer stated. “Order is now assured.”

Claiborne’s first objective was Baton Rouge, the site of the rebellion and the symbolic heart of the republic. He arrived on December 6, 1810. The American column approached Fort San Carlos, where the lone star flag still flew. The fort, damaged from Thomas’s assault two months prior, was now garrisoned by West Florida militia. Claiborne did not order an attack. He established camp nearby and sent a formal written communication to “the authorities in possession of the post.” The document, a copy of which exists in the National Archives, did not address President Skipwith or the Republic. It was directed to “the inhabitants of West Florida.” It announced that, by order of the President of the United States, he was there to take possession of the territory for the union. He requested a meeting.

Inside the fort, the militia commanders faced a decision for which their new constitution had no clear procedure. They dispatched a rider to St. Francisville, twenty-five miles north, to inform President Skipwith. The republic’s entire military strength was concentrated at Baton Rouge, but it was outnumbered and outgunned by Claiborne’s professional force. More critically, the will to fight was absent. The men at the fort were the same settlers who had stormed it for independence; now, for most of them, the ultimate goal—American annexation—was manifesting before their eyes. To fire on the U.S. flag would be to betray their original purpose.

Skipwith received the news in his makeshift office. His personal papers show no record of a furious response. Instead, they contain a hastily written list of points for negotiation. He understood the symbolism of the moment. Claiborne had bypassed him entirely, refusing to acknowledge his presidency. The meeting request was not between two executives, but between a territorial governor and local inhabitants. To attend under those terms was to surrender the republic’s sovereignty at the outset. To refuse was to invite a bloodless siege his own people would not support.

He chose a middle path. He did not go to Baton Rouge himself. He authorized a delegation of convention members, including John Rhea, to treat with Claiborne. The instructions he gave them, referenced in later congressional testimony, were firm: they were to protest the method of annexation as a violation of the republic’s sovereignty, but they were to accept the inevitable union in principle. It was a diplomat’s last maneuver—salvaging honor from capitulation.

At Baton Rouge, the meeting was brief. Claiborne, according to the account he later provided to Madison, was cordial but immovable. He presented Madison’s proclamation. He explained that resistance was futile and that all rights and property would be protected under American law. John Rhea, speaking for the delegation, lodged a formal protest against the high-handedness of the occupation. He then, as Claiborne’s report notes dryly, “acquiesced in the necessity of the situation.”

The transfer was scheduled for December 10. On that morning, the West Florida militia marched out of Fort San Carlos. The lone star flag was lowered. A United States flag was raised in its place. Claiborne’s troops marched in. There was no ceremony, no speeches to the populace. The event was recorded in Claiborne’s ledger-like journal: “At 10 o’clock, took possession of the Fort & Territory.” The Republic of West Florida, in any practical sense, ceased to exist on the bluff overlooking the Mississippi. Its government, ninety miles away in St. Francisville, still functioned on paper. But its sole fortified position, and the loyalty of the armed men who had created it, now answered to a different flag.

The government in St. Francisville did not dissolve with the flag-lowering at Baton Rouge. For Fulwar Skipwith, the formalities of surrender were as important as the act itself. The republic still had a constitution, a senate, and a president. He intended to use these instruments to negotiate the terms of a union he now saw as unavoidable, but on a foundation of law, not mere force. In the days following Claiborne’s occupation, Skipwith composed a series of official letters. His correspondence, preserved in the National Archives, reveals a man clinging to protocol as the ground vanished beneath him.

His first dispatch, dated December 11, 1810, was addressed to Governor Claiborne. It was not a plea, but a protest. Skipwith wrote as the chief magistrate of a sovereign state to the representative of a foreign power. He began by acknowledging the “gratification” many citizens felt at the prospect of joining the United States. Then he pivoted. “But the mode,” he wrote, “in which you have thought proper to execute the orders of your Government, is an outrage against the Lone Star Flag and the constitution of West Florida.” The charge was specific: Claiborne had entered the republic’s territory with armed troops without any prior communication with its legitimate government. He had treated the republic’s militia as a disorderly mob and its officials as private citizens. This method, Skipwith argued, was an insult to the “dignity of a free people” who had, by right of revolution, established a constitutional government. He demanded that Claiborne recognize his authority and negotiate a formal treaty of annexation that would protect the republic’s laws and the property rights established under its brief tenure.

Claiborne’s reply, written from Baton Rouge, was a study in dismissive pragmatism. He ignored the title of “President.” He addressed his letter to “Fulwar Skipwith, Esq.” He stated flatly that the territory had always belonged to the United States and that President Madison’s proclamation was the only instrument required for its possession. The “so-called republic,” in his view, had no legal standing to negotiate. Its constitution was irrelevant. Its flag was a banner of rebellion, not sovereignty. Claiborne offered assurances of personal safety and property rights to all inhabitants, but he refused any official dealings with Skipwith’s government. The message was clear: you may protest, but you will not be heard.

Skipwith did not relent. He convened the republic’s senate in St. Francisville. The minutes of that final session, though brief, show a body still attempting to function. They passed a resolution authorizing Skipwith to continue his diplomatic efforts. They discussed the safekeeping of the public archives. They acknowledged the “overwhelming physical power” of the United States, but they refused to vote for their own dissolution. This was not defiance for its own sake. It was, as Skipwith saw it, a matter of precedent and principle. If a group of citizens could form a government, write a constitution, and elect leaders, then that government deserved a formal end, not a silent erasure. To simply walk away would validate Claiborne’s assertion that it had never been real.

Skipwith’s final state paper, likely drafted in mid-December, was his most poignant. It was an address “To the Citizens of West Florida.” In it, he recounted the events since September: the righteous rebellion against Spanish misrule, the formation of a constitutional republic, and now its “forcible incorporation” by the United States. He stressed that he had always believed annexation to be the ultimate wish of the people. “But,” he wrote, “I have also believed that the manner of that union should be honorable, and should secure the guarantees for which we originally contended.” He listed those guarantees: confirmation of land titles, respect for existing laws, and representation in the territorial government. He concluded by stating that while his official functions were now at an end due to circumstances beyond his control, he urged citizens to demand these conditions from their new American rulers. The document was never widely circulated. By the time it was finished, American officials were already administering the district, and few had the appetite for a political lecture from a president without a country.

The contrast between Skipwith’s and Claiborne’s methods defined the annexation’s character. Claiborne, as documented in his reports to Washington, operated on a theory of efficiency and fait accompli. He believed any recognition of the republic would legitimize future frontier rebellions. His goal was to smother the Lone Star republic in a bureaucratic embrace, to treat it as an administrative error to be corrected, not a government to be superseded. Skipwith, drawing on his diplomatic training, operated on a theory of legitimacy. Even in failure, he believed, a state must act like a state to preserve the dignity of its people and the validity of their acts. His protests were not a denial of reality, but an attempt to shape the historical record and the legal foundations of what came next.

In the end, Claiborne’s version prevailed. On December 27, he formally declared West Florida annexed to the Orleans Territory. He appointed American justices of the peace and began registering land claims under U.S. law. The republic’s officials, including John Rhea, seamlessly transitioned into roles under the new regime. Rhea would soon be elected to the Louisiana state legislature. Philemon Thomas would become a brigadier general in the U.S. Army. The institutions they created in September were simply overwritten.

Only Fulwar Skipwith remained out of place. He returned to his plantation in Feliciana, a former president of a nation that had lasted seventy-four days. He had no official role in the new order. His meticulous protests were filed away in government archives, where they would remain for over a century, footnotes to a foregone conclusion. But in those documents, he achieved a small, personal victory. He forced the annexation to be, on paper, a transaction between two entities, however unequal. He ensured that the Republic of West Florida did not just vanish; it was formally, if unceremoniously, replaced. For a man who believed in the sanctity of forms, that distinction mattered.

The morning of December 10, 1810, was cool and overcast at Baton Rouge. A detail of West Florida militiamen, under the command of officers who had stormed the same fort seventy-eight days earlier, formed a loose line inside the log walls of Fort San Carlos. Across the parade ground, a larger contingent of United States troops, regulars and Orleans Territory volunteers in clean uniforms, stood at ease. Governor William C. C. Claiborne, in civilian dress but with a military escort, waited near the flagpole. The atmosphere, as recalled later by a participant in a letter to the Louisiana Courier, was not one of tension, but of procedural formality. The fight had gone out of the men holding the fort. Their rebellion had been aimed at Spain, not America. The arrival of U.S. troops represented not an invasion, but the successful conclusion of their primary objective: attachment to the union.

At the appointed hour, recorded by Claiborne in his journal as ten o’clock, a militia sergeant stepped forward. There were no speeches. No one represented the Republic’s government. John Rhea and the other convention delegates had already acquiesced to Claiborne’s terms and departed. The sergeant grasped the halyard. The Lone Star flag, a single white star on a field of blue that had been hand-stitched in September, began its descent. The cloth, faded and torn by autumn winds, came down slowly. A United States soldier stepped forward to receive it. The militia detail presented arms in a final, incongruous salute to their own standard. Then they grounded their weapons.

Claiborne gave a quiet order. A new flag, the Stars and Stripes, was attached. It climbed the pole, its colors bright against the grey sky. When it reached the top and caught the river breeze, a United States artillery piece fired a single blank charge—the official salute to the new sovereign. The report echoed off the bluff and over the Mississippi. On the river, the American gunboats answered with shots of their own. The sound was a period at the end of a sentence.

The militiamen then marched out of the fort’s main gate, their personal arms slung over their shoulders. They did not surrender their weapons. Claiborne’s assurance of protection extended to their right to bear them as American citizens. As they filed past the American lines, many of the U.S. troops, according to the same participant’s letter, nodded in acknowledgment. These were not enemies; they were future neighbors, and in many cases, distant kin. The West Florida officers handed Claiborne a manifest of fort property—a few rusting cannons, some barrels of poor-quality powder, and the logbooks kept since September. The transfer was complete.

Claiborne’s journal entry for the day is a model of bureaucratic brevity. “At 10 o’clock, took possession of the Fort & Territory.” He made no mention of a republic, a president, or a constitution. His subsequent report to President Madison, held in the National Archives, elaborated only slightly. He noted the “orderly and peaceful” nature of the transfer and the “apparent satisfaction” of the inhabitants. The flag-lowering was not framed as the defeat of a rival government, but as the correction of a local disturbance. The Lone Star republic was not conquered; it was administratively dissolved.

In St. Francisville, the government quietly ceased to meet. The public buildings reverted to their original purposes. The sense of epochal change that had crackled through the September convention dissipated into the routine of plantation life. The republic died not with a bang, but with a bureaucratic sigh. Yet, on the edges of the territory and in the minds of a few men, the idea of West Florida independence did not vanish entirely. Even as Claiborne consolidated his control, new trouble was stirring to the east, where the lone star flag had already been seen flying over a different, more defiant frontier. The absorption of Baton Rouge was not the end of the story. It was the end of the first act.
