The explosion at St. Bartholomew’s registered as a minor blip on the third monitor, a spike in the local police band chatter. Sister Mercy muted the feed. She hated amateur hour. The secure server room at Kraken Broadcasting smelled of chilled metal and the ghost of ten thousand microwave dinners. The hum of the machines was a constant, low-grade headache.

Her fingers flew across the console, chasing a signal that wasn’t supposed to exist. The main broadcast—her broadcast—was currently airing a pre-recorded segment about a woman in Daytona Beach whose cataracts cleared after sending in a lock of hair and sixty dollars. Boring. Profitable, but boring. The real action was in the UHF bleed, the static between channels where Eden’s ghosts liked to whisper.

A tech’s voice crackled in her earpiece. “We’re getting calls about the St. Bartholomew’s feed. Some kind of… lizard situation.”

“Edit it. Say it was a demonic manifestation. Quote Revelation. Use the scary font.” Mercy didn’t look up from her screens. The lizard was Rex. The handler was the carpenter. This was becoming a pattern, and patterns were vulnerabilities. She toggled a filter, stripping away the layer of evangelical muzak to get at the raw signal beneath.

“The board is asking about liability. The alligator—”

“Is an Eden audit asset. Tell the board to invoice God.” The raw signal was a mess of digital screech. Underneath it, though, a rhythm. Not a voice. A pulse.

Her coping mechanism—tapping the silver cross hanging from her earlobe—kicked in. Tap-tap-tap against her jaw. The cross was cold. It was also a high-gain antenna. A gift from her old mentor, back when exorcism was about saving souls and not siphoning them.

The main studio feed flickered on a secondary monitor. Her own face, five years younger, beamed with saccharine sincerity. She watched her past self lay hands on a man in a wheelchair. The man stood, wept, did a little jig. The studio audience erupted. Mercy remembered the taste of the obedience serum in his coffee that morning. A necessary sacrament.

The UHF pulse strengthened. It wasn’t music. It was a countdown.

“Pull the Daytona segment. Go to the emergency prayer loop,” she said into her mic. She couldn’t have viewers distracted by miracles while she worked.

“But the donations—”

“Do it.”

The screens shifted. The weeping, dancing man vanished, replaced by a slow pan across a generic stained-glass window. Soft harp music oozed from the speakers. Donation tallies, mercifully, began to plateau.

The pulse resolved into a data stream. Ancient Eden protocol. Her stomach did a slow, unpleasant roll—not fear, not excitement, the nameless thing between the two that happened when you found a door you weren’t meant to open. She’d felt it the first time she’d seen a real demon, back when she believed in them. The feeling was the same. Only the furniture was different.

She isolated the frequency. It was piggybacking on her own broadcast signal, a parasitic twin. Clever. And arrogant. Someone at Eden World was using her tower, her bandwidth, to send a message she wasn’t meant to hear.

Decryption was her specialty. The church had trained her to break spiritual codes, to find the demon’s true name. The principle was identical. Demons, like data, hated the light.

Lines of code cascaded down the center screen. Glyphs she hadn’t seen since her apprenticeship flickered amidst the hexadecimal. They were instructions. Fabrication protocols. Organic scaffolding.

A name appeared, then dissolved into static: AM-1.

Mercy stopped tapping her earring. The room felt colder. The hum of the servers climbed half an octave.

“We’ve got a visual on the church,” the tech said. “Police have cordoned it off. There’s… a lot of smoke. And glitter?”

“Ignore it.” Her voice sounded distant, even to her. She was leaning into the screen, her reflection a pale ghost over the scrolling text. The fabrication protocol called for a “primary resonance source.” The specifications matched the unique UHF signature of… well, of a certain carpenter’s bad days. It also required a “stabilizing organic matrix,” with a genetic marker that made her think of reactor coolant and prehistoric rage.

They were building something. And they were using pieces of her problems as the parts.

Her tell—a slight tic at the corner of her right eye, a remnant of a botched exorcism—fired twice. The board wouldn’t understand. They saw ratings, merchandise, serum sales. They didn’t see the architecture beneath the stage. This wasn’t a new revenue stream. This was a new world, and they were pouring the foundation right under her feet without asking for the keys.

The transmission fragmented. Corrupted. She lost the specifics, but the intent remained, burning in the after-image on the screen. Creation. A controlled, programmable genesis.

The harp music from the prayer loop felt like an insult. She slammed her hand on the console, killing the audio. Silence, except for the server hum and the frantic beat of her own heart in her ears.

She needed the rest of that signal. She needed to know what AM-1 was for.

A new window popped up on her screen—an internal Kraken memo. Subject: Containment of St. Bartholomew’s Incident. It outlined a strategy: blame a rogue janitor, issue a refund to the parish, donate to the police athletic league. Standard corporate sin-eating. At the bottom, a footnote: All Eden-related artifacts from the site are to be forwarded to Project Clay for analysis.

Project Clay. That was new.

Mercy leaned back. The leather chair creaked. She stared at the words. The hum from the servers wasn’t just a hum anymore. She could hear the rhythm from the UHF signal inside it, a faint, persistent thrumming, like a mechanical heart buried in the walls.

They were building something. And “Project Clay” was cleaning up the mess.

She made a decision. It wasn’t a feeling. It was a calculation that bypassed her brain and went straight to her hands. She began typing, isolating the fragments of the AM-1 protocol, copying them to an encrypted drive no bigger than her thumbnail. The corporate memo she deleted. Let them have their janitor. She had a bigger fish to fry.

The drive clicked as it ejected. She palmed it, the metal warm against her skin. The static in her headphones resolved, for one second, into a clear, synthesized tone. It sounded like a single, questioning note.

Then it was gone, swallowed back into the noise.

Mercy stood. The prayer loop was still rolling, the stained glass window now a shot of a sun-dappled forest. So peaceful. So fake. She powered down her station, each screen blinking into blackness.

The only light left was the red pulse of an exit sign over the door, and the faint, green glow of the server racks, lining the room like silent, watchful tombs.

She had a name. She had a frequency. And she had a very strong feeling that the lizard and the carpenter were about to become the least of her problems.

The night air outside Kraken Broadcasting hit her like a wet towel. Miami humidity had a weight to it, a physical presence. A neon sign across the street, advertising a bail bondsman, buzzed and sputtered, painting the puddles in the parking lot a sickly pink.

Her car—a vintage Cadillac the color of dried blood—waited under a flickering lamp. As she reached for the door handle, her earpiece, which she’d forgotten to remove, crackled with a final burst of interference.

Through the hiss, a voice. Not a broadcast. A whisper, strained and desperate, riding the last gasp of the UHF signal she’d been chasing.

“—don’t let them finish the—”

Then nothing. Just the empty, hungry sound of static.

Mercy froze, her hand on the cool chrome. A mosquito whined near her ear. Somewhere, a car alarm whooped.

She slid into the driver’s seat, closed the door on the humid night, and placed the encrypted drive on the passenger seat. It sat there, innocuous. A tiny black rectangle holding a blueprint for something that shouldn’t exist.

She started the engine. The radio, set to her own station, smoothly delivered the end of the prayer loop. “—and the Lord shall wipe every tear from their eyes,” a soothing voice promised.

Mercy switched it off. The silence in the car was total. She looked at the drive. Then she looked at the dark, mirrored facade of the broadcast tower, a needle stitching the heavens.

They were building something.

And she was going to find out what.

Mercy’s hidden office wasn’t on any Kraken floor plan. It was a converted storage closet behind the station’s main transmitter, a space that vibrated with a perpetual, tooth-loosening hum. The walls were lined with egg cartons and old hymnal pages—her version of soundproofing. It smelled of ozone, dust, and the faint, sweet decay of the communion wafers she used to snack on during all-nighters.

She slammed the door, cutting off the hum to a tolerable drone. The encrypted drive went into a reader jacked into a Frankenstein’s monster of a console: a church confessional booth computer spliced with military-grade signal intercept hardware she’d “liberated” during a botched exorcism at a defense contractor’s mansion. The screen flickered to life, casting a sickly blue light across her face.

The fragments of the AM-1 protocol reassembled themselves. Glyphs scrolled. This wasn’t just a blueprint. It was a recipe.

Primary Resonance Source: Designate ‘Nazarene Rift Residue.’ The specs listed a UHF frequency range that matched the static she’d pulled from the St. Bartholomew’s incident. They’d harvested it. Like tapping a maple tree for syrup, only the tree was a two-thousand-year-old metaphysical scar and the syrup was the raw stuff of miracles.

Her coping mechanism kicked in. Her fingers found the earlobe cross. Tap-tap-tap-tap. Too fast.

Stabilizing Organic Matrix: Designate ‘Theropod-Cybernetic Hybrid, Code: REX.’ Required: bone marrow extract, neural tissue samples, a full spectrograph of the subject’s aggression-response emissions. They didn’t just want a piece of the dinosaur. They wanted its rage, quantified and bottled.

“You’re making a cocktail,” she muttered to the screen. “And you’re using a chainsaw as a swizzle stick.”

The third component made her stop tapping.

Catalytic Binding Agent: Obedience Hormone Complex (OHC), Minimum Purity 94%. Source: Devotee Donor Pools A through G.

A footnote elaborated. Note: OHC harvest via sustained devotional ecstatic states. Recommended delivery system: broadcast-induced parasympathetic compliance. See Project HOSANNA protocols for mass collection.

Mercy knew Project HOSANNA. She’d written the damn protocols. It was the system that turned prayer into a commodity. Viewers called the 800 number, received a vial, pricked their fingers during moments of peak televised inspiration, and mailed the blood back. For a free prayer cloth. The serum extracted from those samples smoothed out the miracles, made them predictable, broadcast-friendly. She’d told the board it was a “faith concentrate.” A spiritual booster shot.

She’d never asked what they used the leftovers for.

Now she knew. They were using it as glue. To stick a piece of a demigod to a piece of a monster and call it a messiah.

The emotional shift wasn’t a wave. It was a drop in temperature. A cold, solid lump forming behind her breastbone. Appalled wasn’t the right word. This was more specific. This was the feeling you got when you realized you’d been carefully sorting garbage for recycling for years, only to learn the city was just dumping it all in the same wetland. Your effort wasn’t just wasted. It had made you an accomplice.

Her tell fired—the tic at her eye. A tiny, furious spasm.

She dug deeper. The protocol demanded a “broadcast baptism.” The AM-1 construct required activation within a “controlled revelation event,” a live transmission with a minimum audience of 2.3 million compliant souls. Their synchronized OHC levels would create a “resonance cage” for the entity’s consciousness. They weren’t just building a messiah. They were planning its coming-out party during sweeps week.

A memory intruded, irrelevant and sharp. The smell of her mentor’s pipe tobacco, cherry-blend, in a dusty rectory office. He’d said, “True power doesn’t compel. It invites.” He’d been talking about grace. About free will. He’d died confused, wondering why the demons had started quoting Nielsen ratings.

The console beeped. A correlation search finished. It had cross-referenced the OHC collection data with the broadcast logs.

A graph bloomed on the screen. Spikes in OHC purity coincided, with eerie precision, with her most-watched specials. The “Resurrection Sunday Miracle Marathon.” The “Healing the Heartland” telethon. Every time she’d gone live, looked into the camera, and asked for a show of faith… they’d been filling vials.

The highest purity spike—98.7%—was logged during her interview with little Timmy Adler, the boy who’d “beaten” leukemia after his family sent in sixty vials and a lock of hair from their deceased grandmother. Mercy remembered the boy’s wide, trusting eyes. The way he’d hugged her, smelling of hospital soap and grape lollipop.

She pushed back from the console. The chair, a rickety folding thing, squealed in protest.

The hum of the transmitter felt different now. It wasn’t just noise. It was the sound of the extraction pump. A low, industrial digesting of hope.

This was the “serpent static.” It wasn’t just Eden tech. It was the byproduct of this process. The static was the sound of a soul being processed, refined, and fed into a machine that would spit out a puppet god.

She stood, paced the three steps the room allowed. Her knee bumped a stack of old broadcast tapes labeled Praise & Prosperity Hour, ‘83-’84. They toppled, spilling across the floor. She left them there.

She needed to find the carpenter. And she needed to do it before the collection vials for the Global Pentecost special went out in the mail.
