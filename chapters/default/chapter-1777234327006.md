The lie hung in the air between them, a thin, brittle thing. Lucius had offered it like a beggar’s coin. Decimus looked from his face to Marius’s, then to Proculus, who was cradling his arm and staring at the ground as if reading his fate in the dirt. The story was there, ready. A mob of fanatics. A chaotic scuffle in the dark. The stone, rolled by many hands. It was the story that wanted to be told. It would fit the official forms, satisfy the priests, let Pilate wash his hands a second time. It was safe.

The cold seed in his gut turned over.

“We don’t say anything,” Decimus said. His voice was flat. “Not yet.”

Marius blinked. “We have to report.”

“We secure the site first.” Decimus turned toward the tomb. The morning sun painted the limestone a pale, sickly gold. The entrance was a black maw, the great disc of stone tilted on its track like a child’s toy kicked aside. The light from within was gone. That was the worst of it. The crushing pressure, the silent hum, the light that made you see the veins in a leaf from fifty paces—all of it had vanished, leaving only ordinary morning. The garden was just a garden again. Olive leaves rustled in a faint breeze. A bee droned past his ear. The normalcy was an insult.

“Secure it from what?” Proculus’s laugh was a ragged scrape. “The ghost?”

Decimus didn’t answer. He walked toward the opening. His legs moved stiffly, as if the joints had rusted. Each step was a decision. The earth where the strange patterns had been was now just scuffed dirt, thanks to Gaius’s methodical destruction. No evidence. That was good. That was procedure. He focused on the mundane details: the grit under his sandal, the strap rubbing a raw spot on his instep, the way a drop of sweat traced a path from his temple to his jaw. He counted his breaths. In. Out. The disciplined rhythm was a cage for the chaos in his skull.

He stopped a pace from the threshold. The coolness breathed out from the darkness. It smelled of damp rock, of myrrh, of something else—a faint, clean scent like ozone after a lightning strike. Not decay. Never decay. He realized he’d been braced for the smell of death.

“You’re not going in there.” Marius’s voice came from behind him, tight.

Decimus unslung his shield from his back, hefted it on his left arm. With his right, he drew his gladius. The short blade caught the sun, a brief, arrogant flash. The weight of the sword hilt in his palm was an anchor. This was a language he understood. “Watch the approach. If anyone comes—priest, woman, beggar—you hold them at the garden wall. No one passes.”

“Decimus.”

“That’s an order.”

He didn’t wait for acknowledgment. He stepped into the shadow of the antechamber.

The world narrowed. Sound died. The bee, the breeze, the shallow panting of Proculus—all of it vanished, swallowed by the thick silence of carved stone. The light from outside angled in, a sharp diagonal slab that illuminated floating motes of dust and the smooth, tool-marked floor. It reached about five feet in and stopped, defeated by the deeper dark of the burial chamber itself.

His eyes adjusted slowly. The antechamber was small, empty. A few broken pottery lamps lay in a corner, left by the women who’d come to anoint the body before the Sabbath. One was still whole, a simple terracotta dish with a pinched spout. He noted its position automatically, a soldier’s habit: potential weapon, potential obstacle. He moved past it, his sandals whispering on the stone.

The entrance to the burial chamber was a low arch. He ducked his head, his helmet scraping the lintel with a gritty skree that made his teeth ache. He froze, listening. Only the thump of his own heart in his ears.

He straightened inside the chamber.

It was not large. Perhaps eight feet square. The air was colder here, still. The light from the antechamber reached just past the arch, pooling weakly on the floor. The rest was murk. He could make out the shape of the burial bench cut from the living rock on his right, a raised shelf about hip-height. It was a solid, dark mass in the gloom.

Empty.

He knew it before his eyes confirmed it. The room had a quality he’d felt only once before, as a boy exploring an abandoned cistern—a feeling of vacancy, as if the space itself was waiting to be filled. Not just absence. Removal.

He took another step, then another, his shield held forward, sword ready at his side. The weak light from behind him began to creep across the burial bench. It illuminated the stone first, pale and porous. Then it touched the linen.

He stopped breathing.

The burial cloths lay on the slab. Not in a heap. Not torn or scattered. They were collapsed in on themselves, lying flat, as if the body inside had simply… evaporated. The shape of a head, shoulders, torso, legs—it was all there, a ghostly impression in the fine fabric. But the fabric itself was deflated, empty. At the head of the bench, separate, lay the face cloth. It was not tossed aside. It was folded. Neatly. Deliberately. A small, square napkin of linen, set apart from the rest.

No one robbed a tomb and folded the grave clothes.

No one moved a decomposing body and left the wrappings in a perfect, hollow shell.

His mind scrabbled for purchase. Animals? Impossible. The cloth was undisturbed, no drag marks, no tears. A hoax by the followers? To what end? They’d have to move a stone weighing more than twenty men, in silence, past a guard of eight, extract a corpse stiff with rigor and decay, then artfully arrange the linens to suggest a vanishing act. It was the labor of a madman. It made no tactical sense.

The pragmatic explanations rose and shattered, one after another, like glass thrown against a wall.

He forced himself closer. The linen was the expensive kind, a herringbone weave. He could see the intricate pattern even in the bad light. The spices—myrrh and aloes—had dried into the fabric, leaving dark, granular stains. He reached out with the tip of his sword, a reflexive, probing gesture. The blade touched the linen at the chest of the impression. It dipped, meeting no resistance. He lifted the edge of the cloth. Underneath was bare stone, cool and dry. No body. No trick.

He let the fabric fall back. The motion stirred the air. The clean, ozonic smell was stronger here.

His eyes kept returning to the folded face cloth. That fold was a quiet, domestic gesture. It spoke of care. Of time. It was utterly, profoundly wrong in this place of cold stone and ritual death. It was the detail that broke the back of reason. A thief panics. A zealot desecrates. A political operative covers his tracks. None of them fold laundry.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: his mother, in their cramped apartment in the Subura, folding his father’s worn tunic after washing. Her hands, red and chapped, making sharp, precise creases. A small act of order in a chaotic world. The image was so vivid it felt like a trespass here.

He backed away, his heels scuffing the floor. The shield felt suddenly absurd on his arm. He was a man in a room with a folded cloth. The greatest mystery of his life, and it was as quiet as a made bed.

He turned and blundered back toward the arch, toward the light. He needed the sun. He needed the sound of the bee. He ducked through, emerged into the antechamber, and kept going, stumbling out into the full glare of the garden.

The three men were where he’d left them, a tight knot of fear. They stared at him. His face must have told them something. Lucius took a half-step back.

“Well?” Marius’s voice was too loud.

Decimus sheathed his sword. The click of the hilt against the scabbard throat was a definitive sound. He looked at his hands. They were steady. That surprised him.

“It’s empty,” he said.

A collective release of breath. Proculus sagged. “So they did take him. The followers.”

“No.” Decimus looked past them, at the olive trees, at the city wall in the distance. A tendril of cooking smoke rose from the Lower City. The world went about its business. “The burial linens are there. Arranged.”

“Arranged?” Lucius whispered.

“The face cloth is folded.”

Silence.

Marius’s face worked. He was trying to fit this into a box labeled Duty or Regulation and the box was too small. “What does that mean?”

“It means,” Decimus said, the words ash in his mouth, “there is no body to steal. And no one to blame.”

Proculus made a small, choked sound. “Then what do we write in the report?”

Decimus finally looked at them. The seed of ice in his gut had sprouted, sending cold roots through his ribs. He had entered a soldier. He emerged something else—a witness to a verdict he did not understand. The lie Lucius had offered was still there, the safe story. But the truth of that folded cloth was a hook in his mind. It would not let go.

“We don’t write anything,” he said. His voice was calm, detached. “Not until the tribune asks. We wait. We keep the watch. We say nothing.”

“And if he asks what happened?” Marius pressed.

Decimus looked back at the dark entrance. The emptiness inside felt alive now, a presence in itself. It was listening.

“We tell him,” he said, “exactly what we saw.”

He stood there for a long time, just breathing in the cool, ozonic dark. The silence had a texture, a woolly thickness that pressed against his eardrums. From the garden outside came a muffled snatch of argument—Proculus, his voice rising in panic before being shushed. The ordinary sound made the silence inside feel deeper, more deliberate.

His right thumb began to move, rubbing back and forth against the calloused ridge of his left palm. It was a dry, whispery sound in the stillness. His father’s habit, a nervous tic from a man who rarely showed nerves. Decimus had inherited the callus from a lifetime of gripping a sword hilt, but the motion was all his own, a silent signal of a mind working too fast, grinding against a problem with no edges.

He forced himself to move, to be systematic. A soldier secures the area. He takes inventory.

He stepped closer to the burial bench, his sandals scuffing on the fine grit of the floor. He lowered his shield, letting the bottom rim rest against the stone with a soft clack. With his free hand, he gestured toward the linens, a slow, sweeping motion, as if feeling for heat or a draft. Nothing. The air was perfectly still.

The collapsed shape was precise. He crouched, bringing his eyes level with the slab. The herringbone weave of the linen was clearer now. The spices formed a darkened map across the chest and thigh areas, a topography of devotion. He followed the line of the impression from the rounded depression where a head would have been, down the slope of a shoulder, the flat plane of a torso, the twin ridges of legs. The fabric lay smooth. No wrinkles of struggle. No drag lines. It was a perfect negative, a shell.

His thumb kept rubbing.

He thought of the burial practices. He’d seen enough of them. A body was washed, anointed, wrapped tightly in strips of linen with gums and spices between the layers to combat the smell. The bundle would be stiff, cumbersome, a cocoon of cloth and resin. To remove a body, you would have to cut or unwrap it. You would leave a mess—torn fabric, spilled spices, a trail. Or you would take the entire bundle, a grisly, awkward parcel. You would not, you could not, extract the flesh from the wrapping like a snail from its shell, leaving the shell intact and neatly placed.

He reached out, hesitated, then hooked a finger under the edge of the linen at the shoulder. He lifted.

The fabric was heavier than he expected, sodden with the weight of the dried myrrh. It came up in a single sheet. Underneath was more linen, the winding cloth, lying in loose, concentric circles where the strips had fallen once the body within was gone. They, too, lay flat. No lump, no core. He let the outer sheet fall. It settled back into its ghostly shape with a soft sigh, stirring the faint, clean scent.

His eyes went again to the folded face cloth.

It sat at the head of the slab like an offering on a pillow. A square of finer linen, napkin-sized. The fold was crisp, a deliberate rectangle placed upon the stone. He leaned in. The cloth was clean, unstained by spice or sweat. It looked as if it had been laundered and pressed.

A sound escaped him, a hard breath through his nose that wasn’t quite a laugh. It was the absurdity of it. This was a detail from a rich man’s triclinium, not a tomb. A servant folding the master’s napkin after a meal. Order. Propriety. It screamed of a moment taken, a pause. Who, in the act of body-snatching or frantic cover-up, pauses to fold a piece of cloth?

His mind, trained for threats and logistics, spun uselessly. He ran through the variables like checking a duty roster. Motive? Political gain, religious fervor, theft of valuables. None fit. Means? Superhuman strength for the stone, supernatural stealth for the guard, impossible delicacy for the linens. Opportunity? A few hours of darkness, with eight armed men feet away. Every column added up to zero.

The cold was seeping into his bones. It was the damp chill of the rock, but it felt deeper, marrow-deep. The vacancy of the room was no longer passive. It felt attentive. He was the intruder here. The thought was clear and unwelcome. He was a man in armor, smelling of sweat and oiled leather, standing in a space that had been vacated with a quiet finality he could not comprehend.

He straightened, his knees popping. The shield was an absurd weight. He slid his arm free and propped it against the wall, the painted legionary emblem facing the stone, hidden. He needed his hands. He needed to touch the evidence, to prove its physicality.

He picked up the face cloth.

The linen was soft, almost slippery between his fingers. He unfolded it. A simple square. He turned it over. No marks, no stains, no hidden pins or threads. It was just cloth. He refolded it, his hands moving automatically, making the creases less sharp than they had been. He placed it back on the stone, slightly off-center. He immediately wanted to correct it, to put it back exactly as he found it. The impulse shamed him.

He turned his attention to the rest of the chamber, desperate for a distraction, for anything else. The walls were rough-hewn, marked by the chisels of the stonecutters. In one corner, a small, empty niche for an oil lamp. On the floor near the entrance, a brown stain—dried water from a leaking skin, perhaps. A chip in the bench where a tool had slipped. Normal things. Mundane imperfections.

His gaze swept back to the linens. They dominated the room. That quiet, collapsed shape was the only thing that mattered.

He found himself thinking of his father’s execution again, not the horror of it, but the aftermath. The centurion’s cloak and belt, returned to their quarters. They had been laid neatly on his cot by a comrade. The belt was polished, the cloak folded. The empty clothing holding the shape of the man who was gone. A sign of respect. A recognition of absence.

This was different. This was not an absence following violence. This was… a departure. A vacancy that felt intentional, complete.

A sliver of light from the antechamber shifted, crawling another inch across the floor as the sun outside climbed. The motes of dust danced in the new beam. For a fleeting second, he imagined he saw an imprint on the air itself, a faint resonance of the light that had been there before, the terrible, beautiful pressure. But it was just dust and shadow.

His thumb was raw. He made a fist, stopping the nervous motion.

He had seen enough. More than enough. The report would require words, and he had no words for this. Tomb empty. Linens undisturbed. Face cloth folded. It read like a madman’s riddle. The tribune would scoff. Pilate would rage. The priests would seize on the absurdity as proof of some elaborate, disrespectful fraud.

And he, Decimus Valerius, would have to stand there and swear to it.

He retrieved his shield, the familiar grip of the handle a small comfort. He gave the chamber one last look. The folded cloth was a white accusation in the gloom.

He ducked back into the antechamber, the world outside rushing back in a wave of sound and heat. The argument had stopped. He could hear the rough, rhythmic scrape of a whetstone on metal—Gaius, probably, tending to a blade. The normalcy of the sound was a jarring dissonance.

He blinked in the harsh sunlight as he emerged. The three men were still a tight knot, but they had moved, forming a loose perimeter facing outward. They turned as one at the sound of his footsteps.

Lucius opened his mouth, but no question came out. Marius just stared, his eyes searching Decimus’s face for a verdict he could understand.

Decimus didn’t speak. He walked past them to the low garden wall, placing his hands on the warm stone. He looked toward the city gate, breathing in the smells of dust and rosemary and distant dung fires. He needed to anchor himself in the real, the ordinary, the explicable.

That was when he heard it. Not the breeze, not a bird.

The distinct, soft murmur of women’s voices. The clink of pottery. The shuffle of sandals on the path.

Coming closer.

He turned his head slowly. Down the gentle slope, just becoming visible through a screen of cypress trees, were three figures. Women, dressed in the plain, dark shades of mourning. One carried a small jar. Their heads were bowed together in conversation, their pace steady and purposeful.

They were heading straight for the garden.

The image of the folded cloth flashed behind his eyes, bright and searing. Then it was gone, replaced by the immediate, pressing problem of living witnesses.

“Marius,” he said, his voice low and clear.

The legionary was at his side in an instant.

“Hold them at the wall,” Decimus said. His thumb found his palm again, but he forced it still. “No one enters.”

Marius nodded, his face hardening into the familiar mask of duty. He moved toward the garden entrance, hand on his pilum. Decimus watched him go, then looked back at the tomb’s dark mouth. The emptiness within seemed to pulse, a silent heart. The women’s voices grew clearer, a gentle, sorrowful cadence on the morning air. They had no idea what they were walking toward. He almost envied them.

The women came into full view as they rounded the last bend of the path. Three of them. Their steps were measured, heavy with the weight of the jars they carried—unguents and spices for a task that was already obsolete. Decimus recognized the lead woman from the execution site, the one they called Mary from Magdala. Her face was pale, etched with a grief so fresh it looked like a physical wound. The other two were older, their shoulders hunched as if against a cold wind.

He stood by the wall, a statue in iron and leather. Gaius had materialized from the shadows of an olive tree, his presence a silent anchor. Proculus and Lucius flanked the tomb entrance, trying unsuccessfully to look like ordinary sentries and not men who had just stared into a void.

Mary Magdalene was the first to see them. She stopped, her eyes widening briefly at the sight of the soldiers. A flicker of resentment tightened her mouth, then was swallowed by a deeper exhaustion. She said something to her companions, a low murmur. They clutched their jars closer and continued forward, their gazes fixed on the ground a few paces ahead.

Marius stepped into the center of the path, blocking the entrance to the garden proper. He planted the butt of his pilum in the dirt. “Halt. This area is restricted.”

The women stopped. Mary looked past Marius, her eyes searching for the tomb. When she saw the dark, open mouth of the entrance, her breath caught. The stone was rolled back. She hadn’t noticed until that moment.

“We have come to anoint the body of our teacher,” she said, her Aramaic clear though threaded with a Galilean accent. Her voice was hollow, stripped raw.

“No one is permitted,” Marius replied, his own Aramaic serviceable but blunt. “By order of the Procurator.”

“It is our right.” This from the oldest woman, her voice trembling with a defiance that seemed to cost her dearly. “By sunset on Preparation Day, there was no time. The Sabbath… we could not. We must finish the rites.”

Decimus watched. He could feel the tension in Marius’s back from twenty feet away. The procedural response was to turn them away, to cite the order, to threaten arrest. A week ago, Decimus would have barked the command himself. Now, the words felt like ash in his throat. They were coming to tend a corpse that was not there. They were bringing linen to re-wrap a body that had left its wrappings behind. The absurdity of it was a sharp stone in his gut.

Mary Magdalene’s eyes were still on the open tomb. The confusion was dawning, overtaking the grief. “The stone…” she whispered.

“Return to the city,” Marius said, his tone final.

But Mary was no longer listening to him. She took a step sideways, as if to peer around his bulk. Her gaze swept over the garden, over the soldiers, and landed on Decimus. Their eyes met for a fractured second. He saw no accusation there, only a desperate, uncomprehending need. It was worse than hatred. He looked away, his hand going to the hilt of his gladius. The leather binding was smooth under his palm. He centered the weapon in its scabbard, a tiny, precise adjustment. The ritual calmed nothing.

The youngest woman, a girl really, began to weep silently, her shoulders shaking. The sound was a thin wire drawn tight across the morning.

Mary shook her head, not in refusal, but in sheer bewilderment. “We must see,” she said, more to herself than to Marius.

She moved. Not with force, but with the inevitable momentum of a heart breaking. She walked straight past Marius, her steps quickening. He shifted, his hand coming up to grab her arm, but he hesitated. He looked back at Decimus, a question in his eyes.

Decimus gave a single, sharp shake of his head. Let her go. The order was silent, but Marius saw it. He lowered his arm. He watched, stunned, as Mary Magdalene brushed past him, her companions following in her wake like stunned birds.

Decimus’s thumb found the callus on his palm. He rubbed it slowly, his eyes tracking the women as they crossed the small garden. They ignored the soldiers completely now, their world narrowed to the black opening in the rock. The girl’s weeping grew louder, a high, keening note.

Mary reached the entrance first. She paused on the threshold, her body outlined against the darkness. She leaned forward, peering into the antechamber. She called out a name—a Hebrew name Decimus did not recognize. Her voice echoed slightly, a lonely sound swallowed by stone.

She vanished inside.

The other two women huddled at the entrance, clutching each other. One of them set her jar down on the ground with a soft clink. The sound was absurdly loud.

Seconds stretched. A bee drifted lazily over a patch of sage. Somewhere in the city, a donkey brayed. Decimus could hear the rasp of his own breath. He could imagine exactly what Mary was seeing. The empty antechamber. The arch. The weak light falling on the burial bench. The collapsed linens. The folded cloth.

A cry came from the tomb. Not of grief, but of sheer, unadulterated shock. It was short, sharp, animal. Then silence.

The two women at the entrance stumbled in after her.

Decimus forced himself to breathe. In. Out. He kept his eyes on the city wall, on the solid, unmoving line of it. He counted the courses of stone. He noted a crack in the mortar six courses up, shaped like a leaning tree. He focused on it until his vision blurred.

Muffled sounds came from the tomb now—rapid, whispering voices, overlapping, rising in pitch. A sob. Then a frantic scraping, as if someone was dragging their hands over the stone bench. A jar broke, a ceramic shatter followed by the sudden, pungent release of spikenard, a cloyingly sweet smell that cut across the clean scent of rosemary.

He looked at Gaius. The older soldier’s face was a closed door. He gave a minute shrug, a gesture that said, This is not our problem. This is a civilian mess.

Mary Magdalene burst from the tomb. She did not walk. She ran. Her face was a mask of terror, her eyes wide and unseeing. She slammed into the younger woman still standing outside, nearly knocking her over, and did not stop. She fled down the garden path, back the way they had come, her sandals slapping against the hard earth. Her shawl fluttered behind her like a wounded wing.

The remaining two women emerged more slowly, clinging to one another. Their faces were blank with a confusion so profound it had erased all other expression. They looked at the soldiers, at the trees, at the sky, as if the world itself had become unrecognizable. One of them pointed a shaking finger back at the tomb entrance and said something in a voice too low to catch. They collected their remaining jar and, without another glance, hurried after Mary, their steps clumsy with fear.

The garden was quiet again. The bee circled back. The smell of spikenard hung in the air, a funeral scent for a funeral that hadn’t happened.

Proculus let out a long, shaky breath. “What in Hades was that?”

“They saw,” Lucius whispered. “They saw it.”

“They saw an empty hole,” Gaius grunted, but the conviction in his voice was thin, brittle.

Decimus finally took his hand from his sword. His palm was sore. He walked toward the tomb. He didn’t know why. He just needed to stand at the nexus of it again, the place where the impossible had touched the ordinary. He stopped at the entrance. The broken jar lay just inside, a pool of dark oil seeping into the dirt floor. The sharp, floral stench of the spilled ointment mixed with the older smell of myrrh and stone.

From the city, a different sound began to rise. Not the normal morning clamor, but a specific, gathering commotion. Voices calling. The sound of many feet on the stony paths. It was coming from the direction Mary had run.

Gaius heard it too. He moved to Decimus’s side, his eyes narrowed. “That’s a crowd.”

Decimus knew what it was. The news was a spark in dry tinder. The woman’s terror would become a story. The story would become a summons. The followers, the curious, the opponents—they would all come. They would come to see the empty space for themselves.

He looked at his men. Proculus was chewing his lip raw. Lucius had his eyes screwed shut, muttering a prayer to a household god. Marius stood rigid, waiting for an order that would make sense of the hour.

The tribune would have to be informed now. The priests would be in an uproar. Pilate’s brittle peace was cracking under a weight no one could see. And he, Decimus Valerius, would have to stand before them and speak. He would have to choose his words with the care of a man crossing a frozen river. One misstep, and the ice would give way.

The noise from the city swelled, a wave of human sound rolling toward the garden walls. He could pick out individual shouts now, questions sharp with panic and a desperate, terrifying hope.

He turned his back on the tomb. The darkness at his back felt like a second presence, a silent judge.

“Form up,” he said, his voice cutting through the murmurs of his men. “Shields front. Pilums ready. No one enters this garden until I say.”

They moved, the drill of a lifetime overriding their fear. They fell into a line, a barrier of iron and flesh between the empty tomb and the approaching world.

Decimus took his place at the center of the line. He adjusted the grip on his shield. He squared his shoulders. He built the wall of himself, stone by stone, and waited for the storm to break.
