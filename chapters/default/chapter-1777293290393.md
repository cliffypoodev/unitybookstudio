Giovanni knew the basil was weeping. He could tell by the faint, high-frequency shudder in the stem, a vibration transmitted through the marble countertop into the bones of his wrists. He’d been a chef for two hundred and thirteen years, and his sense of taste had died sometime around the Great War, but his other senses had grown compensatory quirks. He could identify oregano by the way it crumbled under pressure, like dried spider legs. He could gauge the fat content of ground beef by listening to its sizzle—a low, wet protest versus a high, desperate scream. And he could feel a plant’s emotional state through its structural integrity. This basil was depressed.

His restaurant, The Underground Roman, occupied a converted storage basement beneath a Las Vegas pawn shop specializing in prosthetic limbs. The air smelled of damp concrete, garlic, and the ghost of a thousand failed business ventures. Frescoes of gladiators battling tigers, painted by a drunk muralist named Vince, peeled in the humidity. Neon signs spelling out “SPQR” and “ALL GLORY TO THE EMPIRE” flickered and buzzed, casting a sickly green light over the stainless steel kitchen. It was Tuesday. The only reservation was for seven o’clock: a party of twelve under the name “The Gastric Ghoul and Associates.”

Giovanni didn’t need a Yelp alert to know what that meant. Zombie food critics.

They were a new demographic, born from some combination of bad buffet sushi and a cursed slot machine payout. They roamed the Strip, their taste buds completely necrotized, their reviews based entirely on texture, presentation, and a vague memory of what flavor used to be. They were impossible to please. A zombie critic once gave a five-star review to a plate of decorative gravel because it “crunched with authority.” Another condemned a perfectly seared steak because it “failed to evoke the melancholy of cemetery moss.”

He checked his watch. Four hours.

His current menu was a tribute to Roman excess: stuffed dormice on tiny skewers (frozen mice from a pet store supplier, a fact he tried not to dwell on), lentil stew with a lead-based seasoning he claimed was “historically accurate,” and his signature dish, the Lazarus Lasagna. The lasagna was his gambit. Legend said it could resurrect dead taste buds. The legend was started by Giovanni himself in a moment of marketing desperation. The secret ingredient was his own tears, which, due to his immortal constitution, carried a faint trace of divine grace. Or maybe just a lot of salt. He hadn’t cried in forty years. The last time was over a ruined cassoulet.

He opened the walk-in cooler. The holy cheese—a wheel of Pecorino Romano blessed by a defrocked priest in 1953—sat on the middle shelf. He reached for it. His hand closed on empty air.

The cheese was gone.

A cold panic, sharp and immediate, locked his ribs. He stared at the blank space. Then he noticed the note, tucked under a tub of ricotta. It was written on grease-proof paper in a looping, archaic script.

Giovanni. The cheese has been recalled. Its holiness was in breach of contractual purity. You will be assessed at seven. Prepare a dish that satisfies both the flesh and the spirit. Or perish.

No signature. The paper smelled faintly of ozone and wilted arugula.

He crumpled the note. The walk-in cooler hummed. The basil continued its silent weeping on the counter. Outside, the distant thump of bass from a club upstairs vibrated through the floor, a rhythm like a dying heart.

Mario, his waiter and sole employee, stumbled in at three. He wore a toga that was mostly safety pins and a perpetual expression of confused betrayal. “The mice arrived,” he announced, holding a cardboard box. “They’re… twitching.”

“They’re frozen,” Giovanni said, not turning from his station. He was trying to whip egg whites into a cloud without any sense of whether they were stiff enough. He judged by sound: the slap of the whisk against the bowl should echo like a slap in an empty cathedral. Today it sounded like a wet sock.

“I think one blinked at me.”

“It’s a trick of the light.” Giovanni stopped whisking. “Mario. The holy cheese is missing.”

Mario set the box down. A faint squeak emerged from it. “Did you eat it?”

“It was stolen. Or recalled. By vegan angels, apparently.”

Mario blinked. “Vegan angels?”

“It’s the only thing that makes sense.” Giovanni gestured at the note on the counter. “They’re purists. No animal products. Holiness must be uncontaminated by dairy. They’ve probably been monitoring me. The zombie critics are just their foot soldiers.”

Mario picked up the note, frowned at the script. “This looks like my grandma’s handwriting. She was a militant vegetarian. She once threw a sausage at my dad. Not to eat. To assault.”

“We need a replacement.” Giovanni’s mind raced, scanning his centuries of culinary memory. “Something that carries emotional weight. That has… narrative resonance.”

“We have emotional cheese?”

“No. But we have emotional ingredients.” Giovanni looked at Mario. “What makes you cry?”

Mario thought. “My student loans. The ending of The Iron Giant. That time I spilled olive oil on my favorite shirt.”

“Physical pain? Sad movies? Financial despair?” Giovanni’s eyes glazed, calculating. “We need a tear substitute. A substance that embodies mortal suffering.”

“The lentil stew is pretty sad.”

“It’s sad. It’s not tragic.” Giovanni paced. The neon SPQR flickered, staining his hands green. “Tragedy requires hope, then loss. We need an ingredient that once believed in a better future.”

Mario opened the fridge. “We have these heirloom tomatoes. They’re from a farm that got foreclosed on.”

“Foreclosure tomatoes.” Giovanni took one. It was a deep, bruised red. He held it to his ear. A faint, hollow sound, like a distant sob. “Good. But not enough. We need something… abandoned.”

His eyes landed on the box of frozen dormice.

“Mario,” he said. “Did you ever have a pet?”

“I had a hamster. Named Bubbles.”

“What happened to Bubbles?”

Mario’s face darkened. “He escaped into the ventilation system. We heard him scratching for weeks. Then silence.”

Giovanni nodded slowly. He opened the box. Twelve frozen dormice lay in a bed of ice chips. Their tiny faces were locked in expressions of mild surprise. He picked one up. It was lighter than he expected. An emptiness in the bones. “These weren’t bred for food,” he murmured. “They were pet store mice. They were meant for children. For little hands. For names.” He felt a peculiar vibration in the rodent’s spine—a resonance of unfulfilled destiny. “They’re perfect.”

“You’re going to cook them with the foreclosure tomatoes?”

“And the depressed basil.” Giovanni placed the mouse on the counter. “We’ll make a sauce. A sauce of shattered dreams.”

Mario looked horrified. “That’s the most Vegas thing I’ve ever heard.”

By five o’clock, the sauce simmered on the stove. It smelled of tomato, regret, and a hint of cedar from the mouse bones Giovanni had crushed into a fine powder. He called it “Soffritto of Lost Potential.” He layered the lasagna: sheets of pasta he’d rolled so thin they transmitted light like stained glass, a ricotta mixture he’d whipped with the air of a forgotten birthday party (achieved by blowing across the surface of the cream while remembering his own 100th birthday, a party no one attended), and the dream-shatter sauce.

He still lacked the cheesy element. The binding agent of hope.

He stared at the empty cooler shelf. The vegan angels had taken the holy cheese because it was animal-derived. Their purity was absolute. They wanted a dish that satisfied flesh and spirit. The zombie critics were their fleshly proxies—they needed texture, presentation, a story. The angels were the spiritual judges—they needed ethical purity, a narrative free of sin.

Giovanni was an immortal. His sin was longevity. He’d outlived his taste, his passions, his reasons to cry. He was a culinary monument to persistence without purpose.

He walked to the small mirror nailed beside the spice rack. He looked at his own eyes. Dry. Worn. Like old leather. He tried to think of something sad. The ruined cassoulet didn’t work anymore. He thought of the first restaurant he’d owned, in Naples. The smell of the sea mixing with garlic. The laughter. Then he thought of the silence that followed, century after century, kitchen after kitchen. The endless repetition of recipes for people who couldn’t taste them either. A wave of something hot moved through his chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name.

A single tear welled in his right eye.

It clung to his lower lid, hesitant. He watched it. A tiny globe of salt and memory. He let it build. It trembled. Then it fell.

He caught it on his fingertip. It was warm. It tasted, to his dead palate, like nothing. But the air around it seemed to shimmer—not in a way you could see, but in a way you could hear. A faint ringing, like a tiny bell tolling in a distant chapel.

He had one tear.

Not enough for a whole lasagna.

He needed to cry properly. He needed a catalyst.

“Mario,” he called. “What is the saddest song you know?”

Mario, who was polishing spoons with a look of existential defeat, said, “‘My Heart Will Go On.’ But the pan flute version.”

“Play it.”

Mario dug out his phone. A tinny, mournful pan flute rendition filled the kitchen. It was the sound of a ghost trying to remember how to breathe.

Giovanni listened. The melody was repetitive, a loop of despair. He felt nothing. His tear ducts remained sealed.

“It’s not working,” he said.

“Try this.” Mario switched tracks. A man’s voice, cracking with emotion, sang about lost dogs and empty porches.

Giovanni’s nose tingled. A pressure built behind his eyes. Then the phone died.

The silence that followed was worse than the song. It was the silence of a dead device, a flat battery, a failed connection.

That did it.

A tear rolled down Giovanni’s left cheek. Another followed. They weren’t the sobbing, generous tears of grief. They were the tight, reluctant tears of technological frustration. He collected them in a small ceramic bowl. Four tears. Each one carried the specific sorrow of a dropped call.

He mixed them into the ricotta. The cream absorbed them without changing color. But the air in the kitchen shifted. The neon buzz softened. The humming cooler quieted. Mario stopped polishing spoons and stared at the bowl.

“It smells different,” Mario said.

“What does it smell like?”

“Like… the moment you realize you’ve left your wallet at home.”

“Perfect.” Giovanni incorporated the tear-infused ricotta into the final layer. He sealed the lasagna with a top sheet of pasta. He scored the surface with a knife, creating a pattern that echoed the cracks in the ceiling above—a map of structural fatigue. He brushed the top with olive oil that he’d infused with the smoke of a burned mortgage application (a trick he’d learned from a chef during the 2008 recession). The lasagna glistened under the green neon, a mosaic of edible despair.

He slid it into the oven. The timer ticked down.

At six forty-five, the doorbell rang. It wasn’t a ring. It was a low, resonant groan, like a stone door sliding open in a tomb.

Mario adjusted his toga. “Should I… greet them?”

“Yes. But don’t smile. Zombies interpret smiling as aggressive optimism. They hate that.”

Mario left the kitchen. Giovanni heard the front door open. A draft of cold, dry air swept into the kitchen, carrying a scent of turned earth and old newspaper.

Then the voices. Not voices. A collective murmur, a sound like pages rustling in a closed book.

Giovanni peered through the service window.

The dining room was lit by candlelight, which flickered over twelve figures seated at the long central table. They were not decomposed, as he’d feared. They were impeccably dressed in suits and dresses from various decades—a 1920s flapper, a 1970s leisure suit, a 1990s power blazer. Their skin was pale, but intact. Their eyes were the problem. They glowed with a faint, yellowish light, like low-wattage bulbs. They did not blink. They stared at the empty table before them with a fixed, hungry vacancy.

At the head of the table sat the Gastric Ghoul. He wore a tuxedo that had faded to a grayish lavender. His hair was thin, combed over a scalp that showed the faint map of his skull beneath. He held a pen in one hand, a notebook in the other. He did not move.

Mario approached. “Welcome to The Underground Roman. Can I… get you anything to start? Water? Perhaps a wine list that echoes the futility of choice?”

The Gastric Ghoul lifted his head. His jaw moved, a slow, grinding motion. “We require… sustenance. Not nourishment. Sustenance.”

His voice was a recording played at half-speed.

“Of course,” Mario said. “Our chef has prepared a special tasting menu. A journey through… emotional bankruptcy.”

The Ghoul’s glowing eyes fixed on Mario. “We are here to assess… the moral weight of your ingredients. The cheese. Where is the cheese?”

Giovanni’s heart hammered against his ribs. He ducked back below the window.

From the oven, the lasagna began to smell. Not a food smell. A smell of crowded subway cars at the end of the line. A smell of unanswered phones. A smell of watching rain through a window when you have nowhere to go.

It was ready.

He opened the oven. The lasagna had risen, golden-brown at the edges, the scored cracks deepened into ravines. He lifted it out. It was heavier than any lasagna he’d ever made. It seemed to pull toward the floor, as if weighed down by its own content.

He carried it to the service window. He nodded at Mario.

Mario announced, “The main course. Lazarus Lasagna. A layered exploration of hope deferred.”

Giovanni brought the lasagna to the table. He set it before the Gastric Ghoul. The glowing eyes lowered to the dish. The other critics leaned forward, a synchronized motion that creaked like old furniture.

The Ghoul picked up his fork. It trembled in his grip. He inserted it into the lasagna, lifted a small square to his mouth. He ate.

His jaw stopped grinding.

The yellow glow in his eyes flickered. Dimmed. Then brightened into a white, sharp light.

He dropped the fork. It clattered on the plate.

“This…” he said. The recorded voice sped up, gaining a human pitch. “This contains no cheese.”

Giovanni’s mouth went dry. “It contains… the memory of cheese. The architectural longing for dairy.”

The Ghoul stood. His chair scraped back. “The cheese was the point of assessment. Its holiness was compromised by bovine exploitation. You have substituted… melancholy rodent and foreclosure tomato.” He pointed a finger at Giovanni. The finger was long, pale, the nail yellowed like old parchment. “This is an evasion. A culinary sleight-of-hand.”

The other critics murmured. Their glowing eyes swiveled to Giovanni. The air grew colder.

“The angels demand purity,” the Ghoul continued. “You offer… pathos. Pathos is not purity. It is contamination by sentiment.”

Giovanni felt something break in his chest. Not a bone. A resolve. He’d spent centuries cooking for people who couldn’t taste. He’d built a mythology around his own inability. This was the final judgment—not of his food, but of his entire, endless endeavor.

He looked at the lasagna. At the faces of the critics. At Mario, who was gripping a spoon like a weapon.

Then he laughed.

It was a short, sharp bark of a laugh. It surprised him. It surprised everyone.

The Ghoul’s glowing eyes narrowed. “You laugh at judgment?”

“I laugh at the premise,” Giovanni said. He stepped forward. The green neon light streaked his arms. “You want purity. Vegan angels. No animal products. Fine. But you sent zombie critics. Dead flesh. You’re judging me for using animal-derived holiness while you employ the walking dead. That’s not purity. That’s a contradiction.”

The Ghoul froze. The other critics rustled.

Giovanni pointed at the lasagna. “That dish is made of lost pets, foreclosure tomatoes, and tears born from a dead phone battery. It’s the purest thing in this room. It’s made of things that failed. You,” he said, pointing at the Ghoul, “are a zombie. You’re a thing that succeeded. You’re dead, but you’re still working. You’re still judging. That’s not pure. That’s stubborn.”

The Ghoul’s mouth opened. Closed. He looked at the lasagna. He looked at his notebook. He set the notebook down.

He picked up his fork again. He took another bite. A larger one.

He chewed. Slowly.

The yellow glow in his eyes softened. It warmed. It became something like candlelight.

He took another bite.

Then he began to cry.

Not a zombie cry. A human cry. Tears, clear and salty, rolled down his pale cheeks. They dripped onto his tuxedo lapel.

The other critics watched. One of them, the flapper, reached out with a trembling hand and took a bite from the platter. She chewed. A tear traced through her powder makeup.

Another critic tried it. Then another.

Soon, all twelve were eating the lasagna. And all twelve were weeping.

Mario stared. Giovanni stood, his own eyes dry again, watching.

The Ghoul finished his portion. He wiped his face with a napkin. The napkin came away stained with tears and mascara (from the flapper) and a bit of leisure suit lint.

He looked at Giovanni. The glow in his eyes was gone. His eyes were now ordinary, brown, wet.

“The assessment,” he said, his voice fully human, “is complete.”

He opened his notebook. He wrote a single word.

Then he stood, and the other critics stood with him. They left the lasagna half-eaten. They walked to the door, a group of ordinary-looking people in outdated clothes, their faces streaked with tears.

They exited without paying.

The door groaned shut.

Giovanni and Mario stood in the silent dining room. The candles guttered. The smell of the lasagna lingered—lost dreams and dead phones.

Mario picked up the notebook the Ghoul had left behind. He opened it. He showed Giovanni the page.

On it, written in shaky script, was the word: ALIVE.

Giovanni looked at the lasagna. He took a fork from the table. He carved a small piece. He put it in his mouth.

He chewed.

For the first time in a century, he tasted something.

It wasn’t cheese. It wasn’t tomato. It wasn’t mouse.

It was the taste of a doorbell that groans like a tomb opening. It was the taste of neon buzzing on damp concrete. It was the taste of a single tear that won’t fall.

It was flavor.

He swallowed. He set the fork down.

Mario said, “Should I… clean up?”

Giovanni looked at the fresco of the gladiator battling the tiger. The tiger was winning. The gladiator’s spear was broken.

“No,” Giovanni said. “Leave it. I think we’re done for the night.”

He walked back to the kitchen. The basil on the counter had stopped weeping. It stood upright, its leaves firm. It smelled, finally, like basil.

The silence in the kitchen was a physical thing. It sat on the counters, thick as the layer of grease on the exhaust hood. Giovanni stared at the upright basil. Its sudden perkiness felt like a taunt.

Mario appeared in the doorway, the notebook dangling from his fingers. “They left this.”

“I saw.”

“The word is ‘alive.’” Mario said it like he was checking a translation.

“I read it.”

“What does it mean?”

Giovanni turned on the sink. The water hit the stainless steel with a crackle that was too loud. “It means they felt something. Which means I made something that could be felt.” He scrubbed his hands, though they weren’t dirty. The soap smelled of industrial lemon, a scent that always reminded him of bus station bathrooms. “It doesn’t mean they liked it.”

“They cried.”

“Crying isn’t a starred review.” He shut off the water. Drops fell from his fingertips, each one tapping a different rhythm on the basin. “It’s a physiological response to profound disappointment. Or onions.”

He looked at the half-eaten lasagna in the dining room. It slumped in the center of the table, a beige monument collapsing under its own symbolic weight. He should box it up. Try to sell it as leftovers on a sketchy app. ‘Gourmet Regret – $25/half-pound.’

The doorbell groaned.

Not the front door. The service entrance. The alley door.

It was a sound that didn’t belong. That door was for deliveries, for trash, for running away. It hadn’t groaned in twenty years. The mechanism was corroded shut.

Giovanni and Mario looked at each other. Mario’s toga slipped off one shoulder.

The groan came again. Longer. More insistent. It was the sound of a large stone being rolled away from a tomb, if the tomb was in a back alley off the Strip next to a dumpster that always smelled of spoiled egg salad.

Giovanni walked to the door. He didn’t want to. His feet moved anyway. He unbolted the top lock. The bottom lock. He turned the handle.

The door opened inward, smoothly, on hinges that suddenly didn’t squeak.

Three figures stood in the alley. They were backlit by the sodium-vapor glow of the street, so he saw them first as silhouettes. Tall. Impossibly slender. They wore what looked like long coats, but the fabric didn’t move like cloth. It moved like water, or like heat haze.

“Giovanni,” the central figure said. Its voice was not a voice. It was the concept of a voice, delivered directly into the space behind his eyes. It tasted, synesthetically, of cold mint and static electricity.

He said nothing. His tongue was a dry sponge.

The figure stepped forward into the kitchen light.

It was not a zombie. It was not human. Its face was a smooth oval, featureless except for two recessed points of light where eyes might be. Its “coat” was a cascade of luminous strands that shifted from grey to silver to a transparent non-color. It had no feet. It hovered an inch above the stained concrete floor. It did not cast a shadow.

The other two followed. One was a shade of blue so deep it hurt to look at. The other was the color of a forgotten memory.

Mario made a small sound, like a mouse being stepped on. He was.

The central being—Giovanni’s mind, scrabbling for a label, supplied “The Assessor”—glided past him into the kitchen. Its light-point eyes scanned the room: the weeping basil (now smug), the box of frozen dormice, the note about breached holiness still crumpled on the counter.

“The cheese was a test,” The Assessor intoned. The words formed in the air, visible for a moment as shimmering letters before dissolving. “Its removal was the first question. Your response was the answer.”

Giovanni found his voice. It came out cracked. “You’re the vegan angels.”

“Vegan is a mortal term. A crude approximation. We are the Curators of Uncontaminated Sustenance. The cheese was divine in origin, but processed through a mammary gland. This introduced a… narrative complication. A bias. We required clarity.”

The blue one drifted to the stove. A tendril of light extended from its form, touched the cooling pot of ‘Soffritto of Lost Potential.’ The sauce hissed softly, like a sigh.

“You attempted substitution,” The Assessor continued. “You utilized mammalian pathos (rodent), botanical despair (tomato), and technological grief (tears). A poignant collage. But not pure.”

“The zombies liked it,” Mario blurted from the doorway. He immediately clapped a hand over his mouth.

The forgotten-memory-colored being turned its light-points toward Mario. “The Gastric Ghoul and Associates were not zombies. They were proxies. Simulacra woven from archived human sorrow and a mild fungal network. Their purpose was to gauge the resonance of your creation within a framework of decay. They were the flesh test. We are the spirit test.”

“Tomorrow,” Giovanni said, looking at the dust on the counter, the peeling paint, the dark neon tube. “We’re changing the menu.”
