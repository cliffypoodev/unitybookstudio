The van's muffler coughed up one last black cloud as Dutch skidded into the alley behind Sister Mercy's shelter. Jesus braced a hand against the dashboard. Not for the first time, he wondered if the Second Coming should have included seatbelts.

"Home sweet hideout," Dutch announced, killing the engine. The shelter's back door swung open before they'd even unbuckled. Sister Mercy stood silhouetted in the doorway, wooden spoon in hand like a scepter.

"You brought reporters."

"Technically," Dutch said, "they followed us."

Mercy's spoon twitched. Jesus could see the exact moment she decided not to ask. She jerked her chin toward the kitchen. "There's dishes."

The sink was piled high with tamale pots and grease-streaked plates. The scent of cumin and lard clung to the air, thick as the memory of Capernaum's market days. Jesus rolled up his sleeves.

Lola leaned against the fridge, arms crossed. "We should be moving."

"Kid needs stitches," Mercy said without looking up from scrubbing a skillet. "You got a med kit in that van?"

Dutch peered at Miguel's arm. "It's barely a scratch."

"It's a bullet graze." Mercy tossed Jesus a dishrag. "You. Dry."

The rhythm of it was familiar—scrub, rinse, pass. The soap bubbles caught the fluorescent light, iridescent for a heartbeat before vanishing. Outside, Rex's tail thumped against the alley wall, rattling the framed Last Supper print above the sink.

Lola's fingers drummed against her holstered Glock. "Every cop in Miami's seen that newspaper."

"Relax, Detective." Dutch rummaged through the pantry. "We're in a church. Sanctuary, baby."

"Baptist shelter," Mercy corrected. "And that only works if the priest doesn't hand you over." She nodded toward the common room, where a gaunt man in a threadbare suit was lecturing an empty chair about fluoride.

Jesus wrung out the rag. "You take everyone?"

"Try not to." She hip-checked him aside to grab a pot. "But they keep showing up."

Through the serving window, he watched a teenager pick at the crusted ketchup on his shirt. The boy's pupils were pinpricks, his fingers twitching like he was plucking invisible strings. A woman rocked in the corner, humming a hymn through broken teeth.

Mercy followed his gaze. "Not what you remember from the good book, huh?"

"The poor are always with you." He handed her a dripping plate. "I just thought there'd be fewer track marks."

She snorted. "Welcome to Reagan's America."

Dutch reappeared with a box of generic cereal. "So here's the play—we rebrand. Church of the Holy Chomp gets a soup kitchen angle. Tax deductible, better optics."

Lola's grip tightened on her gun. "We're not starting a cult."

"Too late." Dutch gestured to the common room TV, where a news anchor stood in front of a Rex-themed mural. The chyron read: HOLY CHOMP MOVEMENT GAINS MOMENTUM.

Mercy dried her hands on her apron. "Kid. A word?" She jerked her head toward the walk-in fridge.

The chill inside smelled of sour milk and old lettuce. Mercy let the door swing shut behind them.

"You're really him, aren't you?"

Jesus studied a crate of wilted celery. "Depends who's asking."

"Cut the crap." She folded her arms. "I grew up Pentecostal. I know a laying-on-of-hands miracle when I see one."

The walk-in's hum filled the silence. A single drop of condensation fell from the ceiling, landing on his wrist like a baptism.

Mercy's voice softened. "Why here? Why now?"

"I woke up in a swamp." He traced the celery's ribs. "I think... I might be lost."

She barked a laugh. "Join the club."

Outside, something shattered. A man yelled in Spanish. Mercy sighed and pushed the door open. "Dishes first. Existential crises later."

The common room had devolved into chaos. A fight had broken out over the TV remote—two men grappling in front of a televangelist's gleaming smile. Rex's fans clustered by the exit, filming everything.

Lola had her gun out. "Enough!"

The room froze. Even the televangelist seemed to pause mid-sermon.

Miguel piped up from the couch. "She's scarier than the dinosaur."

Mercy snatched the remote and clicked off the TV. "Soup's at six. Anyone not eating can leave." The crowd dispersed like scolded children.

Dutch sidled up to Jesus. "So. Healing powers?"

"No."

"Worth a shot." He shrugged and wandered off to chat up the Rex fans.

Lola holstered her weapon. "We need to talk." She led Jesus to a corner booth, its vinyl cracked like a dried riverbed. From her pocket, she pulled a folded memo stamped HOMESTEAD AFB.

"Eden World wasn't just a lab." She smoothed the paper. "It's a front for something called Project LAZARUS. They've been funneling money through shell companies—including one that funds this shelter."

Jesus studied the memo. The numbers meant nothing, but the signature did: Col. A. Cain.

Lola's knee bounced under the table. "Mercy's clean. But if Cain's watching the shelter..."

A crash from the kitchen. They turned to see Dutch standing over a shattered plate, a fan club member snapping photos. "Miracle shards! Limited edition!"

Mercy's spoon made contact with Dutch's skull. "Out."

As the chaos swirled around them, Jesus folded the memo and slipped it into his pocket. The paper felt heavy as a stone.

Mercy dropped into the booth across from him. "You still washing dishes?"

He nodded toward the sink. "Someone has to."

"Damn right." She pushed a mug of coffee toward him. It was bitter, lukewarm, and the closest thing to communion he'd had since the swamp.

Outside, the Rex fans' camera flashes strobed through the window like lightning. Dutch held court by the dumpster, spinning some new scheme. Lola muttered into a pay phone, her back rigid with tension.

Mercy sipped her coffee. "Salvation's messy work."

Jesus watched a soap bubble drift from the sink and pop midair. "It always was."

The spoon clattered against her mug. "Then get back to it."

He stood, rolling up his sleeves again. The dishes waited. The poor waited. And somewhere beyond the shelter's walls, Colonel Cain waited too.

The water ran hot. The suds piled high. For the first time since the rift, Jesus of Nazareth felt something like purpose.

Lola kept her voice low, eyes scanning the shelter's parking lot. "Homestead, this is Vega. Run a plate for me."

Static crackled. Dispatch came back bored. "Make, model?"

"Gray sedan. Ford, maybe. Tag reads..." She squinted through the chain-link fence. The car had been there since sundown, engine off but exhaust still warm when she'd checked. The driver hadn't moved. Just watched. "Whiskey Tango Foxtrot 338."

A pause. Keys clacked. "Registered to Everglades Pest Control."

Lola's knuckles whitened. "Since when do exterminators wear tactical boots?" She'd clocked the shine on the man's laces when he'd "adjusted" his antenna.

Dispatch sighed. "You want a patrol car?"

"No." The last thing she needed was backup seeing her consort with a messiah and a cyborg dinosaur. "Just run the company."

Another pause, longer. "Uh, Vega? That LLC dissolved in '79."

The sedan's window rolled down an inch. A cigarette tip glowed inside.

Lola hung up.

Back in the shelter, Dutch was demonstrating Rex's "holy chomp" to the fan club using two donuts and exaggerated sound effects. Jesus scrubbed a pot with the focus of a man trying to bleach the Second Coming out of existence. Mercy flicked a dish towel at a kid trying to pocket silverware.

Normal chaos, except for the man outside.

Lola slid into the booth where she'd left the Homestead memo. Gone. Her stomach dropped. Then she spotted the corner of it peeking from under Dutch's denim jacket.

She lunged.

Dutch spun, donuts held aloft like sacraments. "Whoa, Detective. Communion's at six."

"Move." She yanked the jacket. The memo fluttered to the floor—along with three Polaroids.

Dutch scooped them up too fast. Not fast enough.

Lola had seen the first shot: Eden World's main gate, rusted but intact. The second: a concrete bunker half-hidden by sawgrass.

She grabbed his wrist. "Where did you get that?"

The photo showed Rex in a steel cage, his cybernetic crown sparking. A figure in a lab coat stood just outside the frame. All she could see was the hand on the control panel. A hand with a distinctive scar across the knuckles.

Dutch's smile didn't reach his eyes. "eBay?"

"Bullshit." She stepped closer, her heel crushing the memo underfoot. "You knew about Project LAZARUS before today."

The fan club edged back. Even Mercy paused mid-swipe.

Dutch's jaw worked. For once, he had nothing to say.

A crash from the kitchen. Jesus dropped a pan. When Lola glanced over, Dutch bolted.

She was on him in three strides, slamming him against the fridge. Condiment bottles rattled. "Talk."

"Okay, okay!" He held up the photos. "I was freelance press, all right? Before the van. Before the..." He waved at Rex's fans. "This."

Lola tightened her grip. "Press don't sneak into black sites."

"Freelance means freelance." He tried a grin. It wilted under her glare. "Look, I heard rumors. Some DOD project out in the Glades. Went to check it out, got these, then..." His voice dropped. "Then they burned my credentials. And my apartment."

She released him. "Who's in the car outside?"

Dutch paled. "What car?"

The shelter's front door burst open.

Everyone turned. The man from the sedan stood there, cigarette dangling from his lips. He wasn't tall. Wasn't broad. Just... hard. Like a bullet casing.

Mercy stepped forward, spoon raised. "We're closed."

The man flicked ash onto her floor. "Not for me." His gaze locked on Dutch. "Long time, Donnie."

Dutch's Adam's apple bobbed. "Hey, Marco. Uh. You get my messages?"

Marco took a drag. Exhaled slow. "You left town owing my boss twenty grand."

The fan club collectively edged toward the fire exit.

Lola's hand drifted to her holster. "This a social call, Marco?"

He finally looked at her. "Detective Vega. Heard you were suspended." His smile showed teeth. "Funny seeing you here."

Jesus set down the pot. "Perhaps we could discuss this outside."

Marco laughed, a short, sharp sound. "Yeah. Let's." He reached inside his jacket.

Lola drew. "Don't."

He froze. Slowly withdrew his hand, holding a business card. "Relax. Just giving Donnie a reminder." He tossed it at Dutch's feet. "Forty-eight hours. Or next time, I bring friends."

The door swung shut behind him.

Silence. Then Miguel piped up, "So... not a Rex fan?"

Mercy whacked Dutch with the spoon. "You brought loan sharks to my shelter?"

Dutch scooped up the card. "Technically, he followed me."

Lola holstered her gun. "Who's his boss?"

"Catalina Morado." Dutch swallowed. "You might know her as La Reina Blanca."

The name hit like a gut punch. Miami's quiet queen. The cartel matriarch who'd left federal witnesses floating in Biscayne Bay.

Lola massaged her temples. "Jesus Christ."

"Present," Jesus said from the sink.

She ignored him. "You borrowed from a narcotrafficker to do what? Buy camera gear?"

Dutch's silence was answer enough.

Mercy threw up her hands. "I'm out. You want to die stupid, do it off my property." She stormed into the kitchen.

The fan club took the hint. They scattered, leaving only Miguel, who seemed to be mentally composing his next tweet.

Lola grabbed Dutch's collar. "You're going to tell me everything. Now."

He glanced at the ceiling. "Uh. Might want to check that first."

She followed his gaze. Above the fire exit, a smoke detector blinked red. Except it wasn't a smoke detector—the lens was too wide, the casing too new.

Lola dragged a chair over, climbed up, and twisted the device. It came away too easily. Wires snaked into the ceiling.

"Son of a bitch." She jumped down, holding the bug aloft. "How long has this been here?"

Dutch shrugged. "Dunno. But Marco's not the surveillance type."

Meaning someone else had planted it. Someone professional.

Lola crushed the device underfoot. The pieces skittered across the linoleum.

Outside, an engine roared to life. Tires squealed. Marco was gone.

But the bug meant others were listening.

She turned to Dutch. "Start talking. Or I let Catalina collect her twenty grand in teeth."

Miguel's eyes widened. "This is way better than the dinosaur."

Jesus sighed and reached for another dish.

Dutch rubbed the back of his neck where Lola's grip had left sweat-damp wrinkles in his shirt collar. The broken surveillance device crunched under his sneaker as he shifted his weight.

Lola's Glock wasn't drawn, but her hand hovered near it. "No. You talk. You've been playing tourist with a cyborg dinosaur while cartel enforcers and black-ops spooks crawl up our asses. Start from the beginning."

Miguel inched closer, phone discreetly angled. Jesus picked up a fallen spoon. The metallic ping echoed like a gavel.

Dutch exhaled through his nose. "Fine. Yeah, I shot those photos at Eden World. Last November. Got tipped by a source at Homestead about weird shipments—construction materials to an abandoned theme park. Thought it was a drug thing. Turns out it was way worse." He kicked the bug fragments. "Military-grade worse."

Lola's eyebrow twitched. "Your source have a name?"

"Had." Dutch's throat tightened. "He's in a landfill now. Ask the guys who torched my apartment."

Jesus set the spoon down. "What did you see in the bunker?"

"They were building something. Not just Rex. A machine. Big, like a..." He mimed a dome with his hands. "Had this... crown thing in the center. Looked like barbed wire made of bone."

Lola went very still. "A Crown of Thorns?"

"How'd you—"

"Homestead memo mentioned Relic-01." Her voice was flat. "You photograph that too?"

Dutch's stomach dropped. "No. But I saw the schematics. They called it the Serpent Engine."

Miguel's phone clicked. Everyone turned. He froze mid-sneak. "Uh. Deleted?"

Lola snatched the phone and ejected the SIM card. She snapped it between her teeth. Miguel whimpered.

Jesus studied Dutch. "Why didn't you tell us?"

"Tell you what? That I accidentally documented a Pentagon black project before becoming the world's worst messiah publicist?" Dutch barked a laugh. "I was kinda hoping you'd miracle us out of this."

Lola tossed Miguel's phone into the sink. It landed with a plop in the dishwater. "You're an idiot."

"Agreed," Mercy said from the doorway. She held a shotgun. Not pointed at anyone. Not not pointed, either. "Now get the hell out of my kitchen."

Dutch spread his hands. "Sister, come on—"

"Don't 'Sister' me. You brought a cartel, a cop, and the Second Coming to my doorstep. My clients don't need that kind of heat." She jerked the barrel toward the back door. "Move."

Lola didn't budge. "That bug wasn't for him. It was for you."

Mercy's grip tightened. "Explain."

"Project LAZARUS funds this place. They've been watching you. Probably since before Dutch crashed your party." Lola nudged the bug fragments with her toe. "Question is, why?"

A silence settled. The fridge hummed. Somewhere in the common room, a chair scraped the linoleum.

Jesus picked up a dish towel. Folded it. Unfolded it. "They knew I'd come here."

Dutch blinked. "How?"

"The same way fishermen know where to cast their nets." Jesus draped the towel over the rack. "You look for the hungry."

Miguel edged toward the exit. "I'm gonna... not be here." He bolted.

Mercy lowered the shotgun an inch. "So what's the play?"

Lola checked her watch. "We're gone in ten. You don't see us leave. You never saw us at all."

"And the twenty grand?" Dutch winced.

"Pray for a miracle." Mercy snorted. "Or run faster than Marco."

Jesus reached into the soapy water and retrieved Miguel's phone. He shook it off, handed it to Lola. "We'll need this."

She stared. "It's fried."

"Fishermen," Jesus repeated. "And nets."

Dutch groaned. "I hate it when he does that."

Lola pocketed the phone. "Gear up. We're hitting Eden World."

Dutch's knees threatened to buckle. "Uh, hard pass? That's where the murder cyborgs live."

"Exactly." Lola headed for the door. "Nobody expects idiots to walk back into the furnace."

Jesus followed, then paused and turned to Mercy.

"Are my problem." She waved the shotgun. "Go be somebody else's."

Dutch hesitated. "You got any spare cash? For the road?"

Mercy's smile was beatific. "I've got twelve gauge and a direct line to God. You want to test which one's faster?"

He didn't.

Outside, the alley smelled of garbage and gasoline. Rex's tail thumped a greeting, scattering a trio of rats. The van's passenger door hung open, the interior light flickering like a bad omen.

Lola tossed Dutch the keys. "Drive."

He caught them, felt the weight. "Where?"

She climbed into the passenger seat. "Wherever the Serpent takes us."

Jesus slid the side door shut. The sound was final, like a tomb sealing.

Dutch turned the key. The engine coughed. Died. Coughed again.

Somewhere behind them, a car door slammed.

He didn't look back. The van sputtered to life.

The last thing he saw in the rearview was Mercy's silhouette in the kitchen window, spoon raised like a scepter. Then the alley swallowed them whole.

Under the seat, Miguel's phone buzzed. Once. Twice.

Lola fished it out. The screen was cracked, waterlogged—and glowing.

THE ENGINE HUNGERS.

The van hit a pothole. The phone died for good this time.

Dutch white-knuckled the wheel. "So. Eden World. Fun."

Jesus gazed out the window. The streetlights strobed across his face like a slideshow of bad ideas. "We'll need bait."

Lola cocked her head. "You volunteering?"

"No." He nodded at Dutch. "He is."

The van swerved. "Excuse me?"

Jesus smiled. It wasn't comforting. "Fisherman."

Lola cracked her window. The night air smelled of salt and exhaust. "And nets."

Dutch groaned. The engine whined.

Somewhere ahead, the Everglades waited.
