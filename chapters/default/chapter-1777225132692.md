Ananias pressed his thumb into the dough, feeling the give of barley flour and water under his fingers. The scent of yeast and olive oil clung to his hands, familiar as prayer. Outside, the morning bustle of Straight Street rose through the open window—donkeys braying, merchants haggling, children shrieking as they darted between carts. A normal day. A blessed day.

He rolled the dough between his palms, shaping it into a round loaf. The rhythm soothed him. Knead, fold, press. The Law in motion.

A shadow crossed the threshold. Elisheva stood there, her widow’s veil pushed back to reveal the sharp lines of her face. She held a basket of dried figs, her knuckles white around the handle.

"You heard," she said.

Ananias didn’t look up. The dough needed six more turns. "Heard what?"

"Saul of Tarsus is in the city."

His hands stilled. A fly landed on the edge of the kneading board, its wings glinting in the thin sunlight.

Elisheva set the basket down with a thud. "Judas the tanner took him in. Three days now, blind as a beggar. They say he came with letters from the high priest to drag us back to Jerusalem in chains."

Ananias wiped his hands on his tunic. The flour left pale streaks across the rough wool. "And yet he’s blind."

"Does that comfort you?" Her voice was a blade. "Nathaniel was whole when they took him."

"I know."

She exhaled through her nose, a sound like a bellows collapsing. "They’re saying a light struck him down on the road. That he heard a voice."

Ananias picked up the loaf, turning it over in his hands. The underside was smooth, unbroken. "What voice?"

"Does it matter?" Elisheva snatched a fig from the basket, crushed it between her fingers. The pulp oozed dark and sticky. "He’s here. And we both know what happens next."

The fly buzzed, circling the ruined fruit. Ananias watched it dip and rise.

"Pack your things," she said. "The others are leaving at dusk."

"No."

Elisheva froze. "What do you mean, no?"

Ananias set the loaf on the baking stone. "I mean I’m not going."

"You’ll die."

"Perhaps."

She grabbed his wrist, her fingers sticky with fig juice. "He killed Nathaniel. He’ll kill you. He’ll kill all of us."

Ananias looked at her hand on his arm. The red-brown stains looked like old blood. "Then why hasn’t he?"

Elisheva recoiled. "What?"

"Three days. Blind. Helpless." He met her gaze. "Where are the arrests? The beatings?"

"Don’t." She backed toward the door, shaking her head. "Don’t do that thing you do."

"What thing?"

"Making it sound like God’s hand is in everything. Even this." She wiped her hands on her robe, leaving smears. "Sometimes evil is just evil."

The fly settled on the loaf, tasting the unbaked dough. Ananias watched its proboscis dart in and out.

Elisheva turned to leave.

"Wait." He reached into the basket, pulled out two figs, pressed them into her palm. "For the road."

Her fingers closed around them. For a moment, she looked like she might say something else. Then she was gone, her sandals slapping against the stone street.

Ananias returned to his kneading. The dough had begun to crust at the edges. He sprinkled it with water, worked the moisture back in. The fly buzzed away, dissatisfied.

A breeze stirred the curtains. The scent of baking bread from a neighbor’s oven mingled with the stink of the tanneries downstream. Damascus breathed around him, indifferent.

He shaped the loaf again, slower now. The rhythm faltered. His hands remembered Nathaniel’s face—the way he’d laughed at his own jokes, the way he’d broken bread with such care, as if each piece might be his last.

The knife slipped.

Ananias hissed, sucking at the cut on his thumb. The blood welled bright, a single drop falling onto the dough. He stared at it. Red on white.

A voice spoke his name.

Not from the street. Not from the doorway.

From the air itself.

"Ananias."

The knife clattered to the floor. The room was too bright, the walls dissolving in a glare that seared his eyes. He staggered back, shielding his face.

"Here I am, Lord."

The light pulsed, a living thing. "Go to the house of Judas on Straight Street. Ask for Saul of Tarsus."

Ananias’s knees hit the floor. The blood from his thumb left a smudge on the tiles.

"He is praying." The voice cut through his protest like a hot wire. "In a vision, he has seen a man named Ananias place his hands on him to restore his sight."

The light pressed against his skin, his lungs. It filled his mouth with the taste of iron.

"Lord," Ananias whispered. "This man is—"

"My chosen instrument."

The words struck him like a blow. He doubled over, forehead touching the cool tile. The blood from his thumb pooled beneath him, a tiny mirror reflecting the impossible light.

Saul. The persecutor. The murderer.

Chosen.

Ananias squeezed his eyes shut. The light burned through his eyelids.

"Go."

The command was absolute.

When he opened his eyes, the room was dim again. The loaf sat on the stone, untouched. The knife lay where it had fallen.

Only the cut on his thumb proved it had been real.

Ananias stood. His legs held.

He picked up the knife. Wiped the blade clean.

Then he reached for his cloak and stepped out into the blinding sun.

Ananias clutched the doorframe, the sun hammering his shoulders. The street swayed—no, he swayed, his knees liquid, his sandals scraping against stone. A donkey brayed somewhere to his left, the sound too sharp, too close. He blinked against the glare. Straight Street stretched before him, dust rising in lazy spirals where sandals and hooves had stirred it.

He should turn left. Toward the tanneries. Toward Judas’s house.

His feet carried him right instead.

The alley swallowed him whole, its shade thick with the reek of urine and rotting melon rinds. He pressed his back against the wall, the rough plaster biting through his tunic. His breath came in short, useless gasps. The cut on his thumb throbbed.

The word lodged in his ribs like a splinter. He’d seen Saul’s work firsthand—the broken doors in Jerusalem, the families huddled in corners whispering about midnight arrests. He’d washed Nathaniel’s body, had counted the bruises, had seen how the stones had crushed the life from his friend’s lungs.

And now—A child darted past, chasing a wooden hoop. The clatter echoed off the walls. Ananias closed his eyes. The light still burned behind his eyelids, that terrible, living glare.

"Lord," he whispered. "You can’t mean this."

The alley offered no answer. A cat slunk along the opposite wall, its tail twitching.

He pushed off the wall, stumbled back into the sunlight. The crowd flowed around him, a current of bodies and barter. A woman bumped his shoulder, her basket of lentils jostling against his arm. She didn’t apologize.

Judas’s house stood near the tanneries, where the stench of curing hides drove down property values. Ananias knew the place—had delivered bread there last winter when Judas’s wife fell ill. He’d stood in that courtyard, had admired the fig tree growing stubbornly in the corner.

Now Saul of Tarsus sat beneath it. Blind. Praying.

Ananias’s stomach twisted. He ducked into a narrow passage between two shops, the shadows cooling his face. His hands shook. He pressed them flat against his thighs.

"Lord," he said again, the word raw. "He’s killed your people."

My chosen instrument.

The reply wasn’t audible. It wasn’t even words. It was a weight, a certainty settling over him like a cloak lined with lead.

Ananias sagged against the wall. A bead of sweat traced his temple.

He could refuse.

The thought slithered through him, dark and shameful. He could walk away. Could join the others on the road to Pella, could leave Saul to his blindness. God would find another way. God always found another way.

But the light had spoken his name. Had known him. Had seen him kneading dough, had seen the blood on the loaf.

Had called him anyway.

A fly landed on his wrist. He flicked it away.

The passage opened onto a smaller street, quieter, where the houses leaned together like conspirators. Ananias hesitated. Left would take him home. Right would take him to the tanneries.

He turned right.

The stench hit him first—a thick, greasy reek that coated the back of his throat. He breathed through his mouth. The street sloped downward, the stones slick with runoff. A pair of tanners hauled a soaked hide between them, their arms glistening with sweat and lye. They didn’t look up as he passed.

Judas’s house was the third on the left, its courtyard wall crumbling at the top. The gate stood ajar. Ananias paused, his fingers brushing the rough wood.

He could still turn back.

A voice drifted from within—murmured words, too low to parse. Saul’s voice.

Ananias’s pulse hammered in his ears. He pushed the gate open.

The courtyard was smaller than he remembered. The fig tree dominated the space, its leaves casting dappled shade over the packed earth. A man sat beneath it, his back against the trunk, his hands resting palm-up on his knees. His robes were travel-stained, his hair matted with dust.

Saul.

Ananias’s breath caught. The man’s eyes were open, but they didn’t track movement. They stared straight ahead, unblinking, milky with whatever the light had done to him.

The voice stopped mid-word. Saul tilted his head. "Who’s there?"

Ananias didn’t answer. His tongue stuck to the roof of his mouth.

Saul’s nostrils flared. "I smell bread."

A laugh bubbled in Ananias’s chest, wild and unmoored. He’d come straight from kneading. Of course he smelled of flour and yeast.

Saul’s hands twitched. "Ananias?"

The name, spoken in that ruined voice, sent a shock through him. He hadn’t announced himself. Hadn’t made a sound.

"You saw me," Ananias whispered. "In your vision."

Saul’s face twisted—something between a smile and a grimace. "You’ll restore my sight."

It wasn’t a question.

Ananias stepped forward. The sun beat down on his neck. "You know who I am."

"I know what I was." Saul’s fingers dug into his knees. "What I did."

"And what are you now?"

A pause. The fig leaves rustled.

"Lost," Saul said at last.

Ananias exhaled. The weight in his chest shifted, not lighter, but different. He crossed the courtyard in three strides, knelt before the man who had murdered his friends.

Saul flinched when Ananias’s shadow fell over him.

"Don’t." Ananias reached for Saul’s face. His hands shook. "Don’t move."

His thumbs brushed Saul’s eyelids. The skin was hot, papery. Saul shuddered but didn’t pull away.

Ananias closed his eyes. "Lord," he murmured.

The words caught. He thought of Nathaniel’s laugh. Of Elisheva’s white knuckles around the fig basket. Of the blood on the dough.

"Then let it be done."

A current surged through his palms—not heat, not light, but something older, something that tasted of creation itself. Saul gasped. His hands flew up, clutching Ananias’s wrists.

For a heartbeat, nothing.

Then Saul’s eyes cleared.

Ananias recoiled. The man’s gaze locked onto him, dark and fathomless, the pupils shrinking in the sunlight.

Saul’s lips parted. No sound came out.

A scale fell from his left eye, then his right. They landed in the dust like tiny coins.

Ananias stared at them. Fish scales. Thin, translucent, catching the light.

Saul blinked. Once. Twice. Then he began to weep.

The sound was unbearable. Ananias stood, backing away until his shoulders hit the courtyard wall. Saul curled forward, his forehead touching the earth, his shoulders heaving.

The fig tree rustled again. A leaf drifted down, landing in Saul’s hair.

Ananias turned. Pushed through the gate. Staggered into the street.

The sun was too bright. The air too thick. He pressed a hand to his mouth, his stomach churning.

Behind him, the weeping stopped.

Saul’s voice, raw but certain.

Ananias didn’t look back. He ran.

Ananias's fingers trembled as he tied his sandals. The leather straps felt foreign under his hands, as if he'd never fastened them before. A drop of sweat slid down his temple and splashed onto the worn reed mat.

No. He wouldn't think about it.

He stood too fast. The room tilted. His kneading bowl sat abandoned on the table, the ruined loaf still inside, the bloodstain darkening at the edges. He should clean it. Should finish the baking. Should do anything but what the voice had commanded.

The cloak hung by the door, rough-spun wool dyed the color of dust. He dragged it over his shoulders, the weight familiar and wrong all at once. This cloak had kept him warm through winter rains, had shaded him from summer suns. Now it would walk him to his death.

The street outside was a living thing—donkeys braying, merchants shouting, children shrieking as they darted between carts. Normal sounds. Human sounds. Ananias stood on his threshold, gripping the doorframe until his knuckles ached. Left would take him to the tanneries. To Judas's house. To Saul.

Right would take him to the eastern gate. To Pella. To safety.

His feet carried him left.

The crowd swallowed him. A spice merchant thrust a basket of cumin under his nose, the sharp scent pricking his eyes. A water seller's cry cut through the din—"Cool water! Sweet water!"—but Ananias's throat was too tight to answer. He kept his eyes on the stones, on the scuffed sandals of the man ahead of him, on anything but the faces streaming past.

Someone jostled his shoulder. He stumbled, his elbow knocking against a pottery stall. The vendor shot him a glare. Ananias mumbled an apology and pressed on. The sun climbed higher, hammering the back of his neck.

A child's laugh rang out, bright and sudden. Ananias flinched. The sound was too much like Nathaniel's boy, the one who'd clung to his mother's skirts at the funeral, too young to understand why his father wouldn't wake up.

The memory hooked into his ribs. He ducked into the shade of a fig seller's awning, pressing the heel of his hand to his sternum. Breathe. Just breathe.

The figs lay piled in wooden trays, their split skins glistening. A fat fly circled the fruit, its wings catching the light. Ananias stared at it. The same fly from his kitchen? No, impossible. Just another creature drawn to sweetness.

"Buy something or move along." The fig seller frowned at him, her hands on her hips.

Ananias reached for his purse. His fingers brushed a coin—enough for two figs, maybe three. He hesitated. Elisheva had taken the others.

"Nothing," he muttered. "Forgive me."

The woman snorted and turned to a more promising customer.

The alley to the tanneries gaped ahead, a dark mouth exhaling the stench of lye and rotting flesh. Ananias slowed. His sandals scuffed the stones. Every step forward was a betrayal—of Nathaniel, of Elisheva, of all the others who'd wept over Saul's victims.

The words coiled in his gut like a live coal. How? Why? The questions buzzed in his skull, but the light hadn't offered explanations. Only a command.

A hand gripped his elbow.

Ananias whirled, his heart slamming against his ribs. A woman stood there, her face lined with years and sun. One of the faithful—Miriam, who sold woven mats near the synagogue. Her lips moved, but the blood roaring in his ears drowned out the words.

He shook his head.

Miriam leaned closer. "They're saying Saul of Tarsus is blind." Her breath smelled of onions and fear. "That God struck him down."

Ananias pulled free. "I don't know."

"You're lying." Her fingers dug into his arm. "I saw your face when the news came. You know something."

The accusation hung between them. Ananias looked past her, to where the alley narrowed into shadow. "I have to go."

Miriam's grip tightened. "If you're going to him—"

"I have to go."

She released him as if burned. Her mouth worked, but no sound came out. Then she was gone, melting into the crowd, her gray head disappearing behind a camel's swaying load.

The stench of the tanneries thickened with each step. Ananias breathed through his mouth, the taste of bile sharp on his tongue. A pair of apprentices passed him, their arms slick with fat and lye, their laughter too loud. He didn't notice the man shaking in their midst.

Judas's house loomed ahead, its courtyard wall crumbling at the top. The gate stood ajar, just as he'd known it would. Ananias stopped three paces away. His knees threatened to buckle.

A murmur drifted through the open gate—low, rhythmic, the cadence of prayer. Saul's voice.

Ananias's hands curled into fists. The cut on his thumb burned. He could still turn back. Could still run to the eastern gate, could still catch up with Elisheva and the others. God would understand. God had to understand.

The prayer inside faltered. A sharp intake of breath. Then silence.

Ananias froze.

"Who's there?" Saul's voice, rough with disuse.

The question hung in the air between them. Ananias didn't move. Didn't breathe.

A dry chuckle from beyond the wall. "You smell of bread."

Saul's voice softened. "Ananias?"

A rustle of fabric from within. Saul's shadow shifted against the wall, tall and gaunt. "You'll restore my sight."

It wasn't a question.

"I know what I was." Saul's voice cracked. "What I did."

The word settled between them, heavy with confession. Ananias exhaled. The weight in his chest shifted, not lighter, but different. He pushed the gate open.

Ananias's breath caught.

Ananias crossed the courtyard in three strides. His shadow fell across Saul's face.

Saul flinched.

"Don't," Ananias said. His voice sounded strange to his own ears—hoarse, but steady. "Don't move."

Saul's breath hitched, but he didn't pull away.

Ananias's thumbs brushed Saul's eyelids. The skin was hot, papery. He closed his own eyes.

Then Saul's eyes cleared.
