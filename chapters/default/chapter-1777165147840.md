The notice appeared on a telephone pole near the San Leandro shipyard where Fred Korematsu worked. It was posted on April 1, 1942. Civilian Exclusion Order No. 34, issued by Lieutenant General John L. DeWitt of the Western Defense Command, commanded that “all persons of Japanese ancestry, both alien and non-alien,” be evacuated from the designated military area by noon on April 7.

Fred was twenty-three. Born in Oakland, he was a Nisei, a second-generation American citizen. His work as a welder was essential to the war effort, a fact noted in his draft classification. He had tried to enlist twice, only to be rejected for health reasons. He lived a quiet life, sharing an apartment with his brother. The order gave him six days to liquidate his existence.

The language of the notice was bureaucratic; its consequences were absolute. According to the San Leandro News-Observer, the local paper that reprinted the full text, the order permitted each evacuee to bring only what they could carry: bedding, toilet articles, extra clothing. No pets. No personal items of substantial size. Everything else—furniture, cars, businesses, tools—had to be sold, stored, or abandoned. The government offered no compensation for losses.

For Fred, the order was an unthinkable rupture. It severed him from his job, his home, and his identity as an American. In later testimony before a congressional commission, he described the moment as one of bone-deep dislocation. He was not an alien. He was a welder. He had a girlfriend, an Italian American woman named Ida Boitano. His life was here.

He made a choice. He would not go.

It was not a political decision, but a personal one. As he explained to researcher Peter Irons decades later, he simply could not accept the premise. He had done nothing wrong. He saw no reason why he should be imprisoned. The mechanics of defiance, however, required grim practicality. He could not simply remain in his apartment. His face, his name, would betray him.

He sought out a doctor in Oakland. For a fee, the doctor agreed to perform minor plastic surgery, altering the shape of Fred’s eyelids in an attempt to make him appear less identifiably Japanese. He changed his name. He became Clyde Sarah, claiming Spanish-Hawaiian heritage. He obtained a fake draft registration card. The surgery was botched, leaving his eyes swollen and painful. The alias was flimsy. He was a welder, not a spy. The tools of deception did not fit his hands.

On the morning of May 30, 1942, Fred Korematsu walked near the corner of East 14th Street and 164th Avenue in San Leandro. He was with Ida. A police officer, suspicious of the visibly injured young man, stopped them. Fred presented his forged documents. They did not hold. He was arrested for violating the exclusion order.

The arrest was not dramatic. It was a procedural footnote. Local police processed him and turned him over to military authorities. He was taken to the Presidio of San Francisco, where he was held in the military police stockade. He was, officially, a criminal. The charge was a felony, punishable by up to five years in federal prison.

In his cell, Fred was approached. The American Civil Liberties Union had been searching for a test case to challenge the constitutionality of the internment. They needed a citizen, someone with no ties to Japan, someone whose only crime was being in the wrong place with the wrong ancestry. Fred Korematsu, the welder who refused to leave, fit perfectly. A young ACLU attorney, Ernest Besig, visited him. Besig later recounted the meeting in his papers. He offered to take Fred’s case, to fight the charge all the way to the Supreme Court. But there was a condition: Fred would have to plead not guilty. He would have to become a defendant, a symbol.

Fred agreed. He was already in a cage. He had nothing left to lose but his name, and that had already been taken.

His rationale was not articulated in legal briefs or political manifestos. It was recorded in the quiet, persistent refusal of a man who believed his life was his own. In interviews conducted by historian Peter Irons in the 1980s, Korematsu’s reasoning emerged as a series of simple, unassailable facts. He was an American. He had a job important to the war. He was in love. The government’s order did not contradict these facts; it erased them. His resistance was an attempt to hold the pieces of his identity together.

The plan spoke to both his determination and his deep naivete. Changing his appearance seemed a logical, if drastic, step. The surgery, performed by a doctor in Oakland whose name is lost to the record, was minor. The goal was to alter the epicanthic fold of his eyelids, a feature he believed made him a target. In his mind, it was a practical adjustment, like changing his clothes. The procedure was painful and crude. According to Korematsu’s own account, it left his eyes swollen and infected for weeks, a visible mark of his transgression that ultimately drew more attention than it diverted.

His new identity was assembled from fragments. He chose the name Clyde Sarah, claiming Spanish-Hawaiian ancestry. He obtained a fake draft registration card. The alias was hollow, a costume that did not fit. He was a welder, accustomed to the real logic of steel and flame. A world of forged documents and invented biographies was foreign, and he moved through it clumsily. Every interaction became a performance. He could not go back to his shipyard job. He took work as a laborer, constantly fearing recognition. The strain was immense. He played a part written for a stranger, and he was a bad actor.

The futility of these measures became clear in the mundane details. His swollen eyes made him memorable. His knowledge of Spanish or Hawaiian culture was nonexistent. His forged papers would not withstand even casual scrutiny from an authority figure, as his arrest would prove. He was not disappearing into a new identity; he painted a target on his back. The extremity of his actions—the surgery, the alias—highlighted the impossibility of his situation. The state had defined him by a single, immutable characteristic. He could no more change his ancestry through surgery than he could wish away the order.

This personal desperation existed in stark contrast to the official portrait of the “Japanese problem” being promulgated by General DeWitt and the War Department. In public statements and a final report to the Secretary of War, DeWitt argued that the “racial characteristics” of the Japanese made assimilation impossible and loyalty suspect. “A Jap’s a Jap,” he famously said. The military necessity was presented as objective, a strategic calculation. Korematsu’s lived experience—his job, his love, his botched surgery—was the human rebuttal to that abstraction. It was not a strategic calculation. It was a man trying to go to work and come home to his girlfriend.

The psychological toll was documented in Korematsu’s later testimonies. The constant vigilance required to maintain his deception exhausted him. He described a state of perpetual anxiety, of scanning every street for police, of rehearsing his story before speaking to anyone. The freedom he sought was negated by the prison of his own fear. He was not living; he hid. The normalcy he craved—the thing he fought for—had become impossible the moment he decided to fight.

His arrest on May 30, 1942, was almost a relief. The performance was over. When Officer Edward Kelly of the San Leandro police stopped him and Ida, Korematsu fumbled with his Clyde Sarah identification. The documents, flimsy under inspection, collapsed. In his papers, ACLU attorney Ernest Besig noted that Korematsu offered no resistance. He was tired. The failed surgery, the failed alias, the failed attempt to simply be left alone—all of it culminated in a quiet submission on a street corner. The legal battle was about to begin, but his personal battle to evade the order was already lost. He had tried to solve a constitutional crisis with a fake ID and a painful operation. The mismatch was tragic, and it underscored the true nature of the government’s power: it could force a citizen to such desperate, hopeless ends.

The arrest report, filed by the San Leandro Police Department and preserved in the National Archives, is a spare document. It lists the time, location, and charge: violation of Civilian Exclusion Order No. 34. It notes the suspect was in the company of Ida Boitano. It does not describe the swollen eyes, the failed alias, or the heavy weariness that, according to Ernest Besig’s later accounts, hung on Fred Korematsu like a weight. The transaction was bureaucratic. Officer Edward Kelly, suspicious of the young man’s appearance, asked for identification. Korematsu presented the forged draft card for Clyde Sarah. The ruse lasted minutes. Kelly took him into custody without incident.

Korematsu was processed at the local jail, then transferred to the military police stockade at the Presidio of San Francisco. His cell was in a converted stable, cold and damp. He was now a prisoner of the U.S. Army, charged with a felony for the crime of remaining in his own city. In this grim space, the abstract injustice of the exclusion order became a physical reality: a locked door, a uniformed guard, a number. The government’s theory of military necessity had culminated in a welder from Oakland sitting on a cot in a former horse stall.

The American Civil Liberties Union had been monitoring such arrests. Its northern California branch, led by director Ernest Besig, was actively seeking a plaintiff for a test case. The ideal candidate was a native-born citizen with no record of disloyalty, someone whose prosecution rested solely on the racial classification of the exclusion orders. When Besig learned of Korematsu’s arrest, he saw the potential. He secured permission from national ACLU leadership to intervene and arranged a visit to the Presidio stockade.

The meeting between the attorney and the prisoner, detailed in Besig’s personal papers and subsequent interviews, was not a moment of soaring rhetoric. Besig found Korematsu despondent. The young man did not see himself as a constitutional pioneer. He was embarrassed, frightened, and isolated. Besig explained the legal strategy. He would challenge the constitutionality of the exclusion order itself. If Korematsu agreed to plead not guilty and fight the charge, the ACLU would provide his defense all the way to the Supreme Court. The case could overturn the internment.

Korematsu was hesitant. As historian Peter Irons documented from their conversations decades later, the prospect of a public trial terrified him. It would expose his relationship with Ida, his failed surgery, his forged documents. It would make him a permanent symbol of defiance within a Japanese American community that was, by and large, complying with the government’s orders in hopes of proving its loyalty. A guilty plea might mean a shorter sentence. A not guilty plea guaranteed a long, brutal legal battle with the full power of the War Department arrayed against him.

Besig was direct. He could not promise victory. The courts, in the fevered climate of war, were likely to defer to the military. But the principle had to be challenged. The choice, Besig emphasized, was Korematsu’s alone. He could accept the felony conviction and serve his time, or he could allow his personal resistance to be transformed into a national test of the Constitution.

Korematsu agreed. His reasoning, as he recalled it to Irons, was characteristically straightforward. He was already in jail. He had already lost his job, his home, his freedom. Submitting quietly had not spared him. If fighting offered even a slim chance of proving he was right, of reclaiming the country that had disowned him, then he would fight. He authorized Besig to take the case. With that decision, Fred Korematsu the evader ceased to exist. Fred Korematsu the defendant was born.

The legal machinery engaged with swift efficiency. Besig filed a habeas corpus petition, arguing Korematsu’s detention was unlawful. The government responded by moving Korematsu from the military stockade into the civilian internment system. He was transported under guard to the Tanforan Assembly Center, a temporary detention facility built on a racetrack in San Bruno. The irony was stark. By challenging his exclusion, he had been placed inside the system he challenged. He was now behind barbed wire, surrounded by thousands of other Japanese Americans, while his attorneys fought in courtrooms to free him.

Life at Tanforan, as described in the copious diaries and letters of detainees, was a lesson in institutional surrealism. Korematsu’s family, who had complied with the evacuation order, was already there. They were assigned to a horse stall, whitewashed but still smelling of manure. Fred was brought to a different barracks. The center was a crowded, makeshift city of tarpaper shacks, with armed guards in watchtowers. His defiance had not spared him the indignities of internment; it had merely added the stigma of criminal defendant to his status.

Within the camp, his notoriety was a complicated burden. Some internees saw him as a hero willing to stand up where they felt they could not. Others viewed him with suspicion or outright hostility, fearing his legal challenge would provoke further retaliation from the authorities and worsen conditions for everyone. The War Relocation Authority, the civilian agency managing the camps, monitored him. He was a problem within the problem. His personal act of refusal had collided with the massive, bureaucratic engine of mass incarceration, and he was now caught in its gears, a single man challenging a system designed to process 120,000 people as a single, suspect category.

His case proceeded alongside this grim new reality. In federal district court in San Francisco, Judge Adolphus F. St. Saviour heard arguments. Besig contended the exclusion order was a blatant racial violation of the Fifth Amendment. The government’s attorney, presented with what historical legal analyses describe as a “principle of racial guilt,” argued that the military’s judgment in time of war was unreviewable. The court had to trust General DeWitt’s assessment of danger. On September 8, 1942, Judge St. Saviour ruled. He found Korematsu guilty. The exclusion orders, he declared from the bench, were a valid exercise of the war power. He sentenced Korematsu to five years’ probation and remanded him into the custody of the War Relocation Authority—effectively, back to the internment camp.

The conviction was expected. The strategy had always been to build a record for appeal. Besig immediately filed. The case moved to the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals, where it was held in abeyance while the Supreme Court considered other challenges to the internment. Fred Korematsu waited. His life was reduced to a legal file number and an internment serial number. He was transferred from Tanforan to the Central Utah Relocation Center, a permanent camp in the desert at Topaz. There, amidst the dust and the isolation, the welder from Oakland became a plaintiff in history, waiting for nine men in Washington to decide if his existence constituted a military threat.

The Supreme Court heard arguments in Korematsu v. United States in October 1944. The war’s tide had turned. American forces advanced across the Pacific. The military justification for the exclusion zones—a potential Japanese invasion of the West Coast—had evaporated. The court, however, was not adjudicating current military strategy. It was reviewing the legality of actions taken in the panicked spring of 1942. The government’s case, as documented in the legal briefs and transcripts, rested on a now-familiar pillar: in times of grave peril, the executive branch must be granted wide latitude. To second-guess the military’s judgment in wartime, the Solicitor General argued, would be to tie the nation’s hands in the face of the enemy.

On December 18, 1944, the Court delivered its 6-3 decision. Justice Hugo Black, a former New Deal liberal, wrote the majority opinion. The language swept in its deference. “Pressing public necessity,” Black wrote, “may sometimes justify the existence of such restrictions; racial antagonism never can.” Having established this principle, the opinion proceeded to find that pressing public necessity was precisely what had driven General DeWitt’s orders. The Court accepted the government’s assertion, later revealed to be knowingly false, that there was evidence of espionage and sabotage among Japanese Americans that justified the wholesale exclusion. Black’s opinion conceded the racial nature of the order but argued that in this singular instance, racism was not its motivator—military urgency was. “Korematsu was not excluded from the Military Area because of hostility to him or his race,” Black wrote. “He was excluded because we are at war with the Japanese Empire… because the properly constituted military authorities feared an invasion of our West Coast.”

The legal sanction was complete. The Court had placed its stamp on a policy of mass incarceration based on ancestry. In his ferocious dissent, Justice Frank Murphy condemned the majority’s reasoning as a legalization of racism, falling “into the ugly abyss of racism.” Justice Robert H. Jackson warned of the precedent’s danger: “The principle then lies about like a loaded weapon ready for the hand of any authority that can bring forward a plausible claim of an urgent need.” For Fred Korematsu, the decision was a final, crushing verdict. He was a criminal in the eyes of the law. The constitutional system to which he had appealed for vindication had ratified his exclusion.

The human consequence of the ruling, however, was already being overtaken by events. On the same day the Korematsu decision was announced, the War Department publicly stated it would end the mass exclusion orders. The internment camps began a slow process of closure. The ruling was a backward-looking justification, not a forward-looking mandate. For the 120,000 internees, freedom was restored not by the Court’s wisdom, but by the war’s progress and gathering political shame. Korematsu’s legal loss was absolute, but its practical effect on the incarceration policy was negligible. A system had already served its purpose.

The personal fallout for Korematsu was severe. With his felony conviction upheld, he was stigmatized. He struggled to find work. His relationship with Ida Boitano did not survive the strain of the case and his subsequent relocation to Utah. He moved to Detroit, then to Salt Lake City, living quietly and avoiding attention. For decades, he carried the conviction like a shadow. He spoke rarely of the case, even to his children. The legal strategy that had transformed his personal defiance into a national symbol had also cemented his identity, in public records and his own mind, as a convicted criminal. The symbol had consumed the man.

This transformation—from a welder making a private choice to a defendant embodying a public principle—illustrates the brutal mechanics of constitutional tests. The state’s emergency power operates on a mass scale, dealing in categories and probabilities. The legal challenge to that power requires a singular human vessel. Korematsu became that vessel not through seeking greatness, but through refusing to accept an absurdity. His value to the ACLU’s strategy was his utter ordinariness. He was not a politician, an activist, or a philosopher. He was a man who wanted to go to work and come home. That normalcy made his case a purer test of the government’s claim: if the state could incarcerate this man, under these circumstances, then its power was essentially limitless.

His journey also reveals the isolating cost of such a role. The Japanese American community’s response to his defiance was, as historical studies by scholars like Roger Daniels have shown, deeply ambivalent. The mainstream community leadership, groups like the Japanese American Citizens League, initially advocated a strategy of cooperation and demonstrated loyalty. Open defiance, they feared, would confirm suspicions of disloyalty and bring worse reprisals. Korematsu’s stand, while admired by some, was seen by others as a dangerous complication. His legal battle was not a communal project; it was a lonely one. He stood against the government, but also somewhat apart from the community he was, in part, defending. The state’s power had successfully splintered its victims, making unified resistance exceptionally difficult.

The Korematsu precedent did indeed lie about like a loaded weapon, as Justice Jackson feared. For nearly forty years, it remained a valid, if odious, part of constitutional law. It was cited cautiously, often distinguished on its facts, but never explicitly overturned. Its enduring power was as a symbol of judicial capitulation. It stood for the proposition that in a true crisis, the Constitution’s guarantees could be set aside if the executive invoked national security with sufficient conviction.

This weapon remained holstered until the 1980s, when a historian, not a lawyer, finally unloaded it. Peter Irons, while researching the internment, obtained from archival collections evidence that government lawyers had suppressed and altered evidence in the 1944 case. The FBI and the Office of Naval Intelligence had concluded there was no evidence of espionage or sabotage among Japanese Americans, and these reports had been knowingly withheld from the Supreme Court. This was not a scholarly disagreement; it was prosecutorial fraud. Irons located Korematsu, now a retired draftsman living in anonymity, and presented his findings. With a team of pro bono attorneys, they filed a writ of coram nobis—a rare legal motion to overturn a conviction based on fundamental injustice.

In a federal courtroom in San Francisco in 1983, the government’s case collapsed. The Department of Justice, faced with irrefutable evidence of its predecessors’ misconduct, joined Korematsu’s legal team in asking the court to vacate the conviction. Judge Marilyn Hall Patel granted the motion. In her ruling, she stated the Korematsu decision remained “a caution that in times of distress the shield of military necessity and national security must not be used to protect governmental actions from close scrutiny and accountability.” The legal stain on Korematsu’s name was erased. The Supreme Court’s 1944 ruling, however, still stood in the law books.

The significance of Fred Korematsu’s defiance is thus twofold, existing in separate areas. In the domain of legal precedent, his case was a victory for state power, a marker of how far the constitutional guardrails could bend under the pressure of fear. It demonstrated the judiciary’s complete reluctance to check executive action during a perceived emergency, even when that action was blatantly discriminatory. The precedent’s value to future presidents was as a warning to courts, not a warning to executives.

In the space of human and historical conscience, however, his story became something else entirely. His vacated conviction, and his decades-long journey to clear his name, transformed the Korematsu case from a symbol of judicial failure into a symbol of the possibility of redress. The welder’s initial, instinctive “no” ultimately spawned a historical and legal reckoning that formally repudiated the government’s wartime lies. His persistence provided the human face that allowed later generations to see the internment not as an abstract “military necessity,” but as a specific injustice done to specific people. He reclaimed his identity, and in doing so, helped the nation reclaim a measure of its own.

The tension between these two worlds—the enduring bad precedent and the powerful good memory—is the central paradox of his legacy. The legal loaded weapon remains in the armory, cited occasionally in dissents and law review articles as the ultimate example of a misstep. Yet the story of the man himself, the welder who refused to leave, serves as its most effective safety catch. To invoke Korematsu as justification for a new emergency measure is to invoke not a legal citation, but a portrait: a young man with swollen eyes, holding a fake ID, waiting wearily for a police officer to finish reading it. The state’s abstract power is countered, in the end, by the indelible concreteness of a single human choice.
