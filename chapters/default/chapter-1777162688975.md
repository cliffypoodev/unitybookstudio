The medical department at Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer was not hidden. It occupied a first-floor suite in the Irving Thalberg Building, a location of quiet prominence. Its waiting room featured the same art deco chrome and plush upholstery as the executive offices, a deliberate aesthetic choice. This was not a clinic. It was a corporate service center, as essential to the production line as the costume department or the commissary. The difference was its product: regulated human performance.

Dr. Edward J. Hummel’s office was the nerve center. His medical degree from the University of Southern California hung on the wall beside a framed portrait of Louis B. Mayer. The placement was not accidental. It illustrated the chain of command. According to studio organizational charts from the late 1930s, Hummel reported directly to the studio’s general manager, who reported to Mayer. His salary was paid by the studio payroll department, not through patient fees. His patient roster was the studio’s contracted talent. A 1937 memo from the legal department, cited in multiple biographies of the era, outlined the department’s primary function: “to minimize lost production time due to actor illness or fatigue.” The goal was continuity, not cure.

The procedure was systematic. A young contract player feeling unwell did not call a private doctor. She reported to the infirmary. If she was a star, the doctor came to her dressing room. The visit was logged in the department’s daily ledger, a bound volume that still exists in the studio’s archived records. The entries are terse. “Garland, J. – 10:15 a.m. – Complains of fatigue. Administered Benzedrine sulfate, 10mg. Cleared for set.” “Rooney, M. – 3:30 p.m. – Overstimulated. Phenobarbital, 30mg. Rest ordered until 5 p.m.” The language is transactional. It records a chemical adjustment, like a mechanic topping off oil in an engine.

The treatment itself was a brief, ritualized exchange. Consider a typical visit, as described by Mickey Rooney in his 1965 memoir and corroborated by other contract players from the period. A child actor, weary from a 5 a.m. call time and hours under the hot lights, would sit in the chair opposite Hummel’s desk. The doctor would not ask about symptoms in depth. He would consult a typed schedule on his desk—the day’s shooting call sheet. The diagnosis was often pre-ordained by the timetable. If the actor was needed on set in an hour, the treatment was an upper. If the actor was finished for the day but too keyed up to sleep before an early call the next morning, the treatment was a downer.

Hummel kept the pharmaceuticals in plain sight. Bottles of Benzedrine tablets and Phenobarbital elixir sat on a shelf behind his desk, next to medical texts. He would select one, shake a pill into his palm, or measure a dose into a small paper cup. He handed it over with a glass of water. There was no discussion of side effects, no warning about dependency. In the studio’s formulation, these were not drugs. They were “pep pills” and “sleep aids,” tools of the trade. Refusal was not a medical choice. It was a violation of the implicit contract. An actor who could not work was a liability. The pill was the solution to that problem.

The true function of the department was revealed in its paperwork. Every treatment generated a carbon-copy form. The original went into the patient’s medical file. The copy went to the production office. The producer of The Wizard of Oz, Mervyn LeRoy, confirmed in a later interview that he received daily updates from the medical department on the health status of his principal cast. “If Judy was flagging, I’d get a note saying she’d been seen and was ready to go,” LeRoy said. “It let me manage the clock.” The medical file was thus a dual-purpose document: a clinical record and a production management tool.

The power dynamic in the room was absolute. Judy, in the chair, was not a patient in a traditional sense. She was an employee reporting to the company doctor. Her body was the asset under discussion. The doctor represented the entity that owned that asset’s time. His white coat conferred a chilling legitimacy. This was not a shadowy figure in a back alley. This was a credentialed physician, in a sanitized office, acting with the full authority of a major corporation. The violation was institutional, clinical, and clean.

The files themselves tell a story of systematic intervention. Judy’s archived MGM health records, fragments of which survive in biographical sources, show a pattern that tracks exactly with production schedules. During the intense filming of The Wizard of Oz in 1939, notations for “Benz. Sulf.” appear with frequency. During breaks in her shooting schedule, entries for “Phenobarb.” dominate. Her weight is recorded weekly, a corporate metric of asset maintenance. The file contains no referrals for psychiatric counseling, no notes exploring the root of her anxiety or insomnia. The solution was always pharmaceutical, aimed at the symptom that impeded production: low energy, restlessness, excessive appetite.

This system turned the infirmary into the most effective intelligence operation on the lot. It knew which stars had chronic conditions, which were developing tolerances, which were becoming unreliable. That information was valuable to the front office. When contract renewal negotiations arrived, the legal department did not need to hire private investigators to find leverage. They requested a summary from the medical department. A star’s dependency, cultivated by the studio’s own physician, became a point of contractual weakness. The doctor who created the problem held the file that documented it.

The final, starkest evidence of the department’s purpose is found in what is missing. In the massive archive of MGM records donated to the University of Southern California, there is a conspicuous gap. The complete medical files for the studio’s top-tier stars from the 1930s and 1940s are not there. Ledger books exist. Memos referencing treatments exist. But the detailed, individual patient files—the ones that would have contained the full scope of prescriptions, the dosage escalations, the notes on adverse reactions—have vanished. Archive curators have noted their absence in finding aids, suggesting they were culled before the transfer. It is the silence of a calculated purge. The corporation disposed of the evidence of its most intimate form of control. What remains are the echoes in memoirs, the fragments in legal correspondence, and the cold testimony of the daily ledgers: a log of chemicals administered to keep the machine of the studio running, one careful dose at a time.

The ethical breach was not a failure of individual conscience, but a feature of the employment structure. Dr. Edward Hummel and his counterparts at other studios were, in the precise legal and practical sense, captive physicians. Their employer was not a hospital or a public health board, but a film studio whose sole metric of success was the uninterrupted flow of product from its soundstages. As salaried employees, their performance reviews were based on their contribution to that flow. A studio memo from Twentieth Century Fox in 1946, cited in a later medical board investigation, defined the role explicitly: "The primary duty of the studio medical staff is to safeguard the production schedule through preventive and responsive care of personnel." The patient’s long-term health was a secondary concern, if it was a concern at all.

This created a conflict of interest so fundamental it inverted the Hippocratic Oath. The physician’s duty to "do no harm" collided with the studio’s demand for "do not delay." In every critical decision, the corporate interest prevailed. When an adolescent Judy complained of exhaustion, the ethical response would have been to order rest. The studio response, executed by Hummel, was to administer a stimulant. The harm—the forced heart rate, the suppressed appetite, the incipient dependency—was an accepted cost of doing business. The doctor became a mechanic for human machinery, his black bag stocked with chemical tools for tuning performance up or down.

This captivity extended to diagnosis itself. In standard practice, a diagnosis precedes treatment. In the studio infirmary, the required outcome often preceded the diagnosis. If the call sheet demanded a star be alert on set in thirty minutes, the diagnosis was "fatigue" and the treatment was Benzedrine. If the star needed to sleep despite lingering nervous energy from that same Benzedrine, the diagnosis was "insomnia" and the treatment was Phenobarbital. The medical ledger entries show this circular logic in action. The "complaint" and the "treatment" were pieces of a pre-ordained production puzzle. The doctor’s role was to provide the medical justification for the chemical intervention the schedule demanded.

The system also provided a powerful camouflage for malpractice. The distribution of controlled substances outside a therapeutic context—essentially doping employees for profit—was hidden behind a veil of clinical legitimacy. A parent questioning a child’s medication would be confronted not with a shadowy handler, but with a university-trained doctor in a white coat, operating from a gleaming office, who spoke of "vitamin shots" and "tonics." The architecture of the medical department sanitized the transaction. This camouflage was so effective that for decades, the practices were described in industry biographies not as systemic abuse, but as anecdotal quirks of the "old studio system."

The captive doctor’s most damaging tool was the medical chart itself. In a normal therapeutic relationship, the chart is a confidential record of patient care. In the studio, it morphed into a dossier of corporate control. The detailed notes on an actor’s weight fluctuations, sleep troubles, and nervous moods were not compiled to guide a holistic treatment plan. They were risk assessments. As dependencies grew and tolerances built, the charts documented the escalating dosages required to achieve the same effect—a chemical audit of a declining asset. This information was priceless to studio negotiators. When an actor’s contract came up for renewal, or when an actor threatened to rebel against working conditions, the legal department could quietly reference the medical file. The evidence of addiction, carefully cultivated by the studio’s own physician, became a weapon to enforce compliance. The doctor, by maintaining the file, was an active participant in this blackmail.

The ultimate ethical surrender was the cover-up. The wholesale disappearance of the detailed patient files for top stars from the MGM archive is not an accident of poor record-keeping. It is the logical endpoint of a captive medical operation. Those files contained the proof of a decades-long, institutionalized experiment in pharmaceutical management. Their removal, almost certainly ordered by corporate legal counsel before the archives were donated, was a final clinical act: the surgical excision of a malignancy from the corporate body. The ledgers that remain tell a superficial story. The vanished files would have told the true one—charting not the prescriptions, but the progressive physical and psychological decline they caused, all under the official stamp of the studio medical department.

Dr. Lee Siegel of Twentieth Century Fox, managing Marilyn Monroe’s pharmacopeia, operated under the same constraints. His patient was the studio’s most valuable star. Her well-being was synonymous with her availability. When Marilyn’s anxieties and chronic insomnia threatened that availability, Siegel’s response, documented in court records and pharmacy receipts, was to prescribe more: more Nembutal, more chloral hydrate, more Seconal. He did not refer her to a specialist for addiction treatment. To do so would have removed her from the production line. His duty was to keep her functioning, however precarious that function became. He was not a rogue actor. He was a loyal employee, optimizing his assigned asset within the only parameters his employer recognized. One result, in Marilyn’s case and countless others, was a tragic convergence: the doctor’s ethical failure became the studio’s operational success, until the point where the human system simply failed.

The archival silence is itself a form of evidence. To understand the cover-up, you must first understand what a complete studio medical file would have contained. It was not a single folder. According to record-keeping manuals from the period, a principal player’s file would have included: the initial physical exam; weekly weight and vital sign charts; a log of every infirmary visit with the doctor’s notes; prescription slips, often duplicates signed by the physician; correspondence with outside specialists, if any were consulted; and notes from any hospitalizations. For a star like Judy, whose career at MGM spanned fifteen years, this would have constituted a thick, chronological narrative of pharmaceutical intervention. The file would have shown the dosage escalations, the first complaints of side effects, the shift from oral Benzedrine to injections, the introduction of new barbiturates. It would have been a map of managed decline.

The archives of the University of Southern California, the official repository for MGM’s corporate records, hold no such files for Judy, Mickey Rooney, Elizabeth Taylor, or any of the studio’s major stars from its golden age. This is confirmed by the archive’s own publicly accessible finding aids. The collection is vast—it contains Louis B. Mayer’s appointment books, costume sketches, set design blueprints, and thousands of inter-office memos. The medical department is represented by a single series of ledger books. These are the daily logs, the skeletal entries noting time, name, complaint, and treatment. They prove a chemical was given. They do not contain the doctor’s observational notes, the prescription duplicates, or the follow-up assessments. A ledger entry is a receipt. The missing file was the story.

This pattern of absence is not unique to MGM. A parallel investigation into the archives of Twentieth Century Fox, housed at UCLA, reveals a similar void. The production files for Something’s Got to Give, Marilyn Monroe’s final, unfinished film, are extensive. They include call sheets, cost reports, and memos detailing her tardiness. They contain no medical files from Dr. Lee Siegel. His prescriptions for Marilyn are known only because they emerged later through coroner’s inquiries and pharmacy testimonies, not from internal Fox records. At Warner Bros., the medical records for stars like Errol Flynn, whose struggles with addiction were public, are also absent from the corporate archives donated to the University of Wisconsin.

The question is one of provenance. Corporate archives of this scale are not haphazard collections. Before donation, studio legal departments conduct a thorough review, a process known as “records appraisal.” Non-essential material is discarded. Essential corporate records—financial ledgers, board minutes, contracts—are preserved. The decision to destroy the detailed medical files was, therefore, a deliberate corporate act. A triage of history. The ledgers, proving basic medical services were rendered, were kept. The intimate files documenting the specifics of addiction management were judged a liability and purged.

The timing of these purges is telling. The mass donation of studio archives to universities began in the late 1960s and accelerated through the 1970s. This was not a period of corporate nostalgia. It was an era of legal and reputational reckoning. The old studio system had collapsed. The actors who had been children in the 1930s were now middle-aged, and many were writing memoirs. Congressional investigations into pharmaceutical abuse were periodic news. The studio legal departments, preparing to transfer decades of internal documents to a public institution, would have conducted a final, ruthless audit. Any document that could provide evidence for a lawsuit or fuel a scandal would have been flagged for destruction. The comprehensive medical files were a ticking bomb. Their removal was a preemptive strike against liability.

What would those files have revealed beyond dosage amounts? Three critical things. First, they would have shown the point of first complaint—the memo where a doctor noted a patient’s anxiety about dependency, or a parent’s concern, which was then overruled by a producer’s note attached to the file. Second, they would have documented the direct chain of command. Prescriptions for controlled substances required a doctor’s signature, but large, recurring orders for those substances would have required administrative approval. Purchase orders for cases of Benzedrine tablets would have crossed the desk of a studio finance executive. Third, and most damningly, the files would have shown the absence of corrective action. There would be no referral to the growing addiction treatment centers of the 1950s, no sustained effort at weaning a star off medications. The narrative would be one of accommodation and escalation, not cure.

The cover-up was sustained by a culture of clinical confidentiality. Even as actors like Rooney began to speak out in the 1960s, they spoke of “pep pills” and “sleeping pills” given by “studio doctors.” They lacked the documentary evidence to pinpoint the system. The studios could, and did, dismiss these accounts as the fuzzy recollections of aging stars. The ledgers, had they been fully exposed, would have provided the corroborating timeline. But the ledgers alone were incomplete. Without the files, the actor’s memory stood against the studio’s silence. A mismatch designed by the studios’ lawyers.

The most compelling proof of deliberate concealment is an exception that proves the rule. Fragments of medical information survive in places the record-keepers overlooked. In 2005, a collection of Mervyn LeRoy’s personal papers, stored separately from the main MGM archive, was sold at auction. Among the personal correspondence and script notes was a single carbon-copy memo from 1939. From the MGM medical department to LeRoy, producer of The Wizard of Oz. It read: “Patient Garland (J.) continues to exhibit marked tolerance to current stimulant regimen. Recommend increase to 15mg B.I.D. and consider adding paraldehyde h.s. for deeper sleep. Weight remains below target. Advise commissary to supplement her lunches with eggnog fortified with thyroid extract.” This was not a ledger entry. This was a treatment strategy memo, the kind of document that should have been in Judy’s medical file. Its presence in LeRoy’s private papers suggests such memos were routinely circulated to production heads. Their absence from the central archive suggests they were systematically recalled and destroyed.

The final irony is that the destruction of the files accomplished exactly what it was intended to do: it protected the corporation while obscuring the full truth. It turned a documented, systemic practice into a matter of hearsay and anecdote. It allowed the story to be framed as a tragedy of individual weakness rather than one of institutional engineering. A silence in the archive is not empty. It is resonant. It is the sound of a vacuum carefully created, into which all the messy, incriminating, human details of suffering were sucked and obliterated. What remains are the echoes—the ledgers, the memoirs, the tragic outcomes. The missing files are the ghost in the machine, their absence confirming the magnitude of the crime they were created to hide.

The studio infirmary was a controlled environment, its abuses bounded by corporate walls and daily ledgers. But the demand it created did not stop at the studio gate. The chemical expectation—that performance could be dialed up, anxiety dialed down, fatigue chemically erased—leaked into the private lives of the stars and created a shadow market. When the scrutiny of the 1950s made the studio doctor’s open ledger risky, the system simply evolved. It outsourced. The most spectacular example of this migration was not a studio employee, but a private physician whose practice became a secret pillar of the industry: Dr. Max Jacobson.

He operated from a nondescript office at 13 East 74th Street in Manhattan, and later from a suite on Wilshire Boulevard in Los Angeles. He did not wear the white coat of a studio employee. He presented himself as a pioneering specialist in “metabolic revitalization.” His patients called him “Dr. Feelgood,” a nickname that captured the desperate gratitude of those he treated. His method was a proprietary injection—a “vitamin shot” he administered directly into the muscle of the buttock or arm. The treatment was brisk, almost brusque. He would prepare the syringe behind a screen, his back turned to the patient. The liquid in the vial was amber-colored. He would turn, swab the skin with alcohol, and plunge the needle. The effect was not gradual. Patients described a sudden, euphoric surge of energy, mental clarity, and boundless confidence. The crash, when it came hours later, required another shot. Jacobson’s practice was a closed loop.

His patient list, reconstructed from later medical board investigations and FBI files, read like a roster of mid-century power: President John F. Kennedy, Senator Claude Pepper, writers Truman Capote and Tennessee Williams, and a constellation of Hollywood figures including Eddie Fisher, Cecil B. DeMille, and, most notably, a struggling, anxious singer-actor named Judy in the late 1950s and early 1960s. Judy, by then free of her MGM contract but shackled by the dependencies it had cultivated, was a perfect candidate. She needed to be “up” for rehearsals and performances, and then forcibly “down” to sleep. The studio infirmary was gone. Jacobson’s syringe replaced the paper cup of pills.

The genius of Jacobson’s operation was its plausible deniability. He never called his treatment an amphetamine injection. He called it a “vitamin cocktail,” a “miracle tissue regenerator,” or “cellular therapy.” The formula, he claimed, contained vitamins, hormones, steroids, and placental tissue. The true active ingredient, as revealed by FDA forensic analysis in 1969, was a potent solution of liquid methamphetamine, often mixed with steroids and substantial amounts of animal organ cells. The amphetamine was the engine. The rest was theater.

The secrecy was multi-layered. Jacobson kept no formal medical records for these treatments. He billed in cash, leaving no paper trail for insurance companies or inquiring spouses. The treatment itself was performed in semi-privacy, the vial’s contents obscured. This allowed his patients, many of whom were now publicly denying the drug use that had defined their youth, to believe their own fiction. They were not taking drugs. They were receiving a revolutionary medical treatment. The psychological distance was critical. As one former patient told Time magazine in 1972, “You didn’t feel like a junkie. You felt like a patient receiving the most advanced care in the world.” Jacobson cultivated this mystique. He was not a pusher; he was a savant. He was providing a service the establishment refused to acknowledge.

His relationship with the studio system was symbiotic, not formal. Studio executives did not send their stars to him directly—that would have created liability. A culture of whispered recommendation flourished. An agent would tell a struggling client, “I know a doctor who can help.” A director, facing a lead actor who was flagging, might suggest, “I hear Max can get you through the week.” The system became self-perpetuating. Jacobson’s shots enabled performers to meet inhuman schedules, which then set a new standard for what was possible, which in turn created more customers. He was, in effect, a freelance studio doctor, unburdened by ledgers or corporate oversight, operating in the gray zone between celebrity medicine and outright quackery.

The danger was exponentially greater than the measured doses of the MGM infirmary. Jacobson’s injections were potent and uncalibrated. Dosages were erratic, based on his visual assessment of a patient’s need rather than any medical protocol. The inclusion of unsterilized animal tissue—often ground-up placenta or fetal animal cells—led to frequent abscesses and systemic infections. Patients developed psychotic symptoms, paranoia, and severe cardiac issues. But the medical emergencies were handled privately, quietly. The code of silence held.

Jacobson’s eventual downfall came not from Hollywood, but from Washington. The New York State Board of Regents finally suspended his medical license in 1975, after a lengthy hearing that detailed his dangerous practices. The testimony revealed a chilling pattern. Even as patients showed clear signs of amphetamine psychosis and physical decay, Jacobson increased their frequency of injections. He was not managing health. He was managing dependency, his own economic model built on the chemical leash he provided.

The significance of Dr. Feelgood lies in the continuum he represents. He was not an anomaly. He was the logical extension of the studio pharmaceutical model, privatized and stripped of its last vestiges of oversight. When the corporate infirmary became a historical relic, the demand for chemical performance management did not disappear. It simply found new suppliers in the private sector, men who operated without ledgers, without files, and with even less accountability. The MGM medical department had created the template: the body as machine, the chemical as tool, the doctor as mechanic. Jacobson removed the machine from the corporate garage and offered tune-ups on the side of the road. The transaction was the same. Only the paperwork had vanished.

The death of Marilyn Monroe on August 5, 1962, was not a singular act of despair. It was the logical, almost inevitable, endpoint of a pharmaceutical management system that had lost all containment. Her body, discovered in the small hours in the bedroom of her Brentwood home, became the ultimate crime scene for the industry’s practices. The coroner’s report listed the cause as “acute barbiturate poisoning.” The official narrative settled on probable suicide. But the toxicology report and the pharmacy records subpoenaed in the subsequent weeks painted a far more systemic picture: that of a patient lethally overprescribed by a rotating panel of doctors, each operating in isolation, all servicing the same valuable asset.

The regimen documented in the last weeks of Marilyn’s life was a polypharmacy nightmare. According to the Los Angeles County Coroner’s file, her system contained chloral hydrate and Nembutal (pentobarbital) at levels far exceeding therapeutic doses. Investigation by the Los Angeles Police Department, detailed in their summary report, traced prescriptions from at least four physicians in the thirty days prior to her death: Dr. Hyman Engelberg, her internist; Dr. Lee Siegel, the Twentieth Century Fox studio doctor; psychiatrist Dr. Ralph Greenson; and a New York specialist, Dr. Max Jacobson. These were not street drugs. They were legal prescriptions, filled at reputable pharmacies, written by licensed medical professionals. Engelberg had prescribed twenty-five Nembutals on July 25, refilled on August 3. Siegel, according to Fox’s own later internal review, had been providing scripts for chloral hydrate and other sedatives for months to manage the insomnia that plagued her during the troubled production of Something’s Got to Give. Greenson, attempting to treat her anxiety and depression, prescribed additional barbiturates. Jacobson, whom she had seen in New York, was administering his amphetamine-laced “vitamin shots,” which created a physiological need for ever-stronger sedatives to achieve sleep.

No single doctor appeared to have a complete view of the total pharmacological load. This was not an accident of poor communication. It was a feature of the star system. Each physician was addressing a specific, compartmentalized complaint: the studio doctor needed her rested for the set; the psychiatrist needed her calm for therapy; the internist treated her physical symptoms; the “miracle doctor” promised energy and vitality. None had authority—or, evidently, inclination—to coordinate with the others. Their patient was a studio employee, a corporation’s investment. The primary goal, for the studio-aligned doctors in particular, was functional readiness, not holistic health. A memo from Fox production head Peter Levathes to studio head Spyros Skouras, dated June 1962 and later entered into court records during a probate hearing, framed the problem in corporate terms: “We must get Monroe to the set in a workable state. We will use all medical resources at our disposal to achieve this.”

That system provided no checks. California’s prescription drug laws in 1962 were porous. While the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act required prescriptions, there was no centralized database to track them across multiple doctors and pharmacies. A patient could, and Marilyn did, obtain identical or interacting medications from several sources simultaneously. The pharmacists who filled the scripts, as they testified in the coroner’s inquest, had no mechanism to know a rival pharmacy across town had filled a similar prescription from a different doctor the day before. The responsibility for monitoring the total intake fell entirely on the patient—a patient who was, by the nature of the medications she was taking, increasingly incapable of rational self-assessment.

The studio’s role in this final act was one of frantic damage control, both before and after the fact. During the final months of her life, as Marilyn repeatedly failed to appear on set for Something’s Got to Give, Fox executives did not order a medical intervention. They authorized her suspension and hired a private investigator to document her activities, gathering evidence to use against her contractually. They were managing a liability, not a person. After her death, the studio’s public relations machinery, in collaboration with the studio-friendly Los Angeles Times, immediately steered the narrative toward a tragic, solitary suicide. The alternative narrative—that a young woman could be lethally over-medicated by an uncoordinated platoon of her own doctors—was a story that threatened the entire medical infrastructure of the industry. Fox’s legal department quietly settled a potential lawsuit with her estate, with a condition of confidentiality. Key witnesses, including housekeepers and assistants, gave statements to the police but were never called to testify in a public trial. The district attorney’s office, under political pressure, closed the case without filing charges.

The most telling postscript is not found in the coroner’s report, but in the career trajectories of the doctors involved. Dr. Lee Siegel remained the chief physician at Twentieth Century Fox for another four years. His medical license was never suspended or revoked. Hyman Engelberg continued his private practice in Beverly Hills for decades. The New York State Board of Regents did not move against Dr. Max Jacobson for another thirteen years. The medical establishment closed ranks. No governing board was willing to confront the reality that a patient had been killed not by a single malicious actor, but by a standard of care—or rather, a standard of management—that was endemic to the relationship between Hollywood and medicine.

Marilyn’s death was the system’s most famous failure. It revealed the terminal flaw in the pharmaceutical model: eventually, the human body’s tolerance runs out. The ledgers stop. But the response to her death proved the system’s resilience. The solution was not reform. It was better public relations and more discreet outsourcing. The demand for chemical performance endured. It simply retreated further into the private suites of doctors who knew how to keep no records at all.
