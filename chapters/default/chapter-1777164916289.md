Walter Freeman arrived at St. Elizabeths Hospital in Washington, D.C., in the early 1930s, and what he found there, according to his own later writings, was a therapeutic void. The asylum’s vast wards housed thousands of patients deemed incurable—schizophrenics, manic-depressives, catatonics.

Their treatments were custodial, not curative: sedatives like chloral hydrate to dampen agitation, hydrotherapy baths for restraint, the occasional and perilous malarial fever induction. The neurologist, a specialist trained at the University of Pennsylvania and Yale, viewed these methods as medieval. The brain, he believed, was an organ. Its disorders were physical. To treat them with baths and fever was as absurd as treating a broken leg with a song.

He was not a man inclined to patience. Photographs from the period show a figure of contained energy: sharp eyes behind round spectacles, a trim mustache, a posture that suggested he was perpetually leaning slightly forward, ready to move. His grandfather, William Williams Keen, had been a pioneering surgeon who attended President Lincoln after the assassination.

The doctor inherited that clinical boldness, but applied it to the most opaque of human systems—the mind. At George Washington University, where he served as chairman of the neurology department, he was known for his lecturing flair and his willingness to challenge orthodoxies. He published papers on brain anatomy, on the effects of trauma, and on the then-novel technique of electroencephalography. Yet in the wards of St. Elizabeths, these academic pursuits felt abstract. The suffering before him was concrete, and his tools were inadequate.

A 1934 memorandum from the neurologist to the hospital’s superintendent, preserved in the St. Elizabeths archives, outlines his frustration. “We are,” he wrote, “engaged in a holding action. We manage symptoms, we do not address causes. The pathology of mental disease is rooted in the frontal lobes, and our current methods are incapable of reaching it.”

He had seen the results of Moniz’s early leucotomy reports from Europe, circulated in specialized journals. They were fragmentary, but suggestive. His correspondence with colleagues reveals a growing fixation. The idea was a surgical strike at the seat of the problem. It was direct. It was biological. It promised, at least in theory, a permanent resolution.

His personality was key. James Watts, the neurosurgeon who would become his partner, described the man in a later interview as “a force of nature.” The surgeon was a traditional doctor, precise, respectful of the boundaries of his specialty.

The neurologist was a diagnostician, not a surgeon. Yet he possessed what his partner termed “an evangelist’s zeal.” He could talk. He could persuade. He could, through sheer conviction, bend institutional resistance. When he first proposed to his colleague that they attempt a prefrontal procedure on an American patient, the surgeon recalled his own hesitation. The intervention was brutal, untested in the U.S., and fell outside the realm of standard neurosurgery. But the argument was pragmatic, and it was framed around a specific case: Alice Hood Hammatt.

Hammatt was a housewife from Topeka, Kansas, admitted to a private hospital in Washington with what the doctor diagnosed as agitated depression. She was, by his description, “tormented by anxiety and melancholia,” a case that had resisted all conventional interventions. In his clinical notes, quoted in medical journals of the time, the neurologist wrote that she was “consumed by a fixed state of dread.”

Here was a patient emblematic of the therapeutic void. To his partner, he presented the treatment not as an experiment, but as a logical, if drastic, extension of their duty. If the cause was biological, and the current treatments were failures, then a biological intervention was the only ethical path. The surgeon, despite his conservatism, agreed. The decision was made.

The partnership was, from the beginning, an alliance of contrasting temperaments. The surgeon handled the surgery—the drilling of burr holes into the skull, the careful insertion of instruments into the brain tissue. The neurologist handled the theory—the selection of patients, the determination of which neural pathways to sever, and the assessment of outcomes.

He also handled the narrative. After the first procedure on Hammatt in September 1936, the doctor immediately began drafting papers for publication. He wrote letters to colleagues across the country. He scheduled presentations. The initial results—the patient's reported placidity, her diminished anxiety—were, for him, not just a clinical outcome. They were a validation of his core belief: that mental illness was a mechanical fault, and that a mechanical correction was possible.

What the doctor brought to the American incarnation of the procedure was not just surgical technique. It was a philosophy of intervention, stripped of ambiguity. In a 1937 lecture to the American Medical Association, he stated the premise plainly: “The troubled mind is a machine in disarray. We are not philosophers; we are mechanics. Our task is to repair the machine.”

This was a language of certainty, of engineering. It bypassed the complexities of psychology, the mysteries of consciousness. It offered, to a profession frustrated by its own impotence, a solid tool.

And it offered something to the neurologist himself: a mission. The lethargy of the asylum wards, the endless cycles of sedation and crisis, the sense of professional stagnation—all of it could now be countered with action. He had a procedure. He had a partner. He had, in Alice Hammatt, what he considered a success. The therapeutic void had been, in his view, breached.

The breach, however, was narrow. The standard prefrontal surgery was a major operation. It required a surgical team, a fully equipped operating room, hours of time, and the precise skills of a neurosurgeon. It could not be scaled. The doctor's ambition was not for a handful of cases. It was for thousands. The asylums were filled with thousands. The problem was one of throughput.

He began to study the mechanics of the operation, searching for a bottleneck. The drilling of the skull, the opening of the dura, the delicate insertion of the leucotome—these were the surgeon's domain, and they were slow. But the core action, the severing of the frontal lobe fibers, was simple in concept. It was a cut. His notes from 1945 show a series of diagrams, increasingly simplified. He was searching for a shortcut, a method that bypassed the skull drill, the surgical theater, and the neurosurgeon.

He found it in an unlikely place: an ophthalmology textbook. The transorbital approach—accessing the brain through the thin bone at the back of the eye socket—was used for rare diagnostic procedures. The doctor adapted it. The instrument he envisioned was not a surgeon’s tool. It was an icepick.

The neurologist first experimented on cadavers. He encountered the concept not in a ward, but in a lecture hall. The details are recorded in his personal correspondence and in the minutes of the 1935 International Neurology Congress in London, which he attended. According to those minutes, the session on “Psychosurgery” was sparsely attended, tucked between more prominent discussions on epilepsy and cerebral localization. The presenter was not Moniz himself—the Portuguese neurologist had not traveled to London—but a colleague summarizing the early results from Lisbon.

The report was tentative. Moniz had performed his first human prefrontal procedure in November of 1935 on a patient with severe anxiety. The work involved drilling two small holes in the skull, above the frontal lobes, and injecting a small amount of alcohol into the white matter to destroy connective fibers.

The results, over several weeks, were described as a “calming” of the patient’s symptoms. The presenter emphasized the experimental nature of the work, the need for long-term follow-up, and the unknown risks. He cited a handful of cases. The tone was one of cautious, scientific inquiry.

The doctor's reaction, documented in a letter he wrote to a colleague days after the conference, was not cautious. “Moniz has found the key,” he wrote. “The principle is sound. The frontal lobes are the seat of the morbid fixed ideas. Severing their connections releases the patient from these ideas. It is a direct attack on the pathology.”

Where the presenter had offered nuance, the neurologist extracted a core principle. Where the report suggested a limited application for intractable anxiety, he saw a universal solution. The asylum wards, crowded with patients diagnosed with schizophrenia and catatonia, were filled with “morbid fixed ideas.” The intervention, in his reading, promised their erasure.

His interpretation relied on a specific, and selective, understanding of brain function. He subscribed to a modular view of the mind. Emotions, impulses, and thoughts were localized in specific brain regions. The frontal lobes, he believed, were the seat of future planning and social conformity. When these lobes were overactive or disordered, they produced the symptoms of mental illness: the agitated pacing, the paranoid delusions, the suicidal despair.

The procedure, by severing the connections of these lobes, would not destroy the patient’s personality, the neurologist reasoned. It would merely unlink the diseased part from the rest of the organ, allowing the healthy mechanisms below to function normally. This was not a theory of personality alteration; it was a theory of surgical quarantine.

The leap from the tentative report to a vision of mass application was intellectual, but it was also emotional. In his biography, the doctor described the moment as a “clarification.” The therapeutic void he had documented was not just a problem of resources or will. It was a problem of technique. The leucotomy was that technique.

It was biological, direct, and, in his estimation, final. A patient cured by the surgery would not need lifelong sedation or management. He or she could, theoretically, be discharged. The economic implication for overcrowded state hospitals was obvious. The moral implication, for the doctor, was paramount: a duty to act.

He returned to Washington and immediately sought out his partner. Their conversation, reconstructed from the surgeon's later memoir, centered not on the uncertainties of the European data, but on its potential. The neurologist presented the treatment not as an experiment, but as a proven method. He downplayed the report’s cautions. He emphasized the reported successes. The surgeon was more skeptical. He questioned the precision of the alcohol injection method. He noted the lack of long-term data. He worried about the irreversible nature of the lesion.

The neurologist's counterargument was pragmatic and forceful. “What are our alternatives?” he asked. “We have patients who are suffering, institutions that are bankrupting states, and a profession that is powerless. This procedure may carry risk, but the current state of affairs is a guarantee of failure. To not try it is a greater ethical failure than to try.” He framed the work as a logical next step in the evolution of neurology, akin to the advent of antiseptic surgery or the discovery of insulin. It was progress, and progress required pioneers.

The surgeon agreed to collaborate, but with conditions. He would refine the surgical technique, replacing the alcohol injection with a physical cutting instrument—a tool he designed himself. He would insist on strict preoperative protocols and postoperative observation.

The doctor accepted these conditions, but his focus was already beyond the operating room. He began drafting a paper for the Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease, outlining the theoretical basis for the procedure in American psychiatry. He started compiling a list of potential candidates from his caseload. Alice Hammatt was at the top of that list.

What the doctor took from Moniz was not just a surgical blueprint. He took a narrative of breakthrough. The leucotomy, in his telling, was not a cautious experiment. It was a discovery. And in that discovery, he found his own mission defined. The problem of mental illness was mechanical. The solution was surgical.

The scale of the problem demanded a scale of solution. The transorbital method, still years away, was the logical end point of this conviction—a procedure so simplified that it could be performed anywhere, on anyone, by anyone with the conviction to do it. That conviction was already fully formed in his mind by 1936. The icepick was merely its eventual, physical expression.

James Watts was, in every professional sense, his partner's opposite. A specialist trained at Harvard and the University of Virginia, the surgeon approached the brain with the reverence of a craftsman. His operating room was a domain of ritual: the precise placement of burr holes, the careful peeling back of the dura, the methodical insertion of his custom-designed tool. For the surgeon, the skull was a fortress to be breached with respect; the brain within was a landscape of delicate structures.

His memoir describes surgery as “a sacred geometry.” He believed in protocols, in follow-up studies, and in the gradual accumulation of evidence. He was a conservative by temperament.

Their partnership began with a shared, cautious optimism. The surgeon agreed with the diagnosis of a therapeutic void. He had seen the same hopeless cases in his own practice. The intervention offered a new direction. But for him, it was a direction to be explored with surgical rigor, not evangelistic speed. The division of labor they established reflected this dichotomy.

The neurologist selected the patients. He made the psychiatric diagnoses, assessed the suitability for surgery, and managed the postoperative evaluation. The surgeon performed the operation. He controlled the operating room, the anesthesia, and the physical act of cutting tissue. It was, initially, a complementary arrangement. The doctor provided the vision and the patients; his partner provided the surgical credibility and technical skill.

The first American lobotomy, performed on Alice Hammatt on September 14, 1936, was a surgical operation. The doctor's clinical notes describe the patient's preoperative state as “a torment of agitation.” The surgeon's notes detail a procedure lasting nearly four hours. Two burr holes were drilled into the top of the skull. The leucotome—a slender instrument with a retractable wire loop at its tip—was inserted into each frontal lobe. The surgeon rotated the instrument’s tip, severing a core of white matter fibers. The action was deliberate. He was cutting specific neural pathways, not making a broad sweep.

The immediate postoperative result was declared a success. The patient's agitation subsided. She was reported as calm, placid, and no longer consumed by the fixed ideas of her depression. For the doctor, this was a definitive validation. He began immediately to promote the surgery, writing articles, giving lectures, and corresponding with asylum directors across the country.

For the surgeon, it was a single case. He insisted on a slow, methodical expansion. They would perform a handful of additional procedures, he argued, each with extensive study, before drawing any broader conclusions.

This tension between the impulse to scale and the insistence on caution defined their early years. Between 1936 and 1940, they performed approximately 80 procedures together. The surgeon's notes for these cases show a pattern of precise documentation. He recorded the exact coordinates of each burr hole, the depth of the instrument, and the number of rotations. He tracked complications: infections, hemorrhages, and seizures. He developed a classification system for outcomes, ranging from “excellent” to “poor.”

The neurologist, meanwhile, was compiling his own statistics, but his focus was on the headline result: the percentage of patients deemed “improved.” His definition of improvement was broader, encompassing any reduction in symptoms that facilitated easier institutional management.

The surgeon's inherent discomfort with his partner's drive is evident in later interviews. “Walter was a brilliant salesman,” the surgeon told an oral historian in 1972. “He could convince administrators, families, even colleagues, that this was the answer. I was the mechanic. My job was to make sure the answer didn’t kill the patient on the operating table.”

He described his partner's energy as “a force that could bend reality.” The doctor would arrive at the operating room with a list of five potential patients; the surgeon would insist on reviewing each case individually, often rejecting several. The neurologist would propose expanding the criteria to include patients with milder conditions; his partner would resist, arguing that the data did not support it.

The partnership was productive. Their combined reputation grew. The Freeman-Watts procedure became the standard version of the operation in the United States. It was performed in major teaching hospitals and on private patients. It required a surgical team and full anesthesia. It had the imprimatur of authority. This period, from 1936 until roughly 1945, represents what historians later termed the “classical” era of the American surgery. It was slow and limited in scale—a fact that increasingly frustrated the neurologist.

The schism between the two men was a gradual divergence. The surgeon saw the operation as a neurosurgical procedure, its value to be determined through long-term study. The neurologist saw it as a psychiatric tool, its utility proven by the immediate relief it offered to institutions. For the surgeon, the goal was precision. For the doctor, the goal was access.

This divergence became concrete in January 1946, when the neurologist first performed his transorbital icepick intervention on a patient in his Washington office. He used an actual icepick from his kitchen drawer. He used electroconvulsive therapy to render the patient unconscious, not general anesthesia.

He performed the procedure alone, without a surgical nurse or his partner. He drove the instrument through the thin bone at the back of the eye socket, into the frontal lobe, and made a sweeping cut. The entire process took minutes.

When the surgeon learned of the experiment, his reaction was one of professional horror. “It was no longer surgery,” he wrote. “It was butchery. It abandoned every principle of precision, of safety, of anatomical respect.” He argued that the transorbital approach was blind and that the use of ECT as anesthesia was barbaric.

The doctor countered that it was efficient, that it could be performed anywhere, and that it opened the procedure to the thousands of patients in state hospitals who had no access to a neurosurgical suite.

Their partnership continued for four more years, but it was a strained collaboration. The surgeon insisted that his colleague restrict the transorbital method to a limited research protocol. The neurologist increasingly used it on his own during his travels to state hospitals. By 1950, the breach was complete.

The surgeon severed the partnership. In a letter to his former partner that year, he wrote, “You have crossed a line from physician to propagandist. The transorbital treatment is not a medical procedure; it is a stunt.” The reply was characteristic. “You are concerned with the tree,” the doctor wrote. “I am concerned with the forest. The forest is burning.”

The Freeman-Watts alliance had provided the surgery with its initial legitimacy. The surgeon's caution had given the work a veneer of medical rigor. But the neurologist's zeal was the engine. Once he found a method that bypassed the need for a surgeon and an operating room, the alliance became obsolete.

The surgeon retreated into conventional work, later expressing deep regret for his role in the early propagation of the procedure. The neurologist accelerated. The respectable era was over. The era of the icepick had begun.

The neurologist's rationale was not born of malice, but of a specific medical worldview. To understand the velocity of his advocacy, one must understand the intellectual terrain of 1930s psychiatry. The field was divided into two camps. The first, psychoanalysis, viewed mental illness as a conflict of unconscious drives. The second, biological psychiatry, viewed mental illness as a defect in the brain’s physical machinery.

The doctor was squarely in the latter camp. His training had taught him that specific brain regions controlled specific functions. A lesion in the motor cortex caused paralysis. It followed, in his logic, that the pathologies of emotion and thought—the constant anxiety, the paranoid delusions, the suicidal impulses—must also be localized. The frontal lobes were the likely seat.

His theory was mechanistic. He argued that certain patients developed “fixed circuits” of pathological thought. These circuits, like a broken record skipping on the same groove, produced repetitive behaviors. Sedation or analysis could not break these circuits. Only a physical interruption could.

The surgery, by severing the connections between the frontal lobes and the deeper emotional centers, would unstick the record. The patient would be freed from the pathological loop. The personality would not be destroyed, he insisted; it would be reset to a simpler, more placid state. This was not a cure, but a management strategy.

A patient liberated from tormenting anxiety might become dull, but he would no longer require constant sedation. He might even be discharged home.

The economic argument was inseparable from the medical one. His correspondence with state hospital administrators reveals a consistent theme: cost. A single procedure, he calculated, cost between $250 and $500. The annual cost of housing a patient was often over $1,000.

A successful operation could transform a violent patient into a docile one, potentially capable of discharge. In a 1948 presentation, he framed it as a mathematical imperative. “We have a duty to the taxpayer as well as to the patient,” he said. “A procedure that offers a chance of rehabilitation is a sound investment in human capital and public funds.”

The ethical landscape of the era provided ground for this calculus. The concept of informed consent did not exist. Medical authority was paramount. Physicians made decisions for patients, often in consultation with families, but the patient’s own voice was rarely a factor.

This was especially true for psychiatric patients, who were legally committed and considered incapable of judging their own interests. His selection process typically involved a diagnosis of intractable illness and the permission of a family member. The patient’s assent was not sought.

In the biological model, the patient was not a person with rights; he or she was a malfunctioning organism requiring correction. The decision to operate was a medical decision.

This philosophy found expression in his handling of risks and outcomes. He acknowledged complications—infection, hemorrhage, death—in his papers. But he framed them as hazards of any major surgical intervention, risks outweighed by the greater good. His definition of improvement was elastic.

In a 1950 follow-up study of 1,000 cases, he categorized outcomes. “Excellent” meant the patient was discharged. “Good” meant the patient was manageable within the institution. “Fair” meant the patient was quieter. A patient who was merely pacified still counted as a success in his statistics.

This scoring system reflected his belief: any reduction in symptom severity was a net benefit. The lobotomy traded a complex, suffering human for a simpler, quieter one. For families desperate for relief, this trade was acceptable.

His advocacy was built on a coherent, three-part argument. First, the medical rationale: mental illness was a biological fault in a brain circuit, correctable by surgery. Second, the economic rationale: surgery was a cost-effective solution to the crisis of overcrowded asylums.

Third, the ethical rationale: the physician’s duty to relieve suffering and restore order justified the irreversible alteration of personality. This argument was persuasive because it addressed real problems with a physical intervention. It bypassed the slow work of therapy. It offered administrators a number to report to legislators. It offered families a hope of ending a nightmare.

The flaw in the doctor's logic was in its foundational assumptions. The fixed circuit theory was speculative. The surgery did not selectively cut pathological pathways; it destroyed broad swaths of tissue, with unpredictable effects.

The economic calculation ignored the long-term costs of caring for a lobotomized patient, who often required lifelong custodial care. The ethical framework privileged institutional convenience over individual autonomy. But in the context of the time, these flaws were not fatal to the argument.

They were merely uncertainties. His zeal transformed these uncertainties into certainties. He was not a skeptic testing a hypothesis; he was a missionary. The surgery was, in his mind, the solution.

The ultimate expression of this conviction was his shift from the precise Freeman-Watts procedure to the transorbital method. His partner had insisted on precision because he believed the brain’s functions were localized.

The adoption of the icepick, a tool that made a blind, sweeping cut, implicitly abandoned that belief. If the goal was merely to disrupt the frontal lobes broadly, then precision was unnecessary. Efficiency was paramount. The transorbital method was the physical manifestation of his philosophy: a direct, mechanical intervention into the machine of the mind.

Alice Hood Hammatt was a sixty-year-old housewife from Topeka, Kansas, living in Washington, D.C., when she was referred to the neurologist in early 1936. Her case file documents a six-year history of agitated depression. Her symptoms included persistent anxiety, insomnia, and ideas of financial ruin.

She was described as unmanageable at home, consumed by a torment that no available treatment could alleviate. She had undergone periods of sedation, each providing only temporary respite. To the doctor, she represented the ideal candidate: a patient with a clear diagnosis, a suffering family, and a therapeutic void.

The process of selecting her was not governed by a formal protocol. According to records, there was no committee review and no independent psychiatric consultation beyond the doctor's own assessment. The decision was made through conversations between the neurologist, the surgeon, and Hammatt’s husband.

His clinical notes from August 1936 frame the rationale in pragmatic terms. “Patient A.H. Presents with a classical picture of affective disorder,” he wrote. “All conventional therapies have failed. The family is desperate. The new procedure offers a possibility of interrupting this cycle.” The notes contain no risk-benefit analysis presented to the patient.

The involvement of the housewife's husband, Charles, was critical. In the absence of an ethical framework, the family’s consent served as the sole moral justification. His correspondence with Charles Hammatt reveals a man worn down by crisis. “I cannot see her continue like this,” he wrote. “We are willing to try anything.”

The language of the consent form he signed was broad. It authorized the doctors to perform “a surgical procedure on the brain intended to relieve mental symptoms.” It did not name the specific operation. Consent, in this context, was an act of trust in medical authority born of exhaustion.

The surgeon's notes from the morning of September 14, 1936, reveal a different preoccupation. His concerns were anatomical and technical. He documented the precise placement of the burr holes and the design of his instrument.

His focus was on the mechanics of the operation, not on the philosophical justification. The two men’s separate notes illustrate the divided nature of the enterprise. The neurologist was selecting patients based on a theory of mental illness as a mechanical fault. The surgeon was performing surgery based on a theory of precise anatomical intervention.

The immediate aftermath declared the operation a success. The patient's agitation subsided. She was described as placid and cooperative. She no longer spoke of financial ruin.

For the doctor, this outcome validated every assumption. The patient’s fixed ideas had been broken. The surgery had corrected the faulty circuit. For his partner, it was a single, cautiously optimistic result. He insisted on a slow expansion of their work, with each new case studied intensively.

But the selection of Hammatt established a template. It demonstrated that a patient could be chosen on the basis of a physician’s subjective judgment and familial desperation. It demonstrated that consent could be secured through a generalized appeal to hope rather than a disclosure of risk.

It demonstrated that success could be measured in immediate, behavioral terms—the cessation of troublesome symptoms. This template was portable. It required only a doctor with a theory, a surgeon with a tool, and a family with no other options.

The case file for the first patient ends with a final note from the neurologist, dated six months post-operation. “Patient continues in a state of quietude,” he wrote. “No recurrence of previous affective storms. Husband reports household management is now possible.”

There is no further record of her long-term condition or her own perception of the change. She disappears from the archives, a quiet woman in a managed household. Her selection did not disappear. It became the first step in a process that would, over the next two decades, select thousands more.
