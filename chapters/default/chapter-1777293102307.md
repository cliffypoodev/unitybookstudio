Odette's left foot hovered three inches above the stage. Not on purpose. The laws of physics had politely excused themselves the moment she'd slipped on her first pair of toe shoes at age six, and now gravity was more of a loose suggestion than a rule.

The Grand Arctic Opera House smelled of singed tulle and stagehands' panic. A chandelier swayed overhead, its crystals tinkling like nervous laughter. Odette adjusted her tutu—starched white with char marks along the hem from last week's incident—and tried to ignore the way her ribcage hummed. That was new. Usually the holy combustion warnings started with her kneecaps.

"Places for Act Two!" The stage manager's voice cracked. A stagehand crossed himself and backed away from the rigging.

Odette took her starting position. Fourth position, arms en bas, right foot pointed so sharply it could puncture the ego of lesser ballerinas. The orchestra's first note trembled through the floorboards. She exhaled.

Her first jeté went beautifully. Her second jeté went through the backdrop.

"Merde," whispered the principal violinist, which was both the traditional ballet good-luck wish and an accurate assessment of the situation. Odette's right leg had phased through the painted Swiss Alps, leaving her dangling midair like a confused angel. She wiggled backward, extracting herself with a wet pop. The backdrop now featured a ballerina-shaped hole edged in celestial fire.

The conductor glared. Odette smiled. The show, as they say in the biz, must go up in flames.

She recovered with a series of rapid piqué turns, each rotation lifting her higher until she spun six feet above center stage. The audience gasped. The fire suppression system gurgled ominously.

A stagehand in the wings frantically mouthed something at her. Odette, mid-arabesque, squinted. The man pantomimed…spraying a hose? Shoveling snow? Performing an exorcism?

The sprinklers erupted.

Not water—no, that would be pedestrian. This was a thick, lavender-scented foam that coated the first three rows in what appeared to be God's own bubble bath. A dowager in pearls spat out a mouthful. "Is this canon?" she demanded of no one.

Odette, now foam-slicked, aborted her grand allegro and improvised a sliding penché that carried her straight into the arms of the bewildered male lead.

"You're on fire," he whispered.

"Always," she sighed, and batted a smoking ember from her sleeve.

The orchestra played louder, as if volume could drown out the chaos. Odette disentangled herself and launched into the fouettés—thirty-two consecutive turns, the make-or-break moment of Swan Lake. The first sixteen were flawless. On the seventeenth, the stage lights flickered. On the twenty-third, a trapdoor sprang open beneath the corps de ballet. On the twenty-eighth, the foam changed color to a concerning shade of puce.

The conductor's baton caught fire. He conducted faster.

Odette's final pirouette sent her levitating upside down near the proscenium arch. The audience cheered. The fire marshal wept. A single, perfect feather drifted from her tutu—and burst into blue flame midair.

The stage manager cornered her during intermission. "We've had complaints."

Odette blotted foam from her décolletage with a sacrificial leg warmer. "From whom?"

"Insurance adjusters. The archdiocese. Three pastry chefs who say the foam ruined their soufflés." He consulted his clipboard. "Also, the foam is now sentient."

A lavender bubble the size of a pumpkin floated past, humming a tune from Carmen.

Odette groaned. "It's not my fault the Vatican cursed me with divine grace."

"Your contract says 'no plagues of locusts during matinees.'"

"That was one time!"

A stagehand skidded in, gripping a fire extinguisher labeled 'FOR NON-DIVINE FIRES ONLY.' "The bishop's box is floating again."

Odette peeked through the curtain. Sure enough, the VIP section had detached from the balcony and now drifted lazily above the orchestra pit, trailing velvet ropes like jellyfish tentacles. The bishop clung to his armrest, chanting in Latin.

The stage manager massaged his temples. "Just finish the ballet. Without—" he gestured wildly, "—whatever this is."

Odette adjusted her tiara, now listing dangerously to starboard. "I'm trying for technical perfection, but God keeps editing my choreography."

Back onstage, the male lead was attempting his solo while dodging falling hymnals. The foam had organized itself into a chorus line.

Her ribcage hummed again—a sharper vibration this time, like a microwave full of vengeful seraphim. The combustion warnings were escalating. She had maybe ten minutes before spontaneous sainthood became spontaneous combustion.

The conductor, now wielding a charred baton in each hand, caught her eye and nodded. The music swelled. Odette took her mark.

This time, she wouldn't just dance perfectly. She'd dance so perfectly even heaven would have to admit defeat.

Or, failing that, at least turn the foam back to a non-sentient state.

The curtain rose. The audience ducked. Odette began to dance.

Odette's fouettés gained altitude with each rotation. The stage lights dimmed, then flared bright as judgment day. A foam bubble the size of a Volkswagen bumped against her hip, whispering the libretto to Don Giovanni in perfect iambic pentameter.

She ignored it. The trick was to treat divine interference like an overeager dance partner—lead firmly, apologize to bystanders later.

Her ribcage vibrated at a frequency that would shatter wineglasses. The combustion warnings had progressed from microwave beeps to what sounded like a full mariachi band tuning up inside her spleen.

The male lead grabbed her waist for the lift. His hands immediately recoiled. "You're hot."

"Thank you," she said, and launched herself from his singed palms into a grand jeté that left a trail of sparkles in the shape of the Virgin Mary's shopping list.

The foam chorus line kicked higher. A rogue bubble popped against the concertmaster's violin, transmuting his Stradivarius into a surprisingly competent ukulele. The orchestra adapted, switching mid-measure from Tchaikovsky to Hawaiian folk music.

Odette landed the jeté—mostly. Her right foot sank through the stage up to the ankle. She wiggled it free, leaving behind a neat hole that emitted the faint scent of cinnamon and regret.

The bishop's box drifted overhead, now trailing incense smoke. A stagehand in the wings frantically waved a clipboard: TEN MINUTES TO COMBUSTION.

She had to finish the ballet before she turned into a holy sparkler.

The music swelled. Odette attacked the next sequence with the fervor of a woman who knew her dry cleaning bill would be apocalyptic. Piqué turns became vertical ascents. Arabesques hovered at a forty-five-degree angle to the floor. Her tutu spontaneously sprouted sequins that rearranged themselves to display live stock market updates.

A jet of lavender foam intercepted her mid-pirouette, spinning her like a top in a car wash. She emerged coated in bubbles that spelled out REPENT in elegant cursive across her collarbone.

"Not now," she hissed, and shook herself like a wet poodle. The bubbles scattered, reforming as PENITENT before popping.

The conductor—down to one baton and what appeared to be a baguette—signaled the final variation. Odette took a deep breath. This was it.

Her left shoe burst into blue flame.

The audience gasped. The fire marshal fainted. The foam chorus line paused its routine to film the spectacle on tiny bubble phones.

Odette didn't miss a beat. She rose onto her flaming pointe and began the turns.

One. Two. Three. Each rotation sent up a geyser of sparks. By the seventh turn, her entire silhouette shimmered with holy fire. By the twelfth, the stage curtains had caught fire in a way that suggested an enthusiastic but inexperienced stained-glass artisan.

On the twenty-fourth turn, the trapdoor beneath the corps de ballet yawned open again. The dancers tumbled through—not into the basement, but into what appeared to be a very surprised-looking Vatican gift shop.

Twenty-eight. Twenty-nine. The bishop's box drifted lower, his mitre now smoking. The foam had turned a vibrant shade of liturgical gold and begun chanting in Gregorian monotone.

Thirty. Thirty-one. Odette's vision whited out. The music became a single sustained note from an overworked kazoo.

Thirty-two.

She struck the final pose—arms aloft, back arched, one flaming leg extended in a perfect attitude. The entire opera house held its breath.

Odette combusted.

Not messily. Not painfully. But with the precision of a soufflé achieving perfect rise at the exact moment the dinner guests walk in.

A pillar of blue fire shot upward, parting around the bishop's floating box like Moses with commitment issues. The foam chorus line evaporated with a sound like a thousand monks sighing in relief. The orchestra's instruments turned briefly into stained glass before reverting to normal, slightly sticky wood.

When the flames cleared, Odette was gone.

The stage lights flickered. The audience rustled programs. A single, pristine pointe shoe sat center stage, glowing faintly.

Then the other shoe dropped.

Literally. It fell from the ceiling with a soft plink, landing upright beside its mate. Inside, a small card read: Reserved for encore performance. See you in three days.

The curtain descended. The fire suppression system gave one last, contented gurgle and shut off.

Backstage, the stage manager stared at the shoes. The clipboard slipped from his fingers.

The principal violinist sniffed the air. "Do you smell…crème brûlée?"

A stagehand crossed himself. "She's coming back?"

The male lead picked up the card, turned it over, and sighed. "The handwriting's in Latin."

Above them, a single lavender bubble floated toward the rafters, humming Ave Maria in the key of existential relief.

Three days later, the Grand Arctic Opera House sold out its midnight revival of Swan Lake. The bishop requested a front-row seat—and a fireproof cassock.

The stage manager added a new line to every performer's contract: Must provide own holy water.

And in the wings, a single pair of ballet slippers waited, laces twitching with anticipation.

The slippers stopped twitching.

Mira didn't notice. Her attention belonged to the stage manager's clipboard. "The understudy's ready. She knows the whole third act."

"I'm fine."

"Your ankle's wrapped. I can see it from here."

Mira flexed her foot. A spike of pain shot up her calf. She kept her face blank. "It's a precaution. From yesterday's rehearsal."

"Yesterday you weren't limping." The stage manager tapped her pen against the clipboard.

"I dance unless the director says otherwise. Has he said otherwise?"

A sigh. The pen stopped tapping. "No. But he's watching. Closer than usual."

Mira turned back to the mirror. Her reflection showed calm. The woman behind it counted seconds until the curtain. Thirty-seven minutes. She could last thirty-seven minutes. She had to.

The call came for places. Dancers flowed from the dressing room like water finding its level. Mira moved with them, each step a calculation. Weight on the ball of the foot. Roll. Push off. Don't favor the right side. Don't let it show.

In the wings, the space where the slippers had waited was empty.

Act One passed in a blur of collective breath and controlled frenzy. Mira's body performed its memorized patterns. Her mind floated somewhere above the stage lights, observing the mechanics. The ankle held. It burned, but it held.

During the blackout for scene change, Elena caught her arm. "You're off."

"I'm tired."

"You're guarding. I can see it from the corps. What happened?"

"Nothing." Mira pulled away. The movement was too sharp. Elena's eyes narrowed.

"If you tear something tonight—"

"I won't."

"You're selfish. You'll ruin the whole—"

The curtain rose. Light swallowed Elena's accusation. Mira smiled, assuming the opening pose of Act Two. Her face became a mask of pastoral joy. Inside, her stomach clenched.

She wasn't selfish. She was desperate. The director's new piece premiered next month. Soloist roles were being cast tomorrow. A pulled ankle tonight meant a hospital visit. A hospital visit meant no audition. No audition meant another year in the corps. Another year watching others leap while she remained ground-bound.

The dance demanded a series of petit jetés. Small jumps. Routine. Her ankle screamed on the first landing. She converted the second jump into a turn, improvising. A gasp from the audience—they thought it intentional. The other dancers adjusted around her, their smiles fixed, their eyes flashing warning.

She finished the sequence a half-beat late. The music covered it.

During her brief exit, she leaned against a flat, breathing through her nose. Sweat dripped into her eyes. She wiped it away. Her fingers came back smudged with black from her eyeliner.

A stagehand offered a water bottle. She shook her head. Drinking now would mean cramping later.

"Mira."

The director stood in the shadow of a fly rail. She hadn't seen him approach.

"Sir."

"That turn. In the jeté sequence."

"I thought it flowed better with the violin line."

The lie tasted metallic. He stared at her. The silence stretched over two bars of music from the stage.

"Interesting choice," he said finally. "Don't make another one without telling me first."

He walked away, disappearing into the dark.

Mira let out a shaky breath. Close. Too close.

Intermission.

The dressing room buzzed with adrenaline and gossip. Mira sat at her station, unwrapping the ankle. The skin was angry and swollen. She re-wrapped it tighter, biting her lip against the pain.

Elena dropped into the next chair. "He knows."

"He didn't say anything."

"He doesn't have to." Elena began removing her pins. "I covered for you on the arabesque promenade. You were supposed to be my rock. You wobbled."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Fix it. Or bow out."

"I can't."

"You mean you won't." Elena stood, her reflection towering over Mira's. "You'll break yourself to prove a point. And for what? A solo? You think dancing injured makes you look dedicated? It makes you look stupid. And dangerous."

She left before Mira could reply.

The warning sat in the room like a bad smell. Mira applied fresh makeup, her hands steady now. The anger helped. It burned cleaner than fear.

Act Three was the mountain. The entire ballet built toward its fifteen-minute pas de deux. Mira's partner, Leo, found her at the water cooler.

"We need to talk about the lifts."

"They're fine."

"The overhead in the coda. You're favoring your right side when you push off. My balance is off."

"I'll fix it."

"Mira." He lowered his voice. "If you can't do the overhead, we change the choreography. Now. Before we're out there with five hundred people watching."

"We're not changing anything."

"It's not just you up there." His jaw tightened. "My career's on that stage too."

"You won't."

She walked away from him, back toward the stage. Her ankle throbbed in time with her heartbeat.

The music began, a slow cello line that felt like a last breath before a fall. Mira let it carry her. She became the music, the story, the character dying of a broken heart. Her own pain folded into the character's, giving it a raw, jagged edge she'd never achieved in rehearsal.

The audience stilled. She felt their collective focus like a physical pressure.

The lifts approached. Leo's hands at her waist were tense. She met his eyes—a flash of warning, of doubt. She nodded, just once.

The first lift went smoothly. The second, a rotational throw, landed clean. Then the overhead. She ran toward him, launched. His hands caught her hips, thrust her upward. Her right ankle buckled as she pushed off. The force was uneven.

She went up crooked.

Leo staggered under the unbalanced weight. For a terrible second, she felt the world tilt. His grip slipped, regained. He muscled her overhead, his arms shaking with the strain.

She held the pose, arms extended, face a mask of ecstasy. The audience applauded. They saw art. They didn't see Leo's trembling muscles or the tear of something in her ankle when she'd pushed.

He brought her down too fast. Her landing was a stumble disguised as a dramatic swoon. She fell against him, her weight on his chest. He held her, his breath ragged in her ear.

"You're done," he whispered. "After this, you're finished."

The music swelled toward the death scene. Her character expired in her lover's arms. Mira let herself go limp, her body a dead weight. Leo lowered her to the stage. His hands were gentle, his eyes hard.

Blackout.

Silence. Then applause, crashing over them like a wave.

Leo stood, bowed, offered her a hand. She took it, letting him pull her up. They bowed together. The curtain fell.

Backstage was chaos. Dancers hugged, cried, laughed. Mira limped toward the dressing room, avoiding everyone.

The director blocked her path. His face was unreadable.

"It worked."

"Did it?" He studied her. "You changed the choreography without consultation. You endangered your partner. The company."

She said nothing. The pain was a white noise filling her skull.

"My office. Tomorrow. Nine a.m." He stepped aside. "Get that ankle looked at tonight. Not tomorrow. Tonight."

He disappeared into the celebrating crowd.

Mira made it to her dressing station. She sat, began removing her shoes. The right shoe stuck. The swelling had trapped it. She tugged, gritting her teeth. It came free with a sickening pull.

The ankle was purple, twice its normal size.

"Idiot."

She looked up. Elena stood in the doorway, holding two ice packs.

"You should be celebrating," Mira said.

"With what? You nearly took out Leo and the whole third act." Elena tossed one pack to her. "Put that on. I'll call a car."

"I can call my own—"

"You can't even put your own weight on your foot. Shut up and ice it."

Elena left to make the call. Mira pressed the cold pack to her ankle. The relief was immediate, profound. She closed her eyes.

When she opened them, she saw the empty spot in the corner. The ballet slippers were gone. She'd almost forgotten them.

Had someone moved them? A stagehand, maybe. Clearing clutter.

She looked around the room. No slippers on any other bench. No stray shoes waiting.

Elena returned. "Car's ten minutes. Can you walk to the stage door?"

"With help."

"You'll have it." Elena leaned against the doorframe. "Why'd you do it? Really?"

Mira looked at her ruined ankle. "I had to dance."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have."

Elena shook her head. "Tomorrow's audition. You know you're out, right? Even if he doesn't fire you, you can't dance on that for weeks."

"I know."

"So what was the point?"

Mira didn't answer. The point was the fifteen minutes before the overhead lift. The point was the absolute silence of the audience. The point was being, for once, undeniable on stage. The cost? She'd count that tomorrow.

They left the theater through the empty stage door. The night air was cold. Mira shivered in her thin coat.

Elena's car arrived first. "You sure you don't want me to wait with you?"

"Go. Celebrate. You earned it."

Elena hesitated, then got in the car. It pulled away, taillights disappearing around the corner.

Mira stood alone under the streetlight. Her ankle pulsed in time with the neon sign above the theater marquee. The show title glowed: Giselle. A ghost story. How fitting.

Her car arrived. The driver helped her into the back seat.

"Hospital?" he asked.

"Please."

As they pulled away, Mira looked back at the theater. A figure stood in the alley beside the stage door. Small, slight. Dressed in dark clothes.

The figure raised a hand. Not a wave. A gesture, sharp and formal. A dancer's salute.

Then the car turned the corner, and the theater vanished from view.
