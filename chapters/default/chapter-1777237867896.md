The fig's skin split under Lucius Valerius's thumbnail, revealing pale flesh flecked with seeds. He flicked the rind over the parapet and watched it spiral down toward the Temple courts. Below, the usual midday press of pilgrims had dissolved into knots of argument. One cluster gestured wildly toward the Essene Quarter. Another hunched near the merchants' stalls, voices low but urgent.

A breeze carried the scent of roasting meat and something sharper—charred parchment, maybe. Lucius leaned against the warm stone, his stylus hovering over the wax tablet. He'd stopped recording the specifics an hour ago. The patterns mattered more than the particulars.

"Another disturbance by the Nazarenes?" The centurion's shadow fell across the tablet.

Lucius didn't look up. "Not directly. They're arguing about one of their own."

"Saul of Tarsus."

That earned the centurion a glance. The man's face was all angles and scars, his helmet tucked under one arm. A recent transfer, then. The veterans knew better than to interrupt an optio's observations.

"You've heard of him."

"Every patrol this week. The priests want him gone. The merchants want him quiet. The Nazarenes..." The centurion shrugged. "They can't decide if he's a gift or a curse."

Lucius scratched a note. The wax held impressions of earlier entries—estimates of crowd sizes, descriptions of key agitators. He'd need to smooth it soon and start fresh.

The centurion shifted his weight. "Should we intervene?"

"Not unless they start throwing stones."

"They did yesterday."

"At each other." Lucius tapped the stylus against the tablet's edge. "Let them exhaust themselves."

The centurion hesitated, then nodded and retreated. His sandals scuffed against the stone, the sound fading toward the barracks.

Alone again, Lucius exhaled through his nose. The Jews and their endless theological squabbles. In Rome, a man could worship Mithras one day and Isis the next without starting a riot. But here, every minor disagreement over scripture or ritual threatened to spill into the streets.

He turned his attention back to the courts. A group of men in Pharisee robes had cornered a younger man near the colonnade. The younger one's hands moved in sharp, defensive arcs. Even from this distance, Lucius recognized the posture—Saul, though he couldn't make out the words. The Pharisees leaned in, their faces tight with something between anger and fascination.

Interesting.

Lucius had seen zealots before. They burned hot and fast, their fervor consuming them within months. But Saul was different. The man carried his conviction like a blade—precise, controlled, and just as dangerous to those who grasped it carelessly.

A shout rose from the courts. One of the Pharisees shoved Saul. The younger man stumbled but didn't fall. He said something that made the others recoil.

Lucius straightened.

Saul stepped forward, his voice carrying now. "You search the scriptures because you think they give you life, yet you refuse to come to me!"

The Pharisees exchanged glances. One spat on the ground. Another grabbed Saul's arm, but Saul shook him off.

"These are the words of Moses—"

"Twisted!" Saul's voice cracked like a whip. "You honor God with your lips, but your hearts are far from him!"

The nearest Pharisee backhanded him.

Saul's head snapped to the side. He touched his lip, examined the blood on his fingers, and smiled.

Lucius's stylus stilled. That smile—cold and bright and utterly without fear—was the most unsettling thing he'd seen all week.

The Pharisees seemed to agree. They drew back, muttering among themselves. After a moment, they dispersed, leaving Saul alone in the colonnade's shadow.

Saul watched them go, then turned toward the Temple itself. His lips moved, though no sound reached the fortress walls.

Lucius set the tablet aside. He'd need to report this, but not yet. First, he wanted to see where Saul went next.

The sun dipped lower, gilding the Temple's white stone. Below, the priests began lighting the evening lamps. Their flames flickered to life one by one, tiny points of light in the gathering dark.

Saul remained where he was, his face upturned.

Lucius rubbed his thumb along the stylus's edge. He'd been stationed in Jerusalem long enough to recognize a man waiting for a sign. The question was whether Saul would recognize it when it came—or if he'd mistake another blow for divine attention.

A door creaked behind him. The centurion again, this time with a message scroll.

"From the prefect."

Lucius took it, broke the seal, and skimmed the contents. Standard orders—increase patrols near the Upper City, monitor the festival preparations, report any disturbances.

He rolled the scroll and tapped it against his palm. "Tell the decanus I'll be in the city tonight."

The centurion's eyebrows rose. "Alone?"

"I'm observing a religious gathering."

"Which one?"

Lucius nodded toward the figure still standing in the shadow of the colonnade. "His."

The centurion followed his gaze. "You think he's dangerous?"

"All true believers are." Lucius tucked the scroll into his belt. "The trick is figuring out who they'll burn first—the rest of us, or themselves."

The house smelled of fish oil and damp wool. Lucius took a seat near the back, his civilian cloak blending with the others'. The room was crowded—men and women packed shoulder to shoulder, their faces lit by a single lamp.

Saul stood near the center, his hands clasped behind his back. He looked worse for wear—his lip swollen, a bruise darkening along his jaw—but his voice was steady.

"You ask why I keep speaking when it brings trouble." He paused, scanning the room. "Because silence would be the greater betrayal."

A woman near the front—the baker from the courts—folded her arms. "Your words stir up the priests. They'll come for all of us now."

"And if they do?" Saul's gaze found hers. "Did our master promise safety?"

Someone coughed. A child whimpered and was shushed.

Lucius leaned forward, elbows on his knees. This was the heart of it, then—not doctrine, but cost. These people had followed a crucified rabbi because they believed his death meant something. Now they had to decide if Saul's persecution would mean something too.

The baker looked away first.

Saul exhaled, his shoulders sagging for the first time all evening. "I don't ask you to trust me. Only to consider whether the message is true, regardless of the messenger."

A murmur ran through the room. Lucius caught fragments—"Damascus," "Stephen," "the high priest's letters."

An old man near the lamp stood slowly. "You say the spirit guides you. Then why does every step you take leave blood behind?"

Saul flinched.

Lucius noted the reaction. Guilt, or just frustration?

Before Saul could answer, a younger man—Barnabas, according to Lucius's notes—rose and placed a hand on the old man's shoulder. "We've all left blood behind. The question is whether we keep walking toward the light, or let the past chain us to the dark."

The room fell silent.

Saul looked at Barnabas, something raw and unguarded flashing across his face. Lucius couldn't tell whether it was gratitude or relief.

Outside, a dog barked. The sound broke the moment. People shifted, whispered, began gathering their things.

Lucius stayed where he was, watching as the crowd dispersed. Saul remained by the lamp, his head bowed. Barnabas spoke to him in low tones, but Saul didn't respond.

Lucius stood and slipped out into the night. The streets were quiet, the moon a pale sliver overhead. He'd file his report in the morning. For now, he had enough to consider.

A man like Saul could destabilize the entire region—not through armies or edicts, but by convincing enough people that their god preferred martyrs to magistrates. Rome had no answer for that kind of rebellion.

He turned the corner and nearly collided with a figure lurking in the shadows.

The baker.

She blocked his path, her arms crossed. "You're not one of us."

Lucius spread his hands, showing empty palms. "Just a traveler seeking truth."

Her eyes narrowed. "Truth has a price."

"So I've heard." He stepped around her. "Good night."

She let him pass, but her gaze burned into his back until he rounded the next corner.

Lucius allowed himself a small smile.

Yes, this would bear watching.

The synagogue hummed with tension, the air thick with the scent of lamp oil and unwashed wool. Lucius lingered near the back, his civilian cloak blending with the shadows. The benches were packed—Hellenists in their finer linens on one side, Aramaic-speaking locals in homespun on the other. Saul stood at the center, his knuckles white where they gripped the scroll.

A man in a Greek-style chiton stood abruptly. "You quote Moses yet disregard the traditions of our fathers!"

Saul didn't flinch. "I honor Moses by showing you the One he foretold."

Murmurs rippled through the crowd. Lucius noted the way the Hellenists leaned forward, fingers drumming against their knees, while the locals sat stone-still.

A woman near the front—the baker from the previous night—spun to face the challenger. "And what tradition did Moses give us? To slaughter lambs forever, or to listen when God speaks?"

The Greek's face darkened. "You would know nothing of tradition, woman. Your people barely speak the language of the Torah."

Saul stepped between them. "The Torah was given in fire and smoke, yet you treat it like a merchant's ledger—counting each stroke of the pen but missing the hand that wrote it."

Lucius's gaze flicked to the back corner. Three men in Nabatean-styled robes whispered among themselves. One traced a finger along the edge of a blade hidden in his sash.

A local elder rose, his voice trembling. "Enough! We didn't gather to watch our children's blood water the streets again."

Saul turned to him, and for the first time, his voice softened. "Abba, it's precisely because of their blood that I must speak."

The room stilled.

Lucius exhaled slowly. Clever. The old man had lost a son in one of the riots last year—everyone knew it. Saul had just turned the debate from scripture to scars.

The Hellenist wasn't finished. "You drag us into your madness. The Romans won't distinguish between your followers and ours when they crack down."

Saul's smile was razor-thin. "Then perhaps you should stop pointing me out to them."

A gasp. The Hellenist lunged, but two others hauled him back.

Lucius straightened. There—the flicker of a hand signal between the Nabateans. Not the haphazard anger of offended devotees, but the crisp motion of men coordinating.

Saul seemed oblivious, his attention on the elder. "You want peace? So do I. But peace bought with silence is slow suffocation."

The baker stood abruptly. "We're leaving."

Half the room followed her toward the door. The remaining Hellenists glared after them, their muttered curses blending with the shuffle of sandals.

The Nabateans didn't move.

Lucius edged closer to the exit. He'd seen enough. This wasn't just theological division—it was a fracture line running straight through Jerusalem's fragile peace. Saul, whether he knew it or not, was hammering the wedge.

Outside, the night air carried the distant bleat of a sacrificial lamb. The baker's group huddled near the olive press, voices low and urgent.

One of the Nabateans emerged from the synagogue, his blade now openly in hand.

Lucius melted into the shadows. Time to file that report.

The garrison's torchlight flickered across the prefect's face as he read Lucius's notes. "You're certain they're Nabatean?"

"Certain enough. Their robes were Damascene, but the knife work was pure Petra."

The prefect tossed the tablet onto his desk. "And the Pharisee?"

"Either the most dangerous man in Judea or the worst fool." Lucius hesitated. "Possibly both."

A shout echoed from the courtyard below. The prefect didn't bother to look. "Keep watching. If Herod's people are involved, this goes beyond religious squabbles."

Lucius nodded and turned to leave.

"Oh—and Valerius?" The prefect's voice stopped him at the door. "Try not to get stabbed this time."

Lucius allowed himself a smirk. "No promises."

The alley stank of urine and crushed herbs. Lucius pressed his back against the cool stone, the rough texture catching on his cloak. Voices carried from the courtyard beyond—not the usual drunken laughter of Greek merchants, but the clipped, urgent tones of men plotting.

"...tomorrow at the third hour." A familiar voice—the Hellenist from the synagogue. "He always takes the lower road after teaching at the tanner's yard."

A grunt. The sound of metal against leather. "And the Nabateans?"

"Distracted. They want him dead, but not before he stirs up enough trouble to justify their own reprisals."

Lucius shifted just enough to see through the gap in the wall. Four men stood in a tight circle, their faces lit by a single oil lamp. The Hellenist held a rolled parchment, tracing a route with his finger. The others wore the short, practical blades of city toughs, not the curved daggers of Nabatean assassins.

One spat into the dust. "You're certain the Romans won't interfere?"

The Hellenist's smile was thin. "They never do when Jews kill Jews."

Lucius's fingers twitched toward his own blade before he caught himself. Intervening now would accomplish nothing beyond revealing his presence. He exhaled through his nose, counting heartbeats until the urge passed.

The tallest thug cracked his knuckles. "No witnesses?"

"Only the usual beggars. They know better than to speak."

A pause. The lamp flickered, casting long shadows across their faces.

The Hellenist lowered his voice. "Remember—make it messy. The more it looks like a Zealot killing, the less anyone digs."

Lucius eased back into the deeper shadows. His sandal scuffed a loose stone. The men went silent.

He held his breath.

A rat skittered across the alley, drawing their attention. The Hellenist muttered something about vermin, and the tension broke.

Lucius waited until their footsteps faded before moving. The moon offered little light, but he knew these streets well enough to navigate by touch. His mind raced ahead—who among Saul's followers could be trusted with this? Not the baker. She'd either confront the killers directly or accuse Lucius of lying. The old man, perhaps, or that Barnabas fellow with the steady hands.

A shape detached itself from a doorway ahead.

Lucius froze.

The baker stepped into the sliver of moonlight, her arms crossed. "You follow him too."

Not a question. Lucius considered denying it, but the set of her jaw told him she'd seen enough. "I observe."

"And now?"

He studied her face—the tightness around her eyes, the way her fingers dug into her elbows. She already knew something was wrong. "Now I consider whether sharing what I've heard will save lives or spill more blood."

Her breath hitched, just once. "They're going to kill him."

Lucius said nothing.

She turned abruptly and strode down the alley. At the corner, she glanced back. "The tanner's yard. Third hour."

Then she was gone.

Lucius rubbed his thumb along the edge of his belt. He'd expected pleading, demands for Roman intervention. This quiet certainty unsettled him more.

The night air carried the distant sound of singing—a psalm, maybe, or a drinking song. He couldn't tell which.

The garrison's gate creaked as Lucius slipped inside. The watchman nodded but didn't speak. Good. The fewer questions, the better.

He bypassed the prefect's office and went straight to the records room. The oil lamp there burned low, its light barely reaching the shelves of scrolls and tablets. Lucius selected a blank scrap of parchment and a stylus.

His hand hovered. What to write? A direct warning would implicate him. A vague one might be ignored.

In the end, he kept it simple: Lower road. Third hour. Four blades.

He rolled the parchment tight and sealed it with a drop of wax pressed flat by his thumb—no official mark, just enough to hold it closed.

The watchman coughed as Lucius passed back through the gate. "Late for messages, sir."

"Not for the courier." Lucius dropped a denarius into the man's palm. "See it reaches the Essene Quarter before dawn."

The watchman pocketed the coin. "Any particular house?"

"The one with the fish carved above the door."

A pause. Then a slow nod. The watchman had been stationed here long enough to know which groups met where.

Lucius stepped into the street. The first hint of gray touched the eastern sky. Somewhere beyond the city walls, a jackal cried.

He could return to his quarters, file a formal report in the morning, let the machinery of Roman bureaucracy grind along at its usual pace.

Instead, he turned toward the tanner's yard.

The streets were empty save for the occasional slave hurrying to light household lamps. Lucius kept to the shadows, his steps silent. He'd seen enough death in his years—some honorable, most not. But this felt like watching a man walk toward a cliff edge, convinced he'd sprout wings before the drop.

The tanner's yard came into view, its vats silent in the predawn hush. Lucius took up position behind a stack of hides, the stench of lye and rotting flesh thick in his nostrils.

He didn't have to wait long.

Saul appeared as the first true light painted the rooftops, his stride purposeful. Alone. No Barnabas, no baker, no old man. Either he hadn't gotten the message, or they'd chosen not to act.

Lucius's jaw tightened.

A flicker of movement near the lower road. The Hellenist's thugs, taking position.

Saul paused at the corner, his head tilted as if listening to some distant voice. Then, inexplicably, he turned left instead of right—away from the ambush, toward the Damascus Gate.

The thugs exchanged confused glances. One started forward, but the Hellenist grabbed his arm and shook his head.

Saul never looked back.

Lucius exhaled. The man's luck wouldn't hold forever, but today, at least, he'd live.

As Saul's figure dwindled in the distance, Lucius caught a glimpse of two shapes following at a discreet interval—Barnabas and the baker, their steps light and quick.

So they'd gotten the message after all.

But so was someone else.
