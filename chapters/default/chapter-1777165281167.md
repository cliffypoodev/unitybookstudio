He died in 1972. The cause was a heart attack. The setting was his small office in San Francisco, the same one he had used for his neurology practice after his surgical privileges were revoked.

The obituaries in the major medical journals were brief and factual. They noted his role in developing the transorbital lobotomy, his thousands of procedures, and his retirement from surgery after the Mortensen incident. They did not contain eulogies. They did not spark debates. They were administrative notices, marking the closure of a file.

His death removed the central figure from the story. But it did not end the story. The patients remained.

For decades, their voices had been absent from the historical record. Their experiences were documented only in his files: the pre-operative photographs, the brief notes on behavior, and the post-operative assessments that measured success by a reduction in noise. Their own accounts—the loss of personality, the struggle to form new memories, and the hollowed-out feeling of a self that had been dismantled—were not part of the medical literature.

They existed in private letters to him, in family diaries, and in the silent adjustments of households that now contained a person who was both present and absent. But these were private documents. They had no public audience. They carried no weight against the statistical arguments of a medical pioneer.

The cultural space for those voices began to open in the years just before his death. It opened not because of a single event, but because of a confluence of forces that eroded the authority of institutional psychiatry.

The first force was pharmacological. Chlorpromazine and its successors had, by the late 1960s, become the standard treatment for psychosis and severe agitation. They were reversible. Their effects could be adjusted. They did not require surgery.

The surgery, as a procedure, was obsolete. This obsolescence changed its status. It was no longer a cutting-edge therapy defended by its inventor. It was a historical artifact. It could be examined, criticized, and judged without threatening current medical practice. A doctor in 1975 could condemn the operation without fearing he was undermining a colleague’s work. The procedure was safely in the past.

The second force was ideological. The anti-psychiatry movement, led by figures like Thomas Szasz and R.D. Laing, gained traction in the 1960s and 1970s. Szasz’s 1960 book, *The Myth of Mental Illness*, argued that many psychiatric conditions were not medical diseases but problems of living, and that coercive treatments were forms of social control.

Laing, in works like *The Divided Self*, framed schizophrenia not as a biological defect but as a rational response to an insane family environment. These arguments attacked the foundational premise of the intervention: that mental illness was a mechanical fault in the brain that required a physical correction. They shifted the debate from medical efficacy to human rights. They asked, not whether the treatment worked, but whether it was ethical to perform it.

The third force was legal. The rise of patient advocacy and informed consent as legal principles began to reframe the relationship between doctor and patient. The 1972 case of *Kaimowitz v. Department of Mental Health* in Michigan established that patients had a right to understand the risks and irreversible nature of such procedures.

The case cited the procedure as a precedent of harm. It treated the patient not as a subject of treatment, but as a citizen with rights that could be violated by medical authority. This legal shift created a language—the language of rights, of consent, of harm—that survivors could use. Their experiences were no longer just medical outcomes; they were potential legal injuries.

These three forces—pharmacological, ideological, legal—did not coordinate. They operated independently. But their combined effect was to dismantle the wall of medical authority that had surrounded the practice.

The wall had been built of professional consensus, reported improvements in patient behavior, and the charismatic defense of its inventor. By the early 1970s, that consensus had fractured. The statistics were questioned. The inventor was gone.

Into this newly opened space, the first voices emerged.

They emerged not as a collective movement, but as isolated instances. A family member writing a letter to a newspaper. A former patient speaking to a local reporter. A memoir published by a small press.

These were not yet the organized testimonies of the 1990s. They were fragments, scattered across the country, often ignored or dismissed as anecdotal. But they existed. They were now audible.

The tone of these early accounts was not accusatory. It was bewildered. A 1974 letter from a woman in Ohio to the *Cleveland Plain Dealer*, reprinted in a follow-up article, described her brother, who had undergone a transorbital lobotomy in 1958 at a state hospital.

“He came home quiet,” she wrote. “That was what they said would happen. But he wasn’t my brother anymore. He could feed himself. He could watch television. But he couldn’t remember our childhood. He couldn’t plan a day. He lost his curiosity. He lost his anger. He lost his joy. They told us it was a success. For sixteen years, we believed that. Now I look at him and I don’t know what they did.”

The language here is not medical. It is personal. It measures success not by the reduction of symptoms, but by the preservation of a self. It describes loss in terms of memory, curiosity, and joy—attributes that had no place in the surgeon’s follow-up forms.

Another fragment appeared in a 1976 interview in a Sacramento newspaper with a man whose wife had been operated on in 1962 for postpartum depression. “She was easier to manage after,” he said. “She didn’t cry as much. She didn’t complain.”

But she also didn’t laugh. She didn’t initiate anything. She became a passenger in her own life. He didn’t understand what had happened until years later, when he read about what that surgery actually did. He thought they had fixed something. “They had removed something.”

These accounts shared a common structure: a period of initial relief, followed by a slow-dawning realization that the relief was a form of erasure. The realization often took years, even decades, to crystallize. It required a shift in the family’s own understanding, a move away from the medical framework they had been given.

It required them to stop seeing the patient as “cured” and start seeing him as “changed.” That shift was possible only when the medical authority that had defined “cured” lost its absolute power.

The media played a hesitant role. Early newspaper articles about these testimonies were often framed as human interest stories, not as investigations. They treated the operation as a curious historical practice, like phrenology or bloodletting.

They did not connect the individual story to the systemic practice of lobotomy. They did not trace the lines of responsibility from the surgeon to the hospitals that hosted him. They presented the survivor as a singular case of medical oddity.

But the space was open. The voices, however scattered, were now part of the record. They were no longer confined to private letters to the surgeon or to internal hospital files. They were in newspapers. They were in local television interviews. They were in the early, tentative books of the anti-psychiatry movement.

This was the beginning of the counter-narrative. It was weak, anecdotal, and disorganized. But it existed. And it would grow.

The letters began arriving at the Freeman home office in the late 1960s, after his surgical privileges were revoked but before his death. They continued to be forwarded to the archives after 1972. They were not sent to newspapers or to medical journals. They were sent directly to him.

Their purpose was not public exposure. It was private explanation. They were pleas for understanding, written by people who had lived for years with the consequences of his work and who could no longer reconcile those consequences with the official story of therapeutic success.

The archives at George Washington University hold hundreds of these letters. They are filed not by patient name, but by date. They form a chronological record of delayed realization. The earliest, from the 1950s, are often expressions of gratitude. The later ones, from the 1970s, are something else entirely.

A letter dated March 12, 1971, from a woman in Oregon, concerns her brother, who underwent a transorbital lobotomy performed by Freeman at the Oregon State Hospital in 1954. She wrote:

“Dear Dr. Freeman,”

You operated on my brother, Robert, at the state hospital in Salem in June of 1954. I was 14 at the time. The doctors told my parents you were a famous surgeon from Washington, D.C. And that this operation would calm his nerves and allow him to come home. He came home. He was calm. For years, we considered it a blessing. He did not have the outbursts that had led to his hospitalization. He worked simple jobs. He lived with my parents.

My parents died last year. I am now his guardian. I have been trying to understand his condition as I arrange for his care. I have read some articles about the lobotomy procedure. I do not understand the medical details. But I understand the result.

Robert is 48 years old. His emotional range is that of a young child. He shows no initiative. He has no memory of our life before 1954. He cannot follow a simple story in a book or on television. He exists in a state of perpetual present. He is docile. He is manageable. He is not a person.

I am writing to ask you: What exactly did you do to him? What parts of his brain were removed or damaged? Is this condition what you intended? Is there any possibility of improvement? I am not writing to blame you. I am writing because I need to know what happened to my brother. “I need to know if this was the planned outcome.”

The letter is typed on a single sheet of plain paper. There is no record of a reply.

The tone is not accusatory. It is forensic. She is gathering evidence. She is trying to reconstruct a medical event that occurred seventeen years earlier, an event whose full implications have only now become clear to her.

The delay is critical. It took the death of her parents, and the assumption of full responsibility for her brother’s care, to force the reevaluation. The “blessing” of his calmness now appeared as the mechanism of his reduction.

The success measured by his return home now appeared as the cause of his permanent dependency. Her questions are technical: “What parts of his brain were removed or damaged?” But their impetus is moral. She is seeking the logic behind the result.

Another letter, from October 1974, was sent by a man in Iowa regarding his wife. A surgeon had performed the surgery on her in 1960 for what was diagnosed as severe anxiety.

“Dr. Freeman,”

My wife, Eleanor, was your patient in 1960. After the operation, her anxiety diminished. She was able to resume some household tasks. Our doctor here told us you had performed a miracle.

The miracle ended in 1968. Eleanor began to lose the ability to perform those tasks. She would stand in the kitchen for an hour, unable to decide how to begin making a sandwich. She would forget the steps of dressing herself. Her personality, which had been muted but present after the surgery, seemed to fade further. She is now like a photograph that is slowly bleaching in the sun. She is there, but she is losing definition.

Our current neurologist here in Iowa has suggested that the surgery may have caused a progressive atrophy or that the initial damage may have made her more vulnerable to later deterioration. He does not have details of the procedure. I am writing to request any records you may have. I need to know what was done so that I can understand what is happening now.

I am not a medical man. I am a teacher. I feel I have been living with a mystery for fourteen years. “I would like to solve it.”

Again, the primary motive is not blame, but comprehension. The metaphor—a photograph bleaching in the sun—is a layman’s attempt to describe a neurological decline he cannot otherwise name.

He is confronting a mystery that his current doctors cannot solve because the original surgical details are missing. The letter frames the treatment not as a discrete event, but as a causative agent in an ongoing process of loss. The “miracle” has a half-life.

A third letter, handwritten in 1973 from a mother in Tennessee, is less forensic and more raw. Her son had been lobotomized by Freeman during one of his tours of Southern state hospitals in 1955.

“Dear Dr. Freeman,”

You saw my son, Michael, for ten minutes at the Mountain View Hospital in 1955. You decided he needed the operation. You did it the next day. He was 19. He had been agitated. He was difficult. After the operation, he was quiet. He was placid. He has remained so for eighteen years.

He is now 37. He lives in a state home. He cannot hold a conversation. He cannot tell me what he wants for breakfast. He smiles when I visit, but it is a smile without a source. It is like a reflex. He has no desires. He has no fears. He has no memories of his childhood. He has no curiosity about the world. He is a blank page.

I have prayed for answers. I have asked God why my son is like this. Now I am asking you. What did you do to him? What did you intend to make him? Did you know he would end up like this? Did you care?

I am an old woman. I will die soon. He will remain in that home, smiling that empty smile, for the rest of his life. I need to know before I die what happened. “I need you to tell me.”

This letter moves from description to direct interrogation. “Did you know he would end up like this? Did you care?” The questions are no longer about surgical technique. They are about intention and foresight.

They imply a moral accountability that transcends medical outcome. The mother’s timeline is stark: the ten-minute consultation, the procedure the next day, the eighteen-year result. The compression of that timeline is the core of her anguish. She is not seeking medical records. She is seeking an answer to a spiritual question: “What did you intend to make him?”

The surgeon's responses to these letters, where they exist, are brief and professional. He often referred the writers to his published papers for technical details. He reiterated the statistical success rates of the procedure. He expressed sympathy for their ongoing difficulties but did not engage with the moral questions.

In a reply to the Tennessee mother, dated April 1973, he wrote: “The transorbital lobotomy was designed to reduce suffering for patients and their families. It succeeded in this aim for many thousands. Individual outcomes vary. I regret that your son’s case has been a difficult one.” He enclosed a reprint of his 1960 article, “The Late Results of Transorbital Lobotomy.”

The exchange reveals the fundamental disconnect. The families were writing from the realm of individual, lifelong experience. He replied from the realm of aggregate, statistical data. He spoke of “many thousands.” They spoke of one. He spoke of “aims.” They spoke of results. He offered published articles. They wanted personal answers.

These letters, and the hundreds like them, represent the first direct confrontation between the lived experience of the procedure and its medical justification. They were private, unpublicized appeals. They did not change policy. They did not trigger investigations.

But they created a documentary record of aftermath that was entirely separate from the surgical record. They translated the medical outcome—the “quiet” patient, the “placid” behavior—into the human cost: the blank page, the bleached photograph, the perpetual present.

This was the counter-narrative in its earliest, most intimate form. It was not a public accusation. It was a private correspondence between a family and a surgeon, conducted across a gap of years and understanding.

The families were not activists. They were witnesses. They were compiling evidence for a case they did not yet know they were building. Their letters were the first affidavits.

Howard Dully’s life after December 1960 was a study in that fractured identity. The twelve-year-old boy who had been driven to the hospital by his stepmother, anesthetized with electroconvulsive therapy, and subjected to his transorbital procedure, awoke into a different world.

In his 2007 memoir, *My Lobotomy*, he described the immediate aftermath not with dramatic clarity, but with the hazy confusion that would become his norm. “I remember coming home,” he wrote. “I remember the headaches. I remember feeling dull. But I don’t remember feeling angry or sad about it. I just didn’t.”

The procedure, performed at his request as a solution to Dully’s supposed defiance and emotional problems, did not produce the tranquil child his stepmother had envisioned. Instead, it produced a vacancy. He was not violent. He was not agitated. He was not himself.

He moved through his teenage years in a state of emotional and cognitive attenuation. His memory, a faculty his surgery specifically targeted, became unreliable. He could not follow sequences. He lost track of conversations. He would begin tasks and forget their purpose. “I’d be fixing a bike,” he recalled, “and then just stop. I wouldn’t remember why I was doing it, or what the next step was. I’d just stand there, holding a wrench, until someone yelled at me to move.”

The family structure around him dissolved. His father and stepmother, dissatisfied with the results—the boy was not the docile, obedient creature they had expected—sent him to a series of institutions: juvenile halls, state hospitals, boarding schools.

He was not a patient there, but a resident. A problem to be managed. He worked odd jobs: janitor, busboy, truck loader. He could perform manual labor if the steps were simple and repetitive. He could not plan. He could not anticipate. He lived in a perpetual present, a condition echoed in the letters from other families. “My life was day-to-day,” Dully wrote. “I didn’t think about tomorrow. Tomorrow wasn’t a concept that held any meaning. I just did what I was told to do right now.”

The emotional flatness was bone-deep. He did not experience rage or deep sorrow. He also did not experience joy or anticipation. “Things happened,” he noted. “I got married. I had kids. I got divorced. I moved. But it all felt like it was happening to someone else. I was watching it. I wasn’t in it.”

This dissociation, this sense of being a spectator to his own life, was the core impact of the procedure. The surgery had not cured a disease. It had severed the connections that allow a person to integrate experience into a coherent self. He could react to stimuli. He could not form a narrative of his life.

Through the 1970s and 1980s, Dully held jobs, maintained relationships of a sort, and appeared, to outsiders, functional. He was not institutionalized. He was not visibly disabled. He was, in the parlance of the time, “adjusted.”

But internally, he lived with a haunting, inarticulate sense of wrongness. He knew something had been done to him. He knew it had happened when he was twelve. He knew it was called a lobotomy. But he did not know what that meant. The medical facts were absent. “I thought it was like an appendectomy,” he wrote. “They took out a bad part. I figured the bad part was my behavior. I didn’t think about my brain.”

The stigma was internalized. He did not speak about the surgery with friends or later with his wife. It was a secret, a mark of shame. He assumed he was uniquely damaged.

He had no context. He did not know that thousands of others had undergone the same procedure. He did not know that his surgeon had performed it on children as young as four. He did not know the specific brain pathways that had been destroyed. His understanding was mythic: a dark event in childhood that had made him different.

This period of silent confusion, lasting nearly thirty years, is critical. It illustrates the lag between the surgical event and the personal reckoning. For Dully, the operation was not a historical fact he could research. It was a personal fog he inhabited.

The questions formed slowly, over years, prompted not by external investigation but by the accumulating weight of his own lived experience. Why could he not remember his childhood? Why did emotions feel distant? Why did he have no sense of future?

These questions were not philosophical. They were practical. They emerged from failed relationships, from employment difficulties, and from the puzzled reactions of those who knew him. “People would say, ‘Howard, you’re not really here,’” he recalled. “I didn’t know what they meant. I was standing right there.”

The dawning investigation was, therefore, intensely personal long before it became archival. In the 1980s, before he ever sought his medical files, Dully began a casual, almost unconscious search for clues.

He would mention the procedure to a doctor during a physical, hoping for explanation. The doctor would shrug, unfamiliar with the details. He would read popular articles about psychiatry, looking for references.

They were scarce. The operation had been relegated to the history books, treated as a curious, obsolete practice. The articles did not describe the long-term cognitive effects. They did not profile survivors. They discussed the surgeon as a historical figure, not as the architect of individual lives.

Without the language of neurology or psychiatry, Dully used the language of sensation. He described his mind as “cloudy.” He described his memory as “a room with missing furniture.” He described his emotions as “flat, like a tire with no air.”

These metaphors were his only tools for self-diagnosis. They were inadequate for understanding cause, but they were precise in describing effect. The fracture was between his experience and the available explanations for it. He lived in a gap.

This gap—between the private reality of the survivor and the public history of the procedure—defined the post-lobotomy decades for many. They were not activists. They were not patients in ongoing treatment. They were individuals navigating a world with a hidden injury.

The injury was not physical disability. It was cognitive and emotional limitation. It was a reduced capacity to form a self. The world interpreted this limitation as laziness, as lack of ambition, or as oddness. The individual interpreted it as personal failure. The medical establishment, having moved on to pharmacology, offered no framework for understanding it.

For Dully, the turning point would come in the 1990s, with a radio documentary and the decision to request his files. But the groundwork for that investigation was laid in the silent, confused decades before.

The sense of wrongness accumulated. The metaphors for his condition solidified. The questions became more urgent. He was, without knowing it, compiling his own evidence. He was living the counter-narrative before he had ever heard the term.

The Kennedy family had guarded the truth for decades. In her 1974 memoir, *Times to Remember*, Rose Kennedy mentioned her daughter Rosemary’s “mental retardation” and her residence at St. Coletta’s, a Wisconsin school for the exceptional.

She did not mention the surgery. The word was absent. The event was erased. Biographers working on the Kennedy dynasty in the late 1970s encountered whispers and veiled references. They found a 1941 gap in Rosemary’s known whereabouts. They discovered cryptic notes in archived family correspondence.

The full story emerged not from a single exposé, but through a slow accretion of details across multiple books and articles throughout the early 1980s.

The first definitive public account came in 1982, with the publication of Laurence Leamer’s *The Kennedy Women*. Leamer, drawing on interviews with former family staff and access to private letters, detailed the procedure.

He reported that in 1941, Joseph Kennedy had consulted doctors. He had been assured a new neurosurgical procedure could calm her erratic behavior. On November 17, 1941, at George Washington University Hospital, Walter Freeman and James Watts performed a prefrontal lobotomy on the twenty-three-year-old woman.

The result was catastrophic. Rosemary lost the ability to walk independently. Her speech regressed to a few words. She required full-time institutional care. The family placed her at St. Coletta’s, where she remained for the rest of her life. Leamer’s account was stark. It named the surgeons. It named the date. It described the outcome.

The news did not break as a front-page scandal. It unfolded as a chapter in a biography. But its impact was distinct. Previous stories of survivors had involved anonymous patients in state hospitals or private citizens.

Their narratives were tragic, but they were private tragedies. Rosemary Kennedy was a public figure, albeit a hidden one. She was sister to a president, to senators. Her family was an American institution.

The practice, when attached to that name, ceased to be a medical curiosity. It became a historical crime.

Media reaction, as traced through newspaper archives from 1982 to 1985, moved from factual reporting to moral indictment. Initial articles summarized Leamer’s findings.

Later pieces framed the event as a betrayal. A 1983 *Washington Post* editorial noted, “The surgery performed on Rosemary Kennedy was not a therapeutic intervention. It was a family management strategy, executed by respected physicians on a vulnerable young woman.”

The vocabulary shifted. The procedure was no longer termed a “lobotomy” in neutral medical language. It was called “the mutilation of Rosemary Kennedy.” It was described as “a surgical solution to a social problem.”

The case provided a recognizable face for the horror of lobotomy. Photographs of Rosemary before the surgery—a smiling, vibrant young woman at family gatherings—were contrasted with the reality of her post-operative life.

The narrative was clear: a powerful family, seeking to control an inconvenient member, had authorized a radical, irreversible brain surgery. The trusted medical establishment had provided the service. The result was not a cure, but a destruction. This story fit a template Americans understood: corruption of power, victimization of the weak, and collusion of authority.

It also exposed the gendered dimension of the practice. Rosemary’s “mood swings” and “difficult behavior” were the cited reasons. These were subjective, behavioral judgments, not psychotic symptoms.

Her lobotomy was not undertaken to relieve hallucinations or delusions. It was undertaken to make her more placid, more manageable, and more acceptable within the family’s social orbit.

As a 1984 *New York Times* analysis put it, “The surgery on Rosemary Kennedy reveals the procedure's function as a tool of social control, particularly for women whose emotions or independence were deemed problematic.”

Their accounts created an unanswerable ethical question. If a procedure leaves a person unable to form a coherent memory, to experience deep emotion, or to plan for a future—but makes them easier to manage—is that a therapeutic outcome? The families' letters offered a unanimous, anguished rejection. The historical record, eventually, agreed.
