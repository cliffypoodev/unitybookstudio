Howard Dully was twelve years old in 1960. He lived in a ranch-style house on a quiet street in San Jose, California. His world was a boy’s world: bicycles, baseball, the smell of cut grass, and the sound of a lawnmower on a Saturday morning.

His father, Lou Dully, worked as a furniture salesman. His stepmother, Lucille, managed the household. In his memoir, written decades later, Howard described a childhood of ordinary friction.

He was not a docile child. He argued about chores and sometimes lied to avoid trouble. He pocketed change from his father’s dresser to buy candy. He would, on occasion, sneak out of his room after bedtime.

These were the standard rebellions of a pre-adolescent boy navigating a blended family. It was a territory marked by unspoken rules and contested authority. The problem, as it emerged in Lucille Dully’s letters and later testimony, was not one of violence or psychosis.

It was a problem of will. Howard did not obey quickly enough. He did not conform to her expectations of a tidy, compliant son. He was, she wrote to doctors, “difficult.” He was “sullen” and “defiant.”

In her descriptions, a normal boy’s resistance became a catalog of symptoms. She documented instances of what she called “stubborn silence”—his refusal to speak when chastised. She noted his “moodiness” and his “inability to settle.”

She reported that he sometimes stared into space, a behavior she interpreted as a form of dissociation. These observations, recorded in letters and consultation notes, formed the core of her evidence. She was building a case.

Lou Dully, according to his son’s account, occupied a middle ground. He was a man of the postwar era, practical and weary. He worked long hours and wanted peace in his home.

He heard his wife’s complaints about the boy's behavior and saw the resistance. The conflict between his son and his wife created a constant, low-grade tension in the house. He sought solutions.

He tried discipline and reasoning — neither produced the calm his wife desired. When the stepmother began suggesting that the boy’s problems might be medical, not merely behavioral, the father listened.

The idea entered the household not as a dramatic pronouncement, but as a gradual shift in language. What was once “acting up” became “a problem.” What was once “stubborn” became “sick.”

Lucille Dully’s pursuit of a medical answer was methodical. She began with local pediatricians and child psychologists. The diagnoses she received were varied and mild: adjustment difficulties, typical adolescent rebellion, and suggestions for family counseling.

These recommendations did not satisfy her. She wanted a definitive correction — in 1960, she learned of Dr. Walter Freeman’s work.

Freeman’s name still carried the faded prestige of the Nobel. His transorbital surgery had been promoted as a tool for managing disordered behavior. It was framed not as a last resort for the violently insane, but as a modern, surgical adjustment for the troublesome.

Freeman himself, in his lectures, had begun to speak of its application in cases of “intractable personality problems” in adolescents. Lucille wrote to the doctor.

Her letters, preserved in Freeman’s files, were precise. She listed the boy’s behaviors and described her own frustrations. She asked if the operation might offer a solution.

The doctor’s reply was encouraging. He suggested an evaluation. He did not dismiss her concerns or caution her about the irreversible nature of the surgery.

He framed it as a potential remedy, a way to restore harmony. The correspondence between a stepmother in San Jose and a neurologist on the other side of the country established a partnership.

It was a partnership built on a shared premise: that a child’s willfulness was a pathology that could, and should, be surgically removed. Howard Dully, unaware of these letters, continued his life.

He rode his bike and played baseball with friends. He did his homework, sometimes reluctantly. He listened to rock and roll on the radio. He was, in his own recollection, a boy puzzled by the growing distance in his home.

He felt his stepmother’s disapproval as a constant, cold pressure. He sensed his father’s helplessness. He did not understand that his behavior was being translated, line by line, into a clinical narrative. He did not know that his childhood was being documented as a case history.

The evaluation appointments began. The stepmother took the child to see the specialist at his clinic. The doctor, in his records, recorded the patient's demeanor.

He noted his resistant attitude. He observed the lack of rapport between the child and his stepmother. He performed a standard neurological exam.

He asked him questions. The patient's answers, the doctor wrote, were sometimes evasive or defiant. His interpretation of these interactions was categorical. He did not see a child caught in a difficult family dynamic. He saw a patient. He saw a candidate.

In one of the consultations, the specialist proposed the transorbital operation to the parents. He explained it not as a brain surgery, but as a “procedure.”

He used the word “adjustment.” He described it as quick. He said it would reduce the son's emotional excesses and promote tranquility.

He presented it as a logical step. The father, faced with his wife’s insistence and the authority of a doctor who spoke with the confidence of a pioneer, agreed.

The decision was made not in a moment of crisis, but in a series of quiet conversations. They framed it as a medical necessity. It was a parental authorization for a surgery that would sever the neural pathways of their son’s frontal lobes.

The son was told he was going to the hospital for tests. He was twelve. He believed what his parents told him.

On the morning of the operation, he was taken to a hospital in San Jose. He was prepared for the work. He was not told what would happen.

The doctor's notes from that day are clinical. They list the patient’s age, weight, and preoperative state. They do not record the child's questions, if he asked any. They do not mention fear.

The path from a suburban home in San Jose to the operating table was not a straight line. It was a winding road of referrals and rejections, a trail documented in letters, appointment records, and insurance forms.

Lucille Dully began her search within the conventional medical system. She first consulted their family pediatrician — the child, after examining him, showed no physiological abnormality.

The pediatrician suggested the boy’s behavior might be a reaction to the new family structure. The stepmother dismissed this. She next sought a child psychologist.

The psychologist diagnosed the pre-adolescent with “adjustment reaction of childhood,” a mild classification that recommended therapy and parental guidance. The stepmother found this insufficient. She wanted a label that carried weight, a diagnosis that mandated decisive action.

Her pursuit intensified. She took the boy to a psychiatrist at a local clinic. The psychiatrist’s notes described him as “a somewhat oppositional pre-adolescent with no signs of thought disorder or psychosis.”

The recommended treatment was again behavioral modification and family counseling. The stepmother’s letters from this period reveal her mounting frustration.

She wrote to a friend that the doctors were missing the root of the problem. She began to describe the boy's behavior in more severe terms. In a letter to another specialist, she no longer called him “defiant.” She called him “unmanageable.”

She wrote that he had “violent moods,” though no record from any school or neighbor corroborated violence. She claimed he was a danger to himself, citing the instance where he had ridden his bicycle beyond the allowed neighborhood boundaries.

The language escalated, matching her growing determination to find a definitive intervention. It was during this search that she encountered the concept of the surgery.

It was not through medical journals, but through popular media. Articles in magazines like Time and Reader’s Digest had for years framed the neurologist's transorbital procedure as a modern miracle.

These stories often used the word “simple.” They described it as an “office procedure.” They spoke of patients becoming “calm” and “cooperative.”

For a woman seeking order, these descriptions held a powerful appeal. The surgery, in this popular imagination, was not a savage mutilation of the mind. It was a surgical tuning, a correction of emotional wiring. It promised a permanent solution.

The stepmother then made a deliberate pivot. She bypassed the local psychiatric consensus and sought out the source. She wrote directly to the specialist.

Her letter was a detailed petition. She listed the boy's behaviors: his disobedience, his lying, his “staring spells.” She expressed her exhaustion.

She asked if his treatment could help correct her son. The specialist's reply was prompt and welcoming. He did not question her characterization of the child. He did not suggest a second opinion from a child psychiatrist.

He invited her to bring the patient to his clinic for an evaluation. In his response, he used the term “intractable behavior.” He wrote that the transorbital surgery had been successful in similar cases. He framed it as a legitimate medical option.

This correspondence marked a critical juncture. A stepmother, acting on her own authority and against the advice of several doctors, had now engaged with the foremost lobotomist in America.

The specialist, for his part, saw not a troubled family dynamic, but a potential case. His practice had declined by 1960. The medical establishment was turning away from psychosurgery.

State hospitals were no longer welcoming his roadshows. Patients like the Dully boy—private, paying, and presented as suffering from personality disorders rather than psychosis—represented a new market.

The neurologist's notes from his first meeting with the family confirm this. He recorded the stepmother's complaints verbatim. He noted the father's passive agreement.

He observed the son's resentful demeanor. In his assessment, the specialist diagnosed the child with “childhood schizophrenia,” a drastic and unsupported leap from the earlier, milder diagnoses.

This new label provided the necessary medical justification for surgery. The stepmother, armed with this authority, now faced the final obstacle: her husband’s consent.

Lou Dully was a man torn. He loved his son. He also wanted peace in his marriage. The specialist's consultation was masterful in its persuasion.

He did not speak of cutting brain fibers. He spoke of “rebalancing” the emotions. He described the treatment as taking only ten minutes.

He said the boy would be home in a few days. He assured the father that the risks were minimal. He presented it not as a radical, irreversible brain surgery, but as a minor psychiatric intervention.

Lou Dully, faced with his wife’s steadfast conviction and the imposing figure of a famous doctor, signed the consent form. The decision was clinical, quiet, and final.

The paperwork was completed — the date was set. The twelve-year-old was scheduled for a transorbital surgery on December 16, 1960, at Peninsula Hospital in San Mateo, California.

The consultation took place in the specialist's clinic. The room was standard for a neurologist’s office: a desk, chairs, medical diagrams on the wall, the smell of antiseptic and paper.

The doctor's records provide a skeletal record of the encounter. They do not capture the room’s temperature, the quality of the light, or the tone of the voices. They record facts, impressions, and conclusions.

The doctor began with observation. He watched the family enter. He noted the physical bearing of each person. The stepmother, he wrote, was anxious and determined.

The father was quiet and agreeable. The child was robust and healthy — his physique was normal for his age. There was no sign of physical illness or neurological deficit.

The neurologist's initial examination followed his standard protocol. He checked the child's reflexes. He tested his coordination. He examined his eyes. All results were normal. The boy was, from a purely physiological standpoint, perfectly ordinary.

The evaluation then shifted from the body to behavior. The doctor engaged the patient in conversation. He asked him questions about his home life, his school, and his interests.

The boy's responses, as he recorded them, were guarded. The patient was evasive and defiant. He showed a lack of rapport with his stepmother.

The neurologist did not interpret this as a natural reaction of a child in a stressful situation. He interpreted it as evidence of a disorder. In his records, he wrote that the boy displayed “emotional incontinence” and “willful resistance.”

These were not medical diagnoses from a standard psychiatric textbook. They were the doctor's own terms, categories he had developed over decades to describe patients he considered suitable for surgery.

The critical moment came when he turned his attention to the stepmother. He listened to her recount the boy's behaviors. She described his disobedience, his lying, and his occasional truancy.

She spoke of his staring spells and his sullen silence. The neurologist did not challenge her descriptions. He did not ask for corroboration from school reports or from the father.

He accepted her account as the primary clinical data. This was consistent with his methodology. He often relied on family members as the chief reporters of a patient’s symptoms. Their frustration was, for him, a symptom in itself.

He then explained the transorbital surgery — his explanation was deliberate in its language. He did not use the word “surgery.” He called it a “procedure.”

He did not describe the insertion of an instrument through the eye socket. He spoke of “adjusting the emotional circuits.” He emphasized its speed and simplicity.

He emphasized its outcome. It promotes tranquility, he would assure them. It reduces the excesses of emotion. He framed it as a technical solution to a technical problem.

The problem was the child's willfulness. The solution was a calibrated disruption of his frontal lobes. The father’s role in this consultation was passive.

His writings mention him only once: “Father agrees.” The father's own recollections were vague. He remembered the neurologist as a confident, persuasive man.

He remembered his wife’s conviction. He remembered his own desire for the conflict to end. He did not remember the specialist detailing the risks of the treatment: the possibility of hemorrhage, infection, permanent apathy, or death.

The consent process did not include a thorough discussion of complications. The doctor believed the benefits outweighed the risks, and he presented that belief as fact.

The specialist's final act in the consultation was to provide a diagnosis. The diagnosis was necessary for insurance purposes and for surgical justification.

The earlier evaluations by pediatricians and psychologists had offered mild labels. His diagnosis was stark. He labeled the boy with “childhood schizophrenia.”

This diagnosis was not supported by any standard symptomatology. The patient exhibited no hallucinations, no delusions, and no disorganized speech.

The doctor's use of the term was a bureaucratic and medical convenience. Childhood schizophrenia was a recognized, severe condition. It justified radical intervention. It transformed the boy's willfulness from a behavioral issue into a brain disease.

The consultation ended with the neurologist scheduling the stay. He told the parents that the boy would need to be hospitalized for a few days.

He explained that electroconvulsive therapy would be used to induce a brief unconsciousness. He assured them that the son would feel no pain during the operation itself.

He was matter-of-fact and reassuring. He presented the operation not as a last resort for the hopelessly insane, but as a logical next step for a manageable problem.

He sold it as a cure. What the records do not show is the boy’s perspective. The son's own memory of the meeting is fragmentary.

He remembered the office as big and cold. He remembered the doctor as a tall man with a serious face. He remembered his stepmother talking a lot.

He remembered the father sitting quietly. He did not understand the purpose of the visit. He thought it was another doctor’s appointment, another attempt to figure out why he was difficult.

He did not know he was being evaluated for brain surgery. He did not know that his childhood was being formally pathologized. He was a subject in a conversation that would decide his future, but he was not a participant in it.

The dynamics of the consultation reveal the core mechanics of the specialist's practice. He was not a detached clinician weighing objective evidence.

He was a salesman — his product was the lobotomy. His customers were families in distress. His evidence was often the distress itself.

He listened to the complaints—the frustration of a stepmother, the helplessness of a father—and he offered a solution. He translated their domestic strife into medical terminology.

Willfulness became “emotional incontinence.” Stubbornness became “childhood schizophrenia.” The solution he offered was permanent, irreversible, and brutal.

But he packaged it in the language of modern medicine: simple, quick, and effective. The consultation also reveals the power imbalance.

The doctor was a nationally known figure. The parents were a middle-class family from San Jose. They had no medical expertise. They were seeking help from an authority.

His authority was unquestioned by them. His diagnosis was accepted. His recommendation was followed. There was no second opinion sought.

The process moved with a swift efficiency from complaint to diagnosis to surgical schedule. The paperwork generated from that consultation was routine.

The specialist filled out a standard evaluation form. He wrote the diagnosis: childhood schizophrenia. He listed the recommended surgery: transorbital lobotomy.

He noted the patient’s age: 12. He signed it — the stepmother provided the insurance information. The father signed the consent forms.

The documents were clean, professional, and complete. They created a legal and medical pathway for what would happen next. They turned a family problem into a surgical case.

They authorized the destruction of a part of a boy’s brain to solve a problem of behavior. The meeting lasted perhaps an hour. At its conclusion, the parents left the clinic.

The twelve-year-old went back to his life, unaware of what had been decided. The father felt a hope for peace. The neurologist added another name to his surgical schedule. He had a new patient. The system had worked.

The morning of December 16, 1960, was cold and clear. The twelve-year-old was taken to Peninsula Hospital in San Mateo. He was admitted to a standard room.

He was told he was undergoing a series of tests. He was not told the nature of the tests. He trusted his parents and the doctors — he did not ask questions.

The preparatory steps followed the established protocol. First, an electroconvulsive therapy machine was brought into the room.

The technique used ECT not for therapeutic effect, but as a crude anesthetic. It induced a seizure, rendering the patient unconscious for the brief duration of the surgery.

A nurse placed electrodes on the boy's temples. The machine was activated. The current surged through his mind — his body convulsed.

The seizure lasted approximately thirty seconds. When it ceased, he was unconscious. He was not under general anesthesia; he was in a postictal state, a temporary coma induced by the electrical storm in his own cortex.

The surgeon waited for the convulsions to subside. He then positioned himself at the head of the bed. He took his instrument from a sterilized tray.

The tool was a leucotome, specifically a modified version called an orbitoclast. It was a slender, stainless steel rod about nine inches long. Its tip was sharp. Its handle was designed for a grip that allowed a precise, levering motion.

He lifted the tool. He positioned it above the boy's left eye. He placed his thumb on the upper eyelid, pushing it back. He inserted the tip of the orbitoclast at the point where the eyelid met the orbital bone, just inside the corner of the eye.

He did not make an incision. He used the steel point to pierce the thin tissue. He then angled the tool upward, aiming for the roof of the orbit.

With a steady pressure, he pushed the orbitoclast through the orbital cavity, past the eye itself, toward the bony plate separating the eye socket from his mind.

He tapped the tool’s tip against the bone. The sound, he wrote in his surgical log, was a “light percussion.” It confirmed the tool had reached the barrier.

He then changed his grip. He applied firm, controlled force. The tool pierced the thin orbital plate — it entered the frontal lobe of his mind.

The neurologist estimated the depth of insertion: approximately five centimeters. He now performed the core action of the surgery. He swept the tool laterally, moving it side-to-side in a fanning motion.

This motion severed the white matter fibers connecting the prefrontal cortex to the rest of the cortex. He levered the tool upward and downward, cutting a wider swath.

The goal was to disconnect the frontal lobes, the seat of personality and emotional regulation. The tissue was soft. The tool moved through it with minimal resistance.

The neurologist completed the fanning motion on the left side. He withdrew the rod from the eye socket. A small amount of fluid followed the rod out. He wiped the tip with a gauze pad.

He repeated the operation on the right eye. He inserted the orbitoclast at the same anatomical point. He pierced the orbital plate. He inserted the rod to the same depth.

He performed the same fanning, levering motion. The process was symmetrical, methodical, and rapid. The entire bilateral operation, from the first insertion to the final withdrawal, took between seven and ten minutes.

The doctor did not use X-ray guidance. He did not have a neurosurgeon present. He worked from memory and tactile feedback, relying on his experience from thousands of prior operations.

The boy, unconscious from the ECT, did not feel the tool piercing his orbital plates. He did not feel the fanning motion inside his mind. He showed no outward sign of pain.

His body lay still on the bed. His eyes were open while the rod passed through them. His breathing was regular. To an observer, the work appeared almost casual.

There was no open skull. There was no visible incision. There was only a doctor standing over a child, moving a metal rod in a precise, practiced sequence.

When he finished, he wiped the eyelids with gauze. There was minimal bleeding. The puncture sites were small — they would heal within days, leaving no visible scar.

The surgeon checked the boy’s pulse and respiration. He noted that both were stable. He wrote a brief entry in his surgical log: “Bilateral transorbital surgery performed. Patient tolerated the operation well.”

He did not note the patient’s age. He did not note the diagnosis of childhood schizophrenia. He did not note the family’s reasons for consent. He recorded the act itself.

The stay was brief — the son regained consciousness within an hour. His memory of the event was fragmented and surreal.

He recalled waking in a hospital room. His head hurt. His eyes were sore. He felt confused. He did not know what had been done to him.

A nurse gave him water — his parents visited. They told him he had undergone a treatment to help him. They told him he would feel better soon. He asked no questions. The hospital discharged him after two days.

The doctor's follow-up assessment recorded his assessment of the outcome. He wrote that the patient was more cooperative.

He noted that the boy’s emotional outbursts had diminished. He described him as quieter. These observations indicated success.

The surgery had achieved its stated goal: it had reduced the patient’s willful resistance. It had produced tranquility.

The son's own experience of the aftermath was different. He described a heavy internal shift — he felt a distance from himself. His emotions seemed flattened.

His motivations faded. He could follow instructions, but he could not generate initiative. He was, in his own words, “blank.” The world seemed muted.

The lobotomy had not cured a disease. It had altered his personality, severing the connections that made him who he was. The true cost was not measured in surgical complications.

It was measured in the loss of a self. The treatment was complete. The medical record was closed — the specialist moved on to his next case.

The family returned home. The conflict in the household diminished. The child was no longer defiant. He was compliant. The problem, as defined by the stepmother, had been solved.

The method, as defined by Walter Freeman, had worked. The boy paid the price. The doctor's post-operative assessment, preserved in his files at George Washington University, was brief and optimistic.

In a follow-up report dated January 1961, he wrote that the patient was more manageable. He noted the boy’s cooperation had improved.

He concluded the surgery had been successful in moderating the emotional excesses. The language was clinical, detached, and focused entirely on behavioral compliance.

The specialist saw a problem corrected — he did not inquire about the boy’s inner state. The child's own recollection of those first weeks presented a starkly different reality.

He returned home to San Jose with a headache that lasted for days. His eyes felt bruised and tender. A low-grade confusion persisted, a mental fog he could not articulate.

He was not violent or defiant. He was empty. The world seemed muffled, as if he were observing it from behind a thick pane of glass.

The stepmother initially expressed satisfaction. The house was quieter. The battles over chores and homework ceased. The child did what he was told. He spoke less. For her, this was the promised outcome.

The father's reaction was silence. He did not ask the boy how he felt. He did not explain what had happened.

The family routine resumed, but with a new, unspoken tension. The child's changed demeanor was not treated as a medical condition requiring care or rehabilitation.

It was treated as a solved problem. No therapist was consulted — no follow-up psychiatric support was arranged.

The neurologist's instructions were minimal: rest, avoid strenuous activity, and return to school when ready. The lobotomy was framed as an isolated event, not as the beginning of a recovery process.

The physical marks healed quickly. The tiny puncture sites at the corners of his eyes faded within a week, leaving no visible scars. The internal damage was invisible.

The student returned to school in January — teachers noted he was more subdued. He completed his assignments, but his previous spark of engagement was gone.

He drifted through his classes. He spent hours alone in his room — he lacked the initiative to pursue hobbies or friends.

The stepmother interpreted this placidity as proof of the surgery's success. In a letter several months later, she reported that the son was much easier to live with.

She did not mention his withdrawal or his apparent depression. The doctor filed that letter in the boy's case folder.

He considered it corroboration of his own assessment. The medical establishment, from his perspective, had functioned perfectly.

A family complaint had been presented, a diagnosis had been made, a surgical solution had been applied, and a positive behavioral change had been observed.

The loop was closed. The treatment was marked as a therapeutic victory. For the boy, the loop was broken.

The surgery had severed not only neural pathways but his connection to a coherent sense of self. He existed in a state of passive obedience.

The emotional excesses the neurologist sought to moderate were not pathological; they were the normal turbulence of a boy navigating a difficult home life.

Their removal left not a cured child, but a hollowed one. The aftermath was not a period of healing, but of erasure.

The family home became a place where his silence was accepted as peace.
