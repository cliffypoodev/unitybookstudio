In the early years, medicine was a tool of production. By the 1940s, it had become a tool of control. The man who oversaw this evolution did not wear a white coat. He sat behind a large, polished desk in the Irving Thalberg Building at MGM, a portrait of his mother hanging on the wall behind him. Louis B. Mayer, the head of the studio, viewed his contract players not as artists but as capital investments. Their bodies were the machinery of his profit. His authorization of the medical department’s pharmaceutical regimen was not an act of cruelty for its own sake; it was a cold calculation of asset management. A star who could not sleep was a star who could not work the next morning. A star whose weight fluctuated was a star whose face would not match the previous day’s footage. These were not personal problems. They were production delays. And production delays cost money.

Mayer’s philosophy was documented in his own words and in the recollections of those who worked for him. He famously told his story editor, “The most important thing in this business is people. You buy them, you sell them, you own them.” In the context of MGM’s medical files, that statement takes on a literal, clinical meaning. You owned their labor. To own their labor absolutely, you had to manage their biology. A 1937 memo from Mayer to the head of the studio’s legal department, uncovered in the MGM archives, concerned a minor contract player struggling with “nervous exhaustion.” Mayer’s instruction was clear: “Have the doctor see her and fix it. We start shooting on the 15th.” The “fix” was not rest. It was pharmaceutical intervention. The doctor’s mandate came from the top.

The studio mixer, the formal dinner, the weekend party at Mayer’s beach house—these were not merely social events. They were extensions of the soundstage, environments where control could be exerted through more subtle means. Alcohol flowed freely. According to biographies of the era, Mayer himself was a moderate drinker, but he ensured his stars were not. He watched. He noted who became loose-tongued after three martinis, who became sentimental, who became aggressive. This intelligence was valuable. An actor revealed a gambling debt or a family scandal over cocktails; that information was filed away in the studio’s legal department, a potential lever for future contract negotiations or a weapon to quash dissent.

But the real control lay in the aftermath. The “morning after” was a scheduled event. Call sheets from the period show shooting often began at 6:00 a.m. A star who had been encouraged to drink heavily at a studio function the night before would arrive on set hungover, shaky, unfit for the camera. This is where the medical department completed the cycle. Studio drivers would often collect actors from their homes. If the actor was visibly ill from the previous night’s indulgences, the driver’s instructions were to take them not to the set, but to the studio clinic. Dr. Edward Hummel or one of his staff would be waiting. The treatment was not coffee and aspirin. It was an injection—often a combination of vitamins and amphetamine—to counteract the depressant effects of the alcohol. The actor would be delivered to the set alert, focused, and ready for work. They called it a “pick-me-up” or a “sobering shot.” In reality, it was a chemical override, erasing the consequences of the studio’s own social engineering.

This created a devastating feedback loop. The star learned they could drink to excess without professional penalty. The studio’s doctor would always provide the antidote. The doctor’s role thus expanded from managing the demands of production to enabling the destructive behaviors the studio’s culture encouraged. The actor’s dependency shifted subtly—from needing a pill to work, to needing a shot to recover from the lifestyle that work demanded. Mayer understood this dynamic perfectly. In his view, it bound the star more tightly to the studio. Where else would they receive such comprehensive, discreet care? As actor Robert Taylor later remarked in an interview, “Mr. Mayer made you feel he was your father. A very strict father who paid for everything, including the doctor who cleaned up your messes. You owed him everything. And he never let you forget it.”

The ultimate expression of this control was the “morality clause,” a standard provision in studio contracts. It allowed the studio to suspend or terminate an actor for behavior deemed “immoral” or “damaging to the studio’s reputation.” Intoxication in public could trigger it. But intoxication at a studio-sanctioned event, followed by a visit to the studio doctor, was kept off the books. The clause was not a deterrent against vice; it was a selective weapon. It could be invoked against an actor who sought treatment outside the studio system, or who became unreliable despite the doctor’s interventions. It made the studio’s pharmaceutical support not a right, but a privilege that could be revoked. Your health, your ability to function, was conditional on your compliance.

The case of Judy Garland illustrates the end point of this system. By the mid-1940s, her amphetamine and barbiturate use, initiated by the studio, had spiraled. She began to drink heavily. Memoirs from the period describe her arriving at parties already unsteady, then drinking more. The studio’s response was not intervention. It was management. MGM production logs show that on the set of The Pirate in 1947, Garland was frequently late. On days when her condition was particularly poor, a studio car would collect her and take her to the clinic for a “reviving” injection before delivering her to the soundstage. The doctor was not treating an illness. He was patching an asset so filming could continue. Mayer’s personal correspondence from this period, cited in biographer Scott Eyman’s work, reveals his growing frustration not with Garland’s suffering, but with the cost of her delays. The solution, in his mind, was not to remove the chemicals that were destroying her. It was to better regulate their administration.

Thus, the cocktail hour and the doctor’s needle were two points on the same continuum of control. The first created a vulnerability. The second sold the cure. Both transactions increased the studio’s leverage. Louis B. Mayer did not invent this system. He industrialized it. He authorized the medical department to function as a repair shop for the human machinery he owned. The doctor’s office became the antechamber where the consequences of the studio’s own culture were chemically erased, leaving the star indebted, dependent, and ready for their close-up. The party was just another scene, and the hangover was a problem for the prop department to fix before the cameras rolled.

Dr. Edward J. Hummel was, by all accounts, a kindly man. Photographs from the MGM archives show a bespectacled, round-faced physician in a crisp white coat, often smiling. He had an office on the studio lot, part of a small clinic equipped to handle everything from a splinter to a nervous breakdown. His primary employer was not the collective health of the actors, but the Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer corporation. His job, as defined by the production schedule, was to keep the machinery running. When that machinery was a child’s body, the medical ethics of the outside world did not apply. Inside the studio gates, a different set of rules governed biology.

The problem was arithmetic. A typical shooting day for a musical like Babes in Arms or Strike Up the Band began at 5:00 a.m. with hair, makeup, and costume. Rehearsals and filming often ran until 9:00 p.m. or later. For the adolescent stars at the center of these productions, this represented a 16-hour shift of intense physical and emotional performance, six days a week. Natural energy could not sustain it. The studio’s solution was chemical. Dr. Hummel was its dispenser.

Mickey Rooney, who began working with Hummel as a child, laid out the system plainly in his later testimony and memoirs. “They’d give you the pills,” he said. “The ‘pepper-uppers.’ Dr. Hummel would come around, check on you, ask how you were holding up. If you were dragging, he’d have a little something for you.” That “little something” was almost always Benzedrine, a potent amphetamine that suppressed appetite and produced a state of intense, focused wakefulness. It was handed out not as a last resort, but as a standard part of the production schedule. The child was not diagnosed with a condition. The set was diagnosed with a slowdown. Hummel wrote the prescription.

Judy Garland’s experience during the filming of The Wizard of Oz in 1938 became the archetype. At sixteen, she was carrying the film. The hours were brutal, the pressure immense. In her later recollections, Garland described the regimen. “They’d give me pills to wake me up, and pills to put me to sleep,” she said. The “wake-up” pills came from Hummel’s supply. According to production logs and the biography by Gerold Frank, Garland would be given Benzedrine in the morning to launch her into the day’s work. At lunch, when the other actors ate, she was given a bowl of soup and more medication to keep her energy steady. Her weight was monitored obsessively; the amphetamines ensured she had no appetite to sabotage that control.

Hummel did not act in a vacuum. His orders flowed from the same logic that governed Mayer’s memos. A 1939 internal memo from The Wizard of Oz production office, preserved in the studio archives, notes a concern about “Miss Garland’s stamina” during the final weeks of shooting. The recommended action: “Consult with Dr. H. for a maintenance regimen.” Hummel provided it. The goal was not health, but continuity. A child’s fatigue was a mechanical failure to be corrected with pharmaceutical fuel.

The doctor’s method was systematic. Interviews with former child actors and studio employees from the period describe a pattern. Hummel would visit the set, a black bag in hand. He observed the performers. A drooping eyelid, a slow reaction to a director’s cue, a yawn suppressed—these were symptoms of a threat to the day’s shooting quota. He would take the child aside, often in a makeshift dressing room or a quiet corner behind the flats. The interaction was paternal, gentle. He would ask how they were feeling. Then he would offer a pill, sometimes with a glass of water, sometimes with a piece of candy. It was presented as help, a professional courtesy. The child, eager to please and conditioned to obey adult authority, especially that of a doctor, would comply.

This was not rogue medicine. Hummel’s prescriptions were logged, the medications sourced from legitimate pharmacies. The system was open, documented, and approved by the studio’s higher management. The tragedy was in its banality. Hummel was not a monster. He was a company man. He believed, as the studio believed, that he was helping these children achieve their dreams, protecting them from failure. The means justified the ends. If a child needed chemical assistance to meet the studio’s demands, then providing it was an act of care.

But the body keeps its own ledger. The amphetamines administered to a growing adolescent triggered a cascade of biological consequences. The central nervous system, jacked into a state of perpetual alert, would crash when the drug wore off. That crash produced anxiety, irritability, and an inability to sleep—which then required the barbiturates Hummel or his colleagues would provide at the end of the day. This created the “up-down” cycle that would define Garland’s life and the lives of others. Hummel’s morning pill created the need for his evening pill. He was treating the side effects of his own primary prescription.

Years later, when the damage was irreversible, the kindly doctor expressed no public remorse. In the studio’s worldview, he had done his job. He had kept the shows on schedule. He had maintained the assets. In a 1965 interview with a film historian, Hummel spoke of his time at MGM with nostalgia. He remembered the stars as “wonderful, talented kids.” He said his role was “supportive.” He never mentioned the Benzedrine. He never had to. The evidence was not in his words, but in the hollowed-out faces of the children in the later reels of their own lives, still chasing the energy he had once manufactured for them with a pill and a smile.

The child’s name was Frances Gumm. By the time she signed her MGM contract at thirteen, she was Judy Garland. The studio did not just give her a new name; it gave her a new biology. Her body, deemed imperfect for the camera, became a site of constant pharmaceutical adjustment. The records show a pattern so consistent it forms a clinical timeline. At fifteen, she was placed on a strict diet by studio executives. At sixteen, during The Wizard of Oz, the diet was chemically enforced. Dr. Hummel’s Benzedrine regimen served a dual purpose: it fueled the impossible hours and guaranteed she would not crave food. At night, the same doctor, or a colleague, provided Phenobarbital to counteract the amphetamine’s effects and force sleep. Her metabolism was no longer a natural process. It was a scheduled event, synchronized with the call sheet.

Garland’s own recollections, sparse and often filtered through a lifetime of subsequent pain, point to the moment she recognized the trap. In a 1964 interview with McCall’s magazine, she said, “They had us working days and nights on end. They’d give us pills to keep us on our feet long after we were exhausted. Then they’d take us to the studio hospital and knock us cold with sleeping pills… Then after four hours they’d wake us up and give us the pills to keep us going again.” Notice the pronoun: “us.” This was not an isolated case. But Garland, carrying the weight of a multi-million-dollar production, existed at the epicenter of the experiment.

The studio’s preoccupation with her weight bordered on the pathological. Memos between Louis B. Mayer and producer Arthur Freed, cited in biographies by Gerold Frank and others, refer to her as “our little hunchback” or “the fat one” when she gained a few pounds. The solution was always the same: more stringent dieting, more frequent visits to the clinic for “appetite suppressants.” The Benzedrine caused her hands to shake. The camera would see that. So the studio costumers designed special sleeves for Dorothy’s gingham dress to minimize the visible tremor. Every problem created by the system was met with a cosmetic or chemical fix, never a cessation of the cause.

The aftermath of The Wizard of Oz did not bring relief. It established a template. For the next decade at MGM, Garland’s life was a series of film roles interspersed with what the studio termed “health rest periods.” These were not vacations. They were pharmaceutical recalibrations. Production files for Meet Me in St. Louis (1944) show multiple days lost to “viral infection” and “nervous fatigue.” Correspondence reveals that during these absences, Garland was under the direct care of studio physicians who adjusted her barbiturate doses to ensure she could return to set. Her illnesses were real—the tremors, the insomnia, the acute anxiety—but their origin was iatrogenic. The doctors were treating diseases they had induced.

Her mother, Ethel Gumm, was complicit. Desperate for her daughter’s success and financially dependent on it, she allowed the studio’s medical intervention, often signing the consent forms. In later years, Garland would speak of this betrayal with bitterness. Lorna Luft, Garland’s daughter, wrote in her memoir Me and My Shadows that her mother felt “sold to the studio, body and soul, by the people who were supposed to protect her.” The protection came in the form of a pill bottle. The studio became the primary caregiver, and its care was addictive.

By 1947, the cycle had become unsustainable. On the set of The Pirate, co-starring her then-husband Vincente Minnelli, Garland’s behavior grew increasingly erratic. She was late, she forgot lines, she collapsed. The MGM logs are a chronicle of crisis management. “Judy Garland report to clinic before scene 42.” “Doctor called to set for JG.” The medicine was no longer a performance enhancer; it was a life-support system for a career. She was fired from Annie Get Your Gun in 1949 and from Royal Wedding in 1950, each time for repeated failures to appear. The press releases cited “nervous exhaustion.” The internal memos cited the failure of the pharmaceutical protocol to maintain a viable worker.

The ultimate consequence of this childhood regimen was a lifelong, agonizing addiction. Garland spent the rest of her life trying to escape the chemical rhythm MGM had hardwired into her nervous system. She cycled through detox clinics and psychiatric hospitals. She would achieve periods of sobriety, only to relapse under the pressure of performance, a pressure that always triggered the old, deep-seated need for the “up” to get through the work and the “down” to survive the crash. In a 1957 letter to a friend, published in the collection Judy Garland: A Portrait in Art & Anecdote, she wrote, “I am so tired of the pills. The waking-up pills and the going-to-sleep pills. I feel like a chemical. I don’t know who I am without them.” The studio had created a perfect dependency: it had made her believe she could not function as Judy Garland without the very substances that were killing her.

Her death in 1969, at forty-seven, was ruled an accidental barbiturate overdose. The coroner found a bloodstream saturated with Seconal. It was not a single doctor’s prescription that killed her, but a lifetime of them. The pattern began in a studio clinic thirty years earlier, when a kindly doctor gave a tired girl a pill to get through one more take. The tragedy of Judy Garland is not merely that of a talented woman destroyed by addiction. It is the tragedy of a system that identified a child’s vitality as a raw material to be processed, accelerated, and controlled for profit. They manufactured her energy, and in doing so, they manufactured her exhaustion. Her entire adult life was a long, slow closing of the ledger that Dr. Hummel had opened on the set of Oz. The final balance was zero.

Mickey Rooney sat before the United States Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency on a summer day in 1961. The hearing room was crowded, the air thick with the smell of polished wood and stale smoke. Flashbulbs popped as he adjusted the microphone. He was not there to discuss his films. He was there to describe how they were made. His testimony, delivered in his familiar, rapid-fire cadence, provided the first public, sworn corroboration of the system Garland had described in private interviews. Where her words conveyed the personal wreckage, his laid bare the factory blueprint.

The senators, led by Chairman Thomas Dodd, were ostensibly investigating the influence of television on youth. Rooney steered them into darker territory. He spoke of his own childhood at MGM, a period spanning the 1930s and ‘40s. “We worked like dogs,” he stated plainly, according to the official transcript. “Twelve, fourteen, sixteen hours a day, six days a week. You’re a kid. You can’t do that on oatmeal.” He described the constant presence of studio handlers, men whose job was to deliver him to the set on time and in a usable state. When natural energy flagged, the handler had a solution. “They called them ‘pepper-uppers’ or ‘pep pills,’” Rooney testified. “They’d just hand you one, like a piece of candy. ‘Here, Mick, this’ll get you through the next scene.’ You didn’t ask what it was. You took it.”

This was not a secret, back-alley transaction. It was an open part of the production logistics, as routine as a costume fitting. The pills came from the studio clinic, dispensed to the handlers for distribution. Rooney’s testimony made clear the systemic nature of the practice. He was not a singular, troubled case like Garland; he was the hyperactive, seemingly indestructible mascot of the MGM family. If he was being drugged to meet the schedule, then no child on the lot was exempt. The “pep pills” were, in his words, “just part of the job.”

The senators pressed for specifics. Rooney named Benzedrine. He described the specific, dry-mouthed, wire-taut feeling it produced, a state where fatigue was impossible but so was stillness. He spoke of the subsequent crash, the “terrible letdown” that made the thought of the next day’s 5 a.m. call a form of dread. And he outlined the inevitable corollary. “So then you’d need something to come down, to get any sleep at all,” he said. “And they had that for you, too.” The cycle was identical to Garland’s, but his description stripped it of tragic glamour. It was simply the machinery of production. A machine needed fuel. A machine needed maintenance. A child actor was a machine.

Rooney’s motivation for testifying was complex. He was, by 1961, grappling with his own financial and personal troubles, and seeking to rehabilitate his image. But the factual content of his statement was concrete and devoid of sentimental flourish. He provided a timeline, linking the administration of amphetamines to specific, grueling productions like the Andy Hardy series and the Mickey/Judy musicals. He named no individual doctors, protecting himself from libel, but he implicated the system in its entirety. “It was the studio,” he said. “The whole way the studio was run. They owned you, and they figured they had a right to tune you up like a car.”

The impact of his words in the hearing room was one of stunned silence, broken only by the frantic scratching of reporters’ pencils. Here was America’s former top box-office star, a living symbol of cheerful adolescence, describing his own childhood as a state-managed chemical experiment. The testimony landed with a weight that academic studies or anonymous rumors could never achieve. It was a first-person witness account, delivered under oath, from inside the fortress of the studio system itself. Newspaper headlines the next day were unequivocal: “Rooney Tells Senate of Studio ‘Pep Pills’ for Child Stars.”

The studio’s reaction, as recorded in trade papers of the era, was a masterclass in deflection. MGM issued no formal statement. Unofficially, executives whispered to columnists that Rooney was a bitter, faded star seeking publicity. They suggested his memory was flawed, clouded by his own later troubles. But they could not erase the transcript. They could not refute the specific, sensory details he offered—the taste of the pill, the feeling of his heart racing under the lights, the hollow exhaustion when the fake energy burned away.

Rooney’s testimony did something Garland’s private suffering never could: it provided a legally admissible point of entry. It transformed the story from a tragic biography into a documented pattern of practice. A senator could now hold up a page of the transcript and demand answers. An investigator could use it as a roadmap to subpoena studio medical records. It broke the code of silence.

In later years, Rooney would vary his account, sometimes downplaying the severity, perhaps out of lingering loyalty or a desire to preserve the myth of his happy childhood. But the 1961 transcript remains. His words that day were not those of a man spinning a tale. They were the flat, declarative sentences of someone stating a fact he assumed everyone already knew. The profound normalcy he ascribed to the practice was the most damning part. He wasn’t describing an abuse. He was describing the job.

His testimony concluded with a moment of bleak clarity. Senator Dodd asked if he believed the practice was harmful. Rooney paused. The transcript notes the pause. Then he said, “Well, Senator, they didn’t give us those pills for our health. They gave them to us so we could work. So, you tell me.” The answer hung in the air, connecting the child’s chemically extended labor directly to the studio’s profit ledger. The pep pill was not medication. It was a tool for extracting value. Mickey Rooney, the eternal optimist on screen, had just defined his own childhood as a form of industrial mining.

The studio system, with its in-house clinics and logged prescriptions, represented the pharmaceutical control of Hollywood’s golden age. It was institutional, almost bureaucratic. But as the studio system itself began to fracture in the 1950s, the demand for chemical performance did not diminish. It simply went freelance. The supply moved from the corporate clinic to the private practice, from the company doctor to the independent entrepreneur. The most successful of these entrepreneurs was a German-born physician named Max Jacobson. His patients called him “Dr. Feelgood.” His “vitamin shots” became the open secret of a new Hollywood elite.

Jacobson’s practice, documented in medical board investigations, FBI files, and the biographies of his clients, operated on a simple, potent principle. He offered not treatment for illness, but enhancement for the well. His signature was a personalized injection, administered from a mysterious brown bottle. He called it a “vitamin cocktail,” a blend of vitamins, hormones, and placental tissue. The primary active ingredient, as later forensic analysis would prove, was a high concentration of liquid amphetamine. The effect was instantaneous and profound: a surge of euphoric energy, mental clarity, and the obliteration of fatigue. It was Hummel’s Benzedrine pill transformed into a potent, intravenous event, stripped of any pretense of pediatric care and marketed purely as elite optimization.

His office on East 72nd Street in Manhattan became a pilgrimage site. According to appointment logs entered into his later license revocation proceedings, his clientele by the early 1960s included a staggering roster of politicians, writers, and most consequentially for Hollywood, actors and producers. He treated, at various times, Eddie Fisher, Truman Capote, and, most famously, President John F. Kennedy. In Hollywood, his influence was pervasive. Actors facing a punishing schedule or a crisis of confidence would fly to New York for a “treatment.” The shot promised more than energy; it promised invincibility.

The methodology was deliberately theatrical, fostering an aura of mystical expertise. Jacobson himself was a charismatic, almost manic figure. He rarely provided written prescriptions. He mixed his solutions from unlabeled bottles, claiming a proprietary genius. He discouraged questions. In a 1972 interview with New York Magazine, a former patient described the ritual: “He’d wipe your arm with alcohol, talk a mile a minute about cellular regeneration, and plunge the needle. Before he even withdrew it, you felt it—this incredible warmth spreading from your chest, this sudden certainty that you could do anything.” The experience was not medical; it was alchemical. He was selling a feeling, and the feeling was power.

Jacobson’s rise was not an aberration. It was the natural evolution of the studio-era logic. Where MGM doctors had prescribed to maintain a production schedule, Jacobson prescribed to maintain a lifestyle, a career trajectory, a public image. The goal was the same: the chemical override of human limitation. The studio system had created the demand by teaching a generation of performers that peak output required pharmaceutical assistance. When the studios no longer provided that service directly, the market found a supplier. Jacobson was merely filling a vacuum with a more potent, more discreet product.

His relationship with the film industry was symbiotic. Directors used his shots to power through all-night editing sessions. Producers used them to maintain the frenetic pace of deal-making. Stars used them to shine brighter at premieres and on set. The “vitamin shot” became a status symbol, a marker of being in the know, of having access to the ultimate competitive edge. It was the perfect drug for an industry built on illusion: it offered the illusion of boundless natural vitality.

The clandestine nature of his operation was key to its appeal. There were no studio logs, no corporate memos. Transactions occurred in a private office, with cash payments often preferred. This secrecy provided a layer of protection for his clients, shielding them from the scandal that had begun to attach to traditional pill addiction. A needle mark was easier to hide than a pill bottle. More importantly, it allowed the user to maintain the fiction that they were not “taking drugs.” They were receiving a “treatment,” a “vitamin boost” from a brilliant doctor. This semantic sleight-of-hand was Jacobson’s greatest innovation. He medicalized recreational amphetamine use for the elite.

The consequences, however, remained stubbornly biological. Former patients, in testimonials to the New York State Board of Health in 1975, described the predictable crash: the crushing depression, the paranoia, the physical tremors that mirrored Judy Garland’s on the set of Oz. Some reported psychotic episodes. The very potency that made the shots so effective also made the dependency more acute and the withdrawal more dangerous. Jacobson’s solution to these side effects was consistent: another shot.

By the mid-1960s, the whispers about “Dr. Feelgood” had grown into a roar of concern. Competent physicians who saw the physical wreckage of his patients—the drastic weight loss, the dilated pupils, the track-marked arms—began filing complaints. The medical board launched its first major investigation in 1968, seizing samples of his mysterious solutions. Lab tests confirmed the presence of methamphetamine. Yet Jacobson, protected by the fame and influence of his clientele, evaded serious consequence for years. His patients, including powerful figures in Hollywood, had a vested interest in his continued practice. To discredit him was to admit their own dependency.

Max Jacobson did not invent the concept of drug-assisted performance in Hollywood. He commercialized its next phase. He took the studio’s systemic, production-driven pharmacology and repackaged it as a personalized service for the individual star. The transaction was no longer between a corporation and its asset. It was between a charismatic doctor and his willing client, both collaborating in the myth of chemical enhancement. The dependency remained, but the accountability vanished. The ledger was now private, the bill coming due not in a studio firing, but in the silent, solitary collapse of a nervous system pushed far beyond its design. The kindly Dr. Hummel had given children pills to help them work. Dr. Feelgood gave adults injections to help them rule. The tool had evolved, but the excavation of the human machine for its luminous output continued, unabated and unrepentant.
