The first shards of glass hit the rug at 11:15 PM.

Attorney General A. Mitchell Palmer had just finished reading in the first-floor library of his R Street home. The windows of the stately Washington residence overlooked a quiet, tree-lined street. He prepared to go upstairs when the world outside the glass ceased to exist.

The explosion was not a distant thud. It was a physical force, a detonation that shattered every window, splintered the front door from its hinges, and blew the façade of the neighboring house into the street. The blast wave lifted Palmer from his feet and hurled him across the room. Books were torn from their shelves. Plaster dust snowed from the ceiling. There was only the deafening ring of silence after the sound.

Then came the secondary percussion of falling debris—brick, wood, glass—raining down on the roof and the sidewalk. A sulfurous, acrid smell seeped into the house, mixing with the dry scent of pulverized plaster. Palmer, shaken but unharmed, stumbled to where his front window had been. A night air was cold through the new opening.

On the sidewalk lay the bomber, torn apart by his own device. According to the Washington Post account the next morning, the man’s limbs were scattered across the lawn and the street. Fragments of the bomb, packed with metal slugs for maximum carnage were embedded in the trunks of trees thirty yards away. The attacker’s head was later recovered from the roof of a house down the block.

The blast had killed the bomber instantly. It had also nearly killed Assistant Secretary of the Navy Franklin D. Roosevelt, who lived directly across the street and had been walking home with his wife Eleanor just minutes before the explosion. Their entryway was filled with glass.

This was not an isolated strike. That same night, just minutes earlier, a similar bomb had exploded at the New York City home of Judge Charles C. Nott, who had recently sentenced anarchists for distributing seditious literature. Within hours, reports reached Washington of other explosive packages discovered before detonation—addressed to a senator, a cabinet official, a mayor. A postal clerk in New York, noticing a leaking package with an Italian return address had triggered a cascade of discoveries. The bombs were crude, deadly, and clearly intended for a coordinated decapitation of American authority.

Palmer stood amid the wreckage of his home, the cold air washing over him. He was a Quaker, a reform-minded Democrat who had championed labor rights. The attack transformed him. The perceived threat was no longer theoretical pamphlets or street-corner rhetoric. It was splintered wood, the metallic smell of explosives, and the proof that ideology could cross the threshold of one’s home. The Post reported his reaction: he was “calm but grim.”

In the following days, investigators pieced together a narrative from the bomber’s remains. Was Carlo Valdinoci, a known anarchist and associate of the militant Luigi Galleani. The Galleanists believed in “the propaganda of the deed”—action that would inspire the masses through terror. They had mailed dozens of package bombs to government and business figures that spring. The June 2nd attacks were their most audacious strike.

The attack provided Palmer with a crystalline mandate. The enemy was not a foreign army but a phantom within, one that spoke a dozen languages and hid in immigrant neighborhoods. The rule of law, with its warrants and evidentiary standards seemed a frail shield against men who delivered their arguments in dynamite. As workers cleared the glass and boarded up his windows, Palmer resolved to answer force with greater force. He would need new tools, new authorities, and a deputy to wield them.

He already had the deputy in mind: a twenty-four-year-old Justice Department attorney named John Edgar Hoover, who ran the department’s alien registration section. Hoover was a bureaucrat of fanatical precision, a compiler of lists. Palmer would give him a task. Find them all.

The bombing of Palmer’s home did not create the national panic; it ignited a fuse that was already laid. The context was a nation profoundly shaken by the recent war and convulsed by the peace that followed. The First World War had mobilized the American economy and psyche for total conflict, framing dissent as disloyalty under the Espionage and Sedition Acts. When the armistice came in November 1918, the machinery of patriotism and suspicion did not simply shut down. It pivoted toward a new, amorphous enemy within.

Demobilization brought not prosperity but turmoil. Four million soldiers returned to an economy struggling to reconvert. Inflation skyrocketed. In 1919, one in five American workers went on strike—a volume of labor unrest the nation had never seen. There were walkouts in the steel industry, coal mines, and the railroads. Most dramatically, in Seattle that February, a general strike paralyzed the city for five days. To many in the press, the political class, and an anxious middle class, these were not purely economic disputes. They were the first skirmishes of a foreign ideology. The headlines told a story of insurrection, not negotiation. “The Seattle strike,” wrote the Washington Post, “is a direct challenge to the government of the United States.”

The foreign source of this apparent challenge seemed. In November 1917, the Bolsheviks had seized power in Russia, withdrawn from the war, and declared a revolution dedicated to global workers’ uprising. Their communiques spoke of sweeping away the capitalist order. To Americans, the link between domestic strikes and international conspiracy felt logical, even obvious. A flood of pamphlets and newspapers from socialist and anarchist groups, many published in immigrant communities in Yiddish, Italian, or German seemed to confirm a coordinated plot. The established press amplified the connection. “It is not a strike,” declared the New York Times of the Seattle upheaval, “it is a revolution.”

This fear attached itself to the nation’s deep-seated nativism. The great wave of immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe that had peaked before the war had brought millions of Catholics and Jews from regions with radical political traditions. They were culturally alien to an America that still defined itself in largely Anglo-Saxon terms. The war had already stoked suspicion of “hyphenated Americans.” Now, in the frantic search for the source of social instability, the immigrant became the revolutionary. The alien and the anarchist blurred into a single threatening figure.

The spring of 1919 provided a rush of events that made the abstract fear terrifyingly concrete. In April, postal officials intercepted thirty-six package bombs addressed to a roster of prominent Americans: Supreme Court Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes, Jr. Attorney General Palmer, industrialist John D. Rockefeller, and others. The bombs were poorly wrapped, leaking acid that ate through the paper and alerted clerks. Their discovery was front-page news, a nationwide shock. Then, on June 2, the coordinated attacks succeeded in part, with the bombs at Palmer’s home and Judge Nott’s residence.

The political pressure on the Wilson administration, and on Palmer specifically became immense. Newspapers demanded action. Headlines screamed “REDS PLANNED WIDE MAYHEM FOR TODAY” and “NATION-WIDE PLOT TO KILL HIGH OFFICIALS IS LAID TO RADICALS.” Editorials questioned the government’s ability to protect its own. “If the Department of Justice cannot catch and jail such conspirators,” asked the Philadelphia Inquirer, “who in this country is safe?” Congress joined the chorus. A Senate resolution called on Palmer to explain what he doed about the threat, while lawmakers drafted bills to deport alien radicals en masse.

Palmer’s own transformation mirrored the national mood. The Quaker pacifist, who had once commuted the death sentence of an anarchist, now internalized the crisis. In public statements, he framed the threat in existential terms. He warned of a “blaze of revolution” that would “burn the altars of churches, the foundations of property, and the existing constitution of law.” The enemy, he argued was not engaged in political speech but in a “war of force.” This rhetorical shift was critical. It moved the conflict from the realm of politics, where the Constitution’s protections applied, to the realm of warfare, where the rules were suspended.

The legal tools for a sweeping response, however were ambiguous. The wartime sedition laws had criminalized certain speech, but deporting alien radicals required a different mechanism. The Immigration Act of 1918 provided it. Passed during the war, the act allowed for the deportation of any alien who advocated the overthrow of the government by force or who belonged to organizations endorsing such doctrines. It was a powerful, flexible statute, but its enforcement required evidence of membership or advocacy—evidence that had to be gathered, compiled, and presented. The bureaucracy was not ready for the dragnet the public and politicians now demanded.

That task of preparation fell to the young chief of the Justice Department’s Radical Division, J. Edgar Hoover. Appointed by Palmer just weeks after the June bombings, Hoover approached the problem with the cold efficiency of an archivist. He understood that the key to mass action was not just suspicion, but a system. He began building a comprehensive index of radical individuals and organizations, scouring radical publications, infiltrating meetings, and compiling reports from a growing network of informants. The goal was a definitive list—a roll call of the enemy. Without such a list, any raid would be chaos. With it, the government could move with the precision of a military operation. Hoover’s filing cabinets became the blueprint for what was to come.

Palmer’s mandate was clear, but his authority was not. The Justice Department’s Bureau of Investigation was small, underfunded, and lacked clear statutory authority to investigate subversion. To launch the campaign he envisioned, Palmer needed two things: money and legal cover. He turned first to Congress. In the anxious summer of 1919, he found a receptive audience. Appearing before the House Appropriations Committee, he presented a stark picture of the threat. He cited the bombings, the flood of radical literature, and the alleged connections to foreign powers. The nation, he argued, faced a “condition of revolutionary terror.” The committee, as documented in its hearing transcripts was swayed. In June, it approved a special $500,000 appropriation—a massive sum—specifically for the Justice Department to investigate anarchistic and seditious activities. The money was Palmer’s war chest.

With funding secured, Palmer needed a legal theory to justify a dragnet that would inevitably sweep up citizens and non-citizens alike. The Sedition Act of 1918 criminalized speech, but Palmer sought a tool for detention and removal. Here, the Immigration Act of 1918 was his instrument. It allowed for the arrest and deportation of any alien who advocated anarchism or belonged to an organization that did. The law’s wording was broad, but its procedures were not. Deportation required warrants from the Labor Department, which oversaw immigration at the time. Palmer moved to circumvent this obstacle. He negotiated a secret agreement with the Labor Department’s Bureau of Immigration led by Commissioner General Anthony Caminetti. Under this arrangement, Justice Department agents would make the arrests. Immigration officials would then rubber-stamp the warrants, often after the fact. The legal process was subordinated to police action.

Directing this machinery was the twenty-four-year-old John Edgar Hoover. Was Palmer’s opposite in temperament and background. Where Palmer was a political animal, gregarious and emotionally transformed by the attack on his home, Hoover was a clinical bureaucrat, a man whose passions were order and information. He had never seen the inside of a bomb-shattered parlor, but he understood the power of a well-organized file. Appointed head of the new Radical Division, he worked from a small, cramped office, building his card index into a weapon. As his biographers later noted, Hoover demanded thoroughness. He instructed agents to gather not just names, but membership records, subscription lists from radical papers, and transcripts of speeches. He believed the list itself would create its own authority. A name on a card was not mere data; it was, in his system, probable cause.

Hoover also understood publicity. In October 1919, he authored a government report titled “Report upon the Illegal Practices of the United States Department of Justice.” The title misleaded; the document was not a critique but a justification. It detailed, without irony, the department’s own investigative methods while arguing for their necessity. More impactful was his fifty-page brief on the Communist Party and the Communist Labor Party, written to support deportation cases. He distilled complex ideology into a simple, damning narrative of revolutionary intent, which Palmer then used publicly. Hoover’s work transformed abstract fear into a target list. He provided the names, the addresses, the organizational charts. He made the phantom tangible.

Palmer, armed with Hoover’s lists and Congress’s money, prepared his first major strike. The target was the Union of Russian Workers, an anarchist group with a few thousand members. The date was set for November 7, 1919—the second anniversary of the Bolshevik Revolution. In a coordinated operation across twelve cities, Bureau of Investigation agents, aided by local police and volunteer deputies, raided meeting halls, homes, and social clubs. They arrested hundreds, often without warrants. In New York, agents stormed the Russian People’s House, a gathering place for immigrants, clubbing those who resisted and hauling away over two hundred men. The arrests were chaotic, the evidence scant—often merely possession of radical literature in a foreign language. But the public response, as seen in newspaper editorials the following day was overwhelmingly positive. The Washington Star hailed the raids as “a good night’s work.” The crackdown had begun with apparent success, validating Palmer’s strategy and Hoover’s methods.

The November raids were a test. The real operation, aimed at the larger Communist parties was planned for a month later. Palmer and Hoover spent December refining their list, coordinating with local authorities, and waiting for the right moment. It came after the New Year, in the deep cold of January 1920. Were ready to move from hundreds to thousands. The list was complete. The warrants were prepared. The public was primed. The machinery of exception built in the shadow of a bomb blast was now set to operate at full scale.

The operation commenced on the night of January 2, 1920. The plan, as later detailed in congressional investigations and agents’ own reports was a coordinated dragnet across more than thirty cities. The targets were members of the Communist Party and Communist Labor Party, identified from Hoover’s card index. At 8:45 PM, a coded telegram went out from Washington to Bureau of Investigation field offices: “Tonight is the night. Take them.”

In Detroit, the raids began at the Finnish Workers’ Hall and the Russian Federation building. According to sworn affidavits collected later by the National Popular Government League, agents and local police, many deputized as temporary immigration officers, broke down doors without presenting warrants. They arrested men attending English-language classes and social gatherings. One detainee, a Finnish-born miner named John Wiita, testified that he was arrested while playing the piano in a social club. His request to see a warrant was answered with a blow from a police club. By morning, over eight hundred men and a handful of women were herded into a single, windowless corridor in the federal building—a space designed for two hundred. Were held there for days with one toilet and no beds, fed only bread and water passed through the bars. Influenza spread through the packed bodies.

The Boston raid focused on the Lithuanian Socialist Hall. Agents confiscated every piece of paper, including personal letters and account books from a nearby grocery. They arrested 114 people. Only 39 had any connection to the organizations on Hoover’s list. The rest, as immigration inspectors later admitted were simply present. In Newark, police and federal agents arrested two hundred at the Russian Workers’ Club. Among them was a man named Samuel Lipman, a known anarchist orator. His arrest was expected. Next to him was a young Jewish tailor’s apprentice, arrested because he was in the building delivering a repaired coat. Was held for five days before an overworked attorney from the Labor Department reviewed his case and ordered his release. No record of his name survived the chaotic paperwork.

The methodology was consistent: violence first, paperwork later. Warrants, when they existed at all were often blanket warrants for an entire organization, not specific individuals. In numerous cases, agents carried blank warrants, filling in names after the arrests were made. The Bureau’s own manual, entered into the congressional record in 1921, instructed agents to “secure all books, records, and papers,” creating an evidentiary haul to justify the arrests post facto. Homes were ransacked, personal libraries seized, family photographs torn apart in searches for hidden ciphers. The New York World reported that in one Bronx apartment, agents confiscated a chemistry textbook as “subversive literature.”

The processing of detainees exposed the system’s brutal efficiency. Arrests were made on Friday night, strategically timed to circumvent the legal system. Courts were closed for the weekend. Lawyers, many of whom did not speak the languages of their clients—Finnish, Russian, Yiddish, Italian—could not secure writs of habeas corpus until Monday. The interrogation and coercion were complete. Detainees were held incommunicado, denied phone calls, and subjected to prolonged questioning without counsel. The goal was not to build a case for trial, but to secure a signed confession of membership or a statement advocating anarchism, which would suffice for a deportation hearing. According to the memoirs of Assistant Secretary of Labor Louis F. Post, who later reviewed thousands of these cases, confessions were often obtained through intimidation, physical exhaustion, or promises of immediate release.

Conditions in the makeshift detention centers were punitive. In Hartford, ninety men were locked in a room twenty by thirty feet for a week. In Chicago, detainees were held in unheated cells in the dead of winter. The bathroom facilities, as described in a report by the ACLU’s precursor, the National Civil Liberties Bureau overflowed buckets. Food was sporadic. Medical attention was nonexistent. These were not prisoners convicted of a crime; they were individuals held on administrative charges, often on the sole basis of their name appearing on a membership list purchased from an informant.

The human cost was documented in fragments. In Detroit, a Ukrainian immigrant named Petro Huban died of pneumonia contracted in the overcrowded bullpen. His death was listed in city records, but no official inquiry linked it to his detention. In New York, a pregnant woman, arrested at a social function, miscarried in a holding cell. Her story was recorded by a nurse from the Women’s Trade Union League who visited the detention sites. The most widespread damage was psychological: the terror of sudden arrest, the confusion of a legal process conducted in a foreign language, the despair of being trapped in a system designed for expulsion.

The legal facade was maintained through hearings before the Bureau of Immigration’s boards of special inquiry. These were not judicial proceedings. As Louis Post described them, the boards were composed of low-level immigration officials acting as prosecutor, judge, and jury. Evidence was frequently the agent’s own report or the suspect’s coerced confession. Testimony from defense witnesses was often curtailed. Translation was haphazard. The outcome was preordained. By the end of January 1920, the Labor Department had ordered the deportation of 591 individuals from the Palmer Raids. The pace was so frantic that the department chartered a vessel, the USAT Buford, nicknamed the “Soviet Ark,” to deport 249 of them to Russia in December 1919, before the larger January raids even began. Among those on board were the famous anarchists Emma Goldman and Alexander Berkman, but the majority were obscure workers whose only crime, as Goldman later wrote in her memoir was “possession of a foreign accent and a radical pamphlet.”

The raids netted thousands, but the yield of genuine revolutionary conspirators was vanishingly small. Of the approximately 10,000 people arrested in the Palmer Raids between November 1919 and January 1920, only 556 were ultimately deported. The rest were released, their lives and reputations shattered, their homes ransacked, their communities traumatized. The government had demonstrated not its precision, but its power. The lesson of the raids was not that the state could surgically remove a threat. That in a climate of fear, the state could suspend the rules entirely, and the public would applaud the spectacle of force. The card index had provided a target list, but the real product was a demonstration: the home, the meeting hall, the body of the alien were no longer sanctuaries. Were territory to be occupied.

The Palmer Raids did not end with a decisive legal rebuke, but with a gradual erosion of public support. The backlash, when it came was led not by the courts but by a coalition of civil libertarians, journalists, and dissenting officials. Assistant Secretary of Labor Louis F. Post became the most effective internal critic. As the official responsible for signing deportation warrants, he began scrutinizing the cases that landed on his desk. According to his 1923 memoir, The Deportations Delirium of Nineteen-Twenty, he found a pattern of systematic abuse: arrests without warrant, evidence consisting solely of paid informant testimony, confessions obtained under duress. He canceled thousands of warrants, angering Palmer but stalling the deportation machine. A report by a group of twelve distinguished lawyers, including future Supreme Court Justice Felix Frankfurter, titled “Report upon the Illegal Practices of the United States Department of Justice,” cataloged the raids’ constitutional violations. Newspapers like the New York World and the Washington Post, initially supportive turned critical as the stories of wrongful detention and brutality emerged.

Palmer’s credibility collapsed in the spring of 1920. He had predicted a massive radical uprising for May Day. He fortified government buildings and mobilized police in major cities. One day passed without incident. The headlines mocked his alarmism. “No Red Revolt Here,” declared the New York Times. The phantom threat, having failed to materialize on his schedule began to look like a fabrication. By summer, a Senate investigation led by Democrat Thomas J. Walsh of Montana subjected Palmer and Hoover to sharp questioning. Palmer’s hysterical testimony, warning of plots that he could no longer substantiate played poorly. The political moment had passed. The economy stabilized. The public, its fear spent turned toward normalcy and the election of Warren G. Harding, who promised a “return to normalcy.”

The conventional narrative marks this as a defeat for overreach, a self-correcting triumph for American civil liberties. The archival record suggests a more durable victory for the architects of exception. The raids themselves were halted, but their institutional and legal legacy was cemented. The most significant creation was the Radical Division itself, which did not disband. Quietly renamed, its files retained, its methodology preserved. J. Edgar Hoover remained its chief. Had learned a tactical lesson from the backlash: publicity was a double-edged sword. Efficiency required secrecy. The next time, the card index would be consulted, the raids conducted, but the press would not be invited. The division evolved into the General Intelligence Division, a permanent fixture within the Bureau of Investigation. The legal theories tested in the raids were not overturned. The Immigration Act of 1918 remained on the books. The principle that membership in a proscribed organization was grounds for deportation, regardless of individual action was upheld by the courts. In Colyer v. Skeffington (1920), a federal judge condemned the conduct of the Boston raids but affirmed the government’s underlying authority to deport alien members of revolutionary groups. The precedent for “guilt by association” was set, not as a wartime aberration, but as a peacetime administrative tool.

Most importantly, the Palmer Raids established the federal government, specifically the Department of Justice, as the primary agent of domestic security. Before 1919, the policing of radicalism was largely a local or state concern. Palmer and Hoover federalized it. They centralized intelligence gathering, coordinated national operations, and asserted Washington’s primacy over a domain once left to city police and private detective agencies like the Pinkertons. The Bureau of Investigation emerged from the episode with expanded jurisdiction and a new mandate: political surveillance.

The raids demonstrated that the most effective limit on such power was not the Constitution, but the cyclical nature of public panic. When the fear subsided, the operations slowed. But the apparatus built to exploit that fear was not dismantled. Mothballed, its blueprints filed, its manager kept in place. The emergency had passed, but the capacity for its repetition was now a permanent line item in the federal budget. The “Soviet Ark” had sailed, but the dock it was built upon remained, waiting for the next perceived invasion from within. The true residue of the Palmer Raids was not the 556 deportees, but the quiet bureaucrat in the Department of Justice who now presided over a roomful of filing cabinets, understanding that in American life, the emergency was always temporary, but the list was forever.
