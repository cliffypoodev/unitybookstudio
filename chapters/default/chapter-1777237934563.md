The van smelled like a cross between a taxidermist's shop and a Sunday school that had caught fire. Dutch pulled into the Cathedral of Prosperous Fire's back lot, his knuckles still white on the wheel. The building was a monument to bad taste and good investments—a sprawling, pink-stucco monstrosity with a gold-plated steeple shaped like a lightning bolt. It screamed 'divine intervention' at a volume usually reserved for jet engines.

"Remember the plan," Lola said, checking the mirror for tails. "We get him inside, get him on air, get him to talk. We don't start a holy war."

"Too late," Mary muttered. She was wedged between Rex and a quietly weeping Kevin. "We're riding with the infantry."

Jesus adjusted the cheap suit Dutch had bought him at a thrift store. The jacket strained across his shoulders. "He will ask about my message."

"Yeah, your message is 'don't shoot us,'" Dutch said. He killed the engine. The silence was immediately filled by Kevin's soft, staticky rendition of "He's Got the Whole World in His Hands."

Mary closed her eyes. "I'm going to disassemble him. Screwdriver by screwdriver."

Rex shifted, making the van list to the left. "THE STRUCTURAL INTEGRITY OF THIS VEHICLE IS COMPROMISED."

"You're telling me." Dutch popped his door. The Miami heat hit him like a wet blanket someone had microwaved. "Okay. Lola and me, we're the talent wranglers."

"A carpenter," Jesus said.

"A carpenter with a messiah complex," Mary finished. She slid out, her boots crunching on the gravel. "I'll be your sniper support. From a distance. A very, very long distance."

Lola came around the van, smoothing her blazer. She looked about as comfortable as a vegan at a pig roast. "Backstage pass says 'Reverend Lux's Special Guest.' They think he's some kind of faith healer from Tallahassee."

"Technically, that's not a lie," Dutch said.

Jesus just looked at the cathedral. His expression was unreadable, but his eyes tracked the gold-plated lightning bolt. He blinked once, slowly. Like a man watching a car crash in slow motion and recognizing his own vehicle.

A side door burst open. A young man with a headset and acne scurried out, waving a clipboard. "You the Tallahassee group? We're live in twenty. Reverend wants a pre-interview."

"No pre-interview," Lola said, her cop-voice snapping into place. "Our guy goes on cold. That's the deal."

The PA’s eyes widened. "But Reverend Lux always—"

"New deal," Dutch said, slinging an arm around Jesus's shoulders. He tried for a showbiz smile. It felt like a grimace. "My client's energy is… spontaneous. You prep him, you kill the vibe."

The PA looked from Dutch's forced grin to Jesus's serene, unsettling calm. He swallowed. "Fine. But if he goes off-script, that's on you. Follow me."

Backstage was a controlled panic. Cables snaked across the floor like neon veins. A woman with enough hairspray to violate the Montreal Protocol yelled into a headset. The air tasted like ozone and ambition. Dutch’s shoe stuck to a patch of spilled soda with every other step.

Jesus stopped walking. He was staring at a giant, backlit photo of Reverend Bobby Lux. The photo showed Lux in a white suit, arms outstretched, a beatific smile plastered on his face. Behind him, a CGI angel blurred into a waterfall of gold coins.

“He looks… shiny,” Jesus said.

“That’s the point,” Dutch whispered, steering him away from the photo. “He sells shiny. Shiny salvation, shiny bank accounts. Don’t stare, it’s rude.”

Lola fell into step beside them, her eyes scanning the crew. “Too many people.”

“They’re looking at the hair and the suit,” Dutch said. “They’re not looking for the Son of God. They’re looking for a rube from Tallahassee.”

A man in a security blazer stepped into their path. He had the neck of a bull and the eyes of a tax auditor. “Guest pass.”

Dutch presented the laminated card. The guard studied it, then studied Jesus. His gaze lingered on the sandals peeking out from under the cheap suit pants.

“Foot condition,” Dutch said quickly. “Doctor’s orders. Open-toed ventilation. It’s a… fungal thing.”

The guard’s lip curled. He waved them through.

They were herded into a green room that smelled of stale coffee and desperation. A platter of sweating cheese cubes sat on a table next to a stack of Lux’s autobiography, You’re Already Rich, You Just Don’t Know It Yet. Jesus picked up a book. He read the back cover. He set it down. He didn’t say anything, but the silence in the room got heavier.

“Don’t get theological in the green room,” Dutch pleaded. “Just… smile. Nod. Talk about the weather.”

“The weather is a manifestation of complex atmospheric systems,” Kevin’s voice crackled from the walkie-talkie on Mary’s belt. She’d kept one channel open. “IT IS NEITHER GOOD NOR EVIL.”

Mary snatched the walkie and thumbed it off. “I’m disconnecting the vocalizer. I swear to God.”

“Which one?” Dutch muttered.

The door swung open. Reverend Bobby Lux filled the doorway. He was shorter in person, but he made up for it in sheer, polished presence. His suit was a blinding ivory. His tan was the color of a new penny. His smile was a weapon—all teeth and calculated warmth.

“My special guest!” he boomed, arms spreading wide. He didn’t enter the room so much as occupy it. “Welcome to the Cathedral of Prosperous Fire!”

Jesus looked at the outstretched arms. He didn’t move.

Lux’s smile didn’t falter, but his eyes flickered—a quick, calculating dart from Jesus’s face to his thrift-store suit to his sandals. The calculation was almost audible. Rube. Possibly crazy. Definitely good TV.

“We are so blessed to have you with us,” Lux said, lowering his arms with practiced grace. “I understand you’ve been performing some remarkable healings in the Panhandle.”

“I wash dishes,” Jesus said.

A beat of silence. Lux’s laugh was a rich, barrel-aged sound. “Humble! I love it. Humility is the cornerstone of true prosperity.” He stepped closer, dropping his voice to a conspiratorial murmur. “Now, on air, I may challenge you a little. It’s for the drama, you understand? The folks at home, they need a story. A journey.”

Jesus just watched him. His gaze was steady, patient. It was the kind of look that made you suddenly aware of your own heartbeat.

Lux cleared his throat. The charm-o-meter dipped a notch. “The key message is that your power is a gift from God. A gift He wants us all to have. To be healthy. To be wealthy. To claim our divine inheritance.”

“The meek shall inherit the earth,” Jesus said.

“Exactly!” Lux’s smile returned, wider. “But inheritance tax is a killer, am I right?” He chuckled, nudging Dutch as if they were in on the joke.

Dutch forced a laugh. It came out as a strangled cough.

A frantic woman with a headset appeared. “Reverend, ninety seconds.”

“Showtime!” Lux clapped his hands together. He gave Jesus one last, appraising look. “Just be yourself, brother. The cameras add ten pounds, but the Holy Spirit subtracts twenty.” He winked and swept out of the room.

The moment the door shut, Dutch let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding. “Okay. He thinks you’re a harmless weirdo. That’s good. That’s our in.”

Lola moved to the door, cracking it open to peer down the hall. “He’s got two security guys by the stage entrance. They’re big.”

“They’re thinking about lunch,” Dutch said, more to himself than anyone. “They’re not thinking about eschatology.”

Jesus was still looking at the door Lux had exited through. His face was calm, but his hands were curled loosely at his sides. One thumb stroked the pad of his forefinger, back and forth. A carpenter’s hand, feeling for a splinter that wasn’t there.

“You don’t have to do this,” Lola said, turning from the door. “We can walk. Right now.”

“Where would we go?” Jesus asked. It wasn’t a challenge. It was a genuine question.

“Anywhere that isn’t a live television studio run by a con artist.”

He considered this. Then he shook his head. “The sheep know the voice of their shepherd. But what if the shepherd only knows the sound of the collection plate?”

Dutch stared at him. “What does that mean?”

“It means I’m going on television.” Jesus stood up. The cheap suit seemed to hang differently on him now—less like a costume, more like a uniform he’d reluctantly agreed to wear.

The headset woman reappeared, breathing hard. “Now! We’re going now!”

They were swept into the pulsating heart of the studio. The air was hotter, thick with the smell of hot lights and crowd sweat. A bank of cameras pointed at a garish set designed to look like a heavenly library. The live audience, a sea of pastel and perms, buzzed with anticipation.

Lux was already in his throne-like chair, miked up, his smile dialed to eleven. He saw Jesus and gestured to the empty chair beside him with a flourish that was pure showmanship.

A stagehand shoved a microphone into Jesus’s hand and pointed. Jesus walked to the chair. He didn’t look at the audience. He didn’t look at the cameras. He just sat down, arranging the microphone cord in his lap with a strange, deliberate care.

From the shadows of the wing, Dutch watched, his stomach doing a slow, greasy roll. Lola stood rigid beside him.

On the set, a man in a director’s chair counted down with his fingers. Five. Four. Three. Two.

A red light glowed on the main camera.

Lux turned to the lens, his face transforming into a mask of beatific authority. “Friends! Brothers and sisters in prosperity! We have a very special guest with us tonight. A man of mystery, of faith… some say, of miracles.” He turned, his smile widening. “Welcome… Jesus of Nazareth.”

The audience gasped. Then they tittered. A few laughed outright.

Jesus looked into the camera. He didn’t smile.

Lux’s eyes glittered with predatory delight. The trap was set. The whole country was watching.

Dutch swallowed, tasting bile and cheese cube.

They were so screwed.

Lola watched from the wings, a hand resting on the butt of her service piece under her blazer. The studio lights were a physical weight, a dry heat that made her scalp prickle. The audience’s laughter died into a confused murmur. They were waiting for the punchline.

Lux leaned forward, oozing concern. “Now, that’s quite a name to live up to, isn’t it? Jesus of Nazareth. Your parents must have had… tremendous faith.”

“I had a mother,” Jesus said. His voice, amplified, wasn’t loud. It was clear. It cut through the studio’s canned atmosphere like a window shattering in a sealed room. “She was afraid, too. At first.”

A woman in the front row, wearing a rhinestone ‘PRAISE’ brooch, stopped fanning herself with her program.

“Fear is a tool of the enemy!” Lux declared, pivoting smoothly to camera two. “A tool to keep you from your God-given abundance!” He turned back, his expression softening into pastoral condescension. “But you’re not here to talk about fear, are you? Our producers tell me you’ve been performing healings. In trailer parks. In laundromats.”

“I wash feet,” Jesus corrected.

“You… wash feet.”

“Yes. The water is usually warm. The towels are usually thin.”

Lux’s smile tightened at the corners. He was a master at rolling with a guest’s weirdness, spinning it into gold. This was different. This wasn’t weird. It was… plain. It refused to be spun. “A beautiful metaphor for spiritual cleansing!”

“It’s not a metaphor. It’s a washbasin. The dirt is real. The calluses are real.” Jesus looked past Lux, his gaze sweeping the audience. He looked at the woman with the brooch, at a young man in a too-tight suit, at an elderly couple holding hands. “You are real.”

The studio was dead quiet. You could hear the hum of the lights, the distant whir of a teleprompter.

Lola felt a weird, unwelcome tug in her chest. She shoved it down. He’s just good, she told herself. A better con than Lux. That’s all.

Dutch, beside her, whispered, “He’s bombing. This is a train wreck. A quiet, boring train wreck.”

“No,” Lola murmured. “Look at them.”

The audience wasn’t cheering. They weren’t clapping on cue. They were just… listening. Their faces were blank screens, waiting for a signal.

Lux sensed it. The predator in him smelled a shift in the herd. His laugh came back, a little forced this time. “Real! Yes! And God wants us to live in the real world of blessing! Of new cars! Of debt-free living!” He sprang from his chair, pacing the stage, a white-suited shark. “You speak of calluses, brother. I speak of anointing oil! You speak of washbasins. I speak of a river of financial breakthrough!” He stopped, pointing a dramatic finger at the ceiling. “The question for our guest, for all of us, is this: do we settle for the mundane… or do we reach for the MIRACULOUS?”

The crowd erupted. It was a Pavlovian response. The word ‘miraculous’ was their dinner bell. The applause was relief. They knew this part.

Jesus waited for the noise to fade. He hadn’t moved. “A widow put two small coins in the temple treasury. All she had to live on.” He paused. The microphone picked up the soft sound of his breath. “The miraculous wasn’t in the amount. It was in the silence after the clatter.”

Lux froze mid-strut. His eyes narrowed, just for a fraction of a second. This wasn’t in the script. This was theology. Worse, it was simple theology. It couldn’t be packaged. He walked back to his chair, his movements slower, more deliberate. “A touching story. Of course, the temple had operating costs. Levites to feed. Incense to buy.” He sat, crossing his legs. “We have satellites to uplink. Millions of souls to reach. The harvest is plentiful, but the workers are… expensive.”

He delivered the last line with a practiced, self-deprecating smirk. A few loyalists chuckled.

Jesus turned his head and looked directly at the camera. Not at the lens, but at the red light atop it. It was an unsettlingly intimate gesture, as if he were looking through the glass into someone’s living room. “You cannot serve both God and money,” he said. His tone was matter-of-fact. A weather report. “You will hate one and love the other. You will be devoted to one and despise the other.”

The air left the room. The control booth behind Lola went silent.

Lux’s face went very still. The tan seemed to darken, like old leather. The smile was gone, replaced by a placid, empty mask. He’d been punched in the gut on live TV, and his body was waiting for his brain to catch up.

Lola’s cop instincts screamed. This was the moment. The pivot from entertainment to threat.

“Strong words,” Lux said, his voice dangerously quiet. The folksy cadence was gone. This was boardroom Lux. “Judgmental words. From a man wearing a thirty-dollar suit.”

“It was nineteen,” Jesus said. “The pants are too short.”

A snort of laughter came from the audience. It was cut off abruptly, choked.

Lux’s eyes flicked to the stage manager off-camera. A silent command. He leaned forward, his hands steepled. “Let’s talk about judgment. You walk in here, a stranger, and you judge my ministry. My outreach. The lives changed.” He ticked them off on his fingers. “Addicts healed. Marriages restored. Checks written.”

“I do not judge,” Jesus said. “The words judge themselves.”

“Oh, they do, do they?” Lux’s mask cracked. A vein pulsed near his temple. “And what about the IRS, brother? Do they judge? Because my ministry is an open book. A blessed, profitable, audited book.”

It was a threat. Clumsy, but clear.

Jesus just looked at him. There was no anger in his face. No triumph. It was worse. It was pity. “The book you should worry about has your name written in it. Not in ink.”

The stage manager was frantically making a slashing motion across his throat. The red light on the camera was still glowing. They were still live. Lux was trapped in his own arena.

The reverend’s composure shattered. He didn’t shout. His voice dropped to a venomous, sibilant hiss, perfectly captured by his lapel mic. “You sanctimonious little freak.”

The director in the booth finally found the button. The theme music for ‘The Hour of Prosperous Fire’—a syrupy swell of strings and synth—blasted through the speakers.

The red light died.

“WE’RE OUT! COMMERCIAL! SIXTY SECONDS!” the stage manager yelled.

The audience sat in stunned silence. Then the noise started—a confused babble of questions, a few angry shouts.

On the set, Lux didn’t move. He stared at Jesus, his chest rising and falling. The beatific mask was in a pile at his feet. What was left was raw, furious ambition.

Jesus slowly unclipped his microphone and set it on the chair. He stood up.

Lola was already moving, Dutch at her heels. They met Jesus at the edge of the stage. “Time to go,” she said, her voice low and urgent. “Right now.”

Behind them, Lux finally moved. He stood, smoothed his suit jacket, and crooked a finger at the bull-necked security guard stationed by the curtain. He didn’t speak. He didn’t need to. He pointed at Jesus, then made a sharp, cutting motion toward the back of the studio. His meaning was unmistakable.

Take him. Make him disappear.

The guard nodded, his hand going to the radio on his belt. His eyes locked on Lola’s group.

“Back door,” Dutch gulped. “Where’s the back door?”

“Forget it,” Lola said, scanning the chaos. Crew members were rushing, avoiding eye contact. The audience was starting to mill. “We go through the crowd.”

“They’re his crowd!”

“They’re confused. Move.”

She grabbed Jesus’s arm and pulled him into the aisle. The woman with the ‘PRAISE’ brooch reached out, her fingers brushing his sleeve. “Are you…” she began, her eyes wide.

“He’s late for a foot-washing,” Dutch snapped, pushing past.

They shoved through the murmuring crowd, toward the main doors at the back of the studio. Lola glanced over her shoulder. The security guard was plowing through the same aisle, his partner appearing from the other side. They were being flanked.

“Faster,” she hissed.

They burst through the double doors into the cathedral’s garish lobby, a marble expanse dominated by a fountain featuring a golden calf spouting water. The place was empty, echoing.

“Van’s in the back!” Dutch wheezed.

“Too far,” Lola said. The security guards would cut them off. She looked around, her mind racing. A door marked ‘PRIVATE – BROADCAST EQUIPMENT’ stood ajar down a side hall. “In here.”

They ducked inside, into a narrow, dim corridor lined with humming server racks and tangled cables. Lola closed the door, plunging them into near-darkness broken only by the glow of LED status lights.

From the lobby, they heard the guards’ voices, sharp and close.

“Spread out. Check the bathrooms. He can’t have gone far.”

Lola leaned against the door, her ear pressed to the wood. Jesus stood calmly in the middle of the corridor, his head tilted. He was listening, but not to the guards. To the hum of the machines. To the distant, tinny sound of a commercial for gold-plated scripture plates still playing in the studio.

Dutch was breathing like a marathon runner. “You. On TV. What was that?”

“A conversation,” Jesus said.

“You sank his billion-dollar yacht with a conversation!”

“The house was built on sand. The storm was already here.”

Lola held up a hand for silence. The footsteps in the lobby faded. For now. She turned, the LED lights casting her face in stark relief. She looked at Jesus, really looked at him. The serene certainty. The quiet, world-breaking calm.

She wasn’t skeptical anymore. She was, against every professional instinct, impressed.

And that terrified her more than Lux’s guards ever could.

“They’re not giving up,” she said, her voice a whisper in the electronic dark. “He’ll send everyone he has. You broke his product on live TV.”

Jesus nodded. He seemed to consider this. “A shepherd should smell like sheep,” he said finally. “Not like hairspray and ambition.”

Outside the door, a walkie-talkie crackled to life. The voice was Lux’s, stripped of its honey, cold and metallic. “Find him. I don’t care how. You bring him to me.”

The order hung in the air of the equipment closet, a chilling, silent weight.

They were no longer just on the run. They were prey.

Dutch pressed his ear against the cold metal door. The walkie-talkie static had died, leaving a silence that was worse. The hum of the server racks was the sound of a mechanical beehive, mindless and hot. A drop of sweat traced a path from his temple to his collar. His shirt was a damp, polyester prison.

“Okay,” he whispered, the word sticking in his dry throat. “Think. They’re sweeping the lobby. They’ll check this hall next.”

Lola was already moving down the corridor, her silhouette a sharp cutout against the blinking LEDs. “This way. Service corridors always dump out near loading.”

“What if it dumps into Lux’s private wine cellar?” Dutch hissed, following. “What if it dumps into his personal firing range?”

“Then we’ll die surrounded by fine cabernet and NRA plaques. Keep moving.”

Jesus walked between them, his sandals whispering on the concrete floor. He paused at a junction, looking left, then right. He pointed right. “There is more air moving from there.”

“Air moving,” Dutch muttered. “Great. We’re being hunted by televangelist goons, and our navigator is a human barometer.”

They took the right. The corridor narrowed, pipes sweating overhead. It smelled of industrial cleaner and, faintly, of the fried chicken that must have been catered for the crew. Dutch’s stomach gurgled. He hated that his body still remembered hunger in the middle of a crisis.

A door slammed somewhere behind them. Voices echoed, distorted by the maze of walls.

“Split up. You two, check the control rooms. He’s gotta be in a hole somewhere.”

Lola picked up her pace. They rounded a corner and faced a heavy steel door with a push-bar. A red EXIT sign glowed above it, but someone had taped a handwritten note over the bar: ALARMED – USE MAIN DOCK DOOR.

“Of course,” Dutch breathed. “The one exit. Alarmed.”

Lola examined the door frame, her fingers probing near the hinges. “It’s a cheap magnetic contact. Probably rings at the security desk.” She looked back the way they came. “They’ll be at the security desk.”

“So we set it off. We run like hell.”

“They’ll know exactly where we are.”

“They already know we’re in the building! They just don’t know where! This gives them a where, but it also gives us an out! It’s a trade!” Dutch’s voice was climbing into a register usually reserved for discovering spider eggs in his cereal.

Jesus was looking at the note. He reached out and peeled the tape off one corner. The paper fluttered to the floor. He placed his palm flat on the push-bar.

“Wait—” Lola said.

He pushed.

No alarm screamed. The door opened with a heavy clunk onto a loading dock bathed in the sickly orange glow of sodium-vapor lights. The night air hit them, thick with humidity and the distant scent of the bay.

“The note was a lie,” Jesus observed.

Dutch stared. “Who lies about an alarm?”

“A man who owns the alarm company,” Lola said, stepping out and scanning the dock. It was cluttered with dumpsters and stacked pallets. “He saves on false-alarm fines. And traps idiots who can’t read.”

A shout erupted from inside the building, bouncing down the corridor they’d just vacated. The guard’s voice was clearer now. “Dock! Check the loading dock!”

“See? Idiots,” Dutch said, and scrambled down the concrete steps.

The dock was a canyon between the cathedral’s bulk and a high chain-link fence topped with razor wire. At the far end, a rolling gate was closed and padlocked. The Wizard Dolphin van was a beautiful, mural-painted mirage on the other side.

“Gate’s locked,” Lola stated.

“You’re a detective! Pick it!”

“I’m not a locksmith, I carry a gun! Shoot it?”

“And tell every cop in Miami exactly where we are? No!”

Jesus walked to the center of the dock. He looked at the fence, then at the dumpster. It was a giant, steel rectangle on wheels, overflowing with black bags and discarded set pieces—a broken polystyrene angel wing, a chunk of fake marble column.

“Help me,” he said.

He put his hands on the dumpster. Dutch blinked. “What are you doing?”

The product was broken. And the people were building something new out of the pieces, right there on the sidewalk.
