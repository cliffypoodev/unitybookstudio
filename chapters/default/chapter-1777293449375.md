Gideon’s mop hummed at a frequency that made his fillings ache. The sound echoed off the marble floors of the Vatican Secret Archives, a low, persistent whine that seemed to annoy the very dust motes dancing in the slanted light from the high, stained-glass windows. The air smelled of lemon polish and something sharper, like the air after a lightning strike. Divine ozone. He’d read about it in the custodial manual. Section 4, Subsection B: “Olfactory Indicators of Active Celestial Presence.” He’d also read the footnote: “Do not engage. Call a priest.”

He was not going to call a priest.

The halo was on a velvet cushion inside a glass case that probably had more security than the Pope’s sock drawer. It glowed with a soft, butter-yellow light, spinning lazily about an inch above the fabric. It looked peaceful. Innocent. Gideon’s mouth flooded with the taste of burnt toast and regret.

Lie.

Everything about this place was a lie wrapped in gilt and whispered Latin. The taste was how he knew. He’d discovered it after the mop incident, after the void where his mother’s laughter used to be. Now lies had flavors. White lies tasted like saccharine. Omissions were flat, like day-old soda. This halo’s lie was complex, a layered pastry of deception that left an ashy aftertaste.

He adjusted his grip on the mop handle. The anti-gravity coil at its base was warm, a dormant beetle of potential chaos. His plan was simple, clean: use the mop’s localized null-gravity field to float the halo out of its case, catch it in his specially insulated cleaning caddy, and be out before the change of the celestial guard. He’d timed their patrols. They were predictably late, always arguing about choir practice.

“You’re overthinking it,” he muttered to himself. The mop gurgled in agreement. Or maybe it was hungry. He tried not to think about that.

He flicked the activation switch with his thumb.

A low thrum vibrated up the handle. The dust motes in a three-foot radius around the mop head stopped dancing and began to drift in slow, confused spirals. Gideon aimed the business end at the display case. The glass didn’t shatter. It just… forgot to be solid. It rippled like jelly. The halo’s spin wobbled.

This was it. The cleanest heist in history. No fingerprints, no broken alarms, just a little applied physics and a complete disregard for sacred boundaries.

A sharp twang echoed down the corridor, followed by a discordant wail.

“I told you, Uzziel, the G-string is flat! It throws off the entire deterrent chord!”

“My harp is perfectly tuned! Your ear is attuned to the mud of the material plane!”

Two cherubs rounded the corner, bickering. They were the size of toddlers, plump and rosy-cheeked, with fluffy white wings that shed feathers like dandelion fluff. They struggled under the weight of a massive, gold-stringed harp one carried, while the other waved a tiny tuning fork like a baton. They stopped short at the entrance to the archive chamber.

Six beady eyes—two for the first cherub, four for the second, which seemed to be a birth defect—fixed on Gideon and his shimmering mop.

“Hey,” said the first cherub, whose nameplate read ‘Zephon’.

“Hey,” Gideon replied, his voice tight.

“What are you doing?” asked the second, ‘Uzziel’, the one with four eyes.

“Spot cleaning.” Gideon kept the mop steady. The halo was now bobbing against the inside of the gelatinous glass. “Persistent stain of… sanctity. Tough stuff.”

Zephon squinted. “You’re the new night-shift guy. Gary.”

“Gideon.”

“Right. Gary.” Zephon poked Uzziel. “See? I told you Maintenance sent someone about the ectoplasmic buildup near the Scholasticism scrolls.”

“That was a different work order,” Uzziel sniffed, two of his eyes watching Gideon, the other two scrutinizing his harp strings. “This work order is for polishing the reliquaries. You have the wrong work order, Gary.”

The taste of burnt toast intensified. Layers of it. Gideon swallowed. “Cross-trained. I’m multi-disciplinary.”

He gave the mop a gentle tug. The halo slipped through the quivering glass like a coin through water. It floated, serene, toward the mop’s gravitational field. Five more feet.

“Hold on,” Uzziel said, his wings fluttering with suspicion. A small cloud of down filled the air. “Polishing requires a soft cloth and approved sanctified unguent. That is… a mop.”

“It’s a multi-surface tool,” Gideon said, improvising wildly. “For multi-surface holiness. You’ve got your flat sanctity on the floors, your curved sanctity on the arches, your concentrated sanctity in the artifacts…” He was losing them. Zephon looked intrigued. Uzziel looked like he was doing celestial math.

The halo was three feet away.

“Where’s your work order?” Uzziel demanded, floating an inch off the ground for added authority. “All non-cherubic personnel require a triplicate-signed work order for Tier-Three Sacred Spaces.”

“I have it right here.” Gideon reached slowly into his janitorial vest with his free hand, fingers brushing past his sponge holster and a bag of certified bio-degradable wipes. He pulled out a crumpled parchment. He’d found it in a wastebasket. It was a requisition form for extra choir robes, size cherub-large.

Uzziel snatched it with a chubby hand. His four eyes scanned the page. His brow furrowed. Then it smoothed. “Ah. Robe inspection. That’s Tier-Two. You’re in the wrong wing, Gary. Tier-Three is for artifacts. Tier-Two is for vestments.” He pointed a stern finger down the hall. “Vestments are that way. Past the disappointed sighs of the early Church fathers. You can’t miss it.”

The halo was two feet away. It began to orbit the mop head in a slow, lazy circle.

“Right,” Gideon said, injecting his voice with grateful confusion. “Wrong wing. Silly me. New guy, you know.” He began to back away, leading with the mop, trying to guide the halo with him like a man using a magnet to steal a hubcap in slow motion.

Zephon strummed a chord on the harp. It sounded like a cat falling down a staircase. “Hey Uzziel. If he’s here for robe inspection, why is his mop… eating the Halo of Saint Barnabas?”

Gideon froze.

Uzziel looked from the work order to the mop, where the halo now spun in a tight, happy orbit. All four eyes widened. “That’s not robe inspection!”

“It’s a very dirty robe,” Gideon tried.

“THIEF!” they shrieked in unison, a piercing sound that made the glass cases rattle.

Zephon fumbled with his harp, trying to bring it to bear. Uzziel dropped the tuning fork and raised his chubby hands, which began to glow with a faint, sparkly light. “Unsanctioned artifact relocation! Code Gold!”

Gideon ran. Or rather, he shuffled backwards with purpose, yanking the mop after him. The halo followed, tethered by invisible forces. He careened around a corner, the mop handle clattering against a bust of a frowning pope. The cherubs gave chase, their wingbeats a frantic whirr.

“Stop him! He’s got the Barnabas!”

“Sound the alarm!”

“I’m trying! The G-string is still flat!”

The corridor opened into a larger chamber—the Hall of Theological Artifacts. It was a forest of display cases: the feather from the wing of the Angel of Mons here, a piece of the True Cross (certified) there, a vial labeled “Tears of a Saint (Probably Peter)” on a nearby shelf. And standing in the center, polishing a flaming sword with a celestial chamois, was a Seraph.

The Seraph was tall, majestic, with six wings that were currently folded in a slightly irritated manner. His sword, currently not flaming, looked like it could slice through dogma itself. He looked up as Gideon skidded into the room, pursued by two shrieking, feather-shedding cherubs.

Gideon’s heart did a thing in his chest that wasn’t a pound. It was more of a frantic, trapped-bird flutter. The taste in his mouth shifted from burnt toast to something metallic and sour, like battery acid. New lie. Bigger lie.

“Unauthorized cleaning protocol, Seraphiel!” Uzziel squeaked, pointing a trembling finger at Gideon. “He was mopping the Barnabas!”

Seraphiel’s eyes, like pools of molten gold, settled on Gideon. Then on the halo merrily orbiting the mop head. He sighed, a sound like a bellows emptying. “Paperwork. Do you have a Form 7-B? ‘Application for Non-Celestial Interaction with Class-A Incorruptible’?”

Gideon blinked. “I… must have left it with my other vest.”

“No form,” Seraphiel said, shaking his head with grave disappointment. He touched a wick on the side of his sword. It burst into holy flame with a whoosh. “Then this is a violation of Celestial Ordinance 12.5, Subsection ‘Grave Robbing, Light’. I’m going to have to incinerate you.”

“Wait!” Gideon yelped, backpedaling. The cherubs fanned out, blocking his retreat. “There’s a misunderstanding! This isn’t theft! It’s… a proactive decontamination!”

Seraphiel paused, his flaming sword held aloft. “Explain.”

The words tumbled out of Gideon, greased by panic. “I’m from the new Hazardous Spiritual Materials division. We got a tip. A possible… leak.” He gestured vaguely at the halo. “In the containment field. Could be toxic. I’m here to safely contain and remove the asset for testing.”

The taste in his mouth became overwhelmingly of licorice and mothballs. A whopper. A glorious, terrible lie.

Seraphiel’s fiery gaze flickered. “A leak? In the Barnabas? That’s a Tier-One stable relic. Its containment field was blessed by the Trinity itself.”

“Even the best blessings can get a hairline crack,” Gideon said, tapping the side of his nose as if sharing trade secrets. “Divine entropy. It’s a silent killer. You’d never see it until the… the sanctimony starts seeping out. Very corrosive to marble.”

Uzziel gasped. Zephon looked worriedly at the floor.

Seraphiel lowered his sword a fraction. “I haven’t received any memo about a Hazardous Spiritual Materials division.”

“Brand new,” Gideon pressed. “Headed by… Archangel Metatron. You know how he is with paperwork. Always behind. Probably stuck in triplicate.” He leaned in. “But between you and me, the ozone levels in here? Off the charts. That’s not holiness, my friend. That’s a containment breach.”

He was making this up as he went, but the pieces were clicking into a bizarre, plausible whole in the face of celestial bureaucracy. The Seraph’s expression shifted from wrathful to bureaucratic concern.

“A breach,” Seraphiel murmured. “That would explain the odd taste in the manna this morning.” He nodded, then his eyes narrowed again. “But protocol is protocol. I still need to see a work order for a Tier-One hazardous removal. Even a verbal order from Metatron logged in the Celestial Register.”

Gideon’s shoulders slumped. He was so close. The halo was right there. He could almost grab it.

A new voice cut through the chamber, deep enough to vibrate the fillings Gideon had left.

“THERE WILL BE NO LOGGING.”

An Archangel filled the far doorway. Not with his size, though he was large, but with his presence. Light didn’t so much shine from him as get politely pushed out of the way. He wore robes of deep blue, and a simple, unadorned circlet of light hovered over his brow. In his hands was a net woven from strands of pure twilight. His face was not angry. It was profoundly, cosmically inconvenienced.

“Archangel Cassiel,” Seraphiel intoned, bowing his head. The cherubs fell to their knees, wings drooping.

Cassiel’s eyes, like twin supernovae in the distant past, locked onto Gideon. “You. Custodian. You have initiated an unsanctioned spiritual artifact relocation event.”

“It’s a decontamination—” Gideon tried.

“Do not,” Cassiel said, the words dropping like stones, “insult me with mortal fabrication. I am the Keeper of the Silent Watches. I observe. I know the taste of a lie in the cosmic wind. And you, Gideon, janitor of the third sub-basement, are marinating in one.”

The metallic taste in Gideon’s mouth vanished. Replaced by nothing. A void. Cassiel’s statement was so true it erased the very possibility of deception.

Cassiel raised the net. “The halo is not a relic. It is a Class-12 Celestial Containment Vessel. That ‘light’ is a stabilization field for a primordial soul-virus, last active during the War in Heaven. The angels you see here?” He gestured with disdain at the cowering cherubs and the confused Seraph. “They are not its guards. They are its warning label. Ineffectual by design. A distraction. The real security is that the virus is harmless… as long as the halo remains here, in this specific geomantic alignment.”

Gideon’s hand was numb on the mop handle. The halo, his beautiful, butter-yellow prize, suddenly looked less like treasure and more like a glowing biohazard symbol. “You… you use halos as petri dishes?”

The cherubs whimpered. Seraphiel looked at his flaming sword as if suddenly realizing it was just a fancy lighter.

Gideon looked at the halo. He looked at the net. He looked at his mop, which gave a hopeful, hungry gurgle.

His mother’s face flashed in his mind. Not as she was at the end, but in the middle, laughing, pointing at a stain on the ceiling he’d missed. “You call that clean, Gid? I can still see the sin!”

He couldn’t clean this. Not really. But he couldn’t put it back, either. Not knowing what it was. The whole place was a lie, a beautiful, polished, lemon-scented lie.

Cassiel took another step. “Do not make me use the net. It is terrible for the floor wax.”

Gideon made a decision. It wasn’t smart. It wasn’t clean. It was the exact opposite of clean.

He swung the mop like a batter going for a foul ball.

The anti-gravity field surged. The halo shot away from the mop head not toward the case, but straight up, zipping toward the vaulted ceiling like a golden coin tossed in a well. Every celestial head in the room snapped up to track it.

“NO!” Cassiel boomed.

He ran for the nearest display case—the one holding the “Tears of a Saint (Probably Peter).” He brought the mop down on the corner of the case. The glass didn’t break. It yawned open, becoming soft and pliable.

He snatched the vial.

“Hey!” shrieked Uzziel.

Gideon turned, uncorked the vial with his teeth, and flung its contents—a single, tear—not at Cassiel, but at the floor between them.

The tear hit the polished marble.

Nothing happened for a second. Then, a small, perfect circle of the floor began to weep. Clear, salty water welled up and spread, a growing puddle of profound, sanctified sadness.

Cassiel, stepping forward to throw his net, hit the puddle. His foot slid. The Archangel of the Silent Watches, the Keeper of Secrets, did a perfect, slow-motion splits. A sound escaped him—less a boom, more a high-pitched, surprised “YIPE!”

The net flew from his hands, arcing through the air toward Seraphiel, who instinctively raised his flaming sword to block. The twilight-net met the holy flame.

There was a sound like the universe tearing a cheap bedsheet.

The net dissolved into a shower of sparkles. The sword’s flame went out with a pathetic puff. Seraphiel stared at the now-smoking, slightly sooty stick in his hand.

The halo, forgotten, reached the apex of its climb and began to fall.

Straight toward the weeping puddle.

Gideon dove. He slid across the dry marble on his janitorial vest, his caddy clattering, his hand outstretched. The mop, still active, tumbled from his other hand and skittered away, buzzing happily toward a display of saintly fingernail clippings.

His fingers closed around the cool, smooth metal of the halo just as it was about to hit the salty water.

He rolled to his feet, halo clutched to his chest. The room was chaos. Cassiel was struggling to rise from his splits, his robes soaked with saint-tear water. Seraphiel was trying to relight his sword by rubbing it on his robes. The two cherubs were trying to help Cassiel up, but were just pulling on his wings in opposite directions.

Gideon looked at the halo in his hand. The butter-yellow light pulsed. He didn’t taste anything. Just the dry, flat flavor of adrenaline.

Cassiel finally found his footing. His majestic face was a mask of pure, undiluted fury. Water dripped from his beard. “YOU… YOU HAVE COMMITTED… UNAUTHORIZED SPIRITUAL ARTIFACT RELOCATION! AND VANDALISM WITH A SACRED TEAR!”

Gideon backed toward the archway he’d entered from. He held up the halo. “You said it was a virus.”

“IT IS CONTAINED!”

“It’s not clean!” Gideon shouted, the words surprising him with their vehemence. “Locking your mess in a shiny box isn’t cleaning! It’s just… hiding the stain!”

He turned and ran.

The shouts and discordant harp twangs faded behind him as he plunged back into the labyrinth of archives, the halo glowing against his dirty vest like a stolen streetlamp. He had it. He’d actually done it.

The taste finally came. Not a lie. Something else. Bitter coffee and cold marble. The taste of a very, very big problem he now owned.

And behind him, over the sound of celestial frustration, he heard Archangel Cassiel’s voice, echoing down the hall with the force of a divine memo.

“CLEANUP ON AISLE FIVE! AND SOMEONE GET ME A FORM 9-Z FOR ‘CATASTROPHIC PROTOCOL BREACH’! IN TRIPLICATE!”

The rooftop was slick with dew, and Gideon’s boots skidded on the lead sheeting. The halo pulsed against his ribs, a heartbeat of stolen light. Behind him, the shouts were less divine fury and more celestial administrative panic.

“He’s got the Barnabas!”
“And the vial!”
“Did anyone log the vial? We need to log the vial!”
“Form 12-C! Unlogged vial removal!”

He scrambled up a slope toward a higher parapet. The Vatican spread out below him, a geometric maze of piety and power. A wind kicked up, cold and smelling of incense and distant traffic. The halo in his hand grew warm. Not a comforting warmth. A fever warmth.

His mop was gone. He’d left it behind, buzzing merrily among the fingernails. He felt its absence like a missing limb. The caddy on his belt held only a sponge, a squeegee, and a bottle of universal solvent labeled ‘For Spiritual & Temporal Grime.’ It was not rated for primordial soul-viruses.

A shadow fell over him, blocking the moon. Gideon looked up.

Cassiel stood on the roof peak above, his robes still damp, his circlet of light flickering with irritation. He wasn’t flying. He was just… there, as if the rooftop had agreed to elevate him out of spite. “You,” Cassiel said, his voice now a low, grating hum, “are a stain.”

“I’m a custodian,” Gideon panted, backing away. “Stains are my business.”

“You have stolen a containment vessel of immense spiritual toxicity. You have vandalized a sacred tear. You have caused an Archangel to perform an involuntary splits.” Cassiel’s eyes narrowed. “That last one is a personal grievance.”

“I can buff it out,” Gideon said, his fingers finding the halo’s edge. It had a seam. A tiny, almost imperceptible line where the light seemed to stutter. “Look, I’ll put it back. Just… tell me what’s in it. Really.”

Cassiel’s expression shifted from inconvenience to something colder. “Why?”

“Because I taste lies,” Gideon said, the words raw in the night air. “And this whole place tastes like saccharine and mothballs. My mom… she got sick. From a lie. A big, shiny, institutional lie. I can’t clean that. But I can clean this. If I know what it is.”

The Archangel studied him. The pursuit squad—Seraphiel and the two cherubs—clattered onto the rooftop behind Gideon, breathing heavily. Seraphiel’s sword was still unlit. He looked embarrassed.

“The halo,” Cassiel said finally, “contains the Soul-Virus ‘Acedia.’ Spiritual sloth. The corrosion of purpose. It is not violent. It does not burn or shriek. It simply… erodes. It makes great works feel small. It makes small sins feel inevitable. It was contained after it rendered a third of the Heavenly Choir… mildly apathetic.”

Gideon blinked. “You put the angelic version of burnout in a halo?”

“Containment is elegant,” Cassiel said. “It looks holy. It inspires reverence. No one questions a halo. They just… polish it.” He gestured to the glowing disc in Gideon’s hand. “That is the Barnabas. It contains ‘Ira.’ Holy wrath. Useful in measured doses. Catastrophic in pure form. It makes martyrs out of accountants. It turns petty grievances into crusades.”

The halo in Gideon’s hand pulsed again. He felt a heat in his palm, not burning, but pressing. A slow, insistent pressure. His own pulse began to thump in his temples, a dull, angry rhythm. He thought about Cassiel’s splits. He thought about the taste of burnt toast. A low, red haze tinged the edges of his vision.

“Oh,” Gideon said.

“You feel it,” Cassiel observed. “The vessel is not perfect. Emotional leakage. It is why we keep them here, in geomantic alignment. The marble, the prayers, the bureaucracy… it dampens the seepage.”

Gideon’s thumb found the seam. It was hot. “So you don’t clean the virus. You just… store the dirty rag in a fancy drawer.”

“Cleanliness is an illusion,” Cassiel said, stepping down from the peak. “Order is the only truth. You have disrupted order. Now you will be ordered.”

Seraphiel finally got his sword to light. It flared, a weak, orange gutter. “Yeah,” the Seraph said, trying to sound fierce. “Ordered!”

Gideon’s head was filling with a single, repeating thought: They deserve to be split again. All of them. Slowly.

That was the virus. Ira. Holy wrath. It was in his head, a hot, righteous leak.

He looked at the halo. The seam was glowing brighter. The containment field wasn’t just leaking; his handling, his panic, the rooftop chill—it was stressing the vessel. A tiny, hairline crack was forming in the light itself.

“It’s cracking,” Gideon said.

Cassiel froze. “Do not joke.”

“I’m not.” Gideon held it up. The butter-yellow light now had a thin, black fissure running through it, like a crack in an eggshell. A faint, dust—no, not dust, a glitter—began to puff from the crack. It smelled like ozone and hot iron.

“DROP IT!” Cassiel roared, genuine fear in his voice now.

But dropping it would crack it more. Gideon knew that in his bones. He’d spent three years in custodial training. You don’t drop a pressurized container. You secure it. You stabilize it.

He had no stabilizer. He had a janitorial caddy.

The glittering dust touched his hand. His skin didn’t burn. It… clarified. Every minor injustice of his life flashed before him: the time his supervisor stole his idea for a more efficient floor buffer, the neighbor who always parked in his spot, the way the coffee machine in the break room never quite cleaned itself. A hot, pure, delicious anger bloomed in his chest. It felt good. It felt right.

That was the problem.

Zephon, the cherub, sniffed the air. His eyes widened. “Ira leakage! Code Crimson!”

Uzziel, the four-eyed cherub, began to fumble with his harp. “What’s the containment protocol for Crimson?”

“I don’t know! I skipped that seminar! There was a free lunch at the ‘Harmony in Paperwork’ lecture!”

Cassiel lunged, not for Gideon, but for the halo. Gideon spun away, his back foot slipping on the dew. He fell, not off the roof, but onto it, hard. The halo bounced from his grip, skittering across the lead sheeting toward the edge.

The crack widened. More glittering dust plumed out, a small, angry cloud.

The cloud touched Seraphiel’s foot.

The Seraph stared at his foot. Then at Cassiel. Then at Gideon. His face, normally set in majestic patience, crumpled into a scowl of profound, petty annoyance. “You,” he seethed, pointing his flaming sword at Gideon. “You made me look foolish. In front of my subordinates.” The sword’s flame grew hotter, brighter, angrier. “I hate looking foolish.”

That was Ira. Turning a celestial warrior’s pride into a grudge.

Cassiel saw it. He saw the dust touching Zephon, who now glared at Uzziel with sudden, intense resentment over the flat G-string argument. He saw the glitter settling on his own damp robes. He felt a fresh, personal fury toward the saint whose tear had made him slip.

“Contain the leak!” Cassiel commanded, but his voice was tight, personal. “Now! Before it spreads beyond the rooftop!”

And tomorrow, there would be another stain.
