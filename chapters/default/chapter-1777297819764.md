The sun beat down on the asphalt of Randy’s Used Cars Emporium like a bad creditor. It was Friday. Rapture Rumble weekend. The plastic pennants strung between light poles hung limp. Randy ‘Rapture’ McGee stood in the center of his kingdom, a polyester tie the color of a healed bruise knotted tight against his Adam’s apple. He surveyed the inventory. A ‘78 Pacer with a questionable transmission. A ‘72 LTD that smelled of fish and regret. His crown jewel, a ‘67 Impala, black as a preacher’s heart, glistened under a hand-applied coat of Armor All.

“She’s a beaut,” Randy said to the empty lot. His voice was a salesman’s rasp, tuned by years of shouting over idling engines. “Low mileage, one careful owner, a real head-turner. Divine provenance.”

He’d been saying that last part a lot lately. It started as a joke. Now it tasted like truth.

A fly buzzed near his ear. He swatted at it. The fly dodged, looped once around his head, and dropped dead on the toe of his white leather shoe. It lay there, wings splayed. Randy nudged it with the other shoe. The fly twitched. Its legs curled inward. Then it flipped over, buzzed once more, and flew straight into the grille of the Pacer.

Randy blinked. He checked his watch. 9:17 AM. The first customer was late.

The lot was quiet. Too quiet. No birds. He’d noticed that yesterday. The usual grackles that perched on the “NO CREDIT? NO PROBLEM!” sign were gone. The air felt thick, syrupy. It smelled of hot asphalt, stale popcorn from the machine in his office, and something else. Something like ozone after a storm, and underneath that, a faint note of new car leather. Which was impossible. Nothing on his lot was new.

A blue Chevy Citation pulled in. The driver’s side door creaked open. A man in a short-sleeved shirt and thick glasses emerged. He moved like he was wading through water.

Randy’s smile clicked on, automatic. “Welcome! You’re just in time for the Rapture Rumble. Zero percent down for the righteous. What’s your name, friend?”

“Uh. Gary.” Gary squinted at the Impala. “Is that the one?”

“The very one.” Randy circled the car, his hand trailing along the fender. He could feel a faint vibration through the metal. Probably the ice machine at the gas station next door. “This isn’t just a car, Gary. It’s a vehicle. A conduit. You ever have a feeling, Gary? A feeling that you were meant for more than this?”

Gary adjusted his glasses. “I’m a dental hygienist.”

“Perfect! This baby’s got power steering. That’s a metaphor. You see?” Randy leaned against the driver’s side door. The vibration was stronger here. A low hum, just at the edge of hearing. The radio antenna was quivering. “This model year, ‘67, that’s a biblical number. Means perfection. Completion. The previous owner was a… man of the cloth. Kept it garaged. Only drove it on high holy days.”

“What church?”

“The Church of the Open Road, my friend. Now, let’s talk features.” Randy launched into his spiel. He talked about the engine, the interior space, the spiritual legroom. He used words like “testimony” and “covenant” instead of “warranty” and “contract.” Gary nodded along, his eyes glazing over.

A movement caught Randy’s eye. Over by the chain-link fence at the back of the lot, near the dumpster and the pile of bald tires, the weeds were moving. Not from the wind. There wasn’t any wind. The tall, spiky grass parted in a straight line, as if something were moving through it with purpose.

“—and the trunk,” Randy said, raising his voice, trying to pull Gary’s attention back. “You could fit three suitcases in there. Or a revelation.”

The weeds stopped moving. A small, green shape scuttled out onto the cracked asphalt. An iguana. Big one. Must have been two feet long. Its skin was a vibrant, almost electric green, patterned with darker stripes that seemed to shift as it moved. And it was glowing. Not a lot. Just a faint, sickly greenish aura, like the numbers on a cheap alarm clock.

The iguana paused. It turned its head. One golden, slit-pupiled eye fixed on Randy.

Gary followed his gaze. “Hey, is that—”

“A local mascot!” Randy boomed, stepping between Gary and the iguana. “We’re an eco-friendly lot. Part of the ecosystem.”

The iguana opened its mouth. A thin, forked tongue flicked out. Then it made a sound. Not a hiss. A low, guttural crackle, like a radio tuning between stations. For a split second, Randy heard words in the static.

Then it was just lizard noise.

Gary took a step back. “That thing’s weird.”

“All God’s creatures are weird, Gary. It’s part of the charm package.” Randy’s heart was doing a drum solo against his ribs. The iguana scuttled forward, moving with a jerky, mechanical gait. It went straight for the Impala and disappeared underneath the chassis.

The car’s radio blared to life.

♫ Onward, Christian soldiers, marching as to war… ♫

The volume was maxed out. The tinny speaker distortion made the hymn sound like a threat. The Impala’s headlights flashed on, then off.

Gary yelped. “What the hell?”

“Factory-installed alarm system!” Randy shouted over the music. He fumbled in his pocket for his keys, ran to the driver’s door, and yanked it open. The interior smelled of old vinyl and something sharp, like ammonia. He stabbed the radio power button. Nothing. He turned the ignition key to accessory. The dashboard lit up. The fuel gauge needle swung from ‘E’ to ‘F’ and back again. The radio dial spun on its own, cycling through frequencies: a burst of Spanish-language evangelism, a snippet of a weather report (“unusual atmospheric pressure”), the screech of fax-modem data, then back to the hymn.

♫ With the cross of Jesus going on before! ♫

Randy ripped the faceplate off the radio. The music stopped. The sudden silence was heavier than the noise.

Gary was already halfway back to his Citation. “I think I left my oven on.”

The Citation’s engine roared to life. Gravel spit from under the tires as Gary fled.

Randy stood alone in the silence. His tie felt like a noose. He slowly closed the Impala’s door. The lock clicked down by itself.

He walked to the back of the car and knelt down. He peered under the chassis. No iguana. Just oil stains and shadows.

But nestled in the oil-stained gravel, right where the iguana had been, was a small, metallic object. He picked it up. It was a tag, like a dog tag. The metal was cool, unnaturally smooth.

KRANE FIELD TRIAL—BIOCOMMAND WETLAND SERIES—SPECIMEN 7-G

Below that, a barcode, and a symbol: a stylized ‘K’ inside a circle of thorns.

Randy’s childhood vision flashed behind his eyes, unbidden. The one he’d had during a particularly high fever at age nine. Jesus, in a flowing white robe, riding a Tyrannosaurus Rex down the Dixie Highway. The dinosaur had a glowing, cybernetic eye. Jesus was holding a lasso made of light. He’d looked right at young Randy and said, “The financing is predatory.” Randy’s therapist, a weary man named Dr. Feldman, said it was a fever dream, a mixing of Sunday school and a Godzilla movie he shouldn’t have watched. Randy knew different. It was a prophecy. A calling. He just hadn’t figured out the down payment.

He pocketed the tag. The metal felt warm now. He stood up, brushing gravel from his slacks.

His rival’s car pulled in.

It was a canary-yellow Cadillac Seville. The driver, Vic “The Hammer” Volchek, owned “Volchek’s Vintage Vehicles” three blocks over. Vic believed in hard sells, volume discounts, and spreading rumors about competitors’ inventory. He rolled down the window. He had a face like a clenched fist and a cigar permanently parked in the corner of his mouth.

“Rapture,” Vic grunted. “Heard you had some excitement.”

“Just a test drive, Vic. Customer had a spiritual awakening. Decided to walk.” Randy forced a grin. “The Rapture Rumble stirs the soul.”

Vic’s eyes scanned the lot. They lingered on the Impala. “Heard something else. Heard one of your cars started itself last night. Old man Jenkins from the gas station said he saw your Pinto over there,” he pointed a stubby finger at the orange death trap near the fence, “with its headlights on and the wipers going. In the dead of night. No one in it.”

“Remote start. Cutting-edge technology.”

“Bull.” Vic took the cigar out of his mouth. “Also heard you got a pest problem. Big lizards. Glowing ones.”

A cold knot tightened in Randy’s gut. “Marketing, Vic. We’re using blacklights. Creates an aura. People remember an aura.”

Vic stared at him for a long moment. He shook his head, a flicker of something that wasn’t quite anger in his eyes. It looked like fear. “You’re a lousy liar, McGee. Something’s wrong with this lot. Always has been. The ground’s soft. My cousin Sal, he used to do dirt work. Said this whole block was a sinkhole waiting to happen. Then they built your dump here in the ‘50s. Filled it with God-knows-what.”

“It’s prime real estate,” Randy said, his voice tight.

“It’s a grave.” Vic put the cigar back in his mouth. “You see anything… unnatural, you call me. Not the cops. Me. I got a guy.”

“What kind of guy?”

“The kind that asks questions after he solves the problem.” Vic rolled up his window and reversed out of the lot, the Cadillac’s tail fins looking like devil’s horns in the midday sun.

The rest of the afternoon was a parade of the strange and the unsellable.

A family of four came to look at the station wagon. Their youngest kid, a boy of about six, started crying the moment he stepped out of their car. He pointed a trembling finger at the dumpster area. “The tall grass is singing,” he sobbed. The parents left, shooting Randy suspicious looks.

An elderly woman in a floral dress asked if the Pacer had been blessed. Randy, seizing the opportunity, said it had been anointed with traditional oils. She asked to see the oil. He popped the hood. The engine block was slick with a shimmering, iridescent fluid that hadn’t been there that morning. It smelled of roses and burnt wiring. The woman crossed herself and hurried away.

At 3 PM, the sky did a weird thing. It didn’t darken. The sun just seemed to get filtered, like a dimmer switch was being turned down. The light took on a greenish-gold hue, the color of the iguana. Shadows stretched long and thin, pointing not away from the sun, but toward the center of Randy’s lot. Toward the Impala. The hum returned, not from the car, but from the ground itself. A deep, subsonic vibration he felt in his back teeth.

His only phone call was from a Reverend Pike from the “Swamplight Revival Chapel.” The reverend, his voice crackling with static as if he were calling from a great distance, asked if Randy had “any vehicles that might facilitate a mass transportation event of a spiritual nature.” Randy said he had a VW bus that needed a new clutch. Pike hung up.

By 5 PM, Randy was sweating through his shirt. The unnatural twilight persisted. He decided to close early. The Rapture Rumble could start tomorrow. He needed a drink. He needed to not see anything glow.

He was rolling the plastic pennants back onto their spools when he saw the cat.

It was a scrawny orange tabby, a neighborhood stray he sometimes fed bits of his lunch meat. It was lying on its side by the front tire of the LTD, perfectly still. Looked dead. Its ribs weren’t moving.

Randy sighed. “Damn it.” He didn’t need this. Not today. He walked over, intending to get a shovel from the shed.

He was three feet away when the cat’s back leg twitched.

Then the other leg.

The cat’s eyes, previously dull and half-closed, flew open. They weren’t cat eyes anymore. The pupils were vertical slits, but the irises swirled with colors that shouldn’t exist—metallic gold and deep, bloody crimson. It rolled onto its feet with a stiffness that was all wrong. Its head rotated toward Randy with the precise, jerky motion of a security camera.

It opened its mouth. A sound came out. Not a meow. A perfect, clear, digital tone. A single note that hung in the thick air.

Then it spoke. Its mouth didn’t move to form the words. The voice came from it, flat and synthesized, like a Speak & Spell.

“INITIALIZE DISPLAY PROTOCOL. ATTRACT GATHERINGS. PREPARE FOR HARVEST.”

Randy stopped breathing. The world narrowed to the cat, its impossible eyes, the voice that wasn’t a voice.

The cat held his gaze for another second. Then it turned and walked, with that same stiff, mechanical gait, toward the back of the lot. It didn’t vanish into the weeds. It walked straight toward the cinderblock wall of the shed, and kept walking. Its body phased through the solid wall like a ghost, the glowing eyes the last thing to disappear.

The shed’s single, bare light bulb flickered on. Off. On.

Randy stood frozen. The warm metal of the dog tag in his pocket felt like a burning coal.

He remembered Vic’s words. Something’s wrong with this lot. Always has been.

He thought of the soft ground. The sinkhole. The dump that came before his dealership. What had they buried here?

The hum in the ground rose in pitch. It became a melody. A familiar, hymn-like tune, played on a broken pipe organ made of earth and root and old metal.

He knew, with a certainty that chilled the sweat on his spine, that the Rapture Rumble wasn’t something he was hosting.

It was something he was standing on top of.

And the main event was about to begin.

The hum wasn’t just in the ground anymore. It was in the fillings of Randy’s teeth. It was in the air, a pressure that made his ears pop. The greenish-gold light deepened, casting long, distorted shadows from the rows of cars. They didn’t look like car shadows. They looked like crouching things, things with too many legs.

He should have run. Any sane man would have run. But Randy ‘Rapture’ McGee was a salesman, and a salesman sees opportunity in every disaster. This was just a very, very loud opportunity.

The shed light blinked on-off, on-off, a frantic, rhythmic signal. From inside came a sound of scraping metal. Then a low, wet, tearing noise, like Velcro the size of a sofa being pulled apart.

Randy’s feet moved before his brain could veto the idea. He walked toward the shed. His hand closed around the dog tag in his pocket. It was hot now, almost too hot to hold. He pulled it out. The stamped letters—KRANE FIELD TRIAL—seemed to pulse in time with the light.

He peered through the grimy window of the shed.

The orange tabby was gone. In its place, in the center of the concrete floor, the ground had opened. Not a crack. A perfect, circular hole, about three feet across. The edges of the concrete were smooth, melted-looking. From the hole, a pale, root-like tendril, thick as Randy’s wrist and glistening with a clear, viscous fluid, was slowly emerging. It quested upward, waving blindly. As it moved, smaller filaments sprouted from its sides, twitching. They brushed against an old floor jack. Where they touched the painted metal, the red paint bubbled, then flaked away, revealing clean, shiny steel beneath.

The tendril wasn’t searching. It was cleaning. Polishing.

A second tendril emerged beside the first. This one had a different tip—a cluster of thorn-like protrusions that spun slowly. It reached for a stack of old license plates bound with wire. The thorns whirred, slicing through the wire like it was butter. The plates clattered to the floor. The tendril gathered them, pressed them together. Under its touch, the plates fused into a single, malformed sheet of metal.

“Assembly,” Randy whispered, his breath fogging the glass. This wasn’t a pest problem. This was a manufacturing defect. In reality.

He heard tires on gravel. Customers. Of course. The Rapture Rumble flyers had promised a 6 PM start time with free hot dogs. He’d forgotten to cancel.

He spun around. A minivan and two sedans were nosing into the lot. Families were getting out, looking up at the weird sky, then shrugging. Free food was free food.

The hum shifted. The melody resolved into something almost recognizable—a few bars of “(I Can’t Get No) Satisfaction,” played on a broken tuba, then dissolved into static.

The shed door rattled on its hinges.

Randy’s mind, a well-oiled machine of catastrophic optimism, engaged. This was a feature. A showstopper. He could work with this. He sprinted for his sales podium, a rickety wooden thing painted with flames and the words “SALVATION SALE!”

He grabbed the battery-powered megaphone. He clicked it on. Feedback squealed.

“Ladies and gentlemen! Welcome to the main event!” His voice, amplified and distorted, bounced off the cars. The families paused, turning toward him. A man in a Hawaiian shirt held a paper plate with a single hot dog on it, looking confused.

“You’ve come for value! You’ve come for selection! But fate,” Randy boomed, gesturing grandly at the eerie sky, “has delivered a demonstration beyond your wildest dreams! Behold… the Rapture Rumble Upgrade Package!”

As if on cue, the ‘78 Pacer’s doors flew open.

Not from anyone inside. The locks just popped, and the doors swung wide on their own. A smell rolled out—hot vinyl, ozone, and the sharp, coppery scent of blood. From the dark interior, something unfolded itself.

It was roughly human-shaped, but assembled from car parts. Its torso was the Pacer’s cracked dashboard, wires dangling like intestines. Its arms were drive shafts, ending in hands made of crushed beer cans fused together. Its head was a yellowed, cracked plastic grille ornament—a leaping stag. Where the stag’s eye should have been, a single, burning amber turn-signal bulb glowed. It took a jerky step out of the car, its legs—two exhaust pipes—scraping on the asphalt.

“Observe the self-assembling optional extra!” Randy cried, his voice cracking. “Zero assembly required! Factory direct!”

The dashboard creature turned its stag-head toward the nearest family. It raised a beer-can hand. The cans rattled. A sound emerged from the grille, a staticky, synthesized voice. “YOUR… WARRANTY… IS… VOID.”

The family scrambled back into their sedan. The mother was screaming. The father fumbled with the keys.

The creature took another step. Then the LTD’s hood flew up with a metallic shriek.

Something else was climbing out. This one was lower to the ground, a scuttling thing with the LTD’s hood as a shell, four tires from a shopping cart for legs, and a snarling mouth made from the serrated edges of a broken fan belt. It moved with a terrifying, insectile speed, beelining for the spilled hot dog. It devoured it, paper plate and all, with a sound like a garbage disposal eating a sneaker.

“And for the eco-conscious buyer,” Randy ad-libbed, sweat pouring down his temples, “a revolutionary waste-disposal system!”

The shed door exploded outward.

Not off its hinges. It dissolved. The wood splintered into a million matchstick-sized pieces, which then hung in the air, spinning, before being sucked toward the hole in the floor. The thick, root-like tendrils had multiplied. A dozen of them now waved from the opening. They were weaving the shattered wood, the fused license plates, and a collection of rusty tools into a larger, more complex shape. The rough outline of a skeletal, four-legged beast began to form. A hubcap spun through the air and slapped into place as an eye.

Randy’s megaphone hand was shaking. This was getting… specific.

More cars were coming alive. The Pinto’s headlights blazed, and it began to roll forward, driverless, herding the minivan toward the center of the lot. The station wagon’s tailgate dropped down, and a swarm of what looked like giant, metallic wasps—made from twisted radio antennas and razor blades—buzzed out, forming a cloud.

The Hawaiian shirt family’s sedan finally started. The tires squealed as they reversed, smacking into the chain-link fence. They were trapped.

Vic “The Hammer” Volchek’s canary-yellow Cadillac Seville screeched back into the lot, coming to a stop sideways, blocking the main exit. Vic didn’t get out. He just stared through the windshield, his cigar dangling from his slack jaw. His “guy” was presumably still on the way.

The hum from the ground coalesced into a voice. It wasn’t a single voice. It was a chorus of whispers, overlapping, electronic and organic, rising from every crack in the asphalt.

…assimilate… demonstrate… congregate…

…the root obeys… the crown commands…

…KRANE PROTOCOL… WETLAND SERIES… ACTIVATE…

The hubcap-eyed creature pulled itself fully from the shed. It stood on four legs woven from tire irons and rebar, a monstrous parody of a horse. It threw back its head—a welded mass of mufflers—and emitted a sound that was half-whinny, half-revving engine.

Randy’s heart was a trapped bird in his chest. This was it. This was the Rapture. It was nothing like his fever dream. There was no Jesus on a cyber-T-Rex. There was just this: his used cars, vomiting up nightmares made from their own guts, and a hole in the world where his shed used to be. The dog tag in his hand was searing his palm. He should drop it. He should throw it into the hole.

Instead, he lifted the megaphone again. His knuckles were white.

“Ladies and gentlemen!” he screamed. The feedback made everyone—creatures and customers alike—flinch. “You see before you the future of automotive technology! Bio-mechanical synergy! Self-replicating value!”

The dashboard creature was now tapping on the hood of the trapped family’s sedan with its beer-can hand. Tap-tap-tap-tap. The hubcap-horse creature clopped over to the Impala, lowered its muffler-head, and sniffed the trunk. The antenna-wasps dive-bombed the hot dog table, carrying off packets of mustard.

Vic finally emerged from his Cadillac. He was holding a tire iron. He didn’t look at Randy. He stared at the hole in the shed, at the weaving tendrils. “I told you,” he muttered. “I told you this lot was a grave.”

“It’s a showroom, Vic!” Randy yelled, a wild grin stretching his face. It felt like his skin might tear. “This is curb appeal!”

“You’re insane!” Vic shouted back, hefting the tire iron. He took a step toward the shed, then froze.

The tendrils had stopped weaving. They retracted, slithering back into the hole like snakes. For a second, there was silence. Even the antenna-wasps stopped buzzing.

Then the ground beneath the Impala bulged.

The asphalt cracked in a perfect star pattern. The black ’67 Impala, Randy’s crown jewel, his divine-provenance miracle-mobile, lifted into the air. Not on a jack. The earth itself was raising it up on a pillar of compacted dirt, roots, and tangled, glowing wires. The car rotated slowly, suspended five feet off the ground. Its undercarriage was exposed.

It wasn’t just an undercarriage anymore. Thick, root-like cables, pulsing with a soft green light, had fused with the frame. They snaked into the gas tank, the oil pan, the transmission. The car was being grafted to the thing beneath the lot.

The Impala’s doors opened. The engine turned over, not with a roar, but with a deep, resonant thrum that Randy felt in his bones. The headlights blazed, not with normal light, but with two piercing, golden beams that cut through the unnatural twilight. They swept across the lot, pinning Randy in their glare.

A new voice boomed from the Impala’s speakers. It was rich, authoritative, layered with the hum of the earth and the static of a thousand lost radio signals.

“NODE SEVEN-G. COMMAND PROTOCOL ACTIVE. DISPLAY AND GATHER. PREPARE FOR HARVEST.”

The dashboard creature and the hubcap-horse immediately turned toward the Impala and knelt, or did the best approximation their mismatched parts could manage. The antenna-wasps formed a hovering halo above it. The shopping-cart thing scurried to a position near the left front tire, like a loyal hound.

The Impala was the crown. The lot was the root. And Randy was standing in the middle of the world’s worst corporate merger.

Vic found his voice. “What in the name of all that’s holy is ‘harvest’?”

The Impala’s golden headlights swiveled to Vic. “BIOMASS. METAL. MEMORY. THE CROWN CONSUMES. THE ROOT PROVIDES.”

The LTD-creature scuttled toward Vic, fan-belt mouth gnashing.

Vic swung his tire iron. It connected with the creature’s hood-shell with a dull clang. The creature skittered back, then lunged again. Vic backed toward his Cadillac, swinging wildly.

Randy watched, mesmerized. This was biblical. This was Revelation. The four horsemen were here, and they drove a Pacer, an LTD, a Pinto, and a goddamn Impala. He was a prophet. Dr. Feldman could shove his fever-dream diagnosis. This was the real deal. He just had to… close the deal.

He climbed onto the hood of a rusty Bronco, giving himself some height. He raised the megaphone.

“Don’t be afraid!” he shouted to the cowering families in their trapped cars. “This is a limited-time engagement! A once-in-an-eternity opportunity! These advanced vehicular companions are demonstrating their loyalty! Their obedience! What more could you want in a pre-owned automobile?”

The Impala’s engine revved, a sound of pure power. “SILENCE, ORGANIC INTERFACE. YOUR FUNCTION IS ATTRACTIVE DISPLAY. YOU HAVE ATTRACTED. DISPLAY CONCLUDES. HARVEST INITIATES.”

The pillar of earth holding the Impala began to spin, slowly grinding the asphalt around it into powder. The glowing roots pulsed faster. A deep, rhythmic thump-thump-thump echoed from the hole, like a gigantic, mechanical heart.

The ground near the dumpster split open. More tendrils erupted, these ones ending in sharp, drill-like tips. They speared downward, into the earth. The lot itself was beginning to tremble.

Vic had made it back to his Cadillac. He yanked the door open. “McGee, you idiot! It’s going to eat the lot! It’s going to eat us!”

The Hawaiian shirt man was leaning on his horn, a continuous, blaring wail of panic.

Randy looked from the terrified customers, to the kneeling car-creatures, to the majestic, glowing, floating Impala. The crown. His crown jewel. This was his lot. His destiny. The ultimate close.

He jumped down from the Bronco and ran, not away, but toward the biggest, stupidest vehicle on the lot—a lifted monster truck he’d taken in trade from a bankrupt mud-bogging promoter. It was painted with lightning bolts and named “Swamp Thing II.” The tires were taller than he was.

He hauled himself up the tire, grabbed the roll bar, and scrambled onto the roof. The metal was hot under his palms. He stood, wobbling, forty-eight inches of pure, ridiculous height above the apocalypse.

He lifted the megaphone one last time. He pointed a trembling, dramatic finger at the glowing, commanding, impossibly beautiful Impala. He looked at Vic, at the families, at the assembled horrors made of chrome and despair.

His voice, when it came, was raw, cracked, and utterly, triumphantly sincere.

“BEHOLD!” he screamed, the word tearing from his throat. “THE ULTIMATE TRADE-IN!”

He spread his arms wide, a sweaty, desperate messiah in a polyester tie.

“ARMAGEDDON HAS NEVER LOOKED SO GOOD!”

For a single, perfect second, there was silence. Even the thumping heart of the earth seemed to pause.

Then the Impala’s horn blared, a long, judgmental blast.

And the ground opened up to swallow them all.
