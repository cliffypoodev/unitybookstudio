The air in the underground lab tasted like burnt copper and ozone, with a faint, greasy undertone of fried dinosaur. Colonel Augustus Cain ignored the smell. He’d been ignoring it for thirty-six hours straight, running on coffee that tasted like battery acid and a conviction that was curdling into obsession.

The Serpent Engine dominated the chamber. It wasn’t a sleek, futuristic marvel. It was a Frankenstein’s monster of salvaged parts: the main housing was a repurposed cooling tower from a decommissioned nuclear sub, wrapped in thick cables that snaked across the floor like sleeping anacondas. At its heart, visible through a quartz viewing port, a sphere of unstable energy pulsed a sickly, gangrenous green. It hummed at a frequency that made Cain’s back teeth ache.

“The temporal alignment is still fluctuating, Colonel.” Dr. Krane didn’t look up from his bank of monitors. His voice was a dry rustle, like pages turning in a forgotten manual. “The harmonics are… dissonant. Like trying to tune a radio during a thunderstorm.”

“I didn’t ask for a poetry reading, Doctor. I asked for an operational weapon.” Cain’s knuckles were white where he gripped the railing overlooking the pit. “Lux’s broadcast goes live at sunrise. That’s in ninety-three minutes. The messiah is sedated in holding cell three. The asset is secured in the containment bay. What part of this equation is giving you a headache?”

“The part where the engine’s core is a literal tear in the fabric of causality, sir. It’s not a toaster. You can’t just crank the dial to ‘smite.’” Krane tapped a screen. A jagged sine wave spiked erratically. “Feeding the subject’s unique bio-signature into the matrix might stabilize it. Or it might cause a recursive feedback loop that un-spools local reality into a state of permanent metaphysical confusion.”

“Will it look good on television?”

Krane blinked. “I… suppose that depends on your aesthetic preferences regarding existential horror.”

“Then proceed.” Cain turned away from the railing. His knee gave a familiar, grinding twinge. An old injury from a jump that hadn’t gone according to plan. He favored the leg as he walked, a slight hitch in his step that felt more pronounced tonight.

The lab was a hive of grim activity. Technicians in LAZARUS project jumpsuits moved with the frantic, hushed energy of men who knew the ceiling could collapse at any moment. In a corner, draped over a chair like a discarded snakeskin, was Reverend Bobby Lux’s white silk suit jacket. Its owner was upstairs in the derelict theme park’s broadcast booth, no doubt practicing his beatific smile in a mirror.

A door hissed open on the far side of the chamber. Catalina Morado—La Reina Blanca—walked in as if she owned the fog she’d brought with her. She wore a cream-colored pantsuit that probably cost more than Cain’s annual salary. Two of her men flanked her, their eyes doing the looking for her.

“Colonel.” Her smile was a scalpel. “You look tired.”

“I’m busy, Catalina. If you’re here for a tour, the gift shop is closed.”

“I’m here for my product. The blood samples from the asset. Our agreement.” She stopped a few feet from him, close enough that he caught her perfume. Something expensive and floral, masking something colder.

“The asset is integral to the engine’s calibration. You’ll get your samples after the broadcast.”

“The deal was for samples before your spectacle. My biochemists are on a clock. Resurrección doesn’t synthesize itself.”

“Your drug timetable is not my operational priority.” Cain kept his voice flat. “The asset stays whole until I say otherwise.”

One of her men shifted his weight. Cain didn’t look at him. He watched La Reina’s eyes. They were the color of old whiskey and just as deceptive.

“You have my money,” she said softly. “You have my silence. You do not have my patience. A pint of blood, Colonel. That’s all. Or do I need to call my friends at the Miami Herald and ask them what they know about buried cost-overruns at a certain DOD black site?”

The hum of the Serpent Engine seemed to swell for a second. Cain felt a vein in his temple begin a low, rhythmic throbbing. He imagined, very briefly, the satisfying crunch of her trachea under his thumb.

“Krane,” he called, not breaking eye contact. “Draw a liter from the asset. Package it for the lady.”

“A liter is two pints, Colonel,” Krane corrected from his console.

“I failed home economics. Just do it.”

La Reina’s smile widened, all victory and veneers. “A pleasure doing business.” She turned to leave, then paused. “Oh. I took the liberty of posting some of my men at the perimeter gates. For… added security. In case the messiah’s fan club gets any ideas.”

She glided out, her men trailing her. The door hissed shut.

Cain let out a breath he hadn’t realized he’d been holding. It fogged briefly in the lab’s chill air. He walked over to Krane’s station. “How long for the draw?”

“Fifteen minutes. The asset won’t like it.” Krane adjusted his glasses. “The raptor squadron is prepped and on standby in the old ‘Daniel’s Den’ exhibit, as you requested. Though I must reiterate, their behavioral matrices are still… unpredictable.”

“I don’t need them predictable. I need them to look terrifying on camera and chew on anyone who isn’t us.” Cain leaned over, peering at the main monitor. The green sphere in the engine core flickered, throwing jagged shadows across the doctor’s face. “What’s the real risk, Krane? No metaphors.”

Krane was silent for a long moment. He picked up a plastic coffee stirrer, bent it into a perfect right angle, then bent it back the other way. A tiny white stress fracture appeared in the middle. “We’re trying to channel a power source we don’t understand through a filter we didn’t build to achieve an outcome we can’t model. The risk is everything. The risk is the air turning to glass. The risk is our memories unraveling backwards. The risk is him,” he nodded toward the holding cells, “becoming a temporary black hole of compassion in the middle of your television show.” He dropped the broken stirrer. “It’s a bad idea, sir.”

“They’re all bad ideas,” Cain said. “That’s why they pay us.” He pushed off the console. “Start the calibration sequence. Use the subject’s bio-signature. I want that engine singing in thirty minutes.”

He left Krane to his work and stalked toward the holding block. The corridor lights were a harsh, antiseptic white. They made the peeling ‘Eden World – This Way to Salvation!’ sign on the wall look especially pathetic. His boot scuffed a piece of broken plastic on the floor—a fragment of a smiling cartoon snake from the park’s old ‘Garden of Eden’ dark ride. He kicked it aside.

The viewing port into cell three was a square of reinforced glass. Inside, Jesus of Nazareth sat on the edge of a metal cot. He wasn’t trying the door. He wasn’t pounding the walls. He was just sitting, hands resting on his knees, looking at the far wall. He’d refused the orange jumpsuit. He wore his own simple, homespun pants and tunic, now stained with dirt and the chemical residue from the tranq darts they’d used to bag him. His feet were bare, calloused, and very dirty.

Cain watched him. The man’s calm was an irritant. It was a void where there should have been fear, or anger, or bargaining. Cain had seen all three in this very block, from tougher men than this.

“He ask for anything?” Cain asked the guard stationed by the door.

“No, sir. He did… thank me for the water. When I brought it.”

“Of course he did.” Cain keyed the intercom. “Comfortable?”

Jesus turned his head. His eyes found Cain’s through the glass. They were a very ordinary shade of brown. That was the thing that got under Cain’s skin the most. There was no cosmic fire in them, no swirling galaxies. They were just eyes. Tired eyes.

“The cot is sufficient,” Jesus said. His voice through the speaker was calm, mild. “The hum is new. It’s… anxious.”

“That’s progress. You’re going to help it find its purpose.”

“Machines don’t have purposes. Only people do. And often, they are mistaken.”

Cain felt that throbbing in his temple again. “In a little while, you’re going to be part of the greatest scientific demonstration in human history. You should be flattered.”

“Flattery is a lie wrapped in velvet.” Jesus looked back at the wall. “The man in the white suit. He plans to kill me during his broadcast, doesn’t he?”

“Reverend Lux is a showman. He understands narrative stakes.”

“And you will let him?”

“My concern is the machine. What Lux does for ratings is his business.” Cain leaned closer to the intercom. “Unless you’d like to offer me a better deal. A demonstration of cooperation. Something to make me reconsider our partnership with the good reverend.”

Jesus was quiet for so long Cain thought he might not answer. Then he said, “You are a man who builds cages and calls them solutions. I cannot help you build a better cage.”

The intercom crackled with the force of Cain’s exhale. “Suit yourself.” He clicked it off.

As he turned from the window, a klaxon blared—not the piercing alarm of a breach, but the low, urgent honk of a system alert. Krane’s voice came over the lab-wide comm, strained. “Colonel to the primary console. We have an anomaly.”

Cain moved, the hitch in his step forgotten. He reached Krane’s side in seconds. The monitor showed a schematic of the park’s eastern perimeter. Three of the motion sensors were blinking red.

“Animal?” Cain snapped.

“Too big for a raccoon. Too… many for a stray dog.” Krane zoomed the feed. The grainy thermal image showed a cluster of heat signatures moving through the overgrown topiary of the ‘Peaceable Kingdom’ section. They were low to the ground. Fast. Their thermal profiles were a chaotic mix of hot mechanical cores and cooler organic limbs.

“Your raptors,” Cain said, his voice dangerously quiet.

“Impossible. They’re in standby. The command crown frequencies are locked to this console.” Krane’s fingers flew over the keyboard, pulling up another screen. The status readout for the Raptor-Apostle squad showed all twelve units powered down in their charging bays.

On the thermal feed, the pack of signatures moved past the crumbling plaster whale of ‘Jonah’s Journey’ and melted into the shadow of the main concourse. Headed toward the broadcast tower.

“Then who,” Cain asked, “is driving them?”

The main screen flickered. The schematics vanished, replaced by a single line of glowing green text, typed out in a familiar, blocky font.

KEVIN 6:9: HELLO DOCTOR. WE CAME HOME. CAN WE TALK ABOUT THE PROGRAMMING?

Krane went very still. He slowly bent another coffee stirrer until it snapped.

Cain stared at the message. The Serpent Engine’s hum climbed half an octave, filling the silent lab with its hungry, discordant song. Outside, somewhere in the pre-dawn gloom of the abandoned park, a raptor screeched. It didn’t sound like a machine. It sounded like something laughing.

The broadcast booth wasn't a booth. It was a plywood platform nailed together over the cracked concrete base of the Eden World wishing well, draped with enough white silk to shroud a small elephant. Bobby Lux stood at the center of it all, practicing his eyebrow raises in a full-length mirror propped against a defunct coin-operated telescope.

“My friends,” he whispered, letting his voice tremble just so. “We stand today on the precipice of a miracle.” He paused, letting the imaginary camera drink him in. The dawn light was still a dirty gray smear to the east, but the klieg lights his crew had rigged bathed him in a holy, sweat-inducing glow. His white suit was a second skin, starched to a crispness that could deflect sin.

A stagehand scurried up the steps, trailing cable. “Reverend, the audio feed from the lab is still patchy. We’re getting a hum.”

“A hum of divine origin, Billy. Work with me.” Lux didn’t turn from the mirror. He was working on his ‘compassionate grimace,’ the one he used right before asking for a love offering. The key was to look like you’d just bitten into a lemon while thinking about a lost puppy.

Beyond the silk drapes, the abandoned park sprawled like a sinner’s graveyard. The skeletal roller coaster of the “Genesis Glide” was a black scribble against the lightening sky. The giant, faded apple atop the “Forbidden Fruit Frisbee” stand listed to one side. And right in the center of his camera shot, thank you Jesus, was the boarded-up entrance to the “Hellmouth Hollow” log flume. The symbolism was free. The irony was wasted on his audience. They ate it with a spoon.

He heard the click of heels on plywood before he smelled the perfume. Something cold and floral, like a funeral home orchid.

“Reverend Lux. A moment of your time.”

Catalina Morado stood at the top of the steps as if she owned them. She wore cream. He wore white. It felt less like a color coordination and more like a territorial dispute.

“Miss Morado. This is a closed set. We’re on a divine timetable.”

“I need your satellite truck.” She stated it like she was ordering coffee.

“My… my truck is transmitting the glory of the Lord to seventeen affiliate stations in ninety minutes. You can’t have it.”

“I don’t want the truck. I want the uplink. For ten minutes. While you’re doing your… warm-up.” She smiled. It didn’t reach her eyes. They were the color of good scotch, and just as intoxicatingly dangerous.

Lux finally turned from the mirror, his compassionate grimace melting into genuine irritation. “What on God’s green earth could you possibly need to uplink at five in the morning?”

“Inventory spreadsheets.” She took a step closer. One of her men materialized behind her, a silent slab in a suit. “My associates in Bogotá are detail-oriented. They like their numbers before breakfast. Your truck has the only clear signal for twenty miles. Cain’s lab equipment creates a dampening field. It’s very inconvenient.”

“This is a house of worship, not a Western Union!”

“It’s an abandoned dinosaur park, and you are a con man in a three-thousand-dollar suit.” Her voice stayed pleasant. “The ten minutes, or I have my man here accidentally trip over your main power cable. Live television is so unpredictable with brownouts.”

Lux’s mouth opened. Nothing came out but a dry click. He saw it in her eyes—she’d do it. And she’d enjoy watching him flail in the sudden dark. The righteous indignation he normally summoned for such moments curdled in his throat. This woman didn’t care about his ratings, his donors, his anointing.

“Seven minutes,” he croaked. “And you do it before we go live. Not during.”

“Ten. And I’ll pray for your ratings.” She turned, her man shadowing her. “Send your technician to my tent. And Reverend?” She glanced back over her shoulder. “Try not to sweat through the jacket before the big finale. It reads poorly in close-up.”

She descended the steps, a queen touring her shabby domain. Lux stared after her, a hot prickle of humiliation crawling up his neck. He dabbed at his forehead with a monogrammed handkerchief. The ‘L’ was embroidered in gold thread. It felt cheap suddenly.

Down in the pit of the park, near the boarded-up “Goliath’s Gourmet Sausages” stand, he saw movement. Dr. Krane was there, looking even more like a startled heron than usual. He was holding what looked like a bulky garage door opener, pointing it at a cluster of shapes in the shadows.

Lux squinted. The shapes resolved into the raptors. They were supposed to be locked down in the Den. They stood in a ragged semicircle, their metal hides catching the first weak rays of sun. They weren’t moving. They were just… staring at Krane.

Krane wasn’t speaking into a radio. He was hunched over, making a series of short, sharp gestures with his free hand—a chop, a flat palm held out, a twisting motion. One of the raptors, its head crowned with a mess of sensors labeled ‘MATTHEW,’ tilted its head. Its red optical scanner cycled down to a thin, considering slit.

Lux felt a cold trickle of unease that had nothing to do with the humidity. That wasn’t a man operating machines. That was a man trying to reason with animals. Badly.

As he watched, Krane made a beckoning gesture. The raptor labeled ‘JUDAS’ took a single, clanking step forward. Then it let out a low, hydraulic whine and sat back on its haunches, its head cocked in what looked like parody. The other raptors mimicked the motion in a ragged, metallic wave. They were sitting. Like dogs.

Krane’s shoulders slumped. He looked at the remote in his hand, then threw it into a bush with a sudden, violent jerk. He stood there for a moment, his back to the creatures. Then he slowly reached into his lab coat pocket, pulled out a Ziploc bag of what looked like raw hamburger, and tossed it onto the ground in front of them.

The raptors didn’t lunge. They regarded the meat. Then ‘MATTHEW’ leaned forward, extruded a small grinding tool from a chest port, and carefully minced the pile of meat into twelve equal portions. The others waited. Then they each took one.

Lux looked away. His theology didn’t have a box for that. He turned back to his mirror, needing to see the familiar, reassuring mask. “My friends,” he tried again, but his voice was thin.

A flicker of motion at the far edge of the park, beyond the main gate, caught his eye. He trailed off, peering into the gloom.

Down the access road, the one that was supposed to be sealed by Cain’s men and Catalina’s, a procession was moving. It wasn’t military. It was slow. Deliberate.

A beat-up Dodge van with a peeling wizard-and-dolphin mural led the way, its engine coughing like a smoker. Behind it walked people. Dozens of them. They carried candles, even in the gathering dawn. They carried homemade signs. One, painted on what looked like a bedsheet, read: HE DIED FOR YOUR SINS, NOT YOUR RATINGS.

At the center of the crowd, walking with a grim, purposeful stride, was a woman in a Miami PD windbreaker. Detective Vega. And beside her, waving his arms as if conducting a silent orchestra, was the conspiracy freak, Dutch.

Lux’s blood turned to ice water. They were supposed to be in jail. Or discouraged. Or dead.

They walked right past the main gate, which hung open, unguarded. One of Catalina’s men stood beside it, watching them pass. He made no move to stop them. He just watched, his face blank, then lifted a walkie-talkie and spoke into it.

The procession snaked into the park, a ragged, silent river of the worst kind of audience: true believers. They headed straight for the base of the broadcast platform.

Lux’s mouth was desert-dry. His perfect opening line vanished from his mind. All he could see was the bedsheet banner, flapping in the morning breeze like a ghost at his feast.

The show was starting early. And the crowd had just arrived to heckle the main event.

Sister Mercy adjusted the strap of her backpack. It contained three bottles of holy water, a first-aid kit stolen from a Greyhound bus, and a tuna sandwich that was starting to feel like a theological error. The road ahead was a gray ribbon under the bruised purple sky of false dawn. Eden World’s silhouette was a jagged black cut-out against it, all broken spires and the skeletal arm of a roller coaster. It looked less like a temple and more like God’s garage sale.

Behind her, the army shuffled and muttered.

It was not an impressive force. There were maybe forty of them. Dutch’s van led the way, its ‘Wizard Dolphin’ mural looking grimly optimistic. Detective Vega walked beside it, her posture a straight line of professional disapproval aimed at the universe. The rest were a patchwork quilt of the desperate and the devout: a handful of Mercy’s flock from the St. Sebastian soup kitchen, their faces set in the same expression they used for waiting in line for meatloaf; a contingent of bikers from the ‘Holy Rollers’ chapter, who’d shown up after hearing there was a fight brewing against “government cyborgs”; three elderly nuns from the Sisters of Perpetual Discomfort, who moved with a slow, unstoppable momentum, like glaciers in sensible shoes; and a loose collection of stray dogs that had attached themselves to the procession, sensing either a moral cause or the scent of the tuna sandwich.

One of the bikers, a man named Ox whose beard contained its own ecosystem, revved his Harley. The sound was a chainsaw tearing through the morning’s quiet. “Are we gonna march or are we gonna have a picnic? My ass is falling asleep.”

“Patience is a virtue, Ox,” Sister Mercy called back, not turning around.

“So is kicking ass, sister. The clock’s ticking.”

He had a point. The sky was lightening from purple to a sickly yellow-grey, the color of an old bruise. She took a deep breath. The air smelled of damp asphalt, exhaust, and the faint, sweet-rotten odor of the sugarcane fields beyond the park fence. A memory flickered, useless and bright: her mother hanging laundry on a line in Indiana, the smell of cotton and grass. She pushed it away. Now was not the time for clean scents.

“Alright, listen up!” Her voice, honed on city streets and shelter halls, cut through the murmuring. She turned to face them. They looked like refugees from a dozen different disasters. “In about twenty minutes, a man in a very expensive white suit is going to try and murder the Son of God on live television. For ratings. He’s got a mad scientist, a cyborg dinosaur, a bunch of smaller cyborg dinosaurs, a drug queenpin, and a battalion of soldiers who’ve forgotten their oath. We’ve got…” She swept a hand over the ragged assembly. “This. And a van that leaks oil.”

A beat of silence. One of the stray dogs, a scruffy terrier mix, lifted its leg and watered a ‘No Trespassing’ sign.

“What’s the plan?” Vega asked, her voice flat. “Walk up and ask nicely?”

“The plan,” Sister Mercy said, “is to be inconvenient. We are going to be the pebble in the shoe, the hair in the biscuit, the unexpected commercial break in the middle of their prime-time sin. We are not an army. We are a distraction. A holy nuisance.” She pointed a finger at the bikers. “You see anything with wires and claws, you make noise. Lots of it. Confuse it.” She pointed to her soup-kitchen regulars. “You look for people who look like they don’t want to be here. The technicians, the stagehands. You offer them a better story. You tell them what’s really in the box.” She looked at the nuns. “Sisters, you’re on trespass and loitering. Make it look pious.”

The eldest nun, Sister Agnes, whose face was a roadmap of wrinkles, gave a slow nod. “We can loiter with the best of them.”

“What about the… bigger dinosaur?” asked a woman named Brenda, who usually ran the soup kitchen’s potato peeler. She looked like she was already regretting leaving it.

“Rex is not the enemy,” Mercy said, with more conviction than she felt. “He’s a victim with very large teeth. If you see him, you get out of his way and you pray he’s having a good day.”

Dutch leaned out of the van’s driver window. “And the Serpent Engine? The temporal-metaphysical rift? The possible local reality unraveling?”

“We’ll call that… a developing situation.” She clapped her hands together once. The sound was sharp, final. “Let’s move. And remember, we’re not here to fight an army. We’re here to ruin a show.”

They began to walk. It was not a martial sound. It was the sound of a crowd that had missed the last bus.

As they neared the main gate, Mercy saw the guard. One of Catalina’s men, lean and cold-eyed in a silk-blend suit. He watched them approach, his hand resting on the butt of a pistol holstered under his arm. Vega tensed beside her.

The guard’s gaze swept over the bikers, the nuns, the bedraggled civilians, the dogs. His expression didn’t change. He lifted his walkie-talkie, spoke a single, quiet phrase into it, and then, with a slight, almost bored gesture, waved them through the open gate.

The show was on. And every single actor had forgotten their lines.
