The van’s tires found the edge of the paved world and kept going. Gravel gave way to mud, mud to something that smelled like a compost heap’s guilty conscience. Jesus braced a hand against the dashboard as they bounced past a sign so sun-bleached only the ghost of a fish skeleton remained.

Clay killed the engine. The silence that followed wasn’t peaceful. It was the quiet of something holding its breath.

“Contractors,” Mary said, nodding toward the riverbank.

Two men in cheap suits stood ankle-deep in the brown water, wrestling a heavy plastic drum off the back of a flatbed truck. The truck’s door bore a peeling Eden World logo—a serpent coiled around a cross, smiling like a used car salesman. One of the men slipped. His shoe came off with a wet plop. He cursed, a word Jesus hadn’t heard since the last time he’d been within earshot of a Roman tax collector.

Rex’s nostrils flared. “Bio-waste. Smells like… regret and formaldehyde.”

“That’s the Everglades’ signature scent,” Clay whispered, sliding down in his seat. “Today’s special: concentrated heresy runoff.”

Jesus cracked his door. The humidity hit him like a wet blanket thrown by an angry god. The air tasted of decay and something sharper, chemical, a tang that stuck to the back of his throat. He watched the contractors heave the drum. Its label, half-scratched off, read: THORN-7 SUBSTRATE—NON-RETURNABLE.

“They’re dumping it in the water,” Mary said. Her voice was flat. “Of course they are.”

The first man got the drum’s lid off. A shimmer of silver smoke escaped, then a viscous, pearlescent sludge that oozed over the rim like liquid pearl. It hit the water with a hiss. The brown river recoiled, bubbling in a perfect three-foot circle.

The second man pulled out a walkie-talkie. “Disposal complete. Site is… hey. You see that?”

The bubbles weren’t stopping. They frothed, multiplying, a boiling patch spreading outward from the drum. Something moved beneath the surface. Not a gator’s slow roll. This was faster, a silvery flicker.

A fish broke the water.

It was a bass, or had been. One eye was three times the size of the other, bulging like a cloudy marble. Its scales gleamed with an unnatural, oily rainbow sheen. A jagged scar ran along its side, fresh and angry.

It hovered for a second, staring at the man with the missing shoe.

Then it spat.

A stream of silvery liquid hit the contractor square in the chest. He yelped, more surprised than hurt, brushing at his tie. The fabric where the liquid landed didn’t stain. It… melted. A tiny, perfect hole appeared, edges smoking faintly.

“The hell?” he said.

The water erupted.

Not with one fish. With dozens. They boiled to the surface—mutated catfish with barbels like copper wires, gar with teeth that glittered like broken glass, sunfish sporting extra fins that fluttered like tiny, desperate flags. All of them bore scars. All of them gleamed with that same sickly rainbow sheen.

And they were all looking at the contractors.

“Regenerative protein,” Rex murmured, his reactor humming a note lower. “They dumped the substrate. It didn’t dilute. It… recruited.”

The first fish launched itself. It didn’t swim. It arrowed through the air, a wet, silvery missile, and latched onto the drum with a sound like a stapler punching through tin. Its teeth scraped metal.

Then another fish hit the man with the walkie-talkie. It didn’t bite. It splattered against his arm, and where its body burst, the silvery liquid ate through his jacket sleeve. He screamed, a high, genuine sound of pain.

Chaos, as a rule, is funny when it’s happening to someone who deserves it. Jesus felt a smile tug at his mouth, thin and unforgiving. He leaned against the van’s hot side panel. “Karma’s a fish.”

Mary shot him a look. “They’re being dissolved.”

“Selectively.”

The contractors were dancing now, a frantic, slapping jig in the muck. Fish flew through the air. They didn’t act like fish. They acted like tiny, vengeful torpedoes. One latched onto a suit jacket and began shaking its head, ripping the fabric with a sound like Velcro giving up on life.

The man who’d lost his shoe was trying to climb back onto the flatbed. A catfish, plump and gleaming, smacked into the small of his back. He arched, howling, as a dinner-plate-sized stain spread across his shirt.

“We should do something,” Mary said, not moving.

“We are,” Jesus said. “We’re observing a parable. The Fisherman’s Revenge. I like it. Has a nice ring.”

Rex’s tail tapped the ground. Thump. “The substrate’s rewriting them. Turning them into… programmable protein. Look.”

He was right. The fish that had burst on the walkie-talkie man’s arm wasn’t just dissolving flesh. The edges of the wound glittered with tiny, moving specks—silver, like the substrate. The man clutched his arm, his face pale. “It’s crawling!”

Clay let out a low whistle. “Eden’s new line of self-replicating guilt. Nice.”

The drum, now covered in a squirming, silvery mass of fish, tipped over. The rest of the substrate slurry poured into the river. The water didn’t just bubble. It seethed. More shapes wriggled to the surface. These were smaller, newer, their mutations even more frantic—a fish with a dorsal fin shaped like a tiny crucifix, another with gills that pulsed with a faint blue light.

The lead contractor, the one now missing most of his pants leg, stumbled toward the truck cab. He fumbled for the door handle, his fingers slipping. “It’s in the system!” he shrieked into his walkie-talkie. “The protein’s regenerative! It’s not disposing, it’s—agh!”

A gar, long as his forearm, smacked into the side of his head. It didn’t bite. It wrapped around his ear, its body coiling, and squeezed. The man screamed, batting at it. He spun, lost his balance, and fell backward into the shallow water.

The fish swarm converged.

It wasn’t a feeding frenzy. It was an absorption. The silvery liquid from their bodies and the substrate in the water seemed to merge. The man’s flailing slowed. His screams turned to gurgles. A sheen, rainbow and slick, began to spread across his skin where the water touched him.

“Okay,” Jesus said, pushing off the van. “That’s enough.”

He didn’t run. He walked toward the riverbank, the mud sucking at his sandals. He picked up a long, discarded pole from the ground—a broken fishing rod, its line tangled in a sad nest.

The second contractor saw him coming. He was crouched on the flatbed, clutching his dissolving arm. “Help! Get them off!”

Jesus ignored him. He waded into the edge of the water, the polluted warmth soaking his robes. He pointed the broken rod at the swarm covering the fallen man. “Hey.”

The fish didn’t stop.

He jabbed the rod into the squirming mass. A few fish detached, hissing through their gills. Their new, wrong eyes fixed on him. One darted toward the rod. Its teeth snagged the line.

Jesus yanked the pole upward, swinging the fish out of the water. It dangled, writhing, a piece of silvery anger on a hook. He looked at it. It looked back, gills pulsing.

“You’ve made your point,” he said to the fish.

He threw the rod, fish and all, in a high arc toward the deeper channel. It landed with a splash. The swarm hesitated. The silvery connection between them and the fallen man seemed to thin, like a thought interrupted.

The man in the water gasped, spluttering. His face was patchy with the rainbow sheen, but he was alive. He scrambled backward, crab-walking through the mud, leaving a trail of glittering water.

Rex waded in then, a looming shadow. He didn’t bother with tools. He simply roared.

It wasn’t his battle-roar. It was lower, a subsonic vibration that made the water’s surface shiver. The fish stilled. For a second, the only sound was the hum of Rex’s reactor and the contractor’s ragged sobs.

The swarm retreated. Not fleeing, but sinking, descending back into the murk with a collective, oily ripple. They weren’t done. They were waiting.

Mary helped the drenched, half-dissolved contractor onto the bank. His partner jumped down from the flatbed, cradling his arm. The walkie-talkie lay in the mud, crackling with a voice saying, “—confirm disposal.”

The first contractor, the one who’d been in the water, pawed at his sheened face. “It’s on me. It’s in me.” He looked at Jesus, his eyes wide with a terror deeper than pain. “It’s programmable protein! You don’t understand! They can point it!”

He fumbled in his sodden jacket, fingers clumsy. A small, black data chip fell from his pocket into the mud. He didn’t notice. He was too busy staring at his own shimmering hands.

Rex’s eye locked onto the chip. His tail stopped its anxious flick. He took a slow, heavy step forward, the mud yielding under his weight. He didn’t look at the weeping contractor. He looked at the little black rectangle, half-sunk in the muck, glinting under the Florida sun like a beetle’s back.

Jesus followed his gaze. Then he looked at Rex’s face—the set of his jaw, the too-careful stillness.

The chip winked up at them, a tiny, plastic secret.

The river burbled. Somewhere downstream, a silvery fin broke the surface, then vanished.

The contractor scrambled for the truck, his partner already inside gunning the engine. The flatbed’s tires spun, spraying a fan of glittering mud that caught the sunrise and made it look cheap. Jesus watched them go. The parable had concluded. The moral was: don’t dump your divine waste products where the local wildlife can develop opinions about it.

He knelt, ignoring the warm seep through his robe, and plucked the data chip from the muck. It was smaller than a thumbnail, featureless.

“Programmable protein,” he said, wiping it on his sleeve. It left a faint, iridescent smear.

Rex loomed over him, blocking the sun. His shadow was cool. “Give it.”

The tone was new. Not a request. A reactor-hum thrummed underneath it, a note of possession. Rex’s clawed hand, usually held with a careful, almost comical delicacy, was outstretched, palm up.

Jesus held the chip between his thumb and forefinger, turning it. “You know what this is.”

“I know what it could be.” Rex’s tail gave one sharp, involuntary flick, carving a trench in the mud. “Lie. Tell.”

Mary waded out of the shallows, her boots making wet sucking sounds. She held the discarded walkie-talkie. “Asset recovery team, ETA three minutes. They sound… corporate.”

“They’re always corporate,” Jesus said, still looking at Rex. “The question is, which corporation? Eden’s dead. This is new growth.”

A silvery flicker darted past his submerged ankle. He didn’t jump. He was tired of jumping. The fish—the protein—was just investigating. Or maybe it was tasting. He kept his eyes on the dinosaur. “You’re scared of a little chip.”

“I’m scared of what’s on it,” Rex rumbled. “And you should be, carpenter. That isn’t data. It’s a recipe.”

Clay called from the van. “Uh. The water’s getting closer.”

He was right. The boiling, seething patch where the drum had emptied was spreading, a slow, silver-tinged stain drifting downstream with the current. The mutated fish swam at its edges, patrols. One of them, the bass with the bulging eye, surfaced and fixed its gaze on Mary. It didn’t blink. Fish shouldn’t have that much focus.

“Fine.” Jesus tossed the chip. It spun, a tiny black star, and landed in the center of Rex’s massive palm. “Cook us up something disturbing.”

Rex’s other hand went to a port on his side panel, a slot Jesus had always assumed was for charging. He slotted the chip home with a soft, definitive click. His good eye dimmed, the light contracting to a pinprick. A low stream of static, like a poorly tuned radio between stations, whispered from his vocal synthesizer.

Mary moved beside Jesus. Her shoulder brushed his arm. It was a practical gesture, for balance in the mud, but she didn’t pull away. “He’s reading it raw.”

“He’s tasting it,” Jesus corrected.

The static from Rex coalesced into words, flat and toneless. “Project Lazarus. Sub-protocol: Fisherman. Objective: Develop a regenerative, target-acquisitive protein matrix derived from THORN-7 substrate and… subject zero biomatter.” The pinprick light in Rex’s eye flickered. “Subject zero designation: REX. Primary chassis.”

The river burbled. A mosquito whined near Jesus’s ear. He didn’t swat it.

Rex’s voice was still the flat, data-read monotone. “Matrix exhibits memory-retention of source material’s base imperatives. In field test, observed imperatives are: protect territory, eliminate contaminants, secure nutrient sources.” The static crackled. “Secondary finding: Matrix can be imprinted with new imperatives via UHF carrier wave. Obedience is a signal.”

He fell silent. The hum of his reactor was the only sound for a long moment.

“They made killer fish juice from your blood,” Clay said, leaning out the van window. “That’s… I mean, that’s rude.”

Jesus watched a droplet of silvery water trace a path down Rex’s armored jaw. It looked like a tear. “What’s the connection to AM-1?”

Rex’s eye-light flared back to life. He looked at Jesus, and the raw, unprocessed horror in that mechanical face was a physical thing. “It’s not a connection. It’s a trial run.” The synthesized voice was his own again, ragged. “The protein matrix is the delivery system. For the obedience hormones. They’re not building a messiah. They’re building a vector.”

Mary snatched the walkie-talkie, her fingers white on the plastic. “Asset recovery is one minute out. We need to go. Now.”

But Jesus was stuck on the word. Vector. A thing that carries something from one host to another. A middleman. A dirty needle.

“Then we poison the well.”
