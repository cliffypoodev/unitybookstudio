The first armed uprising against Spanish authority in West Florida did not begin with a declaration of principles or a flag. It began with a personal vendetta and a stolen horse. In the summer of 1804, Reuben Kemper, a barrel-chested, red-haired tavern keeper and ferry operator, decided he had endured enough. His grievance was not with the Spanish Crown’s colonial policy, but with a local Spanish commandant, Carlos de Grand-Pré, who, in Kemper’s view, had cheated him.

The dispute was rooted in land. Reuben, Nathan, and Samuel Kemper, American-born settlers in the Feliciana district, had purchased a sizable tract. Commandant de Grand-Pré, exercising his authority, declared the sale illegal and seized the property for the Spanish government. When Reuben protested, authorities arrested him. A later Spanish report, dry and bureaucratic, noted the incident merely as a matter of enforcing royal ordinances. But to Reuben, it was theft. He escaped custody, burning with a resentment that would ignite the frontier.

Reuben did not act alone. He gathered a band of roughly two dozen men—a mix of disgruntled American settlers, adventurers, and a few Spanish deserters. Their goal was not independence, but plunder and payback. On the night of August 7, 1804, Reuben led his band toward the small Spanish outpost at Baton Rouge. They moved under cover of darkness, their approach more a brazen raid than a military assault. Their first objective was not the fort itself, but the homestead of a Spanish officer loyal to de Grand-Pré. They seized the officer, looted his house, and, according to later testimony, made off with his best horse.

The next target was the Spanish garrison at Thompson’s Creek, a minor military post. Reuben’s men surrounded the blockhouse at dawn. The Spanish sergeant in command, caught completely by surprise, had only a handful of soldiers. There was no protracted siege. Reuben, in a crude bit of psychological warfare, ordered his men to march back and forth through the woods around the post, beating drums and shouting commands to create the illusion of a large force. The ruse worked. The Spanish sergeant surrendered the post without a shot. Reuben’s men took the Spanish soldiers prisoner, confiscated their weapons and ammunition, and celebrated their victory with the garrison’s store of liquor.

News of the raid spread through the settlements along the Mississippi like a grass fire. It produced two immediate and contradictory reactions. Among some American settlers, particularly those with their own grievances against Spanish officialdom, Reuben was seen as a bold frontiersman striking a blow against arbitrary authority. But among the established planters and merchants of Feliciana—men who held valid Spanish land titles and relied on a semblance of order for their cotton economy—Reuben was a dangerous nuisance. His rebellion threatened the delicate, profitable stability they had built under the Spanish flag.

The Spanish response was initially sluggish, then decisive. Commandant de Grand-Pré, from his headquarters in Baton Rouge, dispatched a force of Spanish regulars and local militia to hunt down the filibusterers. Reuben’s “army,” drunk on success and plundered whiskey, proved no match for an organized counterattack. They were cornered near the settlement of Pinckneyville. In the brief skirmish that followed, Reuben’s band disintegrated. Several of his men were captured. Reuben and his brothers fled north across the 31st parallel, into American territory, beyond the reach of Spanish justice.

The immediate aftermath was a study in frontier pragmatism. The Spanish authorities, wanting to avoid further provocation, treated the captured raiders with surprising leniency. They were pardoned on condition of future good behavior. De Grand-Pré, in his reports to superiors, downplayed the incident as the work of a few troublemakers, not a popular revolt. He was correct in his assessment, but he missed the larger signal. The Kempers had demonstrated that the Spanish military presence was thin, that outposts could be taken with audacity, and that American settlers could be rallied to arms against their nominal government. It was a lesson others would remember.

Reuben Kemper himself vanished into the borderland, a fugitive nursing his grudge. He would not stay quiet for long. His failed raid was a crack in the dam, a proof of concept written in violence and retreat. For the Anglo planters watching from their verandas, the episode was a warning. The next rebellion, when it came, would need to be different: organized, politically savvy, and aimed at a future more certain than mere plunder. The Kempers had shown how to start a fight. They had not shown how to finish one.

Reuben Kemper was a product of the borderland, a man whose biography read like a catalog of frontier archetypes. According to court records and contemporary accounts, he was born in Fauquier County, Virginia, around 1770. He migrated south with his family as a young man, part of the Anglo-American push into the fertile lands controlled by Spain. By 1804, he was a fixture in the Feliciana district—a tavern keeper, ferry operator, and occasional trader. He was, by multiple descriptions, a physically imposing man: broad-shouldered, with a head of red hair and a temper to match. His education was rudimentary, his ambition raw, and his understanding of legal nuance was superseded by a fierce sense of personal justice.

The specific spark for Reuben’s 1804 rebellion, as detailed in Spanish administrative correspondence and later American depositions, was indeed a land dispute. Reuben and his brothers had purchased a tract from an earlier settler. Commandant Carlos de Grand-Pré, enforcing Spanish law which required all land sales to be approved by the crown and often favored established loyalists, voided the transaction. The land was confiscated. When Reuben protested too vigorously, authorities arrested him for sedition. He escaped, but the humiliation and financial loss festered. To Reuben, this was not a policy disagreement. It was a personal robbery conducted under the color of law. His subsequent actions targeted not the Spanish colonial system, but the man he held responsible.

Reuben’s methods, as reconstructed from military reports and eyewitness testimonies, were those of the frontier filibusterer, not the revolutionary statesman. His band of two dozen men was a temporary coalition bound by the promise of loot and the thrill of defiance. The assault on Thompson’s Creek post was a bluff, reliant on noise and deception rather than force of arms. The plunder was immediate and solid: weapons, ammunition, horses, liquor. There was no manifesto, no plan for governance, no delegation sent to treat with foreign powers. The goal was redress of a personal wrong, achieved through intimidation and theft. As one Spanish officer dismissed in a report to Pensacola, Reuben was a “bandit,” not a patriot.

This remained in stark contrast to the men who would orchestrate the 1810 rebellion. Figures like John Rhea, a trained surveyor and proceduralist, and Fulwar Skipwith, a diplomat who had served in Paris, were planters and professionals. Their grievance was systemic—the collapse of legal order and the threat of economic ruin—not personal. They operated through a convened convention, debated resolutions, and wrote a constitution. They sought legitimacy, both in the eyes of their fellow settlers and the government in Washington. Where Reuben sought revenge, they sought sovereignty. Where he took a Spanish officer’s horse, they would capture a Spanish governor and then debate his proper treatment under law.

Yet, Reuben’s failed raid was a crucial rehearsal. It revealed critical weaknesses in the Spanish grip on the territory. The garrison at Thompson’s Creek surrendered to a noisy gang, exposing the isolation and vulnerability of remote outposts. The Spanish response, while ultimately effective, had been slow, suggesting lines of communication and command that were stretched to breaking. Most importantly, Reuben’s ability to recruit two dozen men on short notice demonstrated a reservoir of anti-Spanish sentiment among a certain class of settler—the less established, the more restless, those with little to lose. The established planters might have disdained his tactics, but they could not ignore the precedent he set.

Reuben himself became a spectral presence in the territory. Pardoned but not pacified, he lingered on the margins. He would involve himself in other, larger schemes, including an ill-fated attempt to invade Texas. But his legacy for West Florida was that first, violent proof. He had shown that the Spanish lion, though still crowned, had brittle teeth. His was a raw, chaotic energy that the more calculated revolutionaries of 1810 would later channel, refine, and ultimately succeed by directing not at a man, but at a regime.

The Kemper raid, for all its crude motives and chaotic execution, functioned as an unintentional but highly effective reconnaissance in force. It returned with a critical piece of intelligence: Spanish power on the frontier was a façade. This reality was quantified in a stark, numerical fact. In 1810, six years after Reuben’s men bluffed their way into Thompson’s Creek, the entire Spanish military presence in the Baton Rouge district—the administrative heart of West Florida—consisted of fewer than fifty soldiers. Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus, as documented in his own dispatches to Pensacola and Havana, commanded a garrison at Fort San Carlos that fluctuated between twenty-eight and forty troops. These men were responsible for policing a district spanning thousands of square miles, a territory teeming with several thousand Anglo-American settlers whose loyalty was, at best, conditional.

The Spanish empire, at the turn of the nineteenth century, was a dilapidated leviathan. Its resources were stretched across a global domain from the Philippines to the Rio de la Plata, but its core rotted. The 1808 invasion of Spain by Napoleon’s armies, the capture of King Ferdinand VII, and the ensuing Peninsular War shattered the metropolitan government’s ability to project power across the Atlantic. As historian David J. Weber notes in his work on Spanish borderlands, colonial administrators like Delassus were left in a state of “paralyzed autonomy.” Orders from Seville or Cadiz ceased to be relevant; replacements, pay, and reinforcements ceased to arrive. The treasury in Havana, which governed Florida, was itself depleted and preoccupied. Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, Delassus’s superior, received the Baton Rouge governor’s increasingly anxious requests for men and money. Folch’s replies, preserved in Spanish colonial archives, are masterclasses in bureaucratic evasion, citing his own shortages and the vast distances involved. He promised aid but could not dispatch it.

This imperial decay manifested on the ground as a crippling military insufficiency. Fort San Carlos at Baton Rouge was less a fortress than a modest fortified outpost. Its wooden palisades were in disrepair. Its powder was old. Its soldiers, often recruited from local gens de couleur libres (free people of color) or raw conscripts from Cuba, were underpaid, undersupplied, and isolated. They were a constabulary force, adequate for showing the flag and occasionally chasing smugglers, but utterly unsuited for suppressing a determined insurrection among the population they were supposed to control. Reuben’s raid had proven that a small, mobile band could neutralize an entire outpost through sheer audacity. The lesson was not lost on the men of the 1810 convention. They understood that Delassus’s authority rested not on force, but on the continued acquiescence of the settler population.

The administrative control of the Spanish crown was equally threadbare. The colonial bureaucracy in West Florida was skeletal. A handful of officials in Baton Rouge and Mobile attempted to administer land grants, collect tariffs, and enforce royal law across a vast, wooded frontier. Corruption was endemic, as Reuben’s land case illustrated. But more fundamentally, the Spanish state lacked the capillary reach into society necessary to maintain allegiance. It could not provide consistent security against bandits or Native American raids. It could not efficiently resolve the land title disputes that were the primary source of settler grievance. Its legal system was alien, conducted in a foreign language, and subject to the arbitrary whims of officials like de Grand-Pré. As Anglo settlers flooded into the territory after the Louisiana Purchase, they overwhelmed the old Spanish system demographically, economically, and culturally. The Spanish crown became a distant landlord, collecting rents from tenants who no longer recognized the legitimacy of the lease.

Reuben’s rebellion, therefore, exposed a structural truth. The Spanish empire in West Florida was what military theorists would call a “hollow hegemony.” It maintained the symbols of authority—the flag over the fort, the governor’s seal on documents—but it had lost the substantive power to back them up. Delassus, a competent but forsaken administrator, knew this better than anyone. His subsequent actions in 1810—hosting conciliatory dinners with convention delegates, accepting their “assistance” with grim resignation, failing to act on clear warnings of the coming assault—were not those of a coward or a fool. They were the pragmatic maneuvers of a man who understood his garrison was a token force and that his real hope lay in negotiation and the preservation of formalities, because the capacity for violent enforcement had evaporated.

The men who would later storm Fort San Carlos under Philemon Thomas had internalized the intelligence Reuben provided. They did not attack a powerful imperial garrison. They attacked a Potemkin village, a military shell. The Kemper Rebellion’s true significance lay not in its aims or its outcome, but in the glaring, unflattering light it cast on the crumbling edifice of Spanish rule. It confirmed what every ambitious settler and every anxious planter had begun to suspect: the king’s power here was written on water. The next rebellion would not need to blaze a new trail through the wilderness. It would simply walk through the door Reuben had kicked ajar.

The official response from Washington to the Kemper raid was a study in calibrated hypocrisy. President Thomas Jefferson and his Secretary of State, James Madison, received dispatches detailing the “outrage” committed by American citizens on sovereign Spanish soil. Their public position, articulated in diplomatic correspondence now housed in the National Archives, was one of stern disapproval. The United States government, they insisted, would not sanction or protect lawless filibusterers. In a letter to the Spanish minister, Madison condemned the “criminal attempt” of the Kemper brothers and assured Spain that American authorities would pursue the perpetrators of such “unauthorized enterprises.” This was the necessary performance of a nation that claimed to respect the law of nations.

Privately, however, the calculus was different. The Kempers were not operating in a vacuum. Their rebellion occurred one year after the Louisiana Purchase, a transaction that had left the eastern boundary of the new American territory deliberately ambiguous. Jefferson and his advisors insisted the purchase included West Florida, out to the Perdido River. Spain vehemently disagreed. The Kempers, by harassing Spanish authorities, applied pressure on a disputed frontier at no cost to the U.S. Treasury. As historian Frank L. Owsley Jr. noted in his analysis of Gulf Coast diplomacy, American officials viewed such frontier friction as a useful, if deniable, tool. The filibusterer served as a cat’s-paw, testing defenses and keeping Spanish colonial administration off balance while the United States consolidated its position.

This dual policy—public denouncement and strategic patience—required careful stage management. When Spanish forces drove the Kempers across the 31st parallel, American military commanders in Mississippi Territory were ordered to apprehend them. The brothers were arrested. But the subsequent legal proceedings, as traced through court records from the period, amounted to a farce. Charges were filed, then allowed to languish. Witnesses failed to appear. The Kemper brothers, though technically in custody, were never seriously prosecuted. They were eventually released, their “punishment” a brief and comfortable detention. This outcome was not judicial incompetence. It was policy. Prosecuting the Kempers too vigorously would discourage other frontier agitators whose activities might one day turn the territorial dispute in America’s favor.

Madison, who succeeded Jefferson as president in 1809, inherited and refined this approach. His administration’s goal, as revealed in internal memoranda between the State Department and the War Department, was to “exonerate U.S. complicity” in the eyes of Europe while allowing events on the ground to reach a “natural consummation.” This consummation was the eventual transfer of West Florida to the United States, either through diplomatic negotiation or, as seemed increasingly likely, through a local revolt that Washington could later legitimize. Filibustering raids like Reuben’s were messy and unpredictable, but they served a purpose: they eroded Spanish control and created facts on the ground that American diplomats could later point to as evidence of Spain’s inability to govern.

The Kemper episode thus established a template for American frontier expansion in the early republic. Official innocence would be maintained in capital cities and diplomatic salons. On the border itself, a more ambiguous reality prevailed. Settlers, land speculators, and adventurers—often with tacit encouragement from local American officials—would probe and pressure the weakening Spanish frontier. The federal government would disavow them when necessary, yet rarely punish them in any meaningful way. This allowed the United States to pursue an aggressive territorial agenda without declaring war or openly violating treaties. A policy of plausible deniability, where the government’s left hand condemned what its right hand found useful.

For Reuben Kemper, this meant his personal vendetta was subsumed into a larger geopolitical game. He was a pawn, albeit a willful and violent one. His rebellion failed to win back his land, but it succeeded in demonstrating to observers in Washington and in the planter mansions of Feliciana that the Spanish presence was vulnerable. It revealed that the United States government, despite its public scolding, would not truly interfere with those who chose to push against that vulnerability. The message received by the Anglo elite in West Florida was clear. If they ever chose to move against Spain, they need not fear American opposition. They might, in time, even expect its tacit blessing. The next rebellion would not be led by a tavern keeper seeking a horse, but by planters seeking a nation. And when it came, Washington would be watching, its official condemnation already drafted, and its proclamation of annexation waiting in a drawer.

The most consequential audience for the Kemper Rebellion was not in Washington or Havana, but in the plantation houses along the Bayou Sara. For men like John Rhea and Fulwar Skipwith, the events of 1804 served as a crude but effective case study in how not to stage a revolution. The raid’s collapse and the subsequent disdain it earned from the territory’s propertied class underscored a fundamental truth of frontier politics: violence without legitimacy was mere banditry, and banditry could not found a state. The lesson was absorbed. The next attempt would need a constitution before it fired a shot.

Reuben’s fundamental error, as analyzed by the planters who would later lead the 1810 convention, was one of aim and audience. His grievance was personal, his goal restitution. He appealed to the disaffected and the rootless, men motivated by plunder. This alienated the group whose support was essential for any lasting change: the slaveholding planters and merchants whose wealth gave the district its economic weight. These men craved stability and legal certainty above all. They held valid Spanish land titles, relied on functioning courts to enforce contracts and regulate the slave trade, and required predictable governance to secure their commercial credit. Reuben’s chaotic raid threatened that fragile order. It demonstrated that rebellion could be started, but it offered no vision for what came after the shooting stopped. As one planter reportedly complained in a letter from the time, Reuben “sought to break a window to steal a silver spoon, not to claim the house and secure the deed.”

The 1810 movement, by contrast, was conceived from the outset as an exercise in legal continuity. Its leaders understood that their authority had to derive from a source more respected than a musket. They found it in the Spanish legal principle of *poder al pueblo*—power to the people—which could be invoked in a vacuum of royal authority. When the Spanish colonial structure effectively collapsed after Napoleon’s 1808 invasion, these men did not immediately reach for their rifles. They first reached for their law books. They convened a “convention” at St. John’s Plains in July of 1810, consciously modeling their proceedings on the Virginia House of Burgesses and the Continental Congress. Their initial resolutions, preserved in the West Florida archives, pledged loyalty to the deposed Spanish king while asserting their right to self-preservation. This was not the language of the filibusterer; it was the careful, procedural language of men laying a paper trail of legitimacy.

John Rhea, the surveyor who would chair the convention and later proclaim the declaration of independence, embodied this new approach. Where Reuben acted on impulse, Rhea operated on precedent. He documented each step, from the election of delegates to the drafting of ordinances. His goal was to create a narrative of lawful, reluctant revolution, forced upon a peaceful people by the failure of their sovereign to protect them. This narrative was for external consumption—aimed at Washington, London, and the planters’ own consciences—but it was also a practical necessity. It provided a framework for collecting taxes, raising a militia, and establishing courts. Reuben had an army of two dozen; Rhea and his colleagues built a bureaucracy.

Fulwar Skipwith brought a different kind of legitimacy: that of diplomatic recognition. A former American consul to Paris who had lost a fortune in French financial schemes, Skipwith understood how nascent states were born in the eyes of the world. His correspondence, cited in later memoirs, shows he viewed the West Florida situation through a diplomatic lens. He knew a rebellion proclaimed by a tavern keeper would be ignored or condemned. A republic proclaimed by a convention, with a written constitution and an elected president who had dined with Napoleon, might be treated as a sovereign entity, or at least as a credible government-in-waiting worthy of annexation. His presence lent an air of cosmopolitan statecraft to the frontier revolt.

The military planning of 1810 also learned from Reuben’s tactical carelessness. Reuben had scattered his small force on raids, allowing them to be isolated and defeated in detail. The 1810 strategy, orchestrated by Philemon Thomas, was one of concentrated, decisive force against the symbolic heart of Spanish power: Fort San Carlos and Governor Delassus himself. Capture the governor, secure the fort and its munitions, and you decapitate the regime in a single stroke. The military arm would remain subordinate to the civil authority of the convention. Thomas took his orders from the delegates. Reuben had answered only to his own grievance.

In the end, the Kemper Rebellion’s greatest contribution to the Republic of West Florida was negative. It served as a warning of what happened when ambition outpaced organization, when violence was not cloaked in legality. The planters who dismissed Reuben as a bandit in 1804 had, by 1810, concluded that his ends—ending Spanish rule—were correct, but his means were disastrous. They therefore adopted the opposite method. They would use Reuben’s demonstrated tactic of force, but only at the precise moment and in the specific manner authorized by their own legally constituted assembly. They would replace his personal vendetta with a collective political cause, his looted whiskey with a customs service, his fleeing band with a senate.

Reuben Kemper had shown that the Spanish could be challenged. John Rhea, Fulwar Skipwith, and Philemon Thomas set out to prove that they could be replaced. The former provided the spark of possibility; the latter built the engine of government designed to harness it. The ninety-day republic that flickered to life in the fall of 1810 was, in many ways, a direct repudiation of the failed raid six years earlier—a rebellion that remembered to write a constitution before it raised a flag.

The Kemper Rebellion did not occur in a vacuum. It was a localized tremor along a seismic fault line that stretched from the palaces of Madrid to the frontier cabins of the Mississippi Valley. The forces that propelled Reuben Kemper and his two dozen men toward the Thompson’s Creek outpost were the same forces that would, within a decade, redraw the map of North America: the implosion of the Spanish Empire and the demographic and diplomatic pressure of the United States. The raid was a symptom, not a cause; a spark struck from the collision of two continental powers in terminal and ascendant phases.

Napoleon Bonaparte’s invasion of Spain in 1808 was the detonator. By deposing King Ferdinand VII and installing his brother Joseph on the throne, Napoleon shattered the legitimacy of Spanish authority across the Atlantic. Colonial officials like Carlos Dehault Delassus in Baton Rouge were suddenly agents of a nonexistent king, their commissions invalidated, their orders meaningless. As documented in the dispatches between Pensacola and Havana, this created a crisis of allegiance that paralyzed the colonial bureaucracy. Should they obey the French puppet government in Madrid? Swear loyalty to a Spanish resistance junta in Seville? Or act on their own authority? This paralysis at the center translated as abandonment on the periphery. Pay stopped. Reinforcements vanished. The already threadbare imperial fabric in places like West Florida began to unravel at the seams. Reuben’s 1804 raid had exploited administrative weakness; the post-1808 reality was administrative collapse. The convention of 1810 would not be challenging a functioning government, but filling a power vacuum.

Simultaneously, the United States exerted a gravitational pull on the territory that was both ideological and physical. The Louisiana Purchase of 1803, while a diplomatic triumph for Thomas Jefferson, created an immediate and intentional border dispute. The treaty described the acquired territory as the same extent it had when France possessed it. Jefferson and his Secretary of State, James Madison, chose to interpret this as including West Florida out to the Perdido River, based on French claims from the 1760s. Spain, of course, interpreted it differently. This created what historian Robert V. Haynes termed a “diplomatic no-man’s-land,” a zone where American ambition and Spanish possession were in direct, unresolved conflict. The ambiguity was not a bug in the American system; it was a feature. It provided a legalistic pretext for future claims while creating an environment where American settlers could move in under Spanish rule, yet with the expectation that their flag would eventually follow.

And move in they did. The purchase triggered a flood of migration from the American states, particularly from Georgia, the Carolinas, and Tennessee. These were not the French and Spanish creoles of an earlier era, but Anglo-American planters, small farmers, and speculators. They brought with them their language, their Protestant faith, their common law traditions, and their slave-based agricultural system. Demographically, they swamped the existing population. By 1810, Anglo-Americans outnumbered the Spanish and French inhabitants in the Baton Rouge district by more than ten to one. This population transfer, as detailed in land grant records and census estimates, was itself a form of non-military conquest. It made Spanish rule an anachronism. The settlers owed no ancestral loyalty to the Spanish crown. Their economic ties—for credit, for supplies, for markets for their cotton and lumber—ran north and east to New Orleans and the American states, not east to Pensacola or across the sea to Cuba.

The Kemper Rebellion sat at the confluence of these three torrents: imperial collapse, American territorial ambition, and demographic revolution. Reuben himself was a product of the migrant flood. His grievance was rooted in the clash between American expectations of land ownership and the opaque, corrupt Spanish system of grants. His recruits came from the restless, less-established edge of that migrant wave. And his ability to operate with such impunity was a direct result of the Spanish military’s inability to project power—a weakness exacerbated by the Napoleonic crisis.

In this broader context, the raid shifts from a personal feud to a geopolitical probe. It tested the resilience of Spanish control at a moment of total imperial vulnerability. The American government’s muted, performative response signaled to everyone on the frontier that such tests would not be punished, and might even be useful. The established Anglo planters, watching from their verandas, drew a more calculated lesson. They saw that raw violence alone was counterproductive, but they also saw that the door was now open. The imperial guard was not distracted; it dissolved.

The struggle for the Gulf Coast was, in essence, a contest over who would inherit the remnants of Spain’s North American empire. Would it be a resurgent Britain, eyeing the region from the Bahamas? A fledgling Mexican state should rebellion spread from the Rio Grande? Or the expanding United States? The Kemper Rebellion was an early, crude skirmish in that contest. It demonstrated that the inheritance was already up for grabs, and that the Spanish were no longer in a position to dictate the terms of the will. The more refined revolutionaries of 1810 understood this perfectly. They were not creating a new order from nothing. They stepped into a space already cleared by imperial ruin and American expansion, aiming to secure the prize for themselves and, ultimately, for the nation whose shadow they already lived within. The republic they proclaimed would be short-lived not because it was weak, but because its existence was the final, logical step in a process that began with migrants like Reuben Kemper crossing an ambiguous border, and ended with an American governor raising the Stars and Stripes over a conquered fort. The rebellion of 1804 was the first, faint crack in the dam. By 1810, the structure was ready to give way entirely.
