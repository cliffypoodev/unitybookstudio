In the 1987 edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, the diagnostic criteria for Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder includes a single, illustrative case study. The subject is an eight-year-old boy. For Autistic Disorder, the text’s prototypical example is a three-year-old boy who does not respond to his name. The language of psychiatry in that era, drawn from the predominantly male cohorts of its foundational studies, created a ghost. The ghost was a boy, often white, often loud, often failing in visible, disruptive ways. He haunted every clinic, every classroom, every pediatrician’s office. When a child who did not match this specter walked in—quieter, more observant, more inclined to internalize her chaos—the system often saw nothing at all.

The clinical gaze was myopic, but it was reflecting a cultural script. A 1994 review in the Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology noted that referral rates for ADHD were three to nine times higher for boys than girls. The authors concluded this likely represented a “referral bias,” as teachers and parents were primed to notice hyperactive, disruptive behaviors that violated expectations for male comportment. The behaviors that got girls noticed were different. They were not violations of a script. They were often exaggerated performances of it.

Marta Silva’s report cards from the 1990s, archived alongside her personal diaries, trace this invisible arc. The teacher comments do not mention impulsivity or outbursts. They note a different pattern: “Marta is a sweet, daydreamy girl.” “She tries very hard but often seems overwhelmed by multi-step instructions.” “Her desk is a constant mess, but she is always polite and apologetic.” Her childhood diaries, however, reveal the internal machinery behind this polite exterior. An entry from age ten reads, “Today Mrs. Carlton gave three things to do. I wrote down the first one. By the time I finished thinking about how to write it neat, she was on the third thing. I didn’t hear the second thing. My stomach hurt all afternoon. I am so stupid.” The distress is acute, but its expression is inward-turning—a stomach ache, a silent confession of self-loathing. She was not referred for evaluation. Her suffering was privatized, folded into a narrative of personal failing and excessive sensitivity.

This is the central miscarriage of the era. Neurodivergence in girls and women was not absent. It was miscategorized. The diagnostic criteria were a lock, and the lived experience of girls was often the wrong key. Their ADHD frequently presented as inattentive, not hyperactive—a struggle with organization, time-blindness, and a quietly racing mind mistaken for laziness or lack of effort. Their autistic traits were often channeled into intense social study. They became archivists of nuance, compiling mental notebooks on tone of voice, fashion trends, and conversational scripts. This was not intuitive social fluency. It was intellectualized mimicry, a full-time research project undertaken to avoid the social punishment of being called “weird.”

The cost of this camouflage was a debt paid in exhaustion and identity. A 1999 qualitative study of women later diagnosed with Asperger’s Syndrome found a common refrain: they described feeling like “anthropologists on Mars” in social settings, a phrase one participant borrowed from neurologist Oliver Sacks. They spoke of crafting a “social self” through deliberate observation, a persona so effective it became indistinguishable from their own. The performance was convincing. It convinced teachers, parents, peers, and therapists. It was so convincing that when the inevitable burnout came—the collapse of this cognitively unsustainable facade in late adolescence or adulthood—the system saw only depression, anxiety, or a personality disorder. It rarely saw the neurological difference straining beneath the surface.

The cultural reinforcement was relentless. Girls were expected to be sociable, emotionally attuned, and neat. A girl who met these expectations through Herculean, hidden effort was praised for being “mature.” A girl who failed to meet them was “shy,” “overly dramatic,” or “a tomboy.” Neither label pointed toward a neurological reality. Both located the problem within the girl’s character. The loud, disruptive boy was a problem for the system. The quiet, overwhelmed girl was a problem for herself. The system was designed to manage the first. It had almost no language for the second.

The mechanism has a name now: masking. In the literature of the late 1990s, as the concept of Asperger’s entered the lexicon, researchers began to document this phenomenon of social camouflage. For the girls of the preceding decades, it was an unnamed survival strategy, developed through trial and profound error. It functioned not as a refusal of social rules, but as a hyper-literal adherence to them. Where a neurotypical girl might intuitively absorb the rhythm of a playground conversation, a neurodivergent girl like Marta Silva dissected it. Her diaries contain lists: “Topics 6th grade girls talk about: 1. Boys in our class (who is cute). 2. TV shows (Beverly Hills, 90210). 3. Who is mad at who. 4. Clothes.” Another entry outlines a script: “If someone says ‘What’s up?’, you say ‘Not much, you?’ Then they will tell you. Then you ask a question about what they said.”

This is not socializing. It is data analysis applied to human interaction. The goal is not connection, but the avoidance of a specific negative outcome: exposure. The psychologist Tony Attwood, in his early work with girls on the spectrum, observed they often had one or two close friends who served as “social interpreters,” providing explicit commentary on peer dynamics that the girls themselves could not decode. In the absence of such an interpreter, the girl became her own laboratory. She memorized the outfits in Seventeen magazine, not out of genuine interest, but to build a reference file for acceptable appearances. She practiced facial expressions in the mirror—learning the precise muscle configurations for a sympathetic nod or an interested smile. The performance could be flawless. It was also profoundly isolating, because the self presented to the world was a meticulously crafted artifact, leaving the actual self hidden and utterly alone.

The masking extends beyond social mimicry into the domain of behavior. Perfectionism becomes a primary tool. If you cannot intuit the unwritten rules, you can attempt to master every written one perfectly. A research paper published in the Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders in 1997 noted that girls referred for autism assessments often displayed “fewer overt repetitive behaviors” than boys, but exhibited “rigid adherence to routines of their own making” and “catastrophic reactions to perceived academic failure.” The meltdown does not happen in the classroom when the math worksheet is confusing. It happens at home, in the safety of a bedroom, when the effort to appear competent finally buckles under its own weight. The school sees a diligent, if anxious, student. The private reality is a child consumed by the terror of a single B-plus, because maintaining that grade is the brittle shield preventing total social and academic collapse.

People-pleasing is another pillar of the mask. When your own internal signals are unreliable or overwhelming, you outsource your sense of safety to the moods of others. You become exquisitely attuned to signs of disapproval—a teacher’s slight frown, a mother’s tired sigh, a friend’s cooled tone. You learn to calibrate your behavior not to what you want or need, but to what you infer will keep the peace and maintain your cover. This is often misread as empathy. It is not empathy in the neurotypical sense of shared feeling. It is threat detection. It is a constant, exhausting scan of the environment for social dangers, with appeasement as the primary defense strategy. The 1999 study of women with Asperger’s described this as a state of “chronic hyper-vigilance in interpersonal contexts.”

The consequence of this lifelong camouflage is a specific form of exhaustion that has, in recent years, been termed “autistic burnout.” For girls coming of age in the 80s and 90s, it had no such label. It manifested as a sudden inability to maintain the facade. The straight-A student in high school arrives at university and finds she cannot manage the unstructured schedule; she stops attending classes, not out of laziness, but because the cognitive load of simply finding the lecture hall and deciphering the social landscape of a seminar has consumed all her resources. The perpetually pleasant young woman in her first office job begins having panic attacks during team meetings, overwhelmed by the unspoken rules of corporate politics she never learned to decode.

The system, seeing only the collapse and not the decades of hidden strain that preceded it, applies the diagnoses it has at hand: Generalized Anxiety Disorder. Major Depressive Disorder. And, with devastating frequency for women, Borderline Personality Disorder. The emotional dysregulation from sensory and social overload looks like “affective instability.” The identity diffusion born of a lifetime of mimicking others looks like “unstable self-image.” The desperate efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment, a logical response to knowing your acceptance is contingent on a performance you can no longer sustain, look like “frantic efforts to avoid abandonment.” The mask, in breaking apart, creates a pathology that perfectly obscures its original, neurological purpose.

Fatima Al-Mansouri’s university medical records from 1995 chart this very progression. The initial referral is for “atypical depression and social isolation.” The psychiatrist’s notes describe a “flat affect” and “odd circumstantial speech.” They also note her “excessive concern with routine” and that she wears “the same style of clothing daily.” The diagnosis given is Dysthymia. The treatment is a standard SSRI. There is no evaluation for autism. The concept, for a verbal, intellectually gifted young woman, is not on the clinical radar. Her “flat affect” is the drained residue of a mask worn for twenty years. Her “odd speech” is the result of thinking in precise, logical chains, not social cadences. The system treats the depression as the primary illness. It is, in fact, the logical conclusion of a lifetime spent trying to pass as normal.

Marta Silva’s report cards accumulate in a cumulative folder, year after year, a paper trail of polite confusion. The comments, as documented in her archived school records, form a pattern. “Marta is a bright girl but needs to apply herself.” “She often forgets homework assignments.” “Her desk and backpack are chronically disorganized.” “She daydreams during lessons.” The language is one of moral failing, not cognitive difference. She is not trying hard enough. She is not applying herself. The subtext is clear: her struggles are a choice, a deficit of will.

The clinical literature of the time provides a stark contrast. The Journal of the American Academy of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry in 1991 publishes a study on ADHD subtypes. It describes the “hyperactive-impulsive” presentation with familiar, externalized symptoms: fidgeting, interrupting, running about. These are the behaviors that disrupt classrooms. These are the behaviors teachers, overwhelmed by thirty students, are trained to see and refer. The study also describes the “predominantly inattentive” presentation: forgetfulness, distractibility, disorganization, a tendency to lose things. These behaviors are quieter. They do not puncture the classroom’s social fabric. They simply erode a girl’s academic standing and self-esteem. The hyperactive boy bounces out of his seat. The inattentive girl stays perfectly still, her gaze fixed on the window, her mind traveling somewhere the lesson cannot follow. The system interprets the first as a disorder. It interprets the second as a character flaw.

Marta’s diaries decode the lived experience behind those teacher comments. An entry from sixth grade reads, “Mom says I’m lazy. I spent four hours at my desk last night. I opened my science book. I read the same paragraph about photosynthesis twelve times. Every time I finished, my brain had erased it. It’s like trying to hold water in a sieve. The harder I try, the faster it goes. I am not lazy. I am broken.” The effort is monumental and invisible. The output—a missing worksheet, a failed quiz—is all anyone sees. Her “daydreaming” is not a voluntary escape. It is the inevitable result of a brain struggling to regulate its own attention, pulled inward by a thought, a pattern in the wood grain of the desk, a memory, when the external stimulus of the teacher’s voice proves insufficiently anchoring.

This inattentive presentation in girls with ADHD often coexists with a frantic internal hyperactivity. Psychologist Kathleen Nadeau, in her pioneering 1999 book Understanding Women with ADHD, interviews dozens of women who describe a constant, buzzing static of thoughts, a mental restlessness that is entirely concealed by a physically still body. Their impulsivity does not manifest as shouting out in class. It shows up in a different domain: social impulsivity. They might overshare with acquaintances, purchase items they cannot afford, or cycle through intense, short-lived hobbies. These behaviors, lacking the public, disruptive quality of male-presenting hyperactivity, are again read through a gendered lens. They are “dramatic,” “boy-crazy,” “flighty,” or “scatterbrained.” The neurological root remains unseen.

The parallel process occurs with autistic traits. Where a boy might exhibit overt, physical stimming—hand-flapping, rocking—a girl learns to internalize or camouflage these regulatory behaviors. She might twist a ring on her finger under the desk, chew the inside of her cheek until it bleeds, or develop a “nervous habit” of plucking at her eyelashes. Her sensory sensitivities are similarly privatized. A boy who cannot tolerate the buzz of fluorescent lights might have a public meltdown, triggering a referral. A girl with the same sensitivity endures it, her distress manifesting as a pounding headache, a stomach ache, or a state of irritable exhaustion that she unleashes only in the safety of her home.

Hiro Tanaka’s younger sister, Aiko, appears in family interviews collected for a 1996 university ethnography on immigrant family dynamics. The researcher’s notes describe Aiko as “exceptionally quiet and orderly.” Her parents report she is a “perfect daughter” at home, spending hours in her room organizing her doll collection by precise historical era and sewing minute, period-accurate clothing for each one. The notes mention, in passing, that Aiko has “mysterious stomach ailments” that keep her home from school approximately one day a week. At a school concert, the researcher observes Aiko standing motionless on stage, her face a placid mask while other children fidget. After the performance, the researcher witnesses Aiko in the crowded hallway, pressed against a wall, her hands clamped over her ears as her mother tries to coax her forward. The public compliance is absolute. The private cost is a somatic bill that comes due in solitude or with family.

These intense, systematic interests, common in autistic girls, are often dismissed as harmless hobbies or praised as signs of academic diligence. Aiko Tanaka’s doll costuming is seen as “creative.” Another girl might amass an encyclopedic knowledge of horse breeds or Victorian poetry. The content often aligns with stereotypically feminine pursuits, making it less conspicuous. The difference lies not in the topic, but in the function and quality of the engagement. It is not a pastime; it is a world. It provides predictability, deep comfort, and a reliable source of cognitive engagement in a social landscape that feels chaotic and opaque. It is also monotropic, consuming attention to the exclusion of other developmental tasks. When a boy’s intense focus is on train schedules or dinosaurs, it may be flagged as odd. When a girl’s is on horses or dolls, it blends into the background.

The misattribution is a categorical error with profound consequences. The child is not treated for a neurodevelopmental condition. She is disciplined for laziness. She is therapized for anxiety. She is medicated for depression. She is taught coping skills for a personality flaw she does not have, while the actual neurological wiring that is causing her distress remains unacknowledged and unaccommodated. Every intervention that misses the root cause reinforces her core belief: I am not trying hard enough. I am too sensitive. I am fundamentally wrong.

Marta Silva’s teenage diary entries turn from confusion to a hardened, grim acceptance. “The problem is me,” she writes at sixteen. “I am a messy, forgetful, spacey person. I have to try harder to be less me.” The tragedy is complete. The neurodivergent trait has been successfully relabeled as a personal failing. The system designed to identify difference has instead produced a perfect indictment of the self. She is not struggling with executive dysfunction. She is lazy. She is not experiencing sensory overload. She is fussy. She is not masking to survive. She is a phony. The language of pathology has been inverted; the difference is not in her brain, but in her character. This is the legacy for a generation of girls: a diagnosis of personality, written in the invisible ink of a neurology the world refused to see.

The medical file for Fatima Al-Mansouri, spanning from 1995 to 1998, tells a story in triplicate forms and prescription logs. The primary diagnosis shifts from Dysthymia to Major Depressive Disorder, Recurrent, to Generalized Anxiety Disorder. The treatments follow a standard protocol: a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor, then a different one when the first yields little improvement, then the addition of a benzodiazepine for acute panic attacks. The notes describe symptoms: fatigue, anhedonia, persistent worry, insomnia. They describe behaviors: social withdrawal, avoidance of crowded places, perfectionism that “interferes with task completion.” At no point does the evaluating psychiatrist, as documented in these records, ask a fundamental question: what is this fatigue from?

The depression and anxiety are real. Their pain is documented in Fatima’s own brief notes on intake questionnaires, where she writes “constant tiredness” and “feeling of dread about everyday things.” But in the clinical framing, they are free-floating pathologies, primary diseases to be managed. The context is stripped away. The context is a lifetime of unrecognized autistic and ADHD traits. The fatigue is the exhaustion of a brain operating in a manual-override mode for two decades. The dread is the anticipation of yet another social interaction requiring a script she has not yet memorized, another environment with fluorescent lights and overlapping conversations that will scrape against her nervous system. The depression is not the cause of her struggles. It is the logical, physiological outcome.

Chronic masking is not a psychological choice. It is a cognitive load. A brain diverting constant resources to monitor eye contact, modulate tone of voice, suppress natural stimming behaviors, and translate social cues in real-time has fewer resources left for everything else: for working memory, for emotional regulation, for simply enduring the scream of a nearby vacuum cleaner. This load is unrelenting. There is no off-switch at the end of the school day, because the family dinner requires its own performance. The result is a state of persistent, low-grade stress that psychologists today would call allostatic load—the wear and tear on the body from chronic adaptation. For neurodivergent girls in the 1990s, this load had no name. It manifested in the body. It was migraines. It was gastrointestinal issues like irritable bowel syndrome. It was an immune system that seemed perpetually run-down. The body was keeping score the mind was not allowed to.

The academic struggle completes the trap. Many of these girls tested with high IQs. Their report cards, like Marta Silva’s, often included the phrase “bright but…” This intelligence became a curse. It raised expectations. It provided the very cognitive tools needed to construct more elaborate masks. And it made their failures—forgotten assignments, disorganized notebooks, exam anxiety—inexplicable to adults. “You’re so smart, if only you’d apply yourself,” is a sentence that haunts the memoirs of late-diagnosed women. The disconnect between capability and output is not a motivational problem. It is often a direct reflection of executive dysfunction—a neurocognitive issue with activation, working memory, and task management. But in the absence of that framework, the girl internalizes the critique. She is not applying herself. She is lazy. The anxiety becomes a secondary, desperate attempt to will herself into competence through worry. The depression is the grief of believing your own best efforts are a form of moral failure.

Social misunderstandings pile on another layer. A girl who has studied friendship as a concept may still miss its nuances. She may be too intense, too literal, too prone to monologues about her specific interest. Peers drift away. She is labeled “weird” or “clingy.” She does not understand why. Her response, often, is to redouble her observational efforts, to try harder to crack the code. This cycle of effort, failure, and rejection is a direct pathway to social anxiety. She begins to anticipate rejection before it happens. She avoids unstructured social situations not because she is antisocial, but because they are cognitively unmappable minefields. The clinical result is a diagnosis of Social Anxiety Disorder. The root cause—a neurodivergent communication style and a history of painful social feedback—remains unaddressed.

The system, focused on the visible distress, treats the branches and not the root. Therapy for anxiety teaches breathing exercises to use during panic attacks. It does not ask what sensory or social triggers might be precipitating those attacks. Therapy for depression focuses on correcting negative thought patterns. It does not investigate whether the thought “I am a failure” stems from a cognitive difference in task initiation, not a distorted belief. Medication may blunt the edges of the anxiety or lift the mood, but it does not reduce the cognitive load of masking. It does not provide accommodations for executive function. It does not explain to the girl why she feels so fundamentally out of step.

Fatima Al-Mansouri’s records show a brief hospitalization in 1997 following what is described as a “dissociative episode” in a busy university cafeteria. The discharge summary recommends increased dosage of her antidepressant and ongoing psychotherapy to address “underlying trauma.” The trauma is not a single event. It is the cumulative trauma of a lifetime of being unseen. It is the trauma of having your authentic self-expression stifled not by malice, but by a thousand small corrections—don’t flap your hands, look at me when I’m talking, why are you so sensitive, smile—until that authentic self goes underground, leaving only a hollow, performing shell. The depression that follows is not a chemical imbalance in a vacuum. It is a grief reaction for a self that was never allowed to exist.

By the time these women reached adulthood, the secondary conditions were so entrenched they had become the entire clinical picture. Their neurodivergence was a ghost in the machine of their own psychiatry, invisible to the tools designed to find it. The tragedy is one of misdirected effort. They spent years, decades, trying to cure themselves of anxiety and depression, when what they needed was not a cure for being neurotypical, but a recognition of being neurodivergent. The exhaustion was not a symptom to be medicated away. It was a signal. A signal that the cost of passing had finally exceeded the body’s ability to pay.

The clinical files, the report cards, the diaries—they are not just archives of individual struggle. They are a collective map of a terrain that went uncharted for decades. To look back at the experiences of girls and women in the 80s and 90s with this map in hand is to engage in a profound act of reinterpretation. It is not about retroactive diagnosis, but about retroactive compassion. The goal is to replace the old, punishing narratives of laziness, oversensitivity, and drama with a new understanding: these were often the surface expressions of an unseen neurodivergent neurology, shaped and masked by rigid gender expectations.

Begin with the question of effort. When you recall a girl who was labeled “lazy” or “not living up to her potential,” ask: What was the quality of her effort? Was she spending hours at a desk but producing little, like Marta Silva? Was her disorganization chaotic yet patterned? The issue was likely not a deficit of will, but a clash between her cognitive wiring and the demands of a neurotypical, structured environment. Her “potential” was real; the pathways to access it were blocked by unrecognized executive dysfunction or an attention system that worked differently.

Examine the social memories. The girl who was “painfully shy,” a “bookworm,” or who had one intense friendship that served as her social lifeline. Recall her in groups: was she observant, quiet on the periphery, mimicking the laughter of others a beat too late? This was not necessarily innate shyness. It could have been a social cognitive style that required analysis instead of intuition, leading to a calculated, exhausting performance. Her “odd” moments—a too-honest comment, a monologue on a niche interest, a misunderstanding of slang—were not social failures. They were clues to a brain that communicated with a different grammar.

Look at the behaviors labeled as “control issues” or “fussiness.” The need for a specific brand of sock, the meltdown over a change in dinner plans, the rigid nightly routine. In a boy, this might have been seen as a symptom. In a girl, it was often framed as being “difficult” or “princessy.” These were not bids for control, but desperate attempts to create predictability in a sensory and social world that felt overwhelmingly unstable. The reaction was not brattiness; it was dysregulation.

Most crucially, reframe the emotional landscape. The “overreactions,” the “sensitivity,” the “rollercoaster” moods. Before attributing them to inherent emotional fragility or a personality disorder, consider the sensory and cognitive context. Was she reacting to a texture, a sound, a social demand that others found neutral? Was her mood a downstream effect of hours or days of forced masking? Her “thin skin” might have been a nervous system with a different, more permeable threshold. The anxiety and depression that followed were not the core pathology, but the inevitable erosion of a self trying to exist in a world not built for it.

This reframing is an act of liberation. It allows a woman to look at her own history and separate her essential self from the survival strategies she was forced to adopt. The perfectionism was not a virtue; it was a shield. The people-pleasing was not kindness; it was a threat-detection system. The exhaustion was not weakness; it was the cost of a double life. Understanding this does not erase the pain of those years, but it can drain the poison of self-blame from the memory. You were not broken. You were navigating a world with the wrong set of instructions.

This process mirrors the journey Ben O’Leary began with his father’s ledger—seeing the cryptic notations not as random errors, but as a coherent system born of a different kind of mind. For the women of this generation, their own histories are that ledger. The “symptoms” are the entries: the stomach aches, the forgotten homework, the social faux pas, the burnout. Read with a neurodivergent lens, they cease to be a catalog of failures. They become a record of adaptation, of resilience, of a brain and body doing their absolute best to cope with a mismatch they could not name.

The final, empowering turn is to recognize that this history is not a life sentence. It is a key. It unlocks a new story about who you were, and in doing so, it opens a path to who you can be now—not a cured neurotypical person, but an integrated neurodivergent adult. The mask, once an essential tool for survival, can be examined piece by piece. Some parts may be set down forever. Others may be kept, but now by conscious choice, not desperate instinct. The exhaustion can be listened to, not medicated into silence. The interests that were dismissed as childish or obsessive can be reclaimed as sources of joy and focus.

This is the bridge from then to now. We have traced how the system missed the signs. The next step is to ask what happens when the signs are finally seen. How does a life, built on the foundation of those misunderstood years, begin to incorporate this new truth? The journey moves from historical diagnosis to present-day integration, from why it was missed to what we do now that we know.
