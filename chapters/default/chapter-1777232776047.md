The leather was a stubborn beast. It fought the awl, the needle, the thread. It remembered being skin, and it resisted the geometry of a tent. Saul’s hands moved without him. Poke, pull, tighten. The rhythm was a prison.

Sunlight fell in a hot, dusty bar across the workbench. It caught the motes of hide-dust floating in the air, turning them to gold. He’d been watching them for an hour. The way they drifted, aimless. He blew on a finished seam to clear the dust. His breath just stirred them into new constellations.

Outside, the sounds of Tarsus were a distant argument. A donkey brayed. A potter’s wheel whined. Children’s laughter, sharp and sudden as broken glass. He knew this city. He’d learned the Law in its shadowed colonnades, parsed the words of Gamaliel while the same sea breeze carried the smell of fish and tar. Now it was a cage with familiar bars.

His back ached. A low, familiar throb between the shoulders. He’d been hunched since dawn. He straightened, vertebrae popping like knuckles. The workshop was a single room, clay-brick and whitewash gone grey. Rolls of goat-hide leaned in the corner like sleeping men. Spools of linen thread. A jar of water, warm now. A fly buzzed against the high window, a tiny, frantic metronome.

Poke, pull, tighten.

He’d mastered this. His fingers, once stained with ink from scrolls, were now calloused and scored with tiny cuts from slipped needles. He could stitch a seam straight and strong enough to turn a desert wind. It meant nothing. It was the work of a day laborer, a pauper. A Pharisee of Pharisees did not mend tents. A chosen instrument did not sit in the dust, punching holes in dead skin.

The thought was a worn path. He walked it every day. It led nowhere.

He set the awl down. The silence after its tapping was a physical thing, a wool blanket pressed over his ears. He picked up a scrap of leather, a leftover from a larger cut. It was supple, tanned with oak gall. He ran his thumb over its surface. It felt like a cheek. He dropped it as if it had burned him.

His throat was dry. He drank from the jar. The water tasted of clay and yesterday.

A memory came, not as a flood but as a seepage. Not Stephen’s face this time. Something smaller. A market stall in Jerusalem, the one near the Essene Gate. The smell of cumin and dung. A woman selling lentils, her scales uneven. He’d been young, an apprentice to the council. He’d corrected her, his voice cold with the certainty of the Law. He’d made her pour out the measure, start again. Her hands had trembled. He’d taken a perverse satisfaction in it, a clean, sharp feeling. Righteousness had a taste then. Like cold metal.

Now the memory tasted of ash.

He stood, pushing the stool back with a scrape that set his teeth on edge. He walked to the doorway, leaning against the frame. The street was empty. A cat picked its way along the opposite wall, tail high. Down the hill, the Cydnus River was a brown ribbon, sluggish in the afternoon heat. Beyond it, the sea. The road to the docks was the road he’d walked as a boy, running errands for his father. He knew every cracked paving stone.

He was home. It felt like a sentence.

A letter had come from Barnabas, six months ago. Carried by a wool merchant with kind eyes and a guarded tongue. The brothers in Antioch send greetings. The work grows. The Lord adds to our number. We remember you. It was a careful letter. It said nothing of danger, of plots, of the cold silence that had followed him from Jerusalem. It said we remember you like a man might say the weather is fine. It was not a summons. It was a courtesy.

He’d written back. Laborious, ink-spotted words on a scrap of papyrus. I am well. The work here is steady. Give my peace to the brothers. He’d given it to the wool merchant for the return journey. He had no peace to give. It was a lie stitched as neatly as his seams.

The cat disappeared around a corner.

He’d expected visions here. In the stillness, in the exile. A voice from the rafters. A light in the corner of his eye. A command. Go to Arabia. Go to Cyprus. Go anywhere but this stifling, familiar room. He’d prayed until his knees were stone, until the words were just sounds he made with his mouth. The heavens were brass. God had spoken once, with a violence that shattered the world. Now He was silent. The silence was worse. It left room for the other voice, the one that lived in the hollow of his ribs. A tool left on the bench rusts. A weapon sheathed is forgotten.

He was not praying now. He was waiting. He didn’t know for what.

He went back to the bench. The half-finished tent panel lay across it, a shapeless hide. He picked up his mallet and a copper peg. He positioned the peg at the marked hole in the leather, raised the mallet. The action was pure muscle. The thunk of metal driving through hide into the wooden bench beneath was solid, final. A good sound. He set another peg. Thunk. Another.

Each impact traveled up his arm. Each was a word he couldn’t say.

Why.

Thunk.

Me.

Here.

He drove the last peg in too hard. The copper head bent, mushrooming under the blow. He stared at it. A ruined thing. Useless. He pried it out with the claw of his hammer. The hole was torn, ragged.

He let the hammer drop. It hit the dirt floor with a dull thud.

He put his head in his hands. The smell of his own skin—sweat and leather and salt—filled his nose. He did not weep. The anger was gone, burned out on the road to Damascus. The zeal was gone, spent in the synagogues and the back alleys. What was left was a hollowed-out fatigue. A certainty that he had misunderstood everything. The voice on the road, the scales falling, the fire in his bones to speak—maybe it had been a fever dream. A madness born of sunstroke and guilt. Maybe Ananias had been a kind man duped by his own vision. Maybe he, Saul, was just a man who had broken under the weight of his own certainty and was now patching tents in his father’s city, a quiet joke for the angels.

No. He couldn’t hold that thought. It was too vast, too dark. If that was true, then Stephen had died for nothing. Then the light was just a trick of the mind. Then the peace on Ananias’s face when he called him brother was a lie. Then the emptiness inside him was all there would ever be.

He lifted his head. The bent peg glinted in the sunbeam.

He picked it up. The metal was warm. He turned it over in his palm. A flawed thing. He’d made it. His own strength, misapplied.

A shadow fell across the doorway.

He didn’t look up. It was probably old Manasses from next door, coming to complain about the tapping. Or a boy with a delivery of hides.

“Is this the workshop of Saul of Tarsus?”

The voice was unfamiliar. Not local. A Greek accent, but layered with something else—Syrian, perhaps. A traveler’s voice.

Saul closed his hand around the ruined peg. The edges bit into his palm. He looked up.

A man stood in the doorway, blocking the light. He was tall, lean, dressed in a travel-stained tunic and a cloak thrown back over one shoulder. Dust coated his sandals to the ankle. He had a pack slung over one arm. His face was in shadow, but Saul could see the sharp line of a beard, the tired set of his shoulders.

“I am Saul.”

The man nodded. He didn’t enter. He studied the workshop, the rolls of leather, Saul’s hands. His gaze was slow, thorough. Not a customer’s look.

“They said you were here.” The man’s voice was quiet, but it carried in the still room. “They said you would be working with your hands.”

Saul stood. The peg was a cold lump in his fist. “Who are you?”

The man finally met his eyes. His own were a light brown, the color of river stones. They held no threat. No pity either. Just a steady, weary assessment.

“My name is Jonah,” the man said. “I come from Antioch.” He paused, as if weighing the next words. “Barnabas sent me.”

Jonah left the letter and a small purse of coins—payment for a tent Barnabas had commissioned, he said—and was gone before the sun dipped below the rooftops. Saul stood in the empty workshop, the papyrus scroll in his hand. He didn’t open it. The seal was a simple smudge of wax. He carried it into the back room, set it on the low table beside his pallet, and left it there.

His quarters were a cell. A pallet of straw. A washbasin on a stand. A single oil lamp, its flame guttering now in the evening draft from the courtyard. The walls were bare except for one thing: a shelf, and on it, the tools of his old life. A stack of papyrus sheets. An inkpot. A reed pen, dried and cracked from disuse. A few scrolls of Scripture, brought from Jerusalem. He hadn’t touched them since his return.

He lit the lamp. The flame fought the gloom, pushing shadows into the corners where they pooled like spilled ink. He sat on the pallet, his back against the cool wall. The letter was a pale rectangle in the weak light.

He should eat. He wasn’t hungry.

The silence was different now. It had a shape. It was the shape of the man in the doorway, the way he’d said Antioch. It was the shape of the unopened scroll.

He picked it up. Broke the seal. The papyrus crackled.

Saul, brother.

Grace to you and peace.

The congregation here grows. Greeks are coming to the belief in great number. The hand of the Lord is upon this place. We break bread daily, and there is joy, though the times are uncertain. We remember your zeal in Damascus, your understanding of the Scriptures. We have need of such understanding here. The questions are many. The harvest is great, the laborers few.

I write not to command you, but to remind you. The gift in you is not a memory. It is a fire. It is meant to burn.

If the Spirit moves you, come to us.

Your brother in the Messiah, Barnabas.

Saul read it twice. Then a third time, his lips moving silently over the Greek. Grace to you and peace. The words were a formula. They felt like stones in his mouth.

He saw the synagogue in Damascus again. The stunned faces, the rising fury. He’d argued the prophecies, connected the threads of Isaiah and the Psalms with a brutal, logical clarity. He’d proven, beyond any rabbinic doubt, that the Nazarene was the promised one. He’d done it with the same force he’d once used to condemn. It had felt like vomiting poison.

He dropped the letter. It fluttered to the dirt floor. A fire. It had burned everything. His standing. His purpose. His peace. It had left him here, in this room, smelling of goat hide and loneliness.

He stood, a sudden, restless motion. He went to the shelf. Took down a scroll. The leather case was soft with age. He unrolled it on the table, weighting the corners with a cup and the inkpot. The lamplight fell on the Hebrew characters. Isaiah. He found the place without searching. His fingers knew.

…he was pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities…

He’d read this a hundred times. A thousand. Before, it was a mystery, a closed door. After the road, it was a window thrown open, blinding in its clarity. Now it was a mirror. He saw the face of the man from the market, the one selling lentils. He saw the woman in Philippi whose son he’d had detained for blasphemy. He saw Stephen, not as a martyr but as a boy. The stones hitting his body. The sound they made. A wet, hollow sound, like a melon dropped on stone.

…the punishment that brought us peace was upon him…

What peace?

He had none. He had a hollow where his certainty used to be. He had hands that made tents and a mind that tore itself apart. He had a letter from a good man that felt like an indictment.

He pushed the scroll away. It curled in on itself. He picked up the dried reed pen, turned it over. A useless thing. He’d once written letters of authority with a pen like this. Names. Locations. Condemnations in tidy, formal script. He snapped it between his fingers. The sound was small, final.

He knelt beside his pallet. He pressed his forehead to the packed earth. The dirt was cool, grainy. He prayed. Not the structured prayers of the Pharisee, the eighteen benedictions. This was a raw, silent screaming in the dark of his own skull. Use me. Forgive me. Tell me what to do. Make it stop.

No answer. Just the ache in his knees. The smell of dirt and oil. A spider traversed the wall near his face, a tiny, deliberate speck moving from shadow into lamplight and back into shadow.

He got up. His knees popped. He was thirty-six years old and his body felt like an old man’s. He poured water from the jug into the basin, splashed his face. The water was tepid. It dripped from his chin onto his tunic. He looked at his reflection in the dark surface of the water, a rippling, broken ghost. The face of a stranger. The face of a man who had seen God and been ruined by it.

He blew out the lamp. Lay down on the pallet in the dark. The letter crinkled under his hip. He didn’t move it.

Sleep didn’t come. It never did easily. It was a thief, and it only crept in when he was too exhausted to fight. Tonight, his mind was a loom, shuttling the same dark threads back and forth.

Stephen’s voice. Lord, do not hold this sin against them.

His own voice, younger, harder. The sect must be rooted out.

The voice from heaven. Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?

The face of Ananias, kind and unafraid. Brother.

He turned onto his side. The straw poked through the thin blanket. He stared at the lighter rectangle of the doorway that led to the workshop. He had disciplined his body. He worked until he couldn’t stand. He prayed until his voice cracked. He studied until the letters swam. He had given up everything—comfort, reputation, safety. He had followed every rule of atonement he could devise.

It was a bucket with a hole in it. The peace always drained out, and what remained was the dregs: this acid knowing that he was trying to pay a debt that could not be paid. That his frantic work was just another kind of arrogance. That grace, if it was real, might be a thing you had to sit still to receive, and sitting still was the one thing he could not bear.

The night wore on. The sounds of the city died to a few distant barks, the call of a watchman. He must have slept, because he jerked awake to the grey light of dawn seeping into the room. His mouth was dry. His head throbbed. Another day.

He rose, folded his blanket. He saw the letter on the floor, picked it up, and placed it on the shelf next to the cracked pen. He went into the workshop. The half-finished tent panel waited. He worked through the morning in a fog, his movements precise and empty.

By midday, he needed olives, a little oil. He kept no stock of food. It was an excuse to move, to be among people without having to speak. He washed the dust from his hands and face, put on a cleaner tunic, and stepped into the street.

The market near the river was a chaos of color and noise. The press of bodies, the smell of spices, fish, sweat, and dung. Donkeys brayed. Vendors shouted. He moved through it like a ghost, his coin clenched in his fist. He bought a handful of olives from a woman with a lined face, nodding his thanks when she poured them into a twist of cloth. He was turning to go when he saw the man.

He was at a pottery stall, running his finger along the rim of a large water jar. His profile was sharp, his nose prominent. He had a certain way of holding his head, tilted slightly as he listened to the potter’s pitch. It was the posture of a scholar. Of a scribe.

Saul froze. The crowd jostled past him.

It wasn’t him. Of course it wasn’t. The man from the market in Jerusalem, the one with the uneven scales, would be older now, if he was even alive. This man was younger. His beard was different. But the resemblance was there, in the slope of the shoulders, the delicate shape of the hands examining the clay.

The potter said something, and the man laughed. A soft, genuine sound. He shook his head, patted the potter on the arm, and moved away, melting into the stream of people.

Saul stood rooted, the olives a heavy lump in his hand. The noise of the market faded to a dull roar. All he could see was the space where the man had been. All he could feel was the old, cold certainty in his voice as he’d demanded justice over a handful of lentils. The woman’s trembling hands.

He hadn’t just persecuted the followers. He’d persecuted anyone in the shadow of the Law. He’d been a weapon, and he’d swung blindly. The light on the road had shown him one truth—that the Messiah was Jesus—but it had opened a door to a darker hallway: the countless, ordinary cruelties he had performed in the name of God. He could map them like stars. A constellation of shame.

A cart rumbled past, its wheel catching his foot. The driver cursed. Saul stumbled back, blinking. The market sounds rushed in again, harsh and bright. He turned and pushed his way back through the crowd, not toward the workshop, but away, walking without direction, the taste of olives and guilt sharp and metallic on his tongue.

The sun beat down on the market, a physical weight on his shoulders. The air smelled of drying figs and something sweet, cloying, from a nearby spice stall. He moved through the throng, head down, watching the patterns his sandals made in the dust. The incident with the man at the pottery stall still gnawed at him. A phantom limb of guilt. He had bought dried fish from a vendor with a chipped tooth, paid for it with a single bronze coin, and was now making his way towards the city gate. He needed to walk. The walls of the workshop felt like they were closing in, even after three years.

A group of children darted past, their laughter bright and sharp. One of them, a boy no older than six, bumped into Saul’s leg, nearly sending him sprawling. The boy didn’t look back, already lost in the crowd. Saul watched him go. He remembered running through these same streets, a small terror of a child, always in trouble, always arguing. His father, stern and unyielding, had tried to channel that fiery spirit into scholarship. It had worked too well. He had become a weapon, honed and precise.

He found himself near the textile stalls, the air thick with the scent of dyed wool and linen. Rich merchants haggled in loud voices. A woman with a headscarf the color of pomegranates held up a bolt of deep blue cloth, inspecting it for flaws. Her fingers, dark and slender, moved over the fabric with a practiced grace. His own hands, rough and scarred, felt alien. He curled them into fists inside his tunic. He had learned to make tents, to work with leather and canvas. He had learned to build things, not tear them down. But the tearing down felt more natural. It always had.

A voice cut through the market clamor, a man shouting about the price of olives. It was the same voice as the vendor from yesterday, the one with the chipped tooth. Saul squeezed his eyes shut for a moment. He could still see the woman’s hands trembling when he’d accused her. That small, insignificant cruelty. It had felt like justice then. It felt like ash now. The crowd pressed in, a wall of bodies and noise. He pushed forward, needing open space, needing air that didn’t smell of his own failures.

He reached the edge of the market, where the stalls thinned out and the street opened onto a wider thoroughfare leading out of the city. A small group of men stood talking, their voices low. Three of them were local, their Tarsian dialect thick and familiar. The fourth man was a stranger, his tunic finer, his beard neatly trimmed. He spoke with a slight accent that Saul recognized as Damascene. Saul hesitated, not wanting to intrude, but drawn by the cadence of the foreign voice. He recognized the rhythm of a traveler, a man who had seen other cities, other ways.

“The trouble began with the Greeks,” one of the Tarsian men said, his voice a low grumble. “Always the Greeks.”

The Damascene man shook his head. “Not trouble, my friend. Not for us. For them.” He gestured vaguely towards the east. “The synagogues, they do not know what to do. The new sect, the followers of the Way. They preach in the market squares, in the houses. Even to the Gentiles.”

Saul’s breath hitched. He stopped walking. The bronze coin, still in his fist, dug into his palm. The Way. He hadn’t heard that phrase spoken aloud in years. Not since Damascus.

“Gentiles?” A Tarsian man scoffed. “What need have they for our Messiah?”

The Damascene man smiled, a slow, knowing curve of his lips. “They have need of something, friend. And this ‘Messiah’… he offers it. Peace. Forgiveness. It is a powerful message, particularly where the Roman boot presses heaviest.” He paused, looking directly at the Tarsian man. “And in Antioch, it thrives. They say Barnabas, the Levite, he is there. And with him, a man of great learning. A former Pharisee, they say. One who once persecuted the Way, but now preaches it with fire.”

Saul felt a cold tremor run through him, starting at the base of his spine and spreading outward. Barnabas. Antioch. A man of great learning. A former Pharisee. He knew the words from the letter, lying on his shelf in the workshop. He knew the meaning. It was him. They were talking about him. The Damascene man’s eyes flickered, moving across the crowd. He hadn’t seen Saul, not truly. Just a man standing still in the stream of people.

“A Pharisee, turned apostate?” The Tarsian man chuckled, shaking his head. “A strange tale. What would make a man abandon all he knows?”

The Damascene man shrugged. “Some say he saw a great light. Others, that he was struck blind by the truth.” He looked away from the group then, his gaze sweeping the street, as if searching for someone. His eyes paused, briefly, on Saul. Not a recognition, just a fleeting glance. Saul held his breath, his muscles tight. The Damascene man’s eyes moved on, dismissing him as another nameless face in the market.

“Well, good for them,” the Tarsian man said, waving a dismissive hand. “Here, we have enough gods. No need for new ones.”

The Damascene man offered another small, almost secret smile. “Perhaps. But the wind, my friend, it blows where it will. And in Antioch, the wind carries a new song.” He clapped the Tarsian on the shoulder. “I must go. My caravan departs within the hour.” He nodded to the others. “May your gods keep you.” He turned, walking with purpose towards the city gate, his shoulders straight, his stride long.

Saul watched him go, the words echoing in his head. A man of great learning. A former Pharisee. Preaches it with fire. He had not preached with fire in years. He had hidden in Tarsus, stitching tents, burying himself in the mundane, hoping to outrun the ghost of his past. But the ghost had found him. It had found him in the market, in the words of a stranger.

His heart beat a slow, heavy rhythm against his ribs. A fire. The same fire Barnabas had written about. It wasn’t a destructive blaze, not the consuming fury of his old zeal. This felt different. Warmer. It was the heat of purpose, of direction. A calling, not a condemnation.

He turned, not back towards the workshop, but towards the city gate, the same direction the Damascene man had taken. The dust rose around his sandals, soft and fine. He had spent three years paying a debt, trying to earn a grace he couldn’t comprehend. He had tried to become a shadow, to disappear into the quiet rhythms of Tarsus. But the world, it seemed, would not let him. Antioch. Barnabas. A community. A reason.

The sun was high now, beating down on the road. The gate was a dark arch, a mouth leading out into the wider world. He walked towards it, the taste of olives and guilt still in his mouth, but now, beneath it, a faint, unfamiliar sweetness.
