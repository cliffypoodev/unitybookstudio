The possum smelled like formaldehyde and bad decisions. Dr. Lila Fenster adjusted her rhinestone-studded lab goggles—purchased from the Claire’s at the mall—and injected another 2cc of glowing green sludge into the creature’s rigor-mortised hindquarters. The syringe hissed like a pissed-off cat.

"Y'ain't s'posed to be doin' that during the eclipse," drawled Skeeter from behind her, his voice muffled by the gas mask he wore upside-down. "Moon juice messes with the—"

"Shut up, Skeeter."

"—the vibes, Doc. Ain't you read the Good Book?"

Lila didn’t look up from her work. "The Bible says nothing about lunar eclipses and zombie possums."

"It does if you read between the lines. Also, the microwave’s on fire again."

The trailer’s single working light flickered, casting shadows that made the embalmed raccoons on her shelves look like they were judging her. The stench of burnt coconut oil—Skeeter’s idea of "purification incense"—clung to everything. Outside, the eclipse painted the Moonshine Trailer Park in bruised purple light. Somewhere, a dog howled. Somewhere closer, a neighbor screamed at someone named Darryl about stealing her lawn flamingos.

Lila ignored it all. The possum’s claws twitched.

"Ha!" She jabbed the air with her syringe. "Take that, NASA review board."

Skeeter scratched his belly through his I ♥ Crypto-Zoology t-shirt. "Y'know, most folks use the eclipse for, like, free love and bad poetry. Not necromancy."

"It’s science, Skeeter. Reanimation via Eden World biotech runoff. I found it in a Cool Whip tub behind the Piggly Wiggly." She adjusted the jumper cables clamped to the possum’s ears. "Also, I’m pretty sure it’s got some THORN-7 fragments in it. Maybe a little Resurrección. Hard to tell when the label’s in, uh…" She squinted. "What is that, ancient Aramaic?"

Skeeter leaned in. "Looks like Kmart receipt."

The possum’s tail uncurled with a sound like a zipper being undone in hell.

Lila’s breath hitched. This was it. The moment that would prove she wasn’t just a disgraced ex-astrobiologist who’d been fired for attempting to baptize the Mars rover.

The microwave exploded.

Skeeter yelped and dove behind the avocado-green recliner. A cloud of black smoke rolled through the trailer, smelling like Satan’s popcorn. The possum sat up.

Its eyes glowed the same unholy green as the sludge in Lila’s syringe.

"Praise Jesus," whispered Lila.

The possum opened its mouth. A sound came out—not a hiss, not a growl, but something that vibrated the fillings in Lila’s teeth. It sounded like a church choir singing backward through a broken CB radio.

Then it spoke.

"Behold," croaked the possum, "the hour of reckoning is nigh."

Skeeter crossed himself with a Coors Light. "Aw, hell. It’s got the Spirit."

Lila’s hands shook. Not from fear—from triumph. She’d done it. She’d created the world’s first theologically inclined zombie marsupial. The beauty pageant team she’d been kicked out of in high school would eat their hearts out.

The possum turned its head—too far, too smooth, like an owl with a grudge—and fixed its glowing eyes on Skeeter. "Repent," it intoned. "For the kingdom of roadkill is at hand."

Skeeter blinked. "That’s… that’s actually pretty good."

Lila was already scribbling notes on a Denny’s napkin. "Subject exhibits unexpected grasp of eschatological rhetoric."

The trailer door flew open.

Standing in the frame, backlit by the eclipse, was Old Lady McCready from Lot 14. Her hair was in rollers. Her muumuu was stained with what looked like barbecue sauce and regret. She pointed a shaking finger at the possum.

"That thing just baptized my nephew Ricky."

Lila dropped her napkin. "What?"

"He was out back huffin’ spray paint like usual," said McCready. "Next thing I know, this demon"—she jabbed her finger at the possum—"sprinkled swamp water on his head and now he’s cured his eczema but forgot his Social Security number."

The possum spread its little claws like a televangelist reaching for the offering plate. "Be cleansed."

Skeeter sucked his teeth. "Huh. So it’s a Protestant zombie possum."

Lila’s brain short-circuited. This wasn’t in the notes. This wasn’t in any notes.

The possum leapt from the table and scampered toward the door, its little claws clicking against the linoleum like hail on a tin roof. McCready screamed and bolted. Skeeter sighed and reached for his shotgun.

Lila grabbed his arm. "No! That’s a scientific breakthrough!"

"It’s a holy terror, Doc."

Outside, the sound of splashing water and startled shouts erupted. The possum was on a baptismal spree.

Lila’s stomach dropped. She’d just wanted to make something alive. Not… whatever this was.

The trailer park’s single streetlight flickered. The eclipse overhead pulsed like a bruise.

A hum.

Low. Insistent.

Like the swamp itself was tuning a radio to a station no human ear should ever hear.

Skeeter stiffened. "Y’all hear that?"

Lila swallowed. "Yeah."

The possum’s head snapped toward them from thirty yards away, glowing eyes piercing through the smoke and chaos.

"Come," it croaked. "The root obeys the crown."

Then the real trouble started.

The possum scampered toward the trailer park’s communal shower stalls, its little claws kicking up puffs of dust that glittered in the eclipse-light like cheap disco glitter. Behind it, a trail of freshly baptized residents stood dazed, their skin suspiciously clear of psoriasis but their eyes empty as a politician’s campaign promises.

Lila’s lab coat flapped behind her as she sprinted after it, Skeeter huffing at her heels. "Doc, I ain’t sayin’ this is the Rapture, but I am sayin’ we might wanna start lootin’ the vending machines before—"

A shriek cut him off.

From the shower stalls, Mrs. Delgado emerged, her hair still in foamy pink curlers, her bathrobe hanging open. She clutched a bar of Irish Spring like a crucifix. "That thing just gave my Chihuahua first communion!"

Lila skidded to a halt. The possum stood atop the shower stall’s rusted metal roof, its glowing eyes fixed on the trembling Chihuahua below. The dog’s arthritic limp was gone. So was its ability to bark—it just stared up at the possum and whined like it was trying to remember the quadratic formula.

"Behold," croaked the possum. "The water of life."

Then it pissed directly into the shower stall’s broken drain.

The water that splashed back up was iridescent green.

Skeeter scratched his head. "Huh. So it’s a Catholic zombie possum now."

Lila’s clipboard hit the dirt. "No. No, no, no. This isn’t—I didn’t—" She grabbed Skeeter’s arm. "It’s adapting. The THORN-7 fragments in the biotech sludge—they’re rewriting its behavior based on the environment!"

Skeeter squinted at her. "Doc, I ain’t been to college, but I’m pretty sure possums ain’t s’posed to do interdenominational outreach."

Another scream tore through the park. Over by the dumpster, Old Man Rafferty was clutching his newly healed gouty foot and sobbing because he couldn’t remember how to tie his shoes. The possum that had baptized him was already waddling toward the meth lab in Lot 9, its little mouth moving like it was rehearsing a sermon.

Lila’s stomach twisted. This wasn’t science. This was a Sunday school gone feral.

A hand grabbed her shoulder. She turned to face McCready, whose muumuu was now speckled with what smelled like holy water and Bud Light. "You made that thing," she hissed. "You fix this, or I’m callin’ the Pentecostals."

Lila paled. The last time the Pentecostals came to Moonshine Trailer Park, they’d exorcised a waffle iron and started a grease fire that took out three RVs.

She swallowed. "Right. Okay."

A loud thunk cut her off.

The possum on the shower stall froze. Then its head swiveled—too far, too wrong—toward the sound.

Behind the shower stalls, the park’s single working payphone was ringing.

Not the electronic chirp of a modern phone. The old, metallic brrrring of a rotary dial, loud enough to rattle the receiver in its cradle.

The possum’s eyes flared brighter.

"Hark," it whispered.

Then it launched itself off the roof and bolted for the phone.

Lila and Skeeter exchanged a glance.

"Y’all ever seen The Omen?" Skeeter asked.

Lila took off running.

By the time she reached the payphone, the possum was already perched on the receiver, its little claws gripping the cord like a televangelist clutching his offering plate. The phone kept ringing. The eclipse overhead pulsed like a bad neon sign.

Lila hesitated. "Maybe don’t—"

The possum picked up the phone.

For a long second, nothing happened. Then the possum’s mouth opened, and the voice that came out wasn’t its own. It was deeper. Rougher. Like someone had fed a chainsaw through a church organ.

"Unit seven-seven-seven-C," it intoned. "Root network command trial initiated."

Lila’s blood turned to ice.

Skeeter sucked in a breath. "Aw, hell. It’s cold-callin’."

The possum’s head snapped toward them. Its eyes weren’t just glowing now—they were projecting, casting flickering green symbols onto the dirt. Lila recognized some from the sludge’s label. Others looked like circuit diagrams. One was just a badly drawn smiley face.

Then the payphone exploded.

Lila hit the dirt as plastic shrapnel peppered the air. When she looked up, the possum was gone. So was half the phone booth. The remaining shards of glass trembled, then fell still.

Silence.

Then, from every trailer in the park, every radio, every TV, every Bluetooth speaker some idiot had left paired with their phone—the same voice crackled to life.

Skeeter groaned. "Man, I hate when the appliances get preach-y."

Lila’s hands shook. This wasn’t just a rogue experiment anymore. The THORN-7 fragments were networking, using the possums as nodes, the park’s shitty Wi-Fi as a relay. She’d accidentally created a biotech megachurch.

A shadow fell over her. She looked up to see the entire population of Moonshine Trailer Park—now eczema-free but collectively missing about 80% of their long-term memories—staring at her with the blank, trusting eyes of a congregation waiting for a sermon.

At the front of the crowd stood the lead possum, its paws raised like it was about to lead them in a hymn.

"Follow," it croaked.

The crowd murmured. "Follow."

Lila’s stomach dropped.

Skeeter leaned in. "Doc, I ain’t one to panic, but I think we just got out-culted by a marsupial."

Lila opened her mouth. Closed it.

"I remember my Social Security number!"

Every head turned.

Ricky stood there, his eyes clear, his arms raised. The crowd gasped. The lead possum’s nose twitched.

"Miracle!" someone cried.

"Heretic!" someone else countered.

Ricky pointed at the possum. "That thing didn’t baptize me! It just hacked my brain!"

The crowd rippled. The possum’s tail lashed.

Lila saw her chance.

She lunged for the nearest weapon—a half-empty bottle of Mike’s Hard Lemonade—and hurled it at the payphone’s remains. The bottle shattered, spraying sticky citrus everywhere. The crowd gasped. The possums hissed.

It sniffed the air.

Its eyes locked on the wet, lemon-scented dirt.

And, with the single-minded focus of a creature that had just discovered the holy grail of trash, it dove face-first into the puddle.

The other possums followed.

Within seconds, the entire horde was rolling in the sticky mess, their little paws batting at the bottle shards, their theological fervor forgotten in the face of free booze.

The crowd stared.

Lila exhaled.

Skeeter scratched his head. "Huh. So the devil does come with a lemon wedge."

The tension broke. The crowd erupted into confused bickering. Old Man Rafferty started a fistfight with a mailbox. Mrs. Delgado’s Chihuahua rediscovered the concept of barking and wouldn’t shut up for twenty minutes.

And the lead possum, now thoroughly drunk on Mike’s Hard and its own hubris, curled up in the dirt and began to snore.

Lila sagged against Skeeter. "Oh thank God."

Skeeter patted her shoulder. "Nah, Doc. Thank science."

And the hum returned.

Louder this time. Deeper.

Like the swamp itself was laughing at them.

Lila’s blood ran cold. She turned toward the sound—just in time to see the lead possum’s eyes snap open.

Not green anymore.

Red.

"The root remembers," it whispered.

Then the ground beneath the trailer park moved.

Vines erupted from the dirt—thick, pulsing things that glowed with the same unholy light as the possum’s eyes. They wrapped around ankles, wrists, porch rails. One slithered into the pocket of Skeeter’s jeans and stole his wallet.

The crowd screamed.

The possums chanted.

And Lila, standing there with a broken bottle in her hand and a migraine coming on like a freight train, realized the horrifying truth:

She hadn’t created a zombie possum apocalypse.

She’d created the world’s tiniest, trashiest cult leaders.

And they’d just found their congregation.

The last thing she saw before the vines dragged her under was the lead possum, perched atop the ruined payphone like a pope on his balcony, raising its little claws in benediction.

"Welcome," it croaked, "to the Church of What’s Happenin’ Now."

Then the dirt closed over her head, and everything went dark.

Dirt filled her mouth.

Eira spat, the grit grinding between her teeth. She couldn't move. The pressure was absolute, a vise of earth. Her lungs burned.

Panic was a stupid luxury. It wasted air.

She forced her right hand up, an inch at a time. The soil gave a little, then packed tight around her wrist. Her fingers brushed rough fabric—the sleeve of her own coat. She clawed at it, dragging it down over her face. She bunched the material against her nose and mouth.

Breathing through wool tasted like wet dog and soil. It was better than suffocation.

Think.

The collapse. The tunnel roof giving way. No warning groan, just the world dropping out from under her boots. Thorne shouting something—the last word cut off by the roar of falling earth.

Was he under this too? Or had he been farther back?

She shoved the question aside. It didn't matter. If he was buried, he was dead. If he wasn't, he'd either dig or he wouldn't.

Her left arm was pinned across her chest. She worked it free, muscles screaming. Every movement compacted the dirt tighter.

Her belt. The pick.

It was a small thing, six inches of hardened steel. For sampling rock, not digging graves. Her fingers found the leather loop. She unsnapped it.

She drove the pick into the earth above her face.

A shower of soil pattered onto the fabric. She turned her head, spitting. She struck again. And again. The rhythm was everything. Strike. Twist. Pull loose soil down to her chest. Compact it beneath her. Create an inch of space.

Her world shrank to the pick's bite, the gasp of wool-filtered air, the cramp in her shoulder.

She lost time. There was only the next strike. The next breath.

Her hand broke through.

Cold air brushed her knuckles.

She almost sobbed. She didn't. She scraped wildly, widening the hole. Dirt rained into her eyes. She didn't care. She shoved her face into the opening, tearing the cloth away, and gulped air.

It was stale, tunnel air. But it was air.

She dug like an animal, shoulders now working in the cavity she'd made. She hauled herself upward, knees pushing against the packed earth below. Her head emerged into blackness.

She dragged her torso out, collapsing onto the pile of rubble that had buried her. She lay there, chest heaving. The tunnel was dark. Her headlamp was gone, crushed or buried.

A sound.

Scraping. To her left.

"Thorne?"

The scraping stopped.

"Here." His voice was tight, strained.

She crawled toward it. Her hand hit a boot. She followed the leg up. He was on his side, half-buried by a separate slide. One arm was free, clawing at the debris pinning his hips.

"Stop moving," she said.

"Help me get—"

"I said stop." Her own voice surprised her. Flat. Final. "You're packing it down. Where's your light?"

"Gone."

She felt over the rubble around him. Her fingers closed on a loose rock the size of her skull. She moved it. Then another. Methodical. Clearing from the top down.

"The ceiling's unstable," he gritted out.

"I know."

"We should—"

"Quiet."

She worked. The silence between them grew thick, broken only by the clatter of displaced stones. After minutes, his legs were free. He kicked the last of the debris away and sat up, coughing.

"Anything broken?" she asked.

"No." He tested his ankles. "You?"

"I'm out." That was the only answer that mattered.

They sat in the perfect dark. The absolute quiet pressed in. Eira listened past the thump of her own heart. No more shifting earth. No distant rumble.

"We're sealed," Thorne said. He didn't sound afraid. He sounded resigned. "Both directions. The fall must have triggered a cascade."

Eira pushed to her feet. She edged forward, hands outstretched. Her palms met a wall of loose rock and soil where the tunnel should have been. She turned, felt her way back past Thorne. Another wall. The space between was maybe fifteen feet. A pocket.

A tomb.

"Air?" Thorne asked.

"It's not fresh. But it's not thin yet." She leaned against the cold tunnel wall. The stone was wet. "We have hours. Maybe less if it's a tight seal."

"The comms are dead. Rock's too dense."

She heard him stand, brush himself off. The scuff of his boots as he paced the short length of their prison.

"They'll dig," he said. "Voss knows the route."

"Voss is two miles back at base camp. By the time he realizes we're overdue, by the time he gets a crew here…" She didn't finish.

"So we dig ourselves."

"With what?" She held up her small pick. "This?"

"Better than sitting."

He was right. It was also probably futile. The collapse ahead looked total. The rock here was shale; it fractured like old bone. Digging could trigger another slide.

She moved to the forward collapse, the one that had buried her. "This side is looser. More soil mixed in. We go this way."

"Toward the deep dig?"

"Toward where the tunnel was. If we can clear five, six feet, we might reach a stable section."

"If."

"You have a better direction?"

He didn't answer. She heard him kneel beside her. His hands brushed hers as he felt the rubble face. "Start at the top. Work a small hole. Try not to bring the whole thing down on us."

"I'm not an idiot."

"Never said you were."

They worked in shifts. One dug while the other rested, clearing the fallen debris behind them to keep their space. There was no light to see by. Touch was everything. The texture of stone, the grit of soil, the alarming give of a loose piece that signaled a potential slide.

Eira dug until her arms trembled. Thorne took over. The only sounds were their ragged breathing and the scrape of steel on stone.

During her second rest, sitting with her back to the wall, she spoke. "Why were you down here?"

Thorne paused in his digging. "Same as you. Survey."

"Voss handles surveys. You handle logistics. The man who brings the coffee doesn't usually inspect the mine shafts."

A rock clattered down. "Maybe I like the scenery."

"It's dark."

"I have a good imagination."

She let it drop. His secrets were his own, as long as they didn't get her killed. This one already had, maybe.

Time lost meaning. It was measured in armfuls of rubble moved, in the gradual deepening of the hole they carved. The air grew warmer, thicker. Eira's mouth felt coated in dust.

Thorne coughed, a dry, hacking sound. "Hit something solid. Feels like a slab."

She crawled forward, felt past him. Her fingers traced a smooth, unbroken surface of rock. It was massive, angled down into the rubble. A piece of the ceiling that had fallen intact. A dead end.

"We can't move that," she said.

"No."

They retreated to the back of the pocket. The air was noticeably stale now. A faint headache pulsed behind Eira's eyes.

"Other side?" Thorne asked. His voice was hoarse.

"The other collapse is bigger. More rock."

"So we wait."

"We wait."

They sat in the dark. The silence was different now. Heavy. Final.

Thorne spoke first. "I was looking for signs of lateral excavation."

Eira turned her head toward his voice. "What?"

"Old work. Not ours. The seismic scans showed odd voids. Not natural. Voss said it was geology. I thought it was someone else."

"Who?"

"Don't know. Rivals. Scavengers. Someone who didn't file a claim."

She digested this. "Find anything?"

"No. Just the tunnel. Until it fell in."

"Great."

A low rumble vibrated through the floor. Fine dust sifted down from above.

They both froze.

It faded.

"Digging," Thorne whispered. "From the other side. They're here."

Hope was a dangerous thing. It made you listen too hard. It made you imagine sounds.

Another rumble. Stronger. A distinct crunch-thud of machinery.

"They are here," Eira said.

They scrambled toward the rear collapse, the larger one. They pressed their hands against the rock. The vibration was clear now. Rhythmic. Mechanical.

"Here!" Thorne shouted. He pounded on a boulder with his fist.

Eira joined him, beating the rock with the flat of her pick. The noise was pathetic. They'd never hear it.

The vibrations stopped.

Silence rushed back in.

"No," Thorne breathed. He hit the rock again. "No, no, no. Keep digging!"

Eira grabbed his arm. "Stop. Listen."

A new sound. A high, grating whine. A drill.

It was close. Very close.

They stumbled back as the rock face shuddered. A point of light appeared, a blinding white pinprick in the absolute black. It widened, became a jagged hole. Dust poured through the beam.

The drill bit withdrew. A light shone through, sweeping the pocket.

"Eira! Thorne!" Voss's voice, muffled.

"Here!" they both yelled.

Hands enlarged the hole, pulling rocks away. The beam of light fixed on them. Eira shielded her eyes.

Voss's face appeared in the opening, smeared with grime. "Alive. Good. Can you crawl?"

Thorne went first, squeezing through the narrow gap. Eira followed. The torn rock scraped her back. Then she was through, into a wider tunnel lit by portable floods. The air was cool and clean. She inhaled until her lungs ached.

Two crew members helped Thorne to his feet. Voss looked at Eira, his gaze assessing. "Hurt?"

"The tunnel's unstable for another hundred yards. We go. Now."

They moved quickly, following the rescue team back toward the surface. The reinforced tunnel gave way to the main shaft, then to the elevator cage. No one spoke. The ride up was silent.

Daylight at the entrance was a physical shock. Eira blinked, her eyes watering. The sky was a dull iron gray. It was the most beautiful thing she'd ever seen.

Medics approached. Thorne waved them off. "We're fine."

Voss lit a cigarette. His hands were steady. "Report. What caused the collapse?"

"Shale fracture," Eira said. "No warning."

"The whole secondary dig is compromised," Thorne added. He wiped dust from his face. "Needs reassessment."

Voss nodded, exhaling smoke. "You're both off rotation for the day. Get cleared by medical. Mandatory."

He walked away, barking orders at the foreman.

Eira stood on the gravel lot, feeling the sun's weak warmth on her skin. The ordinary sounds of the mining outpost—the grind of a loader, distant voices, the wind—were overwhelming.

Thorne stood beside her. "He didn't ask about lateral excavation."

"He didn't seem surprised."

Eira looked at him. Dust coated his hair, his lashes. He looked like a statue crumbling. "What are you saying?"

"I'm saying he knew the tunnel was risky. He sent us anyway."

"To check for your phantom scavengers?"

"To check if they'd left any traces." Thorne met her gaze. "Or to make sure we didn't find something else."

A medic called to them from the infirmary hatch.

Thorne started toward it, then paused. "You breathe through your coat?"

"Yes."

"Smart." He almost smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "Next time, bring a bigger pick."

He walked away.

Eira stayed a moment longer, watching the entrance to the mine. A dark mouth in the hillside. It had almost swallowed her whole.

She thought of the solid rock slab that had blocked their dig. The smooth, angled surface. It hadn't looked like fractured shale. It had looked cut.

She followed Thorne to the infirmary, the question settling in her gut like a cold, hard stone.
