He didn’t sleep. The cold on his cheek stayed long after the weight on the bed had faded, a phantom sunburn in reverse. Dawn came, a gray smear through the blinds. He got up, made coffee, and the routine of it—the scoop of grounds, the click of the machine, the slow drip—felt like a lie he was telling the apartment. A performance of normalcy for an audience of empty rooms. The scent of brewing coffee couldn’t touch the other smell, the one that had lingered in the air beside his pillow. Old paper. Cold stone. It was in his sinuses now, a permanent background note.

He spent the morning cleaning again. He scrubbed the bathroom tile grout with a toothbrush. He organized his books not by author but by color, creating a meaningless gradient of spines on the shelf. It was busywork for his hands, a dam against the static in his head. But the dam was porous. Images leaked through, not as full visions, but as sense-memories without context.

The warmth of sun on bare shoulders. Not the weak, filtered light through his apartment window, but a direct, heavy heat. The grit of sand underfoot. The taste of something sweet and tangy on his tongue—pineapple, maybe. A laugh, low and warm, cut off by the sound of surf.

He stopped, the toothbrush hovering over a mildew spot. His own breathing was loud in the small room. These weren’t frightening. That was the problem. The cold breath on his cheek had been terror, pure and simple. This was… nostalgia. A deep, aching homesickness for a place he had never been.

“Stop it,” he said aloud. His voice was flat, absorbed by the tiles.

The afternoon bled away. He tried to read, but the words swam. He’d get to the end of a paragraph and have no idea what it said. His mind was elsewhere, tracing the contour of a memory that wasn’t his. A memory of a face.

He didn’t know her name. Not yet. But he knew the slope of her nose, the way one eyebrow arched slightly higher than the other when she was skeptical. He knew she had a faint dusting of freckles across her cheekbones that only appeared in summer. He knew the exact shade of her eyes—not blue, not green, but the color of sea glass tumbled smooth.

This knowing arrived not as a revelation, but as a stubborn, pre-existing fact. Like remembering his own birthday.

It was madness. He knew it was madness. He gripped the arms of his chair, the fabric rough under his palms. A real thing. He was here, in this chair, in this apartment that smelled of cleaning products and dust. Arthur Brindle died here. That was the haunting. Not some sun-drenched fantasy.

But the fantasy had weight. It had texture. It had the smell of salt and sunscreen.

Frustration boiled over. He shoved himself out of the chair and paced to the window. The street below was quiet, a Tuesday afternoon lull. A woman walked a small, prancing dog. A delivery truck idled double-parked. Normal life, carrying on in its normal channel. He was the one receiving interference.

He needed to see it. To make it external. If it was outside of him, he could maybe look at it, analyze it, prove it was just a construction.

He didn’t own any art supplies. He found a blank page in the back of his notebook and a cheap ballpoint pen. He sat at the kitchen table, the bare bulb overhead casting a hard circle of light. He clicked the pen. Hesitated.

This was giving in. This was what crazy people did—they drew the faces of the voices in their heads.

But the alternative was letting it eat a hole in his sanity from the inside.

He put the tip of the pen to the paper. He didn’t sketch. He didn’t plan. His hand moved, guided by a muscle memory that belonged to a different set of hands. A confident, flowing line for the jaw. A lighter, tentative one for the curve of a smile. He drew the eyes closed, with long lashes fanning downward, because he couldn’t get the sea-glass color right with blue ink, but he knew the shape of the lid, the delicate crease.

He worked in silence, the only sound the scratch of the pen. He didn’t breathe. He was a conduit. The face emerged from the white space not as an invention, but as a recollection. He knew the exact point where her hairline met her temple. He knew the small, almost imperceptible scar through her left eyebrow.

When he finally stopped, his hand was cramping. He leaned back.

A woman looked up from the page. She was sleeping, or resting. Peaceful. She was beautiful, but in a specific, ordinary way. The kind of face you’d see laughing in a grocery store aisle. Real.

A cold nausea, entirely separate from the haunting, washed through him. He didn’t recognize her. And he knew her completely.

He shoved away from the table, the chair legs shrieking on the linoleum. He couldn’t look at it. He left the notebook open, the page exposed, and walked to the sink. Ran the water cold, splashed his face. The water dripped from his chin onto his shirt.

When he turned, the drawing was still there. It hadn’t vanished. It was just a drawing. Ink on paper. His doing.

He approached slowly, as if it were a sleeping animal. He stared at the closed eyes. Who are you?

A name surfaced, not with a bang, but with the soft certainty of a bubble reaching the air. Lilith.

He didn’t know where it came from. It just was. Lilith.

The sound of it in his mind unlocked something. A floodgate of sense-memory, not a vision, but a barrage. The smell of her shampoo—coconut and something herbal. The feel of her calloused fingertips against his palm. The sound of her humming off-key in the kitchen, a song he didn’t know. The weight of her head on his shoulder while they watched a movie, the credits scrolling, forgotten.

It was a life. A dense, detailed, ordinary life. Sunday mornings. Arguments about whose turn it was to do the dishes. The shared, wordless glance across a crowded room.

He gripped the edge of the table. His knees threatened to buckle. This wasn’t terror. This was grief. A profound, devastating sense of loss, as if someone had died. But no one had died. Because no one had existed.

The contradiction was a physical pain, a crack running down the center of his chest. He mourned a ghost who had never lived. He loved a stranger.

The scent of salt and sunscreen grew stronger, overwhelming the apartment’s stale air. For a few heartbeats, it was all he could smell. He could almost feel the sun-warmed wood of a deck under his bare feet, hear the distant cry of gulls.

Then it faded. The familiar chill seeped back in, the dry paper scent of the other presence, the one that shared his bed. It felt intrusive now. Jealous, almost.

He looked from the drawing of Lilith’s peaceful face to the dark hallway leading to his bedroom. Two different hauntings. One of cold intimacy, one of warm absence.

He carefully tore the page from the notebook. The paper made a soft, final sound. He didn’t know what to do with it. Crumpling it felt like a violence. Burying it in a drawer felt like a burial.

In the end, he propped it against the base of his reading lamp on the side table. He turned the lamp on. The warm yellow light fell over the drawing, making the ink lines seem softer, more alive.

He sat in his chair across the room and watched the piece of paper. Waiting, though he didn’t know for what. For the lines to change. For the eyes to open. For the scent of coconut to return.

Outside, a car door slammed. The ordinary world. In here, he kept vigil for a ghost of sunlight, while the ghost of cold stone watched from the shadows of the hall.

The need didn’t go away. It grew. The ballpoint sketch wasn’t enough. It was a shadow of her, a pale carbon copy. He needed color. He needed to capture the freckles, the exact hue of her hair—dark brown with strands of gold bleached by the sun.

Two days after the drawing, he found himself in an art supply store downtown. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. The smell of turpentine and paper was alien and sharp. He moved through the aisles like a sleepwalker, filling a small basket with things he didn’t know how to use: a pad of watercolor paper, a set of paints in little tubes, a few brushes with soft bristles. The cost was absurd. He paid in cash, avoiding the clerk’s eyes.

Back in his apartment, he cleared the kitchen table again. He filled a jelly jar with water. He squeezed blobs of paint onto an old china plate—burnt sienna, yellow ochre, a green he hoped might approximate sea-glass.

He stared at the blank, textured paper. The memory of her face was already softening at the edges, becoming a feeling more than a photograph. He had to hurry. He had to pin it down before it faded for good.

He wet a brush. He mixed a color for her skin. His hand, which had been so sure with the pen, shook. The first brushstroke was a clumsy wash, too dark, too orange. He cursed, a harsh sound in the quiet. He was ruining it.

He closed his eyes. Not thinking. Just feeling. The warmth of her cheek under his lips. The way the light caught the fine hairs on her forearm in the afternoon.

He opened his eyes. He didn’t paint the face. He painted the light around it. A wash of gold, fading to the soft blue of a sky just before dusk. He painted the suggestion of a shoulder, the curve of a neck. He left the face itself blank, an empty space in the center of all that warmth.

It was better. The absence was more accurate. She was a silhouette burned into his senses, a negative space where a life should be.

He sat back, exhausted. The painting was a mess. Amateurish. But the feeling was there, trapped in the runny pigments. Longing.

From the hallway, the floorboard creaked. A slow, deliberate compression of wood.

He didn’t turn. He kept his eyes on the empty space where Lilith’s face should have been.

“I know you’re there,” he said, his voice hoarse from disuse.

The silence that followed was different. It wasn’t listening. It was waiting.

“She’s not yours,” he whispered to the empty space on the paper.

In the hallway, the cold intensified. A draft curled around his ankles. The scent of old paper was suddenly overpowering, blotting out the faint chemical smell of the paint.

It felt like a reply.

He didn’t paint again. The watercolor sat on the table for a day, the paper buckling as it dried, the blank space at its center an accusation. He covered it with a dish towel. The act felt like hiding a body.

But the memory of her didn’t fade. It crystallized. The phantom senses sharpened, becoming more intrusive, more specific. He’d be washing a mug and feel the phantom slickness of sunscreen on his palms. The taste of salt would bloom on his lips when he drank tap water. He found himself staring at his own hands, expecting to see grains of sand caught in the hairs on his knuckles.

The sketch from the notebook, propped by the lamp, was a constant peripheral presence. A sleeping anchor. He started talking to it. Not full conversations—just a word, here and there.

“Morning,” he’d mutter, passing by to get coffee.

Or, “Rain today.”

It was the kind of thing you’d say to a spouse. Domestic, mundane. The horror of it was in the normalcy. The haunting had moved in, taken a chair, and was waiting for the paper to be delivered.

On the third evening, the compulsion returned. The sketch was black and white. It lacked dimension. He needed to see her in three dimensions, to map the planes of her face with something more tactile than ink. He didn’t question the urge. It was a need, as basic as thirst.

He didn’t have clay. He had leftover plumbing putty from a long-forgotten sink repair, a gray, oily compound sealed in a foil pouch in a kitchen drawer. It would have to do.

He worked at the kitchen table under the single bulb, the dish towel now a work mat. The putty was cold and resistant at first, stiff under his fingers. He kneaded it, the heat from his hands making it pliable. The chemical, petroleum smell mixed with the ever-present paper-dust chill from the hall.

He didn’t think. He let his fingers remember.

They remembered the high, smooth arc of a forehead. The subtle dip at the philtrum above the lips. The strong line of a jaw that softened just at the chin. His thumbs found the sockets for eyes, hollowing out two gentle, almond-shaped spaces. He used the rounded end of a pen to press a delicate line for a closed lid, to carve the subtle channel of the upper lip.

He worked for hours. Time dissolved into the rhythm of push and pull, of adding a tiny bit of material here, scraping away a fraction there. The world narrowed to the gray mass taking form under his stained fingers and the precise, internal blueprint he was tracing. It wasn’t creation. It was excavation. He was uncovering a shape that already existed, buried in the medium of his own muscle memory.

Sweat beaded on his temple. His back ached from hunching. He didn’t stop.

When he finally leaned back, his hands were trembling, coated in a fine gray film. On the dish towel sat a face.

It was her. Not a likeness. Not an interpretation. It was the woman from the sketch, rendered in three solemn dimensions. The quiet repose of the features, the slight, knowing tilt at the corner of the mouth that wasn’t quite a smile. It was all there. He had sculpted a stranger’s face with the unthinking accuracy of a man sculpting his own.

The fascination that had carried him through the work curdled, all at once, into a pure and simple dread.

He hadn’t imagined this. His hands had known things his mind did not. The proof was physical, sitting twelve inches from his face, smelling of mineral oil and his own sweat. This wasn’t a symptom. It was evidence. Evidence of a life lived in a body that wasn’t his, in a time that wasn’t now.

He reached out, his finger hovering an inch from the sculpted cheek. He couldn’t bring himself to touch it. The idea of feeling that familiar curve under his fingertip, in cold putty instead of warm skin, was a violation of some deep, unspoken law.

“Who are you?” The question was a dry rasp.

The apartment offered no answer. The hallway was a pool of black silence. The chill there seemed to be holding its breath.

A strange, reluctant acceptance settled over him, heavy as a lead blanket. This was his life now. Not a haunting to be solved, but a reality to be cataloged. He had a ghost of cold stone and old books who shared his bed. And he had a ghost of sunlight and salt who lived in his hands. They were both real. They were both his.

He got up, his joints protesting. He fetched a clean glass from the cupboard, filled it with water, and drank it slowly, staring at the sculpture from across the room. It looked smaller from a distance. More fragile.

He should destroy it. That’s what a sane person would do. Burn the sketch, smash the putty face back into a formless lump, purge the foreign memory from his environment.

He didn’t move.

Destroying it would be a murder. A second death, for a life that had never been. He couldn’t do it. The grief would be too large. It would fill the apartment and drown him.

So he would keep it. He would live with it. This was the compromise, the reluctant acceptance. He would share his space with these two silent tenants, these echoes of lives that pressed against his own from opposite sides of an impossible barrier.

He washed his hands at the sink, the gray paste swirling down the drain. The water was hot, but he couldn’t get the feeling of the putty off his skin. It was in the creases of his knuckles, under his nails.

He dried his hands on a towel. The silence in the apartment was absolute. No traffic outside, no hum from the refrigerator. It was the dead hour, deep in the night.

He was about to turn, to face the sculpture again, to decide what to do with it, when he heard it.

A faint, melodic thread of sound. A woman’s voice, humming.

It was coming from nowhere and everywhere. Not from the hallway. Not from the sketch. It seemed to emanate from the air itself, a vibration in the stillness. The tune was unfamiliar, a meandering, wistful series of notes that rose and fell without a clear rhythm. It was off-key in one spot, just slightly flat.

He knew that hum. He knew the way it caught on that particular note. He’d heard it in a sun-drenched kitchen, accompanied by the clatter of dishes. He’d felt the vibration of it against his shoulder where her head rested.

It was Lilith’s hum. The one she did when she was thinking, or content, or just existing in a moment.

His breath locked in his chest. The certainty was absolute, a cold spike driven straight through his heart. The sound belonged to the face on the table. It was her voice, bleeding through from a beach that didn’t exist, in a time that had never happened.

He didn’t move. He didn’t dare breathe. He just stood at the sink, his damp hands clenched on the edge of the counter, and listened to the ghost of a woman he loved humming in the dark of his apartment.

The melody looped once, softly, then faded into the returning silence, leaving only the echo of its shape in his mind, and the deeper, more profound silence of the cold watching from the hall.

The hum didn’t come back. He waited at the sink until his knuckles ached from gripping the counter, until the silence re-established itself as a solid thing, thicker than before. The chill from the hallway remained, a watchful presence. He turned slowly. The sculpture sat on the dish towel, a gray moon in the lamplight. The sketch by the reading lamp seemed to glow.

He couldn’t leave her like that. Propped against a lamp base. A scrap. She deserved a frame.

The thought was absurd, and he clung to its absurdity. It was a concrete task. A problem with steps. It was a dam against the ocean of grief threatening to break behind his ribs. He found a tape measure in a junk drawer. He noted the dimensions of the sketch. He wrote them on the back of an envelope. The numbers were a lifeline.

The next day, he went to a discount store on the outskirts of the city. The fluorescent lights were a different frequency than the art store’s, harsher, buzzing with a headache-inducing pitch. He walked past aisles of plastic bins and cheap cookware. The frame section was at the back, a wire rack of mass-produced options. Gold, black, fake wood grain. He ran his fingers over a simple black wooden frame. It was cool and smooth. He checked the size. It would fit, with a little room to spare. He didn’t consider the other options. Black felt right. Solemn. Final.

At home, he cleared the kitchen table once more. He laid the frame face down, pried up the little metal tabs with a butter knife. He removed the cardboard backing and the sheet of thin, cloudy plastic that came with it. He placed his sketch in the center. The drawing looked smaller now, isolated in the white matting that wasn’t really a mat, just the cardboard showing around the edges. He hesitated. Should he center it? It was already centered. His hands were shaking. He fitted the backing in, pressed the tabs back down with more force than necessary. The metal squealed in protest.

He turned the frame over.

Lilith, behind glass.

She was sealed in now. Contained. A specimen. The glass created a distance, a barrier he could feel. He could no longer imagine the texture of the paper, the slight raise of the ink lines. She was untouchable. That, he realized, was the point. He couldn’t reach her. He never could.

He carried the frame to the living room. He didn’t have much art on the walls. A generic print of a boat in a harbor, left by a previous tenant. He took it down, the nail left exposed in the plaster. He hung the black frame in its place. It was too high. He adjusted it, his fingers leaving smudges on the glass. He stepped back.

There. On the wall. A part of the apartment’s landscape.

The grief hit him then, not as a wave, but as a settling. A quiet, total collapse of something internal. It wasn’t the sharp pain of loss. It was the hollow, endless ache of aftermath. He sank into his armchair, his eyes fixed on the framed sketch. He had given a ghost a room in his home. He had made space for her. He wasn’t fighting it anymore.

These weren’t hauntings. They were obituaries.

Every phantom sense, every bleed-through memory, was a life he had lived somewhere else. A life that had been real, with its own weight and texture and smell, and was now gone. Not just gone—erased, overwritten, canceled by the fact of his existence here, in this chair, in this wrong and silent apartment. He wasn’t being haunted. He was the cemetery. He was the ground where other versions of himself were buried.

The cold presence in the hallway was another grave. A different life, one of stone and silence and old paper. That version of him had loved, or needed, or been bound to that cold thing. And it had died with him. Now it lingered here, a fossil in the wrong stratum.

He thought of the marble in his coat pocket, the blue ribbon swirl. An artifact that didn’t belong anywhere else. That was him. An artifact. A leftover.

Tears didn’t come. His eyes were dry and hot. The grief was too large for tears. It was a geological fact. A mountain range of loss inside him, range after range of lives he could remember but had never lived. He mourned for Lilith, for the feel of her skin in the sun. He mourned for the cold presence, for whatever bleak companionship it represented. He mourned for a hundred other whispers he hadn’t even identified yet, ghosts waiting in the wings of his consciousness.

He would never be believed. That was the final, absolute truth. He could show them the sketch, the sculpture, he could describe the taste of the sea salt and the smell of the old books until he was blue in the face. They would see a talented, troubled man. They would see delusion given form. The proof was only proof to him. The validation had to come from inside this sealed, silent chamber of his own skull.

It wasn’t solid. It wasn’t immutable. It was a broadcast. And his receiver was the only one picking up the signal drift.
