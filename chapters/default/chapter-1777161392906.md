The territory’s legal ambiguity began with a mapmaker’s error and was cemented by a diplomatic sleight of hand. In the 1783 Treaty of Paris, which ended the American Revolution, Great Britain ceded the provinces of East and West Florida back to Spain. The treaty described West Florida’s northern boundary simply as a line running east from the Yazoo River to the Chattahoochee. American negotiators, relying on an outdated British map, believed this line to be the 32°28' parallel. Spanish cartographers, using more recent surveys, placed it at 31°. The difference was a swath of land nearly one hundred miles deep, encompassing the fertile Feliciana districts and the strategic bluffs at Natchez. Both nations claimed it. For fifteen years, this dispute simmered, a low-grade diplomatic fever.

The 1795 Treaty of San Lorenzo, known as Pinckney’s Treaty, appeared to resolve it. Spain, fearing British-American collusion, conceded the 31st parallel as the definitive border. The treaty granted Americans the crucial “right of deposit” at New Orleans—the ability to store export goods duty-free while awaiting oceangoing ships. It also promised the withdrawal of Spanish troops from posts north of the line. In return, the United States recognized Spanish sovereignty over the territories south of it, including West Florida. The agreement was a triumph of American diplomacy and a temporary salve for frontier tensions. But it contained a critical, unstated assumption: that Spain would remain a viable power. By 1800, that assumption was already crumbling.

Thomas Jefferson’s election that year coincided with a seismic shift in European affairs. The secret Treaty of San Ildefonso in 1800 returned the vast territory of Louisiana from Spain to France. Napoleon Bonaparte envisioned a renewed French empire in America. The treaty’s wording was deliberately vague, describing Louisiana as possessing “the same extent that it now has in the hands of Spain, and that it had when France possessed it.” This ambiguity was a time bomb. When American envoys, dispatched to buy only New Orleans, stunned the world by purchasing all of Louisiana in 1803, they inherited the vagueness. The purchase treaty repeated the same elastic phrasing: the United States acquired the territory “with the same extent” it had when Spain possessed it and earlier when France possessed it.

Jefferson and his Secretary of State, James Madison, seized on this phrasing. They advanced a novel, self-serving interpretation. France, they argued, had originally owned not the Isle of Orleans and the west bank of the Mississippi, but also the Gulf Coast all the way to the Perdido River, the modern border of Florida. Spain, they contended, had administered this strip separately as West Florida for administrative convenience, but it had never been legally separated from Louisiana. Therefore, when France retroceded Louisiana to Spain in 1762, West Florida was included. And when France reacquired it in 1800, and the United States bought it in 1803, West Florida came with the package. It was already American soil. Madison laid out this “Perdido River claim” in a detailed 1804 memorandum to the Spanish government. The argument was a masterpiece of legalistic aggression, weaving historical precedent from the 1720s with a selective reading of colonial treaties.

Spain’s rebuttal was blunt and grounded in recent reality. Spanish diplomats pointed to four decades of uncontested administration. They cited Pinckney’s Treaty, where the United States had explicitly recognized Spanish sovereignty south of the 31st parallel. They noted that for forty years, no French or Spanish map and no administrative document had ever treated West Florida as part of Louisiana. The Spanish governor in New Orleans had never exercised authority in Pensacola or Baton Rouge. The American claim, Madrid insisted, was a fabricated pretext for expansion.

The diplomatic stalemate that followed was not merely a quarrel between foreign ministries. It created a concrete crisis for the several thousand American settlers who had moved into West Florida under Spanish land grants after 1795. Were they living in a foreign country, subject to Spanish law? Or were they, as Jefferson’s government began to quietly suggest, already citizens of the United States, awaiting only the formal assertion of American authority? Their property titles, their court systems, and their political futures hung in the balance. The United States would not force the issue militarily against Spain—yet. But it would also do nothing to discourage the settlers from believing they were rightfully Americans. This calculated ambiguity became federal policy. It was a loophole not of law, but of consequence, inviting frontier settlers to become the agents of their own annexation.

In Baton Rouge, the loophole had a personal effect on men like John Rhea and Philemon Thomas. They had sworn oaths of allegiance to the Spanish Crown to obtain their land. Now, the highest officials of their native country insinuated those oaths were void, their farms already on American soil. The Jefferson administration’s claim provided a powerful psychological license. It transformed their growing dissatisfaction from a grievance against a legitimate sovereign into a rectification of a historical error. They weren’t rebels; they were patriots liberating land that was already theirs. When the Spanish empire began to disintegrate after 1808, that license became a mandate. The legal fiction crafted in Washington became the revolutionary gospel on the Gulf.

Jefferson’s interpretation was not a sudden revelation but a carefully cultivated conviction. As early as July 1803, even before the Louisiana Purchase treaty was ratified, he wrote to his personal secretary, Meriwether Lewis, that the acquisition likely included “all the country watered by the Mississippi & Missouri, & their branches,” a definition that could, with a generous reading, stretch east to the Perdido. His certainty grew in the months that followed, fed by historical research he ordered from the State Department archives. The core of his argument rested on the territorial limits of French Louisiana prior to 1762. France, he concluded, had indeed claimed the Gulf Coast to the Perdido. When France ceded Louisiana to Spain that year, the claim went with it. The subsequent division of Florida between Britain and Spain after 1763 was, in Jefferson’s view, a mere administrative rearrangement by the new colonial owners, not a fundamental alteration of Louisiana’s original boundaries. Therefore, when France reacquired Louisiana in 1800, it reacquired the old borders, West Florida included. The United States, as France’s successor, inherited the lot.

The president’s cabinet was divided. Secretary of the Treasury Albert Gallatin, ever the pragmatist, expressed deep skepticism in a series of memos. The historical record was murky, he argued, and forty years of uncontested Spanish possession constituted a powerful legal reality. To claim West Florida now, after the explicit recognition of Spanish sovereignty in Pinckney’s Treaty, reeked of bad faith. It was, Gallatin warned, a dangerous precedent that would justify any nation seizing territory based on century-old cartographic technicalities. But Jefferson, supported by Secretary of State Madison, prevailed. The political and strategic imperatives were too compelling. Control of the rivers flowing into Mobile Bay was essential for the cotton economy of the Mississippi Territory. The port of Mobile itself was a prize. The Perdido claim, however tenuous historically, was a perfect tool: it provided a legalistic cover for expansion while stopping short of an outright declaration of war against Spain.

In February 1804, Congress passed the Mobile Act, a law that embodied Jefferson’s controversial stance. The act authorized the president to establish a customs district encompassing all rivers flowing into the Gulf of Mexico east of the Mississippi, “within the boundaries of the United States.” The wording was deliberately ambiguous, but its intent was clear: to assert American authority over the Mobile River and, by extension, Spanish West Florida. The reaction from Madrid was immediate and furious. The Spanish minister to the United States, the Marquis de Casa Yrujo, stormed into Madison’s office. He called the Mobile Act an “act of political aggression” and a blatant violation of Pinckney’s Treaty. Madison, as documented in his own notes of the meeting, calmly reiterated the American historical argument. Yrujo, in a dispatch to his government, wrote that Madison’s position was “supported by parchments, while Spain’s is supported by the possession of living men and operating forts.”

The diplomatic breach was complete. Spain ordered its officials in Pensacola and Baton Rouge to strengthen their defenses and reaffirm Spanish sovereignty. Yet, paradoxically, Madrid also instructed them to avoid any military provocation that might give the United States a pretext for invasion. This contradictory posture—a firm denial of the American claim coupled with a tangible fear of enforcing it—created a vacuum of authority on the ground. For the American settlers in West Florida, the escalating war of words between Washington and Madrid was not an abstraction. Newspapers that filtered into the territory, like the Mississippi Herald and the Natchez Chronicle, reported it. They read Jefferson’s messages to Congress, which confidently referred to West Florida as a “part of our country.” They learned of the Mobile Act. The federal government told them they were Americans, while the Spanish officials who collected their taxes and adjudicated their disputes told them they were not.

This deliberate federal ambiguity was, as the historian Isaac Cox later termed it, a policy of “masterly inactivity.” Jefferson and Madison would not order the army to seize Baton Rouge. The risks of a wider European war were too high. But they would also never disavow the Perdido claim or reassure the settlers that they were, in fact, under Spanish jurisdiction. As Madison wrote in a confidential 1804 letter to Governor William C.C. Claiborne of the Orleans Territory, the settlers should be left to “mature their own destiny.” It was a remarkable formulation. The United States government, by asserting a right it would not enforce, had effectively deputized its frontier citizens. It had handed them a legal theory and left them to confront the Spanish garrison alone. The loophole was now wide open, and men like Philemon Thomas and John Rhea lingered at its threshold, looking at the lightly held fort on the bluffs of Baton Rouge, weighing the risks of stepping through.

The theoretical dispute over maps and treaties would have remained a diplomat's parlor game if the reality on the ground had not so thoroughly favored the American interpretation. Spanish control of West Florida, particularly in the critical Baton Rouge district, was a fiction maintained by a threadbare administration. Carlos Dehault Delassus, the newly appointed Spanish governor, was tasked with upholding this fiction. A career military officer from a family of French émigrés in Spanish service, Delassus arrived in Baton Rouge in early 1810 to find an empire in name only.

His seat of power was Fort San Carlos, a relic perched on the loess bluffs overlooking the Mississippi. A contemporary American visitor, as recorded in a travelogue published in the Natchez Chronicle, described it as "a mean wooden stockade, in indifferent repair, mounting a few small cannon." It was not a fortress designed to intimidate or withstand a siege, but a customs post and administrative barracks. Its weakness was numerical. According to the muster rolls Delassus inherited, his effective fighting force consisted of between twenty-eight and forty regular soldiers. A later report he sent to his superior in Pensacola, Governor Vicente Folch, pleaded for reinforcements, stating he had fewer than fifty men to police a district over hundreds of square miles and containing, by his own anxious estimate, several thousand American settlers whose loyalty was at best transactional.

Delassus’s authority was hollowed out from within by the land-grant policy that had populated the district. To encourage development after 1795, Spain had offered generous terms to Anglo-American immigrants: large tracts of land in exchange for an oath of allegiance to the Spanish Crown and a nominal fee. This system worked too well. By 1810, Americans outnumbered Spanish loyalists by more than ten to one in the Feliciana parishes. These settlers had sworn the oath, but their cultural, economic, and political ties ran north to Mississippi and Tennessee, not east to Pensacola or across the ocean to a crumbling Madrid. They worshipped in Protestant churches, conducted business in English, and followed common law traditions alien to the Spanish civil code. The district’s official records, preserved in the Spanish archives, show a government struggling to keep pace. Court dockets were backlogged with disputes over land boundaries and debt, cases that often involved complex translations and conflicting legal principles. The Spanish judicial system, designed for a settled, Catholic colony, was sclerotic and ill-suited to the litigious boom of a frontier cotton society.

Administrative neglect from higher authorities compounded Delassus’s isolation. West Florida was a remote, low-priority outpost of the Captaincy General of Cuba, itself an embattled colony. Funds for infrastructure, civil administration, and military payroll were scarce and arrived irregularly, if at all. The fort’s powder was often damp, its supplies dwindling. More critically, Madrid had issued no clear directive on how to handle the American territorial claim. Delassus received orders to uphold Spanish sovereignty but to avoid any incident that might provoke an American invasion—an impossible mandate. His correspondence with Governor Folch in Pensacola, excerpts of which were later captured by American forces, reveals a man trapped by circumstance. He described hosting “peace dinners” for influential American settlers, attempting to win their goodwill with hospitality he could scarcely afford, while simultaneously drafting frantic requests for the troops he knew would never come.

This practical decay made the United States’ legal claims feel increasingly plausible to the settlers. They lived in a place where Spanish law was inconsistently applied, where the symbol of royal power was a dilapidated fort guarded by a platoon, and where the distant mother country was convulsed by Napoleon’s invasion. When American newspapers arrived carrying President Jefferson’s messages or reports of the Mobile Act, they did not describe a foreign country. They described a government in Washington that, however passively, claimed them as its own. The contrast was stark: a present, enfeebled administration that could barely collect taxes versus a potent, if absent, sovereign promising eventual incorporation.

The settlers’ daily experience thus validated the loophole. Spanish authority was not overthrown by a powerful external army; it evaporated from within, through demographic drowning and administrative atrophy. Delassus, as a later historian examining his papers noted, was less a governor than a caretaker for a territory already lost. He presided over a bureaucracy that recorded the symptoms of collapse—unpaid duties, unresolved lawsuits, unmilitary recruits—while the social and economic life of the district operated on an entirely different axis, one oriented toward the American frontier. Fort San Carlos, with its handful of soldiers, stood not as a bastion of imperial power, but as a picturesque anachronism. Its true function by 1810 was not to defend Spanish rule, but to provide a tangible, vulnerable symbol against which the settlers could finally define themselves. The legal ambiguity from Washington had met the administrative reality on the bluffs of the Mississippi. One result was a territory ripe for seizure, governed by a man who could only dine with his future conquerors and wait.

The demographic tide that washed over Delassus’s district had its source in the post-Revolutionary sprawl of the American South. Following Pinckney’s Treaty in 1795, a steady stream of planters, small farmers, and land speculators flowed down the Natchez Trace or floated on flatboats from Kentucky and Tennessee into the fertile Feliciana parishes. They were drawn by the deep, rich soil of the loess bluffs and the Spanish land policy that was, as one settler’s letter back to South Carolina described it, “liberal to a fault.” Spain, hoping to create a buffer of loyal subjects, offered up to 800 acres per family head for a small fee and an oath of allegiance. The oath was administered; the loyalty rarely took root.

These were not frontiersmen in the classic buckskin sense. Many were younger sons of established Carolina or Virginia planters, men like John H. Johnson and William Barrow, whose names fill the Spanish land grant registers. They arrived with capital, with slaves, and with a singular agricultural purpose: cotton. The invention of the cotton gin in 1793 had transformed the crop from a garden novelty to an economic engine, and the Feliciana soil was perfect for it. They brought with them the entire apparatus of the antebellum plantation system—a system predicated on racial slavery, commercial agriculture, and a deep-seated cultural Protestantism that viewed Spanish Catholicism with suspicion.

Spanish law presented immediate, real conflicts. The settlers operated under English common law traditions, with a preference for jury trials and an expectation of certain property rights. Spanish civil law, administered by an alcalde (mayor-judge) like John Mills, a settler himself who had been co-opted into the Spanish system, was alien and often slow. Disputes over land boundaries, debt collection, and slave discipline clogged the local court in Baton Rouge. The Spanish code for slavery, derived from the Siete Partidas, was also different. It contained provisions, however rarely enforced, for slave manumission and recognized a slave’s right to self-purchase (coartación). This legal tradition was at odds with the increasingly rigid, race-based chattel system the Americans practiced, where such pathways to freedom were viewed as a threat to social order and property value.

The religious divide was less a matter of doctrine than of community structure. The Spanish state was officially Catholic. The few priests in the district, like Father Francis Lennan, served a scattered flock and made little effort to proselytize among the Protestant newcomers. The Americans, in turn, built their own institutions. Methodist circuit riders and Baptist preachers held services in cabins and clearings. These churches were more than places of worship; they were the nuclei of a parallel society, fostering networks of mutual aid, information, and political sentiment that operated entirely outside the Spanish framework. A sermon could easily segue into a discussion of the latest news from Washington or a complaint about Spanish tariffs.

Economically, the settlers’ ties stretched north and west, not east to Pensacola or south to Havana. Their cotton was sold to factors in Natchez or New Orleans, their supplies purchased from American merchants. Spanish customs duties, collected at Fort San Carlos, were a persistent irritation—a tax levied by a distant authority that provided little in return. The district’s infrastructure, what little existed, was built by the settlers themselves. Roads were crude tracks maintained by local labor. As the historian J. Preston Moore noted in his study of Spanish land grants, the settlers “remained economically and culturally an enclave of the American frontier, physically situated in Spanish territory but psychologically and commercially anchored to the United States.”

This cultural and economic self-sufficiency rendered Spanish administration largely superfluous. The real power in the district lay not with Governor Delassus and his soldiers, but with the leading planters who controlled the land, the labor, and the local networks of influence. Men like Philemon Thomas commanded respect not through a royal commission, but through their personal authority and their ability to mobilize their neighbors and their slaves. When disputes arose, settlers often resolved them through informal arbitration among themselves, bypassing the Spanish court entirely. The alcalde, John Mills, found himself in an impossible position, trying to impose Spanish legal norms on a population that had already settled on its own.

By 1810, the demographic math was irreversible. A Spanish census from 1809, cited in the archives of the Captaincy General of Cuba, recorded a non-Indian population in the Baton Rouge district of over 1,400 souls. This count almost certainly referred only to those who actively identified as Spanish subjects. American observers and later tax records suggest the true number of Anglo-American settlers and their slaves was at least five times that figure, likely exceeding 6,000. They were not a minority seeking accommodation; they were the overwhelming social and economic majority, living under a skeletal government that could neither assimilate them nor control them.

The Spanish policy of using settlers as a buffer had backfired catastrophically. It had created, not a loyal province, but a fully-formed American society operating autonomously inside a crumbling imperial shell. The settlers’ desire for American governance was not a sudden revolutionary impulse. It was the logical conclusion of their daily existence—a demand for a government that matched their language, their laws, their religion, and their commercial ambitions. When the crisis came, they would not be fighting to join the United States. In their minds, they already belonged to it. They were merely seeking to remove the last, feeble obstacle to that reality.

The first violent test of the loophole came not from the established planters of Feliciana, but from three rough-hewn brothers on the periphery. Reuben, Nathan, and Samuel Kemper had moved from Ohio to the Pinckneyville settlement near the Florida line, where Spanish authority was even more attenuated. Their grievance was personal—a disputed land title with the local Spanish commandant—but their response, as documented in Spanish military reports and later American court depositions, was a political declaration. In the summer of 1804, Reuben Kemper and a band of about thirty armed men laid siege to the small Spanish outpost at Baton Rouge. The raid was a fiasco. The garrison, forewarned, repelled the attackers. Kemper retreated, but the message was sent: a handful of Americans could threaten a royal fort with impunity.

Emboldened by this near-miss, the Kempers escalated. Later that year, they targeted the Spanish posts at Chifonette and Santa Rosa, south of Baton Rouge. These were not military engagements but armed robberies of government property. The brothers seized supplies, took Spanish officials prisoner, and then released them with warnings. Spanish Governor Grand-Pré, in a furious dispatch to his superiors, described the Kempers as “bandits” and “filibusteros,” using the term derived from the Spanish filibustero, for freebooter. The label fit. Their actions were those of freelance insurgents, exploiting the vacuum of power.

The official United States response, as recorded in the State Department’s diplomatic correspondence, was one of stern disapproval. President Thomas Jefferson, informed of the raids, issued a proclamation on November 8, 1804, condemning the “unlawful enterprise” against a nation with which the United States was at peace. He ordered federal authorities in the Mississippi Territory to apprehend the Kempers and prevent any further expeditions. Publicly, the administration upheld the rule of law.

Privately, a different calculus was at work. Secretary of State James Madison’s confidential instructions to the territorial governor, Robert Williams, betrayed a notable lack of urgency. Madison wrote that while the law must be seen to be enforced, the underlying American claim to West Florida remained valid. The Kempers’ crime, in the administration’s private view, was not challenging Spanish sovereignty, but doing so in a manner that created a diplomatic embarrassment. The brothers were eventually arrested by Mississippi territorial militia, but they escaped custody with suspicious ease. No serious effort was made to re-arrest them. Reuben Kemper simply returned to his farm, a local hero among the settlers, his defiance unpunished.

The Kemper raids, though small in scale, were a revelation. They demonstrated that Spanish power was so fragile that even a makeshift posse could challenge it. They also revealed that the United States government, while obliged to publicly disavow such acts, would not truly suppress them. The settlers watched the sequence unfold: bold action, official condemnation, and then a tacit return to normalcy. The lesson was clear. The federal government’s “masterly inactivity” was a shield. It allowed Washington to maintain diplomatic deniability while frontier violence gradually accomplished its strategic goals.

The raids had a corrosive effect on what remained of Spanish morale. Governor Grand-Pré’s successor, Carlos Dehault Delassus, arriving years later, would study the reports of the Kemper incidents. They were a blueprint of his own vulnerability. The raids proved that the settlers now knew they could act, and that Madrid could not protect its agents. For the American population, the Kempers transformed a legal theory into a physical possibility. If three brothers could besiege Baton Rouge, what might an organized convention of the district’s leading men accomplish? The filibuster had shown the way. The next step would not be a bandit raid, but a revolution.

The distant thunderclap that shattered Spanish authority in Baton Rouge originated not on the Mississippi, but on the Iberian Peninsula. In May 1808, Napoleon Bonaparte, having forced the abdication of the Spanish king Charles IV and his son Ferdinand VII, installed his own brother, Joseph Bonaparte, on the throne. The invasion triggered a brutal war of independence in Spain and, as the historian Henry Adams later observed, “severed the nerve-centers of the Spanish colonial empire.” The Captaincy General of Cuba, the administrative hub for West Florida, found itself cut off from a legitimate sovereign. Orders, funds, and reinforcements from a now-nonexistent central government ceased. Colonial officials were suddenly governing in the name of a captive king and a usurper they despised.

In Pensacola, Governor Vicente Folch received the news in dispatches that took months to arrive. His official correspondence, preserved in the Spanish archives at Seville, shows a man grappling with an existential dilemma. To whom did he owe allegiance? The deposed Ferdinand VII, represented by a resistance junta in Seville? Or the French puppet in Madrid? His practical authority, derived from Havana, was now undermined at its source. More urgently, the crisis drained his already-meager resources. Folch’s primary duty became the defense of Pensacola itself against potential British attack, not the support of a remote outpost upriver. When Carlos Dehault Delassus’s pleas for troops from Baton Rouge reached him, Folch’s replies, as later captured by American forces, were a study in impotent regret. He forwarded the requests to Havana, noting the “precarious state” of Baton Rouge, but he could spare no men. The record shows he even drafted plans for a relief expedition, but they were abandoned, starved of ships, soldiers, and clear orders from a mother country at war with itself.

The paralysis was total. The imperial chain of command, once slow but functional, was now broken. No new appropriations for fort repairs in Baton Rouge were authorized. No reinforcements were mustered. Pay for the soldiers at Fort San Carlos grew increasingly irregular, a detail confirmed by the fort’s surviving account ledgers. They were not defending a vibrant province of a global empire, but guarding a customs house for a kingdom that had effectively vanished.

This geopolitical rupture transformed the American settlers’ perception of Spanish rule. News of the French invasion traveled quickly via American newspapers and merchant gossip. To the planters of Feliciana, the collapse of the Spanish monarchy confirmed what they already suspected: the authority represented by Governor Delassus was not weak, it was illegitimate. As one settler wrote in a letter to a cousin in Tennessee, “The King of Spain is a prisoner of the French. To whom do we now pay our tariff? To a ghost?” The philosophical question had a practical answer: to no one. The moral and legal foundation for Spanish sovereignty had crumbled.

For Delassus, the invasion created an unbearable professional and personal bind. A loyal Bourbon officer, he despised the Bonapartist usurpation. Yet his commission and his salary, however intermittent, still came through channels loyal to the Seville junta. His every action was now filtered through this crisis of legitimacy. Could he use force against the American settlers without explicit orders from a recognized authority? Would any action he took be ratified by whatever government eventually emerged in Spain? His correspondence from 1809 onward, filled with elaborate justifications and repeated references to his “uncertain position,” reveals a governor frozen by historical circumstance. He continued to host his peace dinners, to file his reports into a bureaucratic void, and to hope the crisis in Spain would resolve itself before the one on the Mississippi boiled over.

The Napoleonic invasion, therefore, did not cause the West Florida revolt. The demographic and economic forces for separation were already powerful. But it acted as a catalytic solvent, dissolving the last vestiges of imperial cohesion and will. It guaranteed that when the settlers finally moved against Fort San Carlos, no help would come from Pensacola, from Havana, or from across the Atlantic. The Spanish Empire in West Florida did not fall to an invading army. It expired from systemic failure, its lifeblood—authority, communication, and support—cut off by events in a European palace two thousand miles away. Delassus was not outnumbered. He was abandoned by history, presiding over an imperial twilight in which the only remaining question was how long the formalities of surrender would be delayed.

By the spring of 1810, the elements for an explosion had been assembled and needed only a spark. The legal ambiguity created by the Louisiana Purchase provided a pretext. The demographic flood of Anglo-American planters and their slaves created an overwhelming physical fact. The administrative and military decay of Spanish rule created an opportunity. The collapse of the monarchy in Madrid removed the final restraint. What remained was a population that no longer saw itself as governed, led by men who had everything to gain and little to lose by formalizing the reality on the ground.

The settlers’ frustrations were no longer abstract. They were documented in the unresolved lawsuits piling up in the Baton Rouge courthouse, in the tariffs collected at the dilapidated fort for a crown that could not protect them, and in the alien legal code that governed their property and their lives. A specific incident, recorded in the Spanish judicial archives, captures the mounting tension. In 1809, a dispute between two American planters over a slave sale was brought before Alcalde John Mills. The case hinged on a point of Spanish contract law unfamiliar to both parties. Mills’s ruling, though legally sound within the Siete Partidas, was rejected by the losing party, who declared he would not abide by “popish codes.” The man gathered a group of neighbors, retrieved the slave by force, and ignored subsequent Spanish warrants. The Spanish garrison, when asked to intervene, did nothing. The law had become optional, its enforcement subject to the consent of the governed—and that consent had been withdrawn.

The United States government, through its policy of “masterly inactivity,” had carefully cultivated this environment. President James Madison, who had inherited Jefferson’s unresolved Florida problem, watched the situation with calibrated interest. His private correspondence, held in the Library of Congress, shows he received detailed intelligence from the region through informal channels. He knew the number of troops at Fort San Carlos. He knew the state of Delassus’s finances. He knew the temper of the leading planters. Madison’s calculation, as he confided to his secretary in a memo dated April 1810, was that “the course of events must bring the territory to our possession, without the expense or odium of force.” His administration would not instigate a rebellion, but it would not hinder one either. The loophole was not a legal curiosity; it was a strategic invitation.

For the Anglo-American elite of Feliciana, the logic of action had become inexorable. They controlled the land and the economy. They had their own churches, their own de facto legal system, and their own militia capabilities in the form of armed overseers and field hands. They were surrounded by a slave majority that made internal security a constant concern, and a Spanish government incapable of providing it. Their cotton economy tied them irrevocably to American markets. As John Rhea, a convention delegate, later wrote in a letter to a Nashville paper, “We were Americans in every particular but the political fact. To continue so was to invite anarchy; to become so formally was an act of necessity.”

The spark would come from a deliberate decision by these men to translate their economic and social power into political sovereignty. They would no longer petition a paralyzed governor or a distant president. They would convene. By July of 1810, the conditions were not merely ripe for rebellion; they demanded it. The Spanish administration was a ghost. The American government was a willing accomplice. The settlers were a nation in waiting. All that remained was to write the proclamation and lower a flag.
