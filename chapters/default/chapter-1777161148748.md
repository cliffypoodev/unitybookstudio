The flag was not raised in triumph. In the official record, its creation appears as an administrative line item, a notation of expense for “blue bunting” and “white cambric” in the account book of the provisional treasury. Yet its design—a single white star on a field of blue—was a piece of political communication as deliberate as the declaration of independence. In the weeks following the capture of Baton Rouge, the Supreme Council of the nascent republic faced a problem of perception. They governed from a captured Spanish fort. Their president, Fulwar Skipwith, had not yet arrived from his plantation. Their authority, while real, was provisional, resting on the uneasy alliance of armed planters and the silent acquiescence of a diverse populace. They needed a symbol to replace the Spanish standard that had been hauled down, something to fly from the empty flagpole at Fort San Carlos that signaled a new and singular sovereignty.

The choice of the lone star was neither an accident nor a sudden inspiration. As historian Andrew McMichael has traced, the symbol had deep roots in the iconography of American frontier separatism. It had flown over the short-lived Republic of Vermont in the 1770s. It had been the emblem of the Trans-Oconee Republic, a failed settler state carved from Creek territory in the 1790s. For the Anglo-American planters of West Florida, many of whom had migrated from Kentucky and Tennessee, the lone star was a familiar shorthand for self-assertion beyond the reach of established government. It was a claim staked in cloth.

The convention’s minutes, preserved in the Louisiana State Archives, show no debate over the design. The decision appears as a matter of settled business. This suggests the symbol was already a consensus image, its meaning and utility understood. Its simplicity served practical needs. In a territory with few professional flag-makers, a local seamstress could easily reproduce it from available materials: indigo-dyed cotton or wool for the field, white cloth cut and stitched for the five-pointed star. It was clear and legible at a distance, whether flying over the courthouse or carried by a mounted courier to the scattered settlements along the Pearl River.

But its greatest utility was its ambiguity. The lone star was a perfect vessel for the republic’s central contradiction. For men like John Rhea, who had drafted a constitution with painstaking care, the star could represent the new republic itself—a singular beacon of republican virtue in the Gulf. For the majority of the convention, who saw independence as a temporary step toward annexation, the same star could prefigure the one that would soon be added to the American constellation. It was a symbol of defiant independence and eager union, its meaning shifting with the perspective of the viewer. To Spanish authorities in Pensacola and Havana, it was a calculated insult, a formal rejection of royal authority. To officials in Washington, watching with keen interest, it was a signal of ideological affinity and a plea for incorporation.

The identity of the flag’s maker is lost. No contemporary diary or letter records her name. She was almost certainly a woman of the district, likely from Baton Rouge or the nearby Feliciana plantations, skilled with a needle. Her work was not considered noteworthy enough to document. She produced a tool of state, not an artifact of art. The flag she stitched was a functional piece of a political argument, meant to be seen, not memorialized.

Fulwar Skipwith received word of his election at his plantation, Monte Vista, in the Feliciana district. He was fifty years old, a man whose life had already traced an improbable arc from Virginia planter to American diplomat to Louisiana exile. The record of his initial reaction is sparse. He was not a frontiersman in the mold of Philemon Thomas. He was a diplomat, a man of letters and law, who had served as U.S. Consul General in Paris under three presidents. He had lost a personal fortune in the collapse of French investments during the Napoleonic wars. His relocation to West Florida was, in part, an attempt to recover his standing and wealth through land and cotton. The rebellion offered a different kind of restoration.

He arrived in Baton Rouge in early November, after a journey of several days. The government he was chosen to lead occupied a tense middle ground. The Supreme Council, dominated by men like John Rhea, had drafted a constitution and elected a senate. They controlled the fort and the town, but their military reach extended only as far as the patrols of armed planters would ride. Spanish power was broken locally, but Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola was known to be gathering forces. To the west, across the Mississippi in the Orleans Territory, American officials watched and waited. Skipwith’s first task, as documented in the council minutes, was to deliver an inaugural address.

He spoke not from a balcony to a cheering crowd, but within the council chamber to the assembled delegates. His words, preserved in the Times-Picayune’s later historical retrospectives, were a masterclass in calculated ambiguity. He accepted the office of chief magistrate, pledging to “defend the independence and constitution of West Florida.” This was a clear endorsement of the republic’s sovereignty. In the next breath, he expressed his “ardent desire” for the territory’s annexation to the United States. He saw no contradiction. For Skipwith, the republic was a necessary vessel—a sovereign entity that could negotiate the terms of its own marriage, not be seized as a prize of war.

His presidency was an exercise in this dual reality. He signed commissions for officers of the republic’s modest army. He corresponded with officials in Washington as a head of state. At the same time, he and the council were engaged in a continuous, confidential dialogue with President James Madison and Secretary of State Robert Smith, pleading for annexation. In a letter dated November 14, 1810, the council wrote to Madison, stating the people of West Florida were “as one man” in their wish to become part of the Union. They asked not if, but how.

The answer arrived not as a diplomatic negotiation, but as a fait accompli. On December 7, William C.C. Claiborne, governor of the Orleans Territory, crossed the border with a detachment of U.S. troops under orders from Madison’s October proclamation. Claiborne’s mission was to take possession, not to parley. He treated the republic not as a government, but as a successful insurrection that had conveniently cleared the territory for American absorption.

Skipwith’s reaction was one of principled outrage. He protested not the annexation itself, which he desired, but the method. In a fiery dispatch to Claiborne, he denounced the move as an “outrage against the Lone Star Flag and the constitution of West Florida.” He saw the unilateral American occupation as a betrayal of republican principle. He had envisioned a process of proposal and consent, where the republic’s government would formally agree to terms, preserving a shred of dignity and legal continuity. Claiborne’s soldiers, by simply marching in, rendered the republic’s careful construction of sovereignty—its constitution, its elected senate, its presidency—instantly obsolete. The Bonnie Blue flag, flying over the fort, was now a historical curiosity.

For a few tense days, Skipwith and a faction within the council contemplated armed resistance against the American force. The melodrama of the moment was not lost on him. The president of a republic founded by American settlers was threatening to fight the American army to preserve that republic’s honor long enough to surrender it properly. The council, facing the overwhelming reality of American power and the majority’s true desire, voted against resistance. On December 10, Skipwith oversaw the formal surrender of the republic’s authority. He stepped down from an office that ceased to exist the moment he left it. The lone star flag was lowered. The American flag was raised. In the official U.S. account, Skipwith was not a deposed president, but a helpful citizen who had facilitated a peaceful transition. His protest was filed away, a footnote to the inevitable.

While Philemon Thomas secured the republic by force of arms, John Rhea constructed its legal skeleton. If Thomas was the rebellion’s fist, Rhea was its pen, and he understood that legitimacy for the new nation required more than captured cannons. It demanded documents. As presiding officer of the convention, Rhea’s role was procedural, but his influence was foundational. The minutes from St. John’s Plains and Baton Rouge, preserved in the Louisiana State Archives, show his hand in every stage. He orchestrated the votes, drafted the resolutions, and framed the language that transformed a violent uprising into a constitutional enterprise.

Rhea’s background prepared him for this work. A migrant from Tennessee, he was a surveyor and a man of property, accustomed to the precise language of deeds and boundaries. The convention records reveal a mind obsessed with order. He insisted on formal motions, recorded votes, and written transcripts. When the delegates declared independence on September 26, 1810, it was Rhea who read the proclamation aloud. The text, as published in the American State Papers, avoided the fiery rhetoric of Jefferson’s 1776 document. Instead, it was a legalistic bill of indictment against Spanish rule, listing specific grievances: the failure to provide courts, the insecurity of land titles, the collapse of civil authority. It was less a cry for freedom than a meticulously argued case for divorce.

His greatest contribution was the constitution, drafted and adopted in the tense weeks following the capture of Baton Rouge. The document, a copy of which resides in the National Archives, is a revealing artifact. Modeled closely on the U.S. Constitution and the Kentucky state constitution, it established a bicameral legislature, an independent judiciary, and a chief magistrate. It guaranteed habeas corpus and trial by jury. It also, unequivocally, protected the institution of slavery. Rhea’s constitution was a blueprint for a government that was never meant to endure. Its very conventionality was its purpose. It signaled to the world, and especially to Washington, that these were not lawless frontiersmen but sober republicans capable of self-governance.

Rhea’s correspondence from this period, cited in later historical studies, shows his acute awareness of the audience. In a letter to a contact in Tennessee, he stressed the “order and regularity” of their proceedings. He knew that for President Madison to take them seriously, the rebellion needed to look less like a chaotic filibuster and more like a principled revolution. Every clause Rhea drafted, every procedural rule he enforced, was aimed at building this façade of stability. He was creating a respectable vessel for a foregone conclusion: annexation.

This legalistic approach created a stark, often absurd, contrast with events on the ground. While Rhea and the convention debated the finer points of senatorial terms in the council chamber at Fort San Carlos, Philemon Thomas’s recruits—many of them the delegates’ own sons and overseers—patrolled the muddy streets outside. The constitution provided for a regular army, but the republic’s military was a makeshift militia, its pay and supply a constant crisis. Rhea’s government issued commissions and passed revenue acts, but its actual authority relied on the same armed persuasion that had toppled Delassus.

The tension between Rhea’s legal order and frontier reality peaked in November. Even as the newly elected Senate convened under the constitution, Rhea was secretly drafting the council’s most urgent document: the letter to President Madison pleading for immediate U.S. annexation. The man who had just finished codifying West Florida’s sovereignty was now formally petitioning for its surrender. There was no hypocrisy in this, only a clear-eyed understanding of the project’s endgame. The constitution was not a foundation for a permanent nation; it was a legal bridge, carefully built by Rhea, designed to carry the territory from Spanish rule to American statehood without collapsing into anarchy or appearing as a conquest. His meticulous work ensured that when William Claiborne arrived with U.S. troops, he did not face a mob, but a government—one that could officially, and peacefully, vote itself out of existence.

President James Madison’s proclamation of October 27, 1810, was a document of quiet audacity. Issued from Washington and published in the National Intelligencer, it did not mention the Republic of West Florida, its convention, or its lone star flag. Instead, as archived in the American State Papers: Foreign Relations, it declared that the territory between the Mississippi and Perdido Rivers was “a part of the Territory ceded by France under the name of Louisiana,” and that the United States was therefore taking possession. The rebellion was treated not as a political event to be negotiated, but as a local disturbance that had finally allowed the preexisting American title to be enforced. The proclamation was not a response to the republic’s petition for annexation; it was a unilateral assertion of a claim that had been legally dormant for seven years.

Madison’s move was a masterstroke of political timing and legal fiction. The core of his argument rested on a contested interpretation of the 1803 Louisiana Purchase treaty. The United States had always maintained that the purchase included West Florida, based on France’s vague initial claim to the region when it ceded Louisiana to Spain in 1762. Spain vehemently denied this. Jefferson had blustered but never acted. Madison, now facing the collapse of Spanish authority and the rise of a pro-American settler republic, seized the moment. By ordering Governor William C.C. Claiborne to march troops into the territory, he transformed a diplomatic theory into a military fact before any foreign power could intervene. Crucially, he acted without seeking a declaration of war or specific congressional approval, grounding his authority in his constitutional power as commander-in-chief to enforce the laws of the United States—in this case, the law being the nation’s purported existing sovereignty over the land.

The contrast with his administration’s posture just six years earlier was stark. In 1804, Reuben Kemper and his brothers had led a filibustering raid from American soil into Spanish West Florida, aiming to foment a similar rebellion. President Jefferson and Secretary of State Madison had denounced the Kempers as criminals. American authorities disavowed them and offered to help Spain restore order. The Kempers’ action was lawless; the West Florida convention’s nearly identical action in 1810 was, in Madison’s new framing, a helpful clearing of the chessboard. The difference was not in the act, but in the outcome and the American ability to capitalize on it. The Kemper raid had failed. The Philemon Thomas assault had succeeded, creating a stable, pro-American power vacuum. Madison was willing to embrace the fruits of rebellion he had once condemned.

His motivations, as revealed in his correspondence and cabinet meeting notes cited by historians like Robert V. Haynes, were a tight weave of geopolitics and domestic pressure. Napoleon’s invasion of Spain had shattered the Spanish Empire, leaving its colonies in a state of precarious legitimacy. If the United States did not take West Florida, Britain or France might. The settlers themselves were overwhelmingly American, and their continued agitation—or potential establishment of a truly independent republic—posed an unpredictable threat on the southern border. Annexation satisfied the expansionist ambitions of the western states and solidified control of the vital Mississippi River trade routes. By acting via proclamation, Madison avoided a protracted and potentially divisive congressional debate over the constitutionality of acquiring new territory, a debate that could have stalled action until the strategic window closed.

The proclamation was a brilliant, cynical piece of statecraft. It treated the West Florida government as a nullity. In Madison’s official narrative, Skipwith, Rhea, and the convention were not founders of a nation, but merely American citizens who had helpfully expelled Spanish officials from what was already U.S. soil. This legal fiction allowed the administration to bypass the thorny question of recognizing a breakaway republic. It also denied the West Floridians the dignity of a treaty or a formal act of union. They were not being annexed; they were being reclaimed.

When Claiborne’s troops crossed the border in early December, they carried copies of Madison’s proclamation. Their orders were explicit: to occupy, to announce American sovereignty, and to dissolve the existing authorities. Claiborne’s reports to the Secretary of State, held in the National Archives, show he understood his role. He was not a diplomat meeting a counterpart. He was an American official restoring federal order to a wayward district. The elaborate constitutional structure John Rhea had built, the presidency Fulwar Skipwith inhabited, were irrelevant to his mission. The legal bridge Rhea had constructed was not crossed; it was simply bypassed. Madison had changed the map with a stroke of his pen, rendering ninety days of meticulous statecraft into a historical footnote. The rebellion had succeeded only to make itself unnecessary.

Governor William C.C. Claiborne entered West Florida not as an ambassador, but as a sheriff serving a writ. His reports to Secretary of State Robert Smith, preserved in the National Archives, detail a methodical, almost casual operation. In early December 1810, he crossed the border from the Orleans Territory with a force of U.S. Army regulars from the nearby Fort Adams garrison. Simultaneously, a flotilla of American gunboats under Commodore John Shaw ascended the Mississippi, their guns trained on the riverbank, to secure Baton Rouge from the water. Claiborne’s orders, based on Madison’s proclamation, were unambiguous: take possession, announce American sovereignty, and dissolve all competing authority. He carried no treaty for Skipwith to sign, no terms for the Supreme Council to ratify.

The arrival of American military power was both a relief and an insult to the men in Fort San Carlos. Diaries and letters from planters in the district, referenced in later historical collections, describe a populace weary of uncertainty. The sight of U.S. troops marching in orderly columns was, for many, a promise of stability and a guarantee of their contested land titles. For the government of the republic, however, it was a dismantling. Claiborne’s first act, as recorded in his dispatch of December 7, was to issue a public address. He posted notices throughout Baton Rouge declaring the territory annexed and under the jurisdiction of the United States. He made no mention of the Republic of West Florida. In Claiborne’s official narrative, he was not invading a foreign nation; he was restoring federal order to a district that had temporarily slipped from American control due to Spanish weakness.

President Fulwar Skipwith received Claiborne at the government house in Baton Rouge. The meeting, reconstructed from Claiborne’s reports and Skipwith’s later memoir, was a study in diplomatic dissonance. Skipwith, believing he was hosting a negotiation between sovereign entities, protested the manner of the American entry. He argued, as he would later write, that the “outrage” was not the annexation itself, which he ardently desired, but the “high-handed mode” of its execution. He had envisioned a formal proposal from Washington, a vote by the West Florida Senate, and a ceremonial transfer of authority that would recognize the legitimacy of the republic’s ninety-day existence. Claiborne, according to his own account, listened with polite impatience. He had not been authorized to negotiate. He had been ordered to execute a presidential proclamation. To him, Skipwith was not a president, but the leading citizen of a district in rebellion—a useful citizen, but a citizen nonetheless.

For three days, a farcical tension gripped Baton Rouge. American soldiers patrolled streets still nominally under the control of the Republic’s militia. The Lone Star flag still flew over Fort San Carlos, while Claiborne’s proclamations papered the walls below it. Skipwith and a faction of the council, their pride wounded, briefly debated armed resistance. The debate, noted in council minutes from December 9, was short and stark. Philemon Thomas, the military commander, assessed their forces. They had perhaps two hundred motivated men. Claiborne had more than that in regulars, with naval cannon support. More importantly, as one council member reportedly argued, the people wouldn’t fight their own countrymen to preserve a government whose entire purpose had been to join that country. The vote against resistance was decisive.

The formal end came on December 10, 1810. Under a gray winter sky, a mixed assembly of American soldiers, West Florida militiamen, and townspeople gathered in the parade ground of Fort San Carlos. No detailed eyewitness account of the ceremony exists, but Claiborne’s official report describes the essential facts. At his direction, the Lone Star flag was lowered. The same halyard then raised the Stars and Stripes. No salute was fired from the fort’s captured Spanish cannon for the fallen banner. A brief salute was offered to the American flag. In that sequence was the entire story: the republic’s symbol was removed as an administrative task; the U.S. standard was hoisted with military honor.

Claiborne’s report to Washington, written that evening, was brisk and triumphant. “I have the satisfaction to inform you,” he wrote to Secretary Smith, “that the flag of the United States was this day hoisted on the Fort of Baton Rouge, and that the Government of the United States now exercises sovereignty over the same.” He made a single, telling mention of the republic’s leaders: “The late functionaries of the West Florida government have acquiesced in the change, and have promised their aid and influence to preserve order.” With that phrase—“the late functionaries”—he consigned Skipwith’s presidency, Rhea’s constitution, and the Supreme Council to the past tense. Their acquiescence was not a treaty; it was obedience.

Skipwith watched the proceedings, then returned to Monte Vista. In his memoir, he expressed a bitter satisfaction. The goal of union had been achieved. The method, however, rankled for years. He had played his part in a script written in Washington, his protest a footnote in a margin Claiborne never read. The careful legal architecture John Rhea had built was not used as a bridge to statehood; it was simply declared irrelevant. The republic died not with a battle, but with a flag-raising, its independence extinguished by the very power its founders had hoped would embrace it. The Bonnie Blue flag was folded away, a symbol whose meaning had been settled by force of fact. It was now a souvenir.

The Spanish response to the loss of West Florida was not a story of mobilization, but of paralysis. In Pensacola, Governor Vicente Folch received the desperate dispatches from Baton Rouge as the crisis unfolded. Governor Carlos de Lassus had warned him that without immediate reinforcements, he could not hold the fort. Folch, commander of all Spanish West Florida, had the authority and the duty to respond. The archival record of his correspondence, held in the Spanish colonial archives in Seville, shows a man trapped by a collapsing empire. He promised de Lassus aid. He requested men and money from Havana. And then he waited, as the bureaucratic machinery of a kingdom under occupation ground to a halt.

Folch’s inaction was not personal cowardice; it was institutional failure. The root cause lay across the Atlantic. In 1808, Napoleon Bonaparte’s armies had invaded Spain, deposing King Ferdinand VII and installing Joseph Bonaparte on the throne. The Spanish government fragmented into a pro-French regime in Madrid and a rebellious junta in Cádiz claiming legitimacy. For colonial governors like Folch, this created an impossible dilemma. Who was his lawful sovereign? To whom should he report? More practically, where were his orders and his funds? The chain of command was severed. As historian David Weber notes in The Spanish Frontier in North America, the crisis “left colonial administrators in America confused, isolated, and largely thrown upon their own resources.”

This imperial unraveling translated into concrete military incapacity. Folch’s garrison in Pensacola was itself threadbare, likely insufficient to defend his own post, let alone mount a relief expedition hundreds of miles west to Baton Rouge. His requests to Havana for reinforcements landed on the desk of Captain General Salvador de Muro y Salazar, the Marquis of Someruelos. Cuba had troops, but Someruelos faced his own challenges. The Spanish crisis had ignited revolutionary fervor across Latin America. His forces were needed to secure Cuba itself and to prepare for potential insurrections elsewhere. The documents show a pattern of delay and equivocation. Someruelos did not outright refuse Folch; he simply did not act with any urgency. By the time he might have considered assembling an expedition, the news from Baton Rouge had shifted from a plea for help to a report of total loss.

Folch was also navigating a treacherous local landscape. The American settlers in his own district around Pensacola watched events to the west closely. A large contingent of Anglo-Americans lived in the area, and the success of the Baton Rouge rebellion was an open invitation. If Folch stripped Pensacola of its garrison to march on Baton Rouge, he risked losing Pensacola to a copycat revolt. His primary duty was to hold what he still controlled. This defensive calculation made offensive action unthinkable.

By late October, with Madison’s proclamation public and U.S. troops massing on the border, any Spanish military response became a fantasy. Folch wrote a final, grim assessment to his superiors, concluding that a relief expedition would be useless. The Americans were too numerous, too motivated, and now too officially engaged. To challenge them would not be to suppress a local rebellion, but to start a war with the United States—a war Spain had no hope of winning in its current state. He chose preservation over provocation.

The Spanish government, once it regained a voice, lodged formal diplomatic protests with the Madison administration. They denounced the annexation as a violation of sovereignty and a theft of territory. But as the diplomatic correspondence in the American State Papers makes clear, these protests were pro forma, a matter of saving face. Spain had no military means to reverse the outcome. The conflict with Napoleon would drag on for years, consuming all national energy. The 1819 Adams-Onís Treaty, which finally ceded all of Spanish Florida to the United States, merely ratified a series of facts already established on the ground a decade earlier in West Florida. The empire’s inability to project power in 1810 had made its eventual retreat inevitable. Folch did not lose West Florida; the Spanish crown, fractured and besieged, had already let it slip from its grasp. The republic’s founders had not defeated a powerful adversary; they had stormed an abandoned outpost of a dying regime.

The immediate consequence of the annexation was a sudden, silent rewriting of the map. The Republic of West Florida ceased to exist on December 10, 1810. By an act of Congress on April 14, 1812, its territory was incorporated into the newly admitted state of Louisiana. The land east of the Pearl River was appended to the Mississippi Territory. The border dispute that had festered since 1803 was resolved not by treaty or war, but by a fait accompli engineered by settlers and ratified by presidential fiat. The United States gained the critical eastern shore of the Mississippi, including the strategic post at Baton Rouge, and pushed its effective boundary to the Perdido River. Spain’s foothold north of the thirty-first parallel was irrevocably broken.

For the key actors, the aftermath was a study in quiet assimilation. Fulwar Skipwith, the deposed president, returned to his plantation. He later served as the American consul to France, a position of respect that perhaps salved the wounded pride he expressed in his memoir. John Rhea, the constitutional architect, became a judge in the Louisiana parish system, his talent for order repurposed for the very government that had rendered his masterpiece obsolete. Philemon Thomas was elected to the U.S. House of Representatives, his martial exploits now a political credential in the expanding nation. They suffered no punishment, only a gentle demotion from founders to citizens. Their rebellion was not remembered as treason against Spain, but as a precocious act of American patriotism.

The deeper consequence was the establishment of a template. The West Florida episode demonstrated a new, effective formula for territorial expansion. It required three elements: a region populated by restive American settlers, a weakened imperial power incapable of forceful response, and a U.S. administration willing to capitalize on the resulting chaos with swift, unilateral action. The method avoided the diplomatic entanglements and political costs of formal war. It transformed filibusters into freedom fighters, and conquest into liberation, provided the outcome aligned with American interests. This model would be observed, studied, and emulated. Within a few years, American settlers in Texas would employ a nearly identical playbook against a different enfeebled power: Mexico.

Yet the most persistent consequence was symbolic, nestled in the folds of the flag that was lowered without ceremony at Baton Rouge. The single white star on a field of blue was a practical banner, born of haste and necessity. But symbols, once released, take on lives their creators cannot control. The Bonnie Blue flag was stored away, but it was not forgotten. It resurfaced three decades later in a different context, carried by Mississippi secessionists in January 1861 as their state became the second to leave the Union. It was hailed then as the “first flag of independence” of the South, its West Florida origins remembered but its meaning radically transfigured. The banner of a rebellion designed to join the United States became, ironically, a harbinger of the rebellion that sought to destroy it. The republic’s true legacy was not a political precedent, but a potent, portable image—a lone star whose light would be claimed by later revolutionaries for causes its original bearers could scarcely have imagined.
