Zephyr’s left hand was bleeding again. Not a dramatic, arterial spray, just a persistent seep from a cut along the thumb where a sliver of the God-Shard had bitten. The blood mixed with the powdered gold they were using as a binder, creating a faintly pink, blasphemous paste. The air in the Cumulus Canvas stank of turpentine, ozone, and something else—the smell of a lightning strike hitting a wet dog. It was Tuesday.

The installation—a rickety, multi-tiered scaffold of salvaged dirigible frames and cathedral glass lashed together with aircraft cable—drifted through a low, perpetually weeping cloud. Below, the city was a gray-green smudge, like a watercolor left in the rain. Up here, the only sounds were the groan of stressed metal, the hiss of the drizzle on the glass roof, and the low, atonal thrumming of the seven halo fragments arranged on the central worktable.

The largest fragment, Shard Alpha, was about the size of a dinner plate, if a dinner plate were forged from frozen sunlight and existential dread. It refused to lie flat. It hovered a quarter-inch above the steel table, vibrating at a frequency that made Zephyr’s back teeth ache. The other six smaller pieces orbited it in a slow, resentful ellipse, occasionally bumping into each other with a sound like a wineglass tapped by a fingernail.

Zephyr dabbed the gold-blood paste onto the edge of Shard Beta. “Just… adhere, you sanctimonious splinter.”

They pressed it toward Alpha. The two fragments repelled each other with a soft phump of displaced air. Beta shot across the table, clipped a jar of cadmium yellow medium, and sent it spinning to the grated floor where it shattered. The yellow paint bled into the runoff from the leak in the roof panel above workstation three. That leak was shaped like Portugal. Zephyr had named it Luis.

“Fine.” Zephyr wiped their hands on a rag already stiff with primer and divinity. “Non-adherence as a statement. The fracturing of the divine unity. Post-modern, obviously.”

Their critic, a pigeon who had stowed away on the last supply lift and now seemed permanently installed on a crossbeam, cooed derisively. Zephyr had named him Clive. Clive understood nothing of the sublime struggle.

The problem was the colors. Or the lack thereof. The world, to Zephyr, existed in a spectrum of grayscale outside of their divine visions. The city was charcoal and ash. The clouds were dirty wool. Their own hands were monochrome tools. But when the shards resonated—when they caught the light at a certain angle, or when Zephyr’s own blood made contact—the world exploded.

Last Tuesday, while trying to solder a wire to Shard Gamma (a mistake; the solder had vaporized, and Zephyr’s eyebrows had taken three days to grow back), the fragments had flared. For fourteen seconds, the entire Cumulus Canvas was bathed in a color Zephyr had no name for. It was the sound of a cello played in an empty cathedral, translated directly into light. It made the turpentine fumes smell like pomegranates. It made Clive look like a feathered king. Then it vanished, and the world flattened back into grimy newsprint.

That was the addiction. Not the shards themselves, but the key they turned in Zephyr’s perception. The only time they felt like a real artist—the only time they felt real—was in those stolen flashes of impossible hue. Their last major installation, “Rapture in Three Acts,” had used amplified Gregorian chants and high-frequency lights to achieve a similar effect. It had worked too well. Three patrons at the gallery opening had spontaneously shed their mortal coils, leaving behind only piles of expensive clothes and a profound sense of litigation. The art world had called it “derivative of the ascensionist movement” and “a biohazard.” Zephyr called it a qualified success.

Now, with the actual source code of holiness scattered on their worktable, they were going for the definitive statement. The masterpiece.

Shard Epsilon began to spin faster. A high whine emanated from it, climbing up the register until it was felt more than heard, a vibration in the fillings of Zephyr’s teeth. The leak shaped like Portugal began to drizzle more earnestly.

“Oh, don’t get tonal on me,” Zephyr muttered, reaching for the tuning fork they used as a neutralizer. It was forged from a melted-down church bell. Theory was, it would cancel out the shard’s frequency.

They struck the fork against the steel leg of the worktable. A clear, pure note rang out.

Shard Epsilon did not cancel. It synchronized.

The whine locked into the fork’s note, amplifying it. The other shards shifted their orbits. A glass pane in the north wall, salvaged from a 14th-century chapel depicting the martyrdom of Saint Sebastian, cracked with a sound like a gunshot. Clive took off in a flurry of panic and feathers.

The entire Cumulus Canvas shuddered. A cable snapped somewhere below with a musical twang. The floor listed five degrees to port. Tubes of paint rolled off the tables. The Portugal-shaped leak became a stream.

Zephyr dropped the fork, which was now glowing a dull red. “Right. Counter-harmonics. Should have anticipated that.”

They lunged for the containment box—a lead-lined cooler meant for transporting organ transplants—and swept the orbiting shards toward it with a forearm. The smaller ones clattered inside, their hums muffled. Alpha, the big one, resisted. It pushed against Zephyr’s grip, not with force, but with a profound and unsettling unwillingness. It felt like trying to shove a prideful cat into a carrier.

With a grunt, Zephyr manhandled it over the lip of the cooler and slammed the lid. The immediate dissonance faded. The floor groaned back to something approximating level. The only sounds were the rain, Zephyr’s ragged breathing, and the distant, offended cooing of Clive from a higher beam.

They slumped against the worktable. Their thumb throbbed. The gray world seemed grayer than ever, a punishment for the failed attempt. This was the third containment this week. The shards were getting more reactive. The last supply lift pilot had asked about the “weird magnetic pulses” messing with his altimeter.

Zephyr stared at the cooler. The premise was flawed. You couldn’t assemble God’s halo. It wasn’t a jigsaw puzzle. The thought arrived not as an epiphany, but as a tired, familiar guest. It was the same thought that came every time a project started to eat its own tail. The medium was fighting the message.

But what was the message? Unity? Wholeness? The restoration of divine perfection? That was kitsch. That was poster art for the celestial.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and irrelevant. Their art school mentor, a woman who wore only shades of brown and spoke in a perpetual whisper, leaning over a failed clay sculpture of Zephyr’s. “You are trying to make the mud behave like marble,” she had hissed. “Stop trying to fix the mud. What is the mud saying?”

What were the shards saying?

They were saying they hated each other.

They were saying they were lonely.

They were saying, in their atonal whines and their petty repulsions, that being a piece of a broken whole was a kind of hell. A perpetual, vibrating reminder of what you were supposed to be, and a seething resentment toward the other pieces for not fitting.

Zephyr looked at their hands, pale and blood-streaked in the flat light. They looked at the cooler. A wild, stupid idea began to form in the back of their skull, right where the tension headache was brewing. It wasn’t about assembly.

It was about the break.

They stood up, wincing as the floor creaked. They opened the cooler. Shard Alpha drifted back up, hovering at chest height, pulsing softly. It wasn’t giving off light, but Zephyr could feel its attention, like a sullen child waiting to be scolded.

“Okay,” Zephyr said, their voice quiet in the dripping space. “New thesis. You don’t want to be put back together. You’re not fragments of a halo. You’re fragments of the break. You’re not pieces of God’s glory. You’re pieces of God’s… bad mood.”

The shard did nothing. The rain pattered on Portugal.

“The ego,” Zephyr breathed. The word felt right, a key clicking in a rusty lock. The halo wasn’t a physical object; it was a symbol of perfection, of completion. Of divine self-regard. When it shattered, what broke wasn’t the light—it was the idea. The “I am perfect” idea. These shards were the shattered pieces of that divine ego. Petty, jealous, dissonant, each one convinced it was the most important piece, each one repelling the others because acknowledging them would mean acknowledging it was incomplete.

It explained everything. The repulsion. The dissonance. The way they only showed true color when they were agitated—when the divine temper tantrum flared. They weren’t tools for creation. They were the wreckage of the ultimate creative meltdown.

Zephyr started to laugh. It was a dry, cracked sound. They’d been trying to make a stained-glass window out of the pieces of a smashed mirror. The masterpiece wasn’t in the reassembly. The masterpiece was the smash.

They reached for Alpha, not to force it, but to feel its vibration. The thrum traveled up their arm, into their jaw. The world did not explode into color. It remained a study in gray. But the gray felt different now—not an an absence, but a potential. The blank page before the correct, terrible, beautiful mark.

They would need the biggest canvas. They would need to channel the entire dissonance, the full, petulant frequency of shattered divine pride, into a single, catastrophic moment of artistic honesty. They would not build. They would detonate.

A slow grin spread across Zephyr’s face. Clive, peering down from his beam, ruffled his feathers in concern.

Zephyr turned to gather the other shards from the cooler. As they lifted Epsilon, it vibrated in sympathy with Alpha. A low hum began to build in the room, deeper than before, a fundamental bass note of celestial pique. The glass in the walls trembled in their frames. The puddle from the Portugal leak shivered, sending out concentric rings.

The entire Cumulus Canvas gave a long, deep groan, like a great beast stirring in its sleep. From the depths of the structure, a critical support beam, stressed beyond tolerance by weeks of divine tantrums, cracked with a sound like a frozen tree splitting in a silent forest.

The hum from the shards deepened into a contented purr.

The floor tilted, not a sudden lurch, but a slow, inexorable descent toward the starboard side. A tube of titanium white rolled off the table, hit the grating, and kept rolling into the gloom.

Zephyr clutched the shards to their chest, their heart hammering not with fear, but with a fierce, blazing joy. The masterpiece had begun.

The floor settled at a fifteen-degree list. Everything not nailed down slid into a new, precarious arrangement along the starboard wall. Zephyr’s boots slipped on the wet grating. They hugged the cooler, feeling the shards inside buzz against the lead lining like angry hornets. The sound was different now—not a dissonant whine, but a unified, anticipatory thrum. They’d given the ego a purpose. It liked having a purpose.

A crackle of energy, visible as a jagged line of static, snapped from the ceiling to the central worktable. The air smelled of burnt hair and, inexplicably, fresh mint. A patch of wall next to the Portugal leak stopped being wall. It became a window into a sun-drenched wheat field for three seconds. A gust of hot, dry air blew into the studio, carrying the scent of dirt and growth. Then it snapped back to dripping metal and peeling paint. The wheat field was gone, but a single stalk of golden wheat now lay on the floor, already wilting in the damp.

“Okay,” Zephyr whispered. “Site-specific temporal bleed. That’s new.”

They righted a stool and sat, catching their breath. The plan was simple. Channel the full, petulant frequency of the shattered divine ego into a single, focused burst. Not to destroy the shards, but to let them destroy themselves against the concept of a canvas. The ultimate act of artistic criticism: letting the subject critique itself into oblivion. They just needed to arrange the pieces. And find a canvas big enough to matter.

The largest surface in the Cumulus Canvas was the curved ceiling of cathedral glass. It was currently weeping rain and shuddering with every deep pulse from the cooler. Perfect.

Zephyr stood, knees protesting. They opened the cooler. The seven fragments rose in unison, arranging themselves in a rough circle in the air. They hummed, a chord that was almost musical, if music could convey smug superiority.

“Right,” Zephyr said. “You’re the judges. And the exhibit. Let’s get you into position.”

They reached for Alpha. It didn’t resist this time. It floated into their palm, cool and heavy. The vibration traveled up their arm, into their teeth. They carried it to the center of the slanted floor, directly under the highest point of the glass ceiling. They placed it on the grating. It remained there, hovering, pulsing like a lazy heart.

One by one, they placed the others in a wide ring around it. Each shard found its own spot, a few inches off the ground, and began its own unique oscillation. Epsilon spun. Beta wobbled. Gamma emitted a faint, high-pitched whistle that reminded Zephyr of a boiling kettle.

The air in the studio grew thick. Pressure built, not in Zephyr’s ears, but behind their eyes. The gray light took on a texture, like old velvet. Shadows stopped obeying the single, sad work light. They pooled in the corners, then stretched across the floor toward the ring of shards as if pulled.

Then the whispers started.

It wasn’t a sound you heard with your ears. It arrived directly in the back of the skull, a susurrus of dry, disdainful thought. It felt like reading a critic’s notes over your own shoulder.

…derivative of the celestial schism, really…

…a pedestrian use of sacred geometry…

…the artist’s blood is an obvious crutch, a bid for authenticity that fails to transcend its own biological vulgarity…

Zephyr froze, a shard in each hand. They looked around. Clive was preening, oblivious. The whispers weren’t coming from him.

…the attempt to anthropomorphize the ineffable is, at best, quaint. At worst, a profound failure of imagination…

“Who said that?” Zephyr’s voice sounded small, swallowed by the thickening air.

A shimmer appeared in the space above the shard ring. Not a person. A distortion, like heat haze off asphalt, vaguely humanoid. It had no features, just a suggestion of a head, shoulders, arms folded in judgment.

“We did,” the collective whisper replied. The voice was neither male nor female. It was the sound of a hundred art school syllabi being rustled in disapproval. “We are the consensus. The critique inherent in the fracture. You were close, artisan. It is not ego. It is taste.”

Zephyr blinked. “Taste?”

“Divine taste. The standard by which all creation is measured and found wanting. The halo was not an object. It was a statement of aesthetic perfection. Its shattering was the first, and only, act of divine self-critique. We are the lingering standards. The unmeetable criteria.”

The shimmer drifted closer. Zephyr felt a cold, pinpoint focus on their latest brushstroke—the one on their bandaged thumb. The whisper dissected it.

…amateurish line work, a frantic energy that suggests haste, not passion…

“I was trying to stop the bleeding!”

“Exactly. Utility over artistry. A fatal compromise.”

Zephyr’s face grew hot. This was worse than the gallery review that had called “Rapture in Three Acts” a “biohazard.” This was the source of all reviews. The primal nitpick.

Another shimmer formed near the worktable. This one gestured with a vaporous limb toward the Portugal leak. “The water feature is a trite metaphor for sorrow. The shape is geographically literal. Lacks subtlety.”

“It’s a leak! I didn’t design it!”

“Then you are a curator of accidents. Even worse.”

The chaos escalated. A third critic manifested by the broken jar of cadmium yellow. It condemned the color choice as “emotionally obvious.” The puddle of paint and rainwater was deemed “a lazy abstraction.” The air buzzed with their judgments, overlapping, contradictory, universally disdainful.

The shards, meanwhile, glowed brighter. Each critical pronouncement seemed to feed them. Their hum grew louder, more complex. The floor vibrations shifted from a groan to a rhythmic shudder. A glass pane depicting a particularly sad-looking Saint Sebastian cracked again, this time forming a perfect frown.

Zephyr stood in the center of the ring, bombarded. Their grand plan—the explosive masterpiece—felt small and stupid under this microscopic, eternal scrutiny. A desperate, manic laugh bubbled up in their throat. It came out as a choked snort.

The critics paused. The shards dimmed, just a fraction.

…the artist’s response is incongruous. A failure to engage with the seriousness of the critique…

“You’re pieces of God’s bad Yelp review,” Zephyr wheezed, tears of hysterical frustration in their eyes. “You’re not the break. You’re the commentary on the break. You’re the notes in the margin of the universe that just say ‘TRY HARDER’ in celestial pencil.”

The shimmer above the shard ring solidified slightly. It seemed to lean in.

“And your proposed ‘detonation’?”

“Pathetic,” Zephyr said, the word tasting like ash. “A cheap spectacle. Shock for shock’s sake. You’d hate it. You’d write a thousand-page treatise on its derivative nature and its problematic relationship with its own materials.”

The critics were silent. The only sound was the rain, the creak of the straining Canvas, and the now-attentive hum of the shards.

“Go on.”

Zephyr’s mind raced. The clarity wasn’t profound. It was absurd. It was the punchline to a joke they’d been living for years. The critics couldn’t be satisfied. That was their function. To be the unmeetable standard. Their masterpiece wasn’t to meet the standard, or to destroy it.

It was to agree with it.

“It’s all terrible,” Zephyr announced, their voice gaining strength. They gestured wildly around the tilting, leaking, whispering studio. “This space? A pretentious mess. The shards? Gaudy relics. My technique?” They looked at their bloodied hands. “A fumbling disaster. My whole artistic pursuit? A vainglorious attempt to fill a silence that is, frankly, more elegant.”

They picked up Shard Gamma from the ring. It vibrated warmly in their hand. “You’re right. It’s all wanting. Especially this.”

They turned and threw Shard Gamma at the glass ceiling.

It didn’t hit with a crash. It hit with a soft thump and stuck there, embedded in a pane of blue glass from Chartres. A web of cracks radiated out from it. The cracks glowed with that nameless cello-color for a second.

The critics shrieked—a soundless, brain-scrambling blast of pure outrage. “Vandalism!”

“Critique accepted!” Zephyr yelled back. They grabbed Epsilon and hurled it. It struck another pane, near the Portugal leak. Another thump, another spiderweb of light.

The shards in the ring began to tremble, not with anger, but with a terrible, excited resonance. They were being used according to their true nature. Not for creation. For condemnation.

Zephyr became a whirlwind of agreement. They tossed Beta at a support beam. It sank into the metal, which instantly rusted and bloomed with orange salt crystals. They placed Delta carefully on the floor and stomped on it. It didn’t break. It sank through the grating and into the cloud below, leaving a perfectly square hole that whistled.

The critics were apoplectic, flashing through colors of outrage, swirling around Zephyr in a vortex of hissed corrections. “Not like that! The context is wrong! The methodology is flawed!”

“I know!” Zephyr laughed, pure and loud. They snatched up the final two shards, Alpha and a small one named Zeta. The Canvas was shaking itself apart. Beams groaned. Cables pinged and snapped. The glass ceiling was a mosaic of glowing cracks. The world outside the windows flickered—city, wheat field, a vast ocean, a starless void.

This was the moment. The choice. The artistic ambition was to complete the destructive act, to let the critics consume themselves. The reality was that the critics were part of reality, and this was getting expensive.

Zephyr looked at Alpha, the big one, the source of the standard. It pulsed in their hand, waiting for its final placement in the critique.

Instead, Zephyr turned to the nearest critic-shimmer, the one by the worktable. “You know what your problem is?”

The shimmer froze.

“You have no stakes,” Zephyr said, their voice suddenly calm amidst the roaring chaos. “You judge, but you don’t care. You have nothing to lose. That’s not criticism. That’s just noise.”

They raised Alpha high. Not toward the ceiling. Not toward the critics.

Toward the one thing in the room that was empty, blank, and utterly without pretension.

The lead-lined cooler.

“This,” Zephyr said, “is for Luis.”

They brought the shard down, point-first, onto the cooler’s lid.

There was no explosion of light. There was a sound like a universe sighing. A single, clear, perfect note.

Alpha shattered.

Not into pieces. Into powder. A fine, glittering dust that hung in the air for a moment, catching the light from the cracked ceiling in a final, glorious burst of that impossible color. It was the sound of a cello, and the smell of pomegranates, and the feeling of a key turning in a lock that didn’t need to exist.

Then it fell, a gentle rain of spent divinity, onto the cold, practical lead.

The other shards, in the ceiling, in the beam, in the air, all dissolved at once. The glowing cracks went dark. The critics vanished with a faint, surprised pop of air. The temporal bleeds snapped shut. The wheat stalk disappeared.

The humming stopped.

The violent shuddering of the Cumulus Canvas ceased, settling into a gentle, ordinary sway. The floor remained tilted, but it was a stable tilt. The Portugal leak dripped. Just water.

The silence was immense. It was the silence after a bell stops ringing. It was the silence of a page before the first mark.

Clive cooed, a soft, questioning sound.

Zephyr stood amidst the glittering debris. Their hands were empty. Their head was quiet. The gray world returned, but it was a different gray. It wasn’t flat. It was full of potential shadow and texture. It was a color you could work with.

They looked around. The studio was a wreck. A holy, critiqued, finalized wreck. It was perfect. It was done.

On the worktable, where the shards had once orbited, sat the cooler. And next to it, leaning against the wall, was the one thing Zephyr had forgotten was there. A canvas. Large, primed, untouched. A field of pure, matte white.

It was utterly empty. It was the most beautiful thing Zephyr had ever seen.

They walked over, boots crunching on faintly glowing dust. They ran a finger along the canvas’s edge. It didn’t whisper. It didn’t judge. It just was.

From the hole in the floor where Delta had escaped, a wisp of cloud drifted up, cool and damp. It smelled like rain, and nothing else.

Zephyr picked up a brush. Not the gold-dusted one. A simple, clean, bristle brush. They dipped it into the one tube of paint that hadn’t rolled away—a basic, unassuming phthalo blue.

They looked at the blankness. They looked at their own pale, blood-streaked hand holding the brush.

For the first time in years, they had no plan. No thesis. No statement.

Just a mark they wanted to make.

They touched the brush to the canvas.
