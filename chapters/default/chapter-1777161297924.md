The new government’s first act was not a speech or a proclamation, but a guard roster. Philemon Thomas posted men at the fort’s gates and on the parapets. They were farmers and planters, not soldiers, and their discipline was uneven. Some stood rigid, muskets shouldered. Others leaned against the timber walls, talking in low tones, their eyes drifting to the two canvas-wrapped forms lying near the barracks. The Spanish dead, Lieutenant Luis de Grand-Pré and a corporal had not yet been moved. A fly settled on the edge of one shroud, then buzzed away.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus sat in a chair he had occupied for months. His plain clothes—the dark coat and trousers he had changed into before dawn—were now a prisoner’s uniform. Across from him stood Philemon Thomas and John Rhea. The contrast was stark: Delassus, pale and still; Thomas, mud on his boots, a pistol still warm at his belt; Rhea, neat in a planter’s coat, his face a mask of procedural calm.

The negotiation was brief and conducted in French, the language of diplomacy in the territory. Thomas, according to later accounts from participants, stated the terms. The governor and his remaining soldiers were to surrender all public arms and munitions. They would give their parole—their word of honor—not to take up arms against the new Republic of West Florida. In return, they could keep their personal belongings and would be allowed to remain in Baton Rouge under guard until arrangements could be made for their departure to New Orleans.

Delassus asked one question. His voice was flat. “Would their personal safety be guaranteed?”

Thomas nodded. “It would.”

The governor’s shoulders relaxed by a fraction. He agreed. There was no other choice. The document was produced, its language formal and legalistic. Delassus signed it. The scratch of his pen was the only sound in the room.

Outside, the process of disarmament began. Spanish soldiers filed out of the barracks, their uniforms disheveled. They stacked their muskets in a pyramid on the parade ground. The rebel militiamen watched, their own weapons held ready. The transaction was silent, almost ceremonial. A Spanish sergeant handed over the keys to the powder magazine. That door was opened, revealing a stock that was, as one rebel later wrote in a letter, “less than half what was rumored, and much of it damp.”

The immediate problem was not the Spanish, but the town. Baton Rouge was not a fortified garrison; it was a scattering of houses, taverns, and warehouses along the river. News of the fort’s capture spread through the settlement within the hour. The reaction was not uniform. Anglo planters emerged from their homes, some offering supplies to the rebels. French Creole merchants watched from behind shutters, their allegiances uncertain. A few Spanish loyalists, mostly minor officials and their families, barricaded their doors.

Thomas dispatched squads of militiamen to patrol the main track. Their orders were simple: prevent looting, keep the peace. There was no list of enemies, no plan for a purge. The priority was order. In a dispatch written that afternoon to the convention still assembling at St. John’s Plains, Thomas reported the fort secure and the governor a prisoner. He made no claim of broader control. “The inhabitants are quiet,” he wrote, “but their quiet is of the watching kind.”

John Rhea’s task was different. He moved to a small office adjacent to the commandant’s quarters, commandeered a desk, and began drafting the documents of legitimacy. The first was a public notice, to be posted at the fort’s gate and at the river landing. It declared that Spanish authority was dissolved and that a provisional government now exercised power. It stated that all Spanish laws remained in force. This was a critical, stabilizing lie—they had no other legal code to impose. The notice was signed by Rhea as “President of the Convention.” He did not yet use the title “President of the Republic.” That would come with elections.

The afternoon sun grew hot. The two bodies were finally carried to a shaded spot behind the barracks. A Spanish chaplain, also under parole was allowed to say a brief prayer. The rebels did not interrupt. They stood at a distance, hats in hands. When the prayer ended, the militiamen returned to their posts. The chaplain was led back to the barracks. The dead were left for the burial detail to handle later.

By evening, a semblance of routine emerged. Cooking fires were lit. Sentries changed. Governor Delassus was escorted under guard to his own residence, a house on the fort’s grounds. A door was closed, a rebel posted outside. Through the window, the governor could see the flagpole in the center of the parade ground. The Spanish flag was gone, taken down in the confusion of the morning. The halyard hung empty, slapping against the pole in the breeze.

In the commandant’s quarters, now his headquarters, Philemon Thomas sat at the governor’s desk. He examined the fort’s ledger, the inventory of supplies, the maps. The reality of what he held—and what he did not—began to settle in the quiet of the room. The fort was his. The town was uncertain. The territory beyond was unknown. The only certainty was the loaded pistol on the desk before him, and the sound of his own men, their voices tired and edged with triumph, talking in the courtyard below.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus did not resist because he had nothing to resist with. The archival record shows his predicament in numbers. A muster roll from August 1810, preserved in the Spanish military archives in Seville, listed forty-two soldiers at Fort San Carlos. By September, desertions and illness had reduced the effective garrison to fewer than thirty. His monthly dispatches to his superior, Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, detailed a crumbling infrastructure: rotting palisades, a powder magazine leaking moisture, a payroll months in arrears. When the convention of settlers at St. John’s Plains had presented him with their resolution in July—offering to “assist” him in governing due to his “weakened capacity”—Delassus had feigned acceptance. He hosted the delegates at what later accounts called “peace dinners” at the fort. He drank with them. He listened. The performance was one of cooperation, but its purpose was delay. He waitted for reinforcements from Pensacola that his own reports had already indicated would never come.

His behavior in the final hours before the assault, pieced together from rebel accounts and the fragmentary Spanish correspondence, follows a pattern of calculated surrender. At least two warnings reached him. A Spanish loyalist in the settlement sent a message the evening of September 22, stating that armed men gathered. Delassus doubled the guard but gave no order to arrest the convention’s leaders. Later that night, a scout reported seeing the rebel columns moving through the woods. Delassus did not send out patrols. He did not attempt a sortie. He simply waited. When the first shots echoed in the fog after dawn, he was not in uniform. He had changed into plain civilian clothes—a dark coat and trousers. This was not the attire of a commander preparing for a last stand. It was the attire of a civilian official expecting to become a prisoner.

The swiftness of his capitulation, then was not cowardice. It was a rational assessment of a terminal equation. The Spanish Empire in West Florida was an administrative phantom. Its authority rested on a thin tissue of tradition, a handful of decrees from a king held captive by Napoleon in France, and the loyalty of a scattered population that was overwhelmingly Anglo-American. Delassus understood the demographics. In a confidential report to Havana earlier that year, he had estimated the Spanish-subject population of his district at roughly 1,400. He did not commit to paper his estimate of the American settlers, but other documents from the period put the number above 6,000. The math was inescapable. To fight was to die for a fiction.

His actions after signing the surrender accord reinforce this reading. Delassus gave his parole—his word of honor—not to take up arms against the new republic. He then complied with every demand. He ordered his men to stack their arms. He handed over the keys. He sat in his residence under guard, making no attempt to communicate with Pensacola or to organize any residual loyalty within the town. When the rebel government allowed him, weeks later, to depart for New Orleans, he went quietly. He filed no official protest with Spanish authorities. He sought no court-martial to clear his name. The record indicates he returned to private life. For Delassus, the fall of Fort San Carlos was not a military defeat. It was the final administrative formality in a transfer of power that had already occurred on the ground, in the clearings and fields of the Felicianas.

The broader collapse was mirrored in Pensacola. Governor Vicente Folch had received Delassus’s increasingly desperate requests for troops. Folch’s own correspondence held in the Archivo General de Indias, reveals a parallel paralysis. He wrote to Cuba for men and money. The replies were delayed, then noncommittal. He planned a relief expedition for Baton Rouge but never gave the order to embark. In a letter to a colleague dated October 10, 1810—after news of the fort’s capture had reached him—Folch concluded that a relief force would be “useless.” The province, he wrote was “lost to the influx.” His inaction was not negligence; it was the recognition of a logistical and political impossibility. The Spanish crown, fighting for its own survival in Europe had no resources to spare for a distant, lightly populated strip of Gulf coast. The empire’s reach had exceeded its grasp.

Delassus’s quiet surrender, therefore was the logical endpoint of a chain of failures that stretched from the pine barrens of West Florida to the royal court in Madrid. He was not a villain or a hero. Was a bureaucrat, presiding over an empty file drawer. The rebels did not defeat a Spanish army. They simply walked into a vacuum and announced it was now a republic. Delassus’s decision to change his clothes before the shooting started was the most honest gesture of the morning. An acknowledgment that the role of Spanish governor had already become a costume, and the performance was over.

The convention’s delegates, having received Thomas’s dispatch from the fort did not wait for a formal summons. They rode into Baton Rouge on the morning of September 24, a column of planters and landowners on horseback, their saddlebags stuffed with papers. They gathered not in the fort—which remained a military precinct, but in the largest public room available: the courthouse. A simple wooden structure near the river landing, its Spanish coat of arms still carved above the door. The records of this first provisional session are sparse, but the minutes that survive show a body moving with deliberate, procedural speed.

John Rhea presided. His first motion, passed unanimously was to formally declare the convention the “Supreme Legislative and Executive Council of the Republic of West Florida.” The title was grandiose, but its immediate powers were practical. The council’s initial orders, entered into the record that day were exercises in controlled continuity. All Spanish laws and judicial procedures were to remain in force. All Spanish civil officials—justices of the peace, clerks, surveyors, were invited to retain their posts if they swore an oath to the new republic. This was not revolution as upheaval; it was revolution as administrative takeover. The goal was to keep the machinery of daily life—property disputes, debt collection, marriage licenses, running without interruption. Panic was a greater enemy than Spain.

The second order addressed the Spanish military prisoners. Governor Delassus and his soldiers were to be “paroled on their honor.” They could reside within the district under loose guard, but they must surrender all public arms and pledge not to bear arms against the republic. The council authorized a small stipend for their subsistence, drawn from the seized Spanish treasury. This was a calculated gesture of civility, but also of security. It discouraged desperate resistance and signaled to the wider population—particularly the wary Creole and Spanish residents, that the transition would not be a bloodbath.

The council’s most pressing task was revenue. A government, even a provisional one required money. On September 25, Rhea appointed a three-man committee to inventory the contents of Fort San Carlos’s treasury and powder magazine. The findings documented in a ledger now held at the Louisiana State Archives sobered. The cash box contained slightly over two hundred Spanish dollars. The powder was damp, the muskets old. The real wealth of the district was not in the fort; it was in the plantations and stores of the men sitting in the courthouse. That afternoon, the council passed a resolution “authorizing the receipt of voluntary contributions for the support of the Government and the Guard.” It was, in essence, a request for the rebels to fund their own rebellion. Subscription lists were circulated. The amounts pledged, according to surviving personal account books from delegates like William Barrow and John H. Johnson were modest but immediate. They bought flour, pork, and powder.

Legitimacy, however required more than patrols and payrolls. It required a public declaration. The document drafted in the courthouse on September 26 was not the detailed constitution that would come later. A broadside, a statement of cause and intent. Titled “Declaration of the Independence of West Florida,” it was set in type at a small print shop in the town and posted beside the courthouse door. The text, preserved in several contemporary newspapers, listed the grievances: the failure of Spanish protection, the denial of rights, the “necessity” that had driven them to assume “the station to which they are entitled.” It was signed by John Rhea as president of the convention and attested by the clerk. Its language was derivative of 1776, but its audience was specific: the inhabitants of the four districts, and, crucially, the government of the United States.

By nightfall on the 26th, the mechanics of a state, however rudimentary turned. Courts would open the next day under the old Spanish codes. Militiamen stood watch. A flag—a single white star on a blue field hastily sewn, was raised over the fort. The Spanish prisoners ate their allotted rations. In the courthouse, the council adjourned, having established a committee to draft a proper constitution and set a date for elections. The rebellion had moved from the fog-shrouded assault on a wooden fort to the dry, paper-filled work of building something meant to last. The violence was over. The accounting had begun.

John Rhea, as president of the convention, performed the functions of a chief magistrate by default. He signed orders, received reports, and presided over the council sessions. But his authority was procedural, deriving from the chair, not from a constitutional office. The distinction mattered. When a dispute arose between two planters over the seizure of a stray horse by a militiaman, Rhea could refer it to the council for judgment. He could not issue a binding decree himself. One system was designed for deliberation, not dispatch. In a crisis, a rumor of Spanish troops marching from Mobile, a panic over supplies, deliberation could be fatal.

The council minutes from late September and early October, preserved in the Louisiana State Archives, show a body aware of this fragility. The entries shift from bold proclamations to worried practicality. On September 28, the council authorized the purchase of “one hundred bushels of corn” for the guard. On October 1, it ordered a committee to “inquire into the state of the public arms and report with speed.” On October 3, it resolved to send “agents of confidence” to the outlying districts of St. Tammany and St. Helena to “secure their adherence” to the republic. Each action was a reactive patch on a structure that lacked a central pillar.

The solution was embedded in their first declaration. The September 26 broadside had stated the people’s right to “institute a new Government.” The convention now had to fulfill that promise with a specific framework. A constitutional committee, chaired by Rhea began work on October 5. Their model was the American federal system, but scaled down and simplified for a population of perhaps ten thousand free inhabitants. The draft they produced, as reprinted in the Mississippi Messenger in November, established a bicameral legislature, a judiciary, and—critically, a single executive officer titled “President.” His powers were defined: he would be commander-in-chief of the military have the power to fill vacancies in offices, and execute the laws passed by the legislature. He could not make law. He could not levy taxes. His role was to give the republic a face and a functioning hand.

Elections were set for the first Monday in November. The process, outlined in the council’s October 12 resolution was straightforward. Free white male inhabitants of the four districts would vote for senators and representatives. Those elected legislators would then convene in Baton Rouge and choose the president from among their own number or any other qualified citizen. Its system was indirect, a filter against pure populism. It ensured the president would be a man already acceptable to the propertied class that controlled the rebellion.

In the interim, the executive vacuum shaped decisions. Correspondence with the United States, the republic’s hoped-for protector was handled cautiously. When a letter arrived from Governor William C. C. Claiborne of the Orleans Territory, expressing “interest” in the situation, the council debated for two days before authorizing Rhea to send a noncommittal reply. They needed a president to speak with legitimate authority. Military strategy was also hamstrung. Philemon Thomas argued for sending the guard to secure the Perdido River boundary, the eastern edge of their claim. The council, lacking a commander-in-chief to give definitive orders, deferred the decision. Initiative bled away into committee reports.

One man who would fill the office was not yet in Baton Rouge. Fulwar Skipwith, the Virginia-born planter and former diplomat was at his plantation in the Feliciana district, monitoring events. He had been a quiet supporter of the convention, but not a frontline actor in the assault or the initial council work. His qualifications, however were precisely what the moment required: he had diplomatic experience in France, understood international law, possessed personal wealth, and carried the demeanor of an established statesman. In a republic born from a frontier raid, he offered the appearance of settled legitimacy.

On November 19, the newly elected legislature convened in the Baton Rouge courthouse. Their first order of business, recorded in the journal of the senate was the election of a president. The vote was not unanimous, but it was decisive. Fulwar Skipwith was chosen. They sent a rider to his plantation with the news. The republic, after fifty-seven days of provisional rule, finally had its head of state. The rider also carried a second, more urgent message: the president-elect was requested to come to Baton Rouge with all practicable speed. The paperwork of independence was complete. The work of preserving it, against the pressures already bearing down from Washington and New Orleans, now began.

Fulwar Skipwith received the legislative delegation at his plantation house on the afternoon of November 20. The formal notification of his election as president, along with the urgent summons to Baton Rouge found a man who was neither surprised nor eager. At forty-six, Skipwith was a figure of settled habits and diminished fortune, a Virginian by birth who had seen the inner workings of European power and the chaos of Atlantic revolution firsthand. His diplomatic career documented in State Department archives and his own later memoir had been a study in precipitous falls. Appointed by President Thomas Jefferson as consul general to France in 1801, he had served in Paris during the Consulate, negotiating commercial treaties and aiding stranded American merchants. The collapse of the Peace of Amiens and the resumption of war between Britain and France stranded him without funds or instructions. He spent his own money to support the consulate, a debt the U.S. Government would never fully reimburse. By 1809, he was back in America, his personal finances shattered, seeking a fresh start on the volatile frontier of West Florida.

He found it in the Feliciana district, where he purchased a plantation called “Montecino” and attempted to rebuild his wealth through cotton and land speculation. The political ferment of 1810 did not initially draw him as a firebrand. His name appears not among the raiders at Fort San Carlos nor the signers of the first fiery proclamations. According to the minutes of the St. John’s Plains convention, he was elected as a delegate from Feliciana, a respected voice, not a leader. His correspondence from that summer held by the Louisiana Historical Association, reveals a pragmatic concern for stability and legal process. He viewed the convention not as a vehicle for independence per se, but as a necessary instrument to fill the administrative void left by Spain, preferably as a prelude to American annexation.

His acceptance of the presidency, therefore was an act of duty, not ambition. In a letter to a cousin in Virginia written shortly after the election, he described the office as “a weighty charge, undertaken from necessity, not choice.” The republic he was asked to lead was a patchwork of pressures. To the east, Spanish forces in Mobile and Pensacola watched with hostile intent. To the west, American officials in New Orleans viewed the rebellion with a mixture of opportunity and alarm. Internally, the republic’s finances were a fiction, its military a volunteer militia, its authority untested beyond the immediate vicinity of Baton Rouge. Skipwith’s training was in statecraft, not frontier brawling. He understood that the Lone Star Republic existed in a legal and diplomatic limbo that only recognition could solve.

He arrived in Baton Rouge on November 29. The account of his inauguration, published in the Republican newspaper of St. Francisville was brief. He took the oath of office in the courthouse before the assembled legislature, pledging to “preserve, protect, and defend the independence and constitution of the Republic of West Florida.” There was no parade. No cannon salute. The ceremony lasted twenty minutes. Afterwards, he delivered a short address. No full transcript survives, but contemporary summaries note he stressed the provisional nature of the government, its desire for “union with the American family,” and the paramount importance of maintaining civil order. His tone was sober, managerial. Was not proclaiming a new dawn; he accepted a difficult assignment.

His principles became clear in his first official actions. He moved into a rented house near the fort, not the governor’s residence, maintaining a symbolic distance from the captured seat of Spanish power. He reviewed the financial ledgers and found them bleak. The “voluntary contributions” dryed up. The only substantial asset was the public land of the former Spanish domain. One of his first proposals to the legislature was a plan for its orderly sale to generate revenue, a systematic, legal approach to solvency.

Skipwith’s defining characteristic, evident in the diplomatic dispatches from his Paris years was a formalism that bordered on rigidity. He believed in protocols, in the sanctity of agreements, in the symbols of sovereignty. This would soon collide with the blunt realities of American expansion. Within weeks of his inauguration, President James Madison’s proclamation of October 27—ordering the annexation of West Florida, became operational. American troops under Governor William C. C. Claiborne movved north from New Orleans. Skipwith’s reaction to this looming takeover documented in his later protest to Claiborne was not to deny the inevitability of union, but to protest the manner of it. He viewed Madison’s proclamation as a violation of the republic’s hard-won sovereignty. He believed the proper course was negotiation between two legitimate governments: a treaty of accession, not a military occupation.

When Claiborne’s forces finally approached Baton Rouge in early December, Skipwith’s principled stance would face its ultimate test. He could rally the guard for a futile, bloody resistance in defense of a constitutional abstraction, or he could capitulate to superior force and see the republic erased by fiat. His choice would reveal the central irony of his presidency. Fulwar Skipwith, the diplomat had finally attained the executive authority he was trained to wield, only to discover that in the rough calculus of the frontier, such authority was the most fragile commodity of all.

The flag was the final, necessary act of translation. It turned a local rebellion over taxes and land titles into a visible republic. The exact design was less important than its existence. Descriptions from eyewitnesses, like the merchant John W. Leonard whose account was published in the Natchez Chronicle, agree on the basics: a single white, five-pointed star centered on a field of blue. Some said the blue was deep navy, others a lighter shade. The star was cut from cotton or silk. Sewn, likely by the wives or daughters of the militiamen, in the days immediately following the fort’s capture. Its symbolism was intentionally derivative. It echoed the “bonnie blue flag” of Scottish independence and, more pointedly, the single star on the flag of the short-lived Republic of West Florida’s spiritual predecessor, the Trans-Oconee Republic, a failed settler state carved from Creek territory in the 1790s. For the Anglo-American settlers, it was a familiar emblem of frontier self-assertion.

The flag also performed a critical piece of political communication to the two powers watching from the sidelines. For Spain, the lone star was a deliberate insult, a formal rejection of royal authority. It transformed the rebels from troublesome subjects into a belligerent state, however nascent. This was a calculated provocation, making reconciliation impossible and forcing Madrid to either recognize the republic or conquer it, a conquest for which it had no means. For the United States, however, the signal was one of aspiration and affinity. The star was an echo of the American national emblem. A plea for recognition and, ultimately, for incorporation. In the geopolitical code of the era, raising a flag was the foundational act of sovereignty. By doing so, the convention was not governing; it presented itself as a legitimate entity with which other nations could—and should, treat.

The practical work of the Supreme Council in the courthouse and the symbolic work of the flag over the fort were two halves of the same. The council minutes dealt in bushels of corn and oaths of office. The flag dealt in identity and allegiance. One managed the present; the other staked a claim on the future. Together, they created a feedback loop of legitimacy. Every resolution passed, every court held under the republic’s authority made the flag seem less like a piece of cloth and more like the symbol of a real government. Conversely, every day the flag flew unmolested, it lent gravity to the council’s proclamations.

John Rhea understood this symbiosis. As presiding officer, he ensured that official documents now bore the heading “Republic of West Florida” beneath a printed seal that featured—inevitably, a single star. Philemon Thomas ordered the flag replicated and distributed to militia outposts along the Pearl River, turning guard posts into territorial markers. The symbol began to migrate into the daily life of the district. Within a week, merchants’ invoices and plantation ledgers began dating entries “Year 1 of the Republic.” The flag, as an idea outpaced the fragile institutions it represented.

This was the republic’s central, precarious achievement in those first October days. It had manufactured, through a combination of controlled force, procedural diligence, and potent symbolism, the aura of an established state. It had created facts on the ground and in the mind. The Lone Star over Baton Rouge was a beacon, but not in the romantic sense. A navigational marker, telling the inhabitants which way the political currents flowwed, and signaling to the United States that the channel into West Florida was now clear. The rebellion had seized a fort. The flag claimed a country. The stillness from Madrid and the keen, calculating attention from Washington proved the message had been received.
