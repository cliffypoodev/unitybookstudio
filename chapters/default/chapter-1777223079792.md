Lysander reclined on a new cushion stuffed with Egyptian goose down and tried to enjoy the afternoon quiet. It was the wrong kind of quiet. The house, a sprawling villa of imported marble and brilliant white stucco overlooking the Lechaeum Road, was never silent. Usually, it thrummed with the commerce of his father’s life: the clink of abacus beads from the counting room, the murmur of agents from Alexandria and Rome in the atrium, the rhythmic scrape of the stone polisher’s tools on the peristyle floor. Today, the only sound was the distant, fretful call of a dove from the garden. It was the quiet of absence. His father’s absence.

Marcus, son of Gaius, merchant of Corinth, was at the fuller’s workshop again.

Lysander took a slow sip of unmixed wine. The clay cup was cheap, a plain thing from the kitchen, not the Samian ware he preferred. He had chosen it deliberately, a small, private rebellion against the oppressive perfection of the house. A hairline crack ran from the rim halfway down. He traced it with his thumb. His tutor, a dried-up Athenian with breath like old parchment, had once called him a man of “studied indolence.” Lysander had taken it as a compliment. It was a skill to cultivate boredom so perfectly it became a kind of art. He had mastered the drape of his tunic, the angle of his repose, the languid wave that dismissed a servant. He had everything a young Corinthian of means could want: a generous allowance, a fast horse, invitations to the right symposia. He felt nothing about any of it except a vague, persistent pressure behind his eyes.

A slave girl—Thracian, he thought, by the sharp cut of her cheekbones—entered to trim the wicks of the lamps. She moved with a liquid grace, her bare feet whispering on the mosaic of Dionysus triumphant. She didn’t look at him. He watched the line of her neck, the way her simple tunic fell from her shoulder. There was a story there, probably. A village burned, a father sold for debt. He used to invent such stories for them. Lately, he couldn’t muster the energy. She was just a shape moving through his father’s expensive silence.

The problem was the workshop. Not the fact of it—Marcus owned shares in tanneries, potteries, three shipping syndicates. A fuller’s operation was small change. The problem was that Marcus went there. In person. In the afternoon heat, when respectable men of his station were at the baths or napping. He came home smelling not of incense and oil, but of lye and cheap wool and something else—a scent of close, unwashed bodies and fervent talk.

“He listens to them,” the house steward, a pinched-lipped Cappadocian named Diodorus, had confided yesterday, his voice hushed with scandal. “These... people. Jews, mostly. Some Greeks. Freedmen. A woman who sells fish. They meet in the back room. They talk of a dead Judean prophet and share a common meal. Your father pays the rent.”

Lysander had laughed, a short, brittle sound. “He’s funding a philosophical club. An eccentricity. He’ll grow bored.”

Diodorus had merely inclined his head, his expression saying what his loyalty forbade him to speak: Marcus was not a man of eccentricities. He was a man of ledgers and measured risk. This was different.

The dove called again. A single, repeated note. It sounded like a question.

Lysander set the cracked cup down too hard. A bead of dark wine slid down the side. He stood, the movement abrupt, unsettling the cushion. He needed to move. He paced the length of the andron, his sandals slapping softly on the geometric patterns of the floor. Past the fresco of the sea god, past the bronze statue of a discus thrower his father had won in a bet. Everything here was acquired, traded, calculated. A testament to good taste and better credit. It was a world of surfaces, polished and predictable. His father had built it. Now he was spending his afternoons in a room that likely stank of urine and desperation.

Why?

The question was a pebble in his sandal. He couldn’t ignore it.

He found himself at the door of his father’s study. It was ajar. He pushed it open. The room was neat, austere. A large desk of citrus wood. Scroll racks sorted by trade route. A locked chest for contracts. On the desk, beside a stylus and a wax tablet covered in shipping figures, lay a single sheet of papyrus. It was out of place. Marcus kept his correspondence filed away.

Lysander stepped inside. The air smelled of cedar oil and papyrus. He picked up the sheet. The Greek was simple, the hand unpracticed.

To Marcus, a friend and patron, greetings in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ. We thank you for your generosity. Your gift feeds the brothers and sisters who are in want. We remember you always in our prayers. Peace to you and your household. Signed, Prisca, Aquila, and the church in Corinth.

The word “church”—ekklesia—was odd in this context. It meant assembly, a gathering of citizens, not a gathering of fullers and fishwives. The salutation was stranger. “In the name of the Lord Jesus Christ.” Not “Zeus the Savior” or “the Emperor” or “Fortune.” A specific, foreign name. A dead Judean’s name.

His eyes caught on the final phrase. Your household.

He was part of that household. An item in an inventory of souls, included in the prayers of people he’d never met. The idea was intrusive. It made his skin prickle, as if a stranger had touched him in his sleep. He dropped the papyrus back on the desk as if it were hot. It left a faint, greasy smudge on his thumb. Cheap ink.

This was the first twist, the quiet one: his father wasn’t just a patron dabbling in a curious cult. He was a named friend. His household was being prayed for by name. This was not a financial diversion. It was a relationship.

He left the study, closing the door with a soft click. The hallway seemed longer, the frescoes garish. He needed air. He walked out into the peristyle garden. The colonnade offered shade. A fountain burbled in the center, a marble nymph pouring an endless stream from an urn. The sound usually soothed him. Now it just sounded like wasted water.

He heard the gate from the street groan open. His father’s voice, low and familiar, and another voice—rougher, accented—in reply. Lysander melted back into the shadow of a column.

Marcus entered the courtyard first. He looked ordinary: a man in his late fifties, hair greying at the temples, his features etched by wind and calculation. But his posture was different. The usual merchant’s hunch, the weight of a thousand deals, was gone. He stood straighter. He was listening to the man beside him.

The man was perhaps forty, with the thick shoulders and weathered hands of a manual laborer. A sailor, maybe, or a dockworker. His tunic was plain wool, patched at the elbow. He was speaking with an intensity that made his hands chop the air. “...and so I told him, ‘If you have food and see your brother hungry, what good is your faith?’ He had no answer. No answer at all.”

Marcus nodded, not the nod of a master to a servant, but of one man considering another’s words. “It is a difficult teaching, Cephas. Especially here.”

Cephas. A common enough name. But the way his father said it held a weight.

“All teaching worth anything is difficult,” Cephas said. He had a Galilean accent, rounding the edges of his Greek. He clapped Marcus on the shoulder. A laborer, touching his father. A merchant worth hundreds of thousands of sesterces. And Marcus did not flinch. He smiled, a small, private thing Lysander hadn’t seen in years.

“Next First Day,” Marcus said.

“We will expect you. Bring your questions.” Cephas’s gaze, sharp and dark, swept the garden. It passed over the nymph, the manicured herbs, the expensive shade. It lingered for a fraction of a second on the shadow where Lysander stood. Did he see him? The man’s eyes showed no recognition, only a mild, impersonal assessment of the wealth around him. Then he turned and left, his sandals scuffing on the flagstones.

Marcus stood for a moment, watching the empty gateway. He brought a hand to his face and rubbed his eyes. It was a gesture of fatigue, but also of something else—a kind of saturation, as if he’d drunk from a cup too full.

The second twist, colder and sharper: this wasn’t a distant charity. His father was a student. He brought “questions” to a fuller’s workshop. He accepted the touch of a workingman as a brother.

Lysander stepped out of the shadows. The movement was too loud in the quiet courtyard.

Marcus turned. The look on his face—open, unguarded, still half in another world—vanished. The merchant’s mask slid back into place, but not before Lysander saw it, not before he recognized the expression. It was the same look Lysander had seen on the faces of men coming out of the mysteries at Eleusis: haunted, illuminated, utterly separate.

“Lysander. I didn’t see you.”

“Evidently.” Lysander kept his voice light, bored. “Who was your... friend?”

“A business associate.” The lie was smooth, automatic.

“He seemed passionate about hunger. And faith.”

Marcus’s eyes narrowed. He walked to the fountain, dipped his hands in the basin, rinsed them. A practical gesture, washing away the dust of the city. Or perhaps the touch of the common world. “He is a man of strong convictions. They are building a community here. I find it... interesting.”

“A community of fullers and fish-sellers. How novel.” Lysander leaned against the column. “Praying to a dead criminal. Is that the interesting part?”

His father went very still. He dried his hands on the edge of his tunic, a shockingly common thing to do. When he looked up, his gaze was direct, stripped of its usual mercantile cunning. “Do not,” he said, the words measured and cold, “speak of what you do not understand.”

The rebuke was a slap. It wasn’t the words, but the tone. His father had criticized him before—for laziness, for extravagance—but always with a thread of exasperated affection, the disappointment of a man who expected little and got less. This was different. This was a boundary drawn. This is mine. You are outside.

The third twist, the one that settled in his gut like a stone: this was no passing fancy. This was a line dividing his father’s life, and by extension, his own. He was on the wrong side of it.

Lysander pushed off the column. The studied indolence was a shell around him now, brittle and thin. “My apologies. I wouldn’t dream of it.” He gave a shallow, perfect bow, the kind he used to insult politicians at parties. “I’m for the baths. The atmosphere here seems a little close.”

He walked away, feeling his father’s eyes on his back. He didn’t go to the baths. He went to his room, a spacious chamber overlooking the garden. He stood at the window. The dove was gone. The fountain nymph poured her endless water.

He knew where the fuller’s workshop was. Down near the docks, in a warren of streets where the smell of salt and sewage mixed. A place he would never normally go. A place his father now frequented.

A mix of emotions churned in him, none of them clean. Disgust, yes. A hot embarrassment at the thought of his friends hearing of this. But underneath that, a sharper, more unsettling thing: a curiosity that felt like an itch under his skin. What was in that room? What words were spoken that could straighten his father’s spine and make him lie to his own son?

He couldn’t ask. The wall was up.

So he would look. He would see for himself. Not for understanding, but for ammunition. For the leverage to pull his father back from this embarrassing folly. That’s what he told himself as he watched the afternoon light gild the rooftops of Corinth. He would go to the workshop, discreetly. He needed to see the squalor, the fanaticism, the proof of his father’s decline.

He needed to know what, exactly, he was supposed to be praying to.

The smell hit him first, two streets away. Not the lye and urine of the fuller’s vats, but something earthier: sawdust, olive oil, the sharp tang of human sweat baked by the sun. The workshop was a low, rectangular building of sun-bleached brick, wedged between a potter’s stall and a cobbler’s. A simple wooden sign, carved with a fish, hung above the door. No name. Just the outline, crude and purposeful.

Lysander had chosen a plain, dun-colored tunic from a household slave’s trunk. He’d dirtied his sandals in the alley behind the villa. He felt ridiculous, a player in a cheap mime. He lingered in the cobbler’s doorway, watching. People arrived in ones and twos. A burly man with a shipwright’s calloused hands. A woman with a basket of mending, her face lined from squinting at thread. A young couple, the girl’s hand resting on the swell of her stomach. They greeted each other with a quiet word, a touch on the arm. No one looked wealthy. No one looked like they owned a villa on the Lechaeum Road.

He waited until a group of three entered, then fell in behind them, his head down. The doorway was low. He ducked inside.

The air was thick, warm, close. It smelled of bodies and cheap lamp oil and the faint, sweet scent of wine. The space was larger than he’d expected. Workbenches had been pushed against the walls, their surfaces scarred from tools. Wood shavings made a pale carpet over the packed-earth floor. In the center, perhaps thirty people sat on benches, stools, or directly on the ground. The light came from a few clay lamps, their flames painting restless shadows on the rough brick walls. It was the antithesis of his father’s marbled andron. It was all function, all use.

He slipped onto a bench near the back, behind a man with a neck like a bull. He kept his shoulders hunched, his gaze on his own hands.

They were singing.

It was not a formal hymn, not like the chants at the temple of Apollo. It was a simple melody, a rising and falling line in Aramaic. The voices were untrained, some off-key, but they wove together with a stubborn unity. A woman near the front had a clear, high voice that cut through the murmur. She closed her eyes as she sang. Her face, plain in the lamplight, was transformed by a kind of fierce joy. Lysander felt a prickle of irritation. It was unseemly. Emotion should be private, contained, a matter for the symposium and the wine cup, not shouted to a room of carpenters.

The song ended. A murmur of “Amen” rippled through the room, a soft exhale.

Cephas stood. He was at the front, leaning against a workbench. He looked more solid here, rooted. He scanned the room, his dark eyes missing nothing. They passed over Lysander, paused for a heartbeat, moved on. Did he recognize him? The shadow from the garden? Lysander’s pulse thudded in his throat. He studied a knot in the wood of the bench.

“Peace to you,” Cephas said. His Greek was still accented, but the words were clear, rounded stones dropped into the quiet.

“And to you,” the room responded. The response was automatic, communal.

“Last week,” Cephas began, not raising his voice, yet every ear leaned toward him, “we spoke of the seed falling on different soils.” He picked up a wood shaving from the bench, twisted it in his fingers. “Some of you asked, what is the thorny ground? What chokes the word?” He let the question hang. A fly buzzed near a lamp. Someone coughed. “The thorns are the cares of the world. The deceitfulness of riches. The desire for other things.” He said “riches” not with envy, but with a kind of practical pity, as if describing a cumbersome, foolish burden. “These things enter in. They grow up. They choke the word, and it becomes unfruitful.”

Lysander’s mouth went dry. The deceitfulness of riches. The desire for other things. He thought of the Samian ware cup he’d rejected for the cracked one, a choice that now seemed not like rebellion but another layer of the same obsession. He thought of his horse, his allowance, the precise drape of his tunic. Cares of the world. Desires. Was that all his life was? A carefully curated collection of thorns?

A man near the front, thin and nervous, raised a hand. “Teacher, how do we pull the thorns?”

Cephas smiled, a brief crack in his weathered face. “You don’t. You can’t. Your own hands will bleed. You let the sower do it. You sit in the good soil. You listen.” He tapped his own chest. “The word has power. It does the work. Your job is to be poor soil for the thorns. Let them find no purchase. Give them no water.”

It was nonsense. Mystical agricultural nonsense. Yet the way he said it held a terrible, simple logic. Lysander found his mind following the metaphor against his will. What was the good soil of his life? There was none. Just polished marble.

The talk shifted. They began to share. Not a philosophical debate, but reports from their lives. A dockworker spoke of refusing a bribe to look the other way at a pilfered crate. His voice shook. He’d needed that money. His son was sick. The room listened, not with judgment, but with a focused silence that felt heavier than any critique. When he finished, a woman—the fish-seller from his father’s description, he guessed—said, “My brother, we will care for your boy. Bring him to my house tomorrow. My sister knows herbs.”

No discussion. No voting. A statement of fact.

Another voice, young, female: “My mistress beat me for burning a loaf. I... I wished her dead. In my heart, I cursed her.” The confession was a whisper, swallowed by the room. “I have asked for forgiveness, but the anger is still there. Like a hot coal.”

Cephas nodded slowly. “Keep asking. The coal will cool. Forgiveness is a door. Your anger is your hand on the latch, refusing to turn it. You must choose to let go, even if your fingers ache.”

Lysander watched the girl. She was a household slave, perhaps fifteen. She wept silently, her shoulders trembling. A man beside her, old enough to be her grandfather, put a gnarled hand on her back. He said nothing. He just left it there, a steady weight.

Something hot and sour rose in Lysander’s throat. It was not disgust. It was something more dangerous: a recognition of a need so raw it was obscene. These people were exposing their failures, their fears, their petty hatreds, and the room did not recoil. It absorbed them. It offered not solutions, but presence. A hand on a back. A promise of herbs. It was appallingly intimate. He felt like a voyeur at a surgery, watching organs laid bare under a blunt knife.

Then they brought out the food. A simple loaf of coarse barley bread. A clay jug of wine. Cephas took the loaf, raised it. He did not look at the ceiling. He looked at the people. “On the night he was betrayed,” he said, the words falling into a silence so deep Lysander could hear the sputter of the lamp wick, “the Lord Jesus took bread, gave thanks, broke it, and said, ‘This is my body, broken for you. Do this to remember me.’”

He broke the loaf. The sound was crisp, final.

A server came around with a basket of pieces. When the basket reached Lysander’s row, the bull-necked man in front of him took a piece, then held the basket out to Lysander. Their eyes met. The man’s face was broad, kindly, marked by smallpox scars. He gave a small, encouraging nod.

Lysander panicked. He shook his head, a quick, sharp motion. He looked away.

The man’s expression didn’t change. He didn’t insist. He simply moved the basket along. But the refusal echoed in Lysander’s bones. He had drawn a line. I am not one of you. I will not take your broken bread.

The wine followed. The same cup, passed from lip to lip. He watched an old woman with a palsy in her hands guide the cup to her mouth with trembling care. He saw the young slave girl drink after her mistress’s husband, the social order of the city obliterated by a shared rim of clay. His father, he knew, would take that cup, would drink after the fish-seller, the dockworker, the patched-elbow Galilean.

The ritual ended. They began to talk again, but the tone had changed. It was lighter. They shared news: a brother traveling from Ephesus, a baby due in the house of Chloe. They took up a collection for the family of the dockworker. Coins clinked into a wooden box—asses, dupondii, no gold. It was a pittance. It was a fortune.

Lysander could not breathe. The heat, the smell, the palpable web of connection in the room pressed on him. It was a net. He felt its threads brushing against him, trying to snag. This was what had straightened his father’s spine. Not an idea, but a body. A breathing, sweating, broken body of people who believed a dead man had conquered death and then told them to share their bread and their burdens. It was insane. It was the most terrifyingly coherent thing he had ever witnessed.

He had to get out.

He stood up. The movement was too sudden. The bench scraped loudly on the floor. Conversations hiccupped. Heads turned. Cephas, across the room, looked directly at him. This time, there was no question. The Galilean’s gaze was knowing, patient, and utterly implacable. It saw the fine hair, the soft hands, the panic in his eyes. It saw the villa and the cracked cup and the studied indolence. It saw the thorns.

Lysander turned and pushed his way toward the door. He stumbled on the threshold, out into the cool, blessedly empty twilight. The street air, ripe with garbage and salt, was like a slap. He gasped it in. He walked, then ran, his sandals slapping the uneven stones. He didn’t stop until the workshop street was three turns behind him, swallowed by the labyrinth of the lower city.

He leaned against a wall, chest heaving. He was sweating through the slave’s tunic. His mind was a riot of images: the girl’s trembling shoulders, the broken loaf, the knowing eyes of Cephas. The quiet, devastating question hung in the air he’d fled: What good soil do you have?

He fumbled in the fold of his tunic for a handkerchief, his fingers clumsy. Instead, his hand closed on a small, tight scroll. He pulled it out. It was his father’s, a private memo of accounts due, in Marcus’s own shorthand. He must have picked it up from the study desk without thinking. A piece of his father’s world, its numbers and calculations now absurdly trivial.

He looked at it for a long moment. Then he dropped it. It landed in the gutter, near a trickle of foul water. He left it there, a discarded ledger in a street of need.

He began the long walk uphill, toward the marble and the silence. The pressure behind his eyes was no longer vague. It was a specific, pointed ache. He had gone for ammunition. He had found a mirror. In its dim, unflinching glass, he saw not his father’s folly, but the precise and terrifying outline of his own emptiness.

The study was his sanctuary. Scrolls in cedarwood racks, a wax tablet for accounts, a single good chair of worn leather. The lamp on the desk was bronze, shaped like an owl. Its light pooled on the polished wood, a small, contained sun. Lysander sat in the chair but did not light the lamp. Dawn was a grey smear at the window. He had not slept. The smell of the workshop clung to the back of his throat, a ghost of sweat and coarse bread.

He had come here for proof. Not of faith, but of finance. His father’s private strongbox was set into the wall behind a hanging tapestry of the siege of Troy. The wool was thick, dusty. He knew the combination. His eighteenth birthday gift: access to the family numbers. A test he had failed by disinterest.

His fingers found the brass mechanism. The click was satisfyingly solid. The door swung inward.

He expected ledgers, coin bags, perhaps a few promissory notes. The box was deep. He pulled the contents out onto the desk.

Three ledgers, bound in calfskin. A heavy purse that clinked with gold aurei. And beneath, a stack of papyrus sheets, folded and sealed.

He opened the top ledger. The columns were in his father’s neat, vertical hand. Shipments of Corinthian bronze to Brundisium. Olive oil contracts for Rome. The mundane poetry of commerce. He flipped toward the back. The entries changed.

Not destinations or commodities, but names. For the family of Gaius the dockworker, for medicine. To the common purse, for the rent of the workshop. For the widow Chloe, for her needs. The sums were not vast, but they were consistent. A steady, quiet hemorrhage of silver denarii.

The second ledger was worse. It recorded the disposition of assets. A warehouse near the Isthmus, sold six months prior. The buyer was listed as a Syrian merchant. The proceeds, according to a note in the margin, had been “transferred to the brothers in Jerusalem.” A shipment of Aegean marble, commissioned for a bathhouse in Corinth, had been cancelled. The forfeited deposit was marked Gift.

Lysander’s breath shallowed. This was not charity. This was dismantling. Piece by piece, his father was liquidating the machinery of their wealth and funneling it into that dim, murmuring room.

The third ledger was different. Thinner. Its pages contained no numbers, only words. He recognized his father’s script, but the language was strange, personal.

Cephas spoke today of the camel and the needle’s eye. He looked at me. I felt the needle. It was not a metaphor. It was a physical sensation, a stitch in the fabric of my soul.

Tell me of the soil in Ephesus.
