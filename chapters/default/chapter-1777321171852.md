The motel air conditioner coughed up a lungful of mildewed air directly into Mary's face. She batted at the vent with one hand, the other tapping an impatient rhythm on the keyboard. The screen flickered, casting her face in a sickly blue glow.

"Tell me again why we're not just torching the place?" Rex's tail thumped against the wall, dislodging a framed print of a sad clown fishing.

Jesus leaned over Mary's shoulder, his breath warm against her ear. "Because arson is statistically less effective than blackmail when dealing with televangelists."

"Also, we don't know where the well is," Mary muttered. The login screen taunted her with a spinning crucifix cursor. Eden's encryption wasn't just firewalls and passwords—it was riddles wrapped in scripture, dipped in sacrilege.

Rex snorted. "You're hacking the Vatican's evil twin with a laptop that has a 'Jesus Saves' sticker on it. The irony is palpable."

"Don't say that." Mary jabbed the enter key. The screen flashed red. AGAIN.

Jesus drummed his fingers on the phantom cross-prints in his palms—his tell for when he was about to suggest something profoundly stupid. "What if we—"

"No."

"—prayed for the password?"

Mary swiveled in her chair so fast it squealed like a sinner at a tent revival. "You want me to ask God for the login credentials to a blackmail database?"

"It worked for the loaves and fishes."

"That was catering, not cybersecurity!"

The AC chose that moment to spit out an ice chunk that landed in Mary's lap with a wet plop. She stared at it. Rex stared at it. Jesus, the bastard, grinned.

Mary flung the ice at Jesus's head. He ducked. It hit the wall with enough force to leave a dent shaped like Florida.

"Okay, new plan." She cracked her knuckles. "I'm brute-forcing the theological angle. If their security is based on scripture, their weakness is selective literalism."

Rex's reactor cycled through colors like a disco ball at the world's worst rave. "That sentence made my brain hurt."

"Good." Mary typed faster. "Pain means it's working."

The screen flickered. A prompt appeared: WHAT IS TRUTH?

Mary didn't hesitate. She typed: WHATEVER GETS THE RATINGS.

The screen dissolved into a cascade of files.

Jesus whistled. "You just hacked the kingdom of heaven with a bumper sticker slogan."

"It's not heaven." Mary's stomach dropped as she scrolled. "It's confessionals."

Hundreds of files. Thousands. Each tagged with a name, a date, a sin. Politicians. Preachers. Celebrities. All neatly categorized by denomination, scandal type, and estimated hush-money value.

Rex leaned in, his reactor casting jagged shadows across the screen. "They've been recording confessions?"

"Not recording." Mary clicked open a file at random. A televangelist's face filled the screen, sweat glistening under the studio lights. "Live-streaming."

The video played. The man confessed to embezzling three million in donor funds to pay for his mistress's boob job. The time stamp showed it was recorded during a prime-time healing special.

Jesus rubbed his temples. "They broadcast people's confessions during miracle hours?"

"Worse." Mary opened another file. A senator sobbed about taking bribes from oil companies while the chyron below him read MIRACULOUS HEALING IN PROGRESS. "They edited out the sins and left the tears. Used the raw footage as proof the 'holy spirit was moving.'"

Rex's tail took out another section of drywall. "That's not just blackmail. That's sacramental."

Mary kept scrolling, her pulse pounding in her fingertips. The files were endless. A megachurch pastor's cocaine habit. A reality star's abortion.

She stopped.

A file labeled MERCY, SISTER blinked at her like a warning light.

Jesus went very still. "Don't."

Mary clicked it.

The video showed a younger Sister Mercy in a hospital gown, her face gaunt under the fluorescent lights. She wasn't preaching. She wasn't performing. She was whispering to someone off-camera, her hands shaking.

"They made me heal Nazis," she said, so quiet Mary had to turn the volume all the way up. "After the war. The Americans. They said if I could cure leprosy, I could cure… other things."

The camera panned to show a hospital bed. A withered old man in a faded SS uniform lay there, an IV dripping something thick and dark into his veins.

Sister Mercy reached out. Touched his forehead.

His skin smoothed. His hair darkened. His eyes flew open—bright blue and full of hate.

The video cut to static.

The motel room was silent except for the AC's death rattle.

Mary realized she was holding her breath. She let it out slowly. "Well. That explains the nunchuck thing."

Jesus made a noise like a stepped-on frog. "That's your takeaway?"

"I process horror with sarcasm. You process miracles with fish metaphors. We all have our things."

Rex's reactor hit a frequency that made Mary's fillings ache. "This isn't just blackmail. It's a blueprint. They're not just controlling people—they're recreating the conditions that broke them."

Mary minimized the file. Her hands didn't shake. She wouldn't let them. "Then we burn the blueprint."

Jesus frowned. "I thought you said arson—"

"Not the building." Mary pulled up a command prompt. "The data. We leak it all."

Rex's eye dimmed. "Including Sister Mercy's—"

"Especially hers." Mary typed faster, the keys clacking like gunfire. "If this gets out, Eden can't use it against her. Or anyone."

Jesus opened his mouth. Closed it. Nodded.

The progress bar filled. 10%. 20%.

A new alert popped up: UNAUTHORIZED ACCESS DETECTED.

Mary cursed. "They're locking us out."

Rex shouldered her aside, his claws a blur over the keyboard. "I speak binary and blasphemy."

The screen glitched. A new message appeared: WITNESS PROTECTION PROTOCOL ENGAGED.

All the files began to delete themselves.

Mary lunged for the power cord. "No no no—"

Too late. The screen went black.

CONFESSION IS GOOD FOR THE SOUL. BUT BAD FOR BUSINESS.

The laptop emitted a sound like a choir of angels being fed through a woodchipper. Then it died.

Mary stared at the smoking machine. "Well. That could've gone better."

Jesus picked up a bible from the nightstand—the Gideons were nothing if not optimistic—and flipped it open at random. He read: "'The truth will set you free.'"

Rex snatched the book and chucked it out the window. It hit something with a satisfying thud.

"Wrong verse," he growled. "Try 'Get thee behind me, Satan.'"

Outside, a car alarm started wailing.

Mary sighed. "Next time? We torch the place."

Mary stared at the dead laptop like it had personally betrayed her. Which, technically, it had. The motel room smelled like fried circuits and desperation.

Rex’s tail twitched in a metronome rhythm that meant he was doing math in his head. Bad sign. "They’ll triangulate our location in six minutes. Maybe four if they’ve upgraded their servers since the last time I hacked them."

"Which was?" Jesus asked, poking the laptop with a pen like it might bite.

"1947."

Mary groaned and dragged her hands down her face. "Great. So we’ve got the Roswell IT department hunting us. Fantastic."

The television flickered to life without anyone touching it. Static hissed, then resolved into a test pattern—a crucifix overlaid with a bar graph.

Jesus squinted. "Is that—"

"Eden’s logo," Rex confirmed. His reactor pulsed a warning red. "They’re broadcasting on every frequency. Including this one."

"Hello, seekers!" Her voice was syrup over shards of glass. "Are you feeling lost? Alone? Overwhelmed by the weight of your sins?"

Mary flipped the TV the bird. "Turn this crap off before I throw a shoe at it."

The woman’s smile didn’t waver. "Because here at Eden World Ministries, we believe in second chances! And third chances! And, depending on your donation tier, unlimited chances!"

Jesus made a strangled noise. "They’ve monetized repentance."

Rex’s claws flexed. "They’ve weaponized it."

The broadcast cut to a montage of tearful confessions—politicians, celebrities, a guy who looked suspiciously like the governor of Florida admitting to something involving an alligator and a baptismal font. Each clip ended with the same chyron: PURIFIED BY EDEN.

Mary’s stomach twisted. "They’re not just recording confessions. They’re editing them into ads."

The screen flickered again. This time, it showed a live feed of their motel room. From above.

Rex’s reactor hit a pitch that made Mary’s teeth vibrate. "They’ve got drones."

The televangelist’s voice oozed through the speakers. "Now, friends, let’s talk about trespassing. About prying into places you don’t belong." The camera zoomed in on Mary’s face. "About the sin of curiosity."

Jesus grabbed a lamp and hurled it at the TV. The screen shattered, but the voice kept talking through the wreckage.

The lights died. The AC choked out its last breath.

In the sudden dark, Rex’s reactor cast jagged shadows across the walls. "They’ve cut the power."

Mary’s pulse pounded in her throat. "No. They’ve cut our power."

Outside, car engines growled to life. Not one. Not two. A whole damn choir of them.

Jesus peered through the blinds. "Oh. That’s a lot of SUVs."

Mary yanked her bag onto her shoulder. "Back exit. Now."

Rex didn’t move. "They’ll have the perimeter covered."

"Then we go through them." Mary shoved the dead laptop into her bag. "I didn’t survive six years of Catholic school to get taken out by a televangelist’s goon squad."

The door burst open before she could reach it.

Not a goon. Not even human.

The thing in the doorway was seven feet tall, wrapped in what looked like a choir robe if a choir robe were made of razor wire and bad decisions. Its face was a smooth, featureless oval—no eyes, no mouth, just a single, pulsing red cross where its nose should’ve been.

It raised one hand. The sleeve fell back, revealing a forearm lined with syringe ports.

"Mary Magdalene," it intoned, voice like a dial-up modem screaming into the void. "You are hereby summoned for spiritual realignment."

Mary blinked. "Wow. That’s the worst pickup line I’ve ever heard."

Jesus grabbed her arm. "Run."

They ran.

The motel hallway was a blur of peeling wallpaper and flickering exit signs. Behind them, the thing’s footsteps didn’t sound like footsteps at all—more like a hundred rosary beads hitting the floor at once.

Rex took out a vending machine with his tail as they rounded the corner. "They’ve upgraded their enforcers."

Mary skidded into the stairwell. "No shit!"

The parking lot was worse. SUVs circled like sharks, headlights cutting through the humid dark. More of those robe-wrapped freaks stood between them and the only car that wasn’t actively trying to kill them—Mary’s ancient station wagon, its bumper sticker ("MY OTHER CAR IS A MIRACLE") peeling at the edges.

Jesus grabbed a fire extinguisher off the wall and tossed it to Rex. "Think fast."

Rex caught it in his teeth and crushed the tank like a beer can. White foam sprayed everywhere, filling the air with a chemical blizzard.

Mary didn’t wait. She bolted for the car, keys already in hand.

The engine turned over on the third try—a personal best. Jesus dove into the passenger seat. Rex landed in the back with a thud that bottomed out the suspension.

Mary stomped the gas. The wagon fishtailed, clipping the rear bumper of an SUV. Something exploded. She didn’t look back to see what.

The road ahead was dark. The road behind was full of headlights and righteous fury.

Rex leaned forward, his breath hot on Mary’s neck. "They’ll track the car."

"Then we ditch it." Mary wrenched the wheel, sending them skidding onto a side street.

She reached into her bag and pulled out the only thing she’d salvaged from the laptop: a single flash drive, still warm from the meltdown.

Jesus stared at it. "You got the files?"

"Not all of them." Mary grinned, wild and sharp. "Just the ones that matter."

The flash drive’s label, written in her messy scrawl, glowed faintly in the dashboard lights:

AM-1 BLUEPRINT. DO NOT PRAY OVER.

Rex’s reactor cycled through colors like a disco ball at the apocalypse. "You stole the messiah specs."

Mary tossed the drive into Jesus’s lap. "Merry Christmas. Now let’s go build us a god."

Behind them, the first SUV rounded the corner. Its horn blared the opening notes of "Amazing Grace."

Mary flipped the rearview mirror up so she wouldn’t have to watch the crash.

It came anyway.

The sound was almost musical.
