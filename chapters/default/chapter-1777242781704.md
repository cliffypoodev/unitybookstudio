The van coughed its way onto the main concourse of Eden World. The place was a monument to spiritual bankruptcy left to bake in the Florida sun. A flicker of movement above the ticket booths. He squinted.

He needed to find Mary. The plan, such as it was, involved her creating a ‘diversion’ with the fertilizer-and-pool-chemical explosives he’d helped her cobble together from his now-condemned trailer. He hadn’t asked for details. He’d seen the gleam in her eye and decided some mysteries were healthier left unsolved.

“We just got here! What is this? The plan was a diversion, not a… a nautical remodel.”

Lashed to the stern, between two worried-looking fiberglass giraffes, were six industrial-sized chlorine jugs, duct-taped together with bricks of fertilizer from Dutch’s stash. Wires ran from them to a chaotic nest of toggle switches mounted on a piece of plywood. “I’ve rigged the propellant charges. Sequential bursts. It’ll be like a popcorn popper of salvation.”

“I’m a conspiracy theorist with a leaky van! I don’t navigate assault arks!”

Dutch’s mind went blank. Then it served up a memory of grainy photos and panic. “Behind the Ten Commandments Mini-Golf. There’s a service road. It’s narrow. And it goes past the Den of the Cybernetic Apostles.”

“Even better. We can pick up strays.” She flipped a switch. The ark’s drivetrain whined like a tortured appliance. “Coming?”

He wasn’t. He was absolutely not. He had a van. He had a mostly working understanding of retreat. Then he looked back toward the main gate, where the silk-suited guard was now talking to two other silk-suited men. All three looked in his direction.

“‘We’?”

From the darkness behind PHILIP, pairs of red eyes ignited. Five more raptors clanked into the half-light. They fanned out, not in an attack formation, but like a committee. One, labeled ‘THOMAS,’ nudged a discarded beer can with its snout.

Two more took the starboard. PHILIP and BARTHOLOMEW scrambled onto the roof, flanking the plastic dove. They clung there, like grotesque, metallic barnacles.

The ark sank several inches on its suspension. The golf cart drivetrain groaned a metallic scream of protest.

“This is the worst idea I have ever had,” Mary said, with something like reverence. She slammed the ‘propellant’ toggle.

The first chlorine-fertilizer charge detonated with a WHUMP and a cloud of acrid yellow smoke. The ark shot forward like a scalded cat, the calliope music distorting into a demonic shriek.

Dutch clung to the plastic sheep, the wind tearing at his hair. They blasted past the mini-golf course, where a lone animatronic Moses stood pointing at a water hazard. They skidded onto a broader concourse, the main stage coming into view—a monstrosity of lights and scaffolding, surrounded by black-suited security.

Ahead, a barricade of park benches and metal sawhorses blocked the road. Three of Lux’s security guards stood behind it, rifles coming up. Their faces were a mixture of confusion and horror at the sight bearing down on them: a glowing ark playing circus music, covered in cybernetic dinosaurs, captained by a grinning woman in tactical gear.

The second charge went off, not for propulsion, but for effect. A shower of sparklers and glitter erupted from the ark’s smokestacks, along with a cloud of burning feathers she’d ripped from a nearby ‘Guardian Angel’ display. The glitter cloud enveloped the barricade.

Through the shimmering, fiery haze, Dutch saw the guards coughing, batting at the air. One of them sneezed violently.

Then they were upon them. The ark hit the sawhorses. They splintered. A bench went spinning. The guards dove for cover. A raptor—THOMAS—snatched a rifle from one man’s hands with delicate precision and tossed it into a fake topiary bush.

They were through. The stage was two hundred yards ahead, lit up like a UFO landing site.

Dutch let out a whoop that was half terror, half exhilaration. He turned to yell something at Mary, something about a victory lap, and saw her grin vanish.

She was looking past him, past the stage, into the dense, fake jungle of the park’s ‘Garden of Eden’ exhibit. Her knuckles were white on the steering wheel.

Dutch followed her gaze.

There, moving with a slow, tectonic silence between the plastic ferns and vinyl serpent trees, was a shadow. A vast, familiar silhouette that blotted out the rising sun. It moved on two legs, its head a crown of angular metal and dark scales. It was too big to be an animatronic. Too real.

It was Rex. And he was walking a slow, deliberate patrol around the base of the broadcast tower, his red scanner eye sweeping the ground in a methodical, unblinking pattern. The way a machine would. The way a good weapon would.

The ark’s calliope sputtered and died. The only sound was the grind of their wheels and the rhythmic, hydraulic clunk of a cyborg titan’s footstep in the middle distance.
