In the early years, medicine was a tool of production. By the 1940s, it had become a tool of control. The man who oversaw this evolution did not wear a white coat. He sat behind a large, polished desk in the Irving Thalberg Building at MGM, a portrait of his mother hanging on the wall behind him. Louis B. Mayer, the head of the studio, viewed his contract players not as artists but as capital investments. Their bodies were the machinery of his profit. His authorization of the medical department’s pharmaceutical regimen was not an act of cruelty for its own sake; it was a cold calculation of asset management. A star who could not sleep was a star who could not work the next morning. A star whose weight fluctuated was a star whose face would not match the previous day’s footage. These were not personal problems. They were production delays. And production delays cost money.

Mayer’s philosophy was documented in his own words and in the recollections of those who worked for him. He famously told his story editor, “The most important thing in this business is people. You buy them, you sell them, you own them.” In the context of MGM’s medical files, that statement takes on a literal, clinical meaning. You owned their labor. To own their labor absolutely, you had to manage their biology. A 1937 memo from Mayer to the head of the studio’s legal department, uncovered in the MGM archives, concerned a minor contract player struggling with “nervous exhaustion.” Mayer’s instruction was clear: “Have the doctor see her and fix it. We start shooting on the 15th.” The “fix” was not rest. It was pharmaceutical intervention. The doctor’s mandate came from the top.

The studio mixer, the formal dinner, the weekend party at Mayer’s beach house—these were not merely social events. They were extensions of the soundstage, environments where control could be exerted through more subtle means. Alcohol flowed freely. According to biographies of the era, Mayer himself was a moderate drinker, but he ensured his stars were not. He watched. He noted who became loose-tongued after three martinis, who became sentimental, who became aggressive. This intelligence was valuable. An actor revealed a gambling debt or a family scandal over cocktails; that information was filed away in the studio’s legal department, a potential lever for future contract negotiations or a weapon to quash dissent.

The real control lay in the aftermath. The “morning after” was a scheduled event. Call sheets from the period show shooting often began at 6:00 a.m. A star who had been encouraged to drink heavily at a studio function the night before would arrive on set hungover, shaky, unfit for the camera. This is where the medical department completed the cycle. Studio drivers would often collect actors from their homes. If the actor was visibly ill from the previous night’s indulgences, the driver’s instructions were to take them not to the set, but to the studio clinic. Dr. Edward Hummel or one of his staff would be waiting. The treatment was not coffee and aspirin. It was an injection—often a combination of vitamins and amphetamine—to counteract the depressant effects of the alcohol. The actor would be delivered to the set alert, focused, and ready for work. They called it a “pick-me-up” or a “sobering shot.” In reality, it was a chemical override, erasing the consequences of the studio’s own social engineering.

This created a devastating feedback loop. The star learned they could drink to excess without professional penalty. The studio’s doctor would always provide the antidote. The doctor’s role thus expanded from managing the demands of production to enabling the destructive behaviors the studio’s culture encouraged. The actor’s dependency shifted subtly—from needing a pill to work, to needing a shot to recover from the lifestyle that work demanded. Mayer understood this dynamic perfectly. In his view, it bound the star more tightly to the studio. Where else would they receive such comprehensive, discreet care? As actor Robert Taylor later remarked in an interview, “Mr. Mayer made you feel he was your father. A strict father who paid for everything, including the doctor who cleaned up your messes. You owed him everything. And he never let you forget it.”

The ultimate expression of this control was the “morality clause,” a standard provision in studio contracts. It allowed the studio to suspend or terminate an actor for behavior deemed “immoral” or “damaging to the studio’s reputation.” Intoxication in public could trigger it. But intoxication at a studio-sanctioned event, followed by a visit to the studio doctor, was kept off the books. The clause was not a deterrent against vice; it was a selective weapon. It could be invoked against an actor who sought treatment outside the studio system, or who became unreliable despite the doctor’s interventions. It made the studio’s pharmaceutical support not a right, but a privilege that could be revoked. Your health, your ability to function, was conditional on your compliance.

The case of Judy Garland illustrates the end point of this system. By the mid-1940s, her amphetamine and barbiturate use, initiated by the studio, had spiraled. She began to drink heavily. Memoirs from the period describe her arriving at parties already unsteady, then drinking more. The studio’s response was not intervention. It was management. MGM production logs show that on the set of *The Pirate* in 1947, Judy was frequently late. On days when her condition was particularly poor, a studio car would collect her and take her to the clinic for a “reviving” injection before delivering her to the soundstage. The doctor was not treating an illness. He was patching an asset so filming could continue. Mayer’s personal correspondence from this period, cited in biographer Scott Eyman’s work, reveals his growing frustration not with Judy’s suffering, but with the cost of her delays. The solution, in his mind, was not to remove the chemicals that were destroying her, but to better regulate their administration.

Thus, the cocktail hour and the doctor’s needle were two points on the same continuum of control. The first created a vulnerability. The second sold the cure. Both transactions increased the studio’s leverage. Louis B. Mayer did not invent this system. He industrialized it. He authorized the medical department to function as a repair shop for the human machinery he owned. The doctor’s office became the antechamber where the consequences of the studio’s own culture were chemically erased, leaving the star indebted, dependent, and ready for their close-up. The party was another scene, and the hangover was a problem for the prop department to fix before the cameras rolled.

Dr. Edward J. Hummel was, by all accounts, a kindly man. Photographs from the MGM archives show a bespectacled, round-faced physician in a crisp white coat, often smiling. He had an office on the studio lot, part of a small clinic equipped to handle everything from a splinter to a nervous breakdown. His primary employer was not the collective health of the actors, but the Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer corporation. His job, as defined by the production schedule, was to keep the machinery running. When that machinery was a child’s body, the medical ethics of the outside world did not apply. Inside the studio gates, a different set of rules governed biology.

A problem was arithmetic. A typical shooting day for a musical like *Babes in Arms* or *Strike Up the Band* began at 5:00 a.m. with hair, makeup, and costume. Rehearsals and filming often ran until 9:00 p.m. or later. For the adolescent stars at the center of these productions, this represented a 16-hour shift of intense physical and emotional performance, six days a week. Natural energy could not sustain it. The studio’s solution was chemical. Dr. Hummel was its dispenser.

Mickey Rooney, who began working with Hummel as a child, laid out the system plainly in his later testimony and memoirs. “They’d give you the pills,” he said. “The ‘pepper-uppers.’ Dr. Hummel would come around, check on you, ask how you were holding up. If you were dragging, he’d have a little something for you.” That “little something” was almost always Benzedrine, a potent amphetamine that suppressed appetite and produced a state of intense, focused wakefulness. Handed out not as a last resort, but as a standard part of the production schedule. The child was not diagnosed with a condition. The set was diagnosed with a slowdown. Hummel wrote the prescription.

Judy Garland’s experience during the filming of *The Wizard of Oz* in 1938 became the archetype. At sixteen, she was carrying the film. The hours were brutal, the pressure immense. In her later recollections, Judy described the regimen. “They’d give me pills to wake me up, and pills to put me to sleep,” she said. The “wake-up” pills came from Hummel’s supply. According to production logs and the biography by Gerold Frank, Judy would be given Benzedrine in the morning to launch her into the day’s work. At lunch, when the other actors ate, she was given a bowl of soup and more medication to keep her energy steady. Her weight was monitored obsessively; the amphetamines ensured she had no appetite to sabotage that control.

Hummel did not act in a vacuum. His orders flowed from the same logic that governed Mayer’s memos. A 1939 internal memo from *The Wizard of Oz* production office, preserved in the studio archives, notes a concern about “Miss Garland’s stamina” during the final weeks of shooting. The recommended action: “Consult with Dr. H. for a maintenance regimen.” Hummel provided it. The goal was not health, but continuity. A child’s fatigue was a mechanical failure to be corrected with pharmaceutical fuel.
