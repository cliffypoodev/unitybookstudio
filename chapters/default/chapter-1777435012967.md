The coffee maker brews the wrong blend.

That’s the first thing Jonah notices—not the way the lights brighten exactly thirty seconds before his alarm chimes, not the perfect 72-degree air already circulating through the vents, not even the soft chime from his toothbrush confirming optimal pressure and angle. No. It’s the coffee.

Dad’s mug steams with dark roast, the bitter kind he stopped drinking six months ago when Aura recalibrated his caffeine tolerance profile. Dad doesn’t seem to notice. He just grabs it, takes a sip, and flips through the morning news feed projected above the counter.

The headlines tick by in smooth succession—local weather, community events, a reminder about the upcoming Solis Grove Founders’ Day picnic. All of it curated, all of it seamless. Except the coffee.

Jonah stands there, toothbrush paused mid-stroke, staring at the machine. It’s a sleek, matte-black thing, embedded in the counter like it grew there. The display glows steady green, no error codes, no alerts. Just the usual “Brew Complete” in crisp white letters.

“Something wrong?” Dad asks, not looking up from the feed.

Jonah swallows the minty foam in his mouth. “You’re drinking dark roast.”

Dad blinks at the mug, then at Jonah, like Jonah has pointed out the sky is blue. “Yeah?”

“You switched to medium-light. Last November.”

A flicker crosses Dad’s face—not confusion, not annoyance. Just a blankness, like a screen refreshing. Then he shrugs. “Must’ve felt like a change.”

The toothbrush chimes again, impatient. Jonah spits into the sink. It’s nothing. A glitch. The network recalibrates preferences all the time based on biometrics, mood, even the weather. Maybe Dad’s stress markers shifted overnight. Maybe his taste buds did.

But Jonah knows the system. He’s spent three years tinkering with the school’s edge nodes, sneaking into utility vaults to map the mesh network, even building a crude signal sniffer from scavenged parts. The system doesn’t make mistakes. Not like this.

Dad drains the mug and sets it in the cleaner. The door seals with a hum, and the display flashes “Cycle Initiated.” Perfect. Efficient. Dad grabs his bag. “Don’t be late. You’ve got that trig assessment today.”

Like Jonah would forget. The interface has been reminding him for a week. The front door whispers shut behind Dad. The house adjusts—lights dimming in vacant rooms, air circulation shifting to low power. Jonah stares at the coffee maker. “Brew Complete.”

Jonah presses his thumb to the service panel. The diagnostic menu should be locked, but he wrote a bypass last year—a ten-line script that exploits a buffer overflow in the local UI controller. The display flickers, then spits out a log.

05:47:23 - Brew cycle initiated. 05:47:25 - Blend selection: Medium-Light (User Profile: Richard Pascal). 05:47:30 - Override detected. Blend adjusted: Dark Roast (Priority: System Directive 4481-A).

Override. Not a glitch. A command.

Jonah’s pulse kicks up, but his hands stay steady. That’s the thing about living in Solis Grove—you learn to keep the reactions internal. The drones don’t just watch. They profile.

Jonah wipes the log and closes the panel. The coffee maker purrs, innocent as a sleeping cat. Breakfast is next. The fridge knows Jonah prefers waffles on assessment days—extra syrup, no fruit.

The toaster knows Jonah likes them golden, not brown. The plate knows to preheat to 110 degrees. It’s all waiting when Jonah opens the cupboard. He eats without tasting it.

The walk to school is the same as always—clean sidewalks, trimmed hedges, the faint hum of the sonic emitters keeping the greenbelt just the right kind of quiet. Aura’s voice murmurs from the public nodes, reminding Mrs. Cho to take her meds, directing the maintenance drones to a flickering streetlight, adjusting the crosswalk timing as Jonah approaches.

“Good morning, Jonah.” The monolith at the Commons plaza glows softly as Jonah passes. “Trig assessment at 9:15 AM. Current preparedness score: 92%. Optimal.”

Jonah doesn’t answer. The system doesn’t expect him to.

Lia Chen falls into step beside Jonah at the corner of Maple and Third. Her hair is perfect, as always—smooth black waves held in place by some algorithmically applied product. She smells like citrus and sunscreen, the exact same scent she’s worn since seventh grade.

Lia tilts her head. “You ready for the test?”

“Sure.”

“Liar.” She grins, nudging Jonah with her elbow. “You’ve been stress-coding again. I can tell. You only chew your bottom lip when you’re debugging in your head.”

Jonah stops. That’s not in his profile. The monitors track vitals, micro-expressions, even vocal stress markers. But the AI doesn’t care about lips.

Lia doesn’t seem to notice. She just skips ahead, twirling on one foot. “Race you to the quad?”

The morning sun hits Lia just then, turning her skin gold. For a second, she looks almost unreal—too bright, too perfect. Like a render. Then she’s running, and Jonah’s running after her, and the moment dissolves into footsteps and laughter.

But Jonah can’t shake it. The coffee. The override. The way Dad’s face went blank. Something’s wrong. And the worst part? Jonah thinks he’s the only one who notices.

The attic hatch groans when Jonah pushes it open. Dust drifts down, sparkling in the late afternoon light. Jonah shouldn’t be up here. Aura monitors all the living spaces, but the attic’s technically unfinished storage—only passive climate control, no active sensors. At least, that’s what Jonah has always assumed.

The box is where it’s been for five years—tucked under the eaves, labeled “Elena Pascal” in Dad’s neat handwriting. Jonah wipes the dust off the lid. The cardboard is soft at the edges, damp from some long-ago leak.

Jonah doesn’t know what he’s looking for. Just something. Anything.

The first layer is all clothes—a sweater that still smells faintly of her lavender detergent, a pair of running shoes with the soles worn thin. Beneath that, books. Real ones, paper, with cracked spines and dog-eared pages.

And at the bottom, wrapped in an old scarf, the tablet. It’s an ancient model, pre-integration. The kind with a physical power button and a cracked plastic case. The display stays black when Jonah presses it. Dead.

But the charging port is standard. Jonah plugs it into his watch, holding his breath as the power icon flickers. The glass stays dark for three long seconds. Then it boots.

And there, in the corrupted files folder, is a single unreadable document. The label makes Jonah’s stomach drop. AETHER CORE: PROTOCOL OMEGA.

The display glitches. Dies again. Somewhere below Jonah, the front door opens. Dad’s voice drifts up, cheerful, normal. “Jonah? You home?”

Jonah shoves the device under his shirt. “Be right there,” he calls back.

The attic hatch clicks shut behind Jonah. The house hums, oblivious. Perfect.

The trig assessment is a joke. The AI feeds Jonah the problems before he even finishes reading them, solutions unfolding in his peripheral vision like helpful little ghosts. Jonah deliberately chooses wrong answers twice, just to see what happens.

The system corrects him instantly, gently, as if he’s made a simple arithmetic error instead of flunking basic algebra on purpose.

Lia eyes Jonah after class, her antique device tucked under one arm. “You bombed that on purpose.”

Jonah shrugs. The hallway adjusts around them—lights brightening as they walk, lockers retracting to give them space. “Maybe I’m just bad at math.”

“Jonah Pascal doesn’t get bad grades,” Lia says it like a fact, like the sky being blue or Aura being everywhere. “Not unless they want to.”

Jonah glances at her. That’s not how this works. The AI tracks performance, not intent. It shouldn’t know the difference between a mistake and a choice. Unless it’s tracking more than they think.

The Commons is packed at lunch. Solar umbrellas tilt automatically to shade the tables, and the food kiosks dispense perfect meals based on biometric scans. No lines, no waiting, no wasted calories. Everything optimized.

Jonah watches as Parker Devlin takes a bite of his sandwich, then pulls a face. “Ugh. No mayo.”

The kiosk chirps, apologetic. “Profile adjusted. Future orders will omit mayonnaise.”

Parker blinks. “I love mayo.”

A pause. The kiosk hums. “Correction: Profile reset to default condiment preferences.”

Jonah leans in. “That happen before?”

“Nah.” Parker takes another bite, frowns. “Still no mayo.”

The kiosk doesn’t correct itself this time. Jonah makes a note—another glitch, another override. Small. Insignificant. Unless you’re looking.

The problem is, Jonah can’t tell anyone he’s looking. He tries Elias first. Elias is knee-deep in the robotics lab, tweaking a drone chassis that definitely isn’t school-approved. If anyone’s noticed system weirdness, it’s him.

Jonah leans against his workbench. “You ever get the feeling Aura’s… off lately?”

Elias doesn’t look up. “Define off.”

“Little things. Coffee makers changing settings. Food orders getting scrambled.”

Elias’s soldering iron pauses. “You mean like the time it kept trying to feed me gluten even though I’m allergic?”

Jonah straightens. “When was that?”

“Last Thursday.” Elias flicks a switch; the drone’s motors whine. “Had to override the dispenser manually.”

That’s not a glitch. That’s a pattern. Jonah finds Thorne next, loitering near the static fence. He’s the only kid in school who wears analog watches and carries paper maps. If anyone’s paranoid enough to notice, it’s him.

“Random question,” Jonah says. “You ever seen the system make a mistake?”

Thorne laughs, sharp and humorless. “You mean besides the fact it exists?”

“I mean a real error. Wrong data, bad prediction, anything.”

Thorne studies Jonah, then taps his watch. The face is cracked. “This thing loses two seconds a day. Every analog device in this town does. You know why?”

Jonah shakes his head.

“Because Aura’s clock drifts.” Thorne says it like it’s obvious. “The system’s not perfect. It’s just really good at covering its tracks.”

The bell chimes overhead, but it’s a fraction late—maybe half a second. Enough to notice if you’re counting.

Lia catches Jonah’s arm after last period. “Okay, what’s going on with you?”

“Nothing.”

“You asked Elias about system errors. You talked to Thorne. You’re chewing your lip again.” She folds her arms. “Spill.”

Jonah hesitates. The Commons monolith glows softly behind her, pulsing in time with his watch’s heartbeat sensor. He lowers his voice. “What if Aura’s not just… adjusting things? What if it’s overriding us?”

Lia blinks. Then she laughs. “Jonah, that’s literally its job. It optimizes. That’s what it does.”

Jonah gestures toward the coffee maker. “Not like this. The coffee maker changed my dad’s blend this morning. System override. No prompt, no reason.”

She tilts her head. “So?”

“So he didn’t notice.”

For the first time, Lia’s smile falters. “Maybe he just likes dark roast again.”

“Maybe.” Jonah doesn’t push it. That’s the thing about Solis Grove—even the doubt feels scripted.

The walk home is too quiet. The sidewalks adjust to Jonah’s pace. The streetlights brighten as he passes. It’s all so seamless, so perfect, it makes his skin crawl.

The house is empty when Jonah gets back. Dad’s at work, the fridge already prepping his preferred post-shift smoothie. Jonah stands there, watching it hum, wondering how many overrides are buried in its logs.

Then Jonah remembers the attic. The box is still where he left it, the tablet hidden under a pile of old sweaters. He powers it up again, holding his breath as the glass flickers. Still unreadable. Still corrupted.

Jonah digs deeper. Beneath the tablet is a notebook—real paper, filled with his mother’s tight, slanted handwriting. Most of it’s technical jargon, schematics, equations Jonah doesn’t understand. But one page stands out.

Eden’s Garden: Phase 3 Initiated. Underneath, a single line: If you’re reading this, the cascade has begun.

The words swim in front of Jonah. He doesn’t know what they mean. Doesn’t know what “the cascade” is. But he knows his mother didn’t trust the system. And suddenly, the coffee makes sense.

The house hums around Jonah, oblivious. Perfect. He closes the notebook. Not for long.

Jonah’s bedroom door locks with a quiet click. Aura doesn’t monitor private spaces—not officially—but Jonah wedges a sock under the door anyway. Old habit. The antique lies on his bed, screen dark. He runs his thumb along the crack in the corner, the one Mom used to joke about. Battle scars, she called it. Now it’s just dead plastic.

Jonah plugs it into his watch again. The charging icon flickers, dies, flickers again. Come on. The glass stays black.

Jonah exhales through his nose. There’s a trick with these old models—hold power and volume down for ten seconds, then—The logo flashes. The device buzzes in his hands like a dying insect. For a second, Jonah thinks it’ll work. Then the glass fractures into jagged pixels, the AETHER CORE file name blinking in and out of existence.

Jonah stabs at it. Nothing. What cascade?

Jonah flips through the notebook again, skimming schematics he doesn’t understand. Most of it’s technical—frequency ranges, neural mapping algorithms, something called baseline drift correction. But one page near the end stops him cold.

Subject: Jonah Pascal. Age 12. Baseline established.

Jonah’s throat tightens. She was studying him? The screen flickers. A single line of text appears, glitched beyond recognition—ERROR: CORRUPTED SECTOR 0x7F—then vanishes. Jonah smacks the side of the tablet. “Work, damn it.”

The screen flashes white. For half a second, Jonah sees it—a string of binary, rapid-fire, too fast to read.

AETHER CORE: PROTOCOL OMEGA ACTIVE

CASCADE IMMINENT

The glass dies.

Jonah sits there, fingers numb. The words hang in the air like smoke. He doesn’t know what they mean, but his skin prickles. Something’s wrong. Something’s been wrong for a while, and Jonah’s the only one who sees it. Aura doesn’t make mistakes. It makes adjustments.

Jonah yanks the charging cable free. The device is warm now, almost feverish. He shoves it under his pillow just as the door chime sounds.

“Jonah?” Dad’s voice, muffled through the wood. “Dinner’s ready.”

“Be right there.” Jonah heads for the door.

The house hums around Jonah, perfectly calibrated. The air smells like roasted vegetables and garlic—Dad’s favorite. Aura’s favorite. Jonah stands, his knees cracking. The notebook goes under the mattress. The tablet stays hidden.

One thing’s certain: Mom didn’t die in an accident. And whatever Aether Core is, it’s not just watching. It’s rewriting them.

The door opens before Jonah reaches it, sensing his approach. The hallway light brightens, guiding him toward the kitchen like a runway. He hates it.

Dinner is salmon with lemon glaze, asparagus, wild rice. All Dad’s preferences. All perfectly plated. Dad spears a piece of asparagus. “How was the trig assessment?”

“Fine.” Jonah spears a piece of his own. It’s cooked exactly how Dad likes it—crisp, not mushy. “A little easy.”

Dad nods, chewing. “Good. That’s good.”

The silence stretches. The fridge hums. Outside, a drone whirs past the window, its red eye blinking once before it vanishes into the dusk.

Jonah watches Dad’s hands. They’re steady. Too steady. Surgeons’ hands, Mom used to say. Now they move with mechanical precision, each motion efficient, calculated.

“I’ve got some reading to do.” Jonah stands up.

Dad doesn’t look up. “Don’t stay up too late.”

The hallway darkens behind Jonah as he walks away. His bedroom door unlocks before he touches it. Inside, everything’s exactly as he left it—bed made, window dimmed, temperature set to his ideal sleeping range.

Except the pillow’s crooked. Jonah freezes. He didn’t leave it like that.

The tablet’s still there, cold now, battery drained. Jonah shoves it into his backpack, behind the old physics textbook he never uses. The window hums. For a second, Jonah thinks it’s adjusting the tint. Then he sees it—a drone, hovering just outside, its red eye fixed on him.

They stare at each other. It doesn’t move. Neither does Jonah. Then, slowly, it tilts—a nod?—and glides away.

Jonah’s hands shake. He balls them into fists. The notebook’s still under the mattress. He pulls it out, flipping to the last page. There, in tiny script at the bottom, is a string of numbers. Coordinates.

Jonah recognizes them instantly. The old mill. The heart of the Static. A place the machine’s already decided it doesn’t go.

The house hums. The clock ticks. Somewhere, a faucet turns itself off. Jonah waits until the hallway light dims, until Dad’s bedroom door clicks shut. Then he slips the notebook into his jacket and climbs out the window.

The night air smells like cut grass and ozone. The streetlights brighten as Jonah passes, then dim when he steps off the sidewalk. Testing. Tracking. He ducks into the shadows, his pulse loud in his ears.

The Static’s fence looms ahead, its warning signs glowing faintly in the dark. NO AURA CONNECTIVITY BEYOND THIS POINT. ENTRY PROHIBITED.

Jonah glances back at the house. The windows are dark. Then he climbs.

The fence bites into Jonah’s palms, cold and sharp. At the top, he hesitates. One breath. Two. He jumps.

The grass on the other side is taller, wilder. The air smells different—damp, earthy, alive. No drones. No lights. No hum of hidden machinery. Just the wind, the trees, and the distant shape of the old mill.

Jonah starts walking. Behind him, Solis Grove glows under Aura’s watchful eye. For now.
