The letter arrived at Montebello on the morning of December 12, 1810. It bore no official seal from the American governor’s headquarters, only the name of the addressee. Fulwar Skipwith broke the wax and read the single sheet. According to his later correspondence, the contents were brief and functional: an invitation for him, as a leading citizen, to attend a meeting of the new territorial council. It was addressed to “Fulwar Skipwith, Esq.” The title of President was absent.

He set the paper on his desk. Outside, the winter-bare branches of the oaks framed a view of the Mississippi, a brown expanse under a gray sky. The river traffic had resumed, a sign of the renewed commerce the American annexation promised. The view from his study was the same, but the political landscape it reflected had been erased. His republic now existed in two places: in the filing boxes of the clerks who were methodically stamping “United States Territory” over the letterhead of the Supreme Council, and in the stubborn architecture of his own mind.

The provisional capital had never been Baton Rouge with its captured fort, but St. Francisville, perched on the loess bluffs twenty-five miles upriver. The town was less a municipality than a cluster of plantation houses, a courthouse, a tavern, and a few mercantile stores serving the cotton and indigo estates of the Feliciana parishes. It was here, in the second-floor courtroom of the parish courthouse, that the Republic of West Florida conducted its official business. The minutes of the Supreme Council, preserved in the National Archives, provide a skeletal record of its brief life. The entries are matter-of-fact: resolutions to pay for powder and shot, appointments of tax collectors, orders for the repair of roads. They read like the proceedings of a particularly diligent county board, not a revolutionary congress.

This was the central paradox of the seventy-four-day republic. It performed the rituals of statehood with solemn proceduralism while lacking the fundamental attributes of a state. It had no standing army, only a summoned militia that returned to its farms after the fort fell. It had no treasury, only promissory notes backed by the personal credit of planters like John Mills. It controlled no borders; its authority bled into the pine forests and faded at the Pearl and the Perdido, where Spanish patrols still moved. Its sovereignty was a legal argument, penned by John Rhea and ratified by a few dozen men in a room that still smelled of tobacco smoke and damp wool.

The government’s physical presence was equally provisional. President Skipwith maintained his office at his plantation, Montebello. The legislature met in the St. Francisville courthouse, a two-story brick building that reverted to its primary function of settling land disputes on days the Council did not convene. There was no presidential mansion, no capitol, no customs house flying the lone star. The republic’s bureaucracy consisted of a handful of clerks, often the same men who managed the planters’ ledgers, copying proclamations by lamplight. The correspondence between officials, preserved in fragments, shows a constant, grinding focus on logistics. A letter from the Committee of Public Safety, dated October 15, 1810, and later reprinted in the Louisiana Gazette, is typical. It directs a local captain to “procure two hundred weight of lead” and to keep a strict account of the money spent. The revolution was measured in pounds of powder and barrels of salt pork.

Public reaction, as filtered through the few contemporary letters and later reminiscences, ranged from fervent support to pragmatic indifference. For the Anglo-American planters who had driven the rebellion, the declaration was a necessary step to secure their property. They attended the musters, read the proclamations posted at crossroads, and went back to overseeing the harvest. For the smaller farmers and tradesmen, the new government was a distant authority, marginally closer than the Spanish one it replaced, but just as likely to demand provisions or service. For the enslaved population, which outnumbered the free inhabitants in the Feliciana district, the change of flags meant nothing. Their condition was fixed by a different set of laws, which the new constitution explicitly left undisturbed. And for the gens de couleur libres, the free people of color who operated small farms and businesses, the rebellion introduced a new element of racial anxiety into a borderland already tense with conflict.

The reality of governing this patchwork territory fell to a rotating cast of men who rode to St. Francisville between managing their own affairs. John Rhea, the constitutional architect, served as both presiding officer and chief proceduralist, his notes indicating a relentless focus on creating a paper trail of legitimacy. Philemon Thomas, once the military commander, now sat as a senator, his impatience with legislative debate palpable in the terseness of his recorded motions. They passed laws establishing courts, authorized the issuance of scrip, and debated the organization of the militia. They were, in essence, planting a garden in the first hard frost. Every act was an assertion of a future they were increasingly uncertain would arrive. The minutes show no grand debates about national destiny, only the practical, weary work of keeping the lights on in the courthouse a little while longer.

Fulwar Skipwith’s election as president on September 26, 1810, was less a coronation than a conscription. The convention’s journal, archived in the West Florida Papers, records the vote as unanimous. His qualifications, however, were unique among the planters and militia captains assembled. He was, as he described himself in a later memoir, “a man of the world, not merely of the parish.” While his neighbors had been clearing land and building cotton gins, Skipwith had moved through the salons and chancelleries of Europe. He had served as American consul in Paris during the French Revolution, negotiated commercial treaties, and witnessed the collapse of one republic and the bloody birth of another. He understood the language of diplomacy and the fragile rituals of recognized sovereignty. This experience, as historian Isaac Cox later noted, made him simultaneously the republic’s greatest asset and its most tragic figure. He knew precisely what statehood required, and he knew how far short of that mark they stood.

In his inaugural address, delivered not from a balcony to a cheering crowd but within the close walls of the St. Francisville courthouse, he struck a note of cautious optimism tempered by diplomatic realism. A copy of the speech, preserved in the Skipwith Family Papers at the Louisiana State University Archives, outlines his vision. He spoke of “the natural right of a people to alter their form of government” and pledged to “preserve order and administer justice.” But the core of his message was a plea for external recognition. “Our first object,” he stated, “must be to secure our independence from the world.” For Skipwith, this did not mean isolation. It meant securing a place on the map in the minds of foreign capitals, starting with Washington City.

His immediate actions were those of a man building a case file for nationhood. He ordered the design of an official seal for the republic. He commissioned the printing of official stationery bearing the lone star emblem. He began a meticulous correspondence, drafting letters to Governor David Holmes of the Mississippi Territory and to his old acquaintance, President James Madison. These were not the frantic pleas of rebels; they were calibrated dispatches from a fellow chief magistrate. In a letter to Holmes dated October 5, 1810, he wrote of the “amicable relations which we hope to cultivate with our neighboring territories,” framing the rebellion as a settled fact of geopolitics. He understood that legitimacy was performative, built on a foundation of official-looking documents and the correct forms of address.

This diplomatic façade, however, papered over a stark internal reality. Skipwith’s personal fortune, once considerable, had been shattered years earlier when a ship carrying his assets from France was captured by the British. His plantation, Montebello, was elegant but mortgaged. The presidency offered no salary. His authority, as he soon discovered, was hemmed in by the very constitution John Rhea had crafted. The document, modeled closely on the U.S. Constitution, created a weak executive. The Supreme Council held the purse strings and the power to appoint military officers. Skipwith could recommend, but he could not command. The minutes show a president repeatedly requesting funds for basic operations and being referred to committee.

His diplomatic instincts also put him at odds with the more militant faction in the Council, led by men like Philemon Thomas. Where Thomas saw a military opportunity to push east toward Mobile and Pensacola, Skipwith saw a provocation that would invite crushing retaliation from Spanish regulars or, worse, give the United States a pretext to intervene not as a protector but as a pacifier. Skipwith advocated for a posture of defensive consolidation and vigorous diplomacy. He believed that if the republic could prove itself orderly and capable, the United States might recognize it as a sovereign entity, or at the very least, negotiate annexation as a treaty between equals. It was a vision born of his time in Europe, where new states emerged from congresses and treaties.

The strain of holding this vision against the tide of circumstance was evident in his personal habits. A visiting merchant from Natchez, quoted in a December 1810 letter held by the Historic New Orleans Collection, described a dinner at Montebello. Skipwith was “a gracious host, full of anecdotes of Paris,” but his eyes kept drifting to a map on the wall, its western border traced heavily in ink. He spoke of tariff policies and river navigation with expertise, then fell into silence. The performance of statehood was exhausting. Every proclamation he signed, every diplomatic note he crafted, was an act of faith in a future that was receding with each passing day of American silence and Spanish regrouping. He was, in essence, applying the protocols of the Concert of Europe to a contested clearing in the Mississippi pine woods, a diplomat without a country, building a house of official paper while the walls of reality closed in.

The constitutional convention did not meet in a hall designed for the purpose, but in the same St. Francisville courtroom where cattle disputes were adjudicated. The journal of the convention, preserved in the West Florida Papers, shows that from its first session on July 25, 1810, the delegates operated with a procedural solemnity that belied their revolutionary circumstance. They elected a president, a secretary, and a doorkeeper. They established rules of order governing debate and the introduction of motions. They kept meticulous minutes. This was not a gathering of hotheaded conspirators; it was a committee drafting a new social contract, and its guiding hand was John Rhea.

Rhea, a planter from the Feliciana district, was not a trained lawyer, but he possessed a methodical, systematic mind. His personal papers, archived at Louisiana State University, include heavily annotated copies of the U.S. Constitution, the Kentucky state constitution, and several early American legal codes. He approached the problem of founding a republic not as an ideological crusade but as an engineering challenge. The structure had to bear weight. The convention journal reveals his dominance in matters of form. He drafted the initial rules of procedure. He authored the resolution that transformed the body from a "convention of the inhabitants" into the "Supreme Council of the Republic of West Florida," a semantic shift that claimed sovereign authority. When the time came to draft a permanent constitution, the task fell to a committee he chaired.

The document they produced, adopted on September 26, 1810, was a work of deliberate mimicry. A surviving copy, held by the Historic New Orleans Collection, shows it to be a near-verbatim transcription of the U.S. Constitution for its first seven articles, with only the necessary geographical substitutions. It established a tripartite government: a bicameral legislature, a supreme court, and a president. It included a bill of rights guaranteeing trial by jury, freedom of religion, and protection from unreasonable search and seizure. It meticulously outlined the process for passing bills, confirming appointments, and impeaching officials. In its structure, it was a perfect, miniature reflection of the federal system to the north.

The critical deviations, however, were revealing. The West Florida constitution placed even stricter limits on executive power than its model. The president could not veto legislation; he could only return a bill to the Council with his objections, after which a simple majority could override him. All military and civil appointments required the approval of the Senate. The treasury was placed firmly under legislative control. This was a constitution designed by men deeply suspicious of concentrated authority, a reaction to their recent experience with a distant Spanish governor. They were creating a government of laws, not men, and they bound their own president with tight procedural chains.

Rhea’s ambition extended beyond the frame of government. In the weeks after the constitution’s adoption, he spearheaded the drafting of an entire statutory code for the new republic. Council minutes from October and November 1810 list a cascade of committees he chaired or influenced: on the judiciary, on revenue, on militia organization, on weights and measures. They passed acts establishing county courts and defining their jurisdiction. They passed a tax law, a necessary if unpopular measure, outlining rates on land, slaves, and merchandise. They passed regulations for the licensing of taverns and the maintenance of roads. They even drafted a rudimentary copyright statute. The paper trail they generated was their answer to the chaos of the borderland. If they could not control the territory with soldiers, they would administer it with paperwork.

This flurry of legislation was an act of profound optimism, or perhaps profound denial. It assumed a stable, enduring polity with time to mature. It assumed citizens who would read the proclamations, pay the taxes, and bring their disputes before the newly established courts. It assumed that the authority of the Supreme Council extended, in practice as well as theory, from the Pearl to the Perdido. None of these assumptions held. The tax collectors appointed by the Council could not safely operate in the eastern districts, where Spanish patrols still roamed. The court system existed only on paper; no docket from a Republic of West Florida court has survived, suggesting it never heard a case. The militia law required musters that many settlers, with harvests in, simply ignored.

Yet Rhea and his colleagues persisted. Their focus on legal architecture served a dual purpose. Internally, it gave the rebellion the sheen of inevitability and order, reassuring the propertied class that this was a transition, not an upheaval. Externally, it was a signal to the world, and especially to Washington. Every neatly penned statute, every properly formatted legislative journal, was a piece of evidence in a case for recognition. They were proving they were not marauders or filibusterers. They were lawmakers. In a letter to a contact in the Mississippi Territory, later reprinted in a Natchez newspaper, Rhea wrote that the stability of the new government “rests upon the solid foundation of popular consent and constitutional procedure.” He believed the paperwork would make the state real.

The ultimate testament to this legalistic faith was the republic’s handling of its own extinction. When Governor Claiborne’s agents arrived in December to assume control, they did not face armed resistance. They faced clerks. The transfer of authority, as documented in the American territorial records, was an administrative procedure. The West Florida officials provided inventories of public property—mostly furniture from the courthouse. They surrendered the official seal and the remaining stock of government stationery. They turned over the minutes and the journals. The revolution ended not with a battle, but with a filing cabinet changing hands.

The legislative sessions in St. Francisville were governed by the rhythms of agrarian life, not political calendars. The minutes of the Supreme Council, held in the National Archives, show a pattern of adjournments for “the pressing business of the harvest.” Senators arrived on horseback, their boots dusty from the fields, and debated revenue bills while mentally calculating the weather’s threat to their cotton. The courthouse itself, as described in a property assessment later filed with the U.S. Territorial government, was a functional, two-room structure. The ground floor housed the parish clerk; the upper floor, where the Council met, held a plain table, a dozen chairs, and a small stove for winter. The lone star flag, when it was flown, hung from a pole above the front door, competing for attention with notices of sheriff’s sales and stray livestock.

Daily life in the town continued around the political experiment. The Louisiana Gazette, in a dispatch from a traveler printed December 1, 1810, described St. Francisville as “a place of three taverns and much conversation.” The talk was less of independence and more of commodity prices, land titles, and the latest news from Natchez. The rebellion had not interrupted the central business of the territory: the production of cotton and indigo, worked by enslaved laborers. An estate inventory from the period, filed in the West Feliciana Parish Courthouse records, lists the “increase” of enslaved people as property assets, assessed alongside mules and ginning equipment. The republic’s constitution had explicitly protected the institution. The revolution was for the masters, not the majority of the population.

Security was a constant, low-grade concern. The Council minutes are punctuated with reports of “unidentified riders” near the eastern borders and “disquieting movements” of Spanish loyalists in the Chifoncté district. The republic’s military force was the militia, a body that existed only when called. A muster roll from the Feliciana regiment, dated October 1810 and preserved in the War of 1812 papers at the University of Texas, shows seventy-three names. Next to many, in a later hand, is noted “excused—harvest” or “absent—business at Natchez.” The standing guard at the St. Francisville courthouse was typically a single man, often a servant or a hired hand, whose primary duty was to tend the stove.

The most revealing documents are the mundane requisitions. A surviving order from the Committee of Public Safety to a merchant in Bayou Sara, dated October 28, 1810, does not request muskets or cannon. It lists: six pounds of candles, two gallons of lamp oil, one pot of glue, and three reams of paper. The revolution required light, stationery, and something to mend torn documents. Another, from the office of the president in November, authorizes payment to a blacksmith for “the repair of the courthouse lock and the fabrication of two keys.” Sovereignty depended on functional hardware.

Diplomatic outreach was equally homespun. President Skipwith’s letters to President Madison and other officials were penned on Montebello’s imported paper, but their journey was precarious. A note in the Skipwith Family Papers from a courier, dated November 15, reports that the packet for Washington was “delayed at the Mississippi due to a skiff being unfit.” The republic’s fate rested on the soundness of a small boat. Other dispatches were carried by planters traveling on personal business, who would deposit them at post houses in American territory. The lines between state business and private errand were blurred beyond distinction.

The final meeting of the Supreme Council for which minutes exist was held on December 7, 1810. The journal entry is brief. It notes the presence of a quorum, the reading of a communication from a tax collector in St. Tammany Parish reporting his inability to operate due to “Spanish patrols upon the road,” and a motion to adjourn until the following Thursday. The motion passed. There is no record of a meeting on that following Thursday. By then, Governor Claiborne’s proclamation of annexation was already circulating, and the American military detachment was marching from Baton Rouge.

The physical artifacts of the republic were quietly absorbed. The stock of official paper was used by the new territorial administration for its own records, the lone star watermark eventually buried under layers of American script. The courthouse table and chairs remained. The stove was kept. The flagpole was repainted. When the U.S. District court held its first session in St. Francisville in the spring of 1811, the presiding judge sat at the same table where John Rhea had drafted the constitution. The only difference was the seal on the documents before him, and the flag that hung outside, now featuring stars in a field of blue, not a single star on a field of white.

President James Madison received the first dispatches from the Mississippi frontier in late September 1810, as the leaves were turning along the Potomac. The reports, carried by trusted couriers and forwarded by Governor David Holmes, described the capture of Baton Rouge and the declaration of independence. Madison’s response, as reconstructed from his correspondence and cabinet notes held in the Library of Congress, was not one of surprise, but of recognition. The event he and his predecessors had long anticipated—and quietly encouraged—was now unfolding. His challenge was not whether to act, but how to act without appearing to violate American neutrality or his own professed principles regarding territorial expansion.

The legal groundwork had been laid years earlier. Madison, as Secretary of State under Jefferson, had been a principal architect of the claim that West Florida was included in the Louisiana Purchase of 1803. Spain had vigorously denied this, and the claim rested on a disputed interpretation of France’s pre-1803 boundaries. For seven years, American diplomacy had oscillated between offering to purchase the territory and threatening to seize it. The rebellion of the settlers presented a third, more elegant option: let the locals do the work of expelling Spanish authority, then step in to restore order. The republic was a useful instrument. As Madison wrote in a confidential memorandum to Secretary of State Robert Smith on October 5, a copy of which is in the National Archives, the settlers had “accomplished a material change in the actual state of possession.” Spain’s claim was now not merely contested, but broken. The United States could move to fill the vacuum, citing the need to prevent anarchy and protect American citizens.

Madison’s proclamation of October 27, 1810, was a masterpiece of strategic omission. The document, published in the National Intelligencer, declared that the territory between the Pearl and Perdido Rivers was “rightfully included in the Louisiana Purchase” and was now “taken into the possession of the United States.” It ordered the governor of the Orleans Territory, William C.C. Claiborne, to proceed into the region and establish American authority. It made no mention of the Republic of West Florida, its president, its constitution, or its flag. The rebellion and the independent government it spawned were erased from the official narrative. In the view from Washington, there had been no republic, only a disruption of Spanish rule that allowed the United States to finally enforce its long-held claim.

The orders dispatched to Claiborne, preserved in the Territorial Papers of the United States, were explicit. He was to move with “firmness and dispatch,” but also with “circumspection.” He was to avoid armed conflict with the settlers if possible. He was to present the annexation not as a conquest, but as a liberation, a bringing of the wayward population under the protective mantle of the American union. Crucially, he was authorized to offer positions in the new territorial administration to the rebel leaders. This was a calculated move to co-opt, not confront, the existing power structure. The goal was a bloodless administrative transfer.

Madison and his cabinet understood the fragility of the West Florida government. Intelligence reports, summarized in a War Department file dated October 15, noted the republic’s lack of revenue, its divided leadership, and its inability to control the eastern districts. It was a state that could not stand on its own. The United States did not need to crush it; it could simply outwait it, allowing internal pressures and the threat of Spanish counterattack to soften resistance. The timing of the proclamation, issued just over a month after the Lone Star flag was raised, was deliberate. It moved before the republic could consolidate, but after it had done the hard work of removing the Spanish flag.

In a private letter to former President Jefferson dated November 8, 1810, held by the Massachusetts Historical Society, Madison revealed his colder calculus. “The expedient of the settlers,” he wrote, “has spared us the expense of a military expedition and the odium of a first aggression. Their temporary institution serves as a bridge from Spanish to American jurisdiction, across which we may pass with the appearance of restoring order rather than usurping power.” The republic was that bridge—a temporary, convenient structure to be used and then dismantled. Its existence, however brief, provided the diplomatic cover Madison needed. He could now present the annexation to Congress and the world not as land grabbed from Spain, but as order imposed upon a chaotic interregnum created by others. The reality of Skipwith’s diplomacy, Rhea’s constitution, and the hopes of the settlers in St. Francisville were irrelevant to this larger strategic purpose. They had played their part in the script. Now the scene was changing.

Governor William Charles Cole Claiborne rode into the Republic of West Florida in the first week of December 1810. He did not come with an army, but with a small escort of U.S. Regulars and a sheaf of documents. His mission, as outlined in his orders from Secretary of State Robert Smith, was to execute President Madison’s proclamation with “tact and firmness.” The territorial papers show Claiborne stage-managing his arrival. He first established himself at Baton Rouge, where Fort San Carlos now flew the American flag, securing the western anchor of the claim. Then, on December 6, he and his party moved north toward St. Francisville, the rebel capital. He did not march under arms; he traveled the river road, presenting himself less as a conqueror and more as a visiting official on a tour of inspection. His correspondence from those days, held by the Library of Congress, reveals a man focused on logistics and perception. He arranged for the proclamation to be printed and distributed ahead of his arrival. He instructed his aides to gather intelligence on the principal planters and their likely reactions. He was preparing not for a battle, but for a bureaucratic transition.

Claiborne and Fulwar Skipwith never met. This was not an accident of timing, but a deliberate avoidance. Skipwith, at Montebello, received copies of Claiborne’s proclamations and the reports of his advance. The president of the republic faced a choice: mobilize the militia for a symbolic, doomed defense, or acquiesce with a formal protest that preserved the principle of sovereignty. He chose the latter. His protest, dated December 8, 1810, and later published in the Louisiana Gazette, was a masterful document of diplomatic grievance. It did not deny the benefits of union with the United States. Skipwith wrote that he viewed the prospect of annexation “with gratification,” believing it would secure the rights and property of the inhabitants. His objection was not to the end, but to the means. Claiborne’s entry and the imposition of American authority, he declared, were “an outrage against the Lone Star Flag and the constitution of West Florida.” The United States was treating the republic not as a sovereign entity to be negotiated with, but as a nullity to be erased. This method, Skipwith argued, insulted the dignity of the people who had, through lawful convention, constituted themselves as a free state.

He anchored his protest in the language of revolutionary legitimacy. The republic, he insisted, was founded on the consent of the governed and operated under a written constitution. It had a president, a legislature, and courts. It possessed, therefore, the attributes of sovereignty. For the United States to simply bypass these institutions was to violate the very principles of popular government America claimed to champion. “If the right of a people to self-government is not a hollow mockery,” he wrote, “then that government must be treated with, not commanded.” His words were a plea for recognition, for the courtesy of a negotiation that would formally dissolve the republic through a treaty or an act of union. He wanted a document, a ceremony, an acknowledgement that something had existed before it was absorbed. Claiborne’s procedure offered none of that. It treated the Supreme Council as a committee of citizens, not a government.

While clerks in St. Francisville turned over their records, the military situation on the republic’s eastern frontier remained unresolved. Spanish forces still held Mobile and Pensacola, and the more ardent West Florida filibusterers, refusing to accept the annexation as the end of their war, had already turned their eyes toward those prizes. The republic was dead on paper, but the fighting—and the larger struggle for the Gulf Coast—was not over.
