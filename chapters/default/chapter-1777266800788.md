He stopped trying to sleep.

The bedroom was a bad place. It was the junction. The walls were thin, the static loudest here. The mattress was a fault line. He’d lie down and the pressure would start—not in his head, but in the air. A thickening. A waiting.

So he sat in the armchair he’d dragged from the living room. He faced the bed. The notebook was open on his lap, a cheap spiral-bound thing. The pen was a cheap plastic stick. The lamp on the side table cast a yellow circle on the floorboards, stopping just short of the bedframe. He’d positioned it that way. The bed was in shadow. A stage.

He waited.

The first shift wasn’t a memory. It was a texture. The flannel of his pajama pants against his thighs became something else. Coarser. A rough wool blanket, scratchy and smelling of lanolin and woodsmoke. He didn’t fight it. He let the sensation coat his legs. He wrote in the notebook: Channel: rough wool. Scent: sheep, smoke. Temp: warm. Duration: 7 seconds. He underlined the time. His handwriting was steady.

The wool faded. His own flannel returned, soft and inert.

He breathed. In. Out. The clock on the dresser ticked. A real sound, mechanical. He focused on it. A pin in the map.

The next was auditory. A voice, male, older, rasping with phlegm. “—don’t care what the schedule says, the coupling’s shot.” Not words meant for him. A broadcast from a machine shop, a garage, someplace with concrete floors and grease. The voice hung in the air of his bedroom, superimposed on the silence. He wrote: Channel: male, 50s?, occupational. Content: mechanical complaint. Ambient: echo, metal clang (distant). Duration: 3 seconds.

It vanished. The silence it left behind was different. Richer. It had edges now.

His knee ached. His own knee, the right one, arthritis from a fall he remembered taking on an icy sidewalk three winters ago. Or had he? The memory of the fall was clear—the shocking cold of the pavement through his trousers, the sharp crack of bone—but the winter itself was vague. A gray season among many gray seasons. The pain was real. He massaged the joint, his fingers pressing into the familiar sore spot beside the kneecap. An anchor.

The bed creaked.

Not his doing. He was in the chair. The sound came from the mattress, the left side, near the pillow. A single, low groan of springs. As if a weight had just settled.

He didn’t look at the bed. He looked at the notebook page. He wrote: Channel: physical presence. Manifestation: bedspring compression. Location: left side. Intensity: single event. Time: 2:14 AM. He noted the time from the clock. His hand shook. He made the numbers neat, boxy. The shaking was in the pen, not the letters.

A smell bloomed. Antiseptic. The sharp, astringent tang of hospital-grade cleaner, layered over something sweet and decaying. The scent of the old man, Arthur Brindle, the one who died here. It filled his nostrils, coated his tongue. He’d researched that smell. It was the ghost of a ghost. He wrote: Channel: Brindle. Scent: isopropyl alcohol, necrotic tissue. Persistence: high. Association: cold spot (absent currently).

He breathed through his mouth. The smell clung.

Then, cutting through the chemical rot, a different scent. Salt air. Coppertone sunscreen. Hot vinyl. A beach day. The smell was so vibrant, so specific, it felt like a shove. His lungs hitched. Channel: Gold. Coastal. Lilith. He didn’t write the names. He wrote: sensory spike: olfactory only. Beach signature. Intensity: 9.

It lasted five heartbeats. When it faded, the hospital smell was gone too. The room just smelled like dust and the dry heat of the radiator.

He realized he was leaning forward, elbows on his knees, the notebook almost sliding from his lap. He sat back. The chair creaked, a real sound, his sound.

This was the work. He was not a haunted man. He was a receiving instrument. A faulty radio, picking up every station on the dial at once. His job was not to listen to one, but to log the interference patterns. To map the static.

The concept was a cold comfort. It fit in his head, neat and logical. It did nothing for the hollow feeling behind his breastbone, the ache that wasn’t physical.

Another texture. The skin on his back prickled, not with cold, but with a specific, forgotten sensation. The heat of a sunburn on the second day, tight and glowing, under the gentle friction of a cotton sheet. The sunburn from the life that wasn’t his. He’d had that burn, once. He’d borne the evidence. Now he only remembered the memory of it. A ghost pain. He wrote: Channel: Gold (confirmed). Somatic memory: sunburn day 2. Location: upper back/shoulders.

He was cataloging his own life’s souvenirs. The thought was so ridiculous a laugh bubbled in his throat. He swallowed it. It turned into a cough.

The thumping started.

Not in the living room. Here. At the foot of the bed, in the dark just beyond the lamp’s glow. A slow, rhythmic thump… thump… thump against the floorboards. The dog’s tail. It was here with him now. It had followed the bowl.

He didn’t look. He wrote: Channel: canine. Auditory: tail thump. Location: bedroom, foot of bed. New behavior: migration from living room.

The thumping continued. A metronome. He found his breathing syncing to it. He broke the rhythm deliberately, inhaling sharply on the off-beat.

A taste flooded his mouth. Not memory. Actual taste. The oily, sulfurous aftertaste of eggs. Tuesday eggs. The ones that had tried to kill him. His stomach clenched. Saliva pooled. He spit into the wastebasket beside the chair. He wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. Channel: biological rejection. Taste: egg residue. Somatic: nausea. Note: no recent ingestion. Cross-contamination.

The thumping stopped.

The sudden silence was a physical void. His ears rang with the absence of sound. He stared at the dark space at the foot of the bed. Nothing.

Then, the bed again. Not a creak. A sigh. A human sigh. Weary, heavy, the expulsion of breath after a long day. A woman’s sigh. It came from the right side of the bed, his side.

Lilith.

He froze. The pen tip hovered above the paper. He hadn’t heard her, not directly, since the ambulance. Her channel had been silent for so long. This wasn’t a memory. This was a real-time event in a room that overlapped his own.

He didn’t write. He listened.

The sheets rustled. A soft, sliding sound of fabric on fabric. A body turning over. Then, a whisper. Her voice, sleepy, muffled by pillow. “You’re thinking too loud.”

The words were for him. For the him in that bed, in that life.

His own throat locked. He couldn’t answer. He wasn’t there. He was here, in the chair, a ghost in his own bedroom.

She sighed again, a smaller sound. “Come to sleep, Cass.”

It was the nickname that did it. The casual, intimate shortening. No one had ever called him that. Not in this life. The sound of it was a key turning in a rusted lock. Something gave way in his chest, not an emotion, but a structural fatigue, like a beam finally sagging under a weight it was never meant to hold.

The sensory spike didn’t build. It detonated.

The yellow lamplight bleached out, replaced by the soft blue-gray of moonlight through a large window. The shape of the window was wrong—wider, with a view of dark, shifting ocean. The smell of salt and night air replaced the dust. The feel of the armchair vanished. He was lying down. The mattress was firm, different. A body’s warmth pressed along his left side. Lilith’s warmth. He felt the curve of her hip against his. The weight of a summer quilt, not too heavy.

He was there. In the coastal house. In the bed he shared with her.

He didn’t move. He let the reality settle over him. It was more real than the chair, than the notebook. The details were granular. The faint, rhythmic crash of waves on sand. A moth bumping softly against the screen outside the window. The taste of mint toothpaste on his own tongue. The slight stiffness in his shoulder from a day of sailing—a stiffness he hadn’t earned, but that his body remembered perfectly.

He turned his head on the pillow.

She was facing him, her eyes closed. Moonlight silvered the line of her cheekbone, the slope of her nose. Her hair was dark against the white pillowcase. He knew the exact texture of it between his fingers. He knew she would smell like lavender soap and sea air.

This was not a memory. This was now. For this version of him, it was a Tuesday night in July. For her, he was her husband, lying beside her, thinking too loud.

His hand, not his decision, lifted from under the quilt. It hovered. He wanted to touch her cheek, to confirm the reality. But the touch would be his. It would be an action. It would collapse something.

Her eyes opened. They were dark, reflecting the moonlight. She looked at him, not through him. She saw him. “Hey,” she whispered. “You okay?”

He couldn’t speak. The him in that bed would have. He’d have said, “Yeah. Just thinking.” He’d have kissed her forehead. This him, the observer, was a stone.

A flicker. A line of interference across the image. The blue moonlight stuttered. For a fraction of a second, the yellow lamplight was back, the shape of his own bedroom window—a narrow rectangle of streetlight orange. The smell of salt was cut with dust.

Her face changed. Not her expression, but its certainty. It softened at the edges, like a station tuning out.

“Cass?” she said, her voice tinged with a new concern.

He was losing the signal. The static roared in the space between the wave crash and the radiator’s hiss. He clenched his will, a futile muscle. Stay.

But the protocol was breaking. The zero overlap rule. He was not just receiving. He was present. His presence was causing interference in her reality. He saw the moment she felt it—a tiny furrow appeared between her brows. She reached for him.

Her hand touched his arm, the one lying on the quilt between them.

The touch was electric. Real. Warm and solid.

The connection shattered.

He gasped. The chair. The lamp. The notebook sliding to the floor with a papery slap. His bedroom. The taste of eggs was back, sour in his mouth. The thumping was back, frantic now, a rapid thumpthumpthump at the foot of the bed.

He was shaking. His whole body trembled, a violent, uncontrollable reaction. The phantom warmth of her touch still burned on his arm. He looked at the skin. Nothing.

He bent, fumbling for the notebook. His fingers were numb. He picked it up. The page was blank where he’d stopped writing. He stared at the empty lines.

He couldn’t log that. There was no category for it. Channel: Gold. Event: mutual awareness. Tactile confirmation. Duration: approximately 22 seconds. Result: system error.

He didn’t write it. He closed the notebook. He set it carefully on the side table.

The thumping slowed, settled back into its slow rhythm.

He had been a receiver. Now he was a contaminant. He had leaked back.

The thought was quiet, cold. It sat in the center of him. He looked at the bed, at the empty space where she had been. Where he had been, with her.

A new detail, one he hadn’t noticed in the spike, surfaced in his mind. On the nightstand in that other bedroom, next to a glass of water, had been a small, smooth stone with a white quartz vein running through it. A keepsake. A paperweight.

An anchor.

He stood up. His knees held. He walked to his own dresser, opened the top drawer. He pushed aside socks, found the marble. The clear glass with its single blue ribbon swirl. He held it in his palm. It was cold. It was the only thing that belonged solely to him.

The thumping stopped. The room held its breath.

He wasn’t an instrument anymore. He was a man in a room with a marble, and he had just touched a ghost who felt real. The map was useless. The lines were all wrong.

He carried the marble to the living room. The apartment felt different, stretched thin, like a worn photograph. He sat on the sofa, the one with the faded floral print. The light from the window was gray, the kind of light that promises nothing. He rolled the marble between his fingers. It was smooth. Unyielding.

The dog, unseen, began to scratch at the bedroom door. A low whine followed. Cassius ignored it. His stomach felt hollow, not with hunger, but with a strange, unfamiliar ache. A yearning. It was Lilith. The one from the coast. The ghost wife.

He remembered her. Not as a blur, a phantom, but as a solid presence. He remembered the specific way her hair fell across the pillow, dark against the white. He remembered the curve of her hip against his. He remembered the faint scent of lavender soap and sea salt that clung to her skin. The memory was so vivid it hurt. It was more real than the floral sofa. More real than the cold marble in his hand.

He closed his eyes. He tried to call her back. To conjure the soft blue-gray moonlight, the sound of the ocean. He tried to feel her warmth beside him. Nothing. The static was too loud. The broadcast had ended.

But the echo remained. A taste in his mouth, not of eggs this time, but of mint. The ghost of a kiss. The whisper of her voice calling his name. Cass. The sound of it resonated in his chest, a deep, mournful chord. No one in this life had ever used that name. It belonged to another Cassius. A happier one. A loved one.

He opened his eyes. The living room was exactly as he’d left it. Overstuffed, slightly dusty, filled with the quiet hum of an old refrigerator. The dog was still scratching at the bedroom door. It was a familiar sound, a comforting one in its persistence. But it wasn’t enough to banish the quiet roar of the static in his head.

He picked up the notebook from the coffee table, where he’d tossed it earlier. It fell open to a page he didn’t recognize. His own handwriting. Neat. Precise. But the words were not his. Not from this life.

July 17th. Lilith. Dinner at the Anchor. Discussed remodel. She wants the bay window by next spring, before the big one. Said it would be perfect for viewing the migration. Asked if I remembered the old lighthouse. Said she’d show me sometime.

He read it again. The big one. The migration. The old lighthouse. None of it meant anything to him. No dinner at the Anchor. No remodel. He had never seen a bay window in his life. He had never discussed a migration. He knew no old lighthouse.

Yet the words were his. The ink was dry. The date was yesterday.

He frowned. This wasn’t a bleed-through. This was an insertion. An active interference with his own timeline. The other Cassius, the one with the coastal wife, had written in his notebook. He had reached across the divide.

A cold dread spread through him. Not physical, not somatic, but a creeping certainty that the lines were blurring. He wasn't just a receiver anymore. He was a canvas. And another hand was painting on it.

He remembered the touch. Her hand on his arm. The warmth. The undeniable reality of it. He had felt her. She had felt him. And now, he had this. A tangible piece of another life, written in his own hand.

The dog stopped scratching. A silence fell over the apartment, heavier than before. The refrigerator hummed louder. The gray light seemed to press in on him.

He flipped back through the notebook. Pages filled with his careful logging of the static. The textures, the smells, the voices. All cataloged. All observed. Never authored. Until now.

His heart thumped a slow, heavy rhythm against his ribs. It wasn’t fear. It was something else. A profound sense of disorientation. Like waking up in a house he thought he knew, only to find a new room, fully furnished, with a stranger’s belongings.

He thought of Lilith. The one from this life. The graphic designer with the sharp wit and the penchant for dark coffee. He remembered her laugh, a bright, clear sound. He remembered the way she’d tilt her head when she was thinking, a small furrow appearing between her brows. He had loved that Lilith. He had lost her, years ago, to a mundane accident. A car crash on a Tuesday morning. The one from his memory. The one that was real.

But the Lilith from the coast. The one who called him Cass. She was real too. Just not in this timeline. And she wanted a bay window. Before the big one. The migration.

Not the old, familiar ache of losing the graphic designer Lilith, but a fresh, immediate grief for the wife he had never known. The wife who had touched his arm. The wife who had worried about him. The wife who wanted a bay window.

He traced the words with his finger. The big one. What did it mean? A storm? A seasonal event? A calving of icebergs? He knew nothing of coastal life. He had lived his entire life inland, surrounded by brick and concrete. The closest he’d come to an ocean was a documentary on public television.

The marble felt cold in his palm. He squeezed it. A small, physical anchor in a world that was rapidly unmooring.

He stood up. He walked to the window. The street below was quiet. A lone car drove past, its tires hissing on the damp asphalt. The sky was still gray. Unremarkable. This was his reality. This was the steady signal. But the other signal, the one that hummed beneath it, was growing stronger. More intrusive.

He looked at the notebook again. The entry, so clear, so specific. He remembered the conversation. He remembered her hand in his. He remembered the taste of the mint. The quiet joy of planning a future event. A bay window. For the migration.

He had lived that moment. He had authored it. And then, it had been erased. Or rather, he had been pulled away from it. Back to this. The faded sofa. The insistent hum of the refrigerator. The dog scratching at the bedroom door.

The thought occurred to him: what if the other Cassius was also writing in his own notebook? Logging the static of this life? What if he, this Cassius, was just a phantom memory for another version of himself? A brief, forgotten entry in a journal in a coastal home. July 17th. Slight headache. A strange feeling of being watched. Briefly remembered a drab apartment and a floral sofa. Chalked it up to indigestion.

Not a physical shiver. A mental one. Of being the ghost.

He walked back to the sofa. He sat down. The marble was still cold. He placed it on the coffee table, next to the notebook. He stared at the handwritten entry.

The big one.

He picked up his phone. He typed coastal migration into the search bar. He typed old lighthouses, Maine. He typed bay window installation. He was looking for clues. Trying to piece together a life he had lived, but did not remember. A life that was now intruding on his own.

The screen glowed, indifferent. He scrolled through images of whales, seabirds, historic landmarks. None of it felt right. None of it resonated. It was all just data. Not memory. Not feeling.

He looked at the marble. It sat there, inert. A simple glass sphere. It was the only thing that hadn't changed. The only constant.

He closed the phone. He closed his eyes. He tried to remember the graphic designer Lilith. Her face. Her laugh. The way she’d tilt her head. He tried to hold onto her. To anchor himself to the life that was supposed to be his.

But the image of the coastal Lilith, with her moonlight-silvered cheekbone and her whispered Cass, was stronger. More vivid. More immediate. She was the one who had reached for him. She was the one who had touched him.

He was losing the signal. The one that was supposed to be his own.

He picked up the marble. He held it tight. He looked at the notebook, at the entry he had not written. He felt a profound sense of melancholy. A future he had planned, a future he would never live. A bay window. The migration.

He opened the notebook again, flipping past the unfamiliar entry. He found the last page he had written. His own words. His own observations. The logs of the static. The interference. He picked up his pen.

He wrote: Channel: Gold. New manifestation: direct authorship within primary timeline. Content: future event planning (coastal). Emotional state: profound disorientation, grief. Warning: contamination increasing.

He paused. He added a single word. Lilith.

He underlined it.

He spent the morning at the small table he called his study, the marble a paperweight on the stack of journals. He wasn't looking for ghosts. He was looking for data. The entry about the bay window was an anomaly, a puncture. He needed to map the perimeter of the wound.

He opened the oldest journal, the one from his thirties. The handwriting was tighter, more anxious. He found the first logged phantom: Channel: undefined. Auditory: child's laughter, distant. Duration: 4 seconds. Location: empty park. He flipped forward. Channel: Silver (provisional). Olfactory: pipe tobacco, cherry-scented. Somatic: pressure of eyeglasses on bridge of nose. He had been cataloging symptoms for decades, naming the channels after metals, after colors, giving the static a false order.

He pulled the current notebook closer. He compared entries side-by-side. The coastal life—Gold—had been the most persistent. It had the most sensory detail: sunburn, salt air, mint, the texture of a summer quilt. It also had the clearest emotional signature: contentment, a low-grade marital warmth. It was a complete life, broadcasting on a strong signal.

The other channels were fragments. A life where he wore eyeglasses and smoked a pipe. A life with a child's laughter in a park. A life that tasted of Tuesday eggs and ended in an ambulance. These were weaker, intermittent.

He started a fresh sheet of paper, drawing a rough timeline of his own life along the top. Below it, he attempted to plot the intrusions of Gold. The sunburn memory at age twelve. The smell of salt air at twenty-four. The first auditory fragment of waves at thirty-one. The pattern was clear: the Gold channel had been strengthening, its intrusions becoming longer, more detailed, more recent. Last night was the peak. Not a memory, but a live feed. Mutual awareness.

He leaned back. The chair groaned. The logical conclusion was that the Gold timeline was somehow winning. Its signal was overriding the others, becoming dominant. It made a terrible kind of sense. A happy life would be a stronger broadcast than a sad one. A life filled with love would resonate more powerfully than a life of quiet isolation.

But the egg channel. The one that killed him. That was a strong signal too, visceral and traumatic. It should have been loud. Yet it only appeared as taste, as somatic rejection. It never offered a scene, a moment of living. Just the death.

The timelines weren't just parallel. They were convergent. And they were hostile. His documentation wasn't a map. It was a target. And his anchor had just turned cold.
