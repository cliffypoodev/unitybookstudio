Reverend Quincy T. Mallory’s faith had died on a Tuesday, but the smell of fried baitfish and burning scripture had never left him. It hung in the sweltering air of his shack, a greasy perfume over the damp rot of plywood and the sharp, chemical reek of the aerator tank. He dipped a hand into the cool water, fingers brushing the smooth, silvery sides of a dozen tilapia. They moved in a slow, placid school, their fins brushing his skin like whispered Hail Marys.

“They’re getting fat,” he said to no one. To the room. To the ghost of his old pulpit voice.

A voice that answered was not a ghost. Low, guttural, and bubbled up from the five-gallon bucket in the corner. “Fat ain’t the point, Quincy. Sanctity is. You’re farming filets, not apostles.”

Mallory didn’t look at the bucket. He’d learned that much. Looking at Bartholomew the catfish while Bartholomew talked was a shortcut to a migraine that felt like a railroad spike driven through his third eye. The catfish was two feet of mottled brown sludge-color, with whiskers that twitched with a judgmental rhythm. He’d pulled it from the reservoir six months ago, hooked clean through the lip. Instead of flopping, it had looked him dead in the eye and said, in perfect if gravelly English, “Your zipper’s down, and your soul’s adrift.”

He’d kept it. What else do you do?

“They’re miracle fish, Bartholomew,” Mallory said, drying his hand on his trousers. The fabric was stained with fish scale and something that might have been old communion wine. “They heal. Or they’re supposed to. Mrs. Henderson’s gout cleared up.”

“Mrs. Henderson switched to gin,” Bartholomew burbled. “The fish just gave her gas. You’re peddling piscine placebo, padre. A hustle wrapped in halos.”

The tilapia were different. He’d found them in a backwater creek after the big storm last fall, the water glowing a soft, sickly green. Eden World runoff, the papers had whispered before they stopped talking about it altogether. These fish, if you nicked one with a knife, the cut sealed over in minutes. Leave a scale behind, and it’d grow a whole new, tiny fish in a week. It wasn’t exactly walking on water, but in the economy of desperate hope, it was legal tender.

His operation was simple. He sold the “blessed” tilapia to the sick and the hopeful out of the back of his rusted Oldsmobile. The money, what little there was went to keeping the aerator running and buying the cheap white bread he used for fish food and his own supper. It was a sacrament of subsistence.

A truck engine growled outside, too close cutting through the hum of cicadas. Not the sputter of old man Peabody’s pickup. This was a low, predatory sound. Mallory’s stomach tightened into a cold, hard knot. He shuffled to the window, peeling back a corner of the sun-bleached curtain.

A black Suburban with tinted windows idled in the crushed-shell lot, dust settling around it like a sigh. Two men got out. They wore sunglasses and guayaberas too crisp for the humidity. One scanned the shack, his hand resting on the bulge under his shirt. The other lit a cigarette with a gold lighter.

“Company,” Mallory said, low.

“La Reina’s tax collectors,” Bartholomew said from his bucket. “Told you the miracle market was getting corporate.”

“You didn’t tell me anything!”

“I said ‘the water whispers of teeth and commerce’ last Thursday. You said I was being dramatic.”

Mallory’s heart was a trapped bird in his ribs. La Reina Blanca. The name was a curse in certain Miami circles, a legend in the swamp. Cartel. Biotech pirate. If she knew about the fish, she wouldn’t want them for gout.

A fist hammered on the door, making the whole shack shudder.

“Reverend Mallory?” The voice was smooth, accentless, polite as a funeral director. “We’d like to talk about your aquaculture.”

“Act natural,” Bartholomew hissed.

“I’m a seventy-two-year-old man who talks to a catfish! What part of this is natural?”

The door rattled again. “Open up, please. We’re here to help.”

Mallory’s eyes darted around the shack – to the tank of miracle fish, to the bucket of blaspheming catfish, to the back window swollen shut with paint and humidity. No exit. He smoothed his thinning hair, straightened his stained shirt, and mustered the last of his pulpit authority. He opened the door.

The heat rushed in, a solid wall. The two men filled the doorway. The smoker was older, with a neat silver mustache. The younger one had the eyes of a bored hawk. Both smiles were as warm as a banker’s handshake.

“Reverend. I’m Mr. Cruz.” Silver Mustache extended a hand. Mallory shook it; the grip was firm, dry. “This is my associate, Mr. Soto. We represent a… conservation group. Interested in unique wetland specimens.”

“I just have some tilapia,” Mallory said, his voice too high. “For the local folks. Faith healing, you know.”

“We know,” Soto said. His gaze swept the room, lingering on the aerator tank. “We heard about the regenerative properties. Fascinating. A leap forward in organic medicine.”

“A gift from God,” Mallory said weakly.

“A gift from Eden World’s faulty containment,” Bartholomew grumbled from his bucket.

Cruz’s smile didn’t flicker, but his eyes cut toward the sound. “You have a pet?”

“A catfish. Doesn’t talk.” Mallory shot a desperate look at the bucket. Shut up, he pleaded silently.

“I narrate,” Bartholomew corrected, a bubbling defiance in his tone. “There’s a difference. One is entertainment. The other is a cry for help.”

Soto took a step toward the bucket. Mallory moved, putting himself between the man and the fish. It was an instinct he didn’t question.

“We’d like to purchase your stock,” Cruz said, pulling a thick envelope from his inside pocket. He laid it on the rickety table. It bulged. “All of them. And any eggs, scales, or… related biological matter. Including the vocal one.”

The money was more than Mallory had seen in a decade. It smelled of ink and possibility. He could fix the roof. Buy real food. Stop.

“They’re not for sale,” he heard himself say. The words surprised him. “They’re a ministry.”

Cruz’s smile finally faded, leaving something polished and cold beneath. “Reverend. This isn’t a negotiation. The swamp is being cleaned up. Unlicensed bio-hazards are being confiscated. We can do this with paperwork and compensation,” he tapped the envelope, “or we can do it with county deputies and biohazard suits. You’ll get nothing but a fine. Maybe jail. Who believes a disgraced preacher who says his fish can heal?”

That truth of it landed like a stone in Mallory’s gut. He was a punchline. The Holy Mackerel. The minister who’d accidentally set a choir robe on fire with a particularly fervent amen.

“You can’t just take them,” Mallory said, but the fight was seeping out of him, replaced by a familiar, weary ache.

“We can,” Soto said. He finally looked directly at Mallory. “We will. Tomorrow, noon. Have them ready for transport. Live. All of them.”

They left then, as quietly as they’d come. The Suburban reversed turned, and vanished in a cloud of white dust. Mallory stood in the open doorway, the envelope burning a hole in his periphery.

“Well,” Bartholomew said after a long moment. “That went swimmingly.”

Mallory sank into his cracked vinyl chair. The shack felt smaller, hotter. “They’re going to turn them into something. A drug. A weapon.”

“Probably. La Reina likes her toys obedient and profitable. Your miracle fish in her hands? That’s not a sacrament. That’s a subscription service.”

“What do I do?” The question was for the room, for God, for the catfish.

Bartholomew was silent for a full minute, his gills working slowly. When he spoke, his voice had lost its sarcastic edge, dropping to a somber, watery rumble. “There’s an ark.”

Mallory blinked. “What?”

“A submerged vessel. East of here, in the deep channels where the black water drinks the old light. Eden World debris. Not a boat. An… incubation pod. From the early days. Krane’s wetware experiments. It’s sealed. Protected.”

“How do you know this?”

The catfish’s whiskers twitched. “The water has memory, Quincy. The swamp is starting to remember. I listen. The ark is a sanctuary. A place your fish could be safe. Could grow without being turned into a product.”

Insane. Following a talking fish to a sunken biblical reference in a poisoned swamp to save a tank of regenerative tilapia from a cartel. It was the kind of plan that got you drowned and forgotten.

“Why would you help?” Mallory asked, suspicion cutting through his despair. “You mock everything. Why care?”

Bartholomew went very still. His murky eyes seemed to look through Mallory, through the shack walls, through time itself. A voice that came out was different again—ancient, weary, laced with a bitter grief that had nothing to do with catfish.

“Because I remember cages, too,” it said. “I remember the taste of betrayal in a garden, and the weight of thirty pieces of silver paid for a creature that loved unconditionally. She used to sit on my rock and share her figs. Then he sold her. Sold everything.”

A cold understanding, absurd and terrifying, dripped down Mallory’s spine. “Who are you?”

The catfish turned its head away, a shockingly human gesture of shame. “I was Gecko. Of Iscariot. A pet. The first life traded for coin in that whole sordid mess. My soul got… recycled. Stuck in the runoff. Ended up in this delightful form.” He flicked a fin at his own body. “The demons in your head, Quincy? They’re just my memories. And my grudge. I don’t want your fish to be the next thing bought and sold. Not by her. Not by anyone.”

Mallory loaded the tilapia into a battered ice chest lined with wet burlap, his hands trembling. The ark. Gecko. Judas’s iguana. His mind reeled, but his body moved on autopilot, fueled by a strange, defiant clarity. This was his penance. For the combustion. For the hustle. For losing the plot.

He hefted the chest. Bartholomew, in a water-filled backpack with a clear plastic window hung from a hook by the door. “The skiff’s around back,” the catfish directed. “The channel’s passable if you hug the mangroves. Don’t hit the gator nests.”

“You’re suddenly very helpful.”

“I have a vested interest. If they dissect your fish, they might find me. My… resonance is in the water here. I’m part of the contamination.”

Of course. Even redemption, for a resurrected reptile was selfish.

The skiff was an aluminum coffin with a sputtering outboard. Mallory loaded the chest settled the backpack on the bow seat, and pushed off into the tea-colored water. The sun was a hammer on his neck. He steered east, into the labyrinth of sawgrass and cypress knees, following Bartholomew’s guttural directions.

“Left at the dead egret. Right where the water turns the color of bad wine. You’ll hear the hum.”

And he did. An hour in, a low, sub-audible vibration began to thrum through the hull, through his bones. It made his teeth ache. The tilapia in the chest grew agitated, flicking against the burlap.

“The swamp’s remembering,” Bartholomew said, his voice barely a whisper. “The Serpent Engine’s lullaby. We’re close.”

The channel opened into a small, still lagoon. The water here was black and oily, reflecting no sky. In the center, something angular and metallic broke the surface—a rounded hatch, crusted with algae and mussels. Wires, or perhaps roots, trailed from it into the depths. It looked less like an ark and more like a drowned boiler.

“Krane’s Folly,” Bartholomew intoned. “The Peaceable Kingdom prototype. A biopod meant to seed life. It just… sat here. Waiting.”

Mallory paddled the last few yards, his heart thumping. He tied the skiff to a protruding strut. The hum was louder here, a physical pressure in his ears. He could feel a strange, pulling sensation in his chest—not fear, but a deep, sorrowful yearning that wasn’t his own.

As his fingers brushed the icy metal of the hatch, a searchlight speared through the twilight from the channel mouth.

A fan boat, silent and fast, glided into the lagoon. Cruz stood at the bow, a shotgun loose in his hands. Soto manned the light.

“Reverend!” Cruz called, his voice echoing across the black water. “We said noon! And you brought the whole sermon to us. How thoughtful.”

Mallory froze, one hand on the ark, the other on the ice chest. The tilapia splashed inside. He was trapped on a metal island in a sea of bad decisions.

Bartholomew’s voice, tight and urgent came from the backpack. “The hatch, Quincy. Open it.”

“They’ll shoot me!”

“They’ll shoot you anyway! Open it and tip the fish in!”

The fan boat edged closer. Cruz raised the shotgun, not aiming, just presenting it. “Step away from the pod, Reverend. This is government property now.”

A wild, holy recklessness seized Mallory. The same feeling that used to precede speaking in tongues. He wasn’t a hustler. Not right now. He was a shepherd. A bad one, with a flock of fish and a catfish for a prophet, but a shepherd still.

He heaved at the circular hatch wheel. It groaned, resisted, then broke free with a shriek of rust. Cold, dank air exhaled from the darkness below.

“Last warning!” Cruz barked. Soto brought the boat alongside the ark, ten feet away.

Mallory grabbed the ice chest. With a grunt born of fried fish and failing faith, he upended it over the open hatch. A cascade of silver, a flash of scales in the searchlight, and the tilapia vanished into the black maw of the ark. The splash echoed up, a tiny, final baptism.

Cruz fired. The shot went wide, peppering the metal hull with a sound like angry rain. “You idiot! Those were assets!”

Soto leapt from the fan boat, landing on the ark with a clang. He lunged for Mallory.

From the backpack, Bartholomew let out a sound that was not a croak, not a hiss, but a word in a language that predated words—a command, or a plea. The black water around the ark began to churn.

The hum spiked into a deafening drone. The lagoon erupted.

Not with monsters. With life. Thousands of tiny, glowing fry—the offspring of lost scales—boiled up from the depths, a swirling galaxy of silver and green. Larger shapes moved beneath them: gar with eyes like cracked amber, a soft-shelled turtle the size of a trash can lid, a school of bass moving in perfect, unnatural unison.

They weren’t attacking. They were gathering. Forming a living, churning barrier between the ark and the fan boat.

Soto stumbled back, his eyes wide. “Cruz… the water…”

Cruz stared, the shotgun forgotten in his hands. This wasn’t in the cartel playbook. This was the swamp talking back.

Inside the open hatch, a soft green light began to pulse, rhythmic, like a heartbeat. The tilapia were home. The ark was awake.

Mallory looked from the terrified cartel men to the swirling, obedient water-life, to the backpack where a catfish that was once an iguana hung silent. He understood, with a clarity that felt like ice water in his veins.

The miracle was never in the healing. It was in the choice. The fish had chosen the ark. The swamp had chosen to protect them. He was just the delivery guy.

“The machine has learned to grow,” Bartholomew murmured, his voice filled with dread and awe.

Cruz met Mallory’s eyes across the living moat. He saw no fear there now, only a weary, defiant peace. The cartel man made a decision. He lowered the shotgun. “Soto. Get back in the boat.”

“But—”

“Now. We’re leaving.”

They retreated. The fan boat’s engine whined as it backed out of the lagoon, the searchlight cutting one last, sweeping arc over the scene—the old man on the ark, the pulsing green light, the shield of living fish. Then they were gone, swallowed by the dark channel.

The hum faded to a background thrum. The glowing fry slowly settled, dispersing into the black water. The barrier was gone.

Mallory sank to his knees on the cold metal, his breath coming in ragged gasps. He heard a splash. Bartholomew’s backpack was empty. He scrambled to the edge.

The catfish was swimming slow circles below, just under the surface. “They’re safe, Quincy. For now. The pod will sustain them. Isolate them.”

“What about you?”

Bartholomew—Gecko—paused. “My grudge is spent. My memory is in the water. I think… I’ll stay here. With the ark. Someone has to keep watch.”

It was as close to a blessing as Mallory would get. He nodded, a lump in his throat. He hauled the hatch shut. The wheel turned easier this time, sealing with a final, hollow clang. The green light vanished.

The skiff ride back was silent. The shack, when he reached it felt emptier than ever. The envelope of money was still on the table. He picked it up, weighed it in his hand. The price of his fish. Of his silence.

He walked outside, into the humid night. He pulled the lighter from his pocket, the one he used for the stove. He held the corner of the envelope to the flame.

It caught quickly, the money curling into black ash that floated up toward the stars. The smell of burning scripture filled the air once more, but this time, it smelled like release.

He was a disgraced preacher with no congregation, no miracle, and no catfish. But for the first time in years, Reverend Quincy T. Mallory felt the faint, fragile stirrings of something that wasn’t a hustle.

Quiet. Enough.

The fire died in his fingers, leaving a singed thumb and a pile of ash on the crushed shells. The night air tasted of charcoal and regret. He went back inside, the silence of the shack now a physical presence. The aerator was off. The tank, empty, reflected the single bare bulb overhead. He sat in his chair and listened to the hum of the swamp, which wasn’t a hum at all but a kind of dense, living quiet.

He must have slept, because the sun was spearing through the curtain cracks when the pounding started again.

This wasn’t the polite, menacing knock of Mr. Cruz. This was the sound of the door being tested for structural failure.

Mallory stumbled up, his back screaming from a night in the vinyl chair. He peered through the curtain. Not a Suburban. A mud-spattered airboat, engine silent was beached on the shell lot. A woman stood at his door, fist raised for another assault. She was somewhere between forty and tough, wearing camouflage pants, a faded Lynyrd Skynyrd t-shirt, and an expression of profound impatience. A pistol was holstered on her hip, and she held a metal lunchbox in her other hand.

He opened the door a crack. “We’re closed. The fish are gone.”

“I’m not here for fish, Reverend. I’m here for the catfish.” Her voice was a rasp, like gravel in a coffee can. “Name’s Mary. Sister Mercy sent me. Heard you got a talking one. We need to borrow it.”

Mallory blinked. “Borrow it?”

“For a consultation. There’s a… situation. Involving a misplaced ark and some very confused wildlife.” She peered past him into the shack. “Where is he?”

Mary’s face fell into a scowl. “Well, that’s just perfect. You let a bio-contaminated, possibly demonic catfish access a Krane-era biopod? Do you have any idea what that thing can do?”

“It saves fish,” Mallory said weakly.

“It’s a wetware command node! It doesn’t save things, it organizes them! Your tilapia are probably down there doing synchronized swimming drills for the Serpent Engine’s halftime show.” She shoved the lunchbox at him. Warm. “Get your shoes. We’re going fishing.”

“I’m not going back out there. The cartel—”

“The cartel’s got bigger problems. A protective mutant gator just ate one of their speedboats near Sawgrass Junction. They’re calling it Saint Chomp. It’s a whole thing.” She eyed him up and down. “You look like you could use a miracle that doesn’t involve gastrointestinal relief. Come on. The duck is waiting.”

“The duck?”

“The emotional support duck. Don’t ask. It’s a long story involving a dinosaur and poor life choices.” She turned and marched toward the airboat. “Clock’s ticking, padre. That ark’s been humming like a fridge full of bees since midnight. My sister’s got a migraine from here to Tampa. That means it’s doing something.”

Something in Mallory broke. Or maybe it unclenched. The sheer, constant absurdity of it—the woman named Mary, the duck, the mutant gator, the humming ark—was too vast to fight. He was a cork in a hurricane. He pulled on his boots, grabbed his hat, and followed her.

The airboat was a rust-fringed aluminum nightmare with stadium seats and a giant aircraft propeller encaged at the back. In the front passenger seat, pecking disconsolately at a bag of corn chips was a small, ordinary-looking mallard duck. It glanced at Mallory gave a single, dismissive quack, and went back to the chips.

“That’s Bartholomew,” Mary said, climbing into the pilot’s seat.

“My catfish was named Bartholomew.”

“Yeah, well, this one’s the original. Or the backup. The taxonomy gets fuzzy.” She fired up the engine. The propeller screamed to life, a sound that shredded the morning calm. “Hang on!”

They shot forward, skimming over sawgrass and lily pads, the flat landscape blurring into a green-brown streak. The duck, Bartholomew, tucked his head under a wing. Mallory gripped the handrail, his knuckles white, his stomach left somewhere back at the shack.

Mary shouted over the roar. “Sister Mercy runs the shelters! She hears things! She heard about your fish heard about the cartel sniffing around, then heard about the ark pinging on… well, let’s call it the weird-shit frequency. She figured the catfish was the key. Something about it being a ‘grudge-holder.’ You know what that means?”

“He said he was Judas’s iguana!” Mallory yelled back.

Mary nodded as if he’d said the sky was blue. “Right. Recycled soul, Eden World runoff, classic case of trans-species karmic hitchhiking. Means he’s got an old score to settle with anyone turning life into product. La Reina’s all about product. Your fish were product. The ark is the ultimate product. See the pattern?”

Mallory saw only spray and terror.

They slowed as they entered a narrow channel, the water black and still. The hum was back, stronger, vibrating up through the hull. The duck lifted his head, alert.

“He says we’re close,” Mary said cutting the engine. The silence that followed was thick, pressed down by the drone. “The ark’s around the next bend. But we got company.”

She pointed. Tied to a cypress knee was a familiar black Suburban, now on pontoons. A makeshift raft. Empty.

“Cruz,” Mallory said quietly.

“And friends. Probably brought the heavy hardware this time.” Mary unsnapped her holster. “Okay. Plan. We get to the ark, we open it, we convince your catfish to shut down whatever choir practice he’s got going on down there, and we get out before the cartel turns us into chum. Questions?”

“Yes! How do we convince him?”

“You’re the preacher. Preach.”

They drifted around the bend. The lagoon was unchanged, the ark a metallic pimple on the black skin of the water. But the life around it was not. The fish were there, thousands of them, but they were no longer a chaotic swarm. They moved in precise, concentric circles around the ark, a living, flickering bullseye. On the ark itself, sitting cross-legged beside the closed hatch was Soto. He held a radio. At the far edge of the lagoon, waist-deep in water and wrestling with a tangled net was Cruz. He was shouting at a school of bass that kept darting just out of reach.

“They’re trying to catch them,” Mary muttered. “Idiots.”

Soto saw them first. He fumbled for the pistol at his waist. Mary already had hers out, resting on the gunwale. “Morning fellas! Fishing license?”

Cruz straightened, his face a mask of mud and fury. “You! This is a restricted area!”

“So’s my patience, and you’re in it.” Mary guided the airboat to a stop a few yards from the ark. The circling fish parted for them, then closed ranks. “We’re here for the catfish. Stand down, and we can all walk away without extra holes.”

Soto’s laugh was shaky. “You don’t understand. This isn’t about fish anymore. Look at this!” He gestured at the perfect circles of wildlife. “It’s a system. A bio-acoustic command network. La Reina can use this. We can all use this!”

From the water near the ark, a familiar, bubbling voice arose. “You can’t use a memory. You can only repeat it.”

Bartholomew the catfish surfaced, his whiskers twitching. He looked… larger. His scales had a faint, copper sheen.

“Gecko,” Mallory said.

“Quincy. You brought a duck. How ecumenical.”

Cruz slogged toward the ark, ignoring the fish that nibbled at his pants. “You will interface with the pod. You will amplify its signal. We have a transmitter.”

“You have a death wish,” Bartholomew burbled. “This pod isn’t a microphone. It’s a graveyard. Krane’s first attempt at a Peaceable Kingdom. He put a lion embryo next to a lamb embryo in a nutrient bath, wired their brains together, and tried to make them sing in harmony. They killed each other in the dark. The only thing that grew here was the silence. And my anger.”

Mary leaned over to Mallory. “This is some heavy petting zoo stuff.”

Soto raised his pistol, aiming at the catfish. “Enough stories. Get in the pod and do what you’re told, or I blend you.”

Bartholomew went very still. Then he made that sound again, the ancient, pre-word command. The water around Soto erupted.

Not with fish. With vines. Thick, rubbery, cable-like vines that shot up from the depths, wrapping around Soto’s legs, his waist, his gun arm. They were studded with thorns that glowed a soft, toxic green. He screamed, firing a shot into the air before the vines yanked him off the ark and into the black water. He surfaced once, thrashing, then was pulled under. A stream of bubbles rose, then popped.

Cruz froze, his face pale.

“The swamp remembers the cages, too,” Bartholomew said quietly. “It didn’t like them.”

Mary had her pistol trained on Cruz. “Okay. New plan. You get on your floaty truck and you leave. You tell La Reina the Everglades are closed for business.”

Cruz looked from the spot where Soto vanished to the catfish to Mary’s unwavering gun. The greed in his eyes warred with primal fear. Fear won. He gave a stiff nod turned, and began sloshing back toward the pontoon Suburban.

The duck, Bartholomew chose that moment to take flight. He didn’t go far. He flew three tight circles over the ark, quacking loudly, then landed on Mallory’s head, digging his little webbed feet into the thinning hair.

“What’s he doing?” Mallory asked, frozen.

“He’s blessing the proceedings,” Mary said, not lowering her gun. “Or he thinks you’re a nest. It’s a fifty-fifty shot.”

Cruz reached his raft. He clambered aboard started the outboard motor with a yank, and without a backward glance, puttered away down the channel. The sound faded into the hum.

Silence, again, but for the drone and the gentle slosh of the circling fish.

Mary finally lowered her pistol. “Alright, Scales. Show’s over. Shut it down.”

Bartholomew ignored her. He looked at Mallory. “Open the hatch, Quincy.”

“Why?”

“To see what grew in the silence.”

Mallory’s legs felt like water, but he stepped from the airboat onto the ark. The metal was warm. The duck remained on his head, a strange, living hat. He knelt, grabbed the wheel, and turned. It moved smoothly, well-oiled by recent use. The hatch opened with a hiss.

The green light pulsed up, gentle now. The smell was not decay, but clean, damp earth and ozone. Mallory peered down.

The interior was not a mechanical chamber. It was a grotto. The walls were lined with a bright, moss-like growth. In the center, in a shallow pool, his tilapia swam. But they were not alone. Among them were other, smaller fish—their scale-children. And on a flat, dry stone in the middle of the pool sat two tiny, perfect skeletons, interlocked. A rodent and a songbird, fossilized in an embrace of bone.

“The Peaceable Kingdom,” Bartholomew said, his voice soft. “It only works when the choice is real. When the cage is open. Krane built a cage and called it paradise. He was wrong.”

He swam to the edge of the hatch, looking up at Mallory. “I led you here because I wanted to see if life, even stolen, contaminated life would choose the cage or the key. You gave them the key. You tipped them in.”

“You used me,” Mallory said, but the anger wouldn’t come.

“I used everyone. I’m a grudge with fins. It’s what I am.” The catfish paused. “But the ark is safe now. It’s not a command node. It’s a sanctuary. Your fish chose it. The swamp will protect it. La Reina’s people won’t find it again. The hum will hide it.”

Mary came to stand beside Mallory, looking down. “So the big scary bio-weapon is… a nature preserve?”

“The most dangerous thing in the world,” Bartholomew corrected. “A place where wounded things are left alone to heal. No experiments. No profits. No sermons.” He looked at Mallory. “Just quiet.”

Mallory understood. This was the end of the hustle. The end of the Holy Mackerel. The miracle was the silence after the sale.

“What about you?” Mallory asked.

“My memory is here. In the water. In the hum. I’m tired, Quincy. Thirty pieces of silver is a heavy weight to carry in a fish spine. I think I’ll sleep now.”

Without another word, Bartholomew turned and swam down into the green light, disappearing among the tilapia. The glow pulsed once, brightly, then settled into a steady, slow rhythm. Like a heartbeat. Like a breath.

Mallory closed the hatch. The duck on his head quacked once, softly, then flew back to the airboat and his corn chips.

Mary clapped a hand on Mallory’s shoulder. “Come on, Reverend. Let’s go home. You can start a new ministry. I hear gator wrestling is looking for a chaplain.”

They motored back through the labyrinth, the hum fading behind them. The shack, when they reached it was still empty. Still quiet.

Mary dropped him off. “You need anything, you call Sister Mercy. She’ll find you. Probably with another weird animal.”

He watched the airboat scream away, the duck a small, dark speck on the bow.

He went inside. He righted the empty tank. He swept the floor. He sat in his chair as the afternoon light stretched long across the plywood.

The silence was different. It wasn’t empty. It was full. It was the silence of fish swimming free in a sunken garden. It was the silence of a debt, not paid, but forgiven. It was the silence after the last word of a prayer you finally meant.

Reverend Quincy T. Mallory, disgraced preacher, failed hustler, and deliverer of fish sat in the perfect, humming quiet of the Everglades. He didn’t feel holy. He didn’t feel saved.

He felt, for the first time in a very long time, like he was exactly where he was supposed to be.

Outside, deep in the black water channels, a single root, wrapped around a broken circuit board, pulsed once.

Green.
