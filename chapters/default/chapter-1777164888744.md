The Civil War had settled the question of union. It left unresolved the question of power.

Abraham Lincoln’s emergency actions between 1861 and 1865—the unilateral suspension of habeas corpus, the blockade of Southern ports, the deployment of unappropriated funds to raise armies—created a template. As documented in his official correspondence and the subsequent legal scholarship, his defense rested on a stark proposition: the Constitution was not a suicide pact. The oath to preserve the Union, he argued implied the authority to take extraordinary, even extra-legal, measures when the nation’s life was at stake. This was not theory. It was practice. The arrest of Maryland secessionist John Merryman and Lincoln’s quiet disregard for Chief Justice Roger Taney’s writ in Ex parte Merryman demonstrated that in a true emergency, the executive could act first and let the courts catch up, if they ever could.

The legal and political aftermath of these actions, however produced not clarity but a durable ambiguity. The Supreme Court, in the 1866 Ex parte Milligan decision seemed to draw a line. It declared that military tribunals could not try civilians where the civil courts were open and functioning, a rebuke to Lincoln’s wartime justice. Yet the ruling came after the fighting had stopped, a retrospective scolding that did nothing to hamper the wartime president. The precedent, therefore was not a legal doctrine but a lived experience. The government had wielded vast coercive power against its own citizens for a national objective. It had survived. The republic had not fractured. A memory of what had been done, and what had been endured, entered the bloodstream of the state.

For the next half-century, this precedent lay dormant but not forgotten. It was studied in law schools, debated by scholars, and cited in political oratory. It existed as a potential, a latent federal capacity waiting for a new kind of fear to activate it. The nation faced no existential military threat on its soil after Appomattox. The crises that followed—economic panics, labor unrest, the assassination of a president—were met with a more constrained toolkit. The presidency, as an institution, receded from the radical edge of Lincoln’s assertions.

The landscape of American liberty in 1914 was, on paper, more robust than in 1860. Constitutional amendments had abolished slavery and expanded citizenship. Yet a parallel system of control had also matured. The Progressive Era’s zeal for order and efficiency fostered an expanded administrative state, one increasingly comfortable with regulation and surveillance in the name of public good. Social anxieties swirled around mass immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, and around the growing voices of socialism and anarchism that spoke in foreign tongues. One fear of internal disintegration, which Lincoln had faced from armed rebellion, now took a subtler, ideological form. The enemy was not a Confederate army massing across the Potomac, but an idea, whispered in a tenement or printed in a radical pamphlet.

When war erupted in Europe, the United States watched from a distance. The debate over preparedness and neutrality was fierce. But when German submarines resumed unrestricted warfare in early 1917 and the Zimmermann Telegram was revealed, proposing a German-Mexican alliance against the U.S. the abstract debates collapsed. The threat was external, a foreign empire. The national response, however would be forged in the furnace of this older, internal anxiety. The precedent of emergency power, born of rebellion was about to be reshaped for a new purpose: not just to defeat an enemy abroad, but to purify the nation at home.

The Progressive impulse was, at its core, a reaction to the chaos of unbridled industrial capitalism. As documented in the speeches of Theodore Roosevelt and the sociological studies of the era, reformers sought to apply rational, scientific principles to the mess of human society. They built professional city manager systems created food and drug safety regulations, and advocated for maximum-hour laws. This was government as engineer, tasked with correcting systemic failures for the collective good. The ethos prized efficiency, expertise, and above all, order. The chaotic strike, the unsanitary slaughterhouse, the corrupt political machine—these were not just social ills but forms of waste to be eliminated by competent administration.

This hunger for order, however, carried an implicit suspicion of discord. The same intellectual climate that produced Jane Addams’s Hull House also produced the pseudoscience of eugenics, which gained respectable traction in academic and policy circles. The drive for national unity, necessary to advance ambitious reform agendas, often shaded into a demand for cultural conformity. The massive influx of immigrants from Southern and Eastern Europe between 1880 and 1914—over 20 million people—was seen by many Progressives not just as a humanitarian challenge but as a threat to social cohesion. These newcomers spoke different languages, practiced different religions, and brought with them political traditions, like socialism and anarchism, that were alien to American civic life.

One fear was not of military invasion, but of ideological dissolution. The 1901 assassination of President William McKinley by a self-proclaimed anarchist, Leon Czolgosz, transformed a fringe philosophy into a national specter. Newspaper editorials and political speeches of the period, collected in contemporary archives began to conflate foreign birth with political radicalism, and radicalism with violence. The Immigration Act of 1903, known as the Anarchist Exclusion Act gave the government new power to bar and deport immigrants based on their political beliefs. It was a legal tool designed not to punish a crime, but to preempt a thought.

This suspicion was institutionalized. The Bureau of Investigation, forerunner of the FBI was established in 1908 within the Department of Justice. Its early mandate was modest, focused on antitrust and interstate crime. But its creation reflected a growing federal appetite for internal security intelligence. Private patriotic organizations, like the National Security League founded in 1914, agitated for military preparedness and “100% Americanism,” framing loyalty as an absolute and non-negotiable state. They promoted propaganda in schools and public forums, teaching that true citizenship required not just legal allegiance but ideological purity.

The contradiction was fundamental. The Progressive Era expanded the state’s capacity to protect citizens from economic exploitation and unsafe products. It also expanded the state’s capacity to monitor citizens for ideological nonconformity. One arm sought to uplift and integrate; the other sought to identify and exclude. Both operated from a shared premise: that experts in Washington, armed with data and a mandate could and should manage the complexities of a modern society. This managerial confidence would prove critical. When the external crisis of world war arrived, the government did not need to invent a bureaucracy of control from whole cloth. It could adapt an existing one, shifting the target from unsanitary conditions to un-American thoughts.

The legal philosophy of the time provided a bridge. Scholars like John Randolph Tucker might champion civil liberties, but the prevailing judicial trend, seen in cases like Patterson v. Colorado (1907), gave wide latitude to states to punish speech deemed to have a “bad tendency” to cause public harm. The clear and present danger test, which would later offer some protection to dissent was still years away. The constitutional landscape, therefore was primed. A society already engaged in a passionate, often anxious debate about the boundaries of belonging and the limits of acceptable discourse had, through its reforms and its fears built the administrative and intellectual scaffolding for a more rigorous policing of both.

The nation that voted Woodrow Wilson into office in 1912, and again in 1916 on the slogan “He kept us out of war,” was thus a deeply divided self. An idealistic reformer, confident in its ability to perfect democracy. It was also a nervous gatekeeper, uneasy about the foreign ideologies simmering within its own cities. The coming war would not create these tensions. It would fuse them, transforming the administrative efficiency of the reformer and the nativist anxiety of the patriot into a single, devastating instrument of state power. The precedent of Lincoln’s emergency, designed to save a union fracturing along geographic lines was now poised to be deployed against a union fracturing, or so many feared, along lines of ideology and ancestry.

Woodrow Wilson’s journey from neutrality to war was a conversion of conscience. As documented in his public addresses and private papers, he initially viewed the European conflict as a distant, sordid struggle of empires, anathema to American ideals. His 1916 re-election campaign capitalized on his success in avoiding entanglement. Yet the escalation of German submarine warfare and the revelation of the Zimmermann Telegram forced a reckoning. The threat was now direct and physical. On April 2, 1917, Wilson stood before a joint session of Congress to request a declaration of war. His speech, preserved in the Congressional Record, framed the decision not as a choice of realpolitik but as a sacred duty. The United States, he argued, must fight to make the world “safe for democracy.” The mission was transcendent, a crusade to redeem the principle of self-governance from autocratic militarism.

This moral framing carried deep domestic implications. If the war was a struggle for the soul of democracy abroad, then the home front became a sanctuary of that soul, requiring protection from any internal corruption. As Wilson’s speeches throughout 1917 and 1918 show, he conceived of national unity not as mere policy support but as a spiritual condition. “It is not an army we must shape and train for war,” he told the nation, “it is a nation.” Total war, a concept emerging from the industrialized slaughter in Europe, demanded a total national effort. Every resource, every industry, every thought had to be marshaled toward victory. Dissent was not just political opposition; within this moral universe, it became a form of sabotage, a weakening of the national will that directly aided the enemy.

Congress moved swiftly to arm the executive with the tools for this total mobilization. The declaration of war on April 6, 1917 was followed by a legislative cascade that transformed the federal government’s relationship to the economy and to individual speech. The Lever Act of 1917 gave the president broad authority to control food and fuel supplies setting prices and rationing essentials. The Selective Service Act of May 1917 instituted a national draft, compelling the service of millions of young men. These measures required compliance, and compliance, in the Wilson administration’s view required a uniform mindset.

The most consequential legal instruments for policing that mindset were the Espionage Act of June 1917 and its more draconian amendment, the Sedition Act of May 1918. The Espionage Act, as analyzed in its legislative history was sold as a necessary shield against spies and the leaking of military secrets. Its language, however was elastic. It criminalized willfully causing or attempting to cause insubordination in the armed forces, and willfully obstructing the recruitment or enlistment service of the United States. This could be applied to any anti-war speech that might discourage a potential draftee. The Sedition Act went further, making it a crime to “utter, print, write, or publish any disloyal, profane, scurrilous, or abusive language” about the government, the Constitution, the flag, or the military uniform. The target was not action, but expression. The goal was not just to prevent sabotage, but to enforce ideological conformity.

Wilson entrusted the enforcement of this new order to his Attorney General, Thomas Watt Gregory, and a young, ambitious Justice Department official named J. Edgar Hoover. Gregory believed, as he stated in official reports, that the government needed to pursue “a policy of repression” toward disloyalty. The Bureau of Investigation, its mandate suddenly and vastly expanded became the operational arm of this policy. The administrative state built by the Progressives—the networks of federal agencies, the culture of expert management—was now directed toward a new kind of social engineering: the identification and neutralization of “disloyal” elements. The precedent of Lincoln’s emergency power, invoked to counter armed rebellion was thus formally repurposed. The state now claimed the same sweeping authority to combat rebellion of the mind. The battlefield was no longer just the trenches of France. The immigrant neighborhood, the socialist meeting hall, the editorial office of a dissenting newspaper. The nation, in the name of making the world safe for democracy prepared to suspend democracy’s most fundamental liberties at home.

The Espionage Act of 1917 moved through Congress with a speed that reflected the national mood. Introduced just weeks after the war declaration, it passed the House on June 5 and the Senate on June 7. The legislative debate, recorded in the Congressional Record was not a careful parsing of civil liberties. A debate shaped by urgency and fear. Proponents argued the nation was under a new kind of siege, vulnerable not only to German spies but to the poison of defeatist ideas. The final text, signed by Wilson on June 15 was a blunt instrument. Its most consequential sections had little to do with classic espionage. Section 3 made it a crime, punishable by up to twenty years in prison, to willfully cause or attempt to cause insubordination, disloyalty, mutiny, or refusal of duty in the military or naval forces. It also criminalized willfully obstructing the recruitment or enlistment service of the United States.

The language was a trapdoor. What constituted “obstruction” of recruitment? Did handing out an anti-draft pamphlet count? Did a speech arguing that the war was a capitalist venture qualify as an attempt to cause disloyalty? The law did not say. It left the definitions to federal prosecutors and the judges who would hear the cases. This vagueness was not a flaw but a feature, granting the state maximum flexibility to confront an ill-defined internal threat. As legal scholars like Zechariah Chafee Jr. would later argue in their critiques, the act severed the traditional link between speech and illegal action. A person could now be imprisoned not for plotting violence or for passing secrets, but for expressing opinions that might, in some indirect way, influence another person’s willingness to support the war.

The contrast with pre-war norms was stark. Prior to 1917, the federal government had almost no role in policing political speech. The Alien and Sedition Acts of 1798 had been a brief, despised aberration allowed to expire. States had laws against libel, incitement, and breach of the peace, but these were narrowly construed around imminent lawless action. The First Amendment, while not yet incorporated against the states was understood as a bedrock principle limiting federal power. The Espionage Act bulldozed that understanding. It federalized the control of opinion, transforming dissent from a local nuisance into a federal crime. The Justice Department in Washington, not a county sheriff would now decide which words endangered the republic.

Enforcement began immediately and followed a predictable pattern. The early targets, as court dockets from 1917 show were often the most visible and vocal opponents of the war: socialists, anarchists, and radical labor leaders. Their crime was not sabotage, but persuasion. In July 1917, federal agents raided the national office of the Socialist Party in Chicago, seizing anti-war literature and records. That same month, the editor of a German-language newspaper in Philadelphia was arrested for publishing an editorial that criticized the draft as unconstitutional. The message was clear. The state would not wait for a clear and present danger to materialize. It would act against the potential for danger inherent in certain ideas.

The legal theory underpinning this repression found its champion in Postmaster General Albert S. Burleson. The Espionage Act granted him the authority to declare any material advocating treason, insurrection, or forcible resistance to U.S. Law as non-mailable. Burleson, a conservative Texan with a deep antipathy to socialism, wielded this power with zeal. He banned from the mail entire periodicals, including the Socialist Party’s American Socialist and the Irish nationalist paper The Gaelic American, not for specific articles but for their general editorial stance. Without access to the postal system, these publications were financially strangled and unable to reach their subscribers. This was censorship not through prior restraint, but through economic suffocation, and it was conducted by an administrative official with no judicial oversight.

The judiciary, for the most part, acquiesced. In the first major test, Schenck v. United States in 1919, the Supreme Court would unanimously uphold the Espionage Act convictions of Charles Schenck, a Socialist Party official who had distributed leaflets arguing the draft was a form of involuntary servitude. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr. writing for the Court, articulated the “clear and present danger” test. But in applying it, he found that Schenck’s words in wartime created such a danger, comparing them to falsely shouting “fire” in a crowded theater. The test, born in this context was less a shield for speech than a sword for the state. It gave a constitutional gloss to the suppression of dissent, legitimizing the idea that in times of perceived crisis, the government could criminalize speech for its tendency to cause harm, even if no actual harm resulted.

The Espionage Act represented more than a wartime measure. It institutionalized a new relationship between citizen and state. The federal government now claimed a permanent interest in the ideological alignment of its population, with a legal mechanism to punish misalignment. The precedent set by Lincoln had been concerned with actions—raising armies, suspending habeas corpus for those actively impeding the war. The Espionage Act concerned itself with opinion. It translated the Progressive-era anxiety about social cohesion and the managerial confidence in state expertise into a penal code. The state, acting as the guardian of national security, appointed itself the arbiter of acceptable political thought. The battlefield of ideas was no longer a metaphor. A jurisdiction, and the Department of Justice had just established its police power over every inch of it.

The first test of the new legal machinery was not against a German spy, but against a socialist newspaper in Milwaukee. The Milwaukee Leader, edited by former Congressman Victor Berger had opposed American entry into the war. Its crime, as detailed in its subsequent indictment published editorials that criticized the conflict as a capitalist enterprise and urged resistance to the draft. In July 1917, Postmaster General Burleson revoked the paper’s second-class mailing permit. The order issued from Washington, cited no specific article. It declared the paper’s general stance to be in violation of the Espionage Act. Overnight, The Leader was crippled, unable to reach its subscribers outside the city. Berger and four other Socialists were indicted under the Espionage Act that winter. In February 1918, a federal judge sentenced them each to twenty years in prison. The message was administrative and brutal: dissent was not a political position but a felony.

German-American institutions were enveloped in the same suspicion. The National German-American Alliance, a cultural and social organization with chapters across the country had championed neutrality. After April 1917, its celebrations of German culture were recast as acts of disloyalty. Under pressure from the Justice Department and patriotic groups, it dissolved in 1918. More telling was the fate of the German-language press. Over five hundred German-language newspapers were published in the U.S. In 1914. Burleson’s postal censors scrutinized every issue. As documented in Justice Department files, editors received warnings for any content that could be construed as sympathetic to Germany, including reprinting speeches from German politicians or questioning Allied propaganda. Many papers, to survive began publishing parallel English-language sections filled with pro-war sentiments and patriotic pledges. The language itself became suspect. Several states banned the teaching of German in public schools. In Iowa, the state governor issued a proclamation forbidding the use of any foreign language in public, on trains, or over the telephone. The war against the Kaiser had become, at home, a war against hyphenated identity.

The most systematic and ferocious application of the Espionage Act was reserved for the Industrial Workers of the World. The IWW—the Wobblies—posed a dual threat. They were radically anti-capitalist, advocating for the overthrow of the wage system, and they were fiercely opposed to what they called a “rich man’s war.” Their organizing drives in the timber, mining, and agricultural sectors were seen as impediments to the war production machine. In September 1917, Justice Department agents, in a coordinated nationwide raid, stormed IWW offices in Chicago, Seattle, Spokane, and dozens of other cities. They seized records, literature, and correspondence. The warrants, based on the Espionage Act, alleged the union was engaged in a conspiracy to sabotage the war effort through strikes and anti-draft agitation.

The resulting trial in Chicago, which began in April 1918 was the largest criminal conspiracy case in American history to that date. The government charged 166 IWW leaders, including the charismatic chairman William “Big Bill” Haywood. The indictment, a document, wove together excerpts from IWW pamphlets, speeches at rallies, and even the union’s founding principles to paint the organization as a seditious enterprise. The specific actions cited—calling for strikes in copper mines essential for munitions—were framed not as labor disputes but as deliberate obstructions of recruitment and service. The courtroom became a theater where the abstract language of the Espionage Act was given flesh. A pamphlet arguing that workers had no stake in the war was presented as evidence of a plot to cause military insubordination.

The judge, Kenesaw Mountain Landis allowed the prosecution to introduce the entire canon of IWW literature as evidence of criminal intent. The defense was prohibited from arguing that the Espionage Act itself was unconstitutional or from questioning the motives behind the war. After five months of testimony, the jury deliberated for less than an hour. It found all defendants guilty. Judge Landis sentenced Haywood and fourteen others to twenty years in federal prison. Dozens more received sentences of five to ten years. The IWW as a functioning national union was decapitated. The verdict sent a seismic signal: organized opposition to the war, when couched in the language of class struggle was not dissent but treason.

The human cost was granular and heavy. In a federal courtroom in Cleveland, a farmer named Jacob Frohwerk was convicted for publishing a series of anti-war articles in his small German-language newspaper, the Missouri Staats-Zeitung. The Supreme Court upheld his conviction in 1919, with Justice Holmes writing that even a “little breath of a rumor” could be criminal in wartime. In Montana, a rancher named Ves Hall was charged under the Espionage Act for saying in a barroom argument that Germany was in the right, that the draft was illegal, and that he hoped the Kaiser would win. He was acquitted by a local jury, but only after the judge instructed them that such statements were indeed criminal. The chilling effect was the point. People began to measure their words not against truth, but against the amorphous standard of loyalty.

The pattern, evident in the court records from 1917 through 1919 was. The Espionage Act was not a surgical tool for catching spies. A dragnet for ideology. Its first casualties were not enemy agents but domestic political movements and immigrant communities deemed insufficiently enthusiastic about the national crusade. The state, armed with the precedent of emergency power and the managerial tools of the Progressive Era had successfully translated a fear of external military defeat into a campaign of internal ideological purification. The battlefield, as the prosecutors in the IWW trial made clear was the mind of the worker, the column of the newspaper, the meeting of the local socialist chapter. Security was no longer just about borders and armies. It was about orthodoxy. And the Department of Justice had become its enforcer, one indictment, one postal ban, one twenty-year sentence at a time.
