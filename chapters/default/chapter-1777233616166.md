The water pooled between the tiles, darkening the grout lines in jagged rivers. Cornelius knelt in the center of it, his tunic clinging to his shoulders, hair plastered to his forehead. His hands shook—not from the cold, but from the thing still coursing through him, the fire that had filled his lungs and now left him hollowed out and trembling. A laugh burst from his throat, raw and unguarded. He hadn’t laughed like that since Germania, before the child.

Peter stood over him, dripping. The basin lay overturned near the impluvium, its bronze rim glinting in the slanting light. He’d expected resistance—some final, stubborn refusal from the Law still coiled in his gut—but the moment his hands touched Cornelius’s head, the words had come unbidden, as if someone else spoke through him. The water had done the rest. Now the centurion’s face was transformed, the rigid control he’d worn like armor dissolved into something unbearably open.

Drusilla was next. She stepped forward without being asked, her bare feet leaving damp prints on the stone. The cook followed, then the scribe, then the stable boy who’d been hiding behind a column. They came in a ragged line, their eyes too bright, their movements unsteady. None of them knew the prayers. None of them had fasted. Peter baptized them anyway.

Thomas grabbed his arm as the last servant rose from the water. “This is madness.” His fingers dug in, urgent.

“They spoke in tongues.” Peter didn’t pull away. His voice was calm, but his pulse hammered in his wrists. “You heard them. What would you have me do?”

“Test the spirits! This could be—”

“What? A trick? A Roman conspiracy to infiltrate us with ecstatic babbling?” Peter shook his head. “No demon would cry out to the God of Israel.”

Across the atrium, Cornelius was helping the cook to her feet. The woman—Lydia, someone had called her—wept openly, her broad hands pressed to her mouth. The centurion murmured something to her in Latin, his voice low and steady. A commander comforting his troops after a battle. Except the battle had been inside them, and the victory was one none of them had known to fight for.

Philip edged closer to Thomas, his sandals squeaking on the wet stone. “The water’s unclean now,” he muttered.

“There is no mikveh in Caesarea that would take us.” Thomas’s laugh was sharp, brittle. “Not after this.”

Peter turned away. The tiles underfoot were slick, the mosaics blurred by water—geometric patterns softened into something almost alive. He’d seen floors like this before, in the homes of wealthy Pharisees. Always precise, always ordered. Now the lines ran together, the colors bleeding at the edges.

Cornelius approached, his steps careful on the wet stone. He didn’t speak at first, just stood beside Peter, his breath still uneven. A drop slid from his hair to his collarbone, tracing the old scar there.

“You’ll come to Jerusalem,” Peter said. It wasn’t a question.

“If you’ll have me.”

The words hung between them, simple and impossible. A Roman centurion in the upper room. Peter exhaled, watching the water creep toward the far wall. It would leave stains. The servants would scrub, but the salt would linger in the pores of the stone, a ghost of this moment.

Behind them, someone began to sing. The melody was rough, unpracticed—a Cappadocian lullaby, maybe, or a sailor’s chant. It didn’t matter. The sound filled the atrium, bouncing off the wet walls, and for the first time since the sheet had descended, Peter felt the knot in his chest loosen.

He bent, righted the basin. The water left in it was murky, flecked with lint and strands of hair. People-water. He dipped his fingers in, let the drops fall back into the pool.

“Again,” he said.

The singing spread, hesitant at first, then gaining strength as others joined. A stable boy—barely more than a child—clapped his hands off-rhythm, grinning when an older servant corrected him with a gentle tap to his wrist. The cook, Lydia, wiped her face with her apron, then threw her arms around the scribe, who stiffened before melting into the embrace. The air smelled of wet wool and salt, of bodies pressed close without the usual careful distance between Roman and servant, Jew and Gentile.

Cornelius watched them, his thumb tracing the edge of his belt where the eagle buckle had once sat. The leather was worn smooth from years of this same absent motion. He should have felt something—pride, perhaps, or the quiet satisfaction of a duty fulfilled—but there was only the hollow where the weight of command had been, and the strange lightness that had rushed in to fill it.

Peter scooped water from the basin, let it trickle through his fingers. The drops hit the tiles with a sound like distant rain. “You understand what this means.”

It wasn't a question, but Cornelius answered anyway. “No.”

A laugh, short and surprised, escaped Peter. “Good. Neither do I.”

Across the atrium, Thomas had retreated to the colonnade, his arms crossed tight over his chest. Philip hovered near him, shifting his weight from foot to foot, his gaze darting between the rejoicing servants and the door.

Peter ignored them. He dipped his hand again, flicked water toward a young girl lingering at the edge of the group. She shrieked, delighted, and darted behind her mother's skirts. The mother—a Gaulish woman with sun-bleached hair—smiled and nudged her forward.

“Again,” the girl demanded, peeking out.

Peter obliged. The droplets caught the light as they arced through the air, brief and shining. The girl clapped her hands over her head, laughing when they landed on her shoulders instead.

Cornelius exhaled. His lungs still ached, as if he'd been holding his breath underwater for years. The scar on his collarbone itched—an old wound from a German spear, poorly stitched by a field medic who'd drunk more wine than he'd poured on the cut. He scratched at it absently, his fingers coming away damp.

Drusilla moved to stand beside him. She didn't touch him, but her sleeve brushed his arm, the fabric still clinging with moisture. “They’ll talk,” she murmured. “The neighbors. The cohort.”

He knew what she wasn't saying: the legate. The emperor's men. A centurion's household babbling in foreign tongues, drenched in some eastern rite—it would draw eyes, and not the kind that looked away.

“Let them.” His voice was rougher than he intended.

She studied him, her dark eyes flickering over his face. Then, deliberate, she reached down and took his hand. Her palm was calloused from the loom, the nails short and practical. A matron's hands, though she'd never borne children. He'd never asked why. Some griefs were too heavy to name.

The singing faltered. A hush rippled through the atrium as the servants noticed their joined hands. Drusilla didn't drop his grip. She lifted her chin, her spine straight as a pilum, and began to sing—a lullaby from her childhood in Antioch, the syllables rounded and warm.

One by one, the others joined in. The melody wove through the columns, softening the edges of the room, blurring the lines between slave and free, Roman and foreigner. The stable boy hummed along, off-key but earnest. Lydia swayed, her apron still damp at the hem.

Peter watched them, his throat tight. This wasn't how it was supposed to be. Baptism required preparation, purification, the careful scrutiny of the elders. It was a covenant, not a—a festival. And yet.

And yet.

The water on the tiles shimmered as the afternoon light slanted lower. The mosaics—once crisp geometric patterns—now swirled like a living thing, the colors bleeding at the edges. A boundary dissolved. A threshold crossed.

Thomas stepped forward, his sandals slapping against the wet stone. “We should go.” His voice was low, urgent. “Before the city gates close.”

Peter didn't move. “They’ll have questions in Jerusalem.”

“They’ll have more than questions.” Philip edged closer, his fingers plucking at his tunic.

“Let them want.” Peter turned his palm up, caught a drop of water as it fell from the eaves. It pooled in the creases of his hand, clear and weightless. “We’ll tell them what we saw. What we heard.”

Cornelius released Drusilla's hand. He crossed to the overturned basin, righted it with a scrape of bronze against stone. The last of the water sloshed against the sides, dark with silt. He could still feel it—the press of Peter's palm against his brow, the rush of something ancient and vast filling his chest until he thought his ribs would crack with the pressure of it.

He dipped his fingers into the basin, brought them to his lips. The taste was faintly metallic, tinged with the salt of sweat and the iron tang of the vessel itself. Not the pure, sweet water of the Jordan, but something made holy by use.

Peter met his gaze across the atrium. A silent understanding passed between them, swift as a spear cast.

“Again,” Cornelius said.

The servants surged forward, their voices rising in a dozen tongues, their hands outstretched. The basin emptied quickly. The tiles grew slick. The light deepened to gold, then to amber, and still the singing didn't stop.

Somewhere beyond the walls of the house, a trumpet sounded—the evening watch calling the cohort to order. The world outside continued, unbroken.

Inside, the water settled. The stains would remain.

The singing faded as the last light bled from the sky. The servants drifted toward the triclinium, their steps slow, their voices hushed now. Someone had lit the lamps—too many, wasteful by any household’s measure—and the flickering light caught the damp patches on their tunics, turning them to gold. Lydia pressed a hand to the small of her back, wincing as she lowered herself onto a dining couch. The stable boy curled at her feet, his head against her knee.

Peter stood near the doorway, watching them. They should have been strangers—a ragtag collection of Gauls, Greeks, Syrians, and Romans—but the way they touched each other’s shoulders, the way their laughter tangled together, made them seem like kin. He rubbed his thumb along the edge of his beard, the coarse hair still damp. The Law whispered in his ear like an old wound: Unclean. Unclean. But the word had no teeth anymore.

Cornelius crossed to the sideboard, poured wine into a dozen cups without counting. His hands moved with the same precision they had in Germania, when he’d measured out rations for his men, but his face was different. The lines around his mouth had softened. He handed a cup to the scribe, who took it with both hands, his fingers trembling.

“You’ll stay the night,” Cornelius said, not looking at Peter.

It wasn’t a request. Peter exhaled through his nose. The road to Joppa would be dark, the way treacherous with bandits and uneven stones. But Jerusalem tugged at him, a weight in his gut. James would have questions. The others would have accusations.

Thomas appeared at his elbow, his breath sour with anxiety. “We should leave now. Beat the rumors back.”

Peter shook his head. “And say what? That we fled?”

“That we were prudent.” Philip hovered behind Thomas, his fingers knotting in the fabric of his belt. “This—what happened here—it needs to be framed carefully.”

“Framed?” Peter turned the word over in his mouth. It tasted like cowardice.

Across the room, Cornelius set down his cup. The sound was quiet, but it cut through the murmur of voices. He met Peter’s gaze, held it. No Roman arrogance there, no centurion’s command—just a man waiting to see if he would be left behind again.

Drusilla rose from her couch, her damp skirts clinging to her legs. She touched Cornelius’s arm, murmured something in Latin. He nodded, but his eyes never left Peter’s face.

The knot in Peter’s chest tightened. He could almost hear the voices in Jerusalem: You ate with them? You touched them? He flexed his hands, remembering the press of Cornelius’s brow under his palm, the way the man’s knees had buckled when the Spirit took him. No amount of framing would make that palatable to the council.

Lydia lifted her cup, her broad face glowing in the lamplight. “To the God of Israel,” she said, her Greek thick with her native accent.

The others echoed her, their voices overlapping. The stable boy giggled when wine dribbled down his chin.

Peter closed his eyes. The image rose unbidden: the sheet descending, the voice saying What God has made clean, do not call unclean. He’d thought it was about food. A petty concession, a loosening of dietary restrictions. But this—this was the thing itself. The boundary shattered.

He opened his eyes. “We leave at dawn.”

Thomas exhaled sharply. “Good.”

“With them.” Peter jerked his chin toward Cornelius. “All who wish to come.”

Silence. Even the stable boy stilled, his cup halfway to his lips.

Cornelius straightened. The old discipline settled over his shoulders like a cloak. “The road isn’t safe for women. Or children.”

“The road wasn’t safe for the Messiah either,” Peter said.

Drusilla’s fingers tightened around her cup. She didn’t look at her husband, but Peter saw the question in the set of her jaw.

“I’ll send six men with you,” Cornelius said. “Armed.”

Peter almost laughed. Armed escorts to Jerusalem. What would the Pharisees make of that? But the alternative was leaving these people here, undefended, with only the memory of water to shield them from the world’s teeth. He nodded.

Thomas made a strangled noise. “You can’t be serious.”

“Where else should he go?” Peter rounded on him. “Back to the barracks? To the temple he can’t enter?”

Philip shrunk back, but Thomas held his ground. “This isn’t what we agreed.”

“Not to what?” Peter’s voice dropped low. “Not to witness the Spirit falling on Gentiles as it fell on us at Pentecost? Not to see God move outside the lines we drew?”

Thomas’s mouth worked. His hands flexed at his sides, the knuckles white.

The scribe cleared his throat. “I could write a letter,” he said, hesitant. “To the council.”

“No letters.” Peter ran a hand over his face. The fatigue was a stone in his bones. “This isn’t something to be explained. It’s something to be seen.”

Cornelius moved to the window, looked out at the darkening city. The lamps of Caesarea glittered like distant stars. “They’ll kill you for this.”

The words hung in the air, blunt and unadorned.

Peter smiled, though it felt more like a grimace. “They’ve tried before.”

Drusilla set down her cup with a sharp click. “I’m coming.”

Cornelius turned. His face was unreadable.

She lifted her chin. “You’ll need someone to speak for the women.”

For a long moment, no one moved. Then Cornelius nodded, once. The decision was made.

The stable boy tugged at Lydia’s sleeve. “Can I go too?”

She ruffled his hair, her eyes bright. “Hush, now.”

Peter watched them, the way the boy leaned into her touch. He thought of the child in Germania, the one Cornelius had spoken of in hushed tones the night before. A life cut short by Roman steel. And now here they were, a Roman and a Jew, bound by something deeper than blood or law.

The lamps flickered. The wine cups emptied. One by one, the servants slipped away to pack what little they could carry.

Cornelius remained by the window, his thumb tracing the edge of his belt. The eagle buckle was gone, melted down years ago, but the habit remained. A soldier’s tell.

Peter joined him, their shoulders nearly touching. The night air carried the scent of salt and smoke, of a city that did not yet know how its foundations had shifted.

“They’ll call it heresy,” Cornelius said quietly.

Peter sighed. “Yes.”

“And you’ll go anyway.”

“Yes.”

Cornelius turned his hand palm-up, studying the calluses there. The scars. “Why?”

The question was so simple, so devastating. Peter looked at the man beside him—the clean lines of his profile, the gray threading his temples, the new openness in his eyes. A centurion. A God-fearer. A brother.

“Because someone did the same for me,” he said.

Outside, the watch changed. The trumpet sounded again, farther away this time. The world kept turning.

Inside, the stains remained.

A pale gray light filtered through the high windows of the storage unit by morning. It did nothing for the stains. Lena woke to the ache of concrete against her hip and the sour taste of old fear in her mouth. The blanket she’d stolen from the motel smelled of diesel and someone else’s sweat.

She sat up. Her knife was still in her hand, the grip pressed into her palm like a brand.

Footsteps scraped outside—not the careful shuffle of the night guard, but a steady, purposeful tread. They stopped outside her unit. Lena was on her feet, back to the corrugated metal wall, blade held low. The padlock rattled.

“Lena?”

It was Marek’s voice, rough with morning. She didn’t answer. The lock clicked open. The rolling door juddered upward a foot, then stopped. A paper bag slid through the gap, followed by a cardboard tray with two paper cups. The door rolled back down. The lock snapped shut.

She waited. The footsteps receded.

Only then did she approach the offering. The bag held a greasy breakfast sandwich, cold. One cup was black coffee. The other was water. She drank the water first, all of it. The coffee she poured out in a slow stream onto the concrete floor, watching it seep into the same cracks as everything else.

She ate the sandwich. It tasted of nothing.

An hour later, the door rolled up properly. Marek stood silhouetted against the warehouse glare. He held a canvas duffel.

“We need to talk.”

“We don’t.”

“Got a job.” He tossed the duffel inside. It landed with a soft thump near her feet. “Clothes. Something that doesn’t scream ‘hiding.’”

“I am hiding.”

“Not well enough.” He leaned against the doorframe. “Voss has a lead on the shipment. The real one. Not the decoy you intercepted.”

That got her attention. She kept her eyes on the duffel. “Where?”

“He’s keeping it close. But he needs a driver for the exchange. Tonight. Dock seventeen.”

“He won’t use me.”

“He will if I vouch. And if you look the part.” Marek’s gaze swept over her, from her stained trousers to the tight line of her mouth. “The woman he knew is dead. Be someone else.”

Lena’s laugh was a short, sharp sound. “Who should I be?”

“Someone who isn’t scared of her own shadow.”

“I’m not scared of my shadow.” She finally looked at him. “I’m scared of what’s casting it.”

Marek was silent for a long moment. “Get changed. We leave in twenty.”

The clothes were simple: dark jeans, a black sweater, boots with worn soles. They fit like a second skin, unfamiliar and too light. Lena kept her own knife, transferring it to a new sheath inside the boot. Marek watched from the driver’s seat of an unmarked van, engine idling.

“The meet’s at nine,” he said as she climbed into the passenger side. “Voss will be there with two of his men. You’re just the driver. You stay with the car. You see anything, you flash the headlights twice and get out.”

“What’s the exchange?”

“Information for hardware. A data chip for a case of military-grade pulse rifles. Voss thinks he’s arming a rebellion. He’s just arming his own ego.”

“And you want the chip.”

“I want what’s on it. Shipping manifests. Names. The network that’s been moving contraband through my city.” Marek pulled the van out into traffic. “You get me that chip, your debt is clear. You can disappear for real.”

“And if Voss figures it out?”

“Then we’re both dead.” He said it like he was commenting on the weather. “But he won’t. He trusts me.”

“He shouldn’t.”

“No,” Marek agreed. “He shouldn’t.”

Dock seventeen was a skeleton of rusted iron and rotten wood, jutting into the black water of the industrial canal. Fog hung in gauzy sheets, muffling the distant clang of shipyards. Lena parked the van where Marek indicated, in the shadow of a derelict crane. Its hook swung gently in the breeze, a mute pendulum.

“Remember,” Marek said, hand on the door handle. “Lights. Twice.”

Then he was gone, swallowed by the fog.

Lena waited. The van’s interior grew cold. She counted the drips of condensation from a leak in the roof—one every twelve seconds. At eight minutes past nine, headlights pierced the fog. A long black car glided to a stop fifty yards away. Two men got out. Voss was the shorter of the two, wrapped in a wool coat. The other was muscle, his neck thicker than his head.

Marek emerged from the fog to meet them. No handshakes.

Lena’s fingers tapped a silent rhythm on the steering wheel. Her eyes tracked the shadows between shipping containers. Nothing moved.

Voss held up a small silver case. Marek nodded to the muscle, who went to the trunk of the black car and hauled out a long metal crate. He set it on the wet ground between them. Marek knelt, popped the latches, lifted the lid. Even from this distance, Lena saw the dull gleam of rifle stocks.

Marek stood. He reached into his jacket.

Voss took a step back.

But Marek only pulled out a data chip, holding it between thumb and forefinger. Voss smiled, a thin stretch of lips. He handed over the silver case.

The exchange was done. Marek tucked the chip into his pocket. Voss’s man slammed the crate shut and heaved it into the black car’s trunk.

Then Voss said something. Marek’s posture changed—a slight stiffening of the shoulders. He shook his head. Voss gestured, a sharp, cutting motion with his hand.

Lena’s hand went to the ignition.

Marek took a step back. Voss’s man moved, not toward the car, but toward Marek, his hand going inside his coat.

Lena flashed the headlights. On. Off.

The man froze, looking toward the van. Marek used the distraction. He didn’t run toward her. He turned and sprinted into the maze of containers.

Voss shouted. The muscle went after Marek, drawing a pistol.

Lena gunned the engine. The van lurched forward, tires spraying gravel. She didn’t head for the exit. She aimed for the gap between two stacks of containers, cutting off the man’s pursuit. He skidded to a halt, raising his pistol.

She didn’t stop. The van’s side mirror exploded. Glass peppered the window. She wrenched the wheel, the van’s back end sliding on the wet concrete. A container loomed. Metal shrieked.

Then she was through, into the open space where Marek was running. She slammed the passenger door open. “Get in!”

He dove. Another shot rang out, punching a hole in the door frame. Lena stamped on the accelerator. The van fishtailed, found traction, and shot toward the dock’s exit. In the rearview, Voss stood by his car, already a shrinking figure in the fog.

“Drive,” Marek gasped, pulling himself into the seat. “Just drive.”

She did. They didn’t speak until the docklands were five miles behind them, the van blending with late-night traffic on an arterial road.

“He knew,” Marek said finally. He was clutching his side. His fingers came away dark. “Or he guessed.”

“You’re hit.”

“Grazed. It’s nothing.” He fumbled in his pocket, pulled out the data chip. It was slick with blood. “But he has the guns. And now he knows you’re with me.”

Lena stared at the road ahead. The streetlights blurred into a continuous streak of yellow. “Where to?”

“Somewhere he won’t look.” Marek leaned his head back against the seat. “I know a place.”

“Another storage unit?”

“A clinic. Then a place.” He pressed his hand harder against his side. “We need to see what’s on this chip.”

“We?”

“You flashed the lights.” Marek turned his head to look at her. A faint, pained smile touched his mouth. “That makes you part of this now.”

Lena didn’t answer. She just drove. The stains were still there, on the concrete floor of a forgotten unit. But now there were new ones, fresh and wet on the passenger seat, and they were hers too.
