Dutch grabbed the other side of the dumpster, his fingers slipping on the greasy steel. "You want to hide in it? Because I've got bad news about how many people that works for in the movies."

Jesus shook his head. "We need a ramp."

Lola was already moving, shoving a pallet toward the fence. "The angel wing. Use the broken one."

The polystyrene wing was light but rigid. They wedged it between the dumpster and the fence, creating a rickety incline. From inside the building, the shouts were getting closer. A radio crackled.

Dutch eyed the razor wire. "This is the worst skate park in Miami."

Jesus climbed onto the dumpster, his sandals finding purchase on the dented metal. He reached down. Dutch took his hand—warm, calloused, no different than any other laborer's—and let himself be hauled up. The dumpster groaned under their combined weight.

Lola went last, her boots scraping as she pulled herself onto the dumpster's edge. Below them, the door burst open. Two security guards spilled onto the dock, their flashlights slicing the dark.

"Go," Lola hissed.

Jesus stepped onto the makeshift ramp. The polystyrene creaked but held. He walked up the wing like it was solid ground, grabbed the top of the fence, and swung himself over. The razor wire didn't touch him.

Dutch stared. "Okay, new rule. No more physics-defying miracles without warning." He inched onto the wing. It bowed under his weight. A flashlight beam swept toward them.

Lola shoved him. "Move!"

The wing snapped. Dutch yelped as he slid down the remaining slope, catching the fence at the last second. His shirt snagged on the razor wire, slicing a neat line across his back. He dropped onto the pavement, landing in a heap next to Jesus.

Lola landed beside them, rolling to absorb the impact. She was on her feet instantly, dragging Dutch up. "Van. Now."

The Wizard Dolphin sat twenty yards away, its psychedelic mural glowing under the streetlights. Dutch fumbled for the keys. The engine coughed to life just as the first guard crested the fence.

"Drive," Lola said, slamming her door.

Dutch stomped the gas. The van lurched forward, clipping a trash can. In the rearview, the guard was shouting into his radio.

Mary leaned forward from the backseat, her rifle propped between the seats. "You guys have fun at Bible study?"

"We made a new friend," Dutch wheezed. "His name is Bobby Lux, and he wants us dead."

Rex's voice rumbled from the cargo area. "SECURITY THREAT LEVEL: TELEVANGELIST. RECOMMEND IMMEDIATE DISENGAGEMENT."

Lola twisted in her seat. "Change of plans. We need to find that yacht before Lux's guys triangulate the van."

Dutch took a hard right, nearly sideswiping a parked Cadillac. "And how do we find a boat that doesn't want to be found?"

"Follow the money," Lola said. "La Reina's not running a biotech lab out of a fishing trawler. Look for something big, expensive, and trying too hard to look legitimate."

Mary snorted. "So, a megachurch with a hull."

They circled the marina twice before spotting it—a gleaming white monstrosity docked at the far end, its hull painted with faux-wooden planks and a cartoonish rainbow arching over the name: The Ark of the Covenant.

Dutch slowed the van. "You've got to be kidding me."

A banner flapped from the railings: All Aboard for Faith & Fellowship! Weekly Bible Study Cruise - Donations Welcome. A line of well-dressed attendees queued at the gangplank, clutching leather-bound Bibles.

Lola's mouth twitched. "The Cocaine Ark. Of course."

Mary racked her rifle. "I love it when they make it easy."

Dutch parked the van behind a stack of lobster traps. "Okay, new problem. We're not exactly dressed for Bible study." He plucked at his sweat-stained Hawaiian shirt.

Jesus reached into the footwell and produced three navy blazers, each embroidered with the ship's logo. "The broadcast booth had extras."

Dutch stared. "Did you—were you planning this?"

"I find it helps to be prepared." Jesus handed a blazer to Lola. "The righteous are bold as a lion."

Mary took the smallest blazer, wrinkling her nose at the polyester blend. "I'm not righteous. I'm heavily armed."

They blended into the line of attendees, Dutch sweating under his borrowed blazer. The man in front of him smelled like aftershave and peppermints.

"First time?" the man asked, eyeing Dutch's sneakers.

Dutch forced a smile. "Just moved here. Heard the sermons were… uplifting."

The man nodded sagely. "Brother Micah has a gift. Last week, he did the loaves and fishes, and the buffet line never ran out of crab legs."

At the gangplank, a crew member checked names off a clipboard. Dutch's pulse spiked. Lola stepped forward, flashing her badge. "Detective Vega, Miami PD. Routine inspection."

The crew member blinked. "We already passed the—"

"New city ordinance," Lola said, pushing past him. "All floating Bible studies require a faith-based wellness check."

Jesus followed, nodding solemnly. "Blessed are the compliant."

The main deck was a study in sacrilegious excess. A tiki bar served communion wine in coconut shells. A DJ booth pulsed with sanitized Christian rock. Waiters circulated with trays of hors d'oeuvres shaped like tiny arks.

Mary grabbed a shrimp ark. "This is the most Florida thing I've ever seen."

Dutch spotted a roped-off staircase guarded by two men in blazers cut to conceal shoulder holsters. "Lab's probably downstairs."

Lola nudged Jesus. "Distraction?"

Jesus walked to the center of the deck, where a man in a white suit—Brother Micah, presumably—was adjusting his lapel mic. Without a word, Jesus picked up a pitcher of water and upended it onto the DJ's equipment.

The speakers died with a sizzle. Every head turned.

Brother Micah froze. "My set—"

Jesus held up a hand. "Let us pray."

A hush fell over the crowd. Dutch used the moment to slip toward the guarded staircase, Lola and Mary flanking him.

One guard stepped forward. "Private event, sir."

Dutch grinned. "Praise the Lord." He punched the guard in the throat.

The second guard reached for his gun. Mary disarmed him with a wristlock, then slammed his head into the railing. He slid to the deck, out cold.

Lola grabbed the radio from his belt. "Move."

They descended into a narrow hallway lined with doors. The air smelled of antiseptic and something sweetly chemical. Dutch pressed his ear to the first door—muffled voices, the hum of machinery.

Mary tested the knob. Locked. She stepped back, raised her boot, and kicked it open.

The room beyond was a gleaming lab, stainless steel tables lined with beakers and microscopes. A technician in a lab coat spun toward them, his face paling.

Lola leveled her gun. "Resurrección. Where is it?"

The technician swallowed. "I just do the payroll."

Mary racked her rifle. "Try again."

A door at the back of the lab burst open. A woman stepped through, her tailored suit worth more than Dutch's van. Catalina "La Reina" Morado.

She smiled, her teeth very white. "Dutch Donovan. I was starting to think you'd forgotten about me."

Dutch's mouth went dry. "Catalina. Funny running into you here."

She held up a vial of amber liquid. "Looking for this?"

Rex's voice crackled through the radio on Lola's belt. "BIOSIGNATURE DETECTED. LAB CONFIRMED. ENGAGING EXTRACTION PROTOCOL."

The hull shuddered. Somewhere above them, the sound of splintering wood and screaming attendees echoed through the ship.

La Reina's smile didn't waver. "You brought a dinosaur to a boat fight."

Dutch backed toward the door. "He's more of a spiritual guide."

The wall exploded inward. Rex's massive head filled the gap, his red eye scanning the room. He focused on La Reina. "YOUR PRODUCT IS CONTAMINATED. CEASE PRODUCTION IMMEDIATELY."

La Reina sighed. "Men always ruin the fun." She pressed a button on her watch. The ship's alarm whooped to life. "Time to swim."

The floor tilted. Water gushed through the hole in the hull. Dutch grabbed a lab table as the room flooded around them.

Lola shouted over the chaos. "Jesus! A little help?"

But Jesus was already walking toward the rising water—not away from it, not around it, but straight into it. His sandals touched the surface. And then he kept walking.

Mary stared. "You've got to be—"

Dutch didn't wait. He jumped. The water was cold, the current pulling him under. He kicked toward the surface, gasping. Above him, the Ark of the Covenant listed sharply to starboard, its rainbow paint job peeling in the salt spray.

Rex's head broke the surface beside him, his eye glowing in the dark. "SITUATION: SUBOPTIMAL."

Dutch spat out a mouthful of seawater. "You think?"

A lifeboat splashed down nearby, filled with terrified Bible study attendees. Brother Micah stood at the prow, his white suit ruined, a bullhorn in his hand. "Repent!" he bellowed.

The yacht's bow plowed into a fake Noah's Ark display at the end of the dock. The impact sent a fifteen-foot fiberglass giraffe toppling onto the lifeboat.

Mary surfaced beside Dutch, shaking water from her hair. "I hate Miami."

Lola treaded water, scanning the chaos. "La Reina?"

Dutch pointed. A speedboat roared away from the wreck, Catalina's silhouette at the helm. The vial of Resurrección glinted in her hand.

Rex's eye tracked the boat. "TARGET ACQUIRED. PURSUIT ADVISABLE."

Jesus walked past them, his feet barely disturbing the water. "The storm is coming," he said, almost to himself. "And the house is still on sand."

Dutch dog-paddled toward shore. "Next time, can we just go to a normal church?"

The water reached Lola’s waist before she realized the lab was designed to flood. Clever. Destroy the evidence, drown the intruders. Two birds, one biblical deluge. She grabbed a floating clipboard, scanning the smeared ink—shipment dates, chemical formulas, a crude map of Miami with circles around the docks. Evidence, if it didn’t dissolve first.

La Reina’s enforcers waded toward them, their suits waterlogged but their guns dry in sealed holsters. The lead thug grinned, revealing a gold tooth with a tiny cross etched into it. “You pick the weirdest places to die, Vega.”

Lola shot him in the knee.

He went down with a splash. His buddies hesitated just long enough for Mary to flip a lab table between them, sending glassware crashing into the water. Rex’s tail slammed through the remaining wall, widening the hole. Cold seawater surged in, knocking the enforcers off their feet.

Dutch surfaced, gasping. “We gotta go before this thing—”

The yacht’s engines roared to life. The whole room tilted violently, sending a wave of equipment sliding toward them. Lola ducked as a centrifuge sailed past her head and shattered against the wall.

Mary grabbed her arm. “Stairs are underwater. Only way out’s through Rex.”

The dinosaur’s eye pulsed red. “BOARDING PROTOCOL INITIATED.” He lowered his head, creating a makeshift ramp between his armored spine and the jagged edge of the hull.

Lola didn’t hesitate. She climbed onto his snout, her boots slipping on wet scales, and hauled herself onto his back. Dutch followed, swearing as saltwater stung the razor cuts on his shoulders. Mary covered their retreat, firing warning shots at the enforcers struggling to stand in the rising water.

The lab was half-submerged now, vials of Resurrección bobbing like tiny amber buoys. Lola snatched one, stuffing it into her bra. Not ideal storage, but better than losing it to the Atlantic.

Rex lurched forward, his massive body carving through the flooded hallway. The yacht listed harder, groaning like a dying animal. Above them, the Bible study attendees screamed as the deck tilted, sending tiki umbrellas and fake arks sliding into the sea.

Jesus stood calmly near the shattered railing, watching the chaos. His borrowed blazer floated around his shoulders, but his sandals stayed dry.

Mary cupped her hands around her mouth. “Hey, Messiah! Little help?”

He turned. Smiled. Held out a hand.

The yacht’s bow rose suddenly, propelled by the engines still fighting the flood. A fiberglass Noah—complete with animatronic smile—broke free from its mount and careened toward them.

Lola grabbed Dutch’s collar. “Jump!”

They hit the water just as the fiberglass patriarch smashed into the deck where they’d been standing. Rex twisted away, his tail taking out a lifeboat davit. The lifeboat dropped like a stone, nearly crushing Mary before she kicked off the hull and surfaced, sputtering.

Dutch treaded water, blinking up at the disaster. “This is the worst baptism ever.”

Lola scanned the wreckage. No sign of La Reina. No sign of Jesus. Just the Ark of the Covenant, half-submerged and spewing confused evangelicals into the bay.

Rex’s head emerged beside her, his voice modulator crackling with static. “SPLINTER GROUP DETECTED. JESUS AND MARY DIVERTED TO STARBOARD LIFEBOAT.”

A speedboat engine revved in the distance. Lola squinted—there, cutting through the smoke: Catalina’s silhouette, already a hundred yards out.

Dutch groaned. “She’s got the good stuff.”

Lola spat out a mouthful of saltwater. “And we’ve got a dinosaur, a cop, and a guy who thinks Hawaiian shirts are formalwear. Priorities.”

“Yeah, well, unless you’ve got gills—”

A wave slapped her in the face. When she cleared her eyes, Rex was gone. The water churned where he’d been, then stilled.

Dutch blinked. “Did he just… dive?”

A second later, the speedboat’s engine cut out with a metallic shriek. The boat lurched, then listed sharply to one side. Something massive and dark moved beneath it.

Mary grinned. “Told you he was a good swimmer.”

Lola grabbed a floating life preserver. “Come on. We’re not done yet.”

The yacht gave one final groan and sank beneath the waves, taking the last of the fake arks with it. Somewhere in the distance, Brother Micah’s bullhorn crackled: “And the waters prevailed upon the earth—”

Dutch kicked toward shore. “Shut up, Noah.”

Mary's rifle was waterproof. That was the only thought in her head as she kicked toward the sinking speedboat, saltwater stinging her eyes. The good Lord—or more likely, some overworked engineer at Heckler & Koch—had blessed the HK416 with enough seals to survive a dip in the ocean. Small mercies.

The speedboat listed to port, its hull groaning under the weight of whatever Rex was doing underneath it. Catalina clung to the steering wheel, her perfect hair now a ruined black wave. She shouted something in Spanish that Mary didn't catch over the sound of rending metal.

A hand grabbed Mary's collar from behind. She spun, elbow cocked—"Easy, Rambo," Dutch gasped, spitting out a mouthful of bay water. "We got bigger problems."

He pointed. Three more speedboats sliced through the wreckage toward them, each filled with La Reina's enforcers. Sunlight glinted off rifle barrels.

Mary grinned. "Finally, a fair fight."

She unslung her rifle, checked the chamber, and fired three quick shots. The first boat's outboard motor exploded in a spray of plastic and gasoline. The second shot took out a thug's sunglasses. The third missed entirely because the yacht behind them chose that moment to capsize with a sound like God clearing His throat.

The wave knocked Mary under. She came up coughing, just in time to see the last speedboat flip like a pancake as Rex surfaced beneath it. The enforcers spilled into the water, screaming.

Rex's voice rumbled through the chaos. "MARINE PREDATION SIMULATION: SUCCESSFUL."

Dutch clung to a floating cooler. "Did he just compare himself to a shark?"

"Focus," Lola snapped. She swam toward the overturned speedboat, where Catalina was struggling to keep her head above water. The vial of Resurrección glinted in her fist.

Mary took a deep breath and dove.

Underwater, the world was all bubbles and panic. Catalina kicked wildly, her silk blouse billowing like a jellyfish. Mary grabbed her ankle and yanked. The vial slipped from Catalina's fingers, tumbling toward the murk.

Something massive and scaled slammed into her ribs. Rex. His jaws closed around the vial with terrifying precision, plucking it from the current like a dog catching a treat.

Mary surfaced, gasping. "Give me that, you overgrown iguana!"

Rex tilted his head. The vial sat on his tongue like a communion wafer. "CHEMICAL ANALYSIS REQUIRED."

Lola treaded water beside them. "Later."

A gunshot cracked across the water. The bullet hit the cooler Dutch was clinging to, sending a geyser of Diet Coke into the air.

Mary spun. One of La Reina's goons had made it to a life raft and was lining up another shot.

Jesus walked past her.

Not swam. Walked. His feet barely disturbed the surface. The goon's mouth fell open as Jesus approached, stepping over waves like they were cobblestones.

"Hey," Mary called. "You gonna do the thing?"

Jesus didn't answer. He just kept walking until he reached the life raft. The goon crossed himself.

Then Jesus kicked the raft.

It flipped like a tortoise, dumping the shooter into the water. The gun sank immediately.

Dutch blinked. "That was… surprisingly violent for Him."

Lola grabbed a floating oar. "Less theology, more paddling."

They cobbled together a raft from debris—a door here, a life jacket there, Rex's tail as an outrigger. Mary perched on the makeshift vessel, wringing seawater from her rifle. The shore wasn't far, but neither were La Reina's remaining men.

Catalina clung to a piece of driftwood, her makeup running down her face in black streaks. "You're dead, Donovan. All of you."

Dutch saluted her with a waterlogged shoe. "Love you too, Cat."

The current carried them toward Eden World's crumbling docks. The abandoned theme park loomed in the distance, its fake volcano streaked with bird droppings. Somewhere in those ruins was the lab that started all this—the rift, Rex, the time-displaced Messiah currently wringing out his socks.

Mary checked her ammo. Three rounds left. "Next time, we steal a helicopter."

Lola nodded at the sinking Ark of the Covenant. Its rainbow paint peeled in the sun as it finally slipped beneath the waves. "Couldn't be worse than that."

Rex's head swiveled toward shore. His eye pulsed red. "BIOSIGNATURE DETECTED. COLONEL CAIN'S FORCES DEPLOYING."

Mary sighed. "Spoke too soon."

On the docks, figures in black tactical gear moved with military precision. They weren't La Reina's thugs. These were Cain's men—trained, armed, and very interested in the bedraggled group floating toward them.

Dutch groaned. "Why is it always more guys with guns?"

Jesus stood on the water beside their raft, studying the shoreline. "The storm is coming," he murmured. "And the house is still on sand."

Mary racked her last round. "Yeah, well, this house is about to get a new foundation."

The current carried them inexorably toward the waiting ambush. Somewhere behind them, a fiberglass Noah's head bobbed to the surface, its animatronic smile still intact.

Mary took aim. "Worst. Vacation. Ever."
