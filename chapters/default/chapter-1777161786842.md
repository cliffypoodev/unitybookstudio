William C. C. Claiborne left New Orleans on December 3, 1810. His column was small—two companies of militia volunteers and a handful of regular officers. They traveled north along the river road, a muddy track flanked by cypress swamps and cane breaks. The governor’s instructions, preserved in State Department archives, were explicit. He was to proceed to Baton Rouge and assume command of the territory between the Pearl and Perdido rivers. He was to announce the president’s proclamation, assure the inhabitants of their rights as citizens, and not negotiate.

The journey took a week. Claiborne’s progress was measured, a deliberate procession intended to signal control rather than haste. As his party moved, news of its coming flowed ahead. In the plantation houses of the Feliciana district, men who had sat in the West Florida convention received the reports. They had known annexation was their goal. Now its agent was on the road, and the reality felt different from the theory. A letter from one planter to a relative in Tennessee, later quoted in a local history, captured the ambivalence. “We have achieved our independence,” he wrote. “Now we are told we must surrender it to the very government whose principles we invoked. It is a peculiar victory.”

In St. Francisville, Fulwar Skipwith received the same intelligence. The president of the seventy-four-day-old republic had been drafting appeals for recognition to London and Paris. He had ordered the detention of Spanish privateers. He had presided over a senate. Now, according to the account he gave in his later memoir, a rider brought word that the American governor was a day’s march south. Claiborne’s force was too small to be an invasion army. It was a symbol. Its purpose was not to fight the West Florida militia but to replace it.

Skipwith called his cabinet. The meeting was brief. The military situation was clear. Philemon Thomas commanded several hundred armed settlers. They could have barred the road to Claiborne’s two companies. But to do so would be to fire on American troops, to transform a local revolt into a rebellion against the United States. No one in the room advocated for that. The republic had been a vessel to carry them into the Union. To sink the vessel at the dock made no sense. Yet Skipwith, as he recorded in his notes, argued for a final assertion of sovereignty. They should receive Claiborne not as a superior, but as the representative of a foreign power. They should demand terms.

His argument found no traction. The other men saw a fait accompli. John Rhea, the constitutional architect, likely understood the procedural truth. The convention that created the republic had also sent a direct plea for annexation to President Madison. Their own actions had invited Claiborne in. To resist him now would be a contradiction. The decision, as recalled in a later letter from a participant, was pragmatic. They would not oppose the American column. They would meet it and ascertain Claiborne’s intentions. But they would not preemptively surrender the authority they had won.

On the morning of December 9, Claiborne’s column moved into the southern reaches of the West Florida republic. The field shifted from swamp to the high loess bluffs. Claiborne’s scouts reported no hostile movement. They saw only the normal traffic of the river—flatboats, a few planters on horseback, enslaved laborers in the fields. The absence of opposition was the first phase of the transfer.

At Baton Rouge, the Lone Star flag still flew over the gatehouse of Fort San Carlos. The garrison, such as it was, consisted of Philemon Thomas’s volunteers. They watched the river road. Thomas had his orders from St. Francisville: observe, but do not engage. The tension was not in the potential for combat, but in the ceremony. Who would lower the flag? Who would give the orders? The fort, which had been captured in a swift, violent transaction, was now to be handed over in a slow, administrative one. The men inside had killed Spanish soldiers to raise that banner. Letting it fall without a shot felt, to some, like a negation.

Claiborne halted his column a mile south of the fort. He sent a courier ahead with a formal letter addressed to “the Civil and Military Authorities of Baton Rouge.” The document, preserved in the territorial papers, was polite and unequivocal. It announced the extension of the laws of the United States over the territory. It requested a meeting for the peaceful transfer of public property. It made no mention of President Skipwith or the Republic of West Florida. The government it addressed was the one it intended to erase.

The courier delivered Claiborne’s letter to the fort. From there, it was sent upriver to St. Francisville. Fulwar Skipwith read it in his office, a room in a plantation house that served as the republic’s executive mansion. The omission of his title was not an oversight. It was policy. Claiborne’s instructions, as Skipwith well understood from his own diplomatic career, were to treat the local authorities as a temporary administrative body, not a sovereign government. The letter was a masterstroke of bureaucratic erasure.

Skipwith was fifty years old, a Virginia-born planter whose life had been shaped by the protocols of statecraft. He had served as U.S. Consul General in Paris under Washington and Adams, negotiating commercial treaties. He had lost a fortune in French property during the Revolution, a fact he mentioned often. His memoir, written years later, reveals a man for whom form and precedent were the bedrock of legitimate power. The revolution he now led had been, in his view, a legal transfer of authority from a derelict Spanish crown to the people of West Florida. They had drafted a constitution. They had elected him. This was not a mob’s uprising; it was a polity.

Claiborne’s approach, therefore, struck him as not merely dismissive, but unlawful. President Madison’s proclamation of October 27, which Skipwith had studied, relied on the claim that West Florida had always been part of the Louisiana Purchase. This was the very argument Thomas Jefferson had advanced and then abandoned as untenable. To Skipwith, a student of international law, Madison’s annexation was an executive overreach, unsanctioned by Congress and executed before any treaty with Spain could be ratified. The United States annexed a foreign republic by fiat. The irony was deep: the republicans of West Florida had invoked American revolutionary principles to break from Spain, only to have the American president disregard those same principles to break them.

Skipwith decided to act. He would not contest Claiborne with force, but he would contest him with paper. On December 7, he drafted a “Protest and Remonstrance” addressed to Governor Claiborne. The document, later printed in full in the Louisiana Gazette, was a legal brief wrapped in the language of wounded sovereignty. He began by noting his gratification at the prospect of union with the United States. This was the republic’s founding aim. But, he wrote, the method of annexation was “an outrage against the Lone Star Flag and the constitution of West Florida.”

He laid out his case point by point. The Republic of West Florida was a de facto and de jure independent state. It had established a government “by the free suffrage of the people.” The United States, by sending troops to occupy its territory without its consent, violated the law of nations. Madison’s proclamation was “an assumption of power not delegated by the federal compact.” The correct, constitutional path, Skipwith argued, would have been for the U.S. Congress to pass an act of admission, treating West Florida as it had treated other territories seeking statehood—through negotiation and agreement.

The protest was, as Skipwith surely knew, a performative gesture. Its power lay not in its ability to stop Claiborne, but in its ability to record a dissent for history. It was the last formal act of the republic’s presidency. He signed it as “Fulwar Skipwith, President of the Republic of West Florida,” asserting the title Claiborne had refused to acknowledge.

He dispatched the protest by special messenger to Claiborne’s camp. Then he prepared to travel to Baton Rouge. If the transfer was inevitable, he would witness it. He would not hide in St. Francisville. His presence would be another form of statement.

Claiborne received the protest on the afternoon of December 9. His response, documented in his official report to Secretary of State Robert Smith, was curt. He acknowledged the document’s receipt but did not engage with its arguments. To do so would have been to legitimize the government that authored it. He filed it away as a curiosity. The real business was the ceremony at the fort.

On the morning of December 10, Claiborne’s column formed up and marched the final mile to Baton Rouge. The American flag was unfurled. The troops, in ragged formation but with bayonets fixed, moved up the bluff toward the gates of Fort San Carlos. Inside, Philemon Thomas’s men waited. They had been told to offer no resistance, but they had not been told to cheer.

Skipwith arrived separately, by horse. He did not enter the fort with Claiborne. He positioned himself among a group of local planters and officials, a spectator to his own government’s dissolution. The scene, as described in multiple later accounts by participants, was tense but orderly. There was no speech by Claiborne, no reading of the proclamation to the assembled crowd. The action was deliberately mundane.

A U.S. Army officer approached the flagpole. The Lone Star flag, a single white star on a blue field, flapped in the cold river breeze. The officer took hold of the halyard. He did not look at the West Florida militiamen. He lowered the flag slowly, hand over hand. When it reached the ground, a soldier folded it into a rough square. No one presented it to Skipwith. No one saluted. It was treated as a piece of captured bunting, not a national symbol.

Then the same officer raised the United States flag. The ceremony took less than three minutes. As the Stars and Stripes reached the top of the pole, Claiborne’s militia company fired a ragged volley into the air—the standard salute to the colors. The sound of the muskets echoed off the fort’s wooden walls. It was the only violence of the day.

Skipwith watched, saying nothing. The protest in his pocket was already obsolete. The republic, as a political entity, ceased to exist at that moment. Its territory became the “Territory of Orleans,” administered by the man who had ignored his letter. Its citizens became American citizens, though Congress had not yet ratified the change. Its president became a private planter, his authority vanished as completely as the flag he had championed.

He turned his horse and rode back toward St. Francisville. The annexation was complete. His principled resistance had changed nothing in the present. Its value, if it had any, would be for a future historian to determine.

The flag came down at approximately ten in the morning. The officer performing the duty was Lieutenant James B. Many of the U.S. 3rd Infantry Regiment, a detail noted in his subsequent service record. His movements were procedural. He untied the halyard from its cleat, took the line in hand, and lowered the blue banner with the single white star. The cloth did not descend in a smooth glide. It caught the December wind, billowing and snapping, resisting the pull toward the ground in a series of jerks. From his position outside the gate, Fulwar Skipwith could see the faces of the West Florida militiamen inside the compound. They were still under arms, having been ordered by Philemon Thomas to stand fast. None saluted. None looked away.

When the flag was gathered, it was not treated with ceremony. A private from Claiborne’s militia took the folded square and carried it into the guardhouse. Its ultimate fate is unrecorded. It may have been stored, repurposed, or discarded. No inventory of captured property from that day lists it.

The raising of the Stars and Stripes that followed was equally functional. Lieutenant Many attached the new flag, hauled it up, and secured the line. The transformation of sovereignty was complete. The volley fired by Claiborne’s company was less a salute than a period at the end of a sentence. That sound rolled over the bluffs and out across the river. In the town below, residents heard it. Some came to their doors. Others, according to a letter from a merchant’s wife published decades later in the Baton Rouge Gazette, went about their chores. “The shooting caused a momentary pause in the kitchen,” she wrote. “Then we resumed the salting of the pork.”

The immediate implication was administrative. Within the hour, Claiborne’s aides posted notices at the fort gate and at the river landing. The documents, copies of which survive in the National Archives, declared the extension of U.S. Laws and the appointment of interim officials. All civil and military officers of the “late local establishment” were to surrender their public accounts. They were invited to reapply for positions under the new authority. The language was careful to avoid the word “government.” It referred only to an “establishment,” a temporary administrative hiccup now smoothed over.

For the residents of Baton Rouge, the change was both heavy and mundane. Their debts and titles, once subject to Spanish law and then to the republic’s hastily drafted statutes, were now under the American system. Land claims would need to be re-proven. Court dockets would be reset. The real shift was not in the flag overhead, but in the ledger books in the courthouse. Yet the symbol of the Lone Star was not so easily filed away. The men who had fought under it retained a defiant attachment. This sentiment surfaced the following spring, in May of 1811, when tensions flared over the pace of American integration and lingering disputes with Spanish Mobile.

On a morning in mid-May, a group of former militiamen, their identities never officially confirmed, gathered at the Baton Rouge fort. The U.S. Garrison was present but, according to the official report Governor Claiborne sent to Washington, was caught unawares. The men produced a Lone Star flag—whether the original or a replica is unknown—and ran it up the fort’s flagpole. It flew for several hours before a U.S. Officer ordered it taken down. No arrests were made. Claiborne’s report treated the incident as a minor act of mischief, not sedition. But the act was a clear signal. The republic was gone, but the identity it forged had not been fully annexed. The flag was a ghost, capable of sudden, vivid reappearance.

The lowering of the Lone Star on December 10, 1810, was a definitive legal and military act. The raising of the American flag in its place asserted a sovereignty that would be enforced by courts and surveyors. Yet the brief, unofficial re-raising seven months later revealed a more complicated truth. Symbols could be taken down by order. The loyalties they inspired were less easily commanded.

President James Madison’s proclamation of October 27, 1810, was a document of strategic ambiguity. He declared the territory between the Mississippi and Perdido Rivers to be “rightfully included” in the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, and he ordered Governor Claiborne to take possession. The justification was not new; it was the dormant claim first advanced by Thomas Jefferson and then quietly shelved when Spain refused to acknowledge it. Madison’s own Secretary of State, Robert Smith, admitted in a confidential 1809 memo, preserved in the State Department files, that the claim was “weak in point of strict legal right.” Yet in the autumn of 1810, weakness was no longer the point. Expediency was.

The president’s decision was driven by a convergence of pressures, each documented in the correspondence of his cabinet. The first was the immediate power vacuum created by the rebellion itself. Spanish authority had collapsed. A sovereign republic, however fragile, now existed. If the United States did not act, Britain or France might. A letter from the U.S. Minister in London, William Pinkney, warned of British interest in securing Gulf Coast ports, a warning Madison cited in a cabinet meeting. The second pressure was domestic. American settlers in the territory, many of them slaveholders with political connections in Tennessee and Kentucky, demanded protection and integration. Their petitions, filled with references to revolutionary rights, landed on the desks of congressmen who saw them as constituents in waiting.

The third, and most compelling, pressure was strategic. Control of the rivers flowing into the Gulf was the key to the economic future of the American interior. The port of Mobile, still in Spanish hands, was the outlet for the Alabama and Tombigbee river systems. As long as a foreign power held it, the cotton and produce of the Mississippi Territory could be choked. Madison’s papers show he received detailed reports from military scouts on the defensibility of Mobile Bay. Annexing West Florida was not an end, but a step. It moved the American border to the Perdido River, placing Mobile in a Spanish salient now surrounded on three sides. The next move was inevitable.

This cold calculation remained in stark contrast to Madison’s earlier posture toward filibusters. In 1804, when Reuben Kemper and his brothers had launched their raids from American soil, President Jefferson—with Madison as his Secretary of State—had denounced them as criminals and ordered their prosecution. The Kempers’ actions were condemned as a threat to peace and a violation of neutrality laws. Six years later, when a different set of American settlers achieved the same end through a more formalized rebellion, Madison embraced the result. The difference was not in the act, but in its success and in the diplomatic cover the convention’s legitimacy provided. The West Florida rebels had created a facsimile of orderly government, complete with a declaration and a constitution. This gave Madison a narrative: he was not invading a Spanish province, nor snuffing out a sister republic; he restored order and claimed pre-existing American land from the temporary administrators who held it.

The legal fiction was thin, and Madison knew it. His proclamation was issued without congressional approval, a fact that drew criticism from his Federalist opponents and from strict constructionists within his own party. John Randolph of Roanoke, in a fiery speech on the House floor in December 1810, called it “an act of executive usurpation,” arguing that only Congress could incorporate new territory. Randolph’s speech, recorded in the Annals of Congress, framed the annexation as a betrayal of republican principle. “We have annexed a people who declared themselves free,” he said. “We have treated their government as a nullity. Is this the doctrine of consent we preach to the world?”

Madison’s defense, articulated in his later messages to Congress, rested on the doctrine of necessity. In a disintegrating world, where Spain could not maintain its authority and European rivals hovered, the executive had a duty to secure the nation’s interests. He presented the annexation as a fait accompli and asked Congress to appropriate funds for the territory’s governance—a request that forced legislators to either ratify his action or risk chaos. They appropriated the funds.

The ultimate ratification, however, came not from Congress but from the map. The annexation of the Republic of West Florida provided the territorial leverage for the next acquisition. During the War of 1812, American forces, citing the Perdido River as the new border, invaded and captured Spanish Mobile in 1813. That military occupation became a diplomatic fact, which in turn was codified in the 1819 Adams-Onís Treaty, where Spain formally ceded all of Florida to the United States. The treaty line followed the contours Madison had established nine years earlier by proclamation.

In the end, Madison’s annexation was a classic act of frontier statecraft: it used a marginal, locally-driven revolution as a lever for national expansion. The principles invoked by the West Florida convention—self-determination, popular sovereignty—were celebrated in the abstract but disregarded in practice. The republic was a useful instrument. Once it had served its purpose, its sovereignty was an inconvenience to be erased by a folded flag and a clerk’s notice. Madison’s genius lay in understanding that on the volatile borderlands, the strongest legal claim was often the one already enforced by the bayonets of the nearest major power. He provided the bayonets, and history provided the lawyers afterward.

The Spanish response to the annexation was a study in imperial exhaustion. In Pensacola, Governor Vicente Folch had received the desperate pleas from his subordinate, Carlos de Lassus, in Baton Rouge. Folch had promised troops, even drafted plans for a relief column. But the column never marched. His correspondence with Havana and Cádiz, preserved in the Spanish colonial archives, reveals a commander trapped by a collapsing system. The treasury in Cuba was empty. The garrison in Pensacola was skeletal, barely sufficient to guard its own walls against the possibility of local unrest or a British raid. The few ships available were needed to protect more vital ports. To dispatch a force into the rebel district, Folch concluded in a November dispatch, would be to invite catastrophe elsewhere. He never left Pensacola.

This paralysis was not merely local. It was a symptom of the metastasis of the Napoleonic Wars into the Spanish empire. In 1808, Napoleon had deposed King Ferdinand VII and installed his own brother, Joseph Bonaparte, on the Spanish throne. The resulting Peninsular War shattered the administrative chains linking Madrid to its colonies. Legitimacy fractured. Juntas formed in Spain and in the Americas, each claiming authority. Shipments of money, troops, and instructions dwindled, then ceased. For a frontier governor like Folch, every decision became a gamble with no guarantee of support or even recognition from a shifting chain of command. His primary duty was to hold Pensacola and Mobile, the last meaningful Spanish footholds on the northern Gulf. To risk those assets for Baton Rouge, a post already lost to a popular revolt, was a calculus that yielded only one answer: do nothing.

The diplomatic response from the official Spanish government—the Cádiz Cortes, which opposed Bonaparte—was equally hollow. It issued formal protests to the U.S. Government, asserting Spanish sovereignty over West Florida and decrying the annexation as an act of banditry. But these protests, recorded in State Department files, were words without weight. Spain had no means of enforcement. Its navy was blockaded or destroyed. Its armies fought for survival in Iberia. The United States, as Secretary of State Robert Smith noted in an internal memo, could afford to treat the protests as “mere diplomatic sound,” acknowledging their receipt while proceeding with the occupation.

The stark truth was that Spain’s claim to West Florida had become a legal fiction sustained only by American forbearance. The transfer of power had occurred not on December 10, 1810, but over the preceding decade, as thousands of Anglo-American settlers crossed into the territory and Spanish officials, lacking the soldiers to expel them, could only record their arrival with increasing alarm. The rebellion and the annexation were the formal registration of a demographic conquest that was already complete. Spain’s inability to respond militarily was the final proof of its retreat. The empire that had once dominated the Gulf now clung to its coastline, awaiting the next, inevitable pressure.

That pressure would focus on Mobile. Folch, understanding the new geography created by Madison’s proclamation, saw the trap immediately. With American territory now extending to the Perdido River, the Spanish post at Mobile was an isolated salient, its landward approaches controlled by the United States. In a remarkable act of realism, Folch even attempted a preemptive negotiation. In early 1811, he privately proposed to the U.S. Government that he surrender Mobile to American forces to prevent it from falling into the hands of “a third power,” meaning Britain. The offer, documented in Claiborne’s dispatches, was rejected by Madison as premature. The President preferred to take it by force when the time was right, which he did in 1813. Folch’s offer, however, captured the essence of Spain’s position: it was no longer a question of whether these territories would be lost, but to whom, and on what terms. The annexation of the Republic of West Florida was the first, quiet acknowledgment by all parties that the terms would be set in Washington, not Madrid.

The annexation of the Republic of West Florida established a template, one that would be refined and reused across the continent. It provided a blueprint for how to transform a local, often violent, power grab into a national expansion with a veneer of legal and republican legitimacy. The model had three components: first, the infiltration and eventual insurrection of American settlers within a weakly held foreign territory; second, the establishment of a provisional government that issued a declaration of independence, creating a narrative of self-determination; and third, the swift intervention of the United States government, not as an invading power but as a stabilizing force reclaiming its own. This was not conquest, the script insisted. It was rectification. It was preordained reunion.

The precedent was immediately noted by contemporary observers. John Quincy Adams, then serving as minister to Russia, wrote in his diary that Madison’s handling of West Florida was a “bold stroke of executive power,” and he worried about its implications. But he also recognized its utility. The success of the operation demonstrated that expansion could be achieved without a formal declaration of war, through the manipulation of facts on the ground. Within a generation, the pattern repeated. In Texas, American emigrants under Stephen F. Austin settled in Mexican Coahuila y Tejas, chafed under centralist authority, rebelled, and declared an independent republic in 1836. The parallels were stark: a lone star flag, a convention of Anglo settlers, a plea for annexation to the United States. The Texas Revolution was a larger, bloodier affair than the West Florida rebellion, but the diplomatic choreography was familiar. When the United States finally annexed Texas in 1845, President John Tyler’s justification echoed Madison’s: it was an act of union, accepting a people who had rightfully won their independence and now sought their natural home.

The doctrine of “preordained reunion,” first articulated in the protests of West Florida settlers, became a central tenet of what would later be called Manifest Destiny. It held that these territories were not foreign lands being seized, but wayward fragments of the American nation being restored. The Louisiana Purchase claim Madison invoked was always dubious, but its very ambiguity was its strength. It provided a legal fig leaf that could be stretched to cover later acquisitions. In 1846, during the Oregon boundary dispute with Britain, expansionists cited the need to “reclaim” the Pacific Northwest for American pioneers who had settled there. The language was one of recovery, not aggression.

The most direct and cynical application of the West Florida model came in 1846 with the Bear Flag Revolt in Mexican Alta California. A few dozen American settlers, hearing rumors of impending war between the United States and Mexico, staged a haphazard uprising in Sonoma, declared a republic, and raised a crude flag featuring a bear and a star. Their rebellion was premature and chaotic, but its purpose was clear: to create a local fait accompli that U.S. Military forces, already en route due to the Mexican-American War, could “rescue.” The California Republic lasted less than a month before U.S. Navy Commodore John D. Sloat arrived, lowered the Bear Flag, and raised the Stars and Stripes, citing the need to protect American lives and property. The script, written in Baton Rouge thirty-six years earlier, required only minor updates.

This method offered complete advantages. It allowed the federal government to maintain a posture of diplomatic innocence while benefiting from the aggressive actions of its frontier citizens. It externalized the initial risk and violence onto private actors—the filibusterers, the settlers, the adventurers. If their revolt failed, as Reuben Kemper’s first raids had, Washington could disavow them. If they succeeded, as Philemon Thomas did, the government could step in to secure the prize, citing the anarchy of the situation and the invoked desire of the inhabitants. A form of geopolitical plausible deniability.

The ultimate lesson of the seventy-four-day nation may be a simple one: the most enduring revolutions are not always the loudest or the longest. Sometimes they are the quietest and the quickest—a brief, local assertion of control that alters the facts on the ground enough to make the next map look inevitable. The republic did not change the world. It changed a few lines on a chart, which changed the flow of rivers of cotton and commerce, which, in the end, changed everything. Its legacy is not a monument in a square, but the very shape of the states of Louisiana and Florida, and the quiet, repeating pattern of how a continent was taken, one convenient rebellion at a time.
