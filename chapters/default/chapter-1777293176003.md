Daisy sneezed into her elbow just as the corpse on the embalming table sat up and asked for a gin fizz.

"Bless you," said the corpse.

The formaldehyde fumes always made her sneeze, which meant she could see the translucent figure lounging on the autopsy lamp like it was a chaise lounge. The ghost wore a top hat at a jaunty angle and smoked a cigar that produced no smoke.

"I don't serve spirits," Daisy said, wiping her nose with a handkerchief embroidered with lilies. Her grandmother's handiwork. The lilies looked more alive than half the clients.

The ghost gasped, clutching his pearl-studded cravat. "Madam, that is spectralist."

The police officer leaning against the doorframe cleared his throat. His badge read NOPD - Supernatural Nuisance Division. "Ma'am, we got a situation."

"Apparition," sniffed the ghost.

"—got himself arrested for disrupting a baptism by floating upside-down over the font and singing La Vie en Rose in what witnesses described as 'a deeply inappropriate baritone.'"

Daisy's left eyelid twitched. She'd inherited the funeral home, the embalming tools, and the eyelid twitch from her father. "And you brought him… here?"

"City ordinance 14.7b," said the officer. "All unlicensed hauntings require a funeral to 'lay the spirit to rest.'" He handed her a citation. "You got 24 hours before we exorcise him into a mason jar."

The ghost adjusted his cuffs. "I want a jazz procession. Twelve-piece brass band. Second line parasols. And I refuse to be buried in anything less than a solid gold coffin lined with velvet from the original 1823 production of Les Misérables."

Daisy's scalpel slipped, nicking the actual corpse's thumb. A single drop of blood welled up. She stared at it. The corpse winked again.

The ghost—who insisted on being called Monsieur Delacroix, "of the time-traveling Delacroixs"—floated through the preparation room, poking at Daisy's tools with a translucent finger.

"You're using steel scalpels? How barbaric." He shuddered. "In my time, we use neutrino beams. Much cleaner incisions."

Daisy counted to seven in French. Her therapist's suggestion. She'd gotten to quatorze before lunch. "Monsieur, if you're from the future, why do you dress like a 19th-century dandy?"

He froze mid-float. "Fashion is cyclical, darling. In 2147, everyone wears hoop skirts and plague masks ironically." He drifted toward her grandmother's antique hearse.

"No gold. City budget only covers pine."

"Pine! I might as well be buried in an IKEA."

The officer popped his gum. "Lady, you gotta control your dead."

Daisy's vision tunneled. She grabbed the ghost's cravat—her fingers passed through, but the intent was clear—and hissed, "You will have a simple service. You will not sing. And you will absolutely not mention time travel to the priest."

Monsieur Delacroix's grin widened. "Oh, sweetheart. You think I'm the problem?"

Behind them, the corpse on the table sat up, stretched, and asked if anyone had change for the streetcar.

By sunset, Daisy had three "ghosts" in custody, a police captain threatening to revoke her mortician's license, and a headache that felt like a piccolo solo in her left temple.

The newest ghost—a woman in a neon-green crinoline who claimed to be a "temporal anthropologist"—leaned against the cooler. "Look, we just need to stage a few hauntings, get 'arrested,' and then you give us funerals. It's the perfect cover."

"Cover for what?" Daisy massaged her temples.

The ghosts exchanged glances.

Monsieur Delacroix sighed. "Fine. We're smuggling souls out of the afterlife. The cosmic border patrol can't track us if they think we're just rowdy spirits getting funeral'd."

Daisy's scalpel clattered to the floor. The corpse in the corner started humming Memory from Cats.

The temporal anthropologist patted her shoulder. Her hand phased through Daisy's collarbone. "Relax. Worst case scenario, you get haunted forever. But hey—" She gestured to the chaos. "At least you'll never be bored."

Outside, the brass band started tuning up for the funeral that was absolutely, definitely going to solve everything.

Daisy grabbed the neon-green crinoline ghost by her puffed sleeves—her hands passed through, but the motion sent a ripple through the fabric like disturbed pond water. "Let me get this straight. You're not dead. You're not even ghosts. You're—what—time-traveling soul traffickers?"

Monsieur Delacroix adjusted his cravat. "Smugglers, darling. Trafficking implies a certain lack of consent."

The corpse on the table sat up and cracked its knuckles. "I consented. Hell, I paid upfront. Dying in 1983 was a terrible career move."

Daisy's eyelid twitched in Morse code. She was pretty sure it spelled disaster. "And the NOPD just—what? Arrests you for singing at baptisms and lets you waltz into a funeral home?"

The temporal anthropologist shrugged. "Cosmic bureaucracy. If we get 'laid to rest' via local ritual, the afterlife border patrol assumes we've been processed. It's like laundering money, but with souls."

Outside, the brass band hit a discordant note. The police captain shouted something about permits.

Daisy pressed her palms to her eyes until she saw fireworks. "Fine. You get your funeral. But when you vanish in a puff of anachronistic smoke, I get to keep the gold coffin."

The procession snaked through the French Quarter at the pace of a drunken snail. The "mourners" consisted of six undercover cops, three confused tourists, and a stray dog wearing a Mardi Gras bead necklace.

Monsieur Delacroix floated ahead in a rented glass hearse—"For ambiance!"—waving like a parade queen. The temporal anthropologist had swapped her crinoline for a black veil and kept "accidentally" walking through solid objects. The corpse from the table (who'd introduced himself as "Gary, synth-pop star, 1982-1983, ask me about my unreleased third album") carried his own head under one arm and handed out mixtapes to pedestrians.

Daisy marched behind them, clutching a clipboard like it was the last life raft on the Titanic. The police captain sidled up to her, squinting at the spectacle. "Ma'am, I gotta ask—why's that ghost taking selfies with my officers?"

"Grief," Daisy said automatically. "It manifests in mysterious ways."

Gary's disembodied head piped up from under his arm. "Like when you realize you'll never hear Careless Whisper on a live stage again." A single translucent tear rolled down his cheek.

The captain made a note on his pad. "Right. And the—" He gestured to where the temporal anthropologist was pretending to sob into the chest of a bewildered street performer.

"Denial," Daisy said. "Classic stage."

The band launched into a jazzy rendition of Another One Bites the Dust. The dog howled along.

By the time they reached the cemetery, Daisy had:

1. Convinced a nun that Gary was a "holographic saint,"
2. Stopped Monsieur Delacroix from auctioning off "authentic future relics" (they were gum wrappers he'd folded into origami swans),
3. Fielded seven questions about why the "ghosts" kept checking their non-existent wristwatches.

The temporal anthropologist materialized beside her at the gravesite. "We gotta move. Border patrol's scanning this timeline." She nodded to the sky, where a single cloud hung motionless, pulsing faintly.

Daisy gritted her teeth. "You couldn't have smuggled souls in a library? Or a DMV?"

"Where's the flair?" Monsieur Delacroix swooped in, adjusting his top hat. "Darling, you can't sneak through the fabric of spacetime. You gotta waltz."

The police captain tapped Daisy's shoulder. "Ma'am, we got a problem." He pointed to Gary, who was now leading a conga line of mourners around a headstone. "That ghost's pelvis movements are… concerning."

Daisy grabbed the nearest "mourner"—a teenager wearing earbuds—and shoved a tambourine into his hands. "Distract them."

"How?"

"Improvise."

The kid blinked, then started beatboxing. The band, confused but professional, matched his rhythm.

The sky-cloud pulsed faster. The air smelled like ozone and cheap cologne.

Daisy herded the "ghosts" behind a marble angel. "Okay, escape plan."

Monsieur Delacroix kissed her cheek. His lips felt like static electricity. "You're a gem. When you die—and you will, we checked—we'll save you a spot in the VIP afterlife. Open bar."

Gary shoved his head back onto his neck with a squelch. "Play my mixtape at your funeral. It slaps."

The temporal anthropologist hesitated. "You could come with us."

Daisy looked at the chaos—the cops taking selfies, the dog wearing sunglasses now, the nun doing the Macarena with a street performer. She thought of her father's funeral home, the formaldehyde fumes, the way the lilies on her handkerchief never wilted.

"Nah," she said. "Someone's gotta keep the dead in line."

The band hit the chorus. The "ghosts" shimmered like heat haze. Pop—gone.

The mourners gasped. The police captain dropped his donut.

Daisy exhaled. The air smelled like magnolias again.

Then something clattered at her feet. A small, silver device with a single blinking light. She picked it up. A label read: Temporal Beacon. Point away from face.

Behind her, the corpse from the table (now just a regular, non-animated corpse) slid gracelessly into the open grave. The band, unfazed, transitioned into When the Saints Go Marching In.

Daisy pocketed the beacon. The dog licked her hand.

Somewhere, in some when, a top hat tumbled through the void.

And the funeral—the real one—went on.

Corinne didn't watch them lower the coffin. She turned her back on the whole production—the priest's droning voice, the handful of Agency staff playing their parts. Her heels sank into the damp earth as she walked toward the line of black cars.

A hand caught her elbow. Silas.

"They're expecting you at the reception."

"Let them expect." She pulled free. "I'm not drinking cheap wine in a hotel ballroom while they lie about how much they'll miss her."

"It's protocol."

"Protocol is what got her killed." The words came out flat, factual. She opened the car door herself. "Are you driving me or do I walk?"

He slid into the driver's seat without another word. The engine purred to life. Through the tinted window, the funeral party looked like a cluster of crows picking at something dead.

Silas drove. Not toward the city, but away from it. Corinne didn't ask. She watched the suburbs bleed into industrial parks, then into the skeletal trees of the state forest preserve.

"Where are we going?"

"Somewhere no one expects us."

He pulled off onto a gravel service road that hadn't seen maintenance in years. The car bounced through potholes until they reached a chain-link fence with a broken padlock. Silas got out, snapped the lock with a bolt cutter from his trunk, and pushed the gate open.

Beyond the fence was a clearing. In the center stood a concrete bunker, its door rusted shut.

"What is this?"

"Backup comms station. Decommissioned in '98." Silas produced a key from his pocket. Not Agency issue—old, brass, teeth worn smooth. The lock clicked. "She showed it to me. After Lisbon."

The door opened on darkness that smelled of damp concrete and ozone. Silas flicked a switch. Fluorescent lights buzzed and stuttered to life, revealing a room frozen in the last century. Banks of dead monitors. A rotary phone on the desk. A cot in the corner with a wool blanket still folded at the foot.

On the desk lay a file. Not a digital pad. Paper.

Corinne didn't touch it. "What's that?"

"What she left. For you." Silas leaned against the doorframe, blocking the light. "She knew they'd scrub her systems. Knew they'd sanitize her apartment. So she kept this here. Old school."

"Why you?"

"Because I'm not in her chain of command. Because we…" He rubbed his jaw. "Because twenty years ago, she pulled me out of a ditch in Belgrade. I owed her."

Corinne approached the desk. Her reflection in the dead monitor showed a woman in a black dress, face pale as the paper she was about to touch. She opened the file.

Photographs. Surveillance shots of a man entering a townhouse in Georgetown. Financial records—offshore accounts with transactions highlighted in red pen. A list of dates and locations. And a single sheet of letterhead from the Office of the Director of National Intelligence.

At the bottom, in Mara's tight cursive: They're not just compromised. They're the source.

"Who?"

"Read the list."

Corinne's eyes scanned the names. Three of them. Two she knew—deputy directors, faces from briefings. The third made her stomach drop. "This can't be right."

"Mara thought so too. Until she followed the money." Silas pushed off the doorframe. "That townhouse? It's a dead drop. The accounts feed into a shell corporation that owns a private security firm. The same firm that provided 'local support' for the Kiev operation."

"The operation where she—"

"Where she died. Yes."

Corinne dropped the file. The papers scattered across the concrete floor. "Why tell me? I'm analytics. I push numbers. I don't do… this."

"Mara said you were the only one who'd understand the pattern. Said you built the model that flagged the anomaly in the first place."

"That was theoretical. A training exercise."

"It wasn't." Silas knelt, gathering the pages. He held up the financial records. "You noticed the payment spikes correlated with intelligence failures. You wrote the memo."

"They ignored it."

"They didn't ignore it. They buried it. And then they buried her." He stacked the papers neatly, placed them back on the desk. "She was getting close. Close enough that they had to make it look like an accident."

The buzzing of the lights filled the silence. Corinne stared at the list of names. Her own signature was on the dismissal form for her anomaly report. She'd signed it without reading, buried under a stack of clearance renewals.

"What do you want me to do?"

"Whatever she would have done."

"She's dead."

"So are we if we do nothing." Silas picked up the rotary phone, listened to the dead line. "They'll come for anyone who knew her. Anyone who might have seen what she saw. That includes you."

"I didn't see anything."

"You will now." He nodded at the file. "That's not a gift. It's a target. Once you know, you're part of it."

Corinne touched the edge of the photograph. The man in the shot was smiling, holding a grocery bag like any normal citizen coming home. She'd sat across from him in the executive briefing room last Tuesday. He'd complimented her presentation on Balkan instability.

"We need evidence. More than this."

"Then get it."

"How? I don't have field access. I don't even have clearance for financials anymore after the Singapore incident."

Silas reached into his jacket, pulled out a keycard. Plain white, no markings. "Mara prepared for that too. There's a safe deposit box. Under a different name. This opens it."

"What's in it?"

"The rest." He pressed the card into her hand. Her fingers were cold. His were warm, calloused. "Don't go to your apartment. Don't use your accounts. They'll be watching."

"Where do I go?"

"You figure that out. I've done my part." He moved toward the door. "I'm leaving the country tonight. Suggest you do the same."

"Wait." The word came out sharper than she intended. "You're just… leaving? After dumping this on me?"

"I'm not leaving. I'm following another thread. Mara had a contact in Berlin."

"You're splitting up. That's stupid."

"It's survival. One of us might make it." He paused in the doorway, backlit by the gray afternoon. "The box is at First Metropolitan Bank. Branch on Seventh. It's under the name Eleanor Vance."

"Who's Eleanor Vance?"

"Mara's mother. Died ten years ago. She never closed the account." He glanced at his watch. "Bank closes at five. You have two hours."

Then he was gone. The door clicked shut. The fluorescent lights buzzed like trapped insects.

Corinne stood alone in the bunker. She looked at the keycard. Plain white plastic. She looked at the file. Then she picked up the photograph of the smiling man and tore it in half. Then quarters. The pieces fluttered to the floor like confetti.

She took the file. Left the photograph scraps. Walked out into the fading light.

The gate swung on its broken hinge. Silas's car was gone. Gravel dust hung in the air where he'd been. She started walking. The funeral heels were not made for gravel roads. She kicked them off, left them in the ditch. The stones bit into her stockinged feet.

It took forty minutes to reach a road with traffic. Her phone was in her purse, along with her Agency ID, her credit cards, her keys. All of it traceable. All of it useless now.

She stood at the bus stop, barefoot, holding a file that could get her killed. The bus arrived with a sigh of hydraulic brakes. The driver looked at her feet, her torn stockings, but said nothing. She fed a dollar bill into the fare box.

"Where to?"

"Seventh Street."

"Transfer at Market."

She took a seat in the back. The file sat on her lap like a bomb. Through the window, the city passed in a blur of normalcy—people shopping, eating, living. None of them knew about the bunker. About the list. About Mara in the ground.

Her stop came. She got off, walked the three blocks to the bank. A marble-columned building trying to look older than it was. Inside, it was all hushed voices and polished wood.

A teller with perfect hair smiled. "How can I help you?"

"Safe deposit box. Eleanor Vance."

"Do you have the key?"

Corinne showed the white card. The teller's smile didn't waver. She led Corinne through a door, down a staircase, into a vault lined with brass boxes. "Number 147. Take as long as you need. Press the buzzer when you're finished."

The box was heavier than she expected. She carried it to a private room, set it on the table. The lock clicked open with the keycard.

Inside: a passport. Canadian. Her photo, but the name said Claire Mercier. A stack of euros. A burner phone. And a single sheet of paper with coordinates and a time: 48.8566° N, 2.3522° E. Noon. Thursday.

Paris. Two days from now.

Underneath, another note in Mara's hand: If you're reading this, I'm gone. Don't trust the system. Don't trust anyone. Especially not the ones who smile.

Corinne pocketed the passport, the money, the phone. She left the coordinates sheet on the table, memorizing the numbers before tearing it to shreds and flushing it down the room's small toilet.

The burner phone had one number programmed. No name. She powered it on. A single text message waited: Cleaners are at your apartment. Don't go home.

She deleted it. Powered off the phone.

When she pressed the buzzer, the teller reappeared. "All set?"

"All set."

Back on the street, the afternoon light was failing. She needed clothes. Shoes. A place to sleep that wasn't connected to her name.

She walked into the first thrift store she saw. Bought jeans, a sweater, sneakers that fit poorly. Changed in the restroom, stuffing the black dress into the trash. She looked at herself in the mirror. Claire Mercier stared back.

The motel was called the Starlight. It had neither stars nor light. She paid cash for one night. Room 12 smelled of mildew and old cigarettes.

She sat on the bed, the burner phone in her hand. Powered it on again. No new messages.

Who to call? Silas was gone. Mara was dead. The three names on the list were traitors. The system was poisoned.

She thought about running. Taking the passport, the euros, disappearing into some anonymous European city. Claire Mercier could work in a bookstore. Drink coffee at sidewalk cafes. Forget about bunkers and dead drops and funerals that were lies.

But Mara's face floated behind her eyes. Not the sanitized version in the coffin. The real Mara—laughing at a stupid joke in the breakroom, ink stain on her finger from leaking pens, the way she'd tap her teeth with her thumbnail when she was thinking.

Don't trust anyone.

Corinne lay back on the scratchy bedspread. The ceiling had a water stain that looked like a continent she couldn't name. She counted the cracks in the plaster. One. Two. Three.

Sleep didn't come. Instead, she planned.

The coordinates were a meeting point. Someone would be there. Friend or foe? Trap or lifeline? No way to know.

She needed to get to Paris. Commercial flights were out—her name would flag. Trains took too long. She thought about the money. How much for a private charter? Too much. How about cargo? Freighters?

Too slow.

There was another way. The Agency had safe houses along the eastern seaboard. One of them kept vehicles. If she could reach it, if the codes hadn't been changed yet…

She sat up. The motel room was dark now. Streetlight glow through the thin curtains.

It was stupid. Reckless. They'd be watching the safe houses.

But they'd be watching for Corinne Shaw, analyst. Not Claire Mercier, Canadian tourist. Not someone barefoot on a bus. Not someone who already knew they were coming.

She took the burner phone. Composed a text to the unknown number: Need extraction. Boston docks. Midnight.

Either help would come. Or they would.

She waited an hour. Then left the motel room, leaving the key on the dresser. The night air was cold. She pulled the thrift-store sweater tighter.

The bus station was still open. She bought a ticket to Boston with the euros, got change in dollars. The bus left in twenty minutes.

She sat on a plastic chair, watching the news on a mounted TV. A weather report. A sports score. Nothing about a dead agent. Nothing about a missing analyst. The world continued, oblivious.

Her bus was called. She boarded, took a window seat. As the engine rumbled to life, she saw a black sedan pull into the parking lot. Two men got out. They moved with purpose toward the station doors.

Corinne slid down in her seat. The bus pulled out onto the highway. The sedan grew smaller in the rear window, then vanished around a curve.

She breathed. Her hands shook. She didn't try to stop them.

The Boston docks at midnight were a landscape of shadows and sodium light. Cranes stood like skeletal giants against the sky. Containers formed canyons of steel.

Corinne stood at the appointed location—pier 7, berth 3. The smell of salt and diesel hung thick. Somewhere, a ship's horn sounded, low and mournful.

Headlights approached. Not a sedan. A van. It stopped twenty feet away. The driver's door opened.

A woman stepped out. Late fifties, gray hair cropped short. She wore a pea coat and work boots. "Claire?"

"Who's asking?"

"Mara sent me. A long time ago." The woman came closer. Her face was lined, eyes sharp. "Said you might come. Said if you did, I should get you where you need to go."

"Where's that?"

"Wherever you say." The woman nodded toward the van. "I've got papers. French passport, if that's your destination. Clean."

"Why would you do this?"

"Because Mara did it for my son. When he got in trouble in Damascus." The woman's voice didn't waver. "She brought him home. This is the debt."

Corinne looked at the van. Looked at the woman. Looked at the dark water between the piers.

"They're watching the airports."

"I know. We're not flying." The woman opened the van's side door. Inside were crates labeled Machine Parts. "You'll be in one of these. Ventilated. Six hours to Halifax. From there, a container ship to Le Havre."

"A crate."

"It's not comfortable. But it's not watched either." The woman touched one of the wooden boxes. "Your choice. Get in or walk away."

Corinne thought about the motel room. The bus station. The black sedan. She thought about Mara in the ground. About the three names on the list.

She climbed into the van.

The woman helped her into the crate. It was larger than she expected, lined with foam, a battery-powered light strip, a bottle of water. A small vent near the top brought in cold air.

"Once I seal it, no opening until Halifax. There's a button for the light. Conserve the battery."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet." The woman's face appeared in the opening one last time. "Mara said you were smart. Said you'd know what to do with the truth. I hope she was right."

The lid closed. Darkness. The sound of latches snapping shut. Then the rumble of the van's engine.

Movement. The sway of turns. The vibration of the road.

Corinne lay in the dark, feeling the miles pass beneath her. She counted them. One. Two. A hundred. A thousand.

Somewhere over the Atlantic, in the belly of a ship, she finally slept. And dreamed of a bunker, and a file, and a woman who smiled while carrying groceries.
