Dutch's trailer smelled like failure and bong water. Jesus stood in the doorway, one bare foot on the linoleum, the other still on the cinder block step. The place was a monument to bad decisions—beer cans stacked into unstable towers, a deflated air mattress draped with a Confederate flag (Dutch claimed it was "ironic"), and a coffee table made from a cable spool that still had the Sharpie'd tally marks from some forgotten construction job.

"Home sweet shithole," Dutch said, kicking a pizza box out of the way. "Make yourself at—oh, right. You don’t sit. You just, what, hover?"

Jesus ran a finger along the edge of the counter. The grime came away black. He examined it, then wiped it on his robe. "You live here?"

"Temporarily. Until my ship comes in. Which, coincidentally, might be sooner than expected." Dutch waggled his eyebrows at Jesus, who did not react.

A ceiling fan wobbled overhead, missing two blades. The remaining three thumped unevenly, stirring the thick air. Jesus watched it for a moment, transfixed by its doomed rotation. It reminded him of something—maybe a parable, maybe just the way things fell apart when left unchecked.

Dutch yanked open the mini-fridge. "You hungry? I got…" He peered inside. "Half a burrito. Maybe three days old. Could be four." He sniffed it. "Still good."

Jesus shook his head. His stomach had been empty since the crucifixion, but he wasn’t about to test it on whatever biohazard Dutch was offering.

Dutch shrugged and took a bite. "Suit yourself." He chewed thoughtfully. "Okay, so. Priorities. One, we gotta figure out what the hell you can do besides turn water into drugs—no offense, but that’s kinda niche. Two, we gotta monetize it. Three, we gotta keep you away from the feds, the cartels, and, uh, whatever the hell that dinosaur was."

Jesus wandered over to a folding table covered in plastic bags and a sad-looking plant under a desk lamp. He touched one of the wilted leaves.

Dutch nearly choked. "Hey! Don’t—that’s my crop, man. That’s premium shit."

Jesus tilted his head. "It’s dying."

"It’s stressed. There’s a difference."

The plant was yellow at the edges, its buds sparse and sad. Jesus cupped one in his palm. A faint warmth spread through his fingers.

Dutch didn’t notice. He was too busy rummaging through a drawer. "I got a guy in Little Havana who’ll move anything, but he’s picky."

The plant perked up. The yellow receded, replaced by a deep, vibrant green. The buds swelled, glistening with fresh resin. A rich, earthy scent filled the trailer—like pine and citrus and something deeper, something holy.

Dutch froze mid-sentence. His nose twitched. He turned slowly.

The plant was now twice its original size, its leaves thick and glossy, its buds dense enough to bend the stems. Dutch’s mouth fell open.

"…What the fuck."

Jesus withdrew his hand. The glow faded.

Dutch staggered forward, gripping the table for support. "Did you just—did you bless my weed?"

Jesus examined his palm. "I don’t know."

Dutch grabbed a bud and crushed it between his fingers. The scent exploded—sharp, sweet, with an undertone of something unplaceable. Like incense and lightning. He brought it to his nose, inhaled deeply, and immediately coughed so hard he saw stars.

"Oh my God," he wheezed, eyes watering. "That’s not weed. That’s divine intervention."

Jesus frowned. "Is that bad?"

Dutch clutched the bud to his chest like a sacred relic. "Bad? Dude. This is Burning Bush OG."

Outside, a car door slammed.

Dutch’s grin vanished. He shoved the bud into his pocket and yanked Jesus away from the table. "Shit. That’s not good."

Jesus blinked. "Who is it?"

Dutch peered through the blinds. "Either the cops, the cartel, or my landlord. Either way, we’re screwed."

The knock came—three sharp raps.

Dutch swallowed hard. "Okay. New plan. You stay here. Don’t touch anything. Don’t bless anything. And for the love of God, don’t answer the door."

Jesus nodded.

Dutch took a deep breath, smoothed his shirt, and opened the door.

A woman in a rumpled suit stood on the step, her badge glinting in the sun. "Dutch Donovan?"

Dutch forced a smile. "Depends. Who’s asking?"

She didn’t smile back. "Detective Lola Vega. Miami PD. We need to talk about your dinosaur."

Dutch’s eye twitched. "My what?"

Jesus, unnoticed in the shadows, reached for another wilted plant. His fingers brushed the leaves.

The trailer filled with light.

The light faded, leaving the trailer smelling like a cathedral after a lightning strike. Dutch stared at the plant, now lush enough to be its own ecosystem. His fingers twitched toward his pocket where the blessed bud burned like a holy hand grenade.

"You—" He pointed at Jesus, then the plant, then back at Jesus. "You can’t just do that."

"That!" Dutch flapped his hands at the plant. "The—the glow! The smell! The vibes! You can’t just walk into a man’s home and start performing horticultural miracles!"

Outside, Detective Vega knocked again. "Donovan, open up."

Dutch spun toward the door, then back to Jesus, then to the plant. His brain short-circuited. Priorities scrambled. He yanked the bud from his pocket and tore off a chunk with his teeth.

"Wait—" Jesus reached out.

Too late. Dutch chewed.

The effect was instantaneous. His pupils dilated. His knees buckled. He grabbed the counter to steady himself as the world dissolved into fractals of divine clarity. The linoleum pulsed like a living thing. The Confederate flag on the air mattress whispered repent.

"Oh," Dutch breathed. "Oh no."

Jesus took a cautious step back. "Are you… alright?"

Dutch clutched his chest. "I can see."

"See what?"

"Everything." He staggered forward, eyes wide. "I lied about the flag. It’s not ironic. I’m just racist."

Jesus blinked.

"The burrito was five days old." Dutch pressed his palms to his cheeks. "I don’t have a ship coming in. I don’t even have a kayak." His voice cracked. "I’m thirty-eight and I sleep on a deflated air mattress."

Detective Vega pounded the door. "Last chance, Donovan."

Dutch ignored her. He grabbed Jesus’s shoulders. "You’re real. You’re actually him. Oh God. Oh fuck." He dropped to his knees. "I sold your miracle wine to a guy named T-Bone for meth."

Jesus sighed. "I know."

"You know?"

"I’m omniscient."

Dutch’s face crumpled. "Then you know about the ferret thing."

Dutch made a small, broken noise.

Outside, Vega’s radio crackled. "Unit 12, requesting backup at the Donovan residence."

Dutch shot upright. "No no no—she can’t see this!" He lunged for the plant, then froze. "Wait. If she does see it…" A slow, terrifying grin spread across his face. "We could sell this. Market it as, like, a truth serum. A confession accelerator. Call it Divine Subpoena."

Jesus frowned. "That’s not—"

"Think about it! Cops, lawyers, wives—they’d pay millions." Dutch’s eyes gleamed. "We could franchise. Jesus Juice: Now with 100% More Holy Ghost."

The trailer door creaked open. Vega stood in the threshold, hand on her hip. "You boys done with the choir practice?"

Dutch spun, arms spread. "Detective! Perfect timing. Would you like to try the world’s first sacramental sativa?"

Vega’s gaze flicked to the glowing plant, then to Jesus. Her eyebrow arched. "What the hell is that?"

"That," Dutch said, puffing his chest, "is the literal body of Christ."

Vega sighed. "I’m not paid enough for this." She stepped inside, nudging a beer can with her shoe. "Look, Donovan. We got reports of a dinosaur near Eden World. Witnesses say it was with a guy in a robe." She jerked her chin at Jesus. "That him?"

Dutch gasped. "You saw Rex?"

"Rex?"

"The dinosaur! He’s friendly! Mostly!" Dutch swayed, grinning. "Also, you’re beautiful. Like, really beautiful. Your hair smells like coconuts and justice."

Vega’s hand drifted toward her cuffs. "Are you high right now?"

"Transcendentally."

Jesus stepped forward. "Detective Vega—"

"Lola," Dutch corrected, dreamy. "Her name’s Lola. She’s a warrior. A goddess. She’s also terrified of commitment. Three failed engagements. Daddy issues. Classic."

Vega’s jaw tightened. "That’s it. You’re coming downtown."

Dutch clutched his heart. "I love downtown."

Vega reached for him. Dutch dodged, tripped over the cable spool, and landed ass-first in the mini-fridge. The half-eaten burrito squished beneath him.

"Ew," he said, then giggled.

Jesus pinched the bridge of his nose.

Vega holstered her cuffs and pulled out her radio. "Yeah, I’m gonna need a bigger unit."

Dutch wiggled his fingers at her. "Ask me anything. Anything. The weed won’t let me lie."

Vega hesitated. "Fine. Where’s the dinosaur?"

"Eden World! Hiding in the Tower of Babel ride. Which, by the way, is not abandoned. There’s a secret lab underneath. Military types. Bad vibes." He gasped. "Oh shit. I wasn’t supposed to say that."

Vega’s eyes narrowed. "What lab?"

Dutch clapped his hands over his mouth.

Jesus stepped between them. "Detective, perhaps we should—"

A shadow passed the window. Something big.

The trailer shook. A deep, mechanical growl rattled the walls.

Vega drew her gun. "What was that?"

Dutch peeked through the blinds. His face lit up. "Rex!"

The tyrannosaur’s red eye scanned the trailer. His vocalizer crackled. "QUERY: WHERE IS THE MESSIAH?"

Vega’s grip on her gun wavered. "It talks?"

Dutch flung open the door. "He does! He’s amazing! Also, he’s super into free will now. Like, philosophically." He turned to Jesus. "Dude, your dinosaur is so cool."

Rex’s head tilted. "STATEMENT: THE HUMAN IS INTOXICATED."

Jesus sighed. "Yes."

Dutch spread his arms. "Come at me, bro!"

Rex blinked. "CLARIFICATION: I WILL NOT 'COME AT YOU.'"

Vega backed toward the door. "I’m calling SWAT."

Dutch grabbed her arm. "Wait! Before you go—" He shoved a handful of blessed weed into her pocket. "For the road."

Vega stared at him. "What is wrong with you?"

Dutch beamed. "Everything."

The radio crackled. "Vega, status?"

She hesitated, then lifted it slowly. "Uh. Stand down. False alarm." A beat. "Really false."

Rex nudged the trailer with his snout. "SUGGESTION: WE DEPART BEFORE THE AUTHORITIES ESCALATE."

Jesus nodded. He stepped outside, then paused, looking back at Dutch. "Coming?"

Dutch swayed. "Oh, hell yeah." He tripped over the threshold, caught himself on Rex’s leg, and patted the metal plating. "Good dino."

Rex sighed. A hydraulic hiss. "STATEMENT: THIS IS HUMILIATING."

Vega watched them go, one hand still on her radio. The other drifted to her pocket. The weed hummed against her fingers.

She pulled it out, sniffed it, and—after a long moment—tucked it back in.

"Damn it," she muttered, and followed.

The underground bunker smelled like ozone and regret. Colonel Cain stood before the bank of monitors, each screen flickering with static-laced footage of the trailer park. His reflection stared back at him—a gaunt man with a buzzcut gone slightly asymmetrical from where he’d trimmed it himself in the mirror that morning. The left side was always just a hair shorter.

"Play it again," he said.

The technician—Private Driscoll, 22, acne scars, nervous tic of cracking his knuckles every 47 seconds—swallowed hard. "Sir, we’ve reviewed the—"

"Again."

The footage rolled. Grainy black-and-white. The trailer door opening. Dutch Donovan, grinning like a man who’d just won a lifetime supply of bad decisions. The woman—Detective Vega, according to the facial recognition database—stepping inside. And then, unmistakably, him.

The Messiah.

In a trailer park.

Cain’s eye twitched.

The footage jumped. The tyrannosaur’s massive head filled the frame, his red optical scanner sweeping the area. "QUERY: WHERE IS THE MESSIAH?"

Driscoll cleared his throat. "Sir, the audio confirms Rex’s neural override has completely—"

"I heard what it confirmed," Cain snapped. He jabbed a finger at the screen. "That thing was designed to obey. To kill. Not to… to philosophize."

The word tasted like battery acid.

Driscoll hesitated. "Should we initiate retrieval?"

Cain didn’t answer. He turned instead to the wall of blueprints—Eden World’s original schematics, overlaid with his own modifications. The Tower of Babel ride. The underground labs. The Serpent Engine.

And the Judas Protocol.

He exhaled through his nose. "No. We’re past retrieval."

Driscoll paled. "Sir, the Protocol is—"

"Necessary." Cain’s voice was calm. Deadly calm. The kind of calm that came right before a man did something he couldn’t take back. "Activate it."

Driscoll’s fingers hovered over the keyboard. "Authorization code?"

Cain didn’t blink. "Ezekiel 25:17."

The bunker lights dimmed. A low hum vibrated through the floor. Somewhere deep in the facility, machinery groaned to life.

On the main screen, a single line of text appeared:

JUDAS PROTOCOL ENGAGED. DEPLOYING ASSETS.

Cain adjusted his collar. The fabric was starched stiff enough to draw blood. "It finds him."

"But—"

"It doesn’t matter how." Cain’s smile was thin. "Judas always finds Jesus."

The monitors flickered. One by one, they switched to live feeds across Miami—security cameras, traffic lights, ATM lenses. Each one scanning. Searching.

A chime sounded.

TARGET ACQUIRED: RUSTY GATOR BAR, 8TH STREET.

Cain leaned in. The feed showed Detective Vega stepping inside the bar, her hand resting on the butt of her gun.

Driscoll frowned. "She’s not—"

"No," Cain agreed. "But she’ll lead us to him."

He straightened. "Prep the extraction team. And Driscoll?"

"Sir?"

"Bring the other Rex."

Driscoll’s knuckles cracked. "Other Rex?"

Cain didn’t answer. He was already walking away, his boots echoing down the corridor.

Somewhere above them, beneath the rotting planks of the Tower of Babel ride, something stirred.

Something with teeth.

The Rusty Gator smelled like stale beer and poor life choices. Lola Vega wrinkled her nose as she stepped inside, her eyes adjusting to the dim light. The bartender—a grizzled man with a prosthetic hook for a hand—didn’t look up from polishing a glass.

"Donovan here?" she asked.

The bartender snorted. "Which time?"

Lola slid onto a stool. "The one with the dinosaur."

The glass stopped mid-polish. "Oh. That time." He jerked his hook toward the back booth. "Left ten minutes ago. With the robe guy."

Lola’s fingers brushed the lump in her pocket—the weird-ass weed Dutch had shoved at her. It tingled.

She frowned. "Where’d they go?"

The bartender leaned in. His breath smelled like peppermint and gun oil. "Lady, if I knew, I’d be charging admission."

Lola opened her mouth—The door burst open.

Three men in black suits stood there, their sunglasses glinting under the neon. The lead agent—tall, broad-shouldered, with a voice like gravel in a blender—held up a badge.

"Federal Marshal," he lied. "We’re looking for Dutch Donovan."

The bartender’s hook twitched toward the shotgun under the bar. "Ain’t seen him."

The agent’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. "Funny. Our tracker says otherwise."

Lola’s hand crept toward her own gun. "What tracker?"

The agent’s gaze slid to her. "The one in your pocket, Detective."

Lola froze.

The weed burned.

The agent tilted his head. "Judas always finds Jesus."

Outside, an engine roared.

Something big was coming.

The air changed. It wasn't a sound. The silence tightened like a wire.

She lowered the scope. Her throat was dry. She swallowed, and the click echoed in her helmet.

"Status." Voss's voice crackled in her ear.

"Movement at the edge of the western ridge. Not local." She kept her eyes on the shadow. It didn't move like a man. It flowed.

"Define 'not local.'"

"I can't." She adjusted the focus. The shape resolved into angles that hurt to look at—a wrong geometry against the sandstone. "It's not from here."

A burst of static. Then Voss again. "Fall back to point Gamma. Now."

"Negative. I have visual. If I move, I lose it."

"That's an order, Kaelen."

She ignored him. The thing shifted. It wasn't walking. It was unfolding. One long limb extended, then another, testing the ground like a spider testing a web.

Her finger found the trigger. Cold metal. She counted her breaths. Three. Two.

It stopped. The front of it—if it had a front—turned toward her position. Two miles of open desert between them, and she knew it saw her.

The comms went dead. Not static. Dead.

She ripped the earpiece out and threw it into the dust. Her own breathing was too loud. She forced it quiet.

Down the ridge. Keep the rock between you and it. Move.

She slid down the scree, boots skidding. At the bottom, she ran. The sand gave way under her, sucking at her steps. Every instinct screamed to look back. She didn't.

Gamma was a rusted hatch half-buried in the salt flat. Two hundred meters. Her lungs burned. The air tasted of ozone and decay.

A sound behind her. Not a footstep. A scrape, like metal on stone.

She didn't turn. She ran faster, the hatch growing in her vision. Fifty meters. The scrape came again, closer.

Her hand found the release lever. She yanked. The hatch groaned open. She dropped inside, pulling it shut above her. Darkness. The heavy thud of the seal engaging.

She fumbled for the light panel. The fluorescents flickered on, revealing the small bunker. Empty. Voss wasn't here.

The console in the corner blinked. A single line of text scrolled across the screen.

PROTOCOL SIGMA INITIATED. STAND BY.

"What the hell is Sigma?" she said to the empty room.

The console didn't answer. The air recycler hummed. She checked her weapon. Power cell at eighty percent. She slapped in a fresh cartridge.

A thump echoed from above. On the hatch.

Then another. Not random. A pattern. Tap. Tap-tap.

She aimed at the ceiling. The metal dented inward with the next impact. A sharp, precise point pushed through the alloy.

She fired. The plasma bolt seared the metal, leaving a glowing scar. The tapping stopped.

Silence.

She waited. Ten seconds. Twenty. A drop of sweat traced a line down her temple.

The console beeped. New text.

SIGMA PARAMETERS: CONTAINMENT BREACH. ORIGIN UNKNOWN. TERMINATE ALL CONTACT.

"Terminate with what?" she muttered.

Another beep. A schematic flashed on the screen—the bunker's layout. A section marked SUB-LEVEL ACCESS glowed red. It was behind a wall panel she'd assumed was storage.

She crossed the room, pried the panel open. A ladder descended into blackness. Cold air drifted up.

The thumping started again on the hatch. Harder now. The dent grew deeper.

She climbed down. The rungs were cold. Below, a faint blue light pulsed.

The sub-level was colder. The blue light came from a row of glass cylinders along one wall. Each was empty, save for a thick, clear fluid. Labels at their bases were scratched out.

At the room's center stood a terminal, older than anything upstairs. A physical key was inserted in its side.

She approached. The screen was dark. She touched a key.

It woke with a low whine. Lines of code scrolled, too fast to read. Then it stopped. A prompt blinked.

INPUT CLEARANCE CODE FOR SIGMA BRIEFING.

She had no code. She typed her name. KAELEN.

ACCESS DENIED. SECURITY LOCKOUT IN 2 ATTEMPTS.

The banging from above became a sustained roar. Metal shrieked. They were through the hatch.

She typed VOSS.

ACCESS DENIED. FINAL ATTEMPT.

Her hands hovered over the keys. She thought of the thing on the ridge, unfolding. She typed UNKNOWN.

The screen went black. Then green text appeared.

CLEARANCE ACCEPTED. PLAYING RECORDING SIGMA-7.

A grainy video filled the screen. A man in a lab coat, his face haggard. He spoke directly to the camera.

"If you're seeing this, the field test failed. The entities are not contained. They are adaptive. They learn." He rubbed his eyes. "Sigma was never a weapon. It was a quarantine. This bunker is the lock. You are the key."

The video dissolved into static. When it cleared, the man was gone. The room in the video was this room, but the cylinders were full. Shadows moved inside them.

The terminal beeped. A new message.

SEAL SEQUENCE INITIATED. INPUT CONFIRMATION: YES/NO.

A crash echoed down the ladder shaft. Something heavy hit the floor above. Footsteps. Multiple. Dragging.

She looked at the key in the terminal. She could turn it. Seal herself in with whatever was in those cylinders. Or go up.

The footsteps reached the top of the ladder. A shape blocked the light from above. It started to descend.

She drew her weapon. "Stay where you are."

The thing kept coming. She saw its leg—jointed wrong, gleaming wet in the blue light.

She fired. The bolt lit the room. It struck the thing's chest. It didn't stop. It didn't even slow.

Her back hit the wall. The terminal's prompt glowed. YES/NO.

The thing reached the floor. It stood, unfolding to its full height, head brushing the ceiling. It had no face. Just a smooth, dark surface that reflected the cylinders.

It took a step toward her.

She slammed her palm on the terminal. "Yes!"

Alarms whooped. Red lights strobed. A metal shutter began descending over the ladder shaft, cutting off the exit.

The thing lunged.

She dove sideways, rolling behind a cylinder. Its claw scraped the glass, leaving a white scar.

The shutter was halfway down. She could make it if she ran now.

The thing turned toward her new position. It moved between her and the ladder.

She was trapped.

The terminal screen changed. COUNTDOWN: 60 SECONDS TO PERMANENT SEAL.

Fifty seconds.

She backed toward the far wall. Her hand brushed a metal pipe along the floor—a conduit. She grabbed it, wrenched it free. It was heavy, solid.

Forty seconds.

The shutter was three-quarters down. A narrow gap remained.

Thirty seconds.

Silence, except for the alarm ringing through the upper bunker.

Just heat and silence.

It would have to do.

She started walking east, toward the canyons. The dust cloud followed, matching her pace.
