The first thing Evangelist Ezekiel noticed that morning, before the coffee or the prayer or the application of his industrial-strength underarm sealant, was the smell. It wasn't the usual Monday morning smell of stale anointing oil and despair. It was sugar. Burning sugar. And something else underneath it, a sharp, greasy tang of fried dough and righteous indignation.

He stood in the vast, empty parking lot of the Pearly Gates Megachurch, a seventy-thousand-square-foot monument to fiberglass columns and questionable debt financing. The Oklahoma sun was already a merciless white coin in a sky the color of faded denim. Across the two-lane blacktop, where the old bankrupt feed store used to be, a new sign had sprouted overnight. It was painted in garish, carnival colors on a bedsheet stretched between two telephone poles.

THE DIVINE DEALS FLEA MARKET & HOLY RELIC EMPORIUM. ALL SALES FINAL. NO REFUNDS. MIRACLES SUBJECT TO AVAILABILITY.

Beneath it, a small army of folding tables stood arrayed. A few early-bird vendors were setting up. Ezekiel squinted. Something was off about them. Their movements were… jerky.

His phone buzzed in the silk pocket of his dressing gown. It was Deacon Bob, his voice a tinny panic. “Ezekiel. You see this?”

“I see it.” A bead of sweat traced a path down his temple. It left a faint, glistening trail that, if you looked very closely, spelled out the first three words of Philippians 4:13. He sweated scripture when stressed. It was inconvenient, and it ruined good shirts. “It’s a flea market, Bob. They sell broken toasters and bootleg cassettes. We sell eternal salvation. I fail to see the competition.”

“The squirrel,” Bob hissed. “Look at the squirrel on table three.”

Ezekiel looked. At the third folding table, a large taxidermy squirrel stood upright on a velvet-covered box. It wore a tiny, meticulously stitched robe made from what looked like an altar cloth scrap. In its stiff, little paws, it held a sign: GENUINE MANNA FRAGMENTS – $25 OBO. CASH PREFERRED.

The squirrel’s glass eyes caught the sun. It seemed to be staring right at him.

“That’s… dedicated taxidermy,” Ezekiel said, his voice tighter than he intended. “A gimmick. People love a theme.”

“It winked at me, Ezekiel. I was getting coffee at the truck stop and it winked.”

“Taxidermy doesn’t wink, Bob. You’re projecting. You’re under spiritual attack from… from craft services.” He ended the call. The scripture-sweat was now pooling at his collar, threatening to inscribe a whole psalm.

By ten AM, the Pearly Gates parking lot should have been a third full with the faithful arriving for his weekly “Miracle Monday” service. Instead, a trickle of his regulars—mostly the elderly who couldn’t jaywalk that fast—were being siphoned across the street like iron filings toward a spiritual magnet. He could hear the competing soundscapes from the church steps: his own pre-recorded, swelling orchestral hymn of worship, and from across the road, the tinny carnival bark of a calliope and the distinct, high-pitched chatter of… squirrels?

He retreated to his office, the “War Room,” where the walls were lined with framed photos of him standing beside minor celebrities and very large checks. His pet python, Seraphina, lay coiled in her heated terrarium, a lethargic loop of scales. She’d been listless ever since she’d swallowed his last prophetic vision—a genuinely troubling dream about discount salvation at a warehouse store—whole three weeks ago. Now she just lay there, digesting divine insight, useless.

“The enemy isn’t across the street, Ezekiel,” he told his reflection in the one-way glass overlooking the empty auditorium. “The enemy is doubt. And possibly poor signage.” His own sign, a towering digital marvel that could display hellfire animations, currently read: MIRACLE MONDAY! WITNESS THE POWER! It lacked specificity. The flea market’s bedsheet promised tangible results.

He decided on a frontal assault. He would out-preach them. He would deploy the full arsenal.

At eleven sharp, he took the stage in his cream-colored suit with the faintest pinstripe of gold thread. The auditorium, which could seat two thousand, held maybe eighty souls, scattered like lost sheep in a canyon of empty chairs. The air conditioning hummed a lonely dirge.

“Brothers and Sisters!” he boomed, his voice modulated by ten thousand dollars’ worth of German audio engineering. “Do you seek the genuine article? The real, life-changing, heaven-sent power?”

From across the road, amplified by a cheap bullhorn, came a chittering, squeaky reply. “Limited time offer! Get your authentic tear of Saint Lawrence, wept during his grilling! Preserved in resin! Twenty bucks!”

A few heads in his congregation turned toward the exits.

Ezekiel felt a warm trickle down his spine. John 3:16, probably. He cranked up the charm. “The world offers you trinkets! Baubles! Cheap imitations of grace!” He gestured dramatically toward the large, empty glass tank on stage, ready for the scheduled “Miracle of the Holy Piranhas” later in the service.

A collective gasp from his flock. He smiled, thinking the Spirit was moving.

Then he saw they weren’t looking at the tank. They were looking at the back of the auditorium. A procession was entering. A taxidermy badger, wearing a tiny bishop’s mitre, was pushing a little doll’s pram down the center aisle with its nose. In the pram sat a small, painted figurine of a saint. The badger stopped at the end of a row, looked up at an elderly woman named Mabel, and nudged the pram toward her. It chittered softly.

Mabel, who had been a faithful tither for fifteen years, reached into her purse and handed the badger a five-dollar bill. The badger took it in its teeth, dropped a small, glittery rock into her hand, and turned the pram around to leave.

Ezekiel’s microphone emitted a feedback screech. “This is a house of God!”

The badger paused, looked over its stiff shoulder at him, and seemed to shrug. Then it continued its dignified exit.

Chaos, of a quiet, deeply bizarre variety, ensued. A family of four got up and left, the father muttering about better deals across the street. A taxidermy raccoon, perched on the window sill outside, tapped on the glass with a tiny, preserved claw. It held up a small vial of murky liquid labeled JORDAN RIVER WATER (SALT-FREE).

Ezekiel abandoned his sermon notes. This called for the big guns. Pyrotechnics.

“You want real?” he shouted, his voice cracking. He signaled his stage manager, Gary, in the control booth. “I’ll show you real! Behold the Pentecostal Flame of Pure Faith!”

A hidden switch was thrown. With a whoosh, a pillar of controlled propane fire erupted from a stage vent, soaring twenty feet toward the ceiling. It was impressive. It was also the exact same trick he used every Easter and Christmas Eve.

As the orange flame licked the air, a small, singed piece of paper, blown by the convection, fluttered down from the lighting rig. It drifted on the thermal currents, a lazy, embarrassing snowflake. It landed at Ezekiel’s feet.

It was a Polaroid photograph, slightly melted at the edges. It showed a younger, happier Ezekiel, his arm around a beautiful, laughing woman in a sequined praise dancer’s costume. Gloria. His former star praise dancer. His former fiancée. The one who’d left after “the incident” with the interpretive dance to “Hell’s Bells” and the faulty smoke machine.

Written on the white border in familiar, looping script were the words: Fire is passé, Zeke. We deal in resurrection now.

He stared at the photo. The sweat was a flood now, a torrent of tiny verses cascading down his neck, soaking his collar. The Song of Solomon. Entire passages.

The raccoon at the window tapped again, more insistently. It pointed a claw at the photo, then jabbed it toward the flea market across the street. Its meaning was clear.

The twist landed in his gut, cold and heavy. This wasn’t random. This wasn’t a coincidence. The taxidermy, the relics, the targeted disruption… Gloria had always been theatrically gifted. And she’d taken that taxidermy course after the breakup, said it was therapeutic. “Stuffing things lets me control the narrative, Zeke.”

He looked from the photo to the window to the near-empty pews. The badger was gone. Mabel was holding her glittery rock up to the light, a beatific smile on her face. The pillar of fire guttered out, leaving the smell of propane and defeat.

His phone buzzed. Deacon Bob. “Ezekiel. We have a situation at the donation booth. A… a muskrat. In a little vest. It’s offering to match tithes for anyone who brings a receipt from across the street.”

The final twist, the one that redefined everything, clicked into place with the sharpness of a mousetrap. This wasn’t just competition. This was a hostile takeover. A spiritual corporate raid, orchestrated by a scorned praise dancer with an army of reanimated roadkill. She wasn’t just selling relics. She was selling a better, quirkier, more tangible product. And she was using his own history, his own theatrics, against him.

The bullhorn across the street crackled to life again. But it wasn’t a chitter this time. It was a woman’s voice, smooth as honey and amplified just enough to carry.

“Feeling spiritually unfulfilled? Has your faith become a predictable routine? Come on over! We’ve got a verified splinter from Noah’s Ark, a two-for-one on Veronica’s Veil prints, and a very patient badger who will hear your confession for a small fee! All credit cards accepted!”

Ezekiel looked out at his flock. They were staring at him, waiting for his next move. He had the sound system, the lights, the pyro. He had a python full of indigestible prophecy.

And across the street, Gloria had a talking squirrel selling manna.

He picked up the melted Polaroid. He looked at Gloria’s laughing face. A strange, competitive spark, one he hadn’t felt since she’d beaten him at Bible-themed mini-golf, flickered in his chest. This was no longer about saving souls. This was about market share.

He leaned into his microphone, killing the swelling background music. The silence was abrupt, total.

“Friends,” he said, his voice low, conspiratorial. “It seems we have… new neighbors. And they’re having a sale.” He paused, letting the scripture-sweat dry into a salty map of Lamentations on his back. “Well. We at the Pearly Gates believe in neighborly love. And in competitive pricing. Gary? Initiate the Blue Light Special. And bring out the big tank.”

He wasn’t sure what he meant by that last part. But as he strode out of the auditorium, toward the sunlight and the smell of cotton candy and war, he knew one thing. Miracle Monday was about to get interesting.

The taxidermy squirrel, whose name was Reginald, watched the preacher emerge from the big glass church. He adjusted his tiny cardinal’s hat with a deft flick of a claw. The human was finally engaging. It was about time.

From her command post—a converted ice cream truck painted with peeling icons—Gloria watched through binoculars. She saw Ezekiel’s determined stride, the set of his jaw. She smiled, a real one, not the stage smile she used to flash during the “Dance of the Seven Veils (of Debt Forgiveness).”

“He’s taking the bait,” she murmured, scratching the stuffed head of her assistant, a once-opossum named Alfred who now handled logistics. “Right on schedule.”

Alfred chittered, pointing a claw at a clipboard. Inventory was low on tears of Saint Lawrence. They needed to source more onions.

“The true relic,” Gloria said, not to Alfred, but to the memory of the man across the street, “isn’t a piece of wood or a tear. It’s spite. Beautiful, creative, revenue-generating spite.”

She picked up her own bullhorn. The game, as she had always preferred it, was on.

Barnaby the badger felt the sun warm the felt of his tiny mitre. He also felt the three dollars and seventy-five cents in coins sewn into the lining of his robe. A satisfying weight. From his perch atop the “Gently Used Exorcism Kits” table, he watched the human preacher, Ezekiel, march across the blacktop like a cream-colored missile. The man’s face was the color of a boiled ham. Barnaby approved. Boiled ham was a currency he understood.

His claws tapped a rapid staccato on the tabletop. A signal. Around the flea market perimeter, his colleagues snapped to attention. Reginald the squirrel straightened his sign. The raccoon, Mortimer, stoppered his vial of dubious river water. A trio of sparrows, stuffed and mounted on a mobile made of coat hangers, tilted their wings in unison. They were ready.

Ezekiel skidded to a halt before Barnaby’s table, stirring up a small cloud of glitter from a spilled jar of “Angel Dust (Non-Hallucinogenic).” He pointed a trembling finger. “You. Stop this. This is… theological copyright infringement!”

Barnaby looked at the finger. He looked up at Ezekiel’s sweating face. He slowly, deliberately, reached into the doll’s pram beside him and produced a small laminated card. He held it up.

SPIRITUAL SERVICES PROVIDER – BADGER. RATES NEGOTIABLE. NO PERSONAL CHECKS.

Ezekiel’s eye twitched. “Where is she?” he demanded, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. “I know this is Gloria’s doing. The… the aesthetic is too specific.”

From behind the ice cream truck, a voice, honeyed and sharp, answered. “The aesthetic, Zeke, is called ‘accessible eschatology.’ You should try it.”

Gloria emerged. She wore practical khakis and a faded t-shirt that read WHAT WOULD JESUS TAXIDERMY? in peeling letters. Her hair was piled in a messy bun, pierced through with a single, ornate hatpin that Barnaby recognized as a former prop from the “Crown of Thorns” interpretive number. She carried a cordless power drill.

Ezekiel stared. The sight of her, here, now, holding a drill instead of a tambourine, short-circuited something in his brain. “Gloria. You… you trained them.”

“Trained is a strong word,” she said, walking over to Barnaby and adjusting his mitre. “I provided vocational guidance. They’re independent contractors. Barnaby here has an excellent eye for counterfeit relics. He rejected three pieces of the ‘True Cross’ last week. All pine. Amateurs.”

Barnaby puffed out his chest slightly.

“They’re dead animals, Gloria!” Ezekiel’s shout drew looks from a cluster of customers haggling over a “Lot’s Wife Salt Shaker.”

“And you’re a living one selling eternal life on a payment plan,” Gloria shot back, not missing a beat. She didn’t raise her voice. That was always her weapon. “They’re entrepreneurs. They saw a gap in the market. A demand for tactile faith. Your product is all promise. Ours is receipt.”

“This is sacrilege!”

“This is supply-side spirituality.” She gestured with the drill. “People want a souvenir. A proof they were here. You give them a feeling. I give them a trinket. My conversion rates are better.”

Across the street, the church’s giant sign flickered. Gary had followed instructions. It now read: BLUE LIGHT SPECIAL ON REDEMPTION! TODAY ONLY!

A weak cheer rose from the handful of remaining congregants.

Gloria snorted. “A sale? Really, Zeke? That’s your play?”

“You started a price war!” he sputtered. A verse from Proverbs began to bead on his forehead.

“I started a conversation!” She turned to Barnaby. “Show him the numbers.”

Barnaby, with a solemn air, nudged a small ledger book forward with his nose. It was open to today’s tally. The columns were neat, the figures impressive. Cash, credit, even a few bartered chickens.

Ezekiel’s eyes scanned the page. The anger in his face didn’t fade, but it blurred at the edges, mixed with something else. A professional appreciation. The numbers were good. Very good. He looked from the ledger to the bustling flea market, to Gloria’s defiant stance, to the badger’s expectant glass eyes.

A memory surfaced, unbidden. Gloria, years ago, designing the merch for his “Faith-a-Palooza” tour. She’d wanted light-up crucifix necklaces. He’d vetoed it as tacky. The plain cotton t-shirts they’d sold instead had languished in a storage locker he was still paying for.

The twist in his gut unclenched. The cold weight of betrayal melted, reshaped itself into a different kind of weight. The weight of an opportunity.

“The badger hears confessions?” Ezekiel asked, his voice suddenly calm.

“For a small fee,” Gloria said, wary. “He’s a surprisingly good listener. Very non-judgmental.”

“And the squirrel’s manna fragments. Authenticity?”

“Forty percent cereal binder, sixty percent spiritual intent. We’re working on the ratio.”

Ezekiel nodded. He paced a short circle, his shiny shoes crunching on gravel. The scripture-sweat stopped. His mind, a battlefield moments before, became a boardroom. He saw it not as two opposing forces, but as a poorly integrated conglomerate. Synergies were being wasted. Market penetration was incomplete.

“You’re targeting the low-dollar impulse buyer,” he said, thinking aloud. “The curious, the ironic, the souvenir hunter. I own the high-commitment, lifecycle customer. The tither, the legacy donor.”

Gloria crossed her arms. “Your point?”

“We’re segmenting the market,” he said, stopping to face her. A wild, familiar light was in his eyes—the same light that appeared when he’d conceived the Holy Piranha idea. “But we’re forcing the customer to choose. Why should they? What if… what if they didn’t have to?”

Barnaby’s nose twitched. He smelled a shift in the air, a new scent layered over burning sugar and despair. It smelled like possibility. And markup.

“What are you talking about, Zeke?”

“A joint venture,” he said, spreading his hands. “A… spiritual ecosystem. The Pearly Gates Divine Bazaar. We merge operations. Your relics, my miracles. Your… tactile artisans, my theatrical production. A one-stop shop for all metaphysical needs, from eternal salvation to a fun paperweight. We cross-promote. Buy a verified tear of Saint Lawrence, get ten percent off our premium after-life assurance package. Attend a full sermon, receive a voucher for a complimentary taxidermy blessing.”

The flea market fell silent. Even the calliope tape seemed to hiccup. Mortimer the raccoon dropped his vial. It didn’t break. It was plastic.

Gloria stared at him. The drill hung limp at her side. “You want to… partner. With me. And my army of dead rodents.”

“Entrepreneurial fauna,” Ezekiel corrected smoothly. “Think of the branding. The pathos. The crossover appeal. We could do a living nativity at Christmas. Your animals, my script. We could sell commemorative hay.”

Barnaby saw it first. The numbers multiplied in his mind’s eye. Exponential growth. Diversified revenue streams. A guaranteed supply of felt scraps. He let out a sharp, approving chitter.

The sound broke Gloria’s trance. She looked at Barnaby, then at the ledger, then back at Ezekiel. The old hurt was still there, a fossil in her chest. But layered over it was something more compelling: the sheer, audacious, ridiculous profit potential. It was the most romantic thing he’d ever proposed.

A slow smile spread across her face. It wasn’t the old stage smile. It was real, edged with the same creative spite that had built this empire from a single disgruntled squirrel. “You’ll want a controlling share,” she stated.

“Fifty-one percent,” he nodded. “I have the real estate. You have the inventory.”

“Sixty-forty. My inventory moves. Your real estate has a mortgage the size of the national debt.”

“Fifty-five, forty-five. I’ll throw in the python. She’s full of prophecies. We can market them as limited-edition, digestible insights.”

Gloria considered. She looked at Alfred the opossum, who gave a tiny, logistical shrug. “Deal,” she said.

They didn’t shake hands. Ezekiel pulled a silk handkerchief from his pocket and dabbed his dry forehead. Gloria powered up her drill again, as if ready to build something.

“Then let’s get to work,” she said. “Barnaby? Signal the troops. We’re expanding operations.”

Barnaby didn’t need telling twice. He stood on his hind legs, raised his tiny claws, and let out a series of complex, chittering commands. It was a sound that spoke of mergers, acquisitions, and aggressive merchandising.

Chaos, beautiful and lucrative, erupted.

Mortimer the raccoon began bundling vials of Jordan River water with pamphlets for Ezekiel’s upcoming “River of Life” baptismal retreat. Reginald the squirrel started a new sign: MANNA FRAGMENTS – NOW WITH OFFICIAL PEARLY GATES CERTIFICATE OF AUTHENTICITY. The sparrow mobile spun, directing customers toward the church for the main event.

Ezekiel grabbed his phone. “Gary! New plan! Open the doors! Move the donation booths outside! And for God’s sake, bring out the big tank—we’re doing a live piranha blessing for the first fifty relic purchases!”

Within the hour, the two-lane blacktop was no longer a battlefield. It was a thriving, bizarre marketplace. Parishioners flowed between the flea market tables and the church steps, wallets open. A woman bought a “St. Francis Bird Feeder” from a taxidermy blue jay, then went inside to be anointed with oil that smelled suspiciously of lavender and motor grease. A man purchased a “Guaranteed Forgiveness Coupon” from Ezekiel’s new kiosk, then had it stamped by Barnaby the badger for an additional “sin-specific validation fee.”

The scent of cotton candy mingled with incense. The calliope music wove around the hymns. It was awful. It was glorious. It was turning a profit.

As the sun began to dip, painting the fiberglass columns in shades of gold and pink, Ezekiel found Gloria by the ice cream truck, now serving “Heavenly Hash” and “Salvation Sundaes.” She was counting a thick stack of bills. Barnaby sat at her feet, polishing a coin with a scrap of velvet.

“The piranhas were a hit,” Ezekiel said, leaning against the truck. He was exhausted. He was exhilarated. A single, perfect word of scripture—Amen—had dried on his collar.

“The badger’s confession booth has a forty-minute wait,” Gloria replied, not looking up from the money. “We’re instituting timed tickets.”

A comfortable silence settled between them, filled with the distant sound of cash registers and chittering. The old romance was gone, fossilized for good. What replaced it was more durable. It was a partnership. It was a business model.

Barnaby looked from one human to the other. He saw the tension gone, replaced by the calm focus of shared revenue goals. His work, he felt, was done. For now. He had a new idea involving a hedgehog and a line of “Penitent Pincushion” reliquaries. He’d pitch it after the evening count.

Ezekiel watched a family leave the church, the children clutching glow-in-the-dark crucifix necklaces and stuffed toy piranhas. The parents carried a framed certificate of salvation and a bag of “Manna Bites” snack mix.

“You know,” he said, a note of genuine awe in his voice. “This might just be the most spiritually authentic thing we’ve ever done.”

Gloria finally looked at him. She smiled, just a little. “It’s commerce, Zeke. Pure and simple.”

“Same thing,” he said, and for the first time in years, he almost believed it.

Barnaby chittered softly in agreement. He tucked his polished coin into his robe. The sun set on the first official day of the Sermon Swap Meet. The neon cross on the church glowed. The fairy lights strung around the flea market twinkled. And from the big tank inside, now positioned prominently in the narthex, the holy piranhas swam lazy circles, waiting for their next feeding, and their next chance to bless a paying customer.
