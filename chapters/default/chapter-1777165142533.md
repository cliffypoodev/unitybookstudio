In a 1989 personal diary archived at the University of Texas’s Disability History Center, a college student wrote this entry on a Tuesday night: “I have calculated that if I leave my dorm at exactly 7:14 a.m., walk at a pace of 3.5 miles per hour, and take the stairs two at a time between the second and third floors of the science building, I arrive at my 8 a.m. lecture with precisely one minute to spare before the door closes. This is the only way the noise in the hallway doesn’t make my teeth hurt. Dr. Evans says I have ‘ritualistic tendencies.’ I think I have a system that works.”

This is not a story about pathology. It is a story about engineering.

For the generation that grew up neurodivergent before the concept had cultural currency, survival was not a given. It was a privately funded infrastructure project. The blueprints were drawn from trial and error, the materials scavenged from observation, and the labor was solitary, exhausting, and unseen. When the environment provides no ramp, you build your own, even if it’s a rickety, backstairs thing that takes you twice as long and costs you more to maintain. The coping mechanisms that emerged—the rigid routines, the elaborate organizational schemes, the retreats into fantasy, the risk-seeking, the self-medication—were not, at their origin, symptoms of a disorder. They were logical adaptations to a world operating on a neurological standard to which they had no access.

Consider the central problem: a nervous system that experiences the physical and social world with a different intensity, a different processing speed, a different set of defaults. The mismatch is not a minor inconvenience. It is a chronic state of overload, a form of ambient drowning. The body and mind, in their innate drive for homeostasis, will seek solutions. They will find ways to regulate the unregulatable, to impose order on chaos, to escape the inescapable.

Psychiatric literature of the era often cataloged these adaptations as problems in themselves. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Third Edition, Revised (DSM-III-R), published in 1987, listed criteria for disorders like Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder, which pathologized “preoccupation with details, rules, lists, order, organization, or schedules to the extent that the major point of the activity is lost.” For a child whose autistic or ADHD brain was overwhelmed by the barrage of the everyday, such a list wasn’t a pathology. It was a life raft. The rigidity wasn’t the illness; it was the treatment for the illness of existing in an unpredictable environment.

The same clinical gaze often misread self-soothing. A teenager who disappeared for hours into the intricate worlds of fantasy novels or computer programming wasn’t necessarily “withdrawn” or “escapist” in a pathological sense. She was engaging in monotropism—the deep, single-channel focus characteristic of many autistic minds—which provided a refuge from the exhausting polytropic demands of school and social life. It was a neurological reset, a way to achieve a coherence and control utterly absent elsewhere.

Other adaptations were more visibly fraught. The adolescent with undiagnosed ADHD, whose brain craved stimulation to regulate attention and emotion, might find it in high-speed driving, in shoplifting, in the chaotic thrill of interpersonal drama. The system labeled this “conduct disorder” or “risk-taking behavior.” From the inside, it often felt like a search for a volume knob for a world that was otherwise too quiet or too loud, a desperate attempt to feel something coherent.

And then there were the chemical solutions. The use of alcohol to quiet a racing mind after a day of forced social performance. The reliance on caffeine to artificially stimulate an executive function system that wouldn’t engage on its own. The experimentation with marijuana to blunt the edge of sensory hypersensitivity. Self-medication is a cold, clinical term for what is, in reality, a deeply human experiment in pain management. Without a framework to understand their neurotype, without access to appropriate therapeutic or pharmaceutical support, individuals conducted their own private clinical trials. The side effects—addiction, health consequences, legal trouble—were then diagnosed as primary moral failings, obscuring the original, untreated pain.

To judge the coping mechanism without understanding the context it copes with is to blame the fire escape for the fire. The hyper-organized planner, the ritualistic walker, the daydreamer, the risk-taker, the substance user—each was, in their way, attempting to solve a problem their world refused to name. They were building a bridge across a chasm everyone else insisted wasn’t there. The tragedy was not the bridge they built. The tragedy was that they had to build it alone, in the dark, with whatever materials they could find, while being told the chasm was a figment of their imagination.

Marta Silva’s story is preserved in a series of interview transcripts from a 1997 qualitative study on “Academic Overachievement and Psychological Distress,” housed in the Stanford University archives. She is a sophomore at a highly competitive public high school in Northern California in 1992. Her experience is a master class in survival engineering.

Her world is constructed in color. According to her own annotated planners donated to the study, every academic subject has a designated hue: mathematics is a severe blue, history a somber green, English a cautious yellow. Each color governs not only the notebook and folder but the type of pen used for notes, the highlighters for review, and the tab dividers within the binder. Her weekly schedule, redrawn every Sunday evening on a fresh sheet of graph paper, is a mosaic of blocked time. It allocates minutes for homework, for review, for meals, and for a single thirty-minute block labeled “free time,” which is itself scheduled. Deviations cause what she describes in the interview as “a kind of static—like all the channels coming in at once.”

Teachers and parents see a model student. Her assignments are never late. Her locker is a marvel of order. She receives praise for her maturity, her discipline, her impeccable organization. What the praise misses is the function of the system. For Marta, the color-coding is not an enhancement of learning. It is a containment field. It holds back the overwhelm of multiple, competing demands that her executive functioning struggles to prioritize naturally. The schedule is not a tool for productivity; it is a scaffold that prevents cognitive collapse. Without it, as she tells the researcher, “The whole day just falls into a pile. I can’t pick out what’s important. It’s all just…noise.”

The internal cost is documented in her journal excerpts, included as supplementary material. The meticulous planner who earns straight A’s writes at 2:00 a.m.: “I am so tired. The schedule says sleep now, but I can’t stop checking the equations for the third time. If I don’t, the blue notebook will be wrong, and then the whole system is wrong.” The rigidity that creates external success breeds a relentless internal anxiety. Any threat to the system—a pop quiz, a canceled study hall, a friend’s spontaneous request to meet—is not an inconvenience. It is a systems failure that requires emergency recalibration, a draining cognitive process she must perform invisibly and in real time.

This hyper-organization extends beyond academics. Interview notes describe her methodical approach to social interactions. She prepares conversation topics before parties. She scripts possible responses to common questions. She times her walks through the crowded hallways between classes to avoid the peak congestion at the bell. Each action is a pre-emptive strike against the unexpected, which her brain processes not as novelty but as threat. The result is a performance of competence that is utterly convincing and completely exhausting. She is, in effect, running a continuous simulation of the world in order to navigate it safely.

The 1994 DSM-IV would have categorized such rigidity under Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder, citing a “preoccupation with details, rules, lists, order, organization, or schedules to the extent that the major point of the activity is lost.” But for Marta, the point of the activity is not lost. It is singular and vital: the maintenance of functional coherence. The “preoccupation” is the very mechanism that allows her to function at all. The clinical lens pathologizes the life raft as the symptom of the shipwreck.

The study’s final follow-up with Marta occurs in her first semester of college in 1995. The transition destabilizes her entire architecture. The college lacks the rigid structure of her high school. Class schedules differ daily. Assignments are long-term and self-paced. The color-coded system, designed for a predictable environment, fractures under the ambiguity. Her grades plummet. She describes a feeling of “free-fall” in the last interview transcript. The coping mechanism that allowed her to succeed in a structured system fails when the system changes. The underlying need—her brain’s requirement for externally-imposed order to manage internal chaos—was never addressed. Only the brittle, self-built scaffold was seen, and it was mistaken for the structure itself.

Marta Silva’s experience illustrates a pervasive pattern. For countless undiagnosed neurodivergent individuals, particularly those assigned female at birth and socialized towards compliance, hyper-organization became a primary mask. It translated internal dysregulation into external approval. It transformed a neurological need for predictability into a praised character trait: perfectionism. The cost was a life lived on high alert, where energy meant for learning, connection, and growth was perpetually diverted to the maintenance of the mask itself. They were not organized. They were organizing themselves into existence, minute by scheduled minute, using a system that could collapse at any moment under the weight of the very world it was built to navigate.

Hiro Tanaka’s 1991 journal, part of a digital archive of early internet users at the University of Michigan, contains not a log of his daily life, but a meticulously drawn map. It is a cartographer’s rendering of a fictional continent, complete with mountain ranges, river systems, and city-states, each labeled in a careful hand. The notes alongside it detail trade routes, political histories, and linguistic rules for conlangs he was developing. In an interview for a 1995 Wired magazine article on “The Psychology of World-Building,” Tanaka explained, “School was a barrage of instructions and locker slams and people looking at you expecting a specific reaction. This place,” he said, pointing to the map, “had rules I understood. It was quiet there.”

This retreat into elaborate, self-constructed worlds was not an isolated hobby. For many neurodivergent young people, particularly those on the autism spectrum, fantasy escapism served a critical regulatory function. In a reality that felt sensorially jarring and socially opaque, a book, a video game, or an internal narrative offered a controlled environment. The stimuli were predictable. The social rules were explicit or, in the case of a solitary narrative, irrelevant. The brain could engage in monotropism—that deep, single-channel focus—which acted as a neurological reset. This was not mere avoidance. It was a strategic withdrawal to a place where coherence was possible, allowing the overwhelmed system to restore its equilibrium. The clinical interpretation often pathologized this as “social withdrawal” or “maladaptive daydreaming,” framing the refuge as the problem rather than the solution to a problem the clinician could not see.

The contrast is stark when placed beside another common adaptation: risk-taking. Where fantasy provided escape from too much stimulation, risk-seeking often emerged from a need for more. For the undiagnosed ADHD adolescent, particularly in the inattentive or combined presentation, the world could feel muffled, slow, and under-activating. The brain’s dopamine-mediated reward system, which helps regulate attention and motivation, operates differently. In a state of chronic under-arousal, the individual might subconsciously seek high-stimulus situations to feel alert, focused, and alive. This could manifest as reckless driving, shoplifting, substance experimentation, or plunging into intense, chaotic interpersonal relationships.

A 1993 study published in the Journal of Abnormal Child Psychology followed a cohort of boys diagnosed with “Conduct Disorder.” The researchers noted a subset who reported engaging in risky behaviors not out of anger or defiance, but from a desire to “wake up” or “stop feeling like I’m in a fog.” One participant stated, “It’s like the world goes from black and white to color.” The study did not investigate ADHD as a primary factor, but the description is a textbook account of self-stimulation. The behavior was a flawed, often dangerous tool for regulating a nervous system that couldn’t achieve optimal arousal through conventional means. Society saw a “difficult child” or a “thrill-seeker.” The individual was often conducting a desperate experiment in neurochemical management.

These two coping mechanisms—fantasy escapism and risk-taking—represent opposite poles of the same adaptive spectrum: managing dysregulation. One seeks to reduce an overwhelming world to a manageable size. The other seeks to inflate an underwhelming world to a perceivable intensity. Both are attempts to bridge the gap between the individual’s neurological needs and the environment’s offerings.

The societal interpretation of these adaptations was profoundly shaped by gender and stereotype. The boy like Hiro Tanaka, immersed in fantasy maps or computer code, might be dismissed as a “nerd” or “geek,” his refuge seen as asocial but intellectually benign. A girl exhibiting the same deep retreat into books or imaginary worlds was more likely to be labeled “shy,” “spacey,” or “depressive.” Conversely, the risk-taking boy was often viewed through a lens of masculine expectation—a “handful” or a “rebel.” A girl engaging in similar risky behaviors, particularly relational or sexual risks, faced a far more severe moral judgment, often being pathologized as “promiscuous” or “dramatic,” with diagnoses like Borderline Personality Disorder looming.

In neither case was the behavior traced back to its neurodivergent root. The fantasy was seen as the endpoint, not the coping strategy. The risk-taking was seen as the character flaw, not the regulatory attempt. This fundamental misattribution had lasting consequences. It directed interventions toward suppressing the behavior rather than understanding the need it filled. Therapy might aim to make the daydreamer “more present” or the risk-taker “more responsible,” without providing alternative, healthier methods to achieve the necessary regulation. It was like treating a fever by banning the thermometer.

The tragedy of these self-forged paths is not that they were built. Human beings are ingenious in their survival. The tragedy is that they were built in isolation, without maps, without tools, and without the validation that comes from knowing why the bridge is necessary. Hiro Tanaka built a quiet continent to survive the noise. The risk-taker sought a jolt of color to escape the fog. Each was speaking a solution in a language the world was not yet equipped to hear, their adaptations mistaken for the entirety of their character, their brilliant, desperate engineering reduced to a symptom list.

The story of chemical coping is not one of vice, but of vacancy. Where the environment and the medical establishment offered no viable supports, the nervous system, in its drive for equilibrium, often reached for the nearest available regulator. For the undiagnosed neurodivergent adult in the 1980s or 90s, that regulator was frequently found at the bottom of a glass, in the burn of smoke, or in a pill not prescribed to them. This was not recreational use. It was a pragmatic, if perilous, attempt at occupational therapy.

A 1995 survey of attendees at a private anxiety clinic in Chicago, cited in a Journal of Dual Diagnosis review article, found that 68% of patients reporting "social anxiety" also met research criteria for undiagnosed autism spectrum traits. Their anonymous self-reports consistently described using alcohol not to party, but to perform. "Two drinks," wrote one participant, "and the flood of possible mistakes in a conversation slows to a trickle. I can actually hear what the person is saying." Another noted, "It turns down the volume on the lights, the music, everything. For a few hours, the world isn't sandpaper." The substance did not create euphoria; it performed a sensory and cognitive dampening that the individual could not achieve on their own. It was a chemical interface with a hostile world.

Nicotine served a similarly targeted purpose. A 1989 internal memo from Philip Morris's research division, revealed during later litigation, noted their market research identified a subset of "self-medicating" smokers who reported using cigarettes specifically for "focus" and "mental organization." The memo dryly concluded this segment showed "high brand loyalty due to functional dependency." For the adult with undiagnosed ADHD, the stimulant effect of nicotine could momentarily sharpen a foggy executive function, providing a short-lived window of mental clarity and task initiation. It was a privately administered, over-the-counter stimulant, its therapeutic benefit inextricable from its mortal cost.

The use of marijuana followed a parallel logic. In patient records from a Berkeley, California, harm-reduction clinic operating from 1991-1998, clinicians noted a recurring theme among a subset of clients. These individuals, many later identifying as neurodivergent, reported using cannabis not for a "high," but to blunt the edge of chronic hypervigilance and sensory overload. "It makes the noise in my head quiet down," one client is recorded as saying. "It's the only thing that lets my body relax enough to sleep." The drug was functioning as an anxiolytic and a sensory filter, a role for which no legal, therapeutic alternative was offered to them at the time.

The profound failure of the systems meant to help lies in the diagnostic chain reaction that followed. When these individuals sought help, the presenting problem was the substance use or its consequences: a DUI, marital strife, financial trouble, depressive episodes. The underlying driver—the unmanaged neurodivergent trait—remained invisible. Clinicians, operating from the DSM-III-R and IV, treated the addiction or the mood disorder. They addressed the smoke while the fire—the unsupported autism, the unmanaged ADHD, the untreated sensory processing disorder—continued to burn. The original pain, the one that started the search for chemical relief, was never diagnosed. The self-medication became the primary diagnosis, obscuring its cause entirely.

This misdirection had a cruel symmetry. The very traits that led to self-medication could also pathologize the person seeking help. The autistic adult's flat affect or literal interpretation of questions could be misread as denial or manipulation by a substance abuse counselor. The ADHD client's impulsivity and emotional dysregulation in withdrawal could be labeled as characterological, reinforcing a diagnosis of Borderline Personality Disorder. The solution they had forged became the cage that locked them away from accurate understanding.

To frame this as a moral failing is to misunderstand human neurology. The brain is an organ seeking homeostasis. When it is chronically overstimulated, understimulated, or socially confused, it will seek regulation. If the culture provides no healthy, recognized tools for that regulation, the individual will find their own. The choice is often between a harmful coping mechanism and no coping mechanism at all—between a dangerous equilibrium and a state of constant, debilitating distress. Given that binary, the choice to seek relief, however toxic the source, is a comprehensible act of survival.

The compassion lies in asking the harder question: not "Why the addiction?" but "What was the pain?" The man who needed four beers to sit through a company dinner was not necessarily an alcoholic. He might have been an autistic person trying to endure an unbearable sensory and social gauntlet. The woman who chain-smoked to write her reports was not simply a nicotine addict. She might have been a person with ADHD trying to jump-start a dormant prefrontal cortex. Their solutions were destructive. Their problems were real, unnamed, and untreated.

These private chemical experiments were the darkest form of self-advocacy. They were evidence of a profound need, articulated in the only language the system seemed to understand: crisis. The addiction, the overdose, the failed intervention—these were the desperate, costly telegrams sent from a neurological reality that had no official name. The tragedy is not that they sought to medicate their pain. The tragedy is that a world which offered them no real medicine left them to brew their own poison, and then blamed them for the taste.

This work of re-framing is not an archaeological dig for blame. It is an act of forensic compassion. It asks you to take a behavior from your past—one you may have judged harshly, one that may have caused you or others pain—and hold it up to a new light. Not the glaring light of consequence, but the softer light of context. What was it for? This is the core question that separates shame from understanding.

Begin by selecting one coping mechanism from your own history. It might be the rigid perfectionism, the elaborate fantasy life, the thrill-seeking, the substance use, or something quieter: the need to run the same route every day, the specific food rituals, the hours lost in a single hobby to the exclusion of all else. Write it down. Name it plainly, without the old editorial. Not “my pathetic obsession,” but “the meticulous color-coding of all my school notes.” Not “my reckless phase,” but “the period of driving too fast on country roads.”

Now, on the same page, draw two columns. Label the first: The Old Story. Here, write the narrative you, your family, or your culture attached to that behavior. This is the voice of the time. It often speaks in terms of character and morality. “I was a control freak.” “I was lazy and escapist.” “I was a troublemaker.” “I was weak-willed.” This column is full of judgments, and its function was to explain your difference in the only language available. It is important to acknowledge this story, not to reinforce it, but to see it for what it was: a limited interpretation based on incomplete data.

Label the second column: The Neurodivergent Lens. Here, you reinterpret the behavior not as a character flaw, but as a functional adaptation. This requires moving from “why I was bad” to “what my brain was trying to achieve.” This is not about making excuses. It is about identifying needs. Use the framework of nervous system regulation: was this behavior an attempt to increase stimulation or decrease it? Was it seeking predictability, or managing sensory overload? Was it creating cognitive structure, or providing emotional relief?

Take the example of the color-coded notes. The Old Story might say, “I was obsessive and inflexible.” The Neurodivergent Lens reframes it: “My brain struggles with executive function and prioritization. The color-coding system was an external scaffold I built to impose order on chaotic information. It was a tool to reduce cognitive overwhelm and create predictability in an unpredictable academic environment. The rigidity wasn’t the problem; it was the solution to a problem no one else could see.”

Or take the fast driving. The Old Story: “I was a reckless adrenaline junkie with no regard for safety.” The Neurodivergent Lens: “My ADHD nervous system was chronically under-aroused, leaving me in a mental fog. The high speed and intense focus required for driving provided the stimulation my brain needed to feel alert and present. It was a dangerous but effective method of self-stimulation to achieve a state of cognitive clarity I couldn’t access in calm settings.”

The shift is seismic. You are no longer a villain in your own history. You are an engineer, a problem-solver operating with a cryptic manual and faulty tools, but solving a real problem nonetheless. The behavior may still have been harmful or costly. The compassion lies in acknowledging the ingenuity, not just the damage. You were building a bridge. The critique of the bridge’s design is separate from honoring the necessity of the crossing.

Now, engage in a guided reflection. Find a quiet space and ask yourself these questions, writing the answers without censorship:

What did this behavior allow me to do that I otherwise found difficult or impossible? (e.g., “It allowed me to sit through a loud family dinner.” “It helped me start my homework.” “It gave me a break from constant anxiety.”)
What internal state was it trying to change? (e.g., “To quiet the static in my mind.” “To feel something other than numbness.” “To make a confusing social interaction feel scripted and safe.”)
If that behavior was a message from my younger self to the world, what would it have said? (e.g., “This is too loud.” “I need help focusing.” “I don’t know the rules here.”)

This process uncovers the unmet need. Beneath the “acting out” was a need for regulation. Beneath the “withdrawal” was a need for safety. Beneath the “perfectionism” was a need for predictability. Seeing the need changes everything. You can now grieve the lack of support while honoring your own resourcefulness. You can feel sorrow for the costly methods without contempt for the person who used them.

This is how shame unravels. Shame insists, “I was bad.” Compassion understands, “I was hurting, and I responded to that hurt with the best tools I had.” The former locks you in the past. The latter allows you to collect the wisdom from that past and bring it forward.

The final step is the integration. Write a short, compassionate letter to your younger self from your current, knowing perspective. Do not focus on the behavior’s consequences. Focus on its function. Acknowledge the struggle. Validate the need. You might write, “I see now why you needed that rigid schedule. The world felt like a tidal wave, and you were building a levee, piece by colored piece. You were trying to survive. Thank you for that ingenuity. I wish I could have handed you better tools.”

This is not a one-time exercise. It is a practice. Each time you uncover an old behavior and re-frame it through this lens, you withdraw the power of shame and invest in the power of self-understanding. You are not erasing your history. You are translating it. You are learning to speak the language your brain was using all along, the language of need, adaptation, and survival. The coping mechanisms were not the totality of you. They were the evidence of a brilliant, struggling system doing its best to stay afloat. To see them clearly is to finally see the sailor, not just the storm.
