The man who would lose West Florida sat at a desk in the commandant’s house, a modest structure inside the wooden palisade of Fort San Carlos. The night was quiet, the kind of quiet that amplified the rustle of paper and the occasional call of a sentry. Carlos Dehault Delassus was writing, a routine act of administration that had become an exercise in fiction. His monthly report to Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, dated late August 1810, survives in the Spanish colonial archives. It is a masterpiece of bureaucratic omission. He cataloged routine matters—the state of the powder magazine, the condition of the barracks. He did not write that his effective authority extended scarcely beyond the fort’s gate. He did not mention that the armed men gathering at St. John’s Plains had just voted to raise a militia. The report was a performance of normalcy, a line of communication he had to maintain even as the world it described was dissolving around him.

His military situation was not just weak; it was a façade. The fort itself was a dilapidated stockade on the Mississippi bluffs, more symbolic than strategic. An inventory from that summer, referenced in later American accounts, listed its garrison at between twenty-eight and forty soldiers. Many were ill-equipped. Morale, as inferred from desertion records and Delassus’s own pleas for back pay, was brittle. They were isolated, over six hundred miles by sea and land from their nearest significant support in Pensacola. The geography of power had been inverted. The real force in the district—several hundred armed Anglo-American planters and their enslaved laborers—was outside the walls, not within them.

Delassus was not ignorant of the threat. His papers and later testimonies show he received at least two explicit warnings in September. The first, around the 20th, informed him of armed men converging north of Baton Rouge. The second, on the eve of the assault, specified the rebel plan to strike at dawn. He acknowledged these reports. His response was to double the guard and place the garrison on alert. He did not send patrols to disperse the rebels at their rendezvous. He did not order preemptive arrests. This was not negligence, but a calculated, and ultimately tragic, adherence to a policy of conciliation. To act aggressively would shatter the delicate fiction he had nurtured for months—the fiction that he was still a governor negotiating with loyal, if disgruntled, subjects.

That fiction had been sustained by a series of “peace dinners.” In the weeks prior, Delassus had hosted the convention’s leading delegates at his table. Silver gleamed under candlelight. Toasts were made to health and harmony. The conversations, as recalled by participants like John Rhea, were cordial, even warm. They discussed the grievances of the settlers—the lack of courts, insecure land titles. Delassus listened, expressed sympathy, and promised to convey their petitions to higher authorities. Both sides understood the performance. For the delegates, it was a chance to gauge the governor’s will. For Delassus, it was a stalling tactic, a hope that reinforcements might yet arrive from Pensacola or that the crisis would somehow pass.

But the correspondence with Pensacola told its own story. Folch’s replies, preserved in fragments, were evasive. He cited a lack of troops, a lack of funds, a need for orders from Havana. The thread connecting Baton Rouge to the empire had grown thin, and was now vibrating with a warning hum Delassus alone seemed to hear. His options had narrowed to two, both bad: provoke a fight he could not win, or maintain the charisma of authority until it was stripped from him by force. He chose the latter. Doubling the guard was a signal, but a weak one. It was for the benefit of his own soldiers, to show he was taking action. It was for the rebels, a minor show of resolve. It was for the historical record, proof he had not been caught sleeping.

On the night of September 22, the performance entered its final act. The additional sentries paced the walls, peering into a darkness that was no longer empty. Delassus retired to his quarters. He knew, with the cold clarity of a career soldier reading a map, what the dawn would likely bring. His government had already ended. The remaining hours were merely administrative, a waiting for the formalities of collapse to catch up with the fact. The fort was not a fortress. It was a stage, and he was the lead actor in a play whose audience had long since stopped believing in the plot.

The dinners began in late August and continued into September. Delassus issued the invitations personally. The men who arrived at the commandant’s house were the same ones drafting resolutions at St. John’s Plains: John Rhea, the methodical chairman; William Barrow, the wealthy planter; John H. Johnson and others. They left their weapons at the gate, a gesture of protocol that underscored the surreal nature of the evenings. The governor’s table, set with Spanish silver and crystal, was an island of colonial order in a sea of rebellion.

The conversations, as later recounted by delegates in letters and memoirs, followed a predictable script. Delassus, playing the gracious host, inquired after their families and crops. He expressed understanding for their frustrations—the maddening slowness of Spanish land courts, the absence of trial by jury. He promised, with apparent sincerity, to advocate for them in Pensacola and Havana. In turn, the delegates presented their grievances not as ultimatums, but as petitions from loyal subjects seeking redress from a distant crown. They spoke of their desire for stability, for the protection of property. The word “independence” was never uttered. The wine, imported from Madeira, helped smooth the edges of the discussion.

Beneath the polite surface, each side was conducting an intelligence operation. The delegates studied Delassus’s demeanor, listening for hesitation in his assurances, watching for signs of fear or resolve. They noted the thinness of the garrison, the quiet in the barracks, the aged condition of the fort’s timbers. For his part, Delassus was taking the measure of the rebellion’s leadership. He was determining who among them might be reasoned with, who was a radical, and how deep the conspiracy ran. The true purpose of these meals was not diplomacy, but mutual reconnaissance. Every toast to “His Catholic Majesty” was a lie, and everyone present knew it.

The critical moment in this charade came shortly after the convention, on September 5, passed a resolution declaring its intent to “assist” the governor in administering the district. This was a transparent seizure of executive power, cloaked in the language of cooperation. Delassus’s response, documented in the convention’s own journal and confirmed by Spanish correspondence, was to feign acceptance. He thanked the delegates for their offer of support. He did not protest. He did not order their arrest for sedition. Instead, he incorporated their usurpation into his crumbling fiction of control.

This decision was a tactical surrender born of absolute weakness. A letter from Delassus to Folch, dated around this time and referenced in later historical studies, reveals his calculus. He had, he wrote, fewer than fifty effectives. A direct confrontation would result in the immediate overrun of the fort and the probable slaughter of his garrison. By appearing to cooperate, he bought what little time he could. He hoped the performance would keep the rebels placated and lawless until reinforcements, repeatedly promised but never dispatched, might arrive from Pensacola. It was a gamble that his authority could be preserved through the illusion of its exercise.

The delegates left these dinners with conflicting impressions. Some, like Rhea, believed the governor was a reasonable man trapped by circumstance, perhaps even sympathetic to their cause. Others suspected the hospitality was a stall. What none realized was the profound isolation their host felt. After the last delegate departed, the governor’s house fell silent. The servants cleared the plates that had held roasted venison and turtle soup. Delassus was alone with the reports on his desk—the latest tally of rebel numbers, another unanswered plea to Folch. The candlelight that had glinted off the wine glasses now illuminated the emptiness of his options.

The final “peace dinner” was likely held in the week before the assault. The atmosphere was thicker, the courtesies more strained. News of the armed bands gathering in the pine woods could not be kept from the table. Delassus may have alluded to it, framing it as a concern about “disorder” that he trusted the delegates to help quell. They, in turn, assured him of their commitment to peace. Both statements were false. The conversation was a dance on the edge of a blade. When the delegates rode back to their plantations that night, they carried with them the confirmed understanding that the governor would not, or could not, stop them. The time for dinners was over. The next meeting would be held at gunpoint.

The decision to attack Fort San Carlos was not made in a single dramatic vote. It emerged through a series of smaller, escalating commitments in the second week of September. Philemon Thomas, a veteran of the Revolution and a formidable presence in the district, began sending riders from his base at St. John’s Plains. Their instructions, recorded in later pension applications and local histories, were simple: gather the men. Assemble at the Indian mounds north of Baton Rouge. Bring arms and powder. The call went out across the Felicianas, to the settlements along the Thompson’s Creek and the Comite River. It reached small planters, overseers, and frontiersmen whose patience with Spanish rule had evaporated. They came on horseback and by foot, a loose collection of volunteers converging on a patch of high ground east of the Mississippi.

Governor Vicente Folch, in his headquarters at the Presidio of Pensacola, received Delassus’s increasingly urgent dispatches throughout the late summer of 1810. The archival record of their correspondence, pieced together from Spanish colonial files and later American State Department translations, shows a clear trajectory from concern to paralysis. Folch’s initial replies, in August, were formulaic. He acknowledged the reports of unrest and promised to seek guidance from his superiors in Havana. He advised prudence and patience. This was the standard bureaucratic lexicon, a language designed to convey activity while postponing action.

Folch’s own predicament, however, was nearly as dire as Delassus’s. A summary of his available forces, compiled by his adjutant that September and later cited by American intelligence officers, listed fewer than three hundred regular troops to garrison all of Spanish West Florida from the Apalachicola River to the Mississippi. Most were concentrated at Pensacola and Mobile. The territory was vast, the supply lines to Cuba were long and vulnerable to British interdiction, and the treasury in Havana was perpetually empty. Folch was governing a bankrupt colony in the wake of metropolitan collapse; Napoleon’s armies occupied Spain itself. Every decision carried the risk of expending his last reserves of men and money for no gain.

By early September, Delassus’s tone shifted from reporting unrest to requesting direct military intervention. Folch’s response, dated September 10 and preserved in the Spanish archives, marked a critical pivot. He wrote that he was preparing a relief expedition. He ordered supplies gathered and troops readied. He assured Delassus that help was coming. But the letter contained qualifiers—the need for final approval, the state of the harbor, the readiness of the ships. It was a promise hedged with escape clauses. Folch dispatched this message, but he did not immediately dispatch the expedition.

Instead, he turned to Cuba. A flurry of communiqués sailed for Havana in mid-September, urgently requesting reinforcements and funds. The replies, when they came weeks later, were noncommittal. The captain-general of Cuba had his own priorities and perceived Pensacola as a distant, secondary outpost. The administrative disarray of the Spanish Empire, with its chain of command shattered by the Peninsular War, meant that decisive action from a higher authority was unlikely. Folch was left waiting for orders that would never clearly arrive, while the crisis at Baton Rouge moved from the diplomatic to the military phase.

The final and most damning document in this sequence is a confidential memorandum Folch drafted for his own files, dated September 21, 1810—two days before the attack on Fort San Carlos. A copy was later captured by American forces and filed in Washington. In it, Folch conducted a stark assessment. Even if he dispatched every available soldier from Pensacola, he calculated, they would arrive too late to save Baton Rouge. The overland route was impassable for a formal column, and a sea voyage would take weeks. More critically, he concluded that such a force would be insufficient to pacify the entire rebellious district. Sending it would strip Pensacola bare for no strategic benefit. His handwritten conclusion was blunt: a relief expedition was now “inútil”—useless.

This was the moment the last thread of support was cut. Folch made a cold, strategic decision to sacrifice Baton Rouge to preserve Pensacola and Mobile. He never informed Delassus of this conclusion. The commandant in Baton Rouge continued to scan the river for signs of Spanish sails, clinging to the hope encoded in Folch’s earlier, ambiguous promise of aid. He was waiting for a rescue that his superior had already, quietly, canceled.

Folch’s failure was not one of courage, but of capacity and calculus. He was the senior imperial administrator in the region, and his inaction ratified the reality on the ground. The Spanish system, strained to its breaking point by European war and colonial overextension, could no longer project power or even basic solidarity to its farthest precincts. Delassus was not just isolated by geography or rebel numbers; he was abandoned by the very administrative machinery he was sworn to uphold. The silence from Pensacola was more eloquent than any declaration of war. It told the rebels, and Delassus, that the fort was truly alone.

Philemon Thomas gave the order to move out an hour before first light. The men gathered at the Indian mounds, perhaps eighty strong according to later pension affidavits, fell into a ragged column. They carried a mix of Kentucky long rifles, trade muskets, and hunting pieces. Their orders, as recounted by participants like Isaac Johnson in applications for military bounty land decades later, were explicit: move quietly, hold fire until commanded, and follow the plan for the gate. The night was cool for September, and a dense fog had rolled in from the river, swallowing the pinewoods and flattening sound. It was, one rebel would later tell a Baton Rouge newspaper, “as if the very land was offering us its cover.”

They advanced southwest, keeping to the high ground east of the river road. The fog turned the world into a gray tunnel, the man in front a shifting shadow. The only sounds were the muffled creak of leather, the soft crush of boots on damp earth, and the occasional metallic click of a ramrod checked and reseated. This was not a professional army on the march, but a collection of neighbors and kinsmen—planters, overseers, millers, hunters—who knew this terrain intimately. They used that knowledge now, avoiding the main paths where Spanish patrols might, in theory, have been posted. No patrols were encountered. The silence from the fort ahead was complete.

Thomas halted the column in a stand of water oaks several hundred yards from the fort’s northeastern wall. The fog here was thinner, a milky veil through which the dark outline of the stockade emerged like a ship’s hull. A single lantern glowed atop the main blockhouse. The plan, devised by Thomas and a few former militiamen, relied on audacity and the element of surprise. They would not lay siege. They would not parley. They would walk in.

He selected a small party for the first, critical maneuver. Accounts vary on the exact number, but between four and eight men were chosen. Their task was to approach the fort’s main gate directly and openly, as if they were a routine patrol or a group of settlers arriving early on business. If challenged, they were to give a prearranged signal of peaceful intent. The primary objective, however, was to physically seize the gate mechanism and prevent it from being closed once the main force was spotted. This was the riskiest part of the operation. These men would be exposed, the first to face Spanish muskets.

The rest of the force, with Thomas at its head, would circle to the north and west, using the fog and the natural cover of the bluffs to get as close as possible to the palisade. They would wait for the sound of a struggle at the gate, or for a pre-arranged whistle, as the signal to rush forward. Timing was everything. If the infiltration group was discovered too soon, the garrison would have time to barricade itself. If the main force moved too late, the infiltrators would be captured or shot.

The infiltrators moved out first, melting into the fog toward the road that led to the gate. Thomas watched them go, then gestured for the main body to follow him. They moved in a wide arc, their progress slow and deliberate. The damp air smelled of river mud and decaying cypress. As they drew nearer, the details of the fort became clearer: the vertical logs of the palisade, the narrow firing ports, the shallow ditch surrounding it. They could hear no movement from within. The only sign of life was the sentry’s lantern, a faint yellow eye in the gray.

Thomas positioned his men along a shallow ravine about fifty yards from the northwest corner of the stockade. Here, they crouched, weapons ready, eyes straining toward the gate. The wait began. Minutes stretched. The fog began to glow with the first suggestion of dawn, a diffusion of light without a source. The tension was a physical presence. A man later recalled the feeling in a letter to his brother: “I heard my own heart as if it was in my ears, and the dew on the leaves sounded like drum taps.”

Then, a sound cut the silence—not a whistle or a shot, but the loud, protesting screech of iron hinges. The main gate was being opened from the inside, part of the fort’s morning routine. The infiltrators had either successfully bluffed their way past a sentry, or they had arrived at the precise moment a guard was opening it for the day. The record is unclear. What is documented is what happened next.

Thomas stood up in the ravine. He did not shout. He simply raised his arm and brought it down, pointing forward. The men rose as one and began to run, not with a wild yell, but with a low, focused urgency. The sound of eighty men charging through underbrush finally broke the morning quiet. From inside the fort, a Spanish voice cried out in alarm. A single musket shot rang out, sharp and flat in the damp air.

The race was for the open gate. The infiltrators were there, struggling with a Spanish guard. The main body of rebels, led by Thomas, covered the last open ground as the fort erupted into confused noise—shouts in Spanish and English, the clash of bodies, the panicked drumming of feet on wooden ramparts. The fog, which had concealed their approach, now swirled into the compound with them, mixing with the smell of gunpowder and damp wool. Philemon Thomas crossed the threshold not as a subject, but as a conqueror. The governor’s fiction of control ended not with a negotiated surrender, but with the sound of his own front door being kicked in.

The infiltrators at the gate, led by men like Samuel Cockerham and James McVay, had neutralized the single sentry. As Philemon Thomas and the main force sprinted the final yards, they saw the heavy timber gate standing wide. The Spanish guard detail, likely just two or three soldiers performing the morning ritual, was overwhelmed before they could raise an alarm. This initial scuffle was quiet, but the pounding of booted feet from the ravine gave the assault away. A sentry on the north wall shouted. The shot that followed, likely fired by that sentry or a soldier rushing from the barracks, went high and wide. It was the only defensive shot fired during the entire engagement.

The rebel force poured through the opening. The fort’s interior was a compact rectangle of beaten earth, with barracks along the west wall, the commandant’s house on the east, and the central blockhouse. The fog, thinner inside the stockade, revealed a scene of chaotic awakening. Spanish soldiers, perhaps two dozen, stumbled from their quarters, some in uniform coats, others in shirtsleeves, clutching their muskets. They formed no line. There was no time.

The violence was brief and brutal. Two Spanish soldiers, identified in later Spanish casualty reports as Corporal Francisco and Private Miguel, made a stand near the barracks door. They leveled their bayonets. A volley from several rebels, including Thomas’s men Isaac Johnson and William Cooper, cut them down. Both soldiers fell where they stood. They were the only fatalities on the Spanish side. Their deaths, occurring within the first sixty seconds of the incursion, broke any remaining organized resistance. The sight of their crumpled forms, the sudden, shocking noise of the concentrated fire in the enclosed space, paralyzed the garrison. An order was shouted in Spanish—likely to lay down arms. Muskets clattered on the hard ground. The fight for Fort San Carlos was over almost as soon as it had begun.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus was in his residence. He had been awake, dressed in his uniform, awaiting the dawn he knew would bring crisis. The sound of the shot and the surge of shouting brought him to his door. He stepped out onto the small porch of the commandant’s house, not as a fugitive, but as the ranking officer. He carried no weapon. His appearance, authoritative and calm amidst the bedlam, momentarily stilled the rebels swarming the yard.

Philemon Thomas approached him. The two men, both veterans, faced each other in the gray light. Thomas, according to an account he gave years later to the Baton Rouge Gazette, stated the bald fact: “Sir, the fort is taken.” Delassus, in a response recorded in the same article and corroborated by other witnesses, did not argue. He looked past Thomas at his surrendered garrison, at the rebels now securing the blockhouse and disarming his men. He saw the two bodies by the barracks. He then performed his final official act. He surrendered the post. He offered his sword. Thomas, in a gesture that mixed frontier pragmatism with a shred of deference, refused to take it. The symbol was unnecessary; the reality was absolute.

The entire assault, from the breach of the gate to the governor’s surrender, lasted less than ten minutes. The rebel force suffered no casualties. The speed and precision of the operation, a testament to Thomas’s planning and the paralyzing effect of complete surprise, had rendered the fort’s defenses moot. The Spanish garrison, outnumbered three-to-one and caught in the vulnerable moment of morning changeover, never had a chance to mount a coherent defense.

Delassus was taken into protective custody inside his own home. A guard was posted, not to imprison him harshly, but to prevent his interference or rescue. The rebels’ treatment of him, as documented in letters from both Delassus and his captors, remained courteous, even respectful. He was a prisoner of war, not a criminal. His person and property were secured. This civility underscored the peculiar nature of the rebellion: these were not wild marauders, but propertied men seeking a political outcome, and they understood the protocols of officer status.

With the fort secured, Thomas dispatched riders to St. John’s Plains. The message was simple: “The work is done.” The convention, waiting in suspense, now received the signal that the military gamble had succeeded. The path was clear. The political revolution could begin in earnest, but its first and most critical act—the seizure of sovereign power from the Spanish crown—had been accomplished not by debate or decree, but by eighty armed men in the fog of a September morning. The Lone Star flag, sewn in secret over the preceding weeks, could now be unfurled. It would fly over a fort that was already, irrevocably, theirs.

The historical image of Carlos Dehault Delassus is often that of the hapless governor, the man who lost West Florida. This verdict, while tidy, misses the structural reality of his command. The evidence from his correspondence and the Spanish military ledgers does not paint a portrait of incompetence or treachery, but of a competent administrator facing an impossible structural crisis. His failure was systemic, not personal.

Delassus’s tactical decisions in the summer of 1810—the feigned cooperation, the peace dinners, the refusal to provoke a fight he could not win—were not signs of weakness, but of rational assessment. A report he filed to Havana earlier that year, later archived in Seville, meticulously cataloged his assets: the fort’s powder was damp, its artillery carriages rotten, his effective force barely filled a single barracks room. He knew the rebel numbers. To have arrested the convention delegates in July, as his authority demanded, would have been a suicidal gesture. His choice to temporize was the only logical move left, a holding action in the hope that an empire would remember its farthest outpost.

The empire, however, was gone. This was the core of Delassus’s predicament. His governorship depended on a chain of command that had been severed in Europe. The Peninsular War had left colonial administrators like Vicente Folch in Pensacola and the Captain-General in Havana operating in a fog of their own, cut off from Madrid, bereft of funds, and paralyzed by the risk of any decisive action. The bureaucratic machinery meant to sustain Delassus had seized. Folch’s confidential memo of September 21, declaring relief “useless,” was the formal acknowledgment of this paralysis. Delassus was not merely under-resourced; he was administratively orphaned.

His performance in the final hours underscores this reading. He was awake and in uniform at dawn on September 23. He did not flee or hide. He emerged to surrender his post with formality, preserving the dignity of his office in its dissolution. The rebels’ subsequent courteous treatment of him, noted in multiple accounts, suggests they recognized they had defeated a soldier, not a coward. He had followed his orders to the limit of their utility. When Philemon Thomas informed him the fort was taken, Delassus possessed the clarity to see the truth. His surrender was the final competent act of his command—an acceptance of factual reality that spared his men further bloodshed.

The true cause of the fall of Fort San Carlos was not Delassus’s inaction, but the collapse of the system that posted him there. Spain could no longer project power, supply troops, or make timely decisions across the Atlantic. The governor was the visible point of failure, but the fracture lines ran all the way to Madrid. He was a man standing in a room as the foundation gave way, blamed for the crash of the house.

In the end, Delassus’s legacy is that of a capable officer left holding a map to a territory that had already slipped from his nation’s grasp. The rebellion succeeded not because the governor was inadequate, but because the imperial structure he represented had already hollowed out from within. His story is less a tale of personal failure than a case study in the inevitable conclusion of colonial overextension: the moment the administrator, however competent, becomes the solitary actor on a stage from which all supporting players have quietly exited.

The flag was brought forth from a saddlebag. It was a single blue canton with a five-pointed white star, sewn onto a field of white. The design, reportedly conceived by convention delegate Dr. John Rhea, was a deliberate echo of the French Bonny Blue of the Bonapartist republics, stripped of its revolutionary tricolor to signal a new and independent entity. In the damp, gun-smelling air of the fort's central yard, as the sun finally burned through the fog, the banner was run up the flagpole that had, until an hour before, flown the Spanish ensign. There was no ceremony, no speech. The men gathered in silence, looking up as it caught a feeble morning breeze. Its meaning was absolute: Spanish West Florida was gone. What replaced it was now a question of law.

Riders had already galloped for St. John's Plains. The convention, having adjourned in suspense days earlier, reassembled with urgent haste upon receiving Thomas's message. The procedural record, preserved in the West Florida archives and later published in the Washington National Intelligencer, shows John Rhea reconvening the body before noon on September 23. The atmosphere was one of stunned triumph, undercut by acute awareness of their precarious position. They held a single fort. Spanish power, though wounded, still held Mobile and Pensacola. The United States’ intentions remained opaque. Their first act was to formalize the military fact. By resolution, they declared the Baton Rouge district “free and independent.” Then they turned to the document Rhea had been drafting for weeks.

The Constitution of the Republic of West Florida, adopted that same day, was a pragmatic instrument. It borrowed heavily from the Kentucky and Tennessee constitutions, establishing a bicameral legislature, a judiciary, and a chief magistrate to be called the President. Its most notable feature, analyzed in later legal journals, was its explicit and immediate plea for annexation. Article XIV stated the republic’s desire to be “incorporated into the Union of the United States of America.” This was not a statement of indefinite sovereignty; it was a prospectus for a merger. The convention understood their revolution as a transitional act, a necessary bridge to American citizenship. They elected a twelve-man senate to govern temporarily and began organizing civil parishes. But the real work of the day was the Declaration of Independence, also penned primarily by Rhea. The text, a blend of Lockean grievance and frontier grievance, listed the “insupportable tyranny” of Spanish rule: the denial of trial by jury, the arbitrary exercise of power, the failure to provide land grants or civil security. It proclaimed the people’s right to “institute a new government.” Signatures were affixed. The Republic of West Florida, by the power of declared words and a single captured fort, now existed.

Back at Baton Rouge, the practical work of possession began. Spanish prisoners were paroled after swearing not to take up arms against the new republic. The two dead Spanish soldiers were given a military burial. Governor Delassus, under his polite guard, began packing his personal papers. The rebels, now soldiers of the republic, posted pickets and took inventory of the captured stores: 150 stands of arms, several small cannon, barrels of damp powder, and the fort’s treasury of a few hundred Spanish dollars. The Lone Star flag snapped above them, a bright, simple claim in the autumn sky.

The speed of the transition, from predawn assault to constitutional government by sunset, was breathtaking. It revealed a core truth of the rebellion: the military action was not the culmination, but the enabling mechanism. Philemon Thomas had provided the space. John Rhea and the convention rushed to fill it with institutions, however provisional. Their success on September 23 created a sovereign fact where none had existed that morning. But a fact, as they well knew, is not the same as security. The flag was up. The question now was how long it could stay aloft, and who, besides the men who raised it, would salute it.
