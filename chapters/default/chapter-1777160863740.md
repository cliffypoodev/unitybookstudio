Six years before Philemon Thomas opened the gate at Fort San Carlos, a different kind of violence erupted along the same contested border. It left no declarations, formed no republic, and established no lasting government. It was a raw, personal affair, a spasm of grievance and ambition that demonstrated how easily the frontier could ignite. The archival record of the Kemper raid is thinner, a scattering of Spanish military reports, American diplomatic protests, and later court depositions. But the fragments show a pattern of chaos, not calculation.

Reuben Kemper was a man built for friction. A native of Virginia who had migrated to the Pinckneyville settlement near the Mississippi border, he operated a tavern and a ferry. His business depended on the flow of people and goods across a line that maps disputed and soldiers did not patrol. In 1804, that line was the source of his undoing. Spanish authorities, citing vague customs regulations, seized a flatboat of his goods. Kemper protested. The local Spanish commandant, a man named Juan Ventura Morales, was unmoved. The seizure was not an isolated act of piracy; it was the assertion of a sovereignty that the United States denied and Spain could barely enforce. For Kemper, it was a personal insult and a financial loss. He decided on redress through force.

He did not call a convention. He gathered his brothers, Nathan and Samuel, and a handful of neighbors whose loyalties were to their own interests, not to a flag. Their target was not the administrative heart at Baton Rouge, but the smaller, isolated Spanish outposts along the Manchac and Amite rivers. These were stations, not forts—wooden blockhouses manned by a dozen soldiers at most. The plan, as it existed, was simply to attack.

On a hot afternoon in late July 1804, Reuben Kemper and about twenty men approached the first Spanish post. The details of the assault come from the subsequent testimony of a participant, recorded in an American court. There was no night march, no fog, no coordinated gate-opening. The men approached openly, armed with rifles and axes. The Spanish sentry challenged them. Kemper’s answer was a shot. The door to the blockhouse was barricaded from within. The Kempers and their followers spent the next hour hacking at it with axes, their shouts and the splintering wood the only declaration of intent. When the door gave way, the Spanish soldiers inside—outnumbered and unprepared for a siege—surrendered. The Kempers took them prisoner, looted the station of its arms and powder, and then set it ablaze. The black smoke column rising over the pine flats was the only proclamation.

They moved to a second outpost. The pattern repeated. Surprise was no longer possible; word traveled faster than men on horseback in that flat country. The second garrison had fortified its position. A brief, sharp firefight ensued. Spanish musketry from the loopholes wounded one of Kemper’s men. The return fire from the raiders, less disciplined but more numerous, killed a Spanish soldier. The outpost was overrun, not through tactics, but through weight of numbers and a relentless, angry press. This station was also put to the torch.

The violence was not the clean, surgical strike of 1810. It was arson and plunder. The Kempers took no governor captive, drafted no articles of surrender. They took what they could carry and destroyed the rest. Their prisoners, a handful of terrified Spanish regulars and a local militiaman, were dragged along with the band as it moved erratically through the borderland. The militiaman, a man named Bétizac, would later provide a detailed account to Spanish authorities. He described a party fueled by resentment and alcohol, their leadership fracturing, their purpose diffusing from retaliation into roaming banditry.

Within days, the raid collapsed under its own disorder. The Kempers, expecting a popular uprising among the American settlers, found only wary isolation. The planters of the Feliciana district had grievances with Spain, but they were not prepared to join a burning, looting expedition that invited retribution without offering a stable alternative. The Kempers were left exposed. A Spanish force from Baton Rouge, though small, was mobilized to pursue them. More effectively, the Spanish authorities applied political pressure through the United States government, demanding the filibusterers be apprehended as criminals.

Reuben Kemper and his brothers fled north across the line into American Mississippi Territory. They were not greeted as heroes. American officials, eager to avoid an international incident, issued warrants for their arrest. The Kemper raid ended not with a flag-raising, but with a fugitive scramble. It was a fire that burned only what it touched and then sputtered out.

The contrast with the rebellion of 1810 is etched in method. Philemon Thomas targeted the symbolic center of power. Reuben Kemper attacked the nearest outpost. Thomas secured his prisoners and incorporated them into his new order; Kemper took hostages and then did not know what to do with them. Thomas acted with the tacit support of a convened body of settlers; Kemper acted on his own and assumed support would follow. The 1804 raid was a symptom of the borderland’s disease—the volatile mix of ambiguous authority and armed men. The 1810 rebellion was the diagnosis, and the deliberate cure.

The Kempers’ failure taught a brutal lesson to the men who would later gather at St. John’s Plains. Violence alone was not enough. It had to be directed, timed, and followed by something more substantial than smoke. When Reuben Kemper looked at the Spanish border, he saw a line that had wronged him. When John Rhea and Philemon Thomas looked at it six years later, they saw a line that could be erased. One view produced a raid. The other produced a republic.

Reuben Kemper was a creature of the border, a man whose identity was forged in the friction between maps. He was born in Fauquier County, Virginia, in 1770, a son of the westward tide. By 1800, he and his brothers had settled in the no-man’s-land near the thirty-first parallel, a strip of land claimed by Spain, coveted by the United States, and inhabited by people who gave their primary allegiance to their own acres. A surviving portrait, painted years later, shows a man with a hard, direct gaze and a mouth set in a line that suggests defiance, not deliberation. He did not keep a diary. His motivations must be read from his actions and from the legal depositions of those who rode with him or suffered his raids.

His grievance was not ideological. It was economic. According to court records from Mississippi Territory, Kemper operated a ferry and a tavern at Pinckneyville, a settlement whose very existence was a geopolitical challenge. His livelihood depended on the uninterrupted flow of people and goods across the Mississippi River and along the muddy trails that connected American territory to Spanish West Florida. In early 1804, Spanish customs officials, acting under the authority of Commandant Juan Ventura Morales at Baton Rouge, seized one of Kemper’s flatboats. The cargo was confiscated. The justification was a violation of Spanish revenue laws—laws the American government did not recognize as legitimate for that territory.

For the Spanish, it was a routine assertion of a fading authority. For Kemper, it was a personal declaration of war. He did not petition the Spanish court. He did not appeal to the U.S. Consul. As he later stated in a deposition, he considered the seizure an act of “piracy.” The loss was not catastrophic, but the principle was absolute. On the frontier, a man’s property was an extension of his autonomy. To take it without a fight was to surrender not just goods, but status.

The response was not political, but fraternal. He turned to his brothers, Nathan and Samuel. Their bond was the original compact, stronger than any allegiance to a distant republic or a crumbling empire. They recruited neighbors, but not with a call to liberty. As participant accounts later revealed, the appeal was to shared resentment and the promise of plunder. The group that assembled in July 1804 was a posse, not a militia. They had no Articles of Association, no elected officers beyond the implicit authority of the Kempers themselves. Their goal was retaliation, not revolution.

When they attacked the Spanish outposts, their tactics revealed their mindset. They did not send a formal demand for surrender. They did not attempt to negotiate the release of prisoners for terms. They hacked at doors with axes, a method of brute force that required no finesse. After capturing the small garrisons, they looted the military stores—muskets, powder, shot—and then set the wooden blockhouses ablaze. The fires served no strategic purpose. They were an act of erasure, a physical expression of contempt. The Kempers were not capturing infrastructure for a new state; they were punishing the old one.

The Spanish militiaman, Bétizac, who was taken hostage, provided a crucial witness account to his superiors after his release. He described a band that was volatile and increasingly drunk, its cohesion dissolving as its initial rage spent itself. Reuben Kemper, he reported, argued with his brothers over direction. Nathan wanted to push deeper into Spanish territory; Samuel counseled a return to American soil. Reuben seemed most focused on holding onto their prisoners as bargaining chips, though he had no clear idea whom to bargain with. The leadership was improvisational, reacting to events rather than directing them.

This personal, anarchic quality doomed the enterprise. The American settlers of the Feliciana parishes, who would later form the backbone of the 1810 rebellion, wanted order, not chaos. They needed a system that protected their land titles and regulated commerce. The Kemper raid offered only violence and the certainty of Spanish reprisal. When no uprising materialized, the Kempers were isolated. Their raid became a roaming, desperate affair. They released their exhausted hostages near the Amite River and fled north.

The contrast with the men of 1810 is stark. John Rhea was a proceduralist, a man who believed in the sanctity of meeting minutes. Philemon Thomas was a tactician who planned his assault for maximum symbolic effect with minimal bloodshed. Fulwar Skipwith was a diplomat who thought in terms of constitutions and recognition. Reuben Kemper was a man who thought in terms of a flatboat and an axe. He represented the frontier’s capacity for explosive, self-defeating violence. The rebellion six years later would channel that same violent energy, but through a different vessel—one built of committees, declarations, and a flag. The Kempers showed what happened when the border caught fire. The convention at St. John’s Plains would show how to control that fire, and how to use its heat to forge something new.

The power vacuum Reuben Kemper exploited in 1804 was not an accident of geography, but a product of imperial collapse. Spain’s hold on West Florida had always been more cartographic than corporeal. As Vicente Folch, the Governor of Pensacola, reported to his superiors in Havana that year, the entire Spanish military presence between the Pearl and Perdido rivers consisted of fewer than three hundred regular troops. These men were scattered across a handful of aging forts and blockhouses, their supply lines stretching back to Cuba across a Gulf patrolled by privateers and rival navies. The garrison at Baton Rouge, the district’s administrative heart, typically numbered between twenty-eight and forty soldiers. They manned Fort San Carlos, a modest earthen and timber work more suited to policing a quiet river town than repelling an invasion. When Commandant Morales seized Reuben Kemper’s flatboat, he was performing the only act of sovereignty he could reliably enforce: the levying of customs duties on river traffic. It was a revenue stop, not a border control.

This military anemia was a symptom of a deeper political paralysis. Spain had acquired West Florida from Britain in 1783, a consolation prize at the end of the American Revolution. For Madrid, the territory was a strategic buffer, a thin strip of land intended to keep the aggressive new American republic away from the more valuable ports of Mobile and Pensacola, and from the approaches to New Spain. It was never a colony for settlement. Spanish policy actively discouraged large-scale immigration from non-Spanish subjects. But by 1804, that policy had failed completely. As later census estimates compiled by American officials would show, Anglo-American settlers and their enslaved laborers outnumbered Spanish loyalists in the Baton Rouge district by at least four to one. The Spanish administration, under a succession of governors like Morales and his successor Carlos Dehault Delassus, presided over a population that viewed them as foreign tax collectors, not legitimate rulers.

The legal ground beneath Spanish authority was also shifting. The Louisiana Purchase of 1803, a transaction between Napoleon and President Thomas Jefferson, did not merely transfer a vast territory to the United States. It ignited a fierce diplomatic dispute over its eastern boundary. Jefferson’s administration, citing French archival claims, insisted the purchase included all land east to the Perdido River—absorbing most of Spanish West Florida. Spain, referencing the Treaty of San Ildefonso that had returned Louisiana to France, vehemently disagreed, insisting the Perdido was the western limit of Florida. The result was what one American diplomat called “a cartographic standoff.” On the ground, it meant that American settlers like the Kempers, flooding into the region, believed they were moving onto land that was already, or soon would be, American. Spanish customs duties were thus not just an inconvenience; they were an illegitimate imposition by a foreign power squatting on U.S. soil.

This ambiguity created a permissive environment for filibusters. As military reports from Baton Rouge to Havana indicate, Spanish commanders knew they could not deter a determined armed incursion. Their strategy was one of delay and diplomacy—appealing to Madrid for reinforcements they knew would not come, and to Washington to restrain its own citizens. When Reuben Kemper attacked, the local commandant did not have the troops to pursue him effectively. He could only protest through channels, a process that took months. The United States government, for its part, occupied an awkward position. While President Jefferson coveted the territory, he could not openly sanction armed raids by private citizens without risking war with Spain. So American officials in the Mississippi Territory issued those warrants for the Kempers’ arrest, a gesture of diplomatic propriety. But they did not pursue the brothers with any vigor. The Kempers remained at large, a symbol of the ungoverned space between two arguing governments.

The raid of 1804, therefore, was a test of the vacuum. Kemper’s axe hacking at the blockhouse door was a physical probe of Spanish resolve. The lack of a swift, crushing response was the answer. It demonstrated to every ambitious settler in the Felicianas that Spanish power was a façade. The real authority on the frontier belonged to the men with the most rifles and the least to lose. The lesson was not lost on the more calculating men who would, in a few years, replace random violence with organized rebellion. They saw that Spain could not hold what it claimed. The only question remaining was who would take it, and under what flag.

President James Madison’s public response to the Kemper raid arrived like a formal dispatch: correct, disapproving, and utterly detached from the reality on the ground. In a letter to the Spanish minister in Washington, dated October 1804 and preserved in State Department archives, Madison denounced the “criminal attempt” by “certain individuals of the United States” to violate Spanish territory. The language was diplomatic boilerplate. He expressed the “sincere regret” of the American government and assured Spain that U.S. laws would be enforced against the perpetrators. On paper, it was a repudiation of frontier lawlessness. In the Feliciana parishes, it was read as a necessary fiction.

Madison’s condemnation served a specific, two-level purpose. On the surface, it maintained plausible deniability. The United States could not be seen as sponsoring armed incursions into the territory of a nation with which it was, technically, at peace. Such a perception would have justified Spanish military retaliation and alienated European powers watching the young republic’s expansion. But beneath that surface, Madison’s administration was pursuing a policy that made raids like Kemper’s almost inevitable. The goal was not to stabilize Spanish rule, but to quietly undermine it while preparing for its eventual absorption.

The mechanism was demographic. While Madison’s State Department issued stern notes, his land offices in the Mississippi and Orleans territories were selling titles to settlers who then flowed eastward across the disputed border. No federal agent stopped them. No proclamation warned them they were entering foreign soil. The official U.S. position, articulated by Jefferson and continued by Madison, held that West Florida to the Perdido River was already American by virtue of the Louisiana Purchase. Settlers who believed they were moving onto U.S. land were, from the administration’s perspective, simply ahead of the diplomatic curve. Their presence created facts on the ground—farms, roads, slave labor camps—that any future annexation would simply ratify. Spanish protests about this immigration were met with polite reminders of the boundary dispute, a bureaucratic stalemate that favored the side with the growing population.

Madison’s personal papers, housed at the Library of Congress, reveal a more pragmatic view than his diplomatic correspondence suggests. In memos to his cabinet, he treated the “Florida question” as a matter of timing, not principle. The challenge was to allow the pressure of American settlement to weaken Spanish control to the breaking point, without triggering a war or a European intervention that could snatch the territory away. The Kemper raid was a flare, revealing both the intensity of that pressure and its potential to boil over into uncontrolled violence. Madison’s public denunciation was a bucket of cold water, meant to cool the immediate crisis. It did not mean he wanted the fire extinguished entirely.

This calculated duality shaped the federal response on the frontier. American officials in the Mississippi Territory, like Governor Robert Williams, issued those warrants for the Kempers’ arrest as Madison demanded. But the pursuit was perfunctory. Court records show no serious attempt to apprehend the brothers in the months following the raid. They remained in the territory, their presence a lingering symbol of Spanish impotence. The message to other settlers was clear: while the U.S. government would not officially sanction your violence, it would not seriously punish you for violence against Spanish authority either. The risk of retribution lay with Spain, not Washington.

The long-term strategy hinged on a bet that Spain couldn't recover. Madrid’s attention and resources were consumed by the Peninsular War against Napoleon, a struggle for its own survival. Troops and treasury were not being sent to reinforce Baton Rouge. As Madison noted in a cabinet discussion in 1805, Spain’s hold was “more nominal than real.” The plan, therefore, was to wait for the nominal to collapse under the weight of the real—the demographic and economic reality created by American settlers. When that collapse came, the United States would be positioned to step in, not as an invader, but as the restorer of order for its imperiled citizens. The Kemper raid was an early, messy symptom of the collapse in progress. Madison’s job was to manage the symptom, not cure the disease.

In this light, the raid of 1804 and the rebellion of 1810 were not opposite events in Washington’s view, but points on the same continuum. Kemper represented the chaotic, individual phase of the pressure. The convention at St. John’s Plains represented its organized, political culmination. The first forced Madison to issue a diplomatic apology. The second would allow him to issue a proclamation of annexation. Both served the same end: the transfer of West Florida from a dying empire to an expanding republic. The difference was that in 1810, the settlers would do the work with a precision Madison could publicly bless, or at least, could no longer need to condemn.

The failure of the Kemper raid, as documented in Spanish military reports and the later testimony of participants, provided a crude but effective blueprint for what not to do. It was a case study in how to alienate the very population whose support was essential. The planters of the Feliciana district—men like John Mills, William Barrow, and John Rhea himself—had watched the events of July 1804 not with admiration, but with alarm. Their primary concerns, as revealed in their land deeds and correspondence, were legal and economic stability. They wanted clear titles to their acres, enforceable contracts for their cotton, and open access to the Mississippi River for their exports. Reuben Kemper’s burning blockhouses promised none of that. They promised only retaliatory trade embargoes, military patrols, and a hardening of Spanish suspicion toward all American settlers.

The raid’s aftermath proved their fears correct. Spanish Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, though unable to mount a military expedition, tightened administrative controls. Customs inspections became more stringent, a nuisance that cost merchants time and money. More ominously, Spanish land grant approvals for American applicants slowed to a crawl, creating a backlog of insecure claims. The Kempers had struck at Spanish authority, but the blow reverberated against the economic interests of their neighbors. This backlash clarified a fundamental political reality for the district’s leading men. As John Rhea would later articulate in the convention minutes, a successful challenge to Spain required the consent, not just the compliance, of the propertied class. It needed the semblance of law, not the stench of smoke.

A shift in tactics began to coalesce in the years following the raid, visible in the changing nature of protest. Where Kemper had used an axe, men like Rhea used a petition. In 1805 and again in 1807, delegations of planters from the Feliciana parishes presented formal grievances to the Spanish governor at Baton Rouge, Carlos Dehault Delassus. These documents, preserved in Spanish colonial archives, were models of restrained protest. They complained of bureaucratic delays, unfair duties, and the lack of representative government. They appealed not to American sovereignty, but to the Spanish governor’s own authority, requesting he use his power to remedy abuses. The language was loyalist, but the implication was clear: if the existing system could not address their needs, the system itself might need to be replaced. It was a diplomatic probing, a test of Spanish flexibility conducted with quills instead of muskets.

The lessons were organizational as much as political. The Kemper band had been a temporary alliance bound by personal grievance and the hope of loot. It had no chain of command beyond familial loyalty, no method for making collective decisions, no plan for what followed a victory. Its collapse under internal bickering, as recounted by the hostage Bétizac, was inevitable. The men who would later form the West Florida rebellion observed this failure of structure. When they began their own secret meetings in 1810, the first order of business, according to the St. John’s Plains convention records, was to elect a permanent president and a secretary. They drafted rules of procedure. They kept meticulous minutes. They created committees for finance, for defense, for correspondence. They were building, from the first gavel fall, the skeleton of a state. This was the antithesis of Kemper’s posse. It was a conscious effort to transform volatile individual anger into a sustained, collective political will.

The final, and most critical, lesson was the need for a legitimate alternative. Kemper had offered his followers only the destruction of the old order. He had no vision for what came after the fires died, beyond a vague assumption that American authority would simply fill the void. The settlers of 1810 understood they could not rely on the United States to save them from the chaos they might unleash. They had to be prepared to govern themselves. This understanding led directly to the drafting of a constitution and the election of a president, Fulwar Skipwith, before the first shot was fired at Fort San Carlos. They secured the government buildings before they secured the fort. They designed a flag before they finalized their declaration. Every action was sequenced to present a fait accompli of sovereignty, not just to Spain, but to the watching world and to President Madison.

Reuben Kemper’s legacy, therefore, was not a path to follow, but a ditch to avoid. His raid highlighted every pitfall of frontier rebellion: the lack of broad support, the absence of political structure, the failure to provide a stable successor regime. The men who gathered at St. John’s Plains in the summer of 1810 had learned from his mistakes. They would channel the same borderland resentment, but they would do so through committees, not through a mob. They would target the capital, not the outposts. They would capture the governor, not take hostages. They would raise a flag, not a torch. The Kemper rebellion was the spark that showed the tinder was dry. The convention was the controlled burn meant to clear the land for a new harvest.

The difference was not merely one of scale, but of substance. The Kemper raid was an external assault, a strike from the American side of the border. The men of 1810 were an internal force; they lived on the land they sought to control. Their rebellion was not an invasion, but an insurrection—a distinction that carried profound legal and political weight. This shift from outsider violence to settler revolution transformed the enterprise from a criminal act into a sovereign claim.

The key figures of 1810 were not adventurers like Reuben Kemper, but stakeholders. John Rhea owned thousands of acres in the Feliciana district. His neighbor, William Barrow, held hundreds more, worked by scores of enslaved laborers. Philemon Thomas was a substantial planter. Their wealth was tied directly to the soil of West Florida. When they challenged Spanish rule, they were not risking property elsewhere; they were defending the property beneath their feet. This gave their movement a stability and a patience the Kempers never possessed. They could not afford to burn the blockhouses and flee. They had to seize the government and make it work.

Their method was constitutional from the outset. The secret convention that gathered at St. John’s Plains on July 15, 1810, did not debate tactics for storming Fort San Carlos. Its first act, as recorded in the official minutes, was to elect Rhea as its presiding officer and to appoint a secretary. They established rules of order. They formed committees for finance, defense, and correspondence. They were building, from the first gavel fall, the administrative machinery of a state. This procedural rigor was a direct repudiation of the Kemper model. Where the brothers had operated by shouted consensus, the convention operated by motion and vote. Where the raid had been secretive by necessity, the convention’s secrecy was a political strategy, allowing it to organize beneath the governor’s notice.

The objective was not plunder, but political transformation. The convention’s initial public gambit, in August 1810, was not a raid but a resolution. It presented Governor Delassus with a formal declaration that, due to Spain’s “weak and distracted state” and his own lack of troops, the people had resolved to assist him in governing. It was a brilliantly audacious document—a polite coup d’état disguised as an offer of aid. Delassus, recognizing his impotence, feigned acceptance. This allowed the convention to operate with increasing openness, drafting militia rolls and collecting taxes, all under the thin veneer of Spanish approval. They were practicing sovereignty before declaring it.

When the break finally came, it was framed not as a personal grievance, but as a collective, principled act. The Declaration of Independence, proclaimed by John Rhea on September 26, 1810, was a legalistic document. It cited the “usurpations” and “tyranny” of the Spanish regime, language borrowed directly from the American precedent. It listed specific grievances: the denial of representative government, the imposition of unfair duties, the failure to provide security. It announced the people’s right to “institute a new government.” The contrast with Kemper’s motive—the seizure of his flatboat—could not be more absolute. One was a private loss avenged by force. The other was a public compact enacted by proclamation.

The military action that followed was equally deliberate. Philemon Thomas’s assault on Fort San Carlos on September 23 was not a spontaneous attack. It was a planned operation, timed to coincide with the political declaration. Thomas dispatched riders to gather men from outlying districts, ensuring a show of force. He ordered a contingent to infiltrate the fort before dawn, minimizing bloodshed. The goal was capture, not destruction. They took Governor Delassus prisoner, but treated him with a formality befitting his office. They seized the fort’s military stores intact. The fort itself was not burned; it was occupied. It became the seat of the new republic’s government.

When Philemon Thomas gave the order to advance on the morning of September 23, 1810, he was leading a different kind of army toward a different kind of objective. The target was the same symbol of Spanish power Kemper had once attacked. The result would not be a warrant, but a republic. The rebellion of 1804 had asked a question in the crude grammar of an axe. The rebellion of 1810 would answer it in the precise syntax of a constitution.
