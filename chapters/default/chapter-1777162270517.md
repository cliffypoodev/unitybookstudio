The infirmary at MGM was not a doctor’s office. It was a logistics center.

The medical suite, tucked away on the first floor of the Thalberg Building, functioned as a pharmacy that serviced a single, captive clientele. According to employee records and architectural plans from the period, it contained a standard waiting area, two examination rooms, and a private office for the chief physician. What distinguished it from any private practice was the adjoining dispensary. This was a small, windowless room with floor-to-ceiling shelves stocked not with medical texts but with bulk bottles of pharmaceuticals. A locked steel cabinet held the controlled substances. The room had a compounding counter, a sink, and a small refrigerator for injectables. It was, in every operational sense, a retail pharmacy. The primary difference was that its customers never received a bill, and their prescriptions were rarely written in their own names.

The system ran on paperwork designed for efficiency, not accountability. When a star or contract player required medication, the process was initiated by a call from the producer’s office or the unit manager to the medical department. A nurse or an assistant would then deliver the medication directly to the set or the star’s dressing room. The transaction was logged internally, but the paper trail was engineered to be opaque. Studio archives contain thousands of “House Requisition” forms from the 1930s and 40s. These were multi-part carbon slips used for ordering everything from lumber and paint to costumes and props. The medical department used the same forms.

A typical requisition, dated July 12, 1939, and pulled from the files for The Wizard of Oz, lists under “Description of Material”: “Benzedrine Sulfate, 100 tablets.” The “Charge to” field does not contain a patient’s name. It reads “Production #1060” – the studio’s internal accounting code for the film. The “Authorized by” signature is that of a production manager, not a physician. The “Received by” line is signed by a nurse. These forms were the financial backbone of the operation. They allowed the studio to purchase vast quantities of scheduled drugs wholesale, charge them directly to a film’s budget as a production expense, and distribute them without ever creating an individual patient prescription record. The drugs became a line item, like film stock or electrical cable.

The scale of these orders reveals the systemic, not incidental, nature of the practice. Pharmacy invoices preserved in the MGM archives show bulk purchases that defy any explanation of isolated therapeutic need. An invoice from the Sharp & Dohme pharmaceutical supplier to “Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Studios, Medical Department” dated January 15, 1940, lists: “5000 tablets, Benzedrine Sulfate 10mg” and “3000 capsules, Phenobarbital 100mg.” The order totals several hundred dollars—a significant sum at the time—and is approved for payment by the studio comptroller. This was not medicine. This was inventory management.

The medical department, therefore, served a dual function. To the individual actor, it presented the face of concerned, paternalistic care. To the studio accounting department, it was a cost center for pharmaceutical supplies, its budget justified by the smooth functioning of the production schedule. The chief physician, whether Dr. Edward Hummel at MGM or Dr. Lee Siegel at Fox, occupied a uniquely compromised position. He reported through the studio hierarchy, ultimately to the head of production or the studio chief himself. His performance was measured not by patient outcomes but by the availability of the talent. A memo from a Twentieth Century Fox vice president to Dr. Siegel in 1946, cited in later court proceedings, framed the priority with chilling clarity: “Miss Monroe is required on Stage 4 at 8 a.m. tomorrow for color tests. See that she is rested and alert. You have discretion on method.”

This direct executive oversight severed the standard chain of medical ethics. The doctor’s primary obligation was not to the person in the examination room, but to the corporate entity that signed his paycheck and could revoke his privileged access to the Hollywood elite. The system created a powerful incentive structure. Compliance was rewarded with a high salary, social status, and the implicit protection of the studio. Questioning the ethics of the requisition system would have been tantamount to questioning the production schedule itself—a career-ending move.

The “house” prescription was the legal fiction that greased the wheels. By ordering drugs against a production number rather than a patient’s name, the studio created a buffer of plausible deniability. If questioned, they could claim the drugs were general stock for the infirmary, available for any employee who might need them under a proper diagnosis. The reality, documented in call sheets and memo traffic, was that this general stock was deployed with surgical precision to specific individuals according to the demands of the shooting calendar. The system was designed to be just inside the gray area of contemporary law. Federal narcotics regulations of the era focused on opium derivatives like morphine and heroin. Amphetamines and barbiturates, while prescription-only, existed in a regulatory twilight zone. The Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970, which would establish the strict schedules modern pharmacies follow, was decades away.

Thus, the studio infirmary became a closed loop. It diagnosed the problem—fatigue, insomnia, anxiety—that was created by the studio’s own demands. It prescribed the solution from its own inventory. It delivered the medication through its own employees. And it billed the cost back to the very production that had created the need. The actor was merely the conduit through which the pharmaceuticals flowed, a biological vessel for the chemical fuel that kept the corporate machine running. The dependency was not a medical condition in this context. It was a logistical outcome, as predictable and managed as the delivery of raw film stock to the developing lab.

The paper trail leads out of the dispensary, down the studio corridors, and directly into the executive suites. The requisitions were approved by managers. The invoices were paid by the accounting department. The bulk orders were authorized by senior administrators. The system could not have functioned for a single day without the knowledge and consent of the men who ran the studio’s daily operations. This was not the work of a rogue physician. It was corporate policy, implemented through standard operating procedure and buried in the mundane clutter of administrative paperwork. The drugs did not appear by magic. They were ordered, received, stocked, and distributed by a bureaucracy that reported, in the end, to Louis B. Mayer.

The supply chain began not in Hollywood, but in the manufacturing plants of major pharmaceutical firms. Companies like Smith, Kline & French, maker of Benzedrine, and Abbott Laboratories, which produced Nembutal, maintained robust sales divisions that serviced institutional clients. Studio medical departments, with their consistent, high-volume orders, were prized accounts. Correspondence between MGM’s purchasing agent and the Philadelphia-based Sharp & Dohme company, from 1938, reveals a relationship that was both cordial and routine. The studio was not a clandestine buyer; it was a legitimate commercial customer, paying with corporate checks drawn on the Bank of America.

The ordering process followed standard corporate procurement channels. A physician or the head nurse would determine the need based on current inventory and projected demand from upcoming productions. He would then generate a purchase requisition. This document would travel to the studio’s central purchasing department, where a clerk would convert it into an official purchase order mailed to the supplier. The drugs were shipped directly to the studio gate, received by the medical department, and the invoice was routed to accounts payable. The entire transaction was recorded in the studio’s general ledger under an account such as “Medical Supplies” or “Infirmary Expenses.” It was, on paper, indistinguishable from an order for light bulbs or office stationery.

The quantities, however, were the tell. A single purchase order from Twentieth Century-Fox to Abbott Laboratories, dated March 10, 1954, requested “1,000 tablets, Nembutal Sodium 100mg” and “500 tablets, Dexedrine Sulfate 10mg.” This was not an annual order. Studio archives show similar orders placed quarterly, sometimes monthly, during periods of heavy production. The cumulative volume over a single year could reach into the tens of thousands of doses for a major studio. When you mapped the orders from multiple suppliers—Sharp & Dohme for barbiturates, Smith, Kline & French for amphetamines, Wyeth for Seconal—the scale of chemical acquisition became clear. The studio infirmary was stocking a small hospital, but one with a patient roster comprised almost exclusively of performers under contract.

Payment records remove any lingering doubt about institutional knowledge. The invoices were not paid from a slush fund or a doctor’s private account. They were settled by the studio’s centralized accounting office, often after being audited by a junior clerk who verified the shipment against the purchase order. A paper trail exists in the form of canceled checks and ledger entries. A check stub from MGM, dated November 22, 1941, shows a payment of $487.50 to Smith, Kline & French. The ledger line for that check reads simply “Medical Dept. Supplies.” The amount corresponds almost exactly to the cost of 5,000 Benzedrine tablets at the wholesale price of the time. The transaction was routine, unremarkable, and buried in a stack of similar payments for plumbing repairs and set construction.

This financial normalization was the system’s greatest protection. Because the orders were large but not extraordinary in the world of industrial procurement, they raised no red flags with the suppliers. The pharmaceutical salesmen had quotas to meet. A studio medical department that ordered five thousand pills at a time was a reliable partner, not a subject for ethical scrutiny. The drugs were, after all, legal products with accepted therapeutic uses. The sales correspondence, where it survives, is filled with discussions of pricing discounts and delivery schedules, never questions about patient diagnoses or consumption rates.

The lack of external oversight was nearly total. State pharmacy boards in the 1930s and 40s were underfunded and focused on retail pharmacies, not private corporate facilities. Federal Food and Drug Administration authority over amphetamines and barbiturates was limited to ensuring the drugs were not adulterated or misbranded; tracking their distribution after they left the factory was beyond its mandate. The Bureau of Narcotics, predecessor to the DEA, concerned itself almost solely with opiates and cocaine. The studio dispensary operated in a regulatory vacuum. No inspector ever audited its shelves to reconcile its massive inventory against patient records, because no law required such records to be kept for the “house” stock.

You can see the logical end point of this unfettered supply in the records of a different kind of Hollywood business: the studio-affiliated pharmacy. These were independent retail pharmacies, often located just outside the studio gates, that functioned as overflow valves for the system. The most documented was the Tower Pharmacy on Sunset Boulevard, a favorite of Warner Bros. and Paramount players. Its owner maintained a direct line to several studio medical departments. When the infirmary’s own stock ran low, or when a star needed a prescription filled after hours, a doctor would call the Tower.

The pharmacy’s own supplier invoices, entered as evidence in a later civil suit, show a pattern mirroring the studios’. It purchased barbiturates and amphetamines in lots of thousands of tablets from the same manufacturers. It then dispensed them, filling prescriptions written by a handful of studio doctors. The financial link was direct. The studios often paid the pharmacy bills directly, charging them back to a film’s production budget or to the actor’s account as an “advance.” The Tower Pharmacy was, in effect, a satellite dispensary, extending the studio’s supply chain beyond its physical walls while maintaining the same financial control.

The sheer, repetitive volume of the orders is the conclusive data point. Isolated therapeutic need is random, episodic. It does not generate a consistent demand for thousands of doses of stimulants and depressants every few months. That pattern only emerges from a system of scheduled, prophylactic administration. The chemicals were not being used to treat occasional illness. They were being consumed as a matter of daily routine by a rotating population of performers, their intake synchronized to the call sheet. The supply chain was a mirror image of the production schedule. The manufacturing output of pharmaceutical plants in Philadelphia and Chicago was calibrated, through purchase orders and invoices, to keep the stars of Culver City and Burbank in a state of chemically-maintained readiness.

The paper trail forms a perfect, damning circle. It begins with a bulk order from a manufacturer. It flows through the studio’s accounting ledgers as a line-item expense. It terminates in the body of an actor on a soundstage. And the financial mechanism that completed the circle—the payment of the invoice—provided the studio with a legal receipt for its most essential raw material: the compliant, available performer. The drugs were not a secret vice. They were a budgeted commodity, their acquisition as essential to the product as the screenplay and the camera.

The physician’s authority was the final, essential seal on the system. A bulk invoice was just paper. A requisition form was just a chit. It was the doctor’s signature—or his verbal order—that transformed a commodity into a treatment, that laundered a corporate demand through the white coat of medical legitimacy. Two men exemplified this corrupted role: Dr. Edward J. Hummel of MGM and Dr. Lee Siegel of Twentieth Century-Fox.

Dr. Edward Hummel was not a sinister figure in the archives. Employee records depict a competent, career studio employee. He was a graduate of a reputable medical school, licensed in California, and had served as a military physician before joining MGM in the early 1930s. His personnel file notes his “reliability” and “discretion.” His office in the Thalberg Building was a place of quiet efficiency. It was here that a teenage Judy Garland was brought, not for a childhood illness, but for what the studio termed “weight management” and “schedule adjustment.”

The protocol, as reconstructed from Garland’s own later accounts to biographers and the memoirs of her colleagues, was systematic. To suppress her appetite and maintain energy through the sixteen-hour days of filming The Wizard of Oz, she was given Benzedrine tablets. The pills were provided by a nurse, on Dr. Hummel’s orders, often with a glass of milk. At night, to counteract the stimulants and force sleep so she could wake for the next pre-dawn call, she was given Phenobarbital. This was not a short-term intervention. It became a sustained regimen, continuing through her subsequent MGM musicals. The effect, as she would later describe it, was to permanently disrupt her natural sleep-wake cycle and her relationship with food. She was, in essence, placed on a chemical assembly line. Hummel did not devise this brutal schedule. He merely provided the pharmaceutical tools to enforce it.

His complicity was passive, administrative. He signed the requisitions for the bulk bottles. He authorized the nurses to dispense. He likely seldom injected a star himself. His role was to maintain the medical department’s output, to ensure the machine had the pharmaceutical lubricant it required. A telling detail emerges from the testimony of Mickey Rooney. In his memoir Life Is Too Short, Rooney wrote of the MGM years: “They’d give you pep pills to keep you up, then they’d give you sleeping pills to put you down. The doctors were right there, handing them out like candy.” He did not mention Hummel by name—the doctor was simply part of the institutional “they.” That anonymity was the point. Hummel was a functionary, his medical judgment subsumed by the production schedule.

Dr. Lee Siegel of Twentieth Century-Fox operated in a later, more complex era, but his function was identical. By the 1950s, federal scrutiny of amphetamines was increasing, and the star system was evolving. Siegel’s patient roster included the studio’s most volatile asset, Marilyn Monroe. The paper trail here is colder, more clinical. Fox production memos from the late 1950s, cited in court records during the studio’s later legal battles over Monroe’s contract, repeatedly direct concerns about the star’s “readiness” or “punctuality” to Dr. Siegel. His mandate was not diagnosis and cure. It was maintenance and availability.

Coroner’s reports and pharmacy records from the months before Monroe’s death in August 1962 paint a picture of Siegel’s method. He prescribed barbiturates—Nembutal and Seconal—for her insomnia. He prescribed amphetamines for energy. These were not singular prescriptions. They were overlapping, renewed frequently, often provided in large quantities. Crucially, Siegel was not alone in prescribing for her; he was part of a constellation of physicians, each unaware of or ignoring the others’ prescriptions. But his scripts carried the implicit authority of the studio. He was, in the words of one Fox executive’s deposition, “our man for keeping her on track.”

The dependency this created was not an accidental side effect. It was a calculated form of control. An actor juggling Benzedrine-induced days and barbiturate-crashed nights became physically and psychologically reliant on the very system that supplied the chemicals. Missing a dose meant incapacitating fatigue or crippling insomnia. The source of the remedy was the studio infirmary or its designated pharmacy. To leave the studio, to rebel against the schedule, was to risk withdrawal. The dependency bound the actor to the studio with chains stronger than any contract clause.

The doctors rationalized their roles. In the rare instances where their perspectives were recorded, they used the language of necessity and service. They were helping talented individuals cope with extraordinary pressures. They were preventing breakdowns, not causing them. This self-justification was underpinned by the total absence of consequences. No medical board ever censured Hummel or Siegel for their studio work. No court found them liable. They retired with pensions and reputations intact, their actions shielded by the corporate entity that had authorized them.

Their moral failure was one of inversion. They did not ask, “What does my patient need to be healthy?” They asked, “What does my employer need this person to do today?” The answer was then translated into a chemical formula. The physician’s sacred duty—to act as the patient’s advocate—was transferred to the studio. The patient became the problem to be managed; the drug became the solution that served the corporation.

You can see the ultimate expression of this inverted duty in a memo from a Fox production manager to Siegel, dated July 1961. It concerns Monroe, who was frequently late to the set of Something’s Got to Give. The memo states: “If she cannot report on time under her own power, you are authorized to ensure she is delivered in a functional condition. Use whatever protocol you deem necessary. The cost is not an object.” The “protocol” was a euphemism. The “functional condition” was the deliverable. Dr. Siegel was not being asked to heal a woman. He was being ordered to deliver a product to a specific location at a specific time, and to use his medical license to do it.

The cycle was self-perpetuating. The drugs allowed the studio to demand the impossible schedule. The impossible schedule created the physical and emotional distress that required more drugs. The doctor stood in the middle, writing the scripts, signing the orders, and collecting his salary. He was the licensed mechanic for the human machinery, his black bag stocked with the corporate-approved tools for adjustment and repair. The actors, from the child stars of the 1930s to the icons of the 1960s, passed through his care. They left with their performances captured on film, and their addictions cemented by prescription. The doctor closed his file, ordered more inventory, and waited for the next call from the production office.

But the studio infirmary, for all its bureaucratic efficiency, had limitations. Its walls were physical. Its records, however opaque, existed. Its doctors were employees, their actions theoretically bound by corporate oversight and medical ethics. For an actor who needed a boost away from the lot, or for an executive who required a more potent, discreet, and immediate form of chemical management, a different channel emerged. This parallel network operated in private offices, hotel suites, and dressing rooms. It was personified by men like Dr. Max Jacobson, who would become infamous as “Dr. Feelgood.” His method bypassed the pill bottle entirely. He used a needle.

Jacobson’s practice, centered in New York but extending to Hollywood, represented the pharmaceutical supply chain stripped of its corporate paperwork and distilled to its essence: direct intravenous injection. He catered to an elite clientele of politicians, writers, and film stars. His trademark was the “vitamin shot,” a murky concoction he mixed himself from ampoules containing, as later analyses would reveal, liquid amphetamine, steroids, vitamins, and often ground-up placental tissue. The effect was not a gradual lift from a tablet. It was a sudden, electrifying surge. Patients reported feeling invincible, brilliantly alert, freed from fatigue. The crash, when it came hours later, was correspondingly profound. And it ensured the patient would return, often the next day, for another injection.

The genius of Jacobson’s operation was its framing. He did not prescribe uppers or downers. He administered “vitamin therapies” and “metabolic boosters.” The language was that of wellness and rejuvenation, cutting-edge science, not addiction. A 1957 feature in American Weekly magazine, titled “The Miracle Worker,” portrayed him as a medical visionary, his waiting room filled with famous faces seeking his revitalizing treatments. This public relations veil was impenetrable to casual scrutiny. Who would question a doctor giving vitamins?

The historical record of his Hollywood clients is built from fragments—diary entries, the memoirs of associates, and later testimony before the New York State Board of Regents, which would finally revoke his license in 1975. Eddie Fisher, in his autobiography, described being introduced to Jacobson’s shots by his then-wife Elizabeth Taylor during the stressful filming of Butterfield 8 in 1960. Fisher became a devoted patient, relying on the injections to maintain a brutal schedule of nightclub performances. “I felt like I could conquer the world,” he wrote. “Then I’d collapse.” The pattern was identical to the studio cycle, but accelerated and intensified by the intravenous delivery.

Mickey Rooney, in his later years, gave interviews alluding to “a doctor in New York who gave you a shot that made you feel like a million bucks for a while.” He never named Jacobson on the record, but the chronology and description align. The most credible link to the core studio system comes through the director Otto Preminger. Known for his tyrannical, marathon shooting sessions, Preminger was a documented Jacobson patient. He would reportedly have the doctor flown to location shoots to administer to him and, according to the accounts of several crew members, to key actors who were flagging. Here, the clandestine network intersected with the formal production machine. A star struggling on a Preminger set might not be sent to the studio doctor. He might be introduced to the director’s own private physician, who would deliver a “pick-me-up” on the spot, no prescription, no record.

Jacobson’s appeal to the Hollywood elite was multifaceted. The treatment was fast—minutes versus the hour it might take for a pill to metabolize. It was discreet, leaving no bottle of pills to be found by a maid or a columnist. It carried a perverse prestige, a mark of being connected enough to know about this secret, powerful remedy. And it was utterly deniable. “I was just getting my vitamin B shot” was a plausible, even health-conscious, explanation.

The regulatory blindness that protected the studio infirmary also protected Jacobson for decades. Because he compounded the injections himself, they were not a branded pharmaceutical product subject to federal tracking. The amphetamine he used was a legal substance. His crime, in the eyes of the law for most of his career, was not possession or distribution, but the eventual charge of fraud—for misrepresenting the contents of his injections—and gross malpractice. By the time authorities moved against him, his practice had operated for over thirty years.

The “vitamin shot” phenomenon was not confined to one man. It spawned imitators and variations. In Los Angeles, a subculture of “script doctors” and “on-set physicians” emerged, often retired or marginally licensed, who made house calls to the homes of stars. They carried black bags stocked with syringes and ampoules. They charged exorbitant fees, paid in cash. They asked no questions. Their relationship to the studios was often symbiotic rather than direct. A production manager, facing a star who was non-functional, might quietly suggest the name of “a guy who can help.” The studio maintained deniability. The independent physician assumed the legal risk. The actor got the shot.

This shadow network fulfilled a specific, ruthless demand. The studio system created the need for chemical performance enhancement. The official medical department met that need within the studio gates, creating a dependency. But when that dependency spiraled, or when the actor was working independently, the more flexible, secretive, and potent services of a Max Jacobson were required. He was the emergency valve for a pressure the system itself generated. His injections were simply a more concentrated, efficient form of the same fuel the studios had been dispensing for years. The method was different—a needle instead of a pill, a hotel room instead of an infirmary. The transaction was different—cash instead of a budget line item. But the end result was identical: a human being, chemically altered to meet a professional demand, with the source of the alteration standing by, ready to administer the next dose.

The paper trail here is faint, written in appointment books and canceled checks from personal accounts. But its existence is confirmed by the cumulative weight of anecdotal evidence and the eventual, belated crackdown by medical authorities. The testimony that finally ended Jacobson’s career came from former patients describing rampant addiction and psychological collapse. Their stories were the human fallout of a supply chain that had escaped the warehouse and gone mobile, delivering its product directly into the vein.

The outcome of this supply chain was not measured in ledger entries or inventory reports. It was measured in human wreckage. The chemical management that began as a tool for schedule adherence metastasized, over years, into full-blown, life-consuming addiction. For every star who survived the regimen with their health intact, a dozen found themselves trapped. The dependency fostered by the studio doctors became a pre-existing condition that followed them off the lot, often into the care of men like Max Jacobson. The official and shadow networks converged in the bodies of the performers, with catastrophic results.

The question that hangs over this history is not one of intent, but of accountability. The supply chain was visible, documented, and financially transparent within the studio walls. Yet for over three decades, it operated without meaningful challenge. The doctors rationalized. The executives authorized. The suppliers profited. And the actors, the final links in the chain, consumed the product, their performances brilliant and their lives increasingly unmanageable. They were both the workers and the raw material, their vitality extracted and replaced with chemicals, until the balance tipped, and the system discarded them. The evidence lays out a closed loop of cause and effect, from the manufacturer’s loading dock to the soundstage to the tragic, private finale. Every component had a plausible justification. Together, they formed a machine that could build a star and break a human being with the same efficient, bureaucratic calm.
