Father Aloysius was halfway through a nose-flute rendition of “Dies Irae” when Mrs. O'Leary's dentures shot out of the confessional booth like a porcelain mortar round. The ancient oak door rattled on its hinges. A faint scream Dopplered upward through the church rafters, punctuated by the distant thud of orthopedic shoes breaching the stratosphere.

The dentures landed in the offering plate with a sanctimonious clink.

“That's the third one this week,” muttered Sister Philomena, scooping the prosthetic teeth into her apron pocket like communion wafers. Her glare could have sterilized holy water. “You can't keep absolving people into orbit, Father.”

“It's not me!” Aloysius wiped flute condensation on his stole. The confessional loomed behind him—a hulking ironclad relic from Arizona's bandit-priest era, its velvet curtains now singed around the edges. “The penitent enters, says three Hail Marys, and—” He mimed an explosion with trembling hands. One of his chasuble buttons popped off and rolled into a crack in the flagstones. The crack, he couldn't help noticing, looked like Pope Pius IX doing the Macarena.

The church smelled of frankincense and whatever propellant the Vatican wasn't listing in its bulletproofing specs. Aloysius knelt to retrieve the button. His knees sounded like a bag of pretzels in a tumble dryer. “Perhaps it's a divine trial. Like Job, but with…ballistics.”

Sister Philomena thumped down a ledger marked “MISSING (CELESTIAL).” Pages fluttered open to reveal names, dates, and the increasingly frantic marginalia of a nun at her wit's end. “Florita Gonzalez—vanished mid-mea culpa about stealing her neighbor's zucchini. Warren Peabody—poofed during contrition for illegally trimming a saguaro.” She jabbed a finger at the latest entry. “Now Betty O'Leary's dentures are in my pocket and her femur is probably orbiting Uranus.”

A muffled clang came from the confessional. The iron walls vibrated at a frequency known to shatter stained glass. Aloysius crossed himself with the hand not clutching the nose flute. “Maybe we're misinterpreting the signs.”

“The archdiocese forwarded another complaint.” Sister Philomena slapped a fax onto the altar. The paper curled at the edges like a damned soul in limbo. “Bishop wants to know why we've got more parishioners in the Kármán line than in the pews.”

Aloysius adjusted his glasses. The fax heading read Project Gabriel: Final Notice. He caught phrases like “unauthorized teleportation” and “stop blessing people into the exosphere.” At the bottom, someone had stamped NOLI TANGERE BOOTH in red ink that looked suspiciously like barbecue sauce.

The confessional shuddered again. A thin wisp of smoke escaped the grate.

“You know what they're saying at the diner?” Sister Philomena lowered her voice. “That this is God's version of recycling.” She pointed to a sticky note on the ledger: All “ascended” confessed to (1) overwatering lawns (2) using plastic straws (3) owning gas-guzzlers.

Aloysius felt his left eyelid start to twitch in Morse code. He'd spent twenty years hearing confessions about stolen garden gnomes and impure thoughts about cable knit sweaters. Now suddenly his booth was playing eco-cop with celestial deportation?

A loud bang echoed from the confessional. The iron crucifix welded to its door swung upside down.

“That's it,” hissed Sister Philomena. “I'm calling Rome.”

“Wait!” Aloysius grabbed her arm. A high-pitched whine built inside the booth, like a choir of angels tuning kazoos. “What if this is my fault? Last month I may have, ah, repurposed some holy water coolant for the air conditioning…”

The whine escalated to a frequency known to liquefy candle wax. Sister Philomena's wimple flapped like a seagull in a tornado. Aloysius barely had time to shout “Brace for—” before the confessional exploded in a pillar of blue flame.

When the smoke cleared, the booth stood intact. Sister Philomena's glasses were on sideways. Aloysius's nose flute had fused with a rosary into what appeared to be a liturgical didgeridoo.

And taped to the scorched velvet curtain was a neon Post-it note:

NEXT TIME RECYCLE YOUR OWN DAMN PLASTIC ~GABRIEL

Aloysius stared at the note. Then at the dentures in Sister Philomena's pocket. Then at the faint contrail still visible through the stained glass window depicting Saint Francis of Assisi releasing a cluster of weather balloons.

“Oh,” he said, very quietly. “It's a metaphor.”

The confessional made a noise like a thousand soda cans crushing in unison. Somewhere overhead, a satellite made entirely of guilty horticulturalists drifted past the moon.

Father Aloysius dove for the confessional door just as Hank McCready’s Stetson blew past him like a tumbleweed possessed by the Holy Spirit. The rancher’s knees hit the kneeler inside with a hollow thud. He spoke, the sound through the grate, thick with repentance and chewing tobacco.

“Bless me, Father, for I have sinned—”

“Not now, Hank!” Aloysius wedged his liturgical didgeridoo between the door handles. The metal groaned. A high-pitched whine built inside the booth, the same sound a microwave makes when you accidentally put cutlery in it.

Hank's confession barreled on. “—let my cattle graze on protected BLM land, and also I might’ve—”

The confessional shuddered. A panel flipped open near the ceiling, revealing what looked suspiciously like a NASA launch countdown display. Numbers ticked backward from 30.

Sister Philomena appeared at Aloysius's elbow, her wimple now smoldering at one edge. She thrust a fire extinguisher at him. “Try blessing it with this.”

The extinguisher had a Post-it stuck to the side: For liturgical emergencies only. Do not shake. Aloysius gave it an experimental pump. A stream of greenish foam arced into the confessional, immediately bursting into tiny flames that spelled out RECYCLE ME in midair before winking out.

“Twenty seconds!” Hank hollered, as if he wasn’t the one strapped to the theological equivalent of a SpaceX launchpad.

Aloysius kicked the booth. His sandal left a charred footprint. “Hank, listen carefully. When I say ‘go,’ you need to recite the Act of Contrition backward while hopping on one foot.”

“Father, with all due respect, that sounds like—”

“Fifteen seconds,” Sister Philomena interjected, consulting her watch. “Also, the helicopter’s here.”

A shadow passed over the stained-glass window depicting the martyrdom of Saint Lawrence. One image of the saint being grilled alive now had a papal helicopter superimposed over it, rotors kicking up a whirlwind of collection-plate envelopes.

Aloysius grabbed the confessional’s smoking grate. “Hank do you have any non-biodegradable sins? Anything involving single-use plastics?”

“Uh.” The rancher’s voice turned thoughtful. “Does beef jerky count?”

The countdown hit 10. The booth’s interior lights shifted to an accusing shade of green, the exact color of a Prius owner’s smug smile.

Sister Philomena slapped a hand against the confessional wall. “Think, Hank! Did you ever use those little red Solo cups at a tailgate?”

“Nine! Eight!” The booth chimed in, its voice suspiciously cheerful for something about to commit a felony against the atmosphere.

Hank’s boots scrabbled against the floor. “Well, there was this one time—”

“Not the time for a full inventory!” Aloysius jammed the didgeridoo deeper into the mechanism. The rosary beads fused to it started glowing like reactor rods.

Overhead, the helicopter’s PA system crackled to life. A voice boomed in ecclesial Latin, which roughly translated to, Unidentified clergy, you are in violation of Vatican airspace ordinances.

“Five! Four!”

Aloysius made a decision. He threw open his stole like a matador’s cape and bellowed, “Hank McCready, by the power vested in me by the state of Arizona and/or God, I hereby commute your penance to—to community service! Forty hours picking up litter in the national forest!”

The countdown paused at 2. The booth made a noise like a soda can being crushed against a friar’s forehead.

Hank’s muffled voice came through the grate. “That seems light for three hundred head of cattle on federal—”

“Don’t look a gift absolution in the mouth!” Sister Philomena kicked the booth for emphasis. Her orthopedic shoe left a dent in the shape of the Vatican seal.

The confessional emitted a long, disappointed wheeze. The countdown screen flickered and switched to a pixelated frowny face. With a sound like a thousand aluminum cans being sorted in Purgatory, the booth spat Hank back out. He landed in a heap at Aloysius’s feet, his Stetson miraculously untouched.

The helicopter’s searchlight pinned them like a particularly judgmental spotlight. A rope ladder unfurled, nearly braining Sister Philomena. At the top, a cardinal’s scarlet zucchetto bobbed into view.

Aloysius bent to help Hank up. The rancher’s hands were shaking, his face the color of communion wine. “Father,” he whispered, “I swear I’ll never let my cows near a riparian zone again.”

A voice from above boomed, “Father Aloysius Pfefferbaum?”

Sister Philomena straightened her smoldering wimple. “Too late. They used your full name.”

The cardinal descended like an angry scarlet parakeet. His vestments flapped in the rotor wash, revealing a surprising amount of calf muscle for a man of the cloth. Aloysius had approximately four seconds to decide between dignity and survival.

He chose neither.

“Your Eminence!” Aloysius spread his arms in what he hoped looked like beatific welcome and not like a man about to be tackled by Swiss Guards. “Welcome to St. Jude’s! Would you like to hear about our recycling program?”

The cardinal’s shoe touched down on the scorched flagstones. His gaze swept from the dentures in Sister Philomena’s pocket to the still-smoking confessional to the rancher currently trying to hide behind a potted palm.

Then the helicopter’s landing skid caught the edge of the confessional roof.

Metal screamed. The booth lurched sideways, its foundation crumbling like a sinner’s resolve during Lent. For one glorious moment, the entire structure hung suspended—iron walls groaning, velvet curtains flapping like the wings of a very confused angel—before the helicopter’s torque wrenched it free of the floor.

A geyser of dirt and what appeared to be very old bandit bones erupted from the hole where the confessional had stood. The cardinal, now dangling from his own rope ladder, watched in horror as the booth swung pendulum-like beneath the helicopter, its doors flapping open to reveal a chute labeled CELESTIAL RECYCLING: SINS ONLY. NO COMMINGLING.

Aloysius peered into the newly revealed pit. A network of tunnels spiderwebbed outward, their walls lined with what looked like Prohibition-era sacramental wine casks and several startled gopher snakes. A single neon sign flickered in the depths: LASCIATE OGNE BIODEGRADABILITÀ, VOI CH’ENTRATE.

The helicopter banked sharply. The confessional broke free, tumbling end over end until it lodged itself in the rose window depicting the Annunciation. The Virgin Mary now appeared to be receiving the Holy Spirit via a high-velocity eco-pod.

Silence fell, broken only by the distant sound of Hank McCready hyperventilating into a hymnal.

The cardinal dusted off his sleeves. He looked at Aloysius. He looked at the pit. He looked at the Virgin Mary’s new accessory.

Then he said the six words no priest ever wants to hear from his superiors:

“Explain this in one paragraph.”

Mara didn’t look up. Her hands were wrist-deep in a drift of polystyrene foam, the kind that broke apart into a million impossible-to-catch beads. She worked them into a burlap sack, her movements methodical. The sack was already half-full of water bottles, tangled fishing line, and the brittle carcasses of single-use razors.

“Another one.”

Jax dropped a milky-white detergent jug beside her. It landed with a hollow thud. “Label’s from three countries over. Currents don’t care about borders.”

“They never did.”

She didn’t ask where he’d found it. The answer was everywhere. The high-tide line wasn’t marked by seaweed anymore, but by a slumped ridge of refuse. It stretched in both directions until the haze swallowed it.

Jax crouched, picking at a knot of nylon netting embedded in the sand. His fingers were nicked and calloused, older than his nineteen years. “Council meeting tonight.”

“I know.”

“They’re going to vote on the dredge proposal again.”

Mara finally glanced at him. “And?”

“And nothing. It’ll pass. Like last time. And the time before.” He yanked the net free; a crab skeleton disintegrated in the mesh. “They talk about containment. We’re past containment.”

“What’s your solution, then? Burn it? Sink it? Wish it away?”

“I don’t have one.” He stood, brushing sand from his knees. “That’s the problem. Nobody does. We just… manage the symptom.”

He walked down toward the waterline, where the waves left a lace of foam that looked, for a second, clean.

The council chamber was a converted boathouse. Salt-cured timber beams arched overhead, smelling of old tar and mildew. Maps of coastal currents, annotated with red markers, covered one wall. The red was winning.

Mara took a seat on a back bench, the wood smooth from generations of friction. The room was half-full—mostly elders, a few younger faces like Jax’s, tight with a frustration they hadn’t learned to hide yet.

Councilor Vance stood at the front, pointer in hand. He tapped a map. “—the new intake filters at the river mouth have reduced particulate flow by twelve percent. That’s measurable progress.”

An elder near the front, Elara, shook her head. Mara stiffened at the name, an unbidden echo of the banned list. She forced her attention back to Vance.

“Twelve percent of a flood is still a flood,” a woman called from the side. Lin. She ran the desalination outpost. “My filters clog twice as fast as they did five years ago. The microfiber count is off the charts.”

“The dredge proposal addresses the existing accumulation,” Vance said, his voice a practiced calm. “A controlled collection from the central gyre. Bring it to the surface, process it at the coastal facility.”

“Process it into what?” Jax’s voice cut from the doorway. He leaned against the frame, arms crossed. “More stable landfill? You know it leaches. The groundwaters are already toxic.”

“The proposal includes stabilization—”

“Stabilization is a fancy word for ‘pile it up and hope.’ We’ve been hoping for fifty years.”

Vance’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. “Alternatives are welcome, Jax. Do you have engineering schematics? A cost analysis?”

The room went quiet. The challenge hung there, not about ideas, but about resources. About who had the right to speak.

Mara stood up. All eyes turned to her. She hadn’t planned to speak.

“The satellite passed over today,” she said. Her voice sounded strange in the thick air. “The one with the penitent payload.”

A few people shifted. The satellite was a joke, a gesture from a continent that had stopped sending real aid.

“It’s made of compressed straws,” Mara continued. “A monument to guilt. They shot their guilt into orbit so they don’t have to look at it.” She looked at Vance, then at the map with its red lines. “Your dredge is the same thing. You want to bring the guilt up, look at it, then bury it again. It’s not a solution. It’s a ritual.”

Vance’s pointer tapped lightly against his leg. “What would you have us do, Mara? Abandon the cleanup protocols?”

“I’d have you stop calling it cleanup.” She sat down. The wood felt cold.

The vote was called. The dredge proposal passed. The tally wasn’t even close.

Mara walked the long way home, along the cliff path. The moon was a sliver, offering little light. Below, the ocean was a black expanse, but she could hear it—the grind of plastic against sand, a sound like a million teeth chewing.

Footsteps sounded behind her. She didn’t turn.

“You made them uncomfortable,” Lin said, falling into step beside her. “Good.”

“It doesn’t change anything.”

“No. But it breaks the rhythm.” Lin handed her a small, cold object. A ceramic filter cartridge, its surface pitted and stained. “From my last change-out. Look at the intake side.”

Mara held it up to the vague moonlight. The ceramic was embedded with tiny, colorful fragments, a mosaic of human consumption. A fleck of a candy wrapper. A sliver of a toy.

“It’s in the water column,” Lin said. “Not just on the shore. It’s the water itself now.”

They walked in silence for a while.

“Jax is right, you know,” Lin said finally. “We’re managing the symptom. The disease is still out there.” She gestured toward the dark horizon. “Still making more.”

“What’s the answer, then? Capitulation?”

“Maybe the answer is smaller.” Lin stopped. “The dredge is a big, council-approved solution. It lets everyone feel like they’re doing something. What if we did something they wouldn’t approve?”

Mara looked at the filter in her hand. “Like what?”

“I don’t know yet. But my outpost has a deep-water sonar array. For mapping salinity. It could map… other things. Concentrations. Maybe track it back to source.”

“Source is everywhere.”

“Some sources are bigger than others.” Lin’s voice dropped. “The north-south freight corridor still uses the old polymer pellets. Raw feedstock. They spill containers. It’s a known thing.”

She trailed off. The implication was clear. Easier to clean up on the beach than to confront the origin.

“You’re talking about evidence,” Mara said.

“I’m talking about a different kind of map.”

Jax found her at dawn, back on the beach. She was staring at a new arrival: a medical waste container, cracked open, spilling its sterile, terrifying innards onto the sand.

“Lin talked to you,” he said.

“Yes.”

“And?”

Mara toed the container with her boot. It was stamped with a corporate logo, a stylized leaf. “She wants to make a map. Not of where it ends up. Of where it comes from.”

Jax was silent for a long moment. The waves hissed. “That’s not cleanup. That’s… accusation.”

He squatted, picked up a sealed vial of something blue. “They’ll shut her down. Take the array. Call it a violation of something.”

The desalination outpost hummed, a low-frequency vibration Mara felt in her teeth. It was a squat, concrete structure built into the cliff, pipes snaking down to the sea. Inside, the air was moist and warm, smelling of ozone and wet rock.

Lin’s control room was a nest of screens. One displayed the sonar feed, a green-and-black topography of the seabed. Another showed scrolling columns of data.

“This is the real-time particulate scan,” Lin said, pointing to a third screen. It showed a cloud of white specks suspended in a blue field, like a static snowstorm. “It distinguishes density. Organic matter fades. Synthetics ping brighter, linger longer.”

Jax leaned in. “Can you isolate the pellet feedstock? The nurdles?”

“Theoretically. Their acoustic signature is uniform. Like a billion tiny ball bearings.” Lin’s fingers flew across a keyboard. The snowstorm on the screen shifted, a filter applying. Large, diffuse clouds of white faded to gray. But distinct, concentrated ribbons of bright green appeared, snaking across the display.

“There,” Mara whispered.

The ribbons were currents within currents. Rivers of plastic.

“Track it backward,” Jax said. His voice was tight.

Lin worked. The view on the screen zoomed out, following the green ribbons eastward, past the continental shelf. The scale changed. The ribbons converged, merging into a thicker, pulsating stream.

“The freight corridor,” Lin confirmed. The stream ran parallel to a dashed line on a navigation overlay.

“Can you get a source?” Mara asked. “A specific point?”

“Maybe. If there’s a spill event, the concentration spikes. The data logs go back years.” Lin started typing again, then stopped. A red warning box flashed in the corner of a screen. “Proximity alert. Perimeter sensor.”

All three of them froze.

“Too early for the shift change.” Lin switched a screen to a grainy black-and-white feed. A camera looked down the access road. A single vehicle, lights off sat parked a hundred meters away. No one got out.

“They’re watching,” Mara said.

“They’ve always watched.” Lin killed the main display screens. The room plunged into dim emergency lighting. “The council approves my budget. They monitor throughput. They don’t…” She glanced at the now-dark sonar screen. “They don’t know about this subroutine.”

Lin nodded, already pulling a hard drive from a console. “It’s encrypted. Meaningless without the key.”

“Good. Don’t come back here for a few days. Run the standard diagnostics. Act normal.”

“Normal.” Lin’s laugh was short, brittle. “Right.”

Mara slipped out the sea-level door, a rusted hatch that led onto a narrow service ledge. The waves crashed below, loud enough to cover her footsteps. She climbed down a corroded ladder, the rungs gritty under her palms.

On the beach, she paused, looking back up at the outpost. A light came on in the control room, the normal, soft glow of the standby panels. Lin was playing her part.

Mara turned and walked into the water.

It was colder than she expected. She waded out until it reached her thighs, her jeans clinging heavily. The plastic grind was a vibration here, transmitted through the water. She looked out at the black sea. Somewhere out there, invisible were the green ribbons on the screen. Rivers of pure product, spilled and forgotten, making their inevitable way to her shore.

She hadn’t felt angry in the council chamber. She’d felt tired. Now, standing in the cold, contaminating water, she felt it, a sharp, clean point in her chest. Not a wave. Not a weight. A needle.

A light swept across the water from the cliff top. A flashlight beam, probing. It lingered near the outpost, then swept down toward the beach.

Mara didn’t move. Let them see her. Let them see a woman standing in the night sea doing nothing. It was the most truthful thing she’d done all day.

The light passed over her, paused, and moved on.

She waited until it was gone, until only the moon’s sliver and the distant glow of the penitent satellite remained. Then she walked back to shore, water streaming from her clothes, leaving a darker trail on the sand.
