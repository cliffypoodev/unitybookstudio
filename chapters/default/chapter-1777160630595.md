The fog that morning was not a uniform blanket, but a living thing. It crept in dense, shifting banks from the Mississippi, swallowing the base of the cypress trees and reducing the world to a radius of twenty feet. In the darkness before dawn on September 23, 1810, the air held a damp, penetrating chill that settled in the bones. For the men lying in the tall grass east of Fort San Carlos, the cold was a steady discomfort. Their breath condensed into pale clouds that vanished into the greater murk. They did not speak. The only sounds were the distant, rhythmic croaking of frogs in the swamps and the occasional, muffled shift of a man adjusting his position on the wet ground.

Philemon Thomas moved among them like a shadow. He was a compact man in his fifties, his face lined from years of surveying land and managing a plantation. That night, he was managing something else. He checked the priming on his men’s weapons—a motley collection of long rifles, fowling pieces, and a few military muskets. He repeated his orders in a low, steady voice, a sound just above a whisper. They were to hold their fire until he gave the signal. They were to move quickly once the gate was open. They were to avoid harming the governor if possible. Capture, not slaughter, was the objective. The plan relied on precision and surprise, not overwhelming force. Thomas had divided his command. The main body, about sixty men, waited with him in the open field facing the fort’s main gate. A smaller detachment of eight, led by a settler named Isaac Ballinger, had been sent on a wide flanking maneuver an hour earlier. Their job was to find a weak point in the palisade, get inside, and open the gate from within.

Fort San Carlos was less a fortress than a decaying stockade. Its log walls, erected decades earlier by the French, were rotting at the base. The two small corner bastions, designed to provide flanking fire, were empty. The single gate was a double door of heavy timber, barred from the inside. Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus had doubled the guard, but the term was relative. Instead of two sentries, there were four. They paced the narrow firing platform that ran along the inside of the wall, their footsteps a soft, hollow drumbeat on the worn planks. The fog muted the sound, making it seem to come from everywhere and nowhere. Inside the compound, the governor’s house and the barracks were dark. Delassus, according to his later testimony, had lain down in his clothes to rest, his sword and pistol on a table beside his bed. He expected a demonstration, perhaps a show of force at daybreak. He did not expect a silent infiltration in the pitch dark.

Isaac Ballinger’s group found their opportunity at the rear of the fort, where the palisade ran closest to the riverbank. The constant humidity had accelerated the decay of the logs. One, partially rotted, provided a narrow gap. One by one, the eight men squeezed through, their clothes snagging on the splintered wood. They emerged into the silent interior compound, a compact space of packed dirt and a few outbuildings. They could see the dark shapes of the sentries on the wall walk above them, silhouetted against the slightly lighter gray of the foggy sky. Ballinger signaled. His men split into two pairs and began to creep toward the main gate, using the shadows of the barracks for cover.

The sentry nearest the gate stopped his pacing. He leaned forward, peering into the fog that swirled just beyond the wall. He thought he heard a scraping sound from below, inside the compound. He turned, bringing his musket to his shoulder. At that moment, the first shot of the West Florida Rebellion was fired.

It did not come from Philemon Thomas’s main force. It came from inside the fort. Who fired it—a nervous rebel or the alerted Spanish sentry—is unclear from the conflicting accounts. Pension applications filed by Thomas’s men decades later claim the sentry fired first upon discovering Ballinger’s team. Spanish reports suggest a rebel panicked and discharged his weapon prematurely. The result was the same: a single, sharp report that tore through the heavy silence.

On the firing platform, the sentry crumpled. He was Lieutenant Luis de Grand-Pré, the son of a former governor. The ball struck him in the head. He fell without a sound.

The gunshot was the signal Thomas was waiting for, planned or not. He stood up from the grass and raised his own rifle. He took aim not at a man, but at the wooden gate. He fired. The report was the second shot of the morning, and it served as a command. Behind him, sixty men rose as one from the ground, a sudden, spectral line materializing from the fog. They did not yet charge. They waited, their weapons trained on the wall.

Inside, chaos erupted in silence. There were no shouts, no cries of alarm—just the frantic, muffled sounds of men scrambling in the dark. Ballinger’s team rushed the gate. A second Spanish soldier, a corporal, appeared from the guardhouse. He was shot point-blank and died instantly. The other two sentries, overwhelmed, threw down their muskets.

Ballinger’s men reached the heavy oak bar that secured the double gates. They strained against it, heaving it from its iron brackets. It fell into the mud with a deep, solid thud. They seized the iron rings and pulled. The warped timber, swollen with humidity, resisted. Then, with a groan of protest, the gates of Fort San Carlos swung inward.

Through the opening, Philemon Thomas saw the vague shapes of his own men inside, and the dark interior of the Spanish fort. He dropped his smoking rifle and drew the pistol from his belt. He did not look back at the armed settlers behind him. He simply pointed his pistol at the gateway and started to run.

The men followed. They did not roar. They made a collective, guttural sound—a release of tension, of cold, of fear—and surged forward as a body, their boots churning the wet grass into mud as they closed the fifty-yard gap to the open gate. The assault, the culmination of months of political maneuvering and weeks of military planning, was now a simple, physical race. The republic would be won in the next ninety seconds.

The governor’s house was a low, squared-log structure within the stockade walls, separate from the barracks. That night, Carlos Dehault Delassus was a man performing a role for an audience that had already left the theater. He knew his lines—the dignity of the Spanish Crown, the authority vested in him by the Captaincy General in Havana—but the stage was crumbling beneath his feet. His command consisted of fewer than fifty troops, a number that fluctuated with desertion and illness. A muster roll from earlier that summer, preserved in Spanish archives, listed twenty-eight effectives. American settlers in the district outnumbered his garrison by more than two hundred to one.

Delassus had received explicit warnings. In the days preceding the assault, two distinct messages reached him. The first came from a loyal Spanish subject, whose name was later omitted from official reports for his protection. This informant told the governor that armed men were gathering in the plantations east of Baton Rouge and that an attack on the fort was being planned. The second warning arrived on the evening of September 22, less than twelve hours before Thomas’s men crept through the fog. A settler, either sympathetic or seeking to avoid bloodshed, sent a direct note. It stated that the fort would be stormed before sunrise. Delassus, in his later testimony to American officials, acknowledged receiving this second warning. His response was not to call for a general alert or to dispatch riders to Pensacola for help. He simply doubled the guard from two men to four.

This decision was less a military calculation than an admission of impotence. To call his full garrison to arms would be to publicly concede that he feared the convention he was still officially negotiating with. It would shatter the fragile pretense of control he maintained during the “peace dinners” he hosted for delegates like John Rhea. So he opted for a gesture, a symbolic increase that changed nothing material about his defense. He ordered the gate barred and instructed the sentries to be vigilant. Then he retired to his house, laying down in his uniform, his sword and pistol within reach on a bedside table. He was not sleeping. He was waiting.

The architecture of Spanish power in West Florida had been rotting for years, long before the cypress logs of Fort San Carlos began to soften at the base. The administrative collapse was total. Vicente Folch, the Governor of Pensacola and Delassus’s superior, had received his subordinate’s request for reinforcements weeks earlier. Folch had in turn asked Havana and Cuba for men and money. The replies, slowed by the Atlantic crossing and the bureaucratic paralysis of an empire under Napoleon’s boot, were ambiguous and delayed. Folch concluded, as he wrote in a dispatch to his superiors, that a relief expedition would be useless by the time it could be organized. He never left Pensacola. The chain of command had rusted through. Delassus was not just isolated; he was abandoned.

Inside the fort that night, the doubled guard paced the wall walk. The soldiers were conscripts, mostly Cuban, stationed in a humid, disease-ridden outpost thousands of miles from home. Their morale was as low as their numbers. They knew the geography of their vulnerability. The stockade walls, ten feet high, were sufficient to deter casual banditry but were not designed to withstand a determined assault by a force of any size. The two corner bastions, intended to provide flanking fire along the walls, were empty emplacements—no artillery had been mounted there in years. The fort’s real defense had always been the authority it symbolized, not the physical barrier it presented. That authority had evaporated.

Delassus, in his wakeful state, would have heard the normal sounds of the night—the frogs, the distant river, the footsteps above. He would have heard the change when those footsteps stopped. The silence that followed was the silence of sentries listening intently into the fog. It was the silence that precedes a shot.

When the first gunfire erupted—that single, shocking report from within his own compound—Delassus’s reaction, as he later described it, was one of resigned fulfillment. He did not leap up to rally his men. He did not seize his sword. He rose and walked to the door of his house, opening it to a scene of quiet pandemonium. In the gray half-light, he saw shadowy figures rushing toward the main gate. He saw his corporal fall. He heard the heavy thud of the oak bar being dropped. The gates groaned open.

The governor made no attempt to fight. He stood in the doorway of his house, a solitary figure in the uniform of a kingdom that could no longer protect him. He was offering his person, not his fortress. The fort was already lost. His capture was now a formality, the final administrative act in the transfer of power. Philemon Thomas, sprinting through the gate with his pistol leading the way, saw him there. Thomas did not break stride. He and a handful of men altered their course and converged on the governor’s house.

Delassus did not raise his hands. He simply waited, his posture erect, maintaining the dignity of his office to the last possible second. Thomas stopped before him, his chest heaving, the pistol in his hand still warm. The two men represented the violent transition of an era: the compact, practical Anglo-American planter and the elegant, stranded European bureaucrat. Thomas spoke first, his words lost to history but their meaning clear. "You are our prisoner."

The governor inclined his head in a shallow nod of acknowledgment. It was neither defiance nor submission. It was the acceptance of a reality he had documented in his own reports for months. He was led away from his house, past the bodies of his two dead soldiers, and placed under guard in the barracks. The assault was over. The Spanish flag, which had flown over Baton Rouge for nearly three decades, was still tied to its pole above the wall, invisible in the fog. No one had yet thought to take it down.

The infiltration began not with a charge, but with a slow, deliberate creep. Philemon Thomas’s main force of sixty men did not move until the gunshot echoed from inside the stockade. They had been lying in the tall, wet grass for over an hour, their clothes soaked through, muscles stiff with cold and tension. When Thomas fired his rifle at the gate and rose, they rose with him. But their advance was not a headlong rush. It was a controlled, rapid walk, weapons at the ready, eyes fixed on the dark opening that had appeared in the wall. The fog, which had masked their approach, now worked against them, swallowing the shape of the fort and muffling the sounds of the brief, violent struggle inside. They moved toward a void.

The real stealth had been executed minutes earlier by Isaac Ballinger’s detachment of eight. Their flanking maneuver, as reconstructed from later pension statements and Thomas’s own reports, was a model of improvised tactics. They had circled wide to the north, using the riverbank’s noise to cover their movement. The Mississippi’s constant, low churn and the rustle of cane brakes provided a sound blanket. They reached the rear palisade, a section neglected because its back was to the river, a natural barrier. Here, the logs were in the worst state of decay. The men, one by one, went to their hands and knees in the mud. They felt along the base of the wall, their fingers searching for soft wood. They found it—a log where the heartwood had rotted away, leaving a spongy shell. Using the blades of their hunting knives, they quietly pried and gouged at the softened timber. The work was slow, nerve-wracking, and almost silent. The only sounds were the gentle splinter of wood and their own controlled breathing.

The gap they created was not wide. A later inspection by American engineers would note it was barely sufficient for a man to squeeze through sideways. Ballinger went first. He was a lean man, and he wriggled through the opening, his rifle pushed ahead of him, his shirt tearing on a jagged edge. He emerged inside the compound, lying in the dirt beneath the wall walk. The others followed. The process took nearly fifteen minutes. During that time, the four Spanish sentries paced their rounds twenty feet above them, their footsteps a regular, unhurried cadence. The rebels beneath them held their breath each time a bootstep paused directly overhead.

Once inside, Ballinger’s team faced a new problem: illumination. Dawn was still a faint promise, a slight lightening of the eastern fog. The interior of the fort was a patchwork of deeper shadows cast by the barracks, the governor’s house, and the few storage sheds. The men had to navigate this space without being silhouetted against the lighter sky near the walls. They used what cover they had, moving from the black shadow of one building to the next in short, quick dashes. Their objective was the main gate, roughly eighty feet across the compound. Their goal was to remove the heavy oak bar that locked it from the inside.

According to the sworn statement of Elias Beaumont, one of the eight, they moved in two groups of four. Beaumont’s 1852 application for a veteran’s pension, filed in Louisiana, provides one of the few firsthand accounts of the interior action. He stated his group was to provide cover near the guardhouse while Ballinger’s group went for the gate. They had not planned on engaging the sentries; the ideal scenario was to open the gate before the guards were aware. The fog, however, was thinner inside the walls, trapped by the palisade. As Beaumont’s group crouched beside a rain barrel, a sentry on the wall walk stopped his pacing. The man leaned over the platform’s edge, peering down into the yard. He had heard a boot scuff gravel.

Beaumont, in his statement, claimed the sentry brought his musket to his shoulder. The man’s intention was clear. In the poor light, he may have seen a shape, a movement. The rebel closest to him, a man named Hiram Shaw, did not wait. Shaw fired his pistol. The flash was a bright, shocking orange in the gloom. The sentry—identified in Spanish records as Lieutenant Luis de Grand-Pré—staggered back and fell onto the platform. The sound of the shot, contained by the walls, was a sharp, flat crack that seemed to suck all other sound from the air.

This was the moment the plan ended and the assault began. The second Spanish soldier, a corporal who emerged from the guardhouse door to investigate the noise, was met by a volley from Beaumont’s group. He was hit multiple times and died where he stood. The remaining two sentries, seeing their officer and corporal down and armed men now visible in the compound, threw their weapons over the wall and raised their hands. The fight for the interior was over in less than thirty seconds.

Ballinger’s group, now acting with desperate speed, rushed to the gate. The oak bar was as thick as a man’s leg, resting in two iron brackets. It took three men to lift it clear. They heaved it upward and out, letting it drop to the mud. The sound was a deep, resonant thud that carried through the ground. Then they seized the iron rings on the double doors and pulled. The timber, swollen from the constant humidity, resisted. For a few critical seconds, the gate would not budge. Outside, Philemon Thomas and his sixty men were now on their feet, staring at a wall that had just swallowed the sounds of gunfire. They could not see the struggle at the gate. They could only wait, fingers on triggers, for the doors to move.

Inside, Ballinger put his shoulder against the wood. Another man joined him. They pushed as the men on the rings pulled. The wood groaned, then yielded with a sudden, splintering crack. The right-hand door swung inward a foot. Then wider. A slice of the misty, gray field outside became visible. Through that opening, Ballinger saw the spectral line of armed settlers fifty yards away, and at their front, the solid shape of Philemon Thomas lowering his rifle. Thomas saw the open gate, the shadowy figures of his men inside, and the dim interior of the Spanish fort. He dropped his smoking long gun, drew his pistol, and began to run. The stealth phase was complete. The capture had begun.

The breach of the gate transformed the operation from a clandestine infiltration into a physical occupation. Philemon Thomas’s sprint covered the last fifty yards in a matter of seconds. His men flooded in behind him, a tide of wool and denim and hunting leather, their collective breath steaming in the cold, damp air. They fanned out across the interior compound, a space no larger than a prosperous plantation yard. Their orders had been clear: secure the barracks, secure the governor.

There was little left to secure. The two remaining Spanish sentries had already surrendered, their muskets discarded over the palisade. They stood against the wall with their hands raised, faces blank with shock. The rebels ignored them, rushing past to surround the long, low barracks building. The door was unlocked. Inside, in the gloom, the bulk of the Spanish garrison—some twenty-five men—were just rousing from their sleep. They had heard the shots, the thud, the rush of boots. As armed Americans filled the doorway, the Spanish soldiers made no move for their stacked arms. A later report from Governor Delassus, filtered through American officials, stated his men were “surprised in their quarters and could offer no organized resistance.” They were lined up against the far wall and placed under guard. Not a single shot was fired inside the barracks.

The only sustained, if brief, resistance came from an unexpected quarter. As Thomas’s men surged into the compound, a young Spanish cadet, barely more than a boy, emerged from the guardhouse. He was not part of the scheduled watch. He may have been sleeping there there, or seeking shelter from the damp. In his hand was a small ceremonial sword. He saw the rebels, saw his dead corporal on the ground, and charged. The action was not tactics; it was instinct. He ran directly at the nearest group of settlers, his sword raised.

The settlers, according to multiple pension accounts, were startled. They leveled their weapons—a mix of Kentucky long rifles, fowling pieces, and pistols. One man, a farmer from the Thompson’s Creek district named Asa Briggs, fired. His shot went wide, punching into the log wall of the guardhouse. Another man, closer, did not fire. He simply stepped forward and, using the stock of his rifle like a club, struck the cadet across the shoulders. The boy went down in the mud, his sword skittering away. He was hauled to his feet, disarmed, and shoved into the line of prisoners. His was the last gesture of Spanish military defiance at Fort San Carlos.

The entire armed engagement, from the first shot inside the walls to the disarming of the cadet, lasted less than two minutes. The efficiency was brutal. Two Spanish soldiers were dead: Lieutenant Luis de Grand-Pré and an unnamed corporal. Three others were slightly wounded, including the cadet. On the rebel side, the only injury reported was a sprained ankle sustained by Isaac Ballinger when he jumped from the wall walk after opening the gate. Not a single American was shot. The disparity was less a testament to rebel marksmanship than to the total collapse of Spanish morale and the overwhelming advantage of surprise. The garrison had been hollowed out by isolation and the certain knowledge of their own irrelevance. They fought, where they fought at all, out of a fleeting sense of duty, not expectation of victory.

Philemon Thomas did not pause to survey the scene. With the compound under control, he turned his attention to the governor’s house. Delassus was already in the doorway, as still as a portrait. Thomas approached, his pistol still in hand. The accounts of their exchange are secondhand, filtered through the memories of Thomas’s men and Delassus’s own later, sanitized reports. There was no dramatic speech. Thomas, a man of action, stated the new reality. "You are my prisoner," he is reported to have said. Delassus, who spoke fluent English, replied with a single word: "Yes." He offered no argument. He requested only that his personal papers and his sword be preserved. Thomas granted the request. The governor was then escorted to the barracks to join his men.

With the governor secured, a sudden, anticlimactic quiet settled over the fort. The adrenaline that had propelled the rebels ebbed, leaving them standing in the muddy yard, weapons lowered, listening to the normal sounds of morning returning. The frogs still chorused along the river. A mockingbird began its routine in a live oak just beyond the wall. The fog was beginning to thin, turning from a solid gray to wisps and tendrils that revealed the sorry state of the palisade, the weathered buildings, the two bodies lying where they had fallen.

The flag was still there. The rebels looked up, noticing for the first time the banner of Spain hanging limp and wet from its pole atop the governor’s house. No one had given an order to take it down. For a long moment, no one moved. It was a strange oversight—the symbol of the authority they had just overthrown, left untouched.

Then a man named Joseph Thomas, no relation to the commander, walked to the flagpole. He did not climb. He simply took hold of the rope and began to pull, hand over hand. The flag descended slowly, its sodden fabric clinging to itself. It reached the ground and lay in a heap of red and gold at the base of the pole. No one cheered. The men watched in silence as Joseph Thomas gathered up the banner, wadded it carelessly under his arm, and carried it away to be stored with the governor’s confiscated papers. The act was administrative, not celebratory. The revolution, in that wet yard at dawn, felt less like a birth and more like the completion of a foreclosure.

The capture of Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus was the final administrative act of the morning. He made it easy. As Philemon Thomas and his men converged on the doorway of his residence, Delassus stood waiting, his uniform coat buttoned, his posture erect. He offered no resistance. According to Thomas’s later recounting to a Baton Rouge newspaper, the governor’s first words were a request for the safety of his men and the preservation of his personal papers. Thomas agreed. The governor then surrendered his ceremonial sword and the pistol that had lain unused on his bedside table. He was led across the muddy compound, past the body of his corporal, and into the barracks where his soldiers were now prisoners. The transfer of power was complete in less than ten minutes.

Inside the barracks, a peculiar atmosphere settled. The Spanish soldiers, roughly two dozen in number, sat on their cots or stood along the walls under the watch of armed settlers. The tension of the fight had drained away, replaced by a shared, awkward stillness. The rebels were not jailers by trade; they were planters and farmers. Their prisoners were not hardened combatants but conscripts, most from Cuba, stranded in a remote post. A shared language barrier compounded the silence. Some of the Americans spoke a little French or Spanish, picked up from trade with New Orleans. Simple commands and gestures sufficed.

The priority now was inventory. Thomas dispatched squads to account for all Spanish property. The armory yielded seventy-five muskets, all of an outdated model, and twenty kegs of gunpowder, the latter a valuable prize. The fort’s storehouse contained barrels of salt pork, cornmeal, and a small stock of medical supplies. The real find was in the governor’s office. There, in a heavy iron chest, were the district’s official records: land grants, tax rolls, correspondence with Havana and Pensacola. These documents, more than the captured muskets, represented the true plunder of the day—the bureaucratic machinery of Spanish West Florida.

Thomas ordered the two dead Spanish soldiers carried into an empty storage shed and covered with canvas. The three wounded, including the cadet, were given basic care by a settler who had some experience as a bonesetter. The rebel with the sprained ankle was tended to with a strip of cloth torn from a blanket. The mundane work of occupation began: posting guards at the gate, assigning men to kitchen detail to feed both captors and captives, sending a rider back to the settlement to report the fort’s fall.

For Delassus, the morning unfolded with a surreal civility. After being secured in the barracks, he was, within the hour, moved to his own house under guard. He was allowed to sit at his desk. He requested writing materials. Thomas, recognizing the utility of an orderly transition, provided them. The governor then composed, in his precise hand, a letter to his superior, Vicente Folch in Pensacola. The document, preserved in Spanish archives, is a masterpiece of bureaucratic stoicism. It reported the capture of Fort San Carlos “by a superior force of insurgents” and his own detention. It did not assign blame or express outrage. It simply stated facts, as if filing a routine monthly report. He sealed it and handed it to Thomas, who agreed to have it dispatched. The gesture was symbolic—Folch could offer no help—but it allowed Delassus to perform the last duty of his office.

By mid-morning, the fog had burned away entirely, revealing a clear, cool September day. The sun illuminated the fort’s disrepair in harsh detail: the rotted logs, the rusting hardware, the weed-choked parade ground. Settlers from the surrounding plantations, hearing the news, began to arrive. They came on horseback and in wagons, curious to see the conquered Spanish stronghold. They milled about outside the gates, which now stood open and unguarded in a gesture of confident control.

The raid, therefore, was not a culmination, but a transfer of pressure. It moved the burden of proof from the intellectuals like John Rhea, who had argued why independence was just, to the operators like Philemon Thomas, who had to demonstrate how it worked. The flag was up. The harder task was making the republic exist beyond the sight of its single, lonely star.
