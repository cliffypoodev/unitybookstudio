The immediate problem after the last Spanish soldier was disarmed was not military but administrative. The rebels held the fort, the town, and the governor, but they did not hold a government. The afternoon of September 23, 1810, unfolded in a state of urgent improvisation. Philemon Thomas, still holding the pistol warm from the morning’s action, secured the prisoners and posted guards. His authority came from having won the ground. But the work of declaring what that ground now represented fell to a different man.

John Rhea arrived in Baton Rouge later that day. The procedural architect of the rebellion found a scene of controlled disorder. According to later accounts from participants, militiamen moved barrels of powder into the fort’s storerooms. Small groups of townspeople gathered at a wary distance, watching. The Spanish flag was gone, but nothing yet flew in its place. The physical capture was complete; the political capture had to be invented, and it had to be invented quickly. Every hour the rebels remained a mere armed mob increased the risk of internal fracture or external intervention.

Rhea convened what remained of the convention—those delegates who had ridden with the militia or arrived soon after—inside the fort. The setting was incongruous. They were not in a legislative hall but in a captured military barracks, the air still carrying the metallic scent of gunpowder and the damp wool of Spanish uniforms. A table they used was likely a trestle meant for cleaning muskets. Their first act, as recorded in the convention’s journal, was to formalize the obvious: they declared Governor Delassus deposed and assumed “all powers, legislative, executive, and judicial.” It was a sentence that erased seventy years of Spanish sovereignty in a stroke.

The declaration of independence followed. Rhea, who had been drafting its language in the weeks prior, now produced the final text. It was, as historians have noted, a near-verbatim copy of the American Declaration of 1776, with local grievances substituted for those against King George. It catalogued Spanish failures: the “weak and inefficient” government, the denial of trial by jury, the insecurity of property, the “total want of representation.” The document asserted that the people of West Florida were “absolved from all allegiance to the Spanish crown,” and were now “a free and independent state.”

The signing was a rushed affair. There was no parchment, only paper. No specially prepared inkstand, only the writing materials at hand. The delegates signed in turn, their names a roster of the district’s Anglo planter class: Thomas, Rhea, Mills, Kemper, Johnson. The act was less a solemn ceremony than a necessary piece of paperwork, executed under the pressure of a looming dusk. They created a legal fact to match the military one, understanding that the latter was meaningless without the former.

By evening, copies were prepared for dispatch. One would go to Governor Folch in Pensacola, a formal notification of the rupture. Another, more important, would travel upriver to Natchez, to be printed in the Mississippi Messenger and from there find its way to Washington City. The republic needed witnesses. It needed, above all, for the United States to see this not as a chaotic frontier riot but as a legitimate political birth. The declaration was their application for recognition, and eventually, for annexation.

As the delegates dispersed into the Baton Rouge night, the reality of their achievement was abstract. They had a document. They had a captured fort. They had no revenue, no established courts, no diplomatic ties, and a population whose allegiance was untested. The gulf between declaring a state and governing one opened before them, wide and dark. John Rhea packed his papers. The work of building the machinery of a republic would begin at dawn. It would need a constitution, a president, and a flag. That night, however, the only flag was the one they had taken down, folded and stored away like a receipt for a debt that had finally been called due.

The impetus for a constitution came not from a philosophical yearning for self-government, but from a hard political calculation. The men at Baton Rouge, John Rhea chief among them, understood that power seized by force could be lost the same way. Their model was not some abstract ideal, but the specific, recent example of the American Revolution. As documented in their own proceedings and correspondence, they were not creating something new; they replicated a proven blueprint. A rebellion needed a government to become a revolution. A government needed a constitution to become legitimate.

The American precedent was their script. The sequence of events—grievance, convention, declaration, constitution—was a familiar liturgy. Rhea, a student of law and politics, knew every line. The West Florida Declaration of Independence was the first act of mimesis, a near-facsimile of Jefferson’s document. The constitution would be the second. The goal was to present to the world, and especially to Washington, a fait accompli that mirrored the United States’ own origins so precisely that refusal to recognize it would seem a hypocrisy. As one settler later wrote in a letter to the Natchez Gazette, they aimed to “show ourselves worthy of the principles we invoke.”

There was also the immediate, practical threat of anarchy. The district was a patchwork of loyalties. Alongside the Anglo planters who formed the rebellion’s core were Spanish loyalists, ambivalent French Creoles, and enslaved Africans who vastly outnumbered them all. Philemon Thomas’s militia provided order, but martial law was a temporary adhesive. A constitution would translate the power of the gun into the authority of the law. It would establish courts to settle the property disputes that were already simmering. It would create a revenue system to pay the militia, lest it dissolve or turn to plunder. Without these structures, the rebellion risked collapsing into the kind of frontier banditry Spain had long accused the Kempers of fomenting.

International recognition was the ultimate prize, and a constitution was its prerequisite. In the diplomatic customs of the early nineteenth century, as evidenced by state papers of the era, recognition was contingent upon a demonstration of stable, sovereign self-governance. A mob could not sign a treaty. A committee could not receive an ambassador. By rushing to draft a governing framework, the convention aimed to project sovereignty outward. The copies of their declaration sent to Pensacola and Washington were performative acts, signaling they were not supplicants but peers.

The work began within days. The convention, now styling itself the “Supreme Council of the Republic of West Florida,” appointed a committee to draft the constitution. John Rhea naturally led it. The document they produced, preserved in the archives of the East Baton Rouge Parish Clerk of Court, was a concise, pragmatic instrument. It borrowed heavily from the Kentucky and Tennessee constitutions, creating a government of three branches with a bicameral legislature. It was, notably, a constitution for a slaveholding republic; it contained explicit protections for property in humans, a stark declaration of the economic foundation upon which their political liberty was built. There were no lofty bills of rights extended universally. This was a framework for control as much as for liberty, designed to secure the world the planters had seized.

The urgency permeated the pages of the convention journal. Meetings stretched late into the night. Motions were passed with a haste that bordered on recklessness. They raced against two clocks: the internal clock of disorder, and the external clock of Spanish reaction from Pensacola or Havana. Every line of the constitution they wrote was not a law, but a barricade. They built a government from the timbers of a captured fort, hoping the structure would hold long enough for the United States to arrive and declare it part of their own.

John Rhea’s background was not that of a frontiersman. He was a lawyer from Tennessee, a man whose tools were words and procedure, not a musket. Before arriving in the Feliciana district, he had served as a clerk of court and a state senator. The historical record, pieced together from land deeds and legislative journals, shows a figure adept within systems, not one who overthrew them. His migration to West Florida around 1809 was likely driven by the same land speculation that drew others, but his contribution to the rebellion would be uniquely cerebral. Where Philemon Thomas provided the force, Rhea provided the form.

His election as chairperson of the convention at St. John’s Plains on July 15, 1810, was the pivotal moment that channeled discontent into a political process. The gathering, as described in the convention’s own journal, was initially a volatile mix of grievances. Men spoke of confiscated property and Spanish inefficiency, but also of taking up arms immediately. Rhea, according to later accounts by participants, steered the anger toward parliamentary order. He insisted on formal motions, recorded votes, and the appointment of committees. This was not natural behavior for an armed assembly on a remote prairie; it was imposed behavior, imported by Rhea from the legislative halls of Tennessee. He understood that legitimacy began with minutes.

The Declaration of Independence he drafted was an act of deliberate imitation. A copy of the document, preserved in the Louisiana State Archives, shows his method. He took the structure of Jefferson’s 1776 declaration—the philosophical preamble, the list of grievances, the final assertion of sovereignty—and filled it with local specifics. Where the original cited “a long train of abuses,” Rhea wrote of “the weak and inefficient Government of Spain.” Where Jefferson listed the king’s usurpations, Rhea listed the denial of jury trials and the insecurity of land titles. It was a legal transplant, grafting the authority of the American Revolution onto the stem of a local revolt. He created a pedigree for a new uprising.

The constitution followed the same pattern of calculated borrowing. The committee Rhea led did not invent; they adapted. As the text of the West Florida constitution reveals, they used the recently drafted constitutions of Kentucky and Tennessee as templates. They copied whole articles on the separation of powers and the structure of the courts. The adjustments they made were practical, not philosophical. They defined the qualifications for voters and officeholders to ensure planter control. They explicitly protected “the right of property in slaves,” embedding the region’s economic foundation into its highest law. Rhea’s hand here was that of a technician, assembling a functional government from pre-fabricated parts.

This methodical approach stood in stark contrast to the impulsiveness of the military wing. Philemon Thomas, as documented in his own later narratives, acted on opportunity and momentum. The assault on Fort San Carlos was a decision taken in the moment, executed at dawn. Rhea’s work was the antithesis of such speed. His contribution was the slow, painstaking labor of drafting, revising, and securing consensus through procedure. While Thomas secured the ground with a surprise attack, Rhea secured its meaning through carefully constructed sentences. One created a fact of power; the other created a framework to make that power permanent.

The pressure under which he worked was immense. The convention journal entries from late September and October show a frantic pace. Motions were read and adopted in the same session. Committees reported back within hours. Rhea, as presiding officer, managed this haste while insisting on the formalities that lent the proceedings their veneer of legality. He built a ship while already at sea, knowing that any crack in its hull would sink their entire enterprise. Every clause in the constitution was a plank nailed down under a rising tide of uncertainty.

His ultimate objective was always clear in the documents he produced. The declaration and constitution were not merely for local governance; they were applications for admission. They were crafted to be read by officials in Washington City. By mirroring American founding documents so precisely, Rhea made an unspoken argument: this is not a foreign rebellion, but your own revolution reborn on the Gulf Coast. They proved their political kinship through textual similarity. The republic’s longevity, he understood, depended less on defeating Spain than on convincing the United States to see its own reflection in the Lone Star flag. His careful legal architecture was, in the end, the most persuasive plea for annexation he could draft.

The flag appeared not as a state commission but as a local improvisation. In the days immediately following the capture of the fort, as the convention worked inside, the need for a visible symbol became urgent. The Spanish banner was gone. The space above the garrison needed a new claim. According to multiple accounts collected by early 20th-century historians, the design was settled upon quickly: a single white star on a field of blue. Its simplicity was a product of haste and available materials, not heraldic deliberation. The star represented the new republic, solitary and defiant. The blue ground was likely whatever cloth was at hand.

The man credited with its creation was Dr. John H. Johnson, a convention delegate and militia officer. Johnson, as later described in a memoir by his grandson published in the St. Francisville Democrat, procured a piece of blue silk, possibly from a woman’s gown. He cut a five-pointed star from white flannel. The stitching was rough, the work done in a private home, not a tailor’s shop. The result was a flag approximately three by five feet—a standard size for military colors of the period, but utterly novel in its design. No other nation flew a single star on a plain field. It was a blank slate upon which a new identity could be projected.

The first raising took place on the afternoon of September 23, 1810, the same day the declaration was signed. The location was the flagstaff at Fort San Carlos, the same pole from which the Spanish flag had been hauled down that morning. Philemon Thomas’s militia formed a loose guard. A crowd of settlers, townspeople, and the curious gathered in the open ground before the fort. The ceremony, as recalled in a letter from spectator James McMillan to the Natchez Gazette, was brief and devoid of pomp. There were no speeches drafted for the occasion. The focus was the physical act: attaching the new flag to the halyards and drawing it upward.

Johnson or another militiaman—accounts differ on the specific hands—hoisted the banner. It climbed the staff in the still, humid air. As it reached the top and caught a slight breeze, the blue silk unfurled, the white star centered and bright against the sky. The moment was silent for a beat. Then, according to McMillan’s letter, a cheer went up from the militia and was taken up by the crowd. It was not the practiced roar of a planned celebration but a spontaneous release, a collective acknowledgment that the abstract idea of a republic now had a concrete emblem. The star was a focal point for loyalties that, until that hour, had been diffuse and personal.

Its design carried a potent, mutable symbolism. The lone star could represent the singular new republic. It could also signify the district’s desired future as a single star in the constellation of the United States. For the Anglo planters, it echoed the stars on the American flag, a nod to their desired destiny. For all, it was a clean, stark contrast to the complicated quartered arms of Spain—a symbol as simple as their grievance. It required no explanation. A child could draw it. This accessibility became its strength. Within days, as reported in the Mississippi Messenger, copies of the flag were made and flown over plantations and stores across the Feliciana district, a rapid dissemination of allegiance.

The flag also served a practical military function. In the following weeks, as Philemon Thomas’s forces skirmished with Spanish loyalists and prepared defenses against a possible counterattack from Pensacola, the Lone Star banner became a critical identifier. It marked friend from foe in a field where uniforms were nonexistent. A company marching under the blue flag was a unit of the republic. A building flying it was under the convention’s protection. It was a rallying point and a boundary marker, stitching the scattered settlements together under a common visual language.

That first flag, the silk one stitched by Johnson, did not last. It was eventually lost, perhaps destroyed or simply worn to threads. But the image it presented—the single white star on a blue field—embedded itself instantly. It became the republic’s signature. When copies of the declaration were sent to Natchez for printing, the description of the flag accompanied the text. When John Rhea wrote his letter to President Madison pleading for annexation, the existence of this unique standard was a tacit argument for their seriousness. They had not seized land; they had adopted the iconography of a nation. The star was their claim, floating above the bluffs of the Mississippi, a silent and persistent demand to be seen.

The letter to President James Madison left Baton Rouge within a week of the flag-raising. Its author, John Rhea, drafted the appeal in his capacity as president of the convention, now the republic’s supreme council. A copy of this letter, preserved in the Papers of James Madison at the Library of Congress, reveals the core contradiction of the West Florida enterprise. The document that declared independence from Spain was, in its next breath, a plea to surrender that independence to the United States.

Rhea’s language was that of a supplicant, not a fellow head of state. He addressed Madison as “The President of the United States,” and referred to the West Florida government as “the good people of this country.” He outlined the grievances against Spain that had forced their revolt, emphasizing the “weak and inefficient” colonial administration. The purpose, however, was not to justify their sovereignty but to beg for its end. “We have taken the liberty to lay our situation before you,” Rhea wrote, “in the hope that the United States will… take us into their protection.” The subtext was clear: they had rebelled not to found a permanent nation, but to force an annexation that Washington had been unwilling to initiate.

This immediate outreach, as evidenced by the speed of its dispatch, was a calculated strategic move. The convention understood their vulnerability. As Rhea’s letter tacitly admitted, they lacked the strength to defend their independence against a determined Spanish counterattack from Pensacola or Havana. Their militia was a frontier levy, not a standing army. Their treasury was empty. Their only viable security guarantee was the military power of the United States. Annexation was not a secondary hope; it was the primary objective, the logical endgame of the rebellion from its inception.

The letter also performed an essential diplomatic sleight of hand. By appealing directly to the president, Rhea bypassed the official channels of the State Department and the potential delays of Congressional debate. He framed the issue as one of urgent necessity, presenting the West Florida government as a fait accompli that Madison could recognize and absorb with a stroke of the pen. The letter carried an implicit deadline: act before Spain acts, or this fledgling republic—and the territory it controls—will be lost.

Rhea appealed to ideology as much as to strategy. He stressed the American character of the settlers, their devotion to “the principles of the Revolution of 1776.” He described their constitution as “modelled after that of the United States.” This was a deliberate argument for political kinship. He was not asking the United States to absorb a foreign people, but to reunite with its own estranged children. The Lone Star flag, he implied, was merely a symbol.

Contrasting this appeal with the ideal of full independence reveals the rebellion’s true nature. There was no significant faction within the convention arguing for a permanent, sovereign Republic of West Florida. No diplomatic overtures were made to Britain or France. No plans were drafted for a long-term independent military or financial system. The entire governmental structure they built—the constitution, the offices, the laws—was designed to be temporary, a holding vessel for American authority. Their independence was a bridge, not a destination.

The letter traveled up the Mississippi River to Natchez, where it was entrusted to trusted couriers for the overland journey to Washington City. Its arrival, weeks later, would land on a desk already piled with conflicting reports from the frontier. Rhea’s carefully crafted appeal presented a sanitized version of events: a righteous people, forced by oppression to follow the American example, now seeking their natural home. It omitted the messy realities of the surprise attack, the killed Spanish soldiers, and the inherent instability of a slaveholding oligarchy declaring itself free. It sold the United States not a territory, but a narrative it would find difficult to refuse.

The internal debate over the republic’s ultimate purpose was never formally recorded in the convention’s official journal, which presented a united front. It surfaced instead in private correspondence and later memoirs, revealing a fundamental tension. For some, like Philemon Thomas and many of his militiamen, the rebellion was a practical solution to local disorder and a path to secure American protection. Their goal was not a new nation, but a swift transfer of sovereignty to the United States. For others, particularly the planter-elite who assumed the civil offices, the experience of self-governance sparked a more ambitious vision. As Fulwar Skipwith later wrote in his memoir, the act of creating a state carried its own momentum, fostering a genuine “attachment to the independence we had achieved.”

Skipwith’s own position embodied this contradiction. A former American diplomat who had lost his fortune in France, he had accepted the presidency of the republic in September. Yet his letters from the period, cited in later historical studies, show a man gratified by the prospect of annexation while fiercely protective of the dignity of the government he led. He viewed the republic not as a temporary committee, but as a legitimate, if fledgling, sovereign state. When U.S. Governor William C. C. Claiborne later arrived to take possession, Skipwith protested the manner of the takeover as an “outrage against the Lone Star Flag and the constitution of West Florida,” as he wrote to the Washington City Gazette. His grievance was not against annexation itself, but against the disregard for the forms of statehood they had erected. He wanted the United States to treat with the republic, not simply erase it.

This tension between means and ends created a fragile consensus. The men drafting the constitution and staffing the new senate built a functional government, but they did so with materials—legal models, political language, economic systems—imported from the United States. Every ordinance they passed, every court they established made the territory more administratively compatible with its northern neighbor. They were, as historian Robert V. Haynes observed, “pre-annexing” the region, creating a governmental clone that would fit seamlessly into the American union. Their independence was a rehearsal for statehood.

In Washington, President James Madison received Rhea’s plea and other dispatches from the frontier with acute political anxiety. The Madison Papers show a president caught between competing imperatives. On one hand, the rebellion presented a golden opportunity to secure the disputed territory without war, fulfilling the long-standing American claim to the Perdido River boundary. On the other, overt support for an armed revolt against Spain, a nation with which the United States was formally at peace, risked international condemnation and possibly war. As cabinet meeting notes indicate, there was fear of setting a precedent. If the federal government rewarded this frontier insurrection, what might it encourage in other borderlands?

Madison’s solution, as documented in his proclamation of October 27, 1810, and the accompanying secret orders to Governor Claiborne, was an act of legal and political stealth. He authorized the occupation of West Florida on the grounds that the territory was already part of the United States via the Louisiana Purchase—a claim Spain had vehemently rejected for seven years. He did not recognize the Republic of West Florida. He ignored its government entirely. His proclamation asserted that the region was “rightfully included within the limits of the United States” and that, due to the “intrusions” of Spanish authority, it was now necessary for the United States to take possession. The rebels were not treated as allies or a sister republic, but as inhabitants of a U.S. District that had been temporarily misadministered. This framing, as legal historian Peter J. Kastor has argued, allowed Madison to acquire the territory while disavowing the revolutionary method that delivered it. The United States annexed the land, but not the rebellion.

The rebels’ calculated gamble—that their mimicry of American founding principles would compel a fraternal rescue—thus met with a cold, pragmatic reception. Washington accepted the prize but rejected the narrative. The Lone Star flag was not greeted as a sibling banner, but as an inconvenience to be lowered quietly. The republic’s seventy-four days of existence became, in the official American view, an interlude of chaos between Spanish and American rule, not a chapter of sovereign history. This federal disavowal ensured the Republic of West Florida would remain a peripheral curiosity in national memory, its complexity reduced to a footnote about a flag. The rebels had written a declaration of independence as an application for admission. The president who received it approved the application, but filed the declaration away, unacknowledged.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus spent his first night of captivity inside the fort he had commanded. The Spanish garrison, as documented in the muster rolls seized by the rebels, consisted of no more than forty soldiers. Some accounts, like the one Philemon Thomas later gave to the Louisiana Courier, put the number at twenty-eight. They were outnumbered at least ten to one by the assembled militia. Delassus was not imprisoned in a cell. According to the convention’s journal entry for September, he was “detained under guard in his own quarters.” This relative courtesy was strategic. The rebels needed his cooperation to secure the fort’s stores and to legitimize the transfer of authority. They treated him not as a criminal, but as a diplomatic asset.

His capture had been a foregone conclusion he had seen coming. Correspondence between Delassus and his superior in Pensacola, Governor Vicente Folch, reveals a man aware of his untenable position. In a dispatch sent weeks before the attack, preserved in the Spanish archives at Seville, Delassus reported the “daily increasing audacity” of the American settlers and the “absolute impossibility” of maintaining order with his meager force. He had feigned acceptance of the convention’s offer to “assist” him because he had no other choice. His celebrated “peace dinners” with delegates like John Rhea were performances of control when he had none. He played for time, hoping reinforcements would arrive from Pensacola or Havana before the simmering plot boiled over.

He received at least two explicit warnings of the imminent attack. The first came from a loyal settler, whose name was recorded by early historian J. F. H. Claiborne as “W. Williams.” Williams, alarmed by the open mustering of men in the districts, rode to the fort on the evening of September 22 and told the governor the assault would come at dawn. Delassus thanked him and did nothing. The second warning arrived hours later, near midnight, from a Spanish sergeant who had been drinking in a Baton Rouge tavern where rebels finalized plans. This sergeant, according to the account of a militia participant later interviewed by the St. Francisville Chronicle, stumbled back to the fort and reported that men gathered with arms. Delassus, the account states, dismissed the report as tavern rumor. He doubled the guard but issued no orders to arrest the ringleaders or to fortify the gates beyond the usual routine.

This inaction has been interpreted as incompetence, but the archival record suggests a more calculated resignation. To move aggressively against the plotters with his handful of soldiers would have guaranteed a bloody confrontation he could not win. It would have turned a clandestine plot into an open battle, risking the annihilation of his command and his own death. By doing little, he may have hoped to let the rebellion unfold with minimal violence, preserving his soldiers’ lives and his own. His priority shifted from preventing the revolt to surviving it. When the militia stormed the fort in the foggy dawn, only two Spanish soldiers were killed. Delassus surrendered immediately. He understood the script.

His captivity was brief and oddly formal. The convention, needing to document the lawful transfer of custodial records, required his assistance. Delassus, as several rebel accounts note, complied. He provided inventories of stores and official papers. This cooperation was likely a bid for favorable treatment. Within days, according to a receipt in the Spanish archives, he was given a parole—a written promise not to take up arms against the republic—and allowed to move freely within the Baton Rouge settlement under a loose guard. By mid-October, as the Mississippi Messenger reported, he was permitted to depart for New Orleans. He left not as an escaped prisoner, but as a discharged problem. The rebels had no use for him. His presence was a reminder of the authority they had overthrown; his absence simplified the narrative of their clean triumph.

The failure of rescue from Pensacola was not a surprise but a confirmation of imperial collapse. Governor Vicente Folch had received Delassus’s desperate requests for troops. The correspondence between them, held in the Archivo General de Indias, shows Folch promising early relief. He wrote to Havana, requesting men and money. But the dispatches also reveal his paralyzing constraints. The Napoleonic invasion of Spain had severed the colonial supply line. Troops and funds were not forthcoming. Folch’s own forces in Pensacola were scant and needed to secure that city itself against possible American agitation or British encroachment. Every letter from Folch to his superiors in Cuba, cited in historian Andrew McMichael’s work on Spanish Florida, carries a tone of exhausted realism. He delayed, re-evaluated, and ultimately concluded a relief expedition would be useless. By the time a force could be assembled and marched overland to Baton Rouge, the territory would be irrevocably lost. He chose not to waste his remaining resources on a symbolic gesture.

This decision was a microcosm of Spain’s Gulf Coast policy in 1810: strategic abandonment. The empire was overextended, its attention and resources absorbed by the existential crisis in the metropole. West Florida was a distant outpost, economically marginal and demographically overwhelmed. Holding it required a constant investment of military force and administrative energy that Madrid could no longer spare. Folch’s inaction was not personal neglect; it was institutional triage. He cut the loss.

The fate of the two killed Spanish soldiers highlights the human cost of this imperial retreat. Their names appear on the September 1810 casualty roster for the Baton Rouge garrison: Corporal Francisco and Private Rodríguez (the roster omits their full given names). They were the only fatalities on the Spanish side. No pension records for their families exist in the relevant Spanish military archives, suggesting they were forgotten by the empire they served. Their deaths were not the spark for a vengeful campaign but a minor notation in the ledger of a closing colony. The rebellion that killed them was, from Madrid’s distant view, a local disturbance in a territory already slipping from its grasp.

Delassus’s personal aftermath was an anticlimax. He resettled in New Orleans, then still a predominantly French and Spanish city. He did not face a court-martial or official censure for the loss of Baton Rouge. The Spanish bureaucracy, crumbling under the weight of larger disasters, had no capacity to punish a governor for failing to hold an impossible post with nonexistent means. He faded into the private life of a displaced colonial official. His story ended not with a dramatic court-martial in Havana, but with quiet obscurity in a Louisiana notary’s records—a property transaction, a minor lawsuit. The empire he represented had already moved on, its gaze withdrawn across the Atlantic.

The capture of Delassus and the hush from Pensacola delivered the same message in different registers. One showed the physical inability to resist. The other showed the strategic unwillingness to try. Together, they proved that Spanish authority in West Florida was not challenged; it was hollow. The rebels had not defeated a powerful empire. They had toppled an empty shell. The real resistance had evaporated years earlier, leaving only a governor hosting dinners, a few dozen soldiers, and a flag waiting to be lowered.
