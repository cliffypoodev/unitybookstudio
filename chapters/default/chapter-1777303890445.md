The name came through the tablet’s tinny speaker, warped by static and water but unmistakable. “Jesus of Nazareth. Current asset designation: Rogue Contractor. Please hold for audit.”

The airboat’s engine sputtered. Mary’s hand stayed frozen on the throttle. The giant gator didn’t blink. Its good eye tracked the slow drip of swamp water from Jesus’s elbow to the aluminum deck.

Rex’s low hum climbed an octave. “I believe ‘hold’ is a euphemism.”

“Go,” Jesus said.

Mary didn’t need telling twice. She slammed the throttle forward. The fan screamed, churning the black water into a froth. The boat lunged, throwing Jesus against the dummy. The gator didn’t move. It just watched them flee, its tablet held steady.

They rounded the first bend, the shanty boat’s sinking hulk lost behind a wall of cypress knees and Spanish moss. The canal narrowed, choked with hyacinth and algae so thick it looked like green shag carpet. The airboat plowed through, leaving a trail of shredded vegetation.

“It knew your name,” Mary said, her voice tight. She didn’t look back.

“It read my invoice.” Jesus righted himself, his palms stinging. He flexed his fingers. The phantom cross-prints felt hot, like fresh brand marks. A stupid, psychosomatic flare-up. He focused on the swamp instead. A dragonfly the size of a sparrow hovered near his face, its wings a blur of iridescent blue. It had no business being that big. Nothing here did.

Rex swam alongside, his bulk parting the algae like a mossy plow. “The entity is pursuing. At a leisurely pace. This is insulting.”

Jesus glanced back. A smooth V-wake cut through the green mat fifty yards behind them. No hurry. The gator was herding.

The canal forked. Mary took the left branch without hesitation. The right one looked wider, clearer. The left was a tunnel of overhanging branches and shadow. The water here glowed a sickly, phosphorescent green where the prop churned it up.

“Bioluminescent algae,” Mary said, answering the question he hadn’t asked. “Decay and chemical runoff. Don’t touch the water.”

“Wasn’t planning on a swim.”

The glow intensified. The water itself seemed to pulse with a faint, diseased light. It lit the undersides of the leaves above in eerie emerald. Up ahead, the tunnel opened into a wider pool. Something jutted from the water at its center, angular and man-made.

It was a church. Or what was left of one.

A small, white-clapboard building, its paint blistered and peeling, sat perched on stilts over the glowing pool. A crooked steeple, missing its cross, tilted toward the water like a drinking bird. Light—real, electric light—flickered in one of the stained-glass windows. The glass depicted a fisherman, but the colors had run, making it look like he was drowning in a rainbow oil slick.

“No,” Jesus said.

“Yes,” Mary replied, steering directly for it.

“It’s a church in a bioluminescent swamp. That’s not a refuge. That’s the first sentence of a horror story.”

“The gator’s behind us. The gator has a tablet and knows your social security number. I’ll take the horror story.” She cut the engine. Momentum carried them into the shadow of the stilts. The water here was thick, gelatinous with glow. “We need cover. And maybe a landline.”

“Who are you gonna call? The EPA?”

Rex surfaced beside the boat, his snout dripping luminous slime. He sniffed the air. “There is music. And the smell of fried food.”

Jesus heard it now. A faint, staticky hymn, maybe a recording of “Shall We Gather at the River,” played on a loop. And under it, the sizzle of something in hot oil.

A wooden plank dock, green with mold, led from the water up to a screened door. A hand-painted sign, barely readable, was nailed to a piling: The First Church of Perpetual Rebirth & Fish Fry. All Welcome. Tuesdays Half-Price.

Mary was already tying the boat to a rusted cleat. “Come on.”

“This is a terrible idea.”

“You have a better one?”

He looked back. The V-wake was at the mouth of the tunnel, motionless. Waiting. The gator’s yellow eye caught the glow from the water, shining like a single, baleful coin in the dark.

Jesus grabbed the dummy and hauled it onto the dock. The wood groaned. The dummy’s plastic head bonked against a plank. He half-expected it to start preaching again. It stayed quiet, a sack of heresy and wires.

They clambered up. Rex couldn’t fit on the dock. He sank beneath the surface, only the ridge of his spine visible, a dark, mossy island in the green glow.

The screened door squealed on hinges that hadn’t seen oil since the Carter administration. The smell hit Jesus first—old wood, mildew, cheap fry grease, and underneath it all, the sweet, cloying scent of funeral flowers.

The room was a single open space, pews facing a makeshift altar made from a fiberglass bass boat turned on its end. Paddle oars were crossed above it like swords. The stained-glass fisherman window was on the left, the flickering light coming from a television set perched on a folding chair. It played a VHS tape of a baptism in a muddy river, the image tracking poorly.

A man stood behind a counter at the back, tending a deep fryer bubbling with oil. He wore a grease-stained pastor’s collar over a Budweiser t-shirt. He didn’t look up as they entered.

“Service is Tuesday. Fish fry is always. You’re early, but the oil’s hot.”

“We need a place to lay low for a minute,” Mary said.

The man lowered a basket of breaded fillets into the fryer. The sizzle drowned out the hymn for a second. “Low from what?”

“Gator.”

Now he looked up. His eyes were pale, watery blue. He wiped his hands on a towel. “Eden audit or regular nuisance?”

“Audit. Big one. With a tablet.”

A slow smile spread across the man’s face. He had a gold tooth. “Well, hallelujah. Brother, you have come to the right place.” He gestured around the room. “This here is consecrated ground. Or at least, it’s zoned commercial-miraculous. Their jurisdiction ends at my property line. So long as you’re inside, you’re under my protection.”

Jesus set the dummy down against a pew. It slumped like a drunk parishioner. “And what’s the cover charge for this protection?”

“The First Church of Perpetual Rebirth operates on the principle of mutual benefit.” The pastor—his name tag read Rev. Dale—leaned on the counter. “I provide sanctuary from corporate reptilian oversight. You provide… a fresh perspective. And maybe a story. We get lonely out here.”

Mary eyed the fryer, then the TV. “What’s the rebirth part?”

Rev. Dale’s smile didn’t waver. He pointed a thumb toward a curtained-off doorway behind the counter. “The pool out back. It’s fed by the same aquifer as the glow. Has certain… properties. Some folks come seeking a new start. A cleansing.” He pulled the basket of fillets up. They were golden brown. “Most just come for the fish. It’s very good fish.”

A low, resonant thump vibrated through the stilts beneath the floor. The water in the fryer shivered.

Rev. Dale sighed. “That’ll be your auditor. Right on schedule.” He walked to the fisherman window and peered out. “Yep. That’s a senior field agent. Got the tablet and everything. Looks… disappointed.”

Jesus joined him at the window. The giant gator floated in the glowing pool, just outside the ring of stilts. It had its tablet out, claw tapping. It looked up, met his gaze through the glass, and then slowly, deliberately, shook its massive head. The universal sign for you are causing paperwork.

“Will it come in?” Mary asked.

“Can’t. No spiritual warrant. Also, the steps are too narrow.” Rev. Dale turned back to his counter. “But it’ll wait. They’ve got phenomenal patience. Biblical patience.” He began plating the fish. “You hungry? It’s on the house. Consider it a welcome to the fold.”

Another thump, harder. A stilt creaked.

Rex’s voice, muffled by water and wall, echoed from beneath the building. “The entity is attempting to undermine the foundation. I am… discouraging it.”

A splash. A guttural grunt.

Rev. Dale paused, fork in hand. “You got a dinosaur under my church?”

“He’s with us,” Jesus said.

“Huh. Okay. Just… tell him not to stir up the pool too much. The glow’s sensitive.”

Mary was at the curtain, pulling it back a fraction. Jesus saw her shoulders tense. She let the curtain fall and walked back, her face carefully neutral. She stopped next to him, her voice a low murmur only he could hear.

“The pool’s not empty. There are… things in it. Fish. But not right.”

“What’s that mean?”

“Their eyes are wrong. And they’re watching the door.”

Rev. Dale called over. “The rebirth process requires a willing participant and a catalyst species. The Miraculus ichthyus. They’re harmless. Mostly. They just get excited when someone new comes around. Hopeful, you could say.”

The television’s baptism tape ended, rewound, and started again. The preacher on screen plunged a believer backwards into the muddy water. The believer came up spluttering, a smile on his face.

Jesus looked at the dummy, then at the glowing water outside, then at Rev. Dale cheerfully sprinkling tartar sauce on a plate. His palms throbbed.

The gator thumped the stilt again.

Mary leaned closer. “We can’t stay here.”

“We can’t go out there.”

“The gator’s one problem. This guy is a different one. The fish are hungry, Jesus.”

Rev. Dale brought two plates over, setting them on the pew beside the dummy. “Eat up. Strengthen yourselves. The wait might be long.” He glanced at the dummy. “Your friend don’t eat?”

“He’s on a spiritual fast,” Jesus said.

A tremendous splash erupted outside, followed by a roar that was half-gator, half-frustrated office manager. The whole building shuddered. Rex bellowed back, a sound of pure dinosaur annoyance.

Rev. Dale winced. “Please. The foundation.”

Jesus walked to the door and looked out. The glowing water was churned into a frothing maelstrom. Rex and the gator were a tangled knot of scales and teeth and fury in the middle of it. The gator had its tablet clamped in its jaws, trying to protect it. Rex had its tail in his claws and was attempting to spin, a move Jesus had last seen in a professional wrestling match.

The gator’s tablet flew from its mouth, arced through the air, and landed with a soft plop in the thick algae ten feet from the dock.

It floated. The screen was dark.

Both creatures stopped fighting. They looked at the tablet. They looked at each other.

Then they both lunged for it.

“Now,” Jesus said.

He grabbed the dummy. Mary was already moving, vaulting over the pew and out the squealing screen door. They hit the dock running. The wood bounced and swayed.

“Rex! Disengage!” Mary yelled.

Rex heard her. He gave the gator’s tail one final, mighty shove, sending the beast into a slow, surprised roll. Then he powered towards the airboat, his reactor churning the glowing water into a neon vortex.

Jesus untied the line, threw the dummy into the boat, and shoved off. Mary hit the starter. The engine caught on the second try.

The gator righted itself, snatched its tablet from the algae, and turned its good eye on them. It tapped the screen. The screen stayed black. Water dripped from its seams.

It issued a sound—a long, low, bureaucratic groan of profound inconvenience.

The airboat’s fan spun up. They shot forward, not back into the tunnel, but across the wider pool, toward a channel on the far side that was pitch black, free of the glow.

Jesus looked back once. Rev. Dale stood in the doorway of his glowing church, holding a plate of fish. He gave a small, friendly wave.

Then the gator, tablet clutched to its chest, began to sink beneath the surface. Not chasing. Just… logging the incident. Filing it away.

They hit the dark channel. The world went black and quiet, the only light the fading green radiance in their wake.

Mary let out a breath she’d been holding for three minutes.

Rex surfaced beside the boat, dripping black water now, not glow. “I believe I have ingested several gallons of that substance. My internal diagnostics are… confused.”

Jesus sank onto the bench, the dummy’s cold plastic leg pressing against his. The adrenaline bled away, leaving the sharp ache in his palms and a deeper, wearier ache behind his eyes. He stared into the dark ahead.

A new light appeared in the distance. Not green. A soft, pulsing blue, like the heart of a cheap neon sign. It came from around the next bend.

“What’s that?” Mary asked, her voice thin.

“Trouble,” Jesus said. “The other kind.”

The boat carried them toward the blue pulse. The hymn and the smell of fry grease were gone, replaced by the hum of insects and the drip of water from Rex’s scales. The dark channel had no forks. Only one way to go.

Toward the light, and whatever was waiting there with its own set of rules.

The blue pulse resolved into a flickering neon sign, half-submerged in the black water. It read The Gator’s Last Stand Bar & Grill. The building was a floating shack on oil drums, tethered to a massive cypress. A dozen airboats were tied to a surrounding dock, their fans still. The sound of a jukebox—Waylon Jennings, fighting a losing battle with bad speakers—drifted across the water.

Mary cut their engine, letting momentum glide them in. “We need to get off the water. Regroup.”

“A bar,” Jesus said. “Because a church worked out so well.”

And from deep within the swamp, something began to answer.
