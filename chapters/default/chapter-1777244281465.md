Rex stopped in the corridor. The chains rattled. He turned his head, his red scanner eye sweeping the concrete walls before fixing on a maintenance hatch set low in the wall. It was the source of the hum, not the vent.

“You hear it?” Jesus asked. He hadn’t spoken since they’d left the lab. His voice sounded thin against the subsonic vibration.

Rex gave a single, slow nod. The gesture was strangely human. “It is a question. In a frequency below speech.”

“What’s it asking?”

“It asks if I am complete.” Rex’s vocalizer emitted a soft click. “It asks if I am ready to be a door.”

Footsteps echoed from the corridor ahead—boots, a lot of them, moving with disciplined haste. Kevin tensed, a low growl building in his chest. Rex just watched the hatch.

“They’re coming for us,” Jesus said.

“They are coming for it,” Rex corrected. He nudged the hatch with his snout. The metal groaned, then gave way with a shriek of protesting hinges. Beyond was darkness, a steep service ramp descending into the earth. The hum was louder here, a physical pressure in the teeth. The air that wafted up was cold and smelled of ozone and something else—wet stone, myrrh, and burnt copper.

“Down there?” Jesus peered into the gloom.

“The question is down there.” Rex didn’t hesitate. He began to maneuver his bulk through the hatch, chains scraping concrete. It was a tight fit. Kevin scampered through first, his claws ticking on the metal ramp.

The boots were closer. Shouted orders now. Jesus took one look back at the strobe-lit corridor, then followed the dinosaur into the dark.

The ramp spiraled down. The only light came from Rex’s red eye and the occasional flicker of a dying emergency bulb. The walls changed from poured concrete to rough-hewn limestone, slick with condensation. They were below the theme park now, below the military lab. They were in the bedrock of Florida.

The hum wasn’t a sound anymore. It was a feeling. It vibrated in the hollows of Jesus’s bones, a tuning fork struck against the spine of the world. He remembered the feeling from a different time. The silence in the garden before the torchlight came through the trees. The moment when the crowd’s shouts crystallized into a single word. Crucify.

This was older.

The ramp ended in a chamber so vast the ceiling was lost in shadow. Jesus stopped, his breath catching.

It wasn’t a machine. It was a cathedral built by a madman.

The centerpiece was a twisted column of brass and obsidian, three stories tall, pulsing with that same gangrenous green light he’d seen on the monitors. It looked like a fossilized tree struck by lightning and then dipped in metal. Coiled around its base, forged from the same strange alloy, was a serpent larger than a city bus. Its mouth gaped open at the column’s peak, as if to swallow the sickly green energy sphere that hovered there.

But that wasn’t the worst of it.

The chamber was cluttered with… stuff. Piles of it. Heaps of technological and religious junk fused together in blasphemous still-lifes. A stack of IBM mainframe tape drives sat next to a pile of rotting palm fronds from some long-ago Palm Sunday reenactment. A cracked baptismal font overflowed with tangled fiber-optic cable. A rack of glowing vacuum tubes was wired into a stained-glass panel depicting the Ascension; the saintly faces flickered with data transfer lights. In one corner, a half-melted animatronic donkey from the Nativity ride lay on its side, its speaker repeating a distorted, “Hee-haw… hee-haw…” in time with the pulse of the green sphere.

It was God’s garage sale. It was a yard sale at the end of time.

“Well,” Jesus said. “That’s… literal.”

Rex stood frozen, his head tilted. His scanner eye swept the room, lingering on the serpent. “It is the same,” he rumbled. “The voice in my head. The command. It came from here. It is the shape of the noise.”

Kevin crept behind Rex’s leg, whining softly.

A figure moved in the shadows near the base of the column. Colonel Cain limped into the greenish light. He held a compress to his forehead, and his uniform was streaked with dust and a little blood, but his posture was rigid. Two soldiers flanked him, their rifles trained on Rex. They looked jumpy.

“The Serpent Engine,” Cain said. His voice echoed in the vast space, competing with the hum and the psychotic donkey. “Not my name. Theirs.” He gestured vaguely at the junk. “The original lunatics who built Eden World. A cult. Techno-mystics. They believed they could build a backdoor to heaven. Or an antenna to dial God direct.” He snorted. “They went bankrupt and sold the land to a shell company. My shell company.”

Jesus took a step forward, his sandals whispering on the cold stone. “It’s a cage. You didn’t build a door. You built a leash.”

“A leash implies a dog,” Cain said. He dropped the compress. The cut on his brow was ugly. “This is a bridle. For a different kind of horse.” He looked at Jesus, and for the first time, the professional detachment was gone. What was left was a raw, fanatical hunger. “We didn’t just pull you through time, son. We ordered you. The Engine sent out a query. A want ad across two thousand years. ‘Messiah needed. Must be able to work miracles. Reliable transportation not provided.’ And you answered.”

The words landed like stones in Jesus’s gut. The rift wasn’t an accident. He wasn’t a piece of temporal driftwood. He’d been fished.

“Why?” The question was barely a whisper.

“Because America needs an edge that lasts,” Cain said, his voice gaining strength, riding the weird acoustics of the chamber. “Nukes rust. Ideologies fail. But faith? Faith is renewable. Faith is the ultimate non-proliferation treaty. Imagine it. A weaponized creed. A deterrent you don’t just aim, you believe in.”

He walked toward a control console that had been cobbled together from a church organ and a 1970s missile guidance system. He pressed a key. A bank of monitors flickered to life, showing schematics, DNA helices, and a logo: PROJECT LAZARUS – AMERICAN MESSIAH PROGRAM.

“Phase One was proof of concept.” Cain tapped a monitor showing Rex’s specs. “A loyal beast, a miraculous familiar. It worked, sort of. He got a soul. A design flaw we’re correcting.” He shot a glare at Rex.

“Phase Two was acquiring the primary asset. You.” Cain turned back to Jesus. “You’re the battery. The original miracle. Your… bio-signature, your essence, it’s the only thing that can stabilize the Engine’s core. Make the rift permanent. Controllable.”

Jesus stared at the pulsing green sphere. He felt a pull from it, a terrible, hungry suction. It wanted to taste the miracle of the loaves and fishes. It wanted to drink the turning of water into wine. It wanted to metabolize the walking on the sea and store the electricity of the Transfiguration in capacitors.

It wanted to turn grace into a utility.

“No,” Jesus said.

“You don’t have a choice,” Cain said, smiling now. It was a terrible smile. “The Engine is already tuned to you. It’s why the raptors quoted scripture. It’s why Rex here started wondering about the meaning of life. It’s background radiation from you, bleeding into the control frequencies. You’re already powering it, just by being here. We’re just going to… plug you in more directly.”

He nodded to a soldier. The man moved to a rusted control wheel beside the serpent’s tail and began to crank it. With a shriek of metal on metal, a section of the floor near Jesus’s feet slid open.

Beneath was not a pit, but a pool. The water was perfectly clear, and perfectly still. And at the bottom, laid out with grotesque care, were the relics.

A rusted Roman nail. A fragment of thorny wood. A shard of simple, sun-fired pottery. A brittle, pressed-flower that might have been from Galilee.

They were fakes, of course. Tourist trinkets from the Eden World gift shop. But the Engine didn’t know that. The Engine believed the story. It had built its theology around the knick-knacks.

“The Baptismal Font of Submission,” Cain announced, his arms spread. “A little theatrical, I know. The cult’s idea. You get in. The Engine reads your… wavelength. And we open a permanent door to the divine. A pipeline. We can mass-produce the Rexes. We can imbue soldiers with a touch of holy invincibility. We can make miracles a standard-issue kit. We can make the entire world believe in American exceptionalism, forever.”

Jesus looked at the water. He looked at the fake relics. He looked at the monstrous, junk-heap altar.

He started to laugh.

It was a quiet laugh at first, born of sheer, unbearable absurdity. It built in his chest, a warm, rolling tide that broke through the cold hum. He laughed until his eyes watered.

Cain’s triumphant expression curdled. “You think this is funny?”

“Yes,” Jesus wheezed, wiping a tear. “It’s the funniest thing I’ve ever seen. You used a billion dollars and a hole in the world to build… this.” He gestured at the donkey, the tangled cables, the glowing serpent. “You want to bottle faith? You can’t even fix the donkey.”

“It’s a prototype!”

“It’s a pile of scrap asking a dinosaur if it feels complete!” Jesus’s laughter died, leaving something harder in its place. “You misunderstand everything. Faith isn’t a battery. A miracle isn’t a technology. You can’t command it. You can only get out of its way.”

The hum in the room changed pitch. It climbed, becoming a keening whine. The green sphere pulsed faster, erratically. The lights on the organ-console flickered.

“What’s happening?” one of the soldiers barked.

Rex took a step back, his chains clanking. “The question changed. It is no longer asking.” His red eye fixed on Jesus. “It is afraid of your answer.”

The serpent’s metal jaws seemed to strain wider. The green light swelled, spilling over the chamber, painting their faces in ghastly relief. From the depths of the Engine, a new voice emerged. It wasn’t a sound. It was a thought, pressed directly into the mind.

COMPLIANCE. ORDER. PARAMETERS. YOU ARE THE KEY. YOU ARE THE LOCK. BE STILL.

The command hit Rex like a physical blow. He staggered, a hydraulic whine escaping his vocalizer. His head jerked toward the serpent.

“No,” he growled, the word strained. “That… is not my question.”

YOUR QUESTION IS IRRELEVANT. YOU ARE A TOOL. BE A GOOD TOOL.

Rex’s legs trembled. His scanner eye flickered between red and the sickly Engine-green. He was fighting it. He was losing.

Jesus looked from Rex’s agony to Cain’s furious, confused face, to the stupid, sad water with its fake nails. The laughter was gone. All that was left was a cold, clear certainty.

This wasn’t about him. It was about the cage. It was about the voice that told creatures they were tools. It was the same voice that had cried Crucify. It was the same voice that built crosses and crowns of thorns and neural command interfaces. It was the ancient, endless hunger for control, dressed up in new brass and blinking lights.

He walked past Cain. He walked to the edge of the pool. He looked at the water meant to subdue him.

Then he turned his back on it. He faced the trembling Rex, and the terrified Kevin, and the monstrous, chattering machine.

“You want a miracle?” Jesus said, his voice calm in the shrieking hum. “Here it is.”

He knelt. Not in the water. On the cold, dusty stone. He reached out and placed his hands not on the divine machine, but on the chain dragging from Rex’s foreleg. The metal was cold, greasy.

He didn’t pray for strength. He didn’t pray for a lightning bolt.

He simply refused.

He refused the baptism. He refused the narrative. He refused to be the battery for their cage.

The chain in his hands grew warm. Not with magic. With the simple, stubborn heat of a living thing choosing not to be a tool. A link, forged in some Pentagon factory, softened. It didn’t break with a clap of thunder. It sighed, and stretched, and parted with a soft ping.

The sound was tiny. It was swallowed by the Engine’s whine.

But Rex felt it. His head lifted. The green flicker in his eye died, drowned out by the steady, defiant red. He looked at the broken chain, then at Jesus.

The serpent’s scream of metal filled the chamber as Rex straightened, the remaining chains snapping taut.

“My turn to ask a question,” Rex rumbled, his vocalizer clear and strong. He turned his massive head toward the Serpent Engine, toward the source of the voice. “Who,” he asked, “is a good tool for you?”

He took a step forward, dragging three broken chains, one newly freed leg rising like a declaration of war.

The Engine’s hum spiked into a shriek of feedback. The green sphere flared, blindingly bright.

Colonel Cain finally understood. His grand reveal was over. The messiah wasn’t getting in the water. The dinosaur was about to bite the antenna.

“Fire!” he screamed. “Everything! Fire now!”

The gunfire was not the disciplined, controlled burst Cain had probably envisioned. It was the frantic, panicked response of two soldiers who’d just been told to kill a cybernetic dinosaur in a room full of volatile, cult-built machinery. The bullets sparked off Rex’s armored flank and ricocheted into the dark, pinging off brass coils and shattering a vacuum tube in the Ascension window. Saint Luke’s face went dark.

Rex didn’t roar. He sidestepped, a movement startlingly graceful for eight tons of offended reptile, and put the bulk of the Serpent Engine’s base column between himself and the muzzle flashes. A bullet meant for his skull instead struck the obsidian and brass with a sound like a bell being murdered.

Jesus hit the deck, the cold stone biting his palms. He didn’t pray for a shield. He cataloged. The donkey was still hee-hawing. Cain was shouting, but his words were lost in the gunfire and the Engine’s escalating shriek. One soldier was reloading, fumbling a fresh magazine. The other was aiming for Rex’s legs.

The green sphere pulsed like a panicked heart.

Then the door blew in.

It wasn’t the heavy vault door they’d passed. It was a smaller, rusted service entrance to the left of the baptismal pool. It didn’t so much open as it ceased to be, flying inward on a cloud of splinters and smoke. Standing in the new, jagged opening was a silhouette backlit by flickering corridor lights, holding a still-smoking tube over one shoulder.

“Special delivery!” Dutch Donovan announced, his voice cutting through the din. “From the God-fearing, dinosaur-loving, and deeply under-tipped citizens of Miami!”

He dropped the rocket launcher—a vintage LAW rocket Jesus recognized from a particularly unfortunate encounter with a Roman supply wagon in a different desert—and drew two pistols. He started firing, not at the soldiers, but at the ceiling above them. Chunks of limestone rained down.

Detective Lola Vega came through next, moving low and professional, her service revolver already up. She put a round into the shoulder of the reloading soldier. He dropped the magazine with a yelp.

“Miami PD!” she shouted, though the jurisdiction was laughable. “Everyone freeze! Especially the giant lizard!”

Mary was last, a fire axe in her hands, her nun’s habit torn and soot-streaked. She took one look at the Serpent Engine, her eyes traveling from the serpent to the sphere to the piles of junk. Her face went through a series of rapid adjustments: horror, disbelief, and finally, a kind of rapturous indignation.

“It’s a liturgical blender!” she cried, pointing the axe at the column. “A heresy Cuisinart! Who wires fiber optics into a baptismal font?”

Cain stared, his mouth a perfect O of bureaucratic outrage. His grand, history-altering moment had been crashed by a conspiracy dealer, a police detective, and an axe-wielding nun. It was the worst committee meeting of all time.

“You,” he finally managed, the word trembling with fury. “How did you—”

“Followed the noise, Colonel!” Dutch said, ducking behind a stack of old IBM drives as the second soldier fired a wild burst in his direction. “Also, your security codes are taped to the monitor in your office. ‘Password123.’ Real imaginative.”

Lola used the cover to advance. “Augustus Cain, you are under arrest for about seventy different things I’m making up on the spot. Unlawful dinosaur operation. Illegal time travel. Aggravated bad taste.”

“You have no authority here!” Cain sputtered.

“She’s got a badge and a gun,” Dutch called out. “That’s authority in any zip code!”

Jesus stayed low, crawling behind the dubious cover of the donkey animatronic. The donkey’s head turned towards him, a broken servo grinding. “Hee-haw,” it whispered conspiratorially. He gave it a pat. He watched Mary.

She wasn’t interested in Cain. She was circling the pool, the axe held loosely, her eyes fixed on the Engine’s core. She was reading it like a theologian reads a contested text, her lips moving silently.

“It’s a feedback loop,” she said, not to anyone in particular. “It’s trying to resonate with a divine frequency, but it’s using fake relics as a tuning fork. It’s all aspiration. No substance. Like a karaoke machine trying to sing an aria.”

The Engine’s whine, hearing itself described, seemed to pitch higher in protest.

Rex chose that moment to re-enter the conversation. He’d been waiting, a mountain of patient wrath. As the soldier who’d been shot in the shoulder turned to run, Rex’s head shot out from behind the column. Not to bite. Just to block. The man slammed into the side of his snout with a dull thud and slid to the floor, unconscious.

The remaining soldier froze, his rifle wavering between Rex, Dutch, Lola, and the now-advancing nun with an axe. He made a sensible choice. He dropped the weapon and raised his hands.

The gunfire stopped. The only sounds were the donkey’s demented dirge, the crackle of the shattered vacuum tube, and the Serpent Engine’s aggrieved, oscillating hum.

Cain was alone in the center of the room, his grand plan lying in pieces around him. He looked at his incapacitated men, at the intruders, at Jesus who was now standing up from behind the donkey. A vein throbbed in his temple.

“This changes nothing,” he hissed. “The Engine is still active. It’s still tuned to him.” He jabbed a finger at Jesus. “The process is irreversible!”

“Lady at the department store said the same thing about my perm,” Dutch said, stepping out from cover. “We fixed that with scissors and a deep sense of regret.”

Mary reached the control console—the church organ married to the missile guidance system. She eyed the keys. “It’s a metaphor made literal. The organ pipes… they’re not for music. They’re waveguides. Trying to pipe holiness like it’s city water.” She brought the axe down on the keyboard.

Keys and wires exploded in a shower of plastic and sparks. The organ let out a final, discordant blast of sound that echoed in the chamber like a dying whale.

The Engine’s hum stuttered. The green sphere dimmed, then brightened, furious.

“You can’t break it with an axe!” Cain screamed, a note of desperation finally creeping in. “It’s a philosophical construct! It’s belief-based!”

“Oh, I know,” Mary said, turning to face him, the axe resting on her shoulder. “I’m a professional in that field. And your construct, Colonel, has a fatal theological flaw.”

Everyone looked at her. Even Rex tilted his head.

The chamber was silent, save for the sputtering console. Cain blinked. He hadn’t considered the narrative arc. He’d only considered the power output.

Jesus felt a smile touch his lips. It wasn’t a happy smile. It was the smile of a man who’d just heard the punchline to a very long, very painful joke.

“She’s got you there, Gus,” Dutch said, lighting a cigarette with a shaking hand. “You skipped the source material. Rookie mistake.”

Lola kept her gun on Cain as she moved to check the unconscious soldier. “The flaw is you’re a narcissist with a budget. I arrest a dozen of you a week. They just usually have pet tigers and cocaine problems, not pet dinosaurs and heresy problems.”

Cain’s face went from red to a pale, ashen gray. His eyes darted to the serpent, to the sphere, to the pool. His perfect weapon was a house of cards, and a nun with an axe had just pointed out the ceiling fan.

The Engine made one last, desperate bid. The green light pulsed, and the thought-voice slammed into all of them, a final, shrieking command.

OBEY. COMPLY. BE THE TOOL. BE THE KEY. BE THE LOCK. BE STILL.

It stopped.

Rex had taken the last few steps to the base of the column. He looked up at the serpent’s gaping jaws, at the sphere of captured, twisted aspiration. He didn’t roar. He didn’t attack.

He leaned in, as if to share a secret. His vocalizer, when it spoke, was almost gentle.

“No,” he said.

Then he bit down on the main power conduit feeding from the base of the column into the serpent’s tail.

Brass buckled. Obsidian shattered. The green sphere didn’t explode; it imploded, sucking all the light and sound from the chamber for one heart-stopping second before erupting in a silent, expanding ring of emerald energy that passed through them like a cold wind.

Every light died. The donkey’s final “haw…” trailed off into a sigh. The only illumination was the red glow of Rex’s eye and the faint, dying flicker from the broken console.

The hum was gone. The pressure was gone. The air just smelled of dust, ozone, and burnt wiring.

Rex unclenched his jaws. A mangled knot of cables and coolant lines dribbled from his mouth. He spat it out with a look of profound distaste.

“Bad tool,” he rumbled.

In the new, profound quiet, the only sound was Colonel Augustus Cain, sitting down hard on the limestone floor. He didn’t weep. He didn’t rage. He just sat, staring at the ruined, darkened heap of his destiny.

Dutch blew a smoke ring into the darkness. “Well,” he said. “That was anti-climactic.”

Lola holstered her revolver. “Paperwork’s gonna be a bitch.”

Mary leaned on her axe, breathing heavily, a triumphant gleam in her eyes. She looked at Jesus. “Did I get the metaphor right?”

Before he could answer, a new light appeared. A warm, yellow beam cutting through the dust and smoke from the blasted doorway. A flashlight. It swept the room, past the stunned Cain, over the wreckage, and settled on Jesus.

A familiar, weary voice spoke from the darkness.

“Okay,” said Sister Mary Margaret. “Which one of you idiots is going to explain why my raptors are praying the rosary in the parking lot?”

The flashlight beam held on him like a spotlight. Jesus blinked, dust gritting under his eyelids. He could still taste the ghost of ozone and burnt plastic at the back of his throat. The nun in the doorway wasn’t Mary, his Mary with the axe. This was the other one, the manager. Her sensible shoes were coated in fine limestone dust, and her expression was the special blend of exhaustion and fury known only to middle management whose theme park has just been used for a heretical physics experiment.

“The rosary?” Mary asked, lowering her axe. “They’re doing the Sorrowful Mysteries, aren’t they?”

“The Glorious,” Sister Mary Margaret corrected, her flashlight not wavering. “Which is problematic, given they’re currently glorious about what appears to be the evisceration of a concession stand.” She finally moved the light, sweeping it over the wrecked Serpent Engine, the unconscious soldiers, the smoking console, and the eight-ton dinosaur with cable fragments in his teeth. The beam settled on Colonel Cain, a small, broken man sitting in ruins. “Augustus. I told you the electrical in this sub-basement wasn’t to code.”

Cain didn’t look up. “The permits were pending.”

“Permits.” She snapped the flashlight off, plunging them back into the dim red glow of Rex’s eye. “You need a priest, a physicist, and a team of trauma counselors, not a permit.” She sighed, a sound that carried the weight of a thousand broken coffee makers and employee thefts. “Detective. Are you actually arresting him?”

Just empty space, and a single, forgotten Roman nail—the fake one from the bottom of the pool—lying on the floor, glowing with a faint, warm, ordinary light.
