The road was a pale scar across the brown hills. Dust, fine as ground bone, coated my tongue. It settled in the creases of my knuckles, the sweat-damp collar of my tunic. The sun was a white hammer on the back of my neck. We had walked since before first light, the rhythm of our sandals a dry percussion against the stone paving. Micah walked ahead, a silent, broad-shouldered shadow. Elazar and the others trailed behind, their conversation a low murmur about the price of wine in Damascus compared to Jerusalem. The world had narrowed to heat, dust, and the relentless forward press of my own will.

The letter was a live coal against my chest, wrapped in oiled cloth inside my tunic. I felt its corners with every stride. Authority. Purpose. Each step was an argument against the woman’s stare, against the cold shiver under the stars. The Law was a straight line. My path was its echo.

A lizard darted across the stones, a blur of ochre. It vanished into a crack. My eyes tracked the empty space. The High Priest’s voice surfaced, unbidden. A messiah who fulfills the Law renders it complete. A finished thing. Heresy. A door slammed shut on a room still full of people. My fist clenched. The dust gritted between my fingers.

“How much farther?” Elazar’s voice, edged with a whine. “My water’s half gone.”

“You drank it too fast,” Micah said without turning. “The city is there when it is there.”

I didn’t answer. The hills shimmered in the heat. Damascus was out there, a knot of streets and synagogues and hidden believers. I rehearsed the names on my list. I saw the arrests in my mind—the confrontation, the citation of scripture, the firm application of Temple authority. Clean. Surgical. A purification.

A donkey brayed in the distance, a rusty-hinge sound. The smell of thyme from a scrubby bush beside the road, crushed under someone’s foot. These details were irrelevant. I pushed them aside. They were static, interference in the clear signal of my mission.

My heel throbbed where the blister had broken. The pain was a pinpoint of focus. Good. The body’s complaints were petty. I welcomed them. They were a sacrifice offered on the altar of conviction.

“Saul.” Micah had fallen back beside me. He held out his waterskin. “Drink.”

“I have my own.”

“You haven’t touched it. Your lips are cracked.”

I took the skin, drank. The water was warm, tasting of leather. It did nothing to slake the dust. I handed it back. He watched me, his eyes squinted against the glare. He had that look again—the one from the inn. As if I were a clay vessel he was checking for hairline cracks.

“The men are talking,” he said, his voice low.

“Let them talk.”

“They talk about Stephen. They saw the family at the gate too.”

A hot needle twisted behind my ribs. “What of it? A heretic died. His family grieves a lie. Their grief does not sanctify the error.”

Micah was silent for a dozen steps. The road began a gentle ascent. “I only report what I hear.”

“I did not ask for a report.”

He nodded, falling behind again. The friction was a low heat between my shoulder blades. He was loyal, but his loyalty was to a man, not the mission. He worried about my state. The mission required no state, only action.

The sun climbed. The light changed. It wasn't the gentle gold of morning, or the white fury of noon. It thickened. The air grew heavy, pressing down on the crown of my head. The dust motes hung suspended, glowing. My breath came shorter. Not from the climb.

Something was wrong with the sky.

It was too blue. A depthless, polished blue, like the glaze on a ceramic bowl. The hills lost their detail, becoming flat cutouts against it. No birdsong. The chatter of the men behind me faded, swallowed by the strange, dense silence. Even our footsteps seemed muffled.

I stopped.

Micah stopped a pace ahead. He turned. “What is it?”

I couldn’t name it. The air hummed. Not a sound, but a vibration in the teeth, in the marrow of my wrists. The light wasn’t just bright. It was accumulating, pooling around us. The shadows of the rocks bled away, erased.

“Do you feel that?” My own voice sounded distant.

He looked around, wary. “Feel what? The heat?”

Not heat. A pressure. A presence, vast and focused, condensing in the atmosphere. The letter against my chest burned. The straight line of the Law wavered in my mind’s eye, a scribe’s hand suddenly unsteady.

Then the world dissolved.

No. Not dissolved. It was replaced.

From the center of that impossible blue, light erupted. It was not sunlight. It had no source, no direction. It was simply there, all of it, everywhere at once. A whiteness so absolute it was neither color nor void. It scoured the hills, the road, my companions’ startled faces—it bleached everything to a single, searing fact of illumination.

It hit me in the chest. A physical force.

It threw me backward. My body left the ground. For a suspended instant, I was floating in pure, silent white. No heat. No cold. A terrifying, total clarity.

Then I struck the stones.

The air blasted from my lungs. The world snapped back—but only to sound. The roar was inside my skull, a tidal wave of noise that was also a voice. It was not heard with ears. It was etched directly onto the fabric of my being.

SAUL.

The stones dug into my back. I couldn’t breathe. The white filled my eyes, my nose, my mouth. I was drowning in light.

WHY DO YOU PERSECUTE ME?

The question was not words. It was a universe of meaning delivered in a single stroke. It contained the smell of blood on stones, the peace on Stephen’s face, the dry wax of the Temple seal, the weight of every warrant I had ever signed. It contained the woman’s grief and Micah’s worry and the dusty taste of the road. It was an accusation that was also an invitation, a demolition that was also a revelation.

My mouth worked. “Who are you, Lord?”

The title was ripped from me. Instinct. Protocol before a power so absolute it made the High Priest a guttering candle.

I AM JESUS, WHOM YOU ARE PERSECUTING.

The name landed like a second impact.

Jesus. The Nazarene. The crucified fool. The dead heresy.

But he was not dead. The voice was alive. It was the source of the light. It was the thing I had been hunting, and it had been hunting me. My whole life—my scholarship, my zeal, my righteous fury—was a frantic flight from this. From the living center of everything I thought I defended.

The light did not fade. It changed. It poured into me, through the cracks the question had opened. It filled the dark, armored places where I kept my certainty. It showed them to me. Not as fortresses, but as tombs.

A sound escaped me. Not a word. A fracture.

The white began to recede from the world, but it did not leave my eyes. It stayed there, pooled behind my lids. When I forced them open, nothing changed. The world was gone.

Blackness. A perfect, seamless void.

I pushed myself up on my elbows. Grit pressed into my palms. I heard gasping, shouting—my men. Their voices were smeared, coming from the end of a long tunnel.

“Saul!” Micah’s hands were on my shoulders, shaking. “What happened? What did you see?”

I turned my head toward his voice. I saw nothing. Not shadow. Not shape. An absence so complete it was a presence in itself.

“I see nothing,” I whispered. The words were ash.

My hands came up, touched my face. My eyes were open. I could feel the blink, the scrape of lid on eyeball. But the blackness remained. The brilliant, world-ending light had burned out my sight and left this.

The voice echoed in the emptiness it had created.

Why do you persecute me?

The question was now inside the blackness with me. It was the only thing left.

“Lead me,” I said. My voice was not my own. It was a stranger’s, cracked and thin. “Lead me into Damascus.”

Micah’s hands tightened on my arms. He pulled me to my feet. My legs held, but they were someone else’s legs. The world under my sandals was an abstraction. The road, the city, the mission—all of it was gone. There was only the black void, and the question reverberating in its endless depths.

He began to guide me forward. One blind step. Then another.

The hands on my arms were anchors. Without them, I would have drifted away, a piece of ash on a thermal. Micah’s grip was all that tethered me to the earth. The ground under my feet was a rumor. I heard the scrape of our sandals, the crunch of grit. I felt the pressure against my soles. But it meant nothing. My body was a hollow reed, and the black wind blew through it.

Voices swirled around me, sharp with panic.

“—like a thunderclap, but no sound—”

“—threw him like a doll—”

“What did he say? What did he say to it?”

They were children’s voices, high and thin. These were the men who had followed me from Jerusalem. They had clutched their warrants like holy relics. Now they were clutching each other.

“Keep moving.” Micah’s voice, close to my ear. It was strained, a rope pulled taut. He was not speaking to me. He was herding them. “Elazar, take his other arm. Don’t stand there gawking.”

Another set of hands, hesitant, clammy, gripped my left elbow. Elazar. I could smell the sour fear on his breath. He pulled, out of sync with Micah. I stumbled. My knee hit a stone. The pain was a bright, specific point in the formless dark. I welcomed it. A landmark.

“Sorry, sorry,” Elazar whispered.

We moved again. The rhythm was wrong. Three-legged, staggering. I was a burden between them. My sandals dragged. The glorious, purposeful stride of the morning was a memory from someone else’s life.

The blackness was not empty. It was full of the afterimage. Not light, but its ghost. A negative of the world. When I turned my head, I could almost sense the bulk of the hills to my right, a greater density of nothing. The road was a slight hollow feeling, a worn path in the void. My mind painted pictures on the inside of my skull. The lizard’s ochre flash. The thyme bush. The cracks in the stones. Each memory was a tiny, desperate stitch in the unraveling fabric of reality.

But the voice was the only real thing.

It wasn’t repeating. It was residing. It had taken up space in the newly blasted chambers of my mind. It echoed not in sound, but in consequence. Every breath I took was an answer. A wrong one.

I had spoken to it. Who are you, Lord? The courtesies of the courtroom, automatic. My tongue had betrayed me. It had granted sovereignty to the accused.

The words were not a sound. They were a key. They turned in the lock of my life and every door swung open at once. Stephen’s face, not in death, but in the moment he looked up and said Lord, do not hold this sin against them. The peace there. It wasn’t stoicism. It wasn’t defiance. It was recognition. He had seen this same light. He had heard this same voice. And I, standing there holding the coats, had called it demonic inspiration.

My stomach clenched. A spasm, dry and violent. Nothing came up. There was nothing inside me but dust and the dregs of warm water.

“He’s retching,” Elazar said, his voice tight with disgust.

“He’s not,” Micah snapped. “Keep walking.”

I wasn’t. It was just my body trying to expel the understanding. It wouldn’t go. The truth was not in my stomach. It was in my bones. It had rewritten the marrow.

The sounds of the road began to filter back. The distant donkey again. The buzz of flies. The world was continuing. It was obscene. How could there be flies? How could there be donkeys? The axis of creation had just been sheared in two, and the insects hummed their same oblivious song.

My blindness was a separate terror. It was a prison built inside the ruins of my soul. I tried to force my eyes to work. I squeezed them shut until colors sparked in the pressure—meaningless swirls of ochre and violet—then opened them wide. The result was the same. A black so complete it had texture. A velvet shroud pressed against my face.

I am blind.

The thought arrived plain, factual. Then it unfolded.

I am blind on a road in the desert. I cannot read the Law. I cannot write a warrant. I cannot recognize a heretic’s face. I cannot find my way to the synagogue. I cannot see the stars I used to navigate by. I am a child. Worse than a child—a newborn, helpless, led by the hand.

A noise leaked from my throat. A whimper.

Micah’s hand on my arm tightened. “Not far now. I can see the cypress grove at the spring. The city gate is beyond it.”

His words were a map drawn in the air. I couldn’t see it. The cypress grove was a story. The city gate was a myth. My Damascus was gone. The Damascus of warrants and arrests and righteous purification had vanished in the light. What lay ahead was an unknown city, a maze of streets I would never navigate.

“The letters,” I croaked. My first word since the command to lead me.

“What?” Micah leaned closer.

“The letters. In my tunic.”

His hand hesitated, then moved. I felt the pressure of his fingers through the wool, probing, finding the oiled cloth packet. He drew it out. The slight weight of it leaving my chest felt like the removal of a vital organ.

“I have them.”

“Destroy them.”

A pause. The shuffling of our feet. The other men had gone quiet, listening. “Saul, this is the High Priest’s authority. Sealed.”

“Burn them.” The words were grit in my raw throat. “Tear them. Scatter the pieces. Now.”

It was not an order. It was a confession. The authority was counterfeit. The seal was ash. It had been burned away by a brighter fire.

I heard the rustle of cloth, the sharp, definitive tear of papyrus. Once, twice, again. A sound like a small death. Elazar made a faint, protesting sound. Micah grunted, a sound of effort, and then a handful of scraps, light as moth wings, brushed across my face and chest, caught in the wind of our movement. They fell away behind us.

The last tether snapped. I was adrift.

The mission was over. It had been over before it began. I had been hunting a ghost, and the ghost was alive, and it had been standing behind me the whole time, watching my frantic, murderous pursuit with a patience that now felt like the most terrifying thing of all.

We reached the spring. I knew it by the sudden coolness washing over my skin, by the smell of wet stone and damp earth, a shocking richness after the dust. By the sound of water—a quiet, chuckling melody over rock. A sound of profound indifference. The world did not care about my blindness. The water flowed.

They lowered me to sit on a flat stone. The chill seeped through my tunic. Micah guided my hands to the water’s edge. My fingers touched liquid cold. I cupped them, brought water to my face. It was a shock. It ran down my neck, into my tunic. I did it again. I couldn’t wash the light away. It was inside.

“Drink,” Micah said.

I drank from my hands. The water was sweet, alive. It made the dust in my throat into mud. I coughed.

The men were huddled nearby. I heard their whispers, no longer trying to hide.

“A stroke. The sun—”
“—epilepsy. My cousin had fits. Saw lights.”
“He spoke to it. He called it ‘Lord.’”
“What does that mean for us? We carried the warrants too. Are we… cursed?”

Their fear was a tangible thing, a sour odor in the clean air. They were not afraid for me. They were afraid of me. I was the contaminated one. I had touched the live wire of heaven and it had burned out my eyes, and now they were afraid the charge might jump to them.

I wanted to laugh. The sound would have been madness.

They didn’t understand. The curse was not in the punishment. The curse was in the answer. I am Jesus. The curse was the glorious, unbearable truth. They were still worried about Temple reprisal. I had just met the Temple’s founder, and he had called me a persecutor.

Micah settled beside me. His shoulder pressed against mine. A solid, warm pressure. He didn’t speak. He just sat. He was waiting for me to be Saul again. The Saul who knew what to do.

That Saul was dead. He had died on the stones back there. This was a new creature, blind and gasping in its birth sac of darkness.

“We cannot enter the city like this,” I said. My voice was flat. “Not with me like this.”

“We’ll find the house of Judas on Straight Street. As planned.”

“And say what? That the Pharisee with the letters is blind? That God struck him down on the road? They will think it a trick. Or a judgment. They will bolt. Or they will stone us as imposters.” The practical problems rose up, a pathetic distraction from the cosmic ones. They were the only problems my mind could grasp. Logistics. A broken tool.

Micah was silent for a long moment. “Then what would you have us do?”

I had nothing. No plan. No authority. No sight. The helplessness was a weight, pressing me into the stone. I was a leader who could not lead, a scholar who could not read, a zealot whose zeal had been turned inside out and shown to be the engine of his own ruin.

“I don’t know,” I whispered.

The admission left me hollow. It was the truest thing I had said.

We sat by the water. The men’s whispers died down. The only sounds were the spring and my own breathing, too loud in my ears. The black void was no longer just before my eyes. It was in my future. An endless corridor of nothing. No Law to guide me. No mission to define me. No enemy to fight. Just this dependent, stumbling existence.

The voice did not speak again. It didn’t need to. Its one question was a plough. It had torn up the field of my life, and now I was lying in the furrow, waiting for a seed I could not see and did not deserve.

Micah stood. His knees cracked. “The day is wasting. We need shelter before night. Can you walk?”

I held up a hand. He took it and pulled me to my feet. My legs held. They would do that much. They would carry this ruin toward a city he could not see, to a people he had come to destroy, for reasons he no longer understood.

“Yes,” I said.

One blind step. Then another.

The city announced itself long before we reached the walls. It started as a hum underneath the whisper of the spring, a vibration in the soles of my feet. Then the smells came, layer upon layer, a confusing assault. Woodsmoke, sharp and resinous. Animal dung, sweet and rotten. The metallic tang of a forge. Spices—cumin, coriander—carried on the warm breath of the city. The clean desert air curdled, thickened with the press of humanity.

My grip on Micah’s arm became a vise. Every sound was a potential collision. A cart rumbling by, the axle squealing at a pitch that sawed at my nerves. A merchant’s shout, too close. The bleat of a goat. Laughter from a doorway. The world was a chaotic symphony played by unseen musicians, and I was standing in the middle of the orchestra, waiting to be struck.

“Gate’s ahead,” Micah muttered. “Roman guards. Say nothing.”

The sounds changed. Our footsteps echoed briefly on stone, then were swallowed by a deeper, resonant din. We were under the arch. The air grew cooler, damp. It smelled of urine and old mortar. I heard the clink of armor, the bored mutter of Latin. A hand shot out and grabbed my chest, halting me. Rough wool, the smell of old sweat.

“State your business.” The voice was a gravelly baritone, inches from my face.

Micah answered, his tone carefully neutral, the voice of a man used to borders. “Merchants from Jerusalem. Seeking the house of Judas, a leatherworker, on Straight Street.”

A pause. The guard’s hand remained. “He’s blind?”

“A sun-sickness. It will pass.”

The guard grunted. I could feel his eyes on me. Assessing. A blind man was a beggar, a thief, a liability. I stared into the blackness where his face must be, my own face a rigid mask. Shame, hot and liquid, pooled in my gut. I, Saul of Tarsus, Pharisee, Roman citizen, was being inspected like a dubious piece of luggage.

The hand withdrew. “Move along.”

We moved. The noise exploded.

Damascus was a beast, and we were in its belly. The din was a physical thing, a wall of sound that pressed the air from my lungs. A hundred conversations in Aramaic, Greek, Nabatean, a babbling torrent. The hammering of a coppersmith. The shriek of a saw. Children shrieking as they darted through the crowd. Donkeys braying in protest. It was directionless, source-less. A sound from every angle, a world of activity I could not navigate or parse. I flinched at a sudden clatter to my right—a potter’s stall collapsing? Just a cart hitting a rut. My heart hammered against my ribs.

Micah and Elazar guided me, but it was a clumsy, harrowing dance. They pulled me left to avoid a jostling crowd, then right, then stopped short as something blocked our path. My shoulders bumped into unseen people. Muttered curses followed me. “Watch it, blind man!” The words were a lash. My face burned.

The smells intensified, a nauseating tapestry. Roasting meat from a street vendor. The pungent reek of a tannery. Perfume from a passing woman, cloying and floral. The stench of an open sewer. My empty stomach clenched. I breathed through my mouth, tasting the city’s foul breath.

This was the prize. This bustling, stinking, pagan-infested hive was the jewel I had come to cleanse. I had imagined marching through these streets with the authority of the Temple, the warrants a sword in my hand. I would have seen the fear in the faces of the followers. I would have noted the architecture, the alleyways, the safe houses. Now, I was a piece of driftwood in its torrent, blind and helpless. My great mission had been reduced to the desperate focus of not falling, not vomiting, not crying out.

We turned a corner. The noise muted slightly, the press of bodies lessened. The ground underfoot felt smoother, more even.

“Straight Street,” Micah said, his voice taut with relief.

I had asked it on the road. I had received an answer. But the answer had only deepened the question, turning it inward, making it about every breath I had ever taken. It hung now in the darkness, unanswered, unanswerable, the only signpost in a country where all the maps had burned.
