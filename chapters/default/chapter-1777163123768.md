By the autumn of 1944, the world war that had necessitated Executive Order 9066 had turned. The United States Navy had shattered Japanese carrier forces at the Battle of the Philippine Sea. American Marines were clawing their way across the Pacific at Peleliu. In Europe, Allied armies had broken out of Normandy and were pushing toward the German border. The existential panic of December 1941—the burning fleet at Pearl Harbor, the fear of invasion—had receded into a grim, confident momentum. The war was not over, but the tide was unmistakable. The "military necessity" that had been invoked with such urgency in February 1942 now existed in a different light.

That necessity had always been a specific construct. In the frantic weeks after Pearl Harbor, it was built from a compound of racial fear, political pressure, and the professional estimations of military leaders like General John L. DeWitt. His final report on the evacuation, submitted in 1943, became the government’s official rationale. It was a document that operated on a circular logic. "The very fact that no sabotage has taken place to date," DeWitt argued, was "a disturbing and confirming indication that such action will be taken." The absence of evidence was not proof of loyalty, but proof of a devious, patient plot. This logic transformed every Japanese American—the elderly Issei immigrant barred from citizenship, the Nisei teenager who had never seen Japan—into a potential saboteur awaiting a signal. Their very existence on the coast constituted a military threat.

The War Department and the Justice Department had built their legal defense around this report. When the cases of Fred Korematsu, Gordon Hirabayashi, and Minoru Yasui wound their way to the Supreme Court, the Solicitor General’s office presented DeWitt’s assessment as the unimpeachable judgment of the military on the scene. The court, historically reluctant to second-guess military commanders during wartime, was being asked to defer. The question before the justices was not whether the racial assumptions within the report were true, but whether they could be challenged at all in a time of war.

Yet the foundation of that report was already cracking internally. Other military intelligence officials, including those in the Office of Naval Intelligence, had consistently disagreed with DeWitt’s racial determinism. Even within the Western Defense Command, some of the general’s own staff doubted the factual basis for mass exclusion. These dissents were not presented to the Supreme Court. The government’s briefs and oral arguments streamlined the rationale, presenting a unified front of military imperative. The complex, contested reality behind Order 9066 was reduced to a simpler, more defensible proposition: in a time of peril, the military must be trusted to protect the nation, and its judgments, however harsh, must stand.

The Supreme Court chamber in 1944 was a space of solemn tradition, far removed from the dust of Manzanar or the frantic sell-off of farms in California’s Central Valley. The justices, however, were not immune to the atmosphere of the continuing war. They had already ruled on the curfew violations in Hirabayashi and Yasui, upholding the restrictions in narrow decisions that avoided the broader constitutional catastrophe of the exclusion orders themselves. Korematsu’s case forced the ultimate question. It was no longer about being on the street after dark. It was about the power to remove an entire class of citizens from their homes and imprison them behind barbed wire based solely on ancestry. The court’s docket now held the distilled legal essence of the camps.

Fred Korematsu was not a political activist by design. He was a twenty-three-year-old welder from Oakland, California, the son of Japanese immigrant parents who ran a flower nursery. In the spring of 1942, as the exclusion notices went up, he was in love with an Italian American woman, and he had just had facial surgery to repair a ruptured eardrum. The surgery left him with bruises and swelling, a fact that would later become legally significant. His family prepared to report to the Tanforan Assembly Center, the converted racetrack south of San Francisco. Korematsu did not want to go. He saw no reason why his American citizenship should be voided by his ancestry. With the help of his girlfriend, he assumed a false identity, claiming to be of Spanish and Hawaiian descent, changed his name to Clyde Sarah, and even underwent a clumsy attempt to alter his eye shape with surgery. He found work in a shipyard. His defiance was personal, rooted in a conviction that the order was simply wrong. “I was just living my life,” he would later say, “and the order came out and I thought, ‘This isn’t right.’”

His life as a fugitive lasted less than a month. On May 30, 1942, he was arrested on a street corner in San Leandro. The arresting officer noted his “Oriental” features despite his claimed identity. In his pocket was a membership card for the Boilermakers Union, Local 6. He was taken to the county jail, where he was visited by Ernest Besig, the director of the American Civil Liberties Union’s Northern California chapter. Besig was looking for a test case to challenge the exclusion orders on constitutional grounds. He found in Korematsu a reluctant but resolute partner. Korematsu agreed to become a plaintiff, but his decision was met with immediate isolation. His family, already behind barbed wire, feared his challenge would bring retaliation upon them all. The Japanese American Citizens League, the leading Nisei civic organization, initially opposed his case, believing the most prudent path was to demonstrate loyalty through compliance. Korematsu was ostracized by the very community he believed he was defending.

He was convicted in federal court in September 1942. The judge, Adolphus F. St. Sure, found him guilty of violating Public Law 503, which criminalized disobedience of the military orders. Korematsu was given five years’ probation and handed over to the military police. He was transported to Tanforan, where he was reunited with his family under the stigma of being a convict. They were later sent to the Central Utah Relocation Center at Topaz, a desolate camp in the desert. His legal appeal moved forward, but his daily reality was one of confinement and social shame. He spent his time in camp working as a carpenter’s assistant for nineteen dollars a month. The ACLU attorneys, Wayne Collins and Besig, framed his appeal around the gross violation of the Fifth Amendment’s guarantee of due process and the equal protection principles inherent in the Fourteenth Amendment. They argued the exclusion was a blatant racial discrimination, unsupported by any evidence of individual disloyalty. Korematsu’s swollen face from his surgery became a minor point in the record; his attorneys suggested the military might have mistaken his postoperative bruises for evidence of a beating, implying he was trying to disguise his race under duress. It was a confusing detail that did little to clarify the core, monumental injustice.

While his attorneys crafted the constitutional arguments, Korematsu endured the dust and isolation of Topaz. His act of defiance had cost him his freedom, his standing, and his simple life in Oakland. He was a prisoner twice over: first by the exclusion order, and second by his own conscience, which had compelled him to stand against it. He was not an idealogue, but a man who had made a singular, stubborn choice. As his case advanced toward the Supreme Court, the legal machinery treated “Korematsu” as a set of arguments and precedents. But at its heart was the uncomplicated belief of one welder that the government could not tell him he was not an American because of where his parents were born. That belief had landed him in a jail cell, then in a tarpaper barracks, and now it was the entire foundation of a case that would define the limits of wartime power for generations.

The atmosphere in the Supreme Court chamber on October 11 and 12, 1944, was thick with unspoken precedent. The war still raged, and the justices, as historian Peter Irons later documented from the conference notes and internal memos, were acutely aware of their role in a nation under arms. The government’s case, presented by Solicitor General Charles Fahy, was a masterpiece of strategic omission. He stood before the bench not to defend racism, but to defend deference. The heart of his argument, as recorded in the court’s transcript, was that the judgment of military commanders on the ground, specifically General DeWitt, was entitled to “great weight” and should not be overturned by “untutored” civilian courts. The exclusion was not a racial measure, Fahy contended, but a military one, necessitated by the impossibility of separating the loyal from the disloyal in time of crisis. He presented DeWitt’s final report as the definitive military justification, its circular logic—“the very fact that no sabotage has taken place”—now elevated to a strategic principle worthy of constitutional sanction.

The government’s briefs, however, contained a critical void. Fahy did not inform the court that the Justice Department’s own lawyers had grave doubts about DeWitt’s report. In fact, as internal memos unearthed decades later would prove, officials in the Solicitor General’s office knew the report was riddled with “willful historical falsehoods” and racist assertions. They had even prepared a footnote for their brief acknowledging that some assertions in DeWitt’s report were inaccurate, but Fahy ordered it removed. The military necessity presented to the justices was a sanitized, streamlined version, stripped of its internal dissent. The Office of Naval Intelligence’s consistent objections, the FBI’s own assessments finding no evidence of a coordinated threat, the fact that not a single Japanese American had been charged with espionage—none of this countervailing intelligence was placed before the court. The justices were given a curated reality, one where military judgment appeared unanimous, rational, and purely strategic.

Arguing for Fred Korematsu was Wayne Collins, a pugnacious San Francisco attorney whose style contrasted sharply with Fahy’s polished deference. Collins, in his oral argument and briefs, attacked the foundation head-on. He insisted the exclusion was “a clear case of racial discrimination,” not a legitimate military action. He pointed to the incoherence of the orders: if the danger was espionage, why were Germans and Italians not subjected to mass exclusion? Why were Japanese Americans removed from the coast but then allowed to leave the camps and resettle in Chicago or New York, areas presumably just as vulnerable to sabotage? The logic, Collins argued, collapsed under its own weight; it was not about security, but about race. He challenged the court to look past the label of “military necessity” and see the action for what it was: the imprisonment of citizens without charge, trial, or evidence of individual guilt, a violation of the most basic due process guarantees of the Fifth Amendment.

The tension in the chamber was not in heated exchanges, but in the profound gravity of the choice. The justices, as their later conference discussions revealed, were deeply divided. Some, like Felix Frankfurter, believed fervently in judicial restraint during wartime, fearing that to overturn a military order would undermine the war effort. Others, like Frank Murphy and Robert Jackson, were privately appalled by the racial underpinnings of the policy. Murphy, a former U.S. Attorney General, had seen the intelligence reports. He knew the case was built on a false premise. But the pressure to present a unified front, to not embarrass the government or the military in the midst of a two-front war, exerted a powerful force. The legal question morphed into a political and existential one: could the Supreme Court, in the autumn of 1944, tell the nation that its treatment of 120,000 people had been not only cruel, but unconstitutional? The cost of such a ruling, in the midst of the bloody island-hopping campaign and the push into Germany, seemed incalculable to some on the bench.

The deliberation was cloaked in secrecy, but the fault lines were clear. The government had successfully framed the case not as a test of racial equality, but as a test of institutional competence. The court was not being asked to judge Japanese Americans, but to judge its own right to judge the military. By hiding the flawed and racist genesis of DeWitt’s report, Fahy had made the case a referendum on judicial deference. To rule for Korematsu would be to second-guess the War Department in the middle of a total war—a step the majority of the court, steeped in tradition and mindful of the headlines from Iwo Jima, was unprepared to take. The constitutional catastrophe was not in the act of exclusion itself, but in the court’s imminent decision to legitimize it by refusing to look behind the government’s fabricated curtain of necessity. The scales were tipping, not on the weight of evidence, but on the weight of uniformed authority.

The Supreme Court announced its decision in Korematsu v. United States on December 18, 1944. The vote was 6 to 3. Justice Hugo Black, a former senator from Alabama and a committed New Dealer, delivered the opinion of the Court. His reasoning, as preserved in the official U.S. Reports, was a model of strained deference. Black began by acknowledging the “hardship” of exclusion, calling it a “grave injustice.” But he swiftly pivoted to the core legal principle: “hardships are part of war.” The pressing question, he wrote, was not whether Korematsu suffered, but whether the military authorities had reasonable grounds for their actions. The Court, Black asserted, could not substitute its judgment for that of the military commanders on the scene during a time of “emergency and peril.”

Black’s opinion accepted the government’s framing without scrutiny. He cited General DeWitt’s final report as the definitive military justification, adopting its logic that the inability to separate loyal from disloyal Japanese Americans constituted a military imperative. “We cannot—by availing ourselves of the calm perspective of hindsight—now say that at that time these actions were unjustified,” Black wrote. The exclusion was not rooted in racism, he contended, but in the fact that “residing in the area were a large number of persons of Japanese ancestry whose loyalty to this country was questionable.” The opinion treated citizenship as a conditional status, voidable by group suspicion. Crucially, Black accepted the government’s argument at face value, never probing the disputed facts behind DeWitt’s report. The Court’s role, he implied, was to validate, not investigate. The legal standard became one of possibility, not proof. Because espionage could have occurred, because loyalty could not be immediately ascertained, the wholesale imprisonment of an ethnic group was deemed a constitutional exercise of war powers.

The decision was narrow in a technical sense. Black was careful to state the Court was not ruling on the constitutionality of the entire internment program, only on the validity of the exclusion order Korematsu had disobeyed. This legal hairsplitting was a hollow distinction for the 120,000 people behind wire. In practice, the ruling provided a constitutional shield for the entire system of removal and detention. It established a precedent that in times of war, the judiciary would grant the military and the executive branch nearly unlimited latitude when they invoked “necessity,” even when that necessity was built on racial categorization.

The three dissents that day were not mere objections; they were moral indictments. The most searing came from Justice Frank Murphy, a former U.S. Attorney General and prosecutor. Murphy had access to more intelligence than his colleagues and had been privately disgusted by the case. His dissent, filed concurrently with Black’s opinion, pulled no punches. He began by rejecting the majority’s deference as “a farce.” The exclusion, he wrote, fell “into the ugly abyss of racism.” He dismantled DeWitt’s report point by point, labeling its now-infamous circular logic—“the very fact that no sabotage has taken place”—as evidence of “reasoning reminiscent of the abhorrent and despicable treatment of minority groups by the dictatorial tyrannies which this nation is now pledged to destroy.” Murphy drew a direct line from the internment to the ideologies America was fighting against, calling it “the legalization of racism” and noting that racial discrimination had “no justifiable part whatever in our democratic way of life.”

Murphy’s dissent did not stop at legal critique. He invoked the broader historical stain. “All residents of this nation are kin in some way by blood or culture to a foreign land,” he wrote, arguing that the ruling set a dangerous precedent that could be used against any ethnic group in a future panic. His language was unprecedented in its bluntness for a Supreme Court opinion. He concluded that the exclusion order was “utterly revolting among a free people,” and had been justified by “an accumulation of much of the misinformation, half-truths and insinuations that for years have been directed against Japanese Americans by people with racial and economic prejudices.”

Justice Robert H. Jackson’s dissent took a different, equally damning tack. Jackson, who would soon leave the Court to serve as chief prosecutor at the Nuremberg trials, focused on the precedent set. He warned that the majority’s opinion, by validating a racial distinction, laid down “a loaded weapon” ready for the hand of any authority that could bring forward a “plausible claim of urgent need.” The principle of racial guilt, once embedded in law, would persist long after the wartime emergency had faded. Justice Owen J. Roberts, in a shorter dissent, called the internment a case of “clear violation of Constitutional rights,” arguing that Korematsu was being punished for being present in a location where it was a crime for him to be, a “manifest injustice.”

The impact of the decision was immediate and profound. For the War Relocation Authority and the Roosevelt administration, it was a vindication, however bitter. The constitutional challenge to their most drastic domestic war measure had been defeated. For Fred Korematsu, it meant his conviction was affirmed. He remained a felon. For the tens of thousands still in the camps, the ruling was a final blow, a signal that their own government’s highest court saw their imprisonment as a legitimate, if unfortunate, act of state.

Yet the decision’s fractured nature—the 6-3 split and the ferocity of the dissents—prevented it from being a clean, historical endorsement. Murphy’s dissent, in particular, lodged a permanent protest in the official record. It served as a contemporaneous refutation, ensuring that future generations reading Korematsu would not see a unanimous court, but a deeply divided one, with three justices naming the action for what it was: racism dressed in the uniform of military necessity. The ruling did not settle the constitutional question so much as freeze it in a state of disgraceful tension. It established a precedent that was technically binding but morally bankrupt, a “loaded weapon” indeed, left on the shelf of American jurisprudence, waiting.

The publication of the decision was a quiet event. There were no headlines declaring a great victory for liberty; the nation’s front pages were dominated by the Battle of the Bulge, which had erupted in Europe two days earlier. The legal sanction for the camps was buried in the war news, a final, bureaucratic step in a process that had begun with panic and racial fear. The Supreme Court had spoken. It had chosen deference over scrutiny, authority over equity, and in doing so, it had woven the thread of racial exclusion into the fabric of American constitutional law during crisis. The machinery of internment, now blessed by the highest court, would continue to operate for months more, but its legal foundation, however stained, was secure. The exception had been ratified.

The immediate consequence of the Korematsu ruling was not a change in policy, but a hardening of its legal finality. For the men, women, and children still confined at Topaz, Heart Mountain, and Tule Lake, the Supreme Court’s decision was a psychological extinguishment of hope. Their incarceration was now constitutional. The government had not merely imprisoned them; the nation’s highest court had declared it had the right to do so. As historian Roger Daniels noted in his analysis of the camp newspapers, the announcement was met with a bitter, resigned silence. The legal battle was over. The only path out was the war’s end or individual compliance with a loyalty questionnaire many found impossible to answer with dignity. Fred Korematsu’s personal stigma was now a national one, etched into the U.S. Reports. He remained a convicted felon, a status that would dog him for decades, affecting his ability to find work.

Legally, the decision did more than resolve a single case. It embedded a principle into the constitutional framework for emergencies: military or executive assertions of necessity, especially when framed in terms of time pressure and logistical impossibility, could override the bedrock guarantees of due process and equal protection. Justice Black’s opinion created a roadmap for future overreach. By accepting the government’s argument that it was impossible to separate the loyal from the disloyal, the Court effectively ratified the concept of group guilt based on ancestry. The precedent stated that in times of peril, the Constitution could bend to allow for racial categorization as a tool of security. This was not a side effect of the ruling; it was its core holding. As Justice Jackson had warned, the “loaded weapon” was now on the shelf, its mechanism approved.

The social ramifications unfolded in layers. For many white Americans, particularly on the West Coast, the Court’s sanction provided a post-hoc moral justification for the prejudice that had driven the exclusion movement. The Los Angeles Times, editorializing after the decision, framed it as a vindication of “military prudence” and a rejection of “legalistic quibbles.” The racial calculus that had justified taking homes, farms, and businesses was now cloaked in judicial imprimatur. For Japanese Americans, the ruling was a traumatic lesson in the fragility of their citizenship. The Issei, barred from naturalization by race, saw their children’s birthright nullified by the same logic. The Nisei, like Korematsu, learned that their constitutional promises were conditional, contingent on the absence of a crisis where their ethnicity could be deemed a threat. This lesson in contingent citizenship, documented in countless post-war oral histories and memoirs, created a legacy of silence and assimilation within the community, a strategic avoidance of drawing attention that lasted for generations.

The “dangerous precedent” was not an abstract concern. In the short term, Korematsu directly facilitated the continuation of the camps. The War Relocation Authority used the decision to quash remaining internal dissent and legal challenges. More broadly, it provided a citation for any future invocation of extreme executive power. Government lawyers in subsequent decades would reference Korematsu not for its facts, but for its sweeping deference. During the Cold War, attorneys drafting security provisions for loyalty programs and ideological exclusions had the case at their disposal. In the immediate aftermath of the September 11 attacks, Korematsu was cited in legal memos debating the scope of presidential authority to detain individuals without trial. The case’s value as a precedent lay precisely in its extreme facts; if that was constitutional, then lesser intrusions in future emergencies surely could be.

Yet the decision’s longevity was paradoxically undermined by the very dissents that failed to stop it. Justice Murphy’s furious opinion, labeling the exclusion “the legalization of racism,” served as a permanent, official rebuke within the Court’s own annals. It ensured the decision would forever be taught in law schools as a cautionary tale, a negative star by which to navigate. Over time, as the fabricated military necessity was exposed by historians like Peter Irons through the Freedom of Information Act, and as the nation underwent a fitful reckoning with the internment, Korematsu’s authority eroded. It was never formally overturned, but in subsequent rulings, the Supreme Court distanced itself from its reasoning, treating it as a discredited relic of a different time. In 2018, Chief Justice John Roberts, writing for the majority in Trump v. Hawaii, explicitly repudiated Korematsu, stating it was “gravely wrong the day it was decided.” This condemnation, however, came in a ruling that upheld a different executive security order based on broad deference to presidential authority. The ghost of Black’s reasoning—deference in times of perceived crisis—remained, even as the specific racial disgrace of Korematsu was rejected.

The final, cruel irony of the decision lay in its timing. The Court ruled in December 1944. The following month, the War Department announced it would begin rescinding the exclusion orders, ending the mass incarceration program. The military necessity had evaporated, not because of a court order, but because the tide of the war had turned. The legal sanction arrived just as the practical reason for it was disappearing. This underscored the true function of the ruling: it was less a guide for action than a retroactive shield for an action nearly complete. It served to constitutionalize a historical wrong, ensuring that the episode would leave not just a moral scar, but a legal one. The stain on American jurisprudence was set. The Korematsu precedent stands as a monument to a specific failure: the failure of the judiciary to look behind a government-curated facade during a crisis, and its willingness to sanctify racial fear with the cloak of law. It proved that the Constitution’s most vital protections could be suspended not only by military order or public panic, but by nine justices in a quiet chamber, choosing institutional caution over moral clarity. The loaded weapon remained, its existence a standing invitation for a future authority to pick it up, polish the old rationale, and take aim again.
