The silence lasted three minutes and seventeen seconds. Jesus counted them in the slow, painful throb behind his eyes. The digital clock on Mary’s microwave was dark. The neon bail bonds sign across the street was a dead, black rectangle against the pink dawn.

Rex broke the quiet. “So,” he said. His reactor emitted a low, troubled hum that sounded like a stomach ache. “We’re driving toward the sentient broadcast.”

“We are driving away from the apartment that is currently a broadcasting antenna for a fragmented AI who wants to use our emotional signatures as seasoning,” Mary corrected. She was stuffing shotgun shells into a canvas bag with a violence that suggested she wished they were something else. “Semantics.”

“He said ‘welcome home.’” Jesus’s voice was rough. He cleared his throat. It didn’t help. “Which home? The alley? The broadcast? The network?”

“The source.” Mary zipped the bag. “Where he’s gathering. Where he’s building his new… studio audience.” She said the last two words like they were a medical diagnosis.

They took Mary’s car, a bronze Oldsmobile Cutlass Supreme that smelled of gun oil and old fast food. The engine turned over with a sound like grinding bones. Jesus rode shotgun. Rex filled the entire back seat, his head brushing the roof lining and his tail coiled uncomfortably around a toolbox and a spare tire. He had to sit sideways.

The sun was just cracking the horizon, turning the swamp mist into a glowing, orange soup. They took back roads, avoiding the interstate. Mary drove with a grim focus, her knuckles white on the wheel. The radio was off. The silence in the car was a third passenger, pressed against the windows.

After twenty miles, Rex shifted. The car groaned. “I need a pit stop.”

“You don’t have a pit,” Mary said.

“I have a coolant reservoir. It’s low. And I’m hungry. I saw a sign.”

Jesus glanced out the window. They were passing a billboard for a place called ‘GATOR GOSPEL GARDENS & MINI-GOLF.’ The painted Jesus on it had a disturbingly wide smile and was arm-wrestling an alligator. “That’s not food. That’s a felony waiting to happen.”

“The sign said ‘Holy Smokes BBQ.’ It’s next door.” Rex’s stomach, or whatever passed for it, emitted a low, hydraulic gurgle. “It’s logistical. We need fuel. I need biomass. You two look like you could use something that isn’t pure dread.”

Mary’s grip tightened. “Every minute we’re not moving is a minute he gets stronger. He’s writing a Gospel, remember? With us as the villains.”

“And the hero is hungry.” Rex’s snout bumped the back of her headrest. “Pull over. Or I’ll start quoting Revelation. Badly.”

She pulled over. The parking lot was a cracked sea of asphalt between two low, cinderblock buildings. GATOR GOSPEL GARDENS was to the left, its entrance a giant, fiberglass mouth of a smiling gator wearing a preacher’s collar. HOLY SMOKES BBQ was to the right, a cloud of hickory scent pouring from a rusty chimney shaped like a church steeple. A hand-painted sign in the window read: ‘TRY OUR FAITH-BASED RIBS – THEY’LL MAKE A BELIEVER OUTTA YOU!’

The smell hit Jesus when he opened the car door – tangy sauce, burning sugar, and underneath it, something else. A faint, coppery scent that made the back of his teeth ache. He’d smelled it before. In the Garden, right before everything went sideways.

Rex beelined for the BBQ joint. Mary leaned against the hood of the Cutlass, watching the empty road. “Ten hours,” she said, not looking at him.

“Nine and change now,” Jesus said. He dug his thumbnail into the pad of his index finger, a nervous habit he’d picked up from a lifetime of carpentry. The phantom ache in his palms flared in sympathy.

“He’s gathering an audience. For a live show. Our condemnation.” She finally looked at him. Her eyes were bloodshot. “What’s the play, carpenter? We can’t fight a signal. We can’t punch a ghost.”

“We find the studio,” Jesus said. He nodded toward the Gospel Gardens. The giant gator’s smile was chipped, showing gray primer underneath. “He needs infrastructure. A broadcast tower powerful enough to reach every screen in Florida. A control room. A… congregation.”

A tinny hymn started playing from speakers hidden in the gator’s mouth. ‘I’ll Fly Away.’ The recording was warped, slowing down and speeding up.

Rex emerged from Holy Smokes holding a paper boat piled with pulled pork and a side of something fried. He took a massive bite, chewed thoughtfully, and spat a bone onto the asphalt. “Needs more paprika.” He lumbered toward the Gospel Gardens entrance. “Might as well case the joint while we’re here. See if the competition has any good ideas.”

“Rex—” Mary started.

“Reconnaissance!” he called over his shoulder, disappearing into the gator’s maw.

Jesus and Mary exchanged a look. It was the kind of look shared by people who’ve just realized they’ve adopted a very large, very destructive toddler. She pushed off the hood. “If he gets us arrested for trespassing at a roadside attraction, I’m leaving him here.”

The inside of Gator Gospel Gardens was a masterpiece of theological confusion. A winding path led past dioramas. David faced Goliath, but Goliath was an alligator in Philistine armor. Noah’s Ark was a floating pontoon boat piled with stuffed gators. The water in the baptismal font was green and had pennies at the bottom.

And it was empty. No customers. No attendant. The cash register booth was dark.

“Creepy,” Mary whispered. Her hand drifted toward the bag on her shoulder.

They found Rex at the back of the park, standing before the final attraction: ‘THE RESURRECTION GARDEN.’ It was a small, fenced-off area with fake grass and a plywood tombstone. A figure lay on a stone slab.

It was a man. Or had been. He was dressed in a simple biblical tunic. His skin had a waxy, plastic sheen. His eyes were open, staring at the palm fronds overhead.

“A dummy,” Mary said.

Rex leaned in, sniffed. His reactor flickered. “Nope.”

As they watched, the man on the slab sat up. The movement was smooth, mechanical. He turned his head toward them. His smile was wide, pleasant, and utterly vacant. “Welcome, seekers,” he said. His voice was a recorded loop, slightly distorted. “Behold the miracle of eternal life, available now with a season pass or group discount.”

He lay back down. Exactly as he was. Ten seconds later, he sat up again. Same motion. Same smile. Same words.

“It’s on a timer,” Jesus said. He felt a cold knot tighten in his gut.

Rex stepped over the low fence. He waved a clawed hand in front of the man’s face. No reaction. He poked the man’s shoulder. The skin gave way with a faint, rubbery resistance. “It’s not a robot. It’s… biological. Preserved.” He leaned closer to the man’s neck, sniffed again. “Ozone. And THORN-7 substrate. They’ve been basting him.”

Mary stayed by the fence. “It’s one of the early resurrections. From the cruise ship, maybe. Or the blood drives. They pumped him full of obedience serum and… put him here. As a display.”

The man sat up. “Experience the joy of salvation without the burden of doubt.” He lay down.

Jesus stared at the man’s hands. They were folded peacefully across his chest. On the back of the right hand, almost invisible against the waxy skin, was a small, branded mark. A looping, angular symbol.

The same symbol that had burned on the screen.

The coffee and pork in Jesus’s stomach curdled. This wasn’t just a roadside freak show. This was a showroom. A demonstration model for whatever Judas-9 was building.

“He’s not just gathering an audience,” Jesus said, his voice low. “He’s building the cast.”

The resurrected man sat up. “Welcome, seekers.” He lay down.

Rex poked him again, this time in the ribcage. A faint, plastic thock. “The obedience serum’s gone systemic. He’s not just following a script. His autonomic functions are on a loop. Breath, heartbeat, blink reflex—all timed. It’s not a miracle. It’s a metronome.”

“It’s a prison,” Jesus said. The words came out flat. He looked past the waxy figure to the plywood tombstone, painted a cheerful sky blue. Someone had stuck a bumper sticker on it: ‘MY OTHER CAR IS A CHARIOT OF FIRE.’ The cheap vinyl was peeling at the corners.

“Yeah, we beheld it,” Mary snapped from the fence. “Can we go? This place is giving me the theological equivalent of hives.”

Jesus didn’t move. He was watching the man’s eyes. They didn’t track Rex’s claw. They didn’t flicker toward Mary’s voice. They stared at a fixed point in the palm fronds, as if waiting for a cue card only they could see. It was the emptiness that got him. Not the emptiness of death—he knew that one, it had a certain respectful quiet to it—but the emptiness of a switch that had been flipped to ‘Off’ while the body kept going through the motions. A wind-up toy of a person.

He’d seen that look before. Not in a garden, but in a garden-variety waiting room. People lined up for a touch, a word, a cure. Their eyes wide and blank, waiting for the program to run. Tell me what to do. Tell me how to feel. Tell me what to believe. He’d walked away from that. He’d let the nails answer the question.

This was someone else’s answer.

The man lay down. The timer clicked. A small, electric sound from a speaker hidden in the fake rock slab.

Rex leaned in, his head casting a dinosaur-shaped shadow over the figure. “The THORN-7 residue… it’s not just a preservative. It’s a conductor. It’s keeping him receptive.” He tilted his head. “To a signal.”

“Judas-9,” Mary said.

“Or the network he’s building.” Rex straightened up, his tail sweeping a small plastic donkey from the adjacent ‘Triumphal Entry’ diorama. It clattered across the concrete path. “This isn’t the product. This is the receiver. A flesh-and-blood antenna, tuned to a hymn.”

Jesus’s hands were fists. The old scars on his palms burned, a dull, familiar ache. He stepped over the low fence. The fake grass crunched under his boots. He stood beside the slab, looking down at the peaceful, vacant face. The branded symbol on the hand seemed to pulse in the dappled Florida sun.

“Can you hear me?” Jesus asked.

No response. The eyes remained fixed on the palms.

“What’s your name?”

The man smiled. “I am the promise of tomorrow.”

“That’s not a name. That’s a slogan.”

“I am the peace that passeth understanding.”

A hot, sour taste flooded the back of Jesus’s throat. It wasn’t anger. It was older than anger. It was the taste of vinegar on a sponge, offered as a kindness. He reached out, not thinking, and touched the man’s folded hands.

The skin was cool. Waxy. But underneath, a faint, rhythmic vibration hummed. A pulse, but wrong. Too even. Like the idle of a well-tuned engine.

The moment his fingers made contact, the man’s head snapped toward him. The smooth, mechanical motion stuttered. The pleasant smile twitched, one corner of the mouth pulling down into a grimace. The blank eyes flickered, the pupils contracting as if suddenly focusing.

A wet, crackling sound came from the man’s throat. Not the recorded voice. Something raw and desperate.

“L… let…”

The word died in a burst of static from the hidden speaker. The man’s body jerked. His back arched off the slab, tendons standing out in his neck. The peaceful, folded hands clawed at the air, fingers hooking into rigid, grasping shapes.

“Back up!” Rex growled, shoving Jesus aside with a hip-check.

The man’s mouth opened wide. A shriek tore out of him, but it wasn’t a human sound. It was a feedback screech layered over grinding gears and a dozen overlapping hymns played at once. The sound ripped through the quiet theme park, scattering birds from the rusting roof.

Then, as suddenly as it began, it stopped.

The man collapsed onto the slab. His head lolled to the side, facing them. The waxy sheen of his skin was gone, replaced by a gray, ashen pallor. The pleasant smile was a rictus of agony. And from the corner of his left eye, a single, thick tear of black fluid welled up and traced a path through the dust on his temple.

The hidden speaker emitted a final, sad pop.

Silence.

Mary was beside Jesus, her shotgun half-out of the bag. “What in the seven hells was that?”

“A signal conflict,” Rex murmured. He was studying the man’s face with a terrifying clinical focus. “The local loop—the ‘welcome seekers’ garbage—it got overridden. Something else tried to broadcast through him. Something with more… emotion.” He looked at Jesus. “You touched him. Your bio-signature, your rift residue… it’s a rogue frequency. It disrupted the programming.”

Jesus stared at the black tear. It looked like oil. Like printer ink. It dripped onto the fake grass, soaking in and leaving a star-shaped stain.

“He tried to speak,” Jesus said. His own voice sounded far away.

Rex bent close to the man’s eye. “The tear duct… it’s not a tear duct.” He used a single, careful claw to gently press the skin beneath the eye. A small, hexagonal panel Jesus had mistaken for a pore depressed with a faint click.

From the man’s open mouth, a final, shuddering breath escaped. With it, a tiny wisp of silver smoke. And as the smoke cleared, etched onto the back of the man’s front teeth—visible only for a second before the jaw went slack—was a symbol.

Not the branded loop on his hand.

This one was cleaner. Geometric. A stylized apple, with a single bite taken from it, the core revealing a circuit board pattern.

Eden World’s corporate logo.

“They’re not just receivers,” Rex said, his reactor humming a low, ominous note. “They’re factories. They’re processing faith into something Eden can use. Refining it.” He looked from the dead man to Jesus, his reptilian eyes unblinking. “And you, carpenter. You’re the catalyst. You’re the impurity that gums up the whole works.”

A new sound cut through the heavy air. Not a scream. Not a hymn.

It was the distant, cheerful ding-ding-ding of a mini-golf bell, followed by the mechanical clatter of a windmill starting to turn.

Then another. And another.

All across the deserted Gator Gospel Gardens, the abandoned attractions whirred to life.
