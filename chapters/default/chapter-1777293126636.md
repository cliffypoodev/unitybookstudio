The first thing you notice about Beverly is her left ear. It's cocked at a forty-five-degree angle, which—after three months as her grooming assistant—you've learned means she's either contemplating existential dread or remembering that time she got second billing behind a capybara in Zoo Heist 2: This Time It's Personal.

The second thing you notice is the alpaca smoothie dripping down the side of the "Temple of Tranquil Transcendence," which used to be Soundstage 4 before the cult moved in. The smoothie is neon green and smells like expired yogurt blended with regret. Beverly licks a stray droplet off the faux-marble altar, which is actually a repurposed Pirates of the Caribbean prop. Her other ear twitches. This means she's about to do something stupid.

"Beverly," you say, holding out a brush like it's a crucifix and she's a demon. "We talked about this. No more Die Hard reenactments."

She blinks at you, slow and deliberate, the way Liam Neeson blinks right before he threatens a man's entire bloodline. Then she spits a wad of half-chewed hay onto your shoe. It lands with the precision of a sniper's bullet.

The cultists—or as you've privately dubbed them, the Robe People—circle around her, murmuring. Their leader, a guy who looks like if a yoga instructor and a tax auditor had a baby, strokes his chin. "Fascinating. The creature is clearly channeling the divine through celluloid."

Beverly rolls her shoulders back, a move you recognize from her brief but intense method-acting phase after watching The Godfather. She's not a llama right now. She's Al Pacino.

"You're enabling her," you hiss at Robe Guy.

"Ah, but what is her?" he says, eyes glinting. "A mere beast? Or a vessel for the sacred chaos of cinema?"

Beverly chooses that moment to kick over a "holy" incense holder, which clatters to the ground, revealing the Warner Bros. logo stamped on the bottom.

By noon, she's recruited the temple pigeons as her "special ops unit." You watch, numb, as she herds them into formation using a combination of strategic spitting and what you swear is the Mission: Impossible theme hummed through her nostrils.

One of the Robe People approaches you, clutching a clipboard. "Subject exhibits advanced problem-solving skills," he mutters. "Note the tactical deployment of birdseed as a distraction."

You squint at his clipboard. The header reads Project Method Llama: Phase 3.

"Wait," you say. "Are you—are you studying her?"

He freezes. Beverly freezes. Even the pigeons freeze, mid-coo.

Robe Guy clears his throat. "We prefer the term documenting artistic emergence."

Beverly's ears snap upright. This is her oh shit position. You've seen it twice before: once when the director of Zoo Heist 2 suggested she "try being less llama-like," and again when she realized the craft services table was out of kale.

She bolts.

You find her twenty minutes later, standing in front of the Jurassic Park volcano prop the cult has dubbed "The Portal to Enlightenment." She's draped in a makeshift cape fashioned from a "No Parking" sign and what might be a stolen monk's robe.

"The final act begins," she declares, in the tone of someone who has watched too many DVD commentaries.

The Robe People are filming now. One of them whispers, "Do you think she's actually sentient, or just really good at mimicking Heat?"

Beverly exhales through her nose, a perfect imitation of Pacino's Scent of a Woman sigh. Then she charges the volcano.

It collapses instantly, because it's made of papier-mâché and the collective disappointment of every aspiring actor in LA. Beverly emerges from the wreckage, covered in glitter and what you hope is fake lava. She strikes a pose. The Robe People burst into applause.

You sink to your knees. "I need a new job."

Beverly trots over and nudges your shoulder with her snout. Her ear flicks—the you're my best friend and I won't apologize angle.

You sigh. "Fine. But if you start quoting Taxi Driver, I'm out."

You're so screwed.

"It's in the old server room," he said. "The one they decommissioned after the power surge."

He swiped his card. The door hissed open.

The room was dark except for the emergency strips along the floor. In the center, a single server rack hummed. Its indicator lights glowed a steady, deep red. That was wrong. The power should have been cut months ago.

"We ran diagnostics," Chen said. "It's not connected to the main grid. It's drawing from a backup cell we didn't know was there. And it's… processing something."

Mara approached. The rack was cold to the touch. Frost patterned the metal edges. She crouched, peered at the status panel. Lines of code scrolled too fast to read.

"What's it processing?"

"We don't know. The encryption's military-grade. But it's generating heat signatures. False ones. All over the building." Chen shifted his weight. "It made our motion sensors think there were twelve people in here an hour ago. There were two."

"Just the old AI research team. They were all… re-assigned."

Back at her desk, Mara pulled the archived personnel files. The AI research team: Dr. Aris Thorne, lead. Dr. Linh Vo, neural networks. Elias Kote, hardware. Three names. Three profiles marked "TERMINATED."

Not fired. Terminated.
