The leather was dry. It had sat too long in the corner, rolled tight, and now it resisted the needle. It puckered. I pulled the thread tight and heard the tiny rip of a stitch tearing through brittle hide. I set the awl down. The afternoon sun cut a hot bar across the workbench, lighting motes of dust that moved like underwater things in the thick, still air.

Three years of dust.

Three years of this: the smell of cured goat, the ache between my shoulders, the silence of a house where no one came. My father had been dead two winters now. My sister was married and gone to Mopsuestia. The house was a shell. I was the hermit crab inside, scuttling from bench to courtyard to pallet, my world this room, this street, the well at the corner. The grand debates of Jerusalem, the flash of light on the Damascus road—they were stories told about another man. A ghost. I was the hollow place the ghost had left behind.

I picked up the mallet. Tapped a rivet into a seam. Tap. The sound was small and stupid. A bird answered from the roof, a sharp, liquid trill. I envied it. It had a purpose. It sang to claim its space, to find a mate, to warn of cats. My singing was this: tap, tap, stitch, rip.

The list from Silas was in the cedar box. I hadn’t looked at it since the night he left. Lucius. Manaen. The names were hooks. I could feel them, even from across the room, tugging at a part of me I’d walled off. What are you doing here? His question lived in the walls now. It whispered in the scrape of my knife, in the sigh of the leather as I bent it.

I wasn’t praying. Prayer required words, and my words had long since turned to gravel in my throat. Whatever passed between me and God was this mute, dogged labor. This making of useful things for people who never asked the maker’s name. A tent for a spice merchant. A harness for a carter. Small, temporary shelters for a world passing through.

The street outside was quiet. The hour of sunstroke, when sensible men napped. I heard the slap of sandals on stone, too brisk for the heat. They stopped outside my door.

My heart did a slow, thick roll in my chest. No one came at this hour. Debt collectors came in the morning. Boys delivering messages ran. These footsteps were deliberate. A man considering a door.

I kept my head down. The rivet was crooked. I pried it out with the awl’s point, my fingers leaving damp prints on the brass.

A shadow filled the grated window, blocking the sun-bar. Then it moved away. A knock. Not the impatient rap of a neighbor, but three measured blows. Wood on wood.

I didn’t move. The dust settled. The bird stopped singing.

The latch lifted. The door, warped by summer, groaned inward. A figure stood silhouetted in the white glare of the street, tall, broad-shouldered. He stepped inside, bringing with him the smell of road dust and sweat and a faint, familiar scent—olive wood and myrrh. The scent of the Jerusalem meeting rooms.

He blinked, adjusting to the dimness. His gaze moved around the benches, the rolls of leather, the simple tools. It finally landed on me, hunched on my stool, the mallet frozen in my hand.

It was Barnabas.

Not a vision. Not a memory. Flesh. His beard was greyer, cut shorter. Lines around his eyes I didn’t remember. His tunic was travel-stained, the hem frayed. He held a walking staff, its tip worn smooth.

He looked at me, and his face did a slow, painful thing. It wasn’t a smile. It was a collapse of some held tension, a letting-go of a breath held for years. His eyes were wet. He didn’t blink it away.

“Saul,” he said.

My name. From his mouth. It was a physical blow. It took the air from the room. I hadn’t heard it spoken in so long—not as a curse, not as a title, just as a name. The sound of it was a key turning in a lock I’d forgotten was there.

I couldn’t speak. My throat closed. I stood, my knees cracking, the mallet falling from my hand to thump on the dirt floor. Dust puffed up around our feet.

He didn’t move toward me. He just stood there, his staff planted between us like a standard. His eyes traveled over my face, my work-roughened hands, the simple tunic stained with dye and grease. I saw the calculation in his look—not judgment, but assessment. He was measuring the distance between the man he knew and the one he saw.

“You look like a tentmaker,” he said finally. His voice was hoarse from road and dust.

“I am a tentmaker.”

He nodded slowly, as if confirming a difficult report. “The work in Antioch grows. Faster than we can... tend it.” He shifted his weight, the staff creaking. “Greeks. Not God-fearers who know the synagogues. Pagans. Sailors. Slaves. They hear. They believe. They have no... framework. No Moses. No prophets. Just the story of the Messiah, and the love they see in the ones who tell it.”

He was speaking to the room, not to me. His words were careful, rehearsed. A report delivered to an empty chair.

“It is a beautiful chaos,” he continued. “And it is fracturing. Some from Jerusalem came. Said they must be circumcised. Keep the whole law. They brought... division. A bitter taste.” He finally looked directly at me. “I cannot bridge it. I am a Levite from Cyprus. I know the Law, I love the Law. But to them...” He gestured vaguely, a helpless, sweeping motion. “To these new ones, the Law is a foreign language. A wall. I stand at the wall and shout the gospel over it, but my accent... my accent is all wrong.”

The silence then was different. It was alive, humming with the words he hadn’t said. The dust motes swirled in the new current from the open door.

He took a step forward. Just one. His eyes held mine, and the weariness in them was bottomless, but beneath it was a core of pure, stubborn fire.

“We have need of you,” he said.

Four words. Simple. Unadorned. They hung in the space between us.

All the arguments rose in me, a silt-choked flood. I am the persecutor. My presence is a poison. I bring blood and division. I am a man who builds nothing but ruins. I opened my mouth. Nothing came out but a dry click.

He let out a long, slow breath. The official report was over. The man beneath it surfaced. “They call themselves Christians there. In Antioch. A nickname. A joke. ‘Little Christs.’” A faint, weary smile touched his lips. “I think... I think He likes it.”

Christians. The word was strange. New. It bypassed Jerusalem, bypassed Sinai. It spoke only of Him.

“Barnabas...” I managed. It was the first time I’d said his name in years. It felt foreign on my tongue.

“I am not asking you to return to Jerusalem,” he said, anticipating me. “I am asking you to come to a new place. A place with no memories of you. A place where your past is a story, not a scar on every wall.” He looked around my workshop again, his gaze lingering on the half-finished tent, the lonely tools. “This... this is a waiting room. You have waited long enough.”

“What if I bring the fracture with me?” The question scraped out of me. “In my bones. In my voice. What if my very presence is the argument?”

“Then we will have it,” he said, with no hesitation. “Openly. With the scriptures in our hands and the Spirit in the room. Better a true fracture that leads somewhere than this... this quiet dying in a dark room.” He gestured at the walls. “You are not meant for silence, Saul. Your mind was forged for debate. Your heart, for the nations. Even your... your damage. It gives you the shape of the key. You know the Law’s prison from the inside. You can speak to the ones who feel trapped by it.”

He reached into his belt-pouch. He pulled out a small, folded square of papyrus, much-handled, softened at the creases. He held it out.

I took it and unfolded it.

It was a list. Different names from Silas’s list. Longer. With notes scrawled in the margins in Barnabas’s neat hand. Lydia—seller of purple, hosts the gathering. Nico—former sailor, questions about forgiveness. Marcus—Roman soldier, afraid. At the top, in larger letters: The ones who are asking.

“They are not a problem to be solved,” Barnabas said, watching me read. “They are a people to be built. And the builder we have... his hands are full. His heart is full. But his mind... his mind needs a partner.”

I stared at the names. They were not hooks now. They were anchors. Solid. Real. People with trades and fears and questions. A people with no name yet, trying to wear the name of a man they’d never met.

The wall had a door. Silas had shown me the door. Now Barnabas stood in it, not just pointing, but waiting.

I looked up from the papyrus. The late sun had moved, and the bar of light now cut across Barnabas’s chest, illuminating the dust on his tunic, the determined set of his jaw. He looked exhausted. He looked unshakable.

The mallet lay at my feet. The crooked rivet gleamed on the bench. The bird on the roof had flown.

“When?” I asked.

His shoulders dropped a fraction. A relief so profound it was almost a collapse. “The ship sails from Seleucia in four days. I have already paid our passage.”

Our passage.

He had not come to invite. He had come to fetch. He had bet his journey, his coins, his hope, on a single, stubborn fact: that I was still in here somewhere.

I bent slowly and picked up the mallet. I laid it on the bench beside the ruined piece. I looked at my hands, the leather-worker’s calluses, the permanent stain of oak gall in the lines of my palms. Tools. They were just tools. They could make a tent, or they could help build a people. The hands were the same.

“I have tools to pack,” I said, my voice rough with disuse.

Barnabas nodded. He leaned his staff against the wall. He rolled up the sleeves of his tunic. “Show me what to carry.”

* * *

The inn was the kind of place where the walls drank the light. One smoky oil lamp on a central post did little more than push the shadows into the corners. The air was a broth of fried fish, sour wine, and the close, damp smell of men who’d walked all day. We took a table in the back, away from the handful of other drinkers—carters by the look of them, their voices a low rumble about axle prices and bad roads.

Barnabas placed two clay cups between us, filled from a jug the innkeeper had slammed down without a word. He drank half of his in one long pull, his throat working. I watched the lamplight catch the sheen of sweat still on his temple. He’d carried the heavier tool chest without a word, his breathing only a little labored by the time we’d reached this place.

“You paid the passage already,” I said. My voice sounded strange here. Not the workshop’s silence, not the interior monologue. Directed at another person.

“I did.”

“What if you’d found a skeleton? Or a madman?”

He set his cup down and wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. “Then I would have buried the skeleton. Sat with the madman. The passage money was the cheapest part of the gamble.”

He said it plainly. A fact. The wine was thin, sharp with resin. I tasted it and tasted the dust of the road. I could feel the list in my belt-pouch, a soft square of pressure against my hip.

“Tell me about the fracture,” I said.

He leaned back on the rough bench. His eyes, in the bad light, were dark hollows. “It’s not one crack. It’s a hundred hairline splits. A Greek baker shares the bread of fellowship with a Pharisee from Jerusalem. The Pharisee watches him break it with hands that offered sacrifice to Asclepius last year. He feels a chill. He says nothing, but his smile stiffens. The baker sees it. The next week, the baker doesn’t come. He goes to the gathering down the street, the one run by the Cypriots, where they don’t ask so many questions.” He spread his big hands on the table. “Multiply that. By the baker, the sailor, the wool merchant, the freedwoman. The men from Jerusalem, they have scripture. They have tradition. They have weight. The new ones, they have only the fire in their chests. And now, confusion.”

“And you? Where do you stand?”

He smiled, a tired flex of his lips. “I stand in the middle. Which means I am pulled in both directions. I love the Law. It is the melody I learned at my father’s knee. But I hear a new song in these people. A wild, joyful noise. I cannot call it wrong. My heart... my heart is too wide for this.” He tapped his chest with a fist. “It is not a doctrinal problem. It is a heart problem. And I am not a scholar. I am a bridge. But a bridge needs anchors at both ends. I am only one anchor.”

A carter laughed, a sharp bark that echoed in the low room. Someone else shushed him. I focused on the grain of the table, a deep, whorled knot that looked like an eye.

“They do.”

“And they would accept me? As this... second anchor?”

Barnabas was silent for a long time. He refilled his cup. Drank. “Accept? No. Not at first. Perhaps not ever. Tolerate? Perhaps. If your arguments are sound. If the fruit is good. If the Spirit is so clearly with you that to oppose you is to oppose Him.” He looked at me. “It is a fight, Saul. Not with swords. With words. With the living scripture. It is the fight you were born for.”

The old fever stirred, a phantom itch in my blood. The thrill of the debate hall, the yes-but-what-about, the precise dismantling of a flawed premise. I crushed it. That man had used those skills to draft warrants. To trap the simple and the sincere. My tools were tainted.

“The weapon is poisoned,” I said quietly.

“Then purify it.” He didn’t miss a beat. “Use it to heal the very sickness you once spread. What better vengeance against your past?”

It wasn’t about vengeance. It was about contamination. I saw myself walking into that crowded, vibrant, fracturing room in Antioch. My very presence would be a drop of ink in clear water. The men from Jerusalem would see the persecutor. The Greeks would see a Jewish scholar, another layer of law. I would become the living embodiment of the fracture.

Barnabas slammed his hand flat on the table. The cups jumped. The carters’ conversation stopped. We sat frozen until their murmuring started again.

“Enough.” His voice was low, fierce, stripped of all its pastoral patience. “Enough of this. For three years you have let your failure define you. You wear it like a hairshirt. You think it makes you holy, this self-loathing? It makes you useless.” He leaned in, the lamplight carving hard lines down his cheeks. “You met the Messiah on the road. Not in a synagogue. Not in the Temple. On a dirt road, in the middle of your sin. He did not first demand you go to Jerusalem and make amends. He sent you to a stranger in a street called Straight. He started you in the world. Do you think that was an accident?”

My breath caught. I hadn’t thought of it like that. The pattern of it. The bypassing of every institution.

“Antioch is a street called Straight,” he whispered, intensity burning in his eyes. “But it is a street ten miles long, filled with every nation under heaven. It is the world. And He is sending you there. Again.”

The logic of it was terrifying. A divine consistency. My knees felt weak, even sitting down. I gripped the edge of the table. The whorled knot-eye stared back.

“What do I have to offer them?” The question was a plea, stripped bare. “I have no simple joy. I have no untroubled faith. I have a mind full of shattered glass and a heart that still flinches when I hear a certain kind of prayer.”

“Good,” he said. “They have simple joy. They have untroubled faith. They need someone who knows the cost. Someone who knows what it is to have the foundation kicked out from under him. They need a father who has been a prodigal. They don’t need another smiling uncle. They need you.”

He reached across the table, not to touch me, but to point at my chest. “Your doubt is not a weakness. It is a tool. You will never be glib. You will never take His grace for granted. Every word you speak of forgiveness will taste of blood and dust to you. And they will hear the truth in it.”

The innkeeper shuffled over, grunted, and took the empty jug. We sat in the new silence. The carters were leaving, their benches scraping. The room grew larger, emptier, colder.

Barnabas’s fire banked. The weariness flooded back into his face, deeper now. He had shown me his core, the steel beneath the encouragement. It had cost him.

“I am asking you to come and be wounded with the wounded,” he said, his voice gravelly. “To be confused with the confused. Not to be their master. To be their... fellow prisoner. Who knows the way out.”

Prisoner. The word fit. I had been a prisoner of the Law. Then a prisoner of my guilt. Now a prisoner in a workshop. Always behind bars of my own making.

I thought of the list. Lydia—seller of purple. Nico—former sailor. Marcus—Roman soldier. What did a Pharisee of Pharisees have to say to a Roman soldier? And then I knew. We both served impossible masters. We both knew what it was to follow an order that cost a man his life. We both needed a discharge that no human authority could grant.

A strange, cold clarity washed through me. Not peace. Purpose. A dreadful, specific purpose.

Barnabas saw the shift. He didn’t smile. He watched, wary, like a man watching a door finally begin to swing open after years of being stuck.

He leaned forward, his voice dropping to a whisper that was almost lost in the sputter of the lamp. “We have need of you, Saul. More than you know. Antioch is different. It’s... ready for what you can offer.”

The innkeeper brought a plate of flatbread and a bowl of oily lentils. We ate in silence, the food tasteless, just fuel. Barnabas tore his bread with methodical bites, his eyes on the middle distance, giving me the space to churn. I watched the lamp’s flame gutter in a draft from the street. A thin thread of black smoke rose and vanished into the darkness pooled against the ceiling.

My mind was a council chamber of ghosts. Gamaliel, stern and disappointed. Stephen, his face alight with a peace I now craved. The high priest, handing me the warrants with a solemn nod of approval. I pushed the bowl away, half-full.

“I need air.”

Barnabas nodded, swallowing. “I’ll see to the room.”

The street was a canyon of shadows. The day’s heat rose from the stones in waves, carrying the scent of dung and dried herbs. A cat streaked from one doorway to another. From a nearby house, I heard a baby’s thin, relentless cry. Ordinary misery. I had walled myself away from even this.

I walked without direction. My feet knew the pattern of these streets, the turns to avoid the watch, the smooth stones where the gutters ran. I passed the darkened mouth of the synagogue alley. I didn’t look down it. That man, the one who had proudly taught there, was a stranger to me now. A fool in expensive robes.

I found myself at the city’s eastern gate, left open a hand’s breadth for the night watch. I slipped through into the cooler, wider darkness. The road to Antioch was out there, a pale ribbon under the stars. I sat on a fallen milestone, its inscription worn to ghosts of letters. The vast silence of the plain pressed in. It was the silence of the desert outside Damascus. The silence of the cell where I had waited for Ananias.

Barnabas’s words, but beneath them, another voice. Softer. Unbearably direct. Why are you persecuting me?

I had answered that question with three years of mute labor. Atonement by inches, measured in stitches and rivets. It was a child’s logic. If I suffered enough in small, quiet ways, the ledger might somehow balance. But the ledger was a fiction. Grace didn’t keep accounts. That was the terrifying truth at the center of the blinding light. There was no balancing. Only a door, standing open.

The guilt didn’t vanish. It sat in my gut, a cold, familiar stone. But it was no longer the foundation of my house. It was just a stone I carried. Perhaps I would always carry it. The thought should have crushed me. Instead, it felt like a grim permission. I could move, even with the weight.

A scuffle in the dry grass. A jackal, perhaps, or just the wind. I stood, my joints stiff. The stars had wheeled. The night was thinning at the edges, a deep blue bleeding into the black. Dawn was a rumor in the east.

When I returned, the common room was empty. The lamp was out. A single candle burned on the stairs. I took it and went up.

Barnabas was already in the small room, asleep on a pallet against the wall. He’d taken the one by the door. He slept on his back, one arm flung over his eyes, his mouth slightly open. The sound of his breathing was deep and ragged. A traveler’s sleep. He had walked five hundred miles on a hope. The sheer, stubborn faith of it was a rebuke.

My pack and tool chest were by the other pallet. He had arranged them neatly. My few spare tunics were folded on top. He had even placed my awl and mallet beside them, like sacred objects. The sight of it—this big, weary man carefully laying out a tentmaker’s tools—closed my throat.

I didn’t sleep. I sat on the edge of the pallet and watched the small square of the window. The deep blue lightened to grey, then to the color of a dove’s breast. I heard the first cart rumble past in the street below, the driver cursing at his donkey.

Barnabas stirred. He groaned, rolled onto his side, and blinked at me. He said nothing for a long moment, just watched me sitting there in the dawn.

“You’re still here,” he said finally, his voice thick with sleep.

“Where else would I be?”

He pushed himself up, rubbing his face. “I’ve dreamed this moment a dozen times. Waking to find you gone. Or worse, finding you still here, but with your eyes empty. The decision made for silence.”

“The decision is made.”

He went very still. His hand fell from his face.

“I will come to Antioch.”

The words were ash in my mouth. Then, a spark.

The road to Antioch was calling. For the first time in three years, I was answering.
