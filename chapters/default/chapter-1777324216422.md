The argument ended where all their arguments ended—in a stalemate that smelled of mildew and ozone. The flash drive stayed on the table, pulsing its slow, green heartbeat between them.

“Fine,” Mary said, the word sharp as a snapped twig. She unzipped her duffel bag and started pulling out equipment that had no business being in a duffel bag. A handheld spectrometer, its lens cracked. A coil of copper wire that glinted under the flickering fluorescent light. A Ziploc bag full of what looked like iridescent beetle shells. “If we’re keeping the cursed artifact, we’re using it. We find where they’re brewing the silver juice. We burn it.”

“A location,” Rex said, his reactor cycling through a skeptical orange. “From a schematic of a soul. This is your plan.”

“The substrate needs a catalyst. THORN-7, right?” Mary didn’t look up. She was connecting the spectrometer to her laptop with a frayed USB cable. “It’s not magic. It’s chemistry. Chemistry leaves a residue. A… scent.” She plugged the flash drive into a secondary port. The double-helix schematic bloomed on the screen again. “We tell the drive to look for its own ingredient list. Cross-reference with any active, industrial-scale bio-refining in a fifty-mile radius that also has a high probability of alligator-based security.”

“That describes every third property in the Everglades,” Jesus said. His headache had settled into a dull, familiar throb behind his eyes. He traced a phantom splinter in the pad of his thumb, a habit from a lifetime ago.

“Then we narrow it by power signature.” Mary’s fingers flew across the keyboard. “The obedience hormone isn’t stable. It degrades into a specific kind of static—UHF band, but with a backbeat. Like polka music played on a broken Geiger counter.” She glanced at him. “Your friend Judas broadcast on that frequency. It’s his fingerprint.”

The screen filled with a map of southern Florida, a spiderweb of glowing lines. Most clustered around the coastal megachurches and the sprawling Eden World theme park. One thread, thin and faint, pulsed deep in the Glades, snaking away from the roads into a vast, uncharted smear of green.

“There,” Jesus said. He didn’t point. He knew. The place on the map felt cold, a hollow spot. “That’s where the signal’s hungry.”

Rex leaned in, his shadow swallowing the table. “The topography is wrong. No roads in, no waterways wide enough for barge traffic. How do they ship the product?”

“They don’t ship it.” Mary zoomed in. The satellite image pixelated into a swampy blur, but at its center was an irregular, grayish shape. Not a building. “They grow it. And they feed it.” She tapped the screen. A heat signature overlay bloomed—a deep, biological red at the center, cooling to yellows and greens at the edges. “It’s a bioreactor. Flesh-based. Probably fed by… local wildlife. And donations.”

Jesus’s mouth went dry. He remembered the blood drives, the obedient queues. “They’re not just making the hormone. They’re farming the faith.”

Dawn in the Everglades wasn’t a sunrise. It was a slow leaching of gray into green, the world sweating itself awake. Mosquitoes hung in visible clouds, their drone the baseline of existence. Mary’s station wagon, now sporting a pronounced limp and the scent of burnt wiring, died a quarter-mile from the coordinates.

They walked the rest. The ground wasn’t ground—it was a spongy mattress of peat and root that sucked at their boots. The air thickened with the smell of decomposition and something else, a cloying, metallic sweetness that stuck to the back of the throat.

Rex moved with a surprising delicacy, placing each clawed foot with care to avoid sinking. His reactor glow was dialed down to a faint ember. “The noise here is wrong,” he murmured. “No birds. No frogs. Only the eating sounds.”

Jesus heard it then. Under the mosquito hum, a wet, rhythmic squelching. Like a giant heart digesting.

They found the fence first. It wasn’t chain-link. It was a lattice of woven vines, thick as a man’s wrist, studded with thorns the length of his finger. The thorns glistened with a clear, sticky fluid. A single, fat fly was stuck to one, its legs still twitching.

“Charming,” Mary whispered. She didn’t touch it. She pulled a small pair of pruning shears from her belt—ordinary garden shears, the red plastic handles faded by the sun. “Bio-engineered cat’s-claw. Nasty stuff. The sap’s a neurotoxin. Paralyzes you so the plant can… well, absorb nutrients.”

“Of course it does.” Jesus watched her snip a small opening. The severed vine bled a milky sap that smoked where it hit the damp earth. “Why build a guard dog when you can grow one?”

Beyond the fence, the trees thinned. The facility wasn’t a building. It was an ecosystem.

A vast, pulsating membrane, the color of a week-old bruise, stretched between the cypress knees. It was anchored to the trees by thick, ropy tendons. Across its surface, bulbous sacs of varying sizes throbbed. Some were clear, filled with a silvery, swirling fluid. Inside one, Jesus saw the vague, skeletal outline of a large fish. Inside another, something that might have been a raccoon. Both were perfectly still.

At the center, a larger structure rose—a conical vat of mottled flesh, ringed with what looked like ivory-colored ribs. Pipes, not metal but something chitinous and segmented, ran from the vat into the surrounding swamp. The rhythmic squelching noise emanated from here. A peristaltic pump.

“It’s a stomach,” Rex said, his voice flat. “They built a stomach.”

A figure moved along a narrow walkway made of woven reeds, overlooking the central vat. A woman in a stained lab coat over floral-print scrubs. She carried a clipboard. She stopped, leaned over the railing, and dumped a bucket of dark, chunky material into the open maw of the vat. The squelching intensified for a moment. A satisfied burp of methane wafted toward them.

Mary gagged, covering her nose. “The donations.”

“We need to get closer,” Jesus said. The plan, what little of it existed, was crumbling like wet paper. Burn it? How did you burn something that was mostly water and regret? “See if there’s a control point. A brain to this thing.”

They skirted the edge of the clearing, staying in the tree line. The ground grew softer, warmer. Jesus’s boot sank to the ankle in muck that released a bubble of gas smelling of cloves and rot. He pulled it free with a wet plop.

They were halfway around when the music started.

It tinkled from rusted speakers nailed haphazardly to the trees. Not hymns. Not static. The cheerful, tinny melody of an ice cream truck.

The woman on the walkway straightened up. She set down her bucket, clasped her hands, and smiled blankly at the nearest speaker.

From the shaded areas under the cypress trees, other figures emerged. Men and women in simple, homespun clothes, their faces slack, their movements synchronized. They lined up in an orderly queue before one of the chitinous pipes. As the ice cream tune looped, the pipe extruded a single, gelatinous blob of the silver substrate into each waiting, cupped hand. Each person bowed their head, swallowed the blob in one convulsive gulp, and shuffled away, making room for the next.

“Lunch line,” Mary breathed.

A man near the front of the line hesitated. He stared at the quivering silver mass in his palm. His face contorted—a flicker of confusion, of revulsion. The woman on the walkway stopped smiling. She lifted her clipboard, pressed a button.

A thorn-vine near the hesitant man’s leg twitched. It didn’t strike him. It gently, almost lovingly, coiled around his ankle.

The man’s hesitation vanished. He shoved the silver blob into his mouth, his eyes watering. The vine uncoiled. He shuffled on.

Jesus’s hands curled into fists. The phantom splinter in his thumb became a real pain. “We’re not burning a facility. We’re euthanizing a farm.”

Rex’s low growl vibrated through the ground. “The control is the walkway. The woman. She is the brainstem.”

A sudden, sharp crack echoed across the water.

Not a gunshot. The sound of a branch breaking underfoot. Behind them.

Jesus turned. Three men stood in the trees. They weren’t slack-faced devotees. They wore camo vests over dirty t-shirts, and they held rifles—old, well-kept deer hunting rifles. Their faces were sharp, watchful. Not obedient. Greedy.

The lead man spat a stream of brown tobacco juice into the ferns. His eyes, pale and watery, fixed on Mary’s duffel bag, then on Rex. A slow grin spread across his face, revealing gaps in his teeth.

“Well, shit,” he drawled. “La Reina said you might come poking around. Said there’d be a bonus for the head of the dinosaur.” He raised his rifle, the barrel steady. “Didn’t say nothin’ about takin’ you alive.”

The ice cream truck melody jingled on, obscenely cheerful. The line of faithful continued their silent communion. And the swamp, Jesus realized with a cold clarity, wasn’t just around them anymore. The warm, sucking ground, the digestive sounds, the thorn-vines—they were already inside the thing. The first shot, when it came, would just be the stomach beginning to churn.

The world became a series of sharp, separate details. The lead gunman’s missing canine tooth. A mosquito drowning in the sweat on Mary’s temple. The way Rex’s tail shifted, just an inch, curling around a cypress knee for stability. The ice cream jingle looping into madness.

The gunshot, when it came, was a flat, wet pop that got lost in the swamp’s digestive rumble. The gunman hadn’t fired. Mary had.

She held a modified flare gun, its muzzle smoking. A sizzling orb of magnesium-white light shot past the lead gunman’s head and struck the pulsating membrane of the bioreactor behind him. It stuck, spitting and fizzing, burning a hole that wept silver fluid.

The gunmen flinched, blinded. That was all Rex needed.

He didn’t roar. He moved. A ton of scales and reactor-core muscle became a blur of low-centrifugal force. His tail swept the legs out from under the two men on the left. The sound was like someone dropping a sack of wet cement. The third man, the talker, stumbled back, raising his rifle again.

Jesus was already moving, not toward the man, but toward the thorn-vine fence. He grabbed the severed ends Mary had cut, the sap burning his palms with a cold, electric pain. He yanked.

The living fence shuddered. The disturbance rippled down the line. A dozen thorn-tipped vines, sensing prey, lashed out—not at Jesus, but at the nearest movement. The gunman.

One coiled around his rifle, wrenching it away. Another snagged his camo vest. He screamed, a high, ragged sound, as the neurotoxin hit his system. His legs locked. He toppled forward, face-first into the warm muck, twitching.

The orderly line of faithful dissolved. They didn’t scream or run. They stopped moving entirely, standing like statues with silver drool on their chins, their programming conflicting with the chaos.

The woman on the walkway—the brainstem—wasn’t smiling anymore. She hammered a button on her clipboard. A deep, subsonic hum vibrated through the peat. The chitinous pipes feeding the swamp retracted. The surface of the central vat churned.

Things began to emerge.

They were gators, but wrong. Their hides were patched with glossy, synthetic scales that reflected the gray light. Their eyes glowed with a faint, green UHF static. They moved with a horrible, synchronized purpose, hauling themselves onto the bank, flanking the facility.

“Oh, you have got to be kidding me,” Mary muttered, reloading the flare gun with a cartridge that looked suspiciously like a lipstick tube.

Rex met the first one head-on. His jaws closed on its neck, but the synthetic scales held. They sparked where his teeth struck. The gator thrashed, its tail slamming into Rex’s ribs with a sound like a dropped anvil.

Jesus scanned the chaos. The walkway. They had to get to the walkway, to the control point. But between them and it was a seething moat of pumped sludge and three more cyber-gators. Lola’s voice crackled in his memory, a ghost in his ear. Sometimes you don’t fix the machine, you just jam a stick in the gears.

His hand went to the pocket of his jeans, to the hard, cold weight of the Eden relic—the THORN-7 fragment they’d recovered from the broadcast tower. It hummed against his thigh, a hungry frequency. It wanted to be used. It was a key, or a catalyst. A stick.

Mary fired another flare. It arced over the vat, landing in a stand of dry sawgrass near the perimeter fence. Fire, hesitant at first, then greedy, began to lick at the vines.

“The whole place is wetter than a sponge!” she yelled. “It won’t burn hot enough!”

“It doesn’t need to!” Jesus shouted back. He saw it now. The facility was a closed system. A stomach. And stomachs could be upset. “The control! We need to make it sick!”

Rex flung the first gator into the second. They tangled in a whirl of teeth and sparking scales. He turned, his reactor cycling to a warning crimson. “The walkway is structurally weak.”

“Is just another employee,” Jesus finished. The woman was already fleeing, scrambling down a rope ladder toward a flat-bottomed airboat hidden in the reeds. The real control was the vat itself. The heart of the operation.

Lola’s voice again, stubborn and clear. They’ll just use it. They’ll always find a way to use it. You can’t save the tool from the hand.

The transmission cut. In the sudden silence, Jesus looked down at the dead relic in his soot-stained hand. It wasn’t a victory. It was an exchange. They’d burned a field, and the farmer had just announced she was planting a new crop, engineered specifically for them.
