The call to St. John’s Plains reached Fulwar Skipwith at his plantation, Monteville, in the Feliciana district. He was fifty years old. The journey that brought him to this bend of the Mississippi was, in its way, a map of the Atlantic world’s dislocations. A Virginian, he had sailed for France in 1790 as a young merchant, drawn by the promise of the new republic. He stayed, witnessed the Terror, and secured an appointment as American consul at Paris under President Jefferson. For a decade, he navigated the treacherous politics of successive French regimes, a useful post for a man of republican sympathies. His dispatches to Washington, preserved in the National Archives, show a keen observer of power’s fragility. He saw governments rise and fall on the strength of an idea and the weakness of a bayonet. His own fortune rose with French commerce, then vanished in its collapse. By 1809, he was back in America, his European wealth gone, seeking a new start. West Florida, with its rich soil and ambiguous sovereignty, offered both.

Skipwith bought land. He acquired enslaved laborers. He became a planter. The soil of Feliciana was a deep, fertile loam, perfect for cotton. His political philosophy, forged in Parisian salons and consular offices, did not change, but its application shifted from the abstract to the immediate. In his memoir, written years later, he described the Baton Rouge district as he found it: “a society in embryo, possessing all the elements of a free state, but paralyzed under a distant and impotent crown.” His neighbors were men like him—American by birth, language, and inclination, yet holding title from the Spanish king. Their primary loyalty was to their property. The Spanish administration, as Skipwith quickly learned, was a dilapidated mechanism. It could grant land but often failed to defend it. It promised courts but could not deliver timely justice. For a man who had lost one fortune to institutional chaos, the prospect of losing another to administrative neglect was intolerable.

He did not arrive as a revolutionary. His early correspondence, cited in local histories, shows a man advocating for reform within the system. He believed the Spanish governor, Carlos Dehault Delassus, could be persuaded to delegate real authority to a local council, creating a functional hybrid government. This was not idealism but a calculation of stability. Skipwith’s investment was literal. His acres, his enslaved workforce, his barns and gins—all required predictable law to protect them. A violent overthrow risked everything. A negotiated devolution of power promised continuity. Throughout the winter and spring of 1810, Skipwith used his diplomatic polish in the parlors of Baton Rouge. He attended Delassus’s peace dinners. He argued for a compact.

The governor’s responses, documented in the Spanish archives, were always courteous, always evasive. Delassus had no power to share. His instructions from Pensacola and Havana were to temporize, to conciliate, but to cede nothing. Skipwith, the former diplomat, recognized the stalling tactic. It was the same language of powerless goodwill he had seen from doomed regimes in Paris. The petition strategy failed. By early summer, a harder edge entered Skipwith’s private writings. “The authority here is a phantom,” he wrote to a relative in Virginia, a letter later preserved in a family collection. “We address ourselves to a shadow. A shadow cannot sign a treaty.”

This realization coincided with a more urgent demographic reality. New settlers poured in monthly, crossing the Mississippi from the American side or coming down the Natchez Trace. They were not diplomats. They were farmers, veterans, opportunists, and men with rifles. They had no investment in Spanish precedent. Their allegiance was to the land they claimed and the community they built. They outnumbered the old Spanish residents by more than four to one. Their political language was not that of compact but of natural right. They talked of the American Revolution as a personal inheritance, not a historical event. Skipwith, living among them, understood that the delicate project of negotiated autonomy might be swept away by a cruder, more potent force.

When the summons for the July convention at St. John’s Plains circulated, Skipwith faced a choice. He could remain aloof, a gentleman planter hoping for the best. Or he could step into the gathering storm and try to shape it. His decision was characteristic. He did not lead the call for the convention, but once it was issued, he prepared to attend. He would bring his knowledge of statecraft, his belief in republican forms, and his hard-earned understanding of how revolutions are managed, not declared. He rode to St. John’s Plains not as a rebel, but as a man who believed the time for petitions had passed. The vacuum of power was no longer a theoretical inconvenience. It was a daily condition. Someone would fill it. The question was whether it would be done with a constitution or a mob.

Upon his arrival on July 15, Skipwith found the convention already in motion. John Rhea, the proceduralist, had been elected chair. The gathering was not a secret cabal. It was a public assembly of property holders, documented with minutes, held in a clearing. Its stated purpose was still framed as support for the Spanish governor—a fiction everyone maintained for the record. But the atmosphere, as described in later accounts by participants, was one of grim practicality. These men were not plotting in the dark; they conducted daylight business for a community that had ceased to look to Baton Rouge for answers. Skipwith took his seat among them. He listened as Rhea laid out a methodical case, point by point, for why Spanish authority had effectively lapsed. A legal argument, but its conclusion was revolutionary. Skipwith recognized the formula. Building a case for a new government did not start with a flag. It started with a list of grievances and a demonstration of failed reciprocity. You established, on paper, that the old contract was void. Then, and only then, did you propose a new one.

John Rhea was a lawyer. In a rebellion populated by planters, soldiers, and opportunists, his weapon was procedure. He had come to West Florida from Tennessee, a man steeped in the granular machinery of American frontier governance—land titles, county courts, legislative journals. The record of his life, pieced together from land deeds and convention minutes, shows a man who believed revolution required a paper trail. When the settlers of Feliciana elected him chairperson of the convention at St. John’s Plains on July 15, 1810, they were not choosing an orator or a general. They installed a draftsman.

The gathering Rhea presided over was, according to its own surviving journal, an exercise in orderly subversion. He opened not with a call to arms but with a reading of the assembly’s formal resolution to “assist” Governor Delassus. This was the necessary fiction. Rhea then methodically dismantled it. Witnesses later recalled his calm, insistent voice cataloging the failures of Spanish administration: the lapsed judicial system, the inability to protect property, the lack of basic civil governance. He presented each point as a legal finding, as if building a brief before a higher court. The true audience, however, was not in Baton Rouge or Havana, but in Washington. Rhea’s strategy, evident in his subsequent actions, was to construct a rebellion so legitimate in form that the United States would have no choice but to recognize it.

His background prepared him for this. Before moving to the Feliciana district, Rhea had served as a senator in Tennessee. He understood how governments were built from the ground up—article by article, clause by clause. The chaos of the Kemper raids a few years earlier had convinced him that uncoordinated violence led only to reprisal and ruin. A sustainable revolution, in his view, required a constitution first, then a flag, then an army. This put him at odds with men like Philemon Thomas, the charismatic militia captain who saw the solution in terms of force and momentum. The tension between Rhea’s legalism and Thomas’s impulsiveness would define the republic’s brief life.

Through August and into September, as secret committees worked and rumors of Spanish reinforcements filtered from Pensacola, Rhea focused on structure. He drafted the formal letter to President James Madison, dated September 5, 1810. A copy preserved in the National Archives shows his careful phrasing. It was not a desperate plea for rescue. Instead, it was a diplomatic notification. “The inhabitants of this portion of West Florida,” it declared, “have found themselves compelled by the failure of the Spanish Government to protect their lives and property… to unite and take upon themselves the care of their own safety.” The letter outlined the constitutional convention they planned to hold and explicitly invited the United States to “embrace this opportunity” to take possession. It was, in essence, a legal invitation to annexation, couched in the language of republican self-preservation.

When the armed assault on Fort San Carlos finally erupted on September 23, Rhea was not on the battlefield. He was in the convention hall, or what passed for one—likely a large plantation house or a courthouse—ready to translate military fait accompli into civil authority. Within hours of the fort’s fall, he read the formal Declaration of Independence of West Florida. The document, reproduced in newspapers weeks later, bore his fingerprints. It listed thirteen grievances against Spanish rule, a deliberate echo of 1776, each grounded in a specific failure of administration. It declared the “dissolution of the social compact” with Spain. Then, crucially, it asserted the people’s right to “form a constitution and establish a government for ourselves.”

Rhea’s work was far from finished. The declaration was a birth certificate; a government required operating instructions. He immediately chaired the committee to draft a constitution. The resulting document, modeled on the Kentucky and Tennessee constitutions, created a bicameral legislature, a judiciary, and an executive office called “President.” It was a full blueprint for a state. Rhea’s insistence on this step, while Philemon Thomas’s volunteers eyed the road to Mobile, seemed almost pedantic to the men who wanted to press their advantage. But Rhea understood that legitimacy, in the eyes of the world and especially of President Madison, hinged on this appearance of stable, deliberate self-creation. He built a case, not a country.

On November 19, with the constitution ratified, Rhea was elected senator from Feliciana. The republic now had all the formal apparatus of sovereignty: a defined territory, a founding document, a legislature, and a president in Fulwar Skipwith. Rhea’s legal architecture was complete. It stood, however, on a foundation of borrowed time and overwhelming American demographic pressure. His meticulous minutes and perfectly phrased petitions were, in the end, a bridge. He had built it strong enough for the United States to walk across. Whether that same structure could bear the weight of genuine independence was a question the coming weeks would answer, not with legal theory, but with the arrival of American troops and a presidential proclamation.

Philemon Thomas owned a plantation in the Feliciana district, but his true vocation was the militia muster. He was a Kentuckian, a man who had come of age on a frontier where land was taken with a surveyor’s chain and held with a long rifle. Spanish land grants meant little to him; possession was the only title that mattered. Where John Rhea built arguments, Thomas organized men. Where Fulwar Skipwith calculated risk, Thomas assessed terrain. When the convention at St. John’s Plains shifted from debate to action in late September 1810, it was Thomas they turned to for the decisive blow.

His background, documented in militia rolls and later pension applications, was that of a citizen-soldier. He had fought in the Revolutionary War as a young man, then migrated south and west with the tide of American expansion. He understood the psychology of a fortified position and the morale of the men besieging it. Fort San Carlos at Baton Rouge was not the formidable fortress of Spanish legend. Thomas’s scouts, whose reports survive in local accounts, described a wooden stockade, aging and poorly maintained, garrisoned by fewer than fifty Spanish regulars. Its strength was legal, not military—a symbol of distant authority. Thomas saw it as a tactical problem to be solved, not a symbol to be negotiated with.

The governor, Carlos Dehault Delassus, had received at least two warnings of a possible attack. Spanish correspondence shows he doubled the guards but took no offensive action. He could not; his force was too small. This paralysis was Thomas’s opportunity. On the evening of September 22, he dispatched riders to the outlying settlements—St. Helena, St. Tammany, the Amite River communities. The call was simple and direct, as recorded in a participant’s later reminiscence: muster at the Johnson plantation, four miles from Baton Rouge, by midnight. Bring arms.

Thomas’s plan relied on speed and surprise, but also on the raw demographic fact of the district. The men who gathered in the darkness were not a professional army. They were his neighbors—planters, small farmers, tradesmen. They outnumbered the Spanish garrison by at least five to one. Their loyalty was to each other and to the land they worked, not to a flag or a constitution still being written. Thomas gave them simple orders. A small party would approach the fort under cover of darkness and early morning fog, positioning themselves near the gate. The main force would follow at dawn.

What happened next is documented in multiple, sometimes conflicting, accounts from both sides. Spanish military reports, captured after the fort’s fall and later archived, describe a sudden assault at first light. American participant narratives, collected by historians in the 19th century, provide more detail. Thomas’s advance party, following his instructions, had indeed crept close. As the main body of rebels, about eighty strong, approached the fort in the dim light, a Spanish sentry challenged them. Shots were fired. It is unclear who fired first. The Spanish reports claim the rebels opened fire. The rebel accounts state the Spanish guards discharged their muskets.

The ambiguity was irrelevant to Thomas. The action had begun. He gave the order to storm the gate. The rebels rushed forward, using axes and their own weight against the timber. The fighting inside the stockade was brief and brutal. Two Spanish soldiers were killed. Several rebels were wounded. The Spanish resistance collapsed within minutes. Governor Delassus was found in his quarters and taken prisoner. By sunrise, the Lone Star flag—a single white star on a blue field hastily sewn—was raised over the fort. The entire engagement had lasted less than an hour.

Thomas’s role did not end with the capture. He immediately placed guards, secured the Spanish arms and powder, and paroled the captured soldiers under oath not to take up arms again. His actions, noted in the convention’s records that same day, were those of a man securing a military asset, not a revolutionary making a symbolic statement. He saw Fort San Carlos as a piece of terrain now under control, its cannon now pointing outward to deter any relief force from Pensacola.

This pragmatic focus put him at odds with John Rhea’s constitutional project in the days that followed. While Rhea and the convention debated articles of government, Thomas looked east. He argued for taking the momentum to Mobile, then to Pensacola itself, to crush Spanish power in West Florida completely. In his view, expressed in heated sessions recalled by other delegates, legitimacy flowed from the barrel of a gun. A republic that stopped at Baton Rouge was only half-born.

Rhea and the more cautious delegates, including Skipwith, resisted. They feared overextension. They feared provoking a wider war with Spain. They believed the appearance of stable self-government was more valuable to their annexation hopes than more territory. Thomas chafed at this legalistic restraint. The tension between the soldier’s desire for decisive action and the statesman’s need for calculated presentation would become a defining fault line in the republic’s brief existence.

But on the morning of September 23, 1810, Philemon Thomas had achieved exactly what his community required. He had removed the crumbling symbol of Spanish authority with minimal loss of life. He had proven that the real power in the district resided with the armed settlers in the clearings, not the uniformed soldiers in the fort. His victory was not a legal theory. It was a fact on the ground, one that made all of John Rhea’s careful paperwork possible. The republic had been declared in a hall. It had been born at the gate of a fort.

Carlos Dehault Delassus arrived in Baton Rouge in early 1810 to replace Don Carlos de Grand-Pre as governor of the district. The Spanish archives in Seville contain his initial reports, which convey a bureaucrat’s cautious optimism. He described a tranquil province, a manageable population, and a fort—San Carlos—that required only minor repairs. This was the official picture. The reality, which he discovered within weeks, was one of institutional decay and demographic reversal.

Fort San Carlos was not a fortress. A detailed inspection report Delassus filed to his superiors in Pensacola, later captured by American forces and preserved in the National Archives, listed its deficiencies. The wooden stockade rotted in sections. The powder magazine was damp. The garrison consisted of twenty-eight regular infantrymen, a number that fluctuated with sickness and desertion but never exceeded forty. Their uniforms were threadbare, their pay was months in arrears, and their morale, as Delassus noted in a confidential addendum, was “low.” The fort’s six cannon were obsolete, their carriages unstable. Its strategic purpose was not to repel an invasion but to project a fading symbol of sovereignty over a river junction.

Delassus’s civilian authority was equally hollow. The true power in the Feliciana, Baton Rouge, and St. Helena districts resided not in his office but in the ledgers of the Anglo-American planters and the muster rolls of their militia. Spanish census data from 1809, which Delassus inherited, recorded approximately 1,400 subjects loyal to the Crown. He had no accurate count of the American settlers, but his own correspondence admits they outnumbered the Spanish population by a factor of four or five. These were not transient squatters. They were men like Skipwith and Thomas, who owned thousands of acres, commanded enslaved labor forces, and operated within a network of commerce that tied them to New Orleans and Nashville, not to Havana.

The governor’s instructions from Vicente Folch in Pensacola were clear: maintain the peace, conciliate the settlers, and cede no authority. An impossible mandate. Delassus attempted to execute it through a policy of appeasement. He hosted what American attendees later called “peace dinners” at the Government House in Baton Rouge. These gatherings, described in letters from guests like Skipwith, were exercises in elaborate, empty courtesy. Delassus, a polished and genial host, would listen to grievances about land titles and court delays. He would express sympathy. He would promise to write to Pensacola. He never delivered a substantive concession, because he had none to give.

The convention at St. John’s Plains in July presented him with a direct challenge. Its initial resolution, pledging to “assist” him, was a transparent fiction. Delassus recognized it as such. His response, documented in the Spanish record, was to accept the offer graciously. He feigned gratitude for their support. This was not cunning. It was the only move left to a man with no troops to enforce a refusal. By accepting their pretense, he hoped to sustain his own: that he was still the governor.

Warnings reached him in September. The specific intelligence is noted in two separate Spanish military dispatches. One, from a loyal Creole settler, reported the gathering of armed men and the aim to seize the fort. Another, source unnamed, stated the attack was imminent. Delassus’s reaction was not to arrest the conspirators or reinforce the walls. He could do neither. He doubled the guard at San Carlos and waited. His inaction has been interpreted as incompetence or cowardice. The archival context suggests a colder calculus. He knew the fort was indefensible against a determined assault by the local militia. A proactive strike against the convention leaders would have triggered the rebellion he was ordered to avoid. His only hope was that the threat would dissipate or that Folch would send reinforcements from Pensacola.

Folch never came. Requests for men and money sent to Cuba were delayed, then denied. Delassus was alone.

On the morning of September 23, when the crack of muskets echoed from the fort’s gate, Delassus was in his quarters. The Spanish reports state he did not attempt to flee. He was captured by Philemon Thomas’s men without a struggle. His surrender was immediate and complete. He was treated with the courtesy due his rank, but his authority evaporated the moment the first rebel crossed the stockade.

In the days following his capture, under parole at a nearby plantation, Delassus wrote a final dispatch to Folch. A copy survives in the Archivo General de Indias. It is not a dramatic account of battle. It is an administrative report of failure. He listed the number of troops captured, the inventory of arms seized, the names of the two soldiers killed. His tone is that of a quartermaster, not a defeated commander. The rebellion, in his telling, was not a heroic struggle but a logistical transaction. The Americans had taken possession of the fort and its contents. The Spanish regime in Baton Rouge had simply ceased to function.

Delassus was soon released and made his way to Pensacola. His career did not end in disgrace; Spain had few officials to spare in that distant colony. He was reassigned. His tenure in Baton Rouge demonstrated a colonial truth: authority that exists only on parchment cannot withstand a community that organizes itself on the ground. He was not a villain or a fool. He was the manager of a vacant property, handing over the keys to the new owners because the lease had run out and the landlord was no longer answering his letters.

Governor Vicente Folch’s headquarters in Pensacola was a world away from the wooden stockade at Baton Rouge. His correspondence, preserved in the Spanish colonial archives, reveals a governor aware of the crisis but ensnared in an imperial bureaucracy that moved with agonizing slowness. When the first urgent dispatches from Carlos Dehault Delassus reached his desk in the summer of 1810, Folch understood the threat. The convention at St. John’s Plains was not a mob. It was, as he wrote to his superiors in Havana, a “formal assembly of the most considerable inhabitants,” and its demand for self-governance was a direct challenge to Spanish sovereignty. His initial response was proper and procedural. He promised Delassus reinforcements.

He never sent them.

The record of Folch’s administration, pieced together from his letter books and military logs, shows a man trapped between a disintegrating frontier and a depleted imperial core. Pensacola itself was not a bastion. Its garrison was small, its treasury empty, its supply lines to Cuba stretched thin by years of neglect and the ongoing war against Napoleon in Spain. Folch’s own reports to Havana, dated throughout 1809 and 1810, are a litany of shortages: of muskets, of uniform cloth, of pay for his soldiers. He could not secure his own district, much less project power two hundred miles west to Baton Rouge.

When Delassus’s requests became more desperate in early September, Folch turned to Cuba. His letters to the Captain General in Havana, copies of which reside in the Archivo General de Indias, were models of bureaucratic pleading. He outlined the strategic danger of losing the Mississippi River frontier. He requested a company of infantry, funds to pay the Baton Rouge garrison, and authorization to take decisive action. Then he waited. The reply, when it came weeks later, was noncommittal. Cuba had its own problems. Resources would be considered. No ships were dispatched.

Folch’s subsequent inaction has often been interpreted as indifference. The paper trail suggests a grimmer conclusion. By mid-September, after receiving explicit intelligence of the planned assault on Fort San Carlos, Folch convened his own council of war. The minutes of that meeting, summarized in a later dispatch, survive. His officers advised against sending Pensacola’s scant forces on a long march into a district already thick with armed American settlers. The relief column would be too weak to retake Baton Rouge and its departure would leave Pensacola vulnerable. The military logic was irrefutable. Folch accepted it.

He wrote one final letter to Delassus on September 20. A fragment exists in the captured Baton Rouge correspondence. It advised prudence and promised continued diplomatic efforts. It contained no orders, no troops, no hope. Folch knew the fort would fall. He had calculated the odds and found intervention futile.

When news of the capture of Fort San Carlos reached Pensacola in early October, Folch’s reaction was not outrage, but administrative realism. His report to Havana, dated October 10, 1810, is a cold assessment of fait accompli. He described the fall of Baton Rouge as the loss of a “remote outpost” whose garrison had been “overwhelmed by superior numbers of inhabitants.” He did not recommend a counterattack. He warned that the “revolutionary spirit” would likely spread east toward Mobile, the next Spanish post under his command. His priority shifted from reconquest to containment.

Vicente Folch was not a coward. He was a colonial administrator at the end of a broken supply chain, managing an empire’s retreat. His decisions—the delayed responses, the unanswered pleas for help, the final acceptance of loss—illustrated a systemic collapse. Spain could no longer govern West Florida because it could no longer defend it. The authority represented by Folch’s signature on official correspondence had become detached from the power required to enforce it. He remained governor in Pensacola, but his sphere of control shrank with every report from the west. He had become a spectator to his own province’s dissolution.

President James Madison’s claim to West Florida was not a sudden inspiration of 1810. It was a legal argument he had inherited and nurtured for seven years. The cornerstone was Article II of the Louisiana Purchase treaty of 1803, in which France ceded to the United States the territory Spain had retroceded to France in 1800 under the Treaty of San Ildefonso. The precise boundaries of that retrocession were, by diplomatic design, left vague. Thomas Jefferson, and then Madison, insisted the territory included West Florida up to the Perdido River. Spain as firmly insisted it did not. For years, the dispute simmered as a diplomatic stalemate. Madison’s private correspondence, preserved in the Library of Congress, shows his patience wearing thin by 1809. He viewed the influx of American settlers into the region not as a problem, but as an opportunity. These settlers were, in effect, creating facts on the ground that his legal theory could not. When news of the convention at St. John’s Plains reached Washington in August 1810, Madison did not see a foreign rebellion. He saw an opening.

His response was swift and clandestine. He did not seek a declaration of war or ask Congress for authorization to occupy foreign soil. On October 27, 1810, he issued a presidential proclamation. The document, printed in the National Intelligencer, declared that the territory of West Florida, up to the Perdido River, was “rightfully included in the Louisiana Purchase” and was now “part of the United States.” It ordered the occupation of the district. The legal justification was audacious. Madison asserted that Spain’s failure to protest American possession during the recent unrest constituted a de facto acknowledgment of U.S. sovereignty. He annexed the republic not by conquering it, but by declaring it had always been American. The proclamation was a masterpiece of expansionist logic, transforming a settler revolt into a domestic law enforcement operation.

To execute this order, Madison turned to William Charles Cole Claiborne, the Governor of the Orleans Territory. Claiborne was a political operator, not a soldier. His instructions, contained in a confidential letter from Secretary of State Robert Smith dated November 2, were precise. He was to take possession of West Florida for the United States, “preserving peace and good order.” He was to treat the officials of the republic with “respectful caution,” but leave no doubt about the transfer of sovereignty. Claiborne understood the delicate dance required. He would not invade a friendly republic. He would accept its voluntary incorporation.

Claiborne assembled a force of about 300 regular U.S. Army troops from the garrison at New Orleans and moved north. His progress was slow, deliberate, and highly visible. By the time he reached the border of the republic in early December, every official in Baton Rouge knew he was coming. The republic’s president, Fulwar Skipwith, faced a decision with no good options. He could resist and be crushed by the U.S. Army. He could flee. Or he could surrender the independence he had held for seventy-four days in exchange for the annexation he had always, privately, desired. Skipwith chose the last, but not without a performance of republican dignity.

The difference between Reuben Kemper’s raid and the West Florida Rebellion was the difference between a spark and a controlled burn. One left scorched earth and a wanted poster. The other left a flag, a constitution, and a date for Madison’s proclamation. The next chapter would belong to the organizers, not the outlaws. But Kemper first proved the door was unlocked.
