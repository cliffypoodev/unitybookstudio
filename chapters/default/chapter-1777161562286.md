The decision, once taken, traveled not through official proclamation but along the dirt roads connecting the Feliciana plantations. It moved at the speed of a horse, carried by riders who stopped at each dogtrot cabin and columned portico to deliver the same terse message: The convention had voted. The time was now. Philemon Thomas, appointed commander of the “military force for the protection of the district,” began his rounds. His task was logistical: to turn a list of names, subscribers to the convention’s war chest, into a coherent column of armed men. He rode first to the plantations along Thompson’s Creek and the Amite River, where the wealthiest cotton and indigo planters held sway. According to later accounts collected by historian Andrew McMichael, the response was immediate but quiet. No mustering drums or public calls to arms were heard. Men nodded at the news, told their wives to pack a haversack, and walked to their gun cabinets.

The landscape itself seemed to absorb the mobilization. The Feliciana parishes in late September were a study in oppressive fertility. The cotton stood high in the fields, its bolls beginning to split. It was harvest season, and the call to rebellion interrupted the annual rhythm of debt and profit. For the planters, many of whom owned dozens of enslaved laborers, the risk was calculated in economic terms. A failed rebellion meant the confiscation of their estates. Success promised secure land titles under American law and unfettered access to the Mississippi. Their decisions were made in studies lined with account books, not on patriotic impulse. They provisioned their men from their own smokehouses and granaries. They ordered wagons hitched. The rebellion, at this stage, was a domestic enterprise, funded and fed from private stores.

Thomas’s method, as pieced together from fragmentary letters and memoirs, was systematic. He did not give speeches. He presented the strategic reality: The Spanish garrison at Baton Rouge numbered fewer than fifty. Governor Folch in Pensacola had been informed of their disloyalty and had promised Delassus military relief. The window to act was narrow—strike before any Spanish reinforcements could arrive. Thomas distributed copies of the intercepted plea from Delassus, its request for troops to “overawe the inhabitants” serving as the best recruiting sergeant. For men who had spent years chafing under alien laws and insecure land grants, the document was not just a justification. It was a release from ambiguity. The Spanish governor himself had categorized them as a threat to be crushed. They would now behave as one.

By September 21st, two days before the planned assault, the force began to coalesce. Thomas established a rendezvous point at the plantation of John Mills, several miles north of Baton Rouge. The location was strategic: close enough for a swift march to the fort, but secluded within the dense pine and hardwood forests bordering the Mississippi floodplain. The men arrived in small groups, not as a military column. They came on horseback, in farm wagons, and on foot. They were planters in broadcloth coats, overseers in homespun, and younger sons with no property but a rifle. What unified them was not uniform but armament. Each man brought his own firearm—a Kentucky long rifle, a fowling piece, a military musket retained from earlier service. Their ammunition was handmade, their powder horns filled from personal stock.

The scene at Mills’s plantation, as later described by participant James Cooper, was one of subdued industry, not martial fervor. The men saw to their weapons, cleaning locks and measuring powder. They talked in low clusters, the topics mundane: the weather, the state of the roads, the quality of the powder. The enslaved men and women of the plantation moved through the gathering, bearing water and food, their presence a silent reminder of the social order the rebels fought to preserve. No one discussed the political theories of independence or the rights of man. The conversation was tactical. Who would scale the palisade? Where were the fort’s gates weakest? How many shots could the garrison realistically fire?

Thomas moved among them, a tall, raw-boned figure whose authority derived from competence, not charisma. He issued no written orders. He gave instructions face-to-face, assigning captains to specific tasks. One group was to secure the town and prevent any interference from Baton Rouge’s civilian population, which included many loyal Spanish subjects and free people of color. Another was to block the river road to prevent escape or reinforcement. The main body would approach the fort from the north, through the woods where they now camped. He emphasized speed and surprise. The goal was not a protracted siege, which they lacked the supplies to conduct, but a swift, overwhelming coup de main.

As dusk fell on the 22nd, the force numbered about eighty men. They ate a cold supper. They checked their gear one final time. Then, under a moonless sky, they began the slow, silent march south toward the bluffs of Baton Rouge. They moved in single file along narrow Indian trails, the only sounds the crunch of boots on pine straw and the occasional muffled curse as a man stumbled in the dark. They carried the evidence of their grievance in their minds—the words of the governor’s plea—and the instruments of its redress in their hands. Ahead, in the fort, Governor Delassus drank his chocolate and doubled the guard, a gesture as meaningful as a shrug. The two worlds, the old empire and the new fact, converged through the dark woods, separated now by only a few miles of silence and a handful of hours.

Philemon Thomas was a product of the first American frontier, a man whose life had been shaped by its violence and its opportunities. He was born in Virginia in 1763, the year the Treaty of Paris transferred West Florida to Britain. His family migrated to Kentucky when it was still the hunting ground of the Shawnee and Cherokee. As a young man, he fought in the Revolution, serving under George Rogers Clark in the Illinois campaign—a brutal series of raids and counter-raids that defined warfare in the wilderness. This experience, as historian Robert V. Haynes notes in his study of the rebellion, was Thomas’s formative education. He learned to move men quickly through hostile territory, to value surprise over numbers, and to understand that authority on the frontier rested on a willingness to act first.

By 1810, Thomas was in his late forties, a planter of moderate means in the Feliciana district. He was not among the wealthiest convention delegates. His influence stemmed from a different currency: proven competence in a crisis. When the convention needed a military commander on September 22nd, they chose a man who had spent his life navigating the space between order and chaos. The title they gave him—“Commander of the Military Force for the Protection of the District”—was grandiose. The reality, as Thomas well knew, was a collection of farmers and planters with no unified command structure. His first task, documented in fragmentary orders and later testimony, was to impose one.

He began at his own doorstep, sending his sons and trusted neighbors as riders. Their instructions were specific. They were to proceed to pre-identified plantations and settlements, recite the convention’s resolution, and instruct every able-bodied man to gather at John Mills’s plantation north of Baton Rouge. They were to emphasize the urgency: the Spanish in Pensacola knew of their disloyalty; reinforcements could be sailing from Havana; delay was defeat. The riders were not to debate or discuss. They were to deliver the message and move on. This system relied on a network of personal obligation and shared interest that was more reliable than any formal chain of command. A man might ignore a posted broadside, but he would listen to his neighbor standing in his parlor with dust on his boots.

Thomas himself did not linger. He rode a circuit through the core districts, his presence a physical ratification of the orders. At each stop, he performed a quick assessment. He noted the number of men, the quality of their arms, their access to wagons and provisions. He appointed local captains on the spot, men whose standing in the community would compel obedience. He bypassed the hesitant. The operation, as reconstructed from letters and claims filed after the annexation, had a grim, businesslike efficiency. It was less a call to patriotic arms than the mustering of a posse for a dangerous but necessary job.

His Kentuckian background was evident in his tactics. The plan for the assault on Fort San Carlos was a frontier raid scaled up. It required stealth, precise timing, and the exploitation of a defender’s weakness—in this case, the fort’s small garrison and Governor Delassus’s indecision. Thomas dismissed the idea of a formal demand for surrender. He had seen enough fortifications in his life to know that once a gate was barred and a siege begun, the advantage shifted to those with walls. The only viable strategy was to overwhelm the post before its defenders fully realized they were under attack. He intended to use the terrain—the woods, the fog off the river, the darkness—as a weapon.

On the afternoon of the 22nd, as men filtered into the rendezvous at Mills’s plantation, Thomas made his final dispositions. He ordered a group of volunteers, men familiar with the fort’s layout from years of trading in Baton Rouge, to infiltrate the town after nightfall. Their mission, according to the account of participant John Hunter, was not to fight but to observe. They were to note any changes in the guard routine, identify any sentries posted outside the walls, and report back before the main force moved. This use of scouts was standard practice in wilderness campaigning, but its application against a European-style fortification was audacious. It treated the Spanish garrison not as a sovereign military force but as hostile quarry.

Thomas’s demeanor, witnesses recalled, remained steady and quiet. He issued no inspirational proclamations. He listened more than he spoke. When a young planter expressed anxiety about the morality of attacking a fort flying the Spanish flag, Thomas’s reply was pragmatic. He pointed to the intercepted letter from Delassus. The governor had asked for troops to overawe them. The flag, Thomas reportedly said, no longer promised protection. It promised subjugation. The argument was legalistic, but its delivery was pure frontier logic: a threat identified must be neutralized.

By evening, the force was as ready as it would ever be. Thomas gathered his appointed captains for a final review. He assigned groups to specific gates and sections of the palisade. He reiterated the password—“West Florida”—and the order to minimize bloodshed if possible. But he made no promises of a bloodless victory. Every man there knew the sound of a rifle shot, the finality of it. As the captains returned to their men, Thomas stood alone for a moment, looking south through the pines toward the river. The woods were quiet. Somewhere in the darkness, his scouts moved toward the fort’s walls, and the Spanish sentries began another routine night watch. The machinery of the rebellion, which he had spent the last forty-eight hours assembling from scratch, was now in motion. It would either mesh perfectly or grind itself to pieces against the gates of Fort San Carlos.

The column that left John Mills’s plantation after nightfall on September 22nd was not an army in any conventional sense. It was a procession of individuals, strung out along the narrow trace that wound through the pine barrens toward the Mississippi bluffs. They marched in near-total darkness, the new moon offering no guidance. Each man followed the faint silhouette of the one ahead, stepping where he stepped, avoiding the same roots and sinkholes. The only light came from the occasional glow of a pipe being drawn on, quickly cupped in a palm. The quiet was a collective discipline. Men who had spent their lives cracking jokes around a hearth or haggling loudly at a landing now communicated with touches and gestures. The rustle of wool on leather, the soft clink of a powder horn against a buckle, the steady exhalation of breath—these were the sounds of their progress.

The path they followed was not a road but a hunters’ trail, used by the Choctaw and later by Spanish cazadores. It ran along a low ridge, avoiding the swampy creeks that fed into the Bayou Sara. The air was cool and carried the damp, fungal scent of decaying leaves. For many of the men, the terrain was familiar; they had hunted these woods for deer and turkey. But marching through them at night with a weapon, toward a fortification, transformed the known into the ominous. Every distant cry of a night heron sounded like a challenge. Every rustle in the undergrowth hinted at an ambush. They moved through a field that had only recently ceased to be contested ground between empires, and the darkness seemed to hold the memory of that older violence.

Philemon Thomas walked near the front. He did not ride. To have been on horseback would have placed him above the men and made him a visible target. His authority was now positional, not symbolic. He set the pace—a steady, ground-covering walk that was slow enough to maintain silence but fast enough to cover the seven miles to Baton Rouge before the first hint of dawn. Every half hour, he would halt the column with a raised fist, a signal passed silently back down the line. During these pauses, the men would kneel or lean against trees, listening to the forest and to the pounding of their own hearts. Thomas would confer in a whisper with the local guides, men like John Hunter and James Cooper, who knew every bend in the trail. They listened for the sound of the Mississippi, a low, constant rumble that would tell them they neared the bluffs.

The march was a test of the rebellion’s cohesion. There were no sergeants to enforce order. A man’s commitment was measured by his ability to keep his feet moving in the dark and his mouth shut. The group included veterans of the Revolutionary War and the Indian wars, for whom this was a familiar, unpleasant routine. For the younger planters and overseers, it was a novel and terrifying apprenticeship. The tension expressed itself physically. Men checked and rechecked the priming of their muskets. They touched the knives at their belts. One participant, in a letter to his wife later published in the Louisiana Gazette, wrote that he became acutely aware of the weight of his own body, the sound of his own swallowing. He was afraid not of the Spanish, but of being the one to make a noise that would betray them all.

After three hours, the character of the woods began to change. The tall longleaf pines gave way to a mixed forest of oak and gum, signaling richer soil near the river. The ground began a gentle, persistent slope downward. The air grew heavier, carrying the mud-and-rot smell of the great river. Thomas halted the column. Ahead, through a break in the trees, they could see a constellation of faint, yellow pinpricks: Baton Rouge. The largest cluster of lights marked the compound of Fort San Carlos on its bluff. From this distance, it looked peaceful, even fragile. The sight had a paradoxical effect. For some, it made the mission seem more real and more dangerous. For others, it shrank the Spanish garrison from an abstract imperial power into a small, isolated outpost that could be taken.

Now past midnight, Thomas gave the order for the final approach. They would no longer follow the main trail, which led directly into the town. They split into three pre-assigned groups, using deer paths to skirt the settled areas. Thomas led the largest group, about fifty men, on a wide arc to approach the fort from the north and east, through a tract of uncleared land owned by the late Don Juan de la Villebeuvre. A second group, under the command of John Mills, circled west to block the river road. A third, smaller party detached to enter the town itself, linking up with the scouts who had infiltrated earlier. The separation was the point of greatest risk. If one group was discovered prematurely, the entire plan would unravel.

Thomas’s group moved with agonizing slowness. They were now in broken country, navigating through patches of cane brake and across gullies washed out by summer rains. The lights of the fort disappeared behind the bulk of its own walls and the intervening trees. They navigated by sound, orienting themselves toward the occasional cough of a sentry or the muffled challenge of a password exchanged in Spanish. The ground underfoot became littered with the detritus of settlement: a rusted hoe head, a broken bottle, the bones of a butchered cow. They entered the cleared ground that surrounded the fort, the killing zone where a sentry’s shot would have a clear path.

Just before three in the morning, they reached their final position: a dense copse of young water oaks and wild plum bushes, roughly two hundred yards from the fort’s northern palisade. The men sank into the cover, their dark clothing blending with the shadows. They could see the fort clearly now. The wooden walls, about twelve feet high, were silhouetted against the slightly lighter sky over the river. A single lantern burned above the main gate. They could see the movement of two sentries on the platform walkway, their shapes passing in front of the light at regular intervals. The fort was quiet. No alarm had been raised.

Thomas motioned for the men to settle. The final wait began. This was the hardest part. The cold of the ground seeped into their bones. The adrenaline that had fueled the march ebbed, leaving behind a dull, physical fatigue. Men fought to keep their eyes open. They listened to the sounds of the fort, memorizing the rhythm of the sentries’ footsteps. They checked their weapons one last time, their fingers moving by touch in the dark. The plan was simple. They would attack at first light, a grey time when night sentries were weary and day sentries were not yet fully alert. They would rush the walls from three sides, using axes and grappling hooks to scale the palisade where it was lowest, near the eastern corner. The signal to advance would be a single shot from Thomas’s pistol.

In the fort, Governor Delassus slept, or tried to. He had doubled the guard, a measure that did little but halve the rest period for each of his few soldiers. The sentries paced their rounds, their thoughts likely on the coming dawn and a relieved return to their barracks. They peered out into the darkness, but they saw only the familiar shapes of trees and the distant glimmer of the river. They did not see the eighty men lying in the brush, their breath held at every footfall on the walkway above. The two worlds, separated by two hundred yards of open ground and a wall of cypress logs, were now in the same moment, breathing the same damp air, waiting for the same sun to rise. One world ended its watch. The other was about to begin its war.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus spent the evening of September 22nd in his quarters at Fort San Carlos, a space that reflected the attenuated grandeur of his office. According to the inventory later compiled by American appraisers, the room contained a mahogany desk, a bed with damask hangings, a silver tea service, and a portrait of King Ferdinand VII. It also held a ledger listing the garrison’s strength: twenty-eight regular infantrymen of the Fixed Regiment of Louisiana, a dozen local militiamen of dubious loyalty, and four artillerymen for the fort’s eight cannons. The cannons, as Delassus had reported to his superior in Pensacola, were in a poor state, their carriages rotten and their shot limited. The document was a précis of imperial decay.

Delassus was not ignorant of the threat beyond his walls. The historical record, drawn from his own dispatches and the later testimony of his subordinates, shows he received at least two explicit warnings that day. The first came from a loyal Spanish subject in the town, who reported seeing armed men gathering at the Mills plantation. The second arrived by an unknown channel, warning that an assault was planned for the following dawn. Delassus acknowledged these reports. His response, documented in the fort’s log, was to double the guard from four men to eight. He did not call in the local militia, many of whom were already drifting toward the rebel rendezvous. He did not send a frantic rider to Pensacola. He did not even order the fort’s gates barred and locked prematurely, which would have at least forced the rebels to break them down. He simply added four more sleepy soldiers to the night watch and retired to his quarters.

His inaction was not paralysis; it was policy. Delassus adhered to a script written in Havana and Madrid for managing colonial dissent: show firmness but avoid provocation. To have mobilized aggressively on the basis of a rumor would have been to validate the rebellion’s premise—that he saw the settlers as enemies. His feigned cooperation with the convention delegates over the preceding weeks, hosting them for “peace dinners” and accepting their “assistance,” had been part of this same performance. He played for time, trusting that the appearance of control might forestall the reality of collapse until reinforcements arrived. The intercepted letter he had sent to Governor Folch, pleading for troops to “overawe the inhabitants,” was his genuine assessment. But the letter was also a confession of weakness. Its existence, now in the hands of Philemon Thomas, had stripped his performance of all credibility.

That evening, a junior officer ventured to ask if the governor wished to issue extra ammunition to the men. Delassus declined. The account of this exchange, preserved in a memoir by the officer’s son, suggests the governor’s reasoning was bureaucratic. Issuing ammunition required signing a requisition, which would create a permanent record of his expectation of violence. Such a record could be used against him later by superiors seeking a scapegoat for a disaster. Better to have no battle, or to have one with minimal paperwork, than to be seen anticipating one. This was the mindset of a career administrator in a failing system, where the avoidance of blame had become more urgent than the prevention of catastrophe.

Delassus took another step. He ordered the fort’s flag, the red-and-gold banner of Spain, taken down at sunset as usual and not re-hoisted. He may have believed this gesture would remove a symbolic target. To the rebels shivering in the woods, it would signal abandonment. The flag’s absence at dawn would be the first thing they would see.

He drank his chocolate at nine o’clock, as was his custom. He reviewed a few routine correspondence. Then he attempted to sleep. What his thoughts were in those hours is not recorded. He was a man in his fifties, a career soldier who had served the crown in Louisiana for decades. He had seen the territory change hands from France to Spain, then watched as Anglo-American settlers arrived in an ungovernable tide. His authority had become a polite fiction, and he knew it. The fort around him was not a fortress but a stage set, and he was the lead actor in a play whose audience had already left.

Outside his window, the doubled sentries paced their rounds. They peered into the darkness, but their vision was limited by doctrine and expectation. They watched for a formed body of troops, for the beat of drums or the flash of uniform buttons. They were not trained to see the absence of sound, the unnatural stillness of the woods where no night creatures stirred. They did not see the eighty pairs of eyes watching the faint glow of their match-cords from two hundred yards away.

The system Delassus represented was so brittle that it could only respond to formal declarations of war. It had no protocol for a silent approach through familiar woods. His failure to act, therefore, was not a personal cowardice but a systemic one. The empire could not comprehend the kind of war that was coming for it—a war mustered from smokehouses, led by planters, and timed by the first hint of light in the east. When the sentry on the north wall thought he heard a rustle in the plum thicket just before five o’clock, he dismissed it as a raccoon. He did not raise an alarm. He completed his turn and walked on, his footsteps a measured countdown to the end of Spanish West Florida.

The fog arrived before five o’clock, not as a gradual veil but as a sudden, dense bank rolling up from the river. It swallowed the bottomlands first, then climbed the bluffs, filling the space between the woods and the fort’s walls with an opaque, cottony grey. To Philemon Thomas, watching from the copse, it was a gift. The sentries on the walkway vanished from view. The lantern above the gate became a blurred, faint smudge. The world was reduced to sound: the distant lap of water against the levee, the creak of a boot on wood from the parapet, the low murmur of Spanish from the unseen guards. He gave a hand signal. A dozen men detached themselves from the main group and moved forward in a low crouch. These were the infiltrators, selected for their knowledge of the fort’s layout and their ability to move in silence. Their orders, as recounted by John Hunter in his later deposition to the U.S. Land commission, were to get inside the walls before the assault began. They were not to engage the garrison. They were to position themselves near the main gate and the barracks doors, ready to swing them open or overpower the guards from within when the attack signal came.

They used the fog as a physical pathway. The first pair, Hunter and a man named James Calhoun, crossed the open ground at a run, their forms dissolving into the grey after ten paces. They carried no muskets, only knives and pistols. Their objective was a spot along the eastern palisade where, according to local lore, the logs had been damaged by rot and were lower than elsewhere. They found it by touch, their hands tracing the wet, rough wood until they felt the spongy give of decayed cypress. Here, the wall was barely nine feet high. Hunter cupped his hands. Calhoun stepped into the makeshift stirrup, was boosted upward, and hauled himself over the top. He dropped into the interior courtyard with a soft thud, then turned to help Hunter over. The process took less than a minute. No challenge came from the fog-shrouded walkway above them. The sentries, blinded and disoriented, had pulled their collars up against the damp and huddled near the gate lantern for its meager warmth.

Inside, the fort was a ghost of its own design. The barracks building, the governor’s house, the powder magazine—all were vague, dark shapes in the mist. Hunter and Calhoun pressed themselves against the wall, listening. They could hear the sentries talking softly in Spanish twenty yards away, their voices carrying with unnatural clarity in the saturated air. The speakers were complaining about the cold, about the governor, about the poor rations. They were not discussing the possibility of enemies already within their walls. The two infiltrators moved laterally, keeping to the shadows along the palisade, until they reached the heavy timber braces of the main gate. Its bar, a thick oak beam, rested in iron brackets. Two men could lift it. They settled into the cover of a water barrel to wait.

Other pairs repeated the operation at different points. A group of three men, including the scout James Cooper, found a section of the wall where a recent rain had washed out the earth beneath it, creating a gap large enough for a man to wriggle under. They squirmed through the mud and emerged inside, their clothes soaked and darkened. They made for the barracks, where the bulk of the Spanish soldiers still slept. A single infiltrator, a former Spanish militiaman named Rodriguez who had switched allegiance, walked boldly across the courtyard to the guardhouse beside the gate. He carried an empty water bucket, as if on an errand. A sentry stopped him, recognized his face, and waved him through with a grunt. Once inside the small guardhouse, Rodriguez did not draw water. He stood by the single window, watching the courtyard, a pistol heavy in his coat pocket.

When the sentry on the north wall heard a rustle in the plum thicket and dismissed it as a raccoon, he followed a lifetime of training. Raccoons were a common nuisance. Armed planters materializing from fog were not in the manual. His dismissal was the last official act of Spanish West Florida—a minor, instinctive decision that ratified the colony’s end. He completed his turn and walked on, his footsteps marking the final, unacknowledged transition from peace to war, from governor to prisoner, from empire to memory. The system did not break with a crash. It ended with a man shrugging at an unfamiliar sound in a familiar dark.
