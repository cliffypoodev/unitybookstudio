They called it the continuous bath.

He entered a room lined with porcelain tubs, their drains set into the floor. A nurse would guide him, or more often, force him toward one of the tubs. The water was always cold.

The approach manuals from that era stated the temperature was maintained between 92 and 95 degrees Fahrenheit, but patient accounts from St. Elizabeths Hospital in Washington, D.C., described a chill that seeped into the bones after the first hour. He was stripped of his clothes.

A canvas sheet was placed over the tub, pinned tight around his neck like a collar, so that he lay suspended in the water, unable to sit up or roll over. His head rested on a wooden block at the tub’s edge.

The point was restraint. The point was time.

He might be left there for four hours. He might be left there for eight. Some records from the Western State Hospital in Washington note sessions lasting twelve hours.

A nurse would check his pulse at intervals. She might adjust a trickle of incoming water from a hose. Otherwise, he was alone in a room full of other people alone in their tubs.

He heard the drip of taps. He heard the low moan of a man across the room. He heard the squeak of a nurse’s shoes on the wet floor. He felt the canvas tighten across his chest with every slight movement.

His skin grew soft, wrinkled, pale. The cold became a deep ache in his joints. This was not punishment. This was therapy.

Hydrotherapy was part of a regime that included insulin shock, where he was injected with enough insulin to induce a coma; and malaria therapy, where he was infected with a disease to produce a fever that might, somehow, burn away his mental illness.

The logic was physical. The problem was in the body, and the body could be shocked, heated, frozen, or sedated into correction.

A 1936 textbook on psychiatric nursing described the continuous bath as a means to reduce “excitement” and promote “sleep.” It did promote sleep. Exhaustion did that.

But the sleep in the tub was not restful. He drifted into a numb wakefulness. The ceiling above the tubs was often painted a dull green. The light came from frosted bulbs in wire cages.

There were no windows. Time was measured by the arrival of meals—a bowl of broth passed over the canvas sheet, a slice of bread placed on the wooden headrest—and by the shift changes of the nurses.

Their faces were professional and detached. They wrote notes on clipboards. They did not speak to him unless giving an instruction.

The protocol, as outlined in the nursing texts, forbade unnecessary conversation. Silence was part of the procedure.

After his hours were complete, they would unpin the canvas. They would help him stand. His legs would not hold him at first. The water had leached the strength from his muscles.

They would wrap him in a rough blanket and walk him, shuffling, back to the ward. His skin would prickle in the drier air. His fingers would tremble.

The ward was a long room lined with beds. The windows here were barred. The floors were linoleum, cracked in places, smelling of soap and urine.

At night, he heard the coughs and murmurs of forty other patients. He heard the footsteps of the night nurse making her rounds. He saw the beam of her flashlight pass across the ceiling.

He knew the schedule: bath days, days for the fever cabinet.

The fever cabinet was a metal box like a vertical coffin, with a door that sealed. He sat inside on a stool. They closed the door; they turned on the heat.

The air grew thick and dry. They raised his body temperature to 105 degrees, holding it there for an hour.

The goal, as one hospital superintendent explained in a 1934 journal article, was to replicate the fever of malaria without the risk of the parasite. The theory was that high heat could kill the toxins of mental disease.

The effect was a pounding headache, a dizziness that made the metal walls swim before his eyes, and a thirst so profound his tongue stuck to the roof of his mouth. When they opened the door, he stumbled out into the cooler air.

His gown was soaked with sweat. They gave him a glass of water. Then he went back to his bed.

These procedures were not rare. They were standard. They were administered in state institutions across the country to thousands of patients diagnosed with schizophrenia, manic-depression, severe anxiety.

The medical journals of the 1930s debated their efficacy. The debate was about statistics, about percentages of “improvement.”

The experience inside the tub, inside the cabinet, was not part of the data. The experience was the water, the heat, and the slow passage of hours with no promise of change.

He endured it because there was no alternative. The hospitals were overcrowded; the staff was overwhelmed.

The procedures, however brutal, were considered advanced, humane compared to the chains and padded cells of the previous century. They were a system trying to solve a problem it did not understand.

It was a system waiting for a better tool. That tool was coming.

It would arrive not as a fever or a shock, but as a cut. A precise, surgical cut into the gray matter. It would be presented as clean, modern, and merciful.

It would promise to end the need for baths and cabinets forever. Its name was leucotomy. Later, they would call it lobotomy.

Its first American advocate was a man already moving through the corridors of these same facilities, watching the routines, noting their failures. His name was Dr. Walter Freeman; he believed he had found the answer.

The most celebrated of these fever approaches targeted a specific, terrifying diagnosis: general paresis of the insane, the final stage of tertiary syphilis.

The disease, having incubated for years, announced its presence not with physical sores but with a disintegration of the mind. Patients exhibited grandiose delusions and a rapid cognitive decline that led to death.

Psychiatry had no answer for it. Until, in 1917, a young Austrian psychiatrist named Julius Wagner-Jauregg observed a curious pattern.

According to his later Nobel lecture, he noted that syphilitic patients who contracted other infections—particularly malaria—sometimes showed a startling remission of their psychiatric symptoms.

The fever, he hypothesized, might kill the spirochete bacteria. Wagner-Jauregg began to experiment. He would inject paretic patients with blood taken from a soldier infected with malaria.

The patient would then develop the cyclical fever of the disease—chills, sweating, temperatures spiking to 105 or 106 degrees Fahrenheit. After eight to ten paroxysms, the malaria was terminated with quinine.

The results, he reported in medical journals, were remarkable. Some patients emerged with their sanity apparently restored.

The method spread across Europe and, by the 1920s, arrived in American state facilities. It was not without cost.

Wagner-Jauregg’s own data, compiled from the Vienna Psychiatric Clinic, showed a mortality rate of approximately 15 percent. Patients died from the strain of the fever, from the malaria itself, or from the underlying syphilis.

But for a profession desperate for a cure, a 15 percent mortality rate was acceptable if it promised a chance of recovery for the remainder.

The process was documented with cold precision. A 1932 protocol from the Pennsylvania State Hospital for the Insane at Byberry outlined the steps.

A patient diagnosed with general paresis would be transferred to a special fever ward. The infecting agent was Plasmodium vivax, a strain of malaria chosen for its predictability.

The blood was drawn from a carrier patient—often another inmate who had been previously infected and maintained as a “malaria reservoir.” The infected blood was then injected intramuscularly into the new subject.

The wait began. The fever would rise, often after a week. Nurses recorded temperatures every two hours.

The charts, preserved in hospital archives, show jagged peaks and valleys. At 104 degrees, the patient became delirious. At 105, he might convulse.

Hospital staff would apply ice packs, but they were instructed not to suppress the heat too aggressively; the temperature was the therapeutic agent.

The patient endured days of violent chills and drenching sweats. He was often restrained to prevent injury during febrile seizures.

After the prescribed number of cycles, quinine was administered. The malaria receded.

The patient, exhausted and weakened, was then observed for signs of neurological improvement. One documented case involved a forty-year-old man admitted to the Manhattan State Hospital on Ward’s Island.

He had been a successful accountant before his behavior became erratic. He believed he was the president of the United States. He issued orders to the hospital staff.

His motor coordination deteriorated until he could not feed himself. His diagnosis was general paresis. He was selected for malarial therapy.

The records note that he received 5 cc of blood from a carrier patient on March 12, 1930. His first febrile paroxysm occurred on March 20.

His temperature reached 105.8 degrees. He became incoherent, shouting orders to imaginary aides. Over the next twelve days, he experienced eight full cycles of fever.

After the quinine was given, his temperature returned to normal. His physical weakness was profound. But his delusions, according to the discharge summary, had vanished.

He could hold a coherent conversation. He recognized his wife. He was transferred to a convalescent ward and, six weeks later, discharged to his family. The article listed him as a successful cure.

He died eighteen months later from a heart condition attributed to the strain of the procedure. The mortality data, still, was often obscured in the enthusiastic literature.

A comprehensive review of malarial therapy outcomes, published in the American Journal of Psychiatry in 1937, analyzed over two thousand cases. It found an improvement rate of roughly 30 percent.

It also noted a direct mortality rate of 12 percent, with an additional subset of patients dying within a year from complications related to the ordeal. The authors concluded the therapy was valuable for a select group.

The calculation was clear: for a fatal disease, an approach that killed 12 percent outright but offered a chance of survival to a minority was a net benefit.

The individual experience—the weeks of raging fever, the delirium, the bone-deep exhaustion—was not part of the equation.

The logic was consistent. Mental illness was a physical poison. The poison could be burned out, frozen out.

The body was a battlefield, and the treatments were assaults upon it. The organ itself remained a black box, its mysteries untouched.

The surgeries of the time were for tumors, for injuries. No one operated on the gray matter for madness. It was too delicate, too dangerous.

Walter Freeman, a neurologist with a background in neuropathology, understood this terrain. He had visited these fever wards. He had seen the malarial patients shivering in their beds.

He had reviewed the charts; in his early writings, he expressed a deep dissatisfaction. The therapy was crude, its outcomes uncertain, its horrors obvious.

He believed medicine needed a more direct approach. The answer, he would soon argue, lay not in attacking the body to influence the mind, but in attacking the mind itself.

The brain was not just an organ to be purged by fever. It was a structure to be altered by a knife.

The insulin coma ward operated on a different principle. The fever cabinet attacked a pathogen. Insulin shock attacked the mind directly by starving it.

The procedure was documented in training films and nursing manuals. He was brought to a treatment room in the morning. He had not eaten since the previous night.

A nurse would inject a large dose of insulin into his arm. The hormone’s purpose is to lower blood sugar. In a therapeutic dose, it lowered it to zero.

His body would begin to consume itself. Within an hour, he would slip into a stupor. His skin grew clammy.

His breathing became shallow. He lost consciousness. He was now in a hypoglycemic coma.

They moved him to a ward. It was a large room, lined with beds. Each bed held a patient in a coma.

According to nursing protocols from the Western State Hospital in Washington, the beds were arranged in rows for efficient observation.

A nurse walked the aisles, wiping sweat from foreheads, watching for the signs of a seizure. The air smelled of sweat and antiseptic and the faint, sweet odor of glucose solution.

The windows were high, letting in flat light. The floor was linoleum, easy to clean. He remained unconscious for an hour—sometimes two.

The coma was the therapy. The theory held that the shock of insulin-induced hypoglycemia could reset a malfunctioning organ.

Initial claims were met with enthusiasm. By the mid-1930s, insulin shock units were established in dozens of state facilities.

The process was industrial. At Greystone Park Psychiatric Hospital in New Jersey, records show that on a typical morning, twenty to thirty patients would be comatose in the ward simultaneously.

Nurses monitored them in shifts. The primary danger was that the coma would go too deep. Without intervention, deprived of glucose, he would suffer irreversible damage.

Patients could die. The nurses watched for the twitch of a limb, the roll of an eye, the onset of a convulsion.

When a seizure began, they acted. The nurse would immediately administer a glucose solution via a stomach tube or an intravenous line. The seizure would usually subside.

The patient would gradually wake—the waking was not gentle. A patient described the experience in a letter to a relative, later archived by the Connecticut State Hospital.

“You come back as if from a deep hole. Everything is blurred. Your head aches. Your body feels bruised—they give you sugar water.”

“They sit you up. You are so weak you cannot stand. They put you in a wheelchair and take you to the recovery room.”

“You sit there for an hour, shaking. Then they give you a meal—you eat like a starving man.”

The protocol was repeated. Guidelines called for a series of comas, often fifty or sixty sessions over several months.

Some patients received more. A 1941 audit of the program at the Illinois State Hospital at Manteno found that one patient had undergone 120 insulin comas over eighteen months.

He had experienced forty-two seizures during the process. His post-treatment evaluation noted he was more manageable but demonstrated significant memory loss and confusion.

He remained institutionalized. The mortality rate was a quiet statistic. American studies placed it between four and eight percent, depending on the skill of the staff.

Deaths occurred from prolonged seizures, from cardiac arrest, from aspiration during the administration of glucose. These deaths were considered medical casualties.

They were factored into the cost-benefit analysis of the protocol. What the statistics did not capture was the ward itself.

The visual field was one of rows of unconscious bodies. The sound was the low hum of ventilation, the rustle of nurses’ skirts, the occasional moan from a patient.

The rhythm was dictated by the injection schedule, the coma cycle, the seizure response. It was a factory for producing medical crises. Each patient was a unit on the line.

The specialist was familiar with these wards—he had toured them as a consultant. In his early writings, he expressed a pragmatic view.

Insulin shock, he wrote in a 1937 letter, was a vigorous and often effective approach, but it was labor-intensive, costly, and carried a significant risk profile.

It required specialized nurses and a dedicated ward. It was not scalable for the masses of patients flooding state institutions.

The need, he argued, was for a treatment that could be applied more broadly, more quickly, with less infrastructure. He found his answer not in a chemical, but in a procedure.

It would require no coma ward. It would need no team of nurses monitoring for seizures. It would take minutes, not hours.

It could be performed in an operating room, a clinic, even an office. It would be, he would soon proclaim, the solution to the industrial problem of mental illness.

It would turn the patient not into a body in a coma, but into a brain on a table. The numbers told the story.

In 1930, the population of state mental hospitals in the United States stood at approximately 266,000 patients. By 1940, it had swelled to over 410,000.

This was not a gradual increase—it was a flood. According to reports, admissions were outpacing discharges by a factor of three.

The architecture of care, designed for custodial containment, buckled under the weight. At Milledgeville, the Georgia State Sanitarium, designed for 2,000 patients, housed over 6,000 by 1935.

At Greystone Park in New Jersey, patients slept in hallways. At the Worcester State Hospital in Massachusetts, they were housed in converted barns.

The Depression emptied private coffers and stripped state budgets. Families who had once paid for private custodial care could no longer afford it.

They turned to the public system. State legislatures, facing their own fiscal catastrophes, cut hospital appropriations.

A 1934 survey found per-patient spending had fallen by nearly 40 percent since 1929. Superintendents pleaded for more resources.

Their letters, preserved in state archives, read like dispatches from a besieged fortress. Dr. George Stevenson, superintendent of the Binghamton State Hospital in New York, wrote to his commissioners in 1936.

“We are housing men in wards built for sixty with ninety. The attendants are overwhelmed.”

“The therapeutic programs we once envisioned—occupational therapy, organized recreation—have been abandoned for lack of staff and space. We are reverting to mere custody.”

Dr. William Bryan of the Western State Hospital in Washington echoed the sentiment in his 1938 annual report. “The tidal wave of admissions has forced us to prioritize containment over treatment.”

“Our nurses are now guards. Our physicians are triage officers—we are storing human beings, not healing them.”

The public perception hardened. The chronically mentally ill were not seen as patients with a disease that might be cured. They were seen as incurable burdens.

Newspaper editorials referred to state hospitals as dumping grounds. Political cartoons depicted them as vast warehouses for the unfit.

This was not mere rhetoric. It was a policy driver—the goal shifted from rehabilitation to management.

The question became not “How can we cure them?” but “How can we house them cheaply and keep them quiet?”

The therapeutic arsenal of the 1930s was inadequate to this new scale. Hydrotherapy required dedicated tub rooms and attendants.

Malaria therapy required a reservoir of infected patients and careful monitoring. Insulin coma therapy demanded a full medical team and a recovery ward for dozens of patients simultaneously.

These approaches were resource-intensive. They could only be applied to a fraction of the population.

For the vast majority, care meant confinement: a bed, a meal, a sedative. The sedative was often paraldehyde, a pungent liquid tranquilizer, or barbiturates like sodium amytal.

They kept patients docile. They did not improve their condition. In the pages of psychiatric journals, debates raged about the efficacy of shock therapies.

A quieter, more pervasive consensus formed among the administrators on the ground. As Dr. John Grimes stated bluntly in a 1939 conference address: “For the majority of our patients, we have no treatment that offers a reasonable hope of recovery.”

“Our duty is to provide humane custody and to protect society.” The term humane custody became a mantra. It meant clean floors and the prevention of overt violence.

It did not mean cure. This was the vacuum into which Walter Freeman would step. He was not an administrator burdened by budgets.

He was a neurologist, an ideologue, a man with a solution. He saw the overcrowded wards, the demoralized staff, the patients cycling through ineffective and dangerous procedures.

He understood the economic logic. In a 1938 paper titled “The Surgical Approach to the Psychiatric Problem,” he laid out the case with cold arithmetic.

“A single leucotomy procedure,” he wrote, “requires one surgeon and one hour of operating time. It can be performed in a standard surgical suite.”

“The patient requires no prolonged coma, no weeks of fever, no months of hydrotherapy. He recovers in days. He can be discharged in weeks.”

He presented leucotomy not merely as a medical advance, but as an economic one. It was a machine for increasing throughput.

The data on outcomes was still sparse, based largely on early reports from Portugal. He acknowledged the risks: infection, hemorrhage, personality change.

But he framed these risks against the backdrop of the existing system. “What is the alternative?” he asked. “A lifetime of confinement in a state hospital, costing the public treasury thousands of dollars.”

“Or a series of dangerous shock treatments with their own mortality rates. Leucotomy offers a chance of a return to home and community.”

His argument was pragmatic, even mercantile. It addressed the superintendent’s dilemma directly. The superintendents listened.

They were desperate for tools that could reduce their populations, not just manage them. A treatment that promised discharge was a treatment that promised relief from budgetary and spatial pressure.

His salesmanship was tailored to this audience. He did not dwell on the nuances of prefrontal brain function. He emphasized efficiency, scalability, and results.

When he began touring state hospitals in the late 1930s, he was a doctor demonstrating a technique. He was a consultant offering a systemic fix.

Walter Freeman arrived at St. Elizabeths Hospital in Washington, D.C., in the mid-1930s as a consultant neurologist.

The hospital was a monument to custodial despair. Its halls echoed with a silence born not of peace, but of resignation.

He walked through the continuous bath rooms, where patients lay submerged for hours in tepid water. He observed the fever cabinets, their glass doors sealed, the patients inside sweating under electric coils.

He reviewed the charts in the insulin coma ward, noting the cycles of unconsciousness and violent awakening. He saw these procedures not as therapy, but as elaborate acts of torture with negligible returns.

His background shaped his contempt. He was not a psychiatrist—he was a neurologist, trained in neuropathology.

Neurology dealt with the tangible: tumors, lesions, strokes, the physical architecture of the nervous system. Psychiatry, to him, was a soft science, helpless before the biological realities of severe mental illness.

The shock therapies, in his view, were assaults on the whole body in hopes of influencing the mind. They were indirect, crude, and inefficient.

In a 1935 lecture, he argued that psychiatry needed to move from metaphor to mechanism. The brain, he insisted, was an organ.

Its malfunctions must have an organic basis. And an organic problem demanded a physical intervention.

His ambition was evident from his early career. He was charismatic, energetic, and possessed of a certainty that bordered on zealotry.

His colleagues noted his unyielding drive. Dr. James Watts, the neurosurgeon who would later become his partner, described him as a man who could not abide stagnation.

“He saw a problem and he wanted to solve it, definitively, and he wanted to solve it now.” The problem the consultant saw at St. Elizabeths and other institutions was one of scale.

The routines he witnessed could only handle a handful of patients at a time. They required specialized equipment and teams of nurses. They were, in economic terms, unscalable.

His writings reveal a man fixated on efficiency. He calculated the cost-per-patient of insulin coma therapy, factoring in the salaries of the nursing staff, the glucose solutions, the monitoring equipment.

He compared it to the cost of a surgical procedure, which required only an operating room, a surgeon, and an hour. The arithmetic was compelling.

But the surgical option did not yet exist in American psychiatry. Then, in 1935, he read the reports from Portugal.

Dr. António Egas Moniz, a neurologist in Lisbon, had performed a new operation he called a prefrontal leucotomy.

The theory was that certain mental illnesses were caused by entrenched pathological thought patterns lodged in the frontal lobes. By severing the connections between these lobes and the rest of the mind, one could break the patterns.

Moniz reported on a series of twenty operations. His results were dramatic: patients with chronic anxiety, depression, and schizophrenia showed marked improvements.

They were calmer. Their obsessive thoughts diminished; some could return home. He seized on the reports.

According to his correspondence with Moniz, he requested detailed procedural notes and anatomical diagrams. He studied the technique: burr holes drilled into the skull, a leucotome—a wire loop—inserted to cut white matter tracts.

He saw in it the direct, physical intervention he believed necessary. It was a mechanical solution to a mechanical problem. It bypassed the body and targeted the hardware.

The neurologist was not a surgeon—to perform the operation, he needed a partner. In 1936, he recruited Dr. James Watts, a young, board-certified neurosurgeon.

Watts was cautious, meticulous, trained in the precise anatomy of the organ. He was the ideologue, Watts the technician.

Their partnership was built on a shared frustration with the status quo. Watts, in his memoir, recalled their early discussions.

“We stood in those wards at St. Elizabeths and looked at those patients—the chronic cases, the ones who had been through every shock treatment and remained tormented.”

“He said, ‘We have to try something else. Something direct.’ He showed me Moniz’s papers—I saw the risks. But I also saw the possibility.”

He and Watts selected their first patient: a woman named Alice Hood Hammatt. She was a housewife from Topeka, Kansas, suffering from severe anxiety, depression, and agitation.

She had been through various treatments without success. Her husband had brought her to Washington.

The operation took place on September 14, 1936, at George Washington University Hospital. Watts drilled the burr holes.

He guided the leucotome into the brain, based on Moniz’s descriptions. They made six separate cuts in each frontal lobe.

The immediate aftermath was promising—Alice Hammatt was calm. Her anxiety seemed to have evaporated. She was discharged within weeks.

The partners published a triumphant report in the Journal of the American Medical Association. They declared the operation a success.

They framed it not just as a medical breakthrough, but as a humanitarian one. It offered a release from the prison of fixed ideas.

But the doctor was not satisfied with the procedure. It was still a formal neurosurgical operation.

It required a full operating theater, a surgical team, and Watts’s expertise. It was not yet the scalable solution he envisioned.

He began to sketch ideas for a simpler method. He wanted a procedure that could be performed quickly, without a neurosurgeon, in any hospital with a basic surgical suite. He wanted to industrialize the cure.

The system was waiting for him. The superintendents of the overcrowded state hospitals, drowning in numbers and shrinking budgets, read his papers.

They saw the discharge of Alice Hammatt. They began to write him letters—they asked about the technique.

They inquired about costs. They wondered if it could be applied to their chronic cases. He answered each letter with zeal. He had found the vacuum, and he was ready to fill it with a knife.
