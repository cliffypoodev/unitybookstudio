The fog that morning was a gift.

It had settled over the Mississippi sometime after midnight, a cold, wet blanket that blotted out the moon and muffled sound. By four a.m., it had crept inland, swallowing the cypress stands and the dirt tracks that connected the plantations to the Spanish fort at Baton Rouge. A man could vanish in it at ten paces. Philemon Thomas, a planter from the Feliciana district, counted on that.

He had gathered his men in the woods west of the fort. They were not soldiers. They were neighbors—planters, smallholders, a few merchants from the settlement—armed with their own hunting rifles, pistols, and a cold determination. Thomas had dispatched riders for days, calling men in from the outlying districts. The response, as recorded in later accounts, was immediate. They came not for Spain, whose authority had grown thin as the fog, but for the land they had claimed and improved. They came because a convention of their peers, meeting in secret for months, had declared that the time for petitions was over.

Fort San Carlos was less a fortress than a statement. A wooden palisade on a low loess bluff overlooking the river, it housed the administrative heart of Spanish West Florida’s Baton Rouge district. Its garrison, according to Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus’s own reports, numbered fewer than fifty men, many of them local recruits of dubious loyalty. The fort’s real power was symbolic: the flag of Spain flying over the eastern bank of the Mississippi, a threadbare claim to an empire that unraveled an ocean away.

Thomas gave the order quietly. Some of his men, the most agile, were to scale the palisade where the fog was thickest. Others would rush the main gate at the signal. They moved not as a unit but as a collection of shadows, their progress marked only by the soft suck of boots in damp earth and the occasional rustle of wet leaves.

Inside the fort, Delassus knew they were coming. He had received at least two explicit warnings in the preceding days. A local man had told him outright that the American settlers intended to take the place. The governor, a career soldier now in his sixties, had done little to stop them beyond doubling the guards. His capacity was broken. He had written to his superior in Pensacola, Vicente Folch, begging for troops. Folch had written to Havana, begging for money and men. The replies, if they came at all, spoke of delay. The Spanish empire, with its king a prisoner of Napoleon, was a body whose nerves were dead; the command to move a limb never reached the muscle.

So Delassus waited. He had hosted “peace dinners” for the convention delegates just weeks before, playing the gracious host while they discussed stripping him of power. He had feigned acceptance of their resolutions. The performance was over.

The first shot cracked the silence just before dawn.

It came from inside the compound. One of Thomas’s men, having scaled the wall, had encountered a sentry. That sound was sharp. It triggered a brief, chaotic volley. Muzzle flashes bloomed orange in the gray fog. Men shouted in Spanish and English. The main gate shuddered as bodies threw themselves against it from the outside.

The fight lasted minutes. Two Spanish soldiers were killed. The rest, outnumbered and overwhelmed, threw down their arms. There was no sustained defense. The record does not show whether Delassus ordered a surrender or if his men simply understood the arithmetic of their situation.

Thomas found the governor in his quarters. Delassus was dressed, as if expecting company. The capture was formal, almost polite. Thomas informed him he was a prisoner of the free and independent people of West Florida. The governor, according to a later newspaper account of the event, offered no resistance. He was led from his office, past his own bewildered troops, and into the custody of the men whose land he had nominally governed.

By full sunrise, the fog burned off the river. The flag of Spain came down. In its place, raised on a makeshift pole over the fort’s gate, went a new banner: a single white star on a field of deep blue. They called it the Lone Star flag.

It was September 23, 1810. The Republic of West Florida, such as it was, had been born in a few minutes of gunfire and a change of flags. But the ground for that birth had been prepared for decades, in treaties ignored, in borders disputed, and in the steady, silent migration of people who carried their own laws in their wagons. The assault was not a beginning. It was an ending.

Inside the fort, the work was less symbolic and more administrative. Philemon Thomas secured the armory, a meager collection of muskets and powder. He posted guards, using both his own men and the surrendered Spanish soldiers, whom he treated with a curt professionalism. The real damage was already done. Two Spanish lay dead; a handful of rebels had minor wounds. The bloodshed had been minimal, almost perfunctory. This was not a battle of annihilation but a transfer of title.

One man who would make that transfer legal was not a soldier. John Rhea, a planter and former Tennessee congressman, arrived at the fort later that morning. He had been the presiding officer of the secret convention at St. John’s Plains, the group that had plotted this coup for months. Rhea carried with him a document, drafted in the preceding days and now to be proclaimed as law. He was a proceduralist, a man who believed revolution required proper form.

In the fort’s former council room, now smelling of gunpowder and damp wool, Rhea faced the assembled rebels and a handful of local civilians who had hurried to the scene. The Spanish governor, Carlos Dehault Delassus, was present under guard, a silent witness to his own erasure. Rhea did not shout. He read. His voice, according to a participant’s later recollection, was steady and clear, filling the wooden chamber.

“We, the representatives of the people inhabiting the portion of West Florida situated on the left bank of the Mississippi River… solemnly publish and declare, that the said territory is and of right ought to be a free and independent state.”

The Declaration of Independence of the Republic of West Florida, dated September 26, 1810, was a compact, legalistic document. It listed grievances: the failure of Spanish protection, the denial of rights, the “anarchy” that threatened their property. It asserted their natural right to self-government. It made no mention of the United States. Its authority derived solely from the people in the room and the armed men outside.

When Rhea finished, there was a moment of quiet. Then came the approval—a vote by acclamation. The republic existed. Its territory stretched from the Pearl River in the west to the Perdido River in the east, and from the 31st parallel north to the Gulf. It claimed land that Spain held, that the United States coveted, and that Great Britain, across the Atlantic, still considered within its sphere. The claim was audacious, and its viability rested entirely on the actions of the next few hours.

Rhea immediately drafted a letter. He addressed it to President James Madison. He explained what had happened, described the new republic, and pleaded for annexation. “We have taken the post of Baton Rouge,” he wrote, “and declared ourselves independent… We look to the United States as our natural allies.” The letter was both a declaration of independence and a request for absorption. The paradox was not lost on the men in the room, but survival demanded it. They could not stand alone.

Outside, the mood was more celebratory. A barrel of rum was broached. Men who had been neighbors and now were countrymen clapped each other on the back. The Lone Star flag snapped in a rising breeze. For them, the meaning was immediate and personal. They had removed a distant, ineffective master. They had secured their farms, their slaves, their timber claims. The complex geopolitics of empires were less important than the simple fact that the flag over the fort was now theirs.

But a single incident, recorded in a memoir years later, hinted at the fragility beneath the triumph. A rebel, his spirits high, approached the captured Governor Delassus. He offered the Spaniard a drink from his cup. Delassus refused, his posture stiff. The rebel insisted, his gesture turning from celebration to confrontation. For a long moment, the two men lingered, locked in the silent understanding of the power shift—the victor trying to force a ritual of camaraderie, the vanquished refusing to perform it. Delassus finally took a small sip, his face unreadable. The rebel walked away satisfied. The new republic had its flag, its declaration, and its first, uneasy act of forced hospitality. The real work of building a state, of creating something that lasted beyond a morning’s euphoria, had not yet begun.

Governor Carlos Dehault Delassus was, by the summer of 1810, a man presiding over a fiction. He had replaced Don Carlos de Grand-Pré as the Spanish governor of the Baton Rouge district earlier that year, assuming command of a territory that was already slipping from Madrid’s grasp. His headquarters, Fort San Carlos, was a wooden stockade on a bluff, more a customs house and administrative office than a defensible position. The garrison under his direct command, detailed in his own dispatches to Pensacola, numbered between twenty-eight and forty soldiers. Many of these were local recruits, their loyalties divided between a distant crown and the neighboring plantations where they had family or sought future employment.

Delassus was a career soldier, now in his sixties, with a record of service in Europe. He understood the mechanics of power, which made his situation all the more galling. The district he was sworn to protect stretched from the Pearl River to the Iberville, and north to the 31st parallel. The Spanish census, a document he maintained with bureaucratic precision, listed approximately 1,400 subjects. He did not, because he could not, officially account for the nearly 6,000 Anglo-American settlers who had moved into the same territory over the preceding decade. They were squatters in the eyes of Spanish law, but they were also the economic engine of the region. Their plantations produced the cotton and timber that flowed through New Orleans. To evict them was impossible; to govern them was an exercise in polite pretense.

His strategy, as the tensions of 1810 mounted, was one of calibrated appeasement. When a group of influential settlers called for a convention to discuss “public safety,” Delassus did not forbid it. He invited its delegates to dinner. These “peace dinners,” held at the fort, were scenes of elaborate, mutual dissimulation. According to accounts preserved in local histories, Delassus played the gracious host, offering food and wine while his guests discussed resolutions that would effectively strip him of authority. He feigned acceptance of their proposals to “assist” him in governing, a euphemism for usurpation. The performance was meant to buy time, to maintain a veneer of control while he awaited reinforcements that never came.

His correspondence that summer reveals a man trapped by a collapsing imperial chain. He wrote urgent letters to his immediate superior, Governor Vicente Folch in Pensacola, pleading for troops. Folch, himself overseeing a threadbare province, forwarded the requests to Havana. The replies spoke of delays, of shortages, of priorities elsewhere. The Spanish Empire, with its King Ferdinand VII a prisoner of Napoleon, was paralyzed. The bureaucratic pathways that had once moved armies now carried only excuses.

The warnings arrived with chilling specificity. At least twice in the days before the assault, Delassus was told directly of the settlers’ intentions. A local man came to him and stated that the Americans planned to take the fort. The governor’s response, as documented in the subsequent investigation, was not to arrest the plotters or to fortify his walls beyond doubling the guards. He could not. Arresting the ringleaders would require a reliable force to carry out the orders and a functioning jail to hold them. He had neither. Any overt move might spark the conflict he tried to postpone. His inaction was not ignorance; it was the arithmetic of impotence.

On the night of September 22, as Philemon Thomas’s men gathered in the woods, Delassus sat in his quarters at Fort San Carlos. He knew they were coming. He had written his last dispatches. The sentries on the walls were nervous, peering into a fog that swallowed everything beyond the palisade. Delassus, the soldier, understood the tactical reality. His few dozen men could not repel a determined assault by hundreds. His authority, which had existed only as long as the settlers chose to tolerate it, was about to expire.

When the first shot broke the quiet before dawn, it was not a surprise. It was an acknowledgment. The brief fight that followed was less a battle than a formality. The governor surrendered his sword and his person. His capture was the logical conclusion of a year of diminished returns, of empty fortresses and full dining tables, of warnings heeded but impossible to act upon. He had not failed to govern. The mechanism of governance itself had ceased to function around him, leaving him as a solitary figure waiting in a room for the inevitable knock at the door.

The men who raised the flag were, almost to a man, newcomers. They had not been born under Spanish rule, nor had they sought it out. They had arrived in a wave that began in the late 1790s and crested in the decade after the Louisiana Purchase, crossing into West Florida because the land was fertile, cheap, and—crucially—available. Spain, desperate to populate its vulnerable borderland, had offered generous terms: headrights of up to 800 acres for a nominal fee, provided the settler swore an oath of allegiance to the crown, professed the Catholic faith, and improved the property. The Anglo-Americans who came took the land and made the improvements. The oath and the faith were often forgotten by the first harvest.

They settled most densely in the Feliciana Districts—East and West—a rolling country of piney woods and rich alluvial soil north of Baton Rouge. The name itself was a relic of earlier hopes; “Feliciana” meant “happy land,” coined by Spanish officials in a more optimistic time. By 1810, the happiness was of a particular, prosperous kind. The district had become a patchwork of cotton plantations worked by enslaved Africans and governed by a planter aristocracy whose cultural compass pointed north and west, not east across the Atlantic.

A Spanish census of 1809, preserved in the archives at Seville, recorded the demographic reality with sterile clarity. In the Baton Rouge district, which included the Felicianas, Spanish subjects numbered 1,420. The same document, in a separate note, estimated the “Anglo-American population” at 5,700. The settlers outnumbered the sovereign’s people by more than four to one. They were not itinerant squatters. They were the economic and social fabric of the region. Their plantations, carved from the wilderness with slave labor, produced the staple crops that fed into the global market via New Orleans. Their sawmills sent timber down the Mississippi. Their presence was a fait accompli that Spanish policy had encouraged and Spanish administration could not reverse.

Their culture was a transplant from the American upland South. They were largely Protestant—Baptist, Methodist, Presbyterian—and they built meetinghouses long before they contemplated a fort. Their language was English. Their legal traditions were English common law, with its emphasis on property rights and jury trials, not the Spanish civil code. Their political habits were those of the American republic: they formed committees, held elections, and believed in a version of sovereignty that resided in local assemblies, not a monarch in Madrid. As one settler later testified in a land claim case, Spanish officials were viewed as “administrators of the soil, not governors of the people.”

This created a gray zone of authority, a space where daily life operated under one set of rules while formal sovereignty claimed another. Spanish law, for instance, required all marriages to be performed by a Catholic priest. The Protestant planters largely ignored this, celebrating unions in their homes or meetinghouses. Spanish law governed slave codes, but planters often managed their enslaved populations according to their own customs. Spanish officials collected export duties at New Orleans, but within the Felicianas, the real power to settle disputes, organize labor, and maintain order rested with the planters themselves. The Spanish governor was a distant figure to be managed, not a proximate authority to be obeyed.

The catalyst for conflict was not religion or language, but land and legacy. Under Spanish law, land titles were conditional and subject to review. A settler’s claim could be challenged or invalidated if he failed to meet the conditions of the original grant, including the oath of allegiance. For the Anglo-Americans, who had invested years of labor and capital into their estates, this conditional tenure became a source of profound anxiety. Their wealth was tied to land they did not fully, irrevocably own. The uncertainty was compounded by the geopolitical turmoil after 1803. The United States claimed West Florida as part of the Louisiana Purchase. Spain vehemently denied it. The settlers lived on territory claimed by two nations, effectively governed by neither. Their property existed in a legal limbo, its future dependent on the resolution of a diplomatic dispute between empires.

This insecurity bred a specific kind of politics. The planters began to organize not as petitioners to Spain, but as a body representing their own interests. They formed committees of public safety. They convened extralegal conventions. The gathering at St. John’s Plains in July 1810 was not the first, but it was the most decisive. Its delegates were not radicals dreaming of a new nation; they were property owners seeking to secure their assets. Their grievances, as listed in the declaration John Rhea read, centered on the failure of Spanish protection—from “the savages,” from bandits, from the “anarchy” that threatened their homes. The phrase “anarchy” was a euphemism. It meant the collapse of a system that guaranteed their titles. They rebelled not to create a utopia, but to prevent a void.

The republic they proclaimed was, in its essence, a land title company with a flag. Its constitution, drafted in the weeks after the coup, devoted attention to property rights, courts for settling disputes, and the inheritance of estates. It protected the institution of slavery without apology. The Lone Star flag symbolized not a dream of independence so much as a demand for clarity—a clear, unambiguous authority that would confirm their ownership of the soil they had transformed. When they looked to the United States for annexation, they sought not liberation, but validation. America was the power most likely to ratify the facts they had already created on the ground.

That world into which the Lone Star flag was raised was not a blank space on a map, but a palimpsest of failed imperial ambitions. The Gulf Coast, from the Mississippi to the Perdido, was a borderland that had been claimed by every European power with a ship and a charter, yet fully controlled by none. France had planted its fleur-de-lis first, establishing Biloxi in 1699 and New Orleans in 1718. Spain, entering from the east, held Pensacola. Britain, after the Seven Years’ War in 1763, took possession of everything east of the Mississippi, including West Florida, and governed it from Pensacola for twenty years. Then, in the reshuffling that followed the American Revolution, the Treaty of Paris in 1783 returned the province to Spain. The flags changed, but the essential fact remained: the authority projected from distant capitals grew faint by the time it reached the muddy banks of the Mississippi. This was a frontier where empires overlapped, and their borders were not lines but zones of contested influence.

Spain’s hold was the most fragile. For decades, Madrid had pursued a deliberate policy of using land grants to attract Anglo-American settlers into its northern provinces, a calculated risk to create a buffer against other powers and to develop the economy. The strategy worked too well. By 1810, the demographic tide it had encouraged was now drowning Spanish administrative capacity. The empire itself unraveled at its core. In 1808, Napoleon Bonaparte’s armies invaded Spain, deposed King Ferdinand VII, and installed Joseph Bonaparte on the throne. The mother country descended into a savage guerrilla war and a crisis of legitimacy that echoed across the Atlantic. Colonial governors like Vicente Folch in Pensacola and Carlos Dehault Delassus in Baton Rouge found themselves in an impossible position. Their orders, when they arrived at all, were contradictory. The flow of troops, money, and administrative direction from Cadiz or Havana slowed to a trickle. As documented in the copious, increasingly frantic correspondence between Folch and his superiors, the imperial machinery seized up. A governor could request soldiers, but none would come. He could proclaim the king’s authority, but the authority had no force behind it beyond the walls of his own fort. Spain’s claim to West Florida in 1810 was a legal fiction sustained by habit and the hope that no one would call its bluff.

Into this vacuum stepped the United States, a republic whose western border was a matter of ardent dispute. The Louisiana Purchase of 1803 was the catalyst. President Thomas Jefferson, and his successor James Madison, insisted the acquisition included West Florida, arguing that the territory had been part of the French province of Louisiana when France ceded it to Spain in 1762, and therefore had been retroceded to France before the sale to the U.S. Spain rejected this interpretation outright. The true boundary, Madrid maintained, was the Iberville River and Lakes Maurepas and Pontchartrain, well west of Baton Rouge. The treaty language was ambiguous, a diplomatic fudge that allowed Napoleon to sell a territory whose extent he did not clearly know. For seven years, American diplomats pressed the claim, and Spanish officials stonewalled. The dispute created a zone of intentional ambiguity—a legal no-man’s-land where neither power exercised clear sovereignty, lest it provoke a war neither wanted.

This ambiguity was the oxygen for the West Florida rebellion. The American settlers understood that their land titles, granted by Spain, might be invalidated if Spain retained control. But if the United States annexed the territory, those same titles would likely be confirmed under American law. Their rebellion was, in one sense, an attempt to resolve the diplomatic stalemate by creating a new, pliable political entity that could be easily absorbed by the United States. As John Rhea’s immediate letter to Madison demonstrated, the rebels saw themselves not as founders of a permanent nation, but as facilitators of a real estate transaction. They exploited the gap between a dying empire and an expanding republic that had not yet decided how to seize its prize.

The geopolitical vacuum was not just political; it was military. With Spain paralyzed and the United States unwilling to risk an open invasion, there was no overwhelming force to impose order. The few Spanish troops were isolated. The nearest American army was in New Orleans, under the command of General James Wilkinson, a man whose loyalty was famously fluid and who was under orders to avoid provoking Spain. This left a power gradient that local actors could climb. The rebellion succeeded not because the rebels were strong, but because the forces that might have crushed them were absent, distracted, or deliberately restrained.

The Republic of West Florida was thus a product of precise historical timing. It emerged in the narrow window between the collapse of one imperial order and the consolidation of another. A frontier anomaly made possible by Napoleon’s cannons in Spain, by Jefferson’s expansive reading of a treaty, and by the calculated patience of American presidents waiting for fruit to ripen and fall. The men who stormed Fort San Carlos were not just fighting for their deeds. They were actors on a stage set by the retreat of European power from the North American continent, seizing a moment when the old rules were suspended and new ones had not yet been written. Their seventy-four-day republic was the ephemeral signature of that transition.

Fulwar Skipwith was, by the autumn of 1810, a man with a ruined fortune and a resuscitated sense of purpose. A Virginia-born planter and diplomat, he had lost nearly everything in the commercial chaos following the French Revolution, a disaster that had sent him retreating to the relative frontier of the Feliciana district to rebuild. He owned Hope Farm, a plantation worked by enslaved laborers, and possessed a polished, cosmopolitan demeanor that set him apart from his more rough-hewn neighbors. His diplomatic service as American consul in Martinique and commercial agent in Paris had given him an understanding of statecraft and international recognition. When the convention sought a president for their new republic, they turned not to the military leader Philemon Thomas, but to Skipwith. His value, as the convention minutes suggest, was his perceived ability to lend legitimacy to their enterprise and, more pragmatically, to negotiate their annexation into the United States. He was a bridge between the armed men in the pine woods and the drawing rooms of Washington.

John Rhea operated in a different register. A lawyer and landowner from Tennessee who had settled in the region, Rhea was a proceduralist. He was elected president of the initial convention at St. John’s Plains in July, a role that placed him at the center of the delicate process of transforming a protest into a government. Where Skipwith offered worldly credibility, Rhea provided legal architecture. His skill, evident in the orderly minutes he kept, was in guiding a roomful of passionate, propertied men through the steps of founding a state without triggering a premature crackdown from Spanish authorities who were still, nominally, in charge. He managed the debate that produced the September 26 declaration of independence, a document that carefully listed practical grievances—the lack of protection from “the savages,” the failure of commerce, the “anarchy” threatening their property—rather than abstract principles of liberty. Under his gavel, rebellion was framed not as sedition but as a necessary assumption of neglected governmental functions.

The two men’s approaches were complementary, a combination of form and forum. Skipwith, upon his election in late September, immediately set about performing the rituals of statehood. He delivered an inaugural address, printed proclamations, and designed a presidential seal. He understood that sovereignty required the trappings of authority. In a letter to his friend, former President Thomas Jefferson, he framed the rebellion as a just assumption of popular sovereignty, a narrative tailored for American consumption. Yet his personal correspondence, cited in later biographical sketches, reveals a more calculated aim. He saw the republic as a temporary vessel, a “trust” to be conveyed into the hands of the United States. His presidency was an exercise in holding the door open for annexation.

Rhea, meanwhile, focused on constructing the legal framework that would make annexation palatable to the American government. He chaired the committee that drafted the republic’s constitution in November, a remarkably detailed document for a state that would exist for only seventy-four days. It protected slavery, established courts, and outlined property rights and inheritance laws. Its purpose was not to inspire a nation for the ages, but to demonstrate to Washington that this was a stable, orderly, and above all, propertied society worthy of incorporation. A prospectus, written in legal code, designed to assure the United States that absorbing West Florida meant gaining productive citizens, not unruly bandits.

Their partnership was one of mutual necessity. Skipwith’s diplomatic grace needed Rhea’s legal structure to have substance. Rhea’s constitutional clauses needed Skipwith’s statesmanship to have meaning beyond the page. Together, they transformed a chaotic land grab into a plausible political entity. Yet a tension existed between their visions. Skipwith, the diplomat, was prepared to dissolve the republic at Madison’s word. Rhea, the constitutionalist, had helped build institutions that implied permanence. This divergence would surface quietly in December, when American troops finally arrived at the border. Skipwith would argue for immediate submission to avoid bloodshed. Some of Rhea’s fellow framers, having built their own state, would briefly balk at its dissolution. They had created something real enough to inspire a flicker of patriotism for the Lone Star flag they had designed. The success of Skipwith and Rhea had been too complete; they had legitimized the rebellion so effectively that some began to believe in the republic they had intended only as a means to an end.
