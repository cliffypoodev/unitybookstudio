Mary's fingers hovered over the keyboard like a faith healer unsure which organ to cure first. The screen flickered, casting a sickly green glow across the half-empty coffee cups and the single, stubborn roach that had survived three rounds of holy water spray. It waved its antennae at her from atop a stack of stolen phone books. She flipped it off.

The Eden World firewall was a bastard—encrypted layers wrapped around more encrypted layers, like a communion wafer dipped in epoxy. She cracked her knuckles and went back to work, the keys clacking under her nails. A bead of sweat rolled down her temple. The apartment's AC had died sometime during the Reagan administration, and the humidity made everything stick—her shirt to the chair, the chair to the floor, her patience to the very last thread.

"Come on, you sanctimonious piece of—" The screen flashed red. A cartoon alligator in a clerical collar popped up, wagging its finger. ACCESS DENIED: UNAUTHORIZED MIRACLE WORKER.

Mary slammed her fist on the desk. The roach fled. "I'm authorized by God, you digital swamp rat."

She yanked open the desk drawer, rummaging past a Glock, three rosaries, and a dog-eared copy of Hacking for Dummies before pulling out a vial of murky liquid. The label read HOLY WATER – BATCH #666. She uncorked it with her teeth and dumped a few drops onto the keyboard. The keys sizzled. The screen glitched.

"Oldest trick in the book," she muttered. The Vatican's firewalls had better security, but Eden's systems had one fatal flaw—they'd been designed by engineers who thought divine intervention followed logical syntax.

The screen flickered again, then resolved into a shipping manifest. Mary leaned in, squinting at the tiny text. "Relic transport… Miami docks… scheduled for—" Her stomach dropped. "Today?"

A truck honked outside. She bolted to the window, peeling back the duct-taped curtain just in time to see a black van with EDEN WORLD SECURITY emblazoned on the side roll past the laundromat. The back doors were reinforced steel. The kind you'd use to transport either nuclear waste or something equally unholy.

Mary's rosary beads clicked against each other in her pocket—seven rapid taps. Her tell for panic. She grabbed the skateboard leaning against the wall, its deck sanded down and painted with a crude approximation of the Last Supper. Jesus looked like he was mid-kickflip.

The van turned the corner. Mary was already out the door, board under one arm, the other hand fishing for the Bluetooth earpiece buried in her mess of curls. She jammed it in, tapped twice, and waited for the dial tone.

It rang once before a familiar voice answered, thick with sleep and what sounded like tequila. "This better be a booty call."

"Jesus," Mary said, hopping onto the skateboard as she hit the sidewalk, "I need you to steal a truck."

A pause. Then, resigned: "Which one of God's commandments are we breaking this time?"

"The one about coveting thy neighbor's holy artifacts." She swerved around a pothole, the wheels rattling like a snake's warning. "Eden's moving something big. And we've got about ten minutes before it's gone for good."

Another pause. She could practically hear him rubbing his temples. "Define 'big.'"

Mary kicked off harder, the wind whipping her hair into her mouth. "Old Testament big. Plagues-and-pestilence big."

A shadow fell across the sidewalk. The van idled at the stoplight ahead, its brake lights glowing like demon eyes.

"—that's right in front of me." She crouched lower, picking up speed. "You still got that holy water grenade?"

Jesus sighed. "You mean the one you told me was a terrible idea?"

"Yeah, that one." The light turned green. The van lurched forward. Mary pushed off with everything she had, tucking the phone between her shoulder and ear. "Meet me at the docks. And bring the dinosaur."

She hung up, stuffed the phone in her bra, and gripped the board tighter. The van's exhaust fumes burned her throat. Fifty feet. Forty. Close enough to see the driver's mullet in the side mirror.

Mary grinned. "Time for a miracle."

Then she ollied onto the van's bumper.

The door to the chapel was a slab of corroded iron, its surface pitted with age and chemical rain. A single glyph, a circle bisected by a vertical line, was stamped into the metal at head height. The paint had flaked away, leaving only a ghost of intent.

Silas placed his palm flat against the cold metal. He didn't push. He waited.

“You expecting an invitation?” Voss’s voice was a dry scrape behind her.

“Protocol.”

“Protocol for a dead god.”

Kaelen went first, pistol low and ready. The chamber beyond was a vault. Conduits, thick as her thigh, ran along the ceiling before plunging into the floor.
