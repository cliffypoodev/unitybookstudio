The airboat engine coughed, sputtered, and died somewhere between a drainage canal and the back lot of a strip mall called “Sunshine Plaza.” The sign out front featured a sun with a smiley face, which looked less cheerful and more like it was having a stroke.

“Out of gas,” Rex announced, as if the sudden silence and the boat’s slow drift into a bank of cattails hadn’t been a clue.

Mary stood, scanning the cracked asphalt horizon. “Perfect. We’re a mile from the target, downwind of a dumpster, and our getaway vehicle is a glorified fan.”

Jesus climbed out. His sandals sank into muck that released a smell somewhere between rotten eggs and cheap perfume. “Could be worse.”

“How?”

“Could be raining.”

A fat drop of water hit his forehead. Then another. The sky opened up with the sudden, vindictive fury of a spurned lover. Within seconds, the three of them were soaked.

Rex blinked rainwater from his ocular sensors. “You had to say it.”

The strip mall was a monument to late-80s architectural despair. Faded murals of dolphins and palm trees peeled from stucco walls. Between a nail salon and a store that sold only beepers, a garish banner stretched across a cluster of white tents: PRAISE-A-PALOOZA COMMUNITY HEALTH & WELLNESS FAIR! FREE SALVATION WITH BLOOD DONATION!

Beneath it, in smaller print: All major sins screened. Walk-ins welcome.

A line of people snaked out from the largest tent. They held umbrellas and looked damply hopeful.

“That’s it,” Mary said, wiping water from her eyes. “The blood drive. Sister Mercy’s people run it every month. ‘Give the gift of life, receive the gift of grace.’ They process the obedience hormones on-site.”

Rex’s tail flicked, splattering mud up Jesus’s robe. “So we walk in and ask for a tour? ‘Hello, we’re interested in your blasphemous bio-harvesting operation.’”

“We blend.” Mary dug into a soggy duffel bag and produced three bright yellow volunteer vests. They had cartoon suns on the chest and the words PRAISE PATROL! in perky block letters.

Jesus stared at his. The fabric was a synthetic blend that felt like wearing a cheese grater. “I’d rather get crucified again.”

“Noted. Put it on.”

They blended like three wolverines at a poodle convention. Rex’s vest strained across his chest, the armholes ripping before he even got it over his head. Mary’s was too big, swallowing her frame. Jesus looked like a particularly disgruntled crossing guard.

A woman with a clipboard and a perm so rigid it defied the rain intercepted them at the tent flap. Her name tag read DEBBIE – SALVATION COORDINATOR. Her smile didn’t reach her eyes. “New volunteers? Bless your hearts. We’re swamped. The Lord provides the sinners, but the paperwork is all ours.” She thrust clipboards into their hands without looking up. “You’re on donor intake. Name, address, sin history, and blood type. Standard liability waiver on the back. Any questions?”

“What’s the sin history for?” Jesus asked.

Debbie’s smile tightened. “To tailor the prayer. Gluttony gets a different scripture than covetousness. Efficiency in grace.” She finally looked at Rex, her gaze pausing on his claws, his snout, the faint glow from his reactor under the vest. Her smile didn’t falter. “Costume’s a bit much, hon, but the kids’ll love it. Try not to scare the elderly. They have weak constitutions and strong opinions about dinosaurs.”

She vanished into the crowd.

The inside of the tent was a special kind of hell. Industrial fans pushed the humid air around without cooling it. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, casting a sickly green glow on rows of folding chairs occupied by people with their sleeves rolled up. Televisions mounted on poles played a continuous loop of Sister Mercy’s sermon on the virtues of charitable plasma donation. Her voice was a smooth, honeyed contralto that somehow cut through the noise.

The smell hit him first—antiseptic, sweat, and underneath it all, the coppery tang of blood. But there was another note, sweet and chemical, like overripe fruit and rubbing alcohol. Obedience serum, being refined somewhere close.

Mary elbowed him toward a rickety table. “Start taking names. I’ll case the collection area.”

Rex stared at his clipboard. “I can’t write with these.”

“Figure it out.”

Jesus’s first donor was a man in his sixties named Earl, who smelled of cigars and regret. Earl squinted at the form. “Sin history? Do they mean this week or overall?”

“Let’s start with recent,” Jesus said, his pen hovering.

Earl leaned in conspiratorially. “I looked at my neighbor’s new Cadillac with covetousness in my heart. Also, I took the Lord’s name in vain when the Dolphins blew that field goal. That count?”

“I’ll put it down.”

“And there was the… impure thoughts.” Earl’s voice dropped to a whisper. “About the lady who does the weather on Channel 7. She has a very… authoritative front.”

Jesus wrote LUST in the box. His handwriting, never great, looked like a seismograph recording a minor earthquake.

The line moved with the grim efficiency of a factory line. Jesus heard confessions of petty theft, gossip, parking in handicap spots without a permit. A woman admitted she’d secretly enjoyed her sister’s divorce. A teenager confessed to downloading a bootleg copy of Top Gun. The banality of it was overwhelming. Two thousand years of theological debate, of parables and and martyrdoms, boiled down to checking boxes next to “Envy” and “Sloth.”

His fingers started drumming on the table—three taps, pause, two. The phantom nail prints in his palms itched.

At the next station over, Rex was causing a bottleneck. He held a donor’s arm gently in his claws, peering at the crook of the elbow. “Fascinating vascular structure. Like tiny blue highways.”

The donor, a pale man in a trucker hat, looked ready to faint.

“Just find a vein, Rex,” Mary hissed, passing by with a tray of empty blood bags.

“I’m looking for the interstate.” Rex poked with a cotton swab. The man yelped.

Jesus turned back to his next donor, a young woman with tired eyes and a baby on her hip. She filled out her form in silence, then paused at the bottom. “What do they do with it? The blood?”

“Help people, I’m sure,” Jesus said, the lie sticking in his throat.

“They said I’d get a fifty-dollar grocery voucher.” She nodded toward a table at the back, where a man in a cheap suit was handing out coupons next to a stack of pamphlets titled Your Debt, Your Cross to Bear. “Baby needs formula.”

She offered her arm to the waiting phlebotomist, a bored-looking kid with a rattail haircut. The needle went in. The dark red blood flowed through a tube into a bag that wasn’t like any blood bag Jesus had seen before. A small, clear chamber in the line contained a gelatinous purple filter. As the blood passed through it, the purple darkened almost to black.

The obedience hormone extractor.

He watched the bag fill, his own pulse thudding in his ears. This wasn’t charity. This was a refinery. They were paying people in coupons to mine their own veins for the chemical component of faith. It was so brazen, so utterly mundane in its evil, that he felt a laugh bubble up—a dry, humorless thing that he choked back.

Mary slid into the empty chair beside him, pretending to check a form. Her voice was low. “Collection’s in the back tent. They’ve got portable centrifuges and a filtration unit bigger than my first apartment. The filtered plasma goes into cooled cases. Eden World logo on the side.”

“How many cases?”

“Enough to baptize a small city in compliance.” She nodded toward Debbie, who was now overseeing the loading of a white van parked behind the tents. Two men in matching polo shirts carried the sleek, metal cases. “They’re moving it now.”

Rex abandoned his post, leaving the trucker clutching a cotton ball to his arm. “We follow the van?”

“We need proof,” Mary said. “Something that links this directly to the drones, to Project Shepherd.”

Jesus watched Debbie. Her efficient smile was gone, replaced by a focused, cold expression as she checked a manifest on her clipboard. This was the business end of salvation. His eyes drifted to the monitor next to her, displaying the donation totals. Numbers scrolled—pints collected, sin categories tallied, estimated “grace yield” in units.

The screen flickered.

For a fraction of a second, the spreadsheet was replaced by a simple, familiar symbol: a serpent coiled around a stylized cross. The logo of Eden World’s Bio-Divinity Division.

Then it was gone, back to blinking numbers.

Debbie hadn’t noticed. She waved the van driver on.

Jesus stood up. The folding chair screeched against the concrete. The sound cut through the ambient noise. Debbie’s head snapped up. Her eyes met his across the crowded tent. They narrowed, just for a second, not in recognition but in assessment. A wrinkle in her smooth operation.

She started toward him, her clipboard held tight against her chest.

“We’re blown,” Mary murmured.

“Not yet,” Jesus said. He pasted on what he hoped was a convincing, dopey volunteer smile and pointed toward the restroom sign. “Just a bathroom break, ma’am. Too much free coffee.”

Debbie slowed, her professional mask sliding back into place. She gave a tight, dismissive nod.

He turned, the fake smile dropping like a stone. Mary and Rex fell in beside him, not toward the restrooms, but toward a gap in the tent flaps near the back, away from the van, away from Debbie’s watching eyes.

The rain had softened to a drizzle. The dumpster smell was stronger here.

“She’ll check the volunteer list in about two minutes,” Mary said.

Rex ripped the yellow vest off. It came apart with a satisfying shredding sound. “Good. This was chafing my scales. What’s the play?”

Jesus looked back at the tent, at the line of people still waiting to trade their blood for coupons. The symbol on the monitor burned in his vision. “We don’t follow the van. We find where they’re brewing the serum. The source has to be close. You don’t transport unstable hormones across town.”

A memory surfaced. The chemical smell. Sweet, fruity, sharp. He knew that smell from somewhere else, long ago. Not a lab. A kitchen.

“Follow your nose,” he said, and started walking toward the strip mall’s loading dock, where a large industrial vent was pumping out not steam, but a faint, sweet-smelling vapor that made the hair on his arms stand up.

The loading dock smelled of rotting produce and diesel, but underneath it, the sweet-chemical stink was a trail. It led to a service door propped open with a cinderblock. A hand-lettered sign read AUTHORIZED PERSONS ONLY – BLOOD OF THE LAMB PROCESSING.

Inside, the air was cold enough to raise goosebumps. The room was a brutal clash of aesthetics: folding tables held gleaming centrifuges and chromatography columns, their hum competing with a gilded painting of the Good Shepherd on the far wall. The shepherd’s eyes were pixelated, a cheap digital print. Cases stamped with the Eden World serpent-cross were stacked by a rear exit.

But the centerpiece was the cooker.

It was a stainless-steel brewing vat, the kind used for craft beer or biodiesel. Clear tubes ran from it into a condenser, then dripped into a collection vessel containing a viscous, iridescent purple fluid. The obedience serum. A digital thermometer read 98.6°F. Body temperature.

“Homebrew salvation,” Mary whispered. She was already at a worktable, flipping through a laminated protocol sheet. “They’re heat-treating the filtered plasma with a catalyst. Something called ‘THORN-7 substrate.’ Sound familiar?”

It did. The crown. The memory was a hot needle behind his eyes. He forced his hands still, stopping the drumming on his thighs.

Rex sniffed at the vent on the vat. His ocular sensors whirred. “The catalyst is mineral-based. Contains traces of… hematite. And fossilized bone marrow. They’re using ground-up relics to catalyze the hormonal reaction. It’s not just chemistry. It’s sympathetic magic.”

“It’s blasphemy,” Jesus said. The word felt too small, too old.

“It’s a product line.” Mary held up a small vial from a rack. It glowed with that same soft purple light. The liquid inside moved sluggishly, like syrup. “This is the finished concentrate. One vial per fifty pints of donor blood. The yield is terrible. No wonder they need a constant supply.”

A door clicked open on the other side of the room.

They froze.

Debbie walked in, followed by a man built like a retired linebacker stuffed into a salmon-colored polo shirt. His name tag said TROY – SECURITY & SPIRITUAL ENRICHMENT. He held a walkie-talkie.

Debbie’s perm seemed to tighten. “I had a feeling. The volunteer list had a ‘Rex’ and a ‘Mary.’ No last names. And the handwriting for ‘Jesus’ was in Aramaic cursive.” She didn’t smile. “Sister Mercy likes to meet proactive volunteers. Come with us.”

Troy cracked his knuckles. The sound was like snapping twigs.

The exit behind them was blocked by the stack of cases. The only way out was past Debbie and Troy, through the door they’d just entered.

Rex’s tail gave a single, slow flick. His social mask, the ‘lovable monster’ shtick, slid into place. “A meeting! How delightful. I’ve been told I’m very enriching.” He took a step forward, his bulk making the floor creak.

Troy’s hand went to his belt. Not to a weapon, but to a small, boxy device with a single red button.

“Don’t,” Mary said, her voice low. “He’s got a neural scrambler. Eden tech. It’ll turn your dinosaur brain into microwave popcorn.”

Rex stopped. His reactor hummed a pitch higher.

“Smart girl,” Debbie said. “Now. The hard way, or the spiritually fulfilling way?”

“We change the recipe.”
