The first shipment of chlorpromazine arrived at St. Elizabeths Hospital in Washington, D.C., in the spring of 1954. The drug was packaged under its American brand name, Thorazine. The cardboard cartons were stacked in a pharmacy storeroom, their labels printed with the manufacturer’s promise: a chemical agent for calming agitated patients without sedation.

The hospital’s medical director, Dr. Winfred Overholser, had authorized a limited trial. According to administrative memos, the initial protocol was cautious. The drug would be administered to a select group of patients from the violent wards—individuals whose behavior had previously made them candidates for the surgical schedule.

The contrast was physical. The surgery required an assembly line of preparation: the selection of subjects, the scheduling of the neurologist, the procurement of the electroconvulsive machine for anesthesia, the clearing of a room. Thorazine required only a nurse, a syringe, and a vial.

On a morning in May, a patient was selected. His case file, drawn from hospital records, identified him as a thirty-two-year-old man with a diagnosis of chronic schizophrenia. He had been institutionalized for eight years.

His chart noted episodes of severe agitation, combativeness, and self-harm. In the previous fiscal year, his name had appeared on a list of individuals recommended for prefrontal lobotomy to reduce management costs. The procedure had been postponed due to a scheduling conflict with the visiting surgeon.

A nurse prepared the first injection according to the dosage guidelines. The patient, according to the nurse’s notes, was restrained in a seclusion room. He had been shouting for hours.

The injection was intramuscular, delivered in the deltoid. The nurse observed the man for the next thirty minutes, recording his vital signs and behavior on a new form provided by the pharmaceutical company.

The initial effect was not dramatic. The shouting subsided into muttering, then silence. The patient’s rigid posture relaxed.

He allowed himself to be led from the seclusion room to a chair in the day hall. He sat passively. He did not sleep. He was awake, but the agitation that had defined his existence on the ward for years was absent.

The nurse’s notes described the change as “a rapid diminution of psychotic excitement without apparent clouding of consciousness.” The patient ate his lunch without incident. He did not strike the attendant who helped him to his bed that afternoon.

The staff reaction, documented in meeting minutes and later interviews, was a mixture of curiosity and skepticism. Some physicians questioned whether the effect was merely a stronger form of sedation. Others noted the patient’s alertness.

The drug’s mechanism was unfamiliar. It was not a barbiturate. It was not an analgesic. It was classified as a “neuroleptic”—a term new to American psychiatry.

Its action was hypothesized to block certain neural pathways without destroying them. The possibility of reversibility was its central feature. If the drug caused adverse effects, the administration could be stopped. The patient’s brain would remain intact.

Within a week, the trial expanded to ten patients. The results were consistent. Agitation decreased. Combativeness ceased.

Patients who had required physical restraints or isolation became manageable on the open ward. They could be approached. They could be fed without struggle.

They could, in some cases, participate in limited occupational therapy. The change was observable within hours of injection. It was repeatable.

And it was, according to the hospital’s pharmacy logs, cheaper than the cost of a lobotomy procedure, which included the surgeon’s fee, the operating room supplies, and the extended postoperative care.

Dr. Overholser received a report from his clinical staff in June. The summary, preserved in the hospital’s archives, stated that chlorpromazine “offers a promising alternative to surgical intervention for the control of acute psychotic symptoms.”

The language was measured. But the implication was clear. A chemical agent could produce the same behavioral result as the surgical procedure—patient compliance, ward tranquility—without drilling through the orbital bone, without severing the frontal lobes, without creating an irreversible change.

Walter Freeman, by then, was a frequent visitor to St. Elizabeths. He performed lobotomies there regularly. He was not consulted on the Thorazine trial.

When he learned of it, his reaction, captured in a letter to a colleague, was dismissive. He referred to the drug as “a chemical straitjacket.”

He argued that its effects were temporary, that patients would relapse, and that it addressed only symptoms while his surgery addressed the root cause of the illness in the brain’s structure. He wrote that medications were a treatment of convenience for the institution, while surgical intervention was a treatment of permanence for the patient.

The hospital’s data began to accumulate. By the end of 1954, over one hundred patients at St. Elizabeths had received chlorpromazine. The surgical schedule, according to hospital logs, was reduced.

Patients previously marked for surgery were placed on the medication protocol instead. The change was not announced as a policy shift. It occurred as a quiet administrative realignment, documented in budget memos that noted the decreased need for surgical supplies.

The compound did not cure schizophrenia. It did not restore a patient’s personality or cognitive function. It produced a state of chemical calm.

But that state, for the hospital administration, achieved the same operational goal as the surgery: a pacified patient who required less staffing, less restraint, and less disruption. And it could be turned off.

Freeman’s dismissal was not a private bias; it was a public campaign. In the pages of The American Journal of Psychiatry and in his speeches to medical societies, he framed the new medications as a regression.

“We are trading a permanent cure for a temporary tranquilizer,” he told an audience at a neurological conference in 1955, according to the published transcript. He argued that the new medications were mere palliatives.

They masked symptoms, he said, while the operation excised the disease itself. The analogy he favored was surgical: you did not treat a gangrenous limb with aspirin; you amputated it. The prefrontal lobes, in his view, were the diseased core of the psychotic mind.

His correspondence from this period, preserved in his personal archives at George Washington University, reveals a deepening ideological rigidity. In a letter to a former student in October 1955, he wrote, “These ‘neuroleptics’ are a gift to the administrators, not to the patients.”

They allow the overcrowded wards to be quieted without the expense and trouble of surgery. But the patient remains a patient. “The procedure makes him a person again.”

He cited cases he considered successes: women who had returned to their households as dutiful wives, men who had resumed their trades as compliant workers. He never referenced the failures—the apathetic, the inert, the vegetative—unless to blame the subject’s underlying disease for the poor outcome.

Still, the medical mainstream was moving in a direction Freeman refused to follow. The year 1955 saw the publication of over two hundred clinical studies on chlorpromazine in peer-reviewed journals.

The consensus, as summarized in a Journal of the American Medical Association review that December, was that the medication “represents a significant advance in the management of acute psychotic states, offering a reversible and controllable intervention.”

The word “reversible” was the pivot. Surgery was irreversible. Pharmacology was adjustable. Dosage could be titrated. Side effects could be monitored. If a patient developed a severe reaction, the drug could be withdrawn.

He read these studies. His marginal notes on the articles, available in his archived papers, are dismissive. On the JAMA review, he scribbled, “Adjustable? So is a straitjacket.”

On a study detailing improved discharge rates for treated subjects, he wrote, “Discharge is not recovery. They are merely quiet enough to be sent home to burden their families.”

His skepticism was rooted in a fundamental difference of goal. For the doctor, recovery meant a restoration of the pre-morbid personality—the energetic housewife, the productive worker. For the emerging pharmacological model, recovery meant the mitigation of symptoms to allow other therapies to take effect.

He escalated his advocacy. In 1956, he published a paper titled “The Durability of Lobotomy.” He presented data from follow-up studies on his own patients, claiming that sixty percent showed “sustained improvement” over five years.

The paper was criticized in subsequent issues for its methodological looseness; he had defined “improvement” as any reduction in institutional management costs, not as a measurable psychiatric or functional outcome. But the paper served his purpose: it was a counterargument to the transient effects of chemical agents.

His travel schedule intensified. While drug companies were distributing samples to hospital pharmacies, he was packing his station wagon—the “Lobotomobile”—for trips to state hospitals in the Midwest and South.

These institutions were often the last to adopt new pharmaceutical protocols due to budget constraints. They still had overcrowded wards, understaffed units, and administrators desperate for any solution that promised order.

The neurologist offered them a permanent one. At a hospital in Missouri in March 1956, according to local newspaper coverage, he performed twenty-seven transorbital procedures in a single day.

The article noted that the hospital’s medical director had expressed interest in trying the new medications, but they were not yet funded by the state’s budget. The surgeon's intervention, by contrast, was a one-time cost.

The contrast between the two modes of treatment became physical in the spaces where they were administered. The medication unit was a place of routine: the morning medication line, the nurse with her clipboard, the patient swallowing a pill.

The neurologist’s operating theater—often just a converted examination room—was a place of ceremony. He still used electroconvulsive therapy for anesthesia.

The hammering of the ECT machine, the swift lift of the orbital pick, the percussive tap through the bone, the sweeping motion of the blade—it was a performance. It had drama. It promised a definitive end to suffering. The pill promised only daily management.

By 1957, his position was becoming isolated. The American Medical Association issued a cautious but clear statement on psychosurgery, noting that “its use should be considered only after all other therapeutic modalities have been exhausted.”

The “other modalities” now explicitly included pharmacotherapy. Neurosurgical boards began to require more stringent prerequisites for surgical candidates, including documented failure on drug regimens.

He saw these guidelines as capitulations. In a speech to a gathering of psychiatric nurses in Pennsylvania, recorded in their association newsletter, he said, “We are becoming a profession of chemists, not healers. We are content to mask the fire with a blanket of drugs, rather than put it out by removing the fuel.”

His rhetoric grew more vivid, more defensive. He began to describe the operation not just as a medical procedure, but as a philosophical stand against a culture of chemical dependency.

He warned of a future where mental hospitals would be “silent warehouses of drugged souls,” where patients would be perpetually medicated but never truly freed. He, in contrast, offered freedom.

It was a freedom defined by his own metrics: the patient discharged, the ward bed emptied, the family relieved of the financial burden of care.

But the data continued to accumulate against him. Longitudinal studies from hospitals like St. Elizabeths showed that treated subjects had higher rates of eventual discharge than surgical patients.

They also showed that those discharged surgical patients often required permanent custodial care by their families, while drug-treated individuals could, in some cases, return to limited employment.

The economic argument he had long wielded—that surgery saved long-term costs—began to crumble under actuarial analysis.

A 1957 report from the Public Health Service concluded that the lifetime care costs for a surgical patient were, on average, higher than for a subject managed pharmacologically, due to the permanent disabilities the surgery often induced.

He never cited that report. He continued to tour. He continued to publish. He continued to wield his orbital picks.

He framed the conflict as a battle between two visions of medicine: one that sought to manage illness, and one that sought to eradicate it. In his vision, he was the eradication. The treatments were the management.

He could not concede that his eradication often destroyed the person, while the management sometimes allowed the person to remain.

The data from Western State Hospital in Washington state is preserved in a series of annual reports bound in gray cloth. The volumes for 1953, 1954, and 1955 sit side by side in the archive. The numbers tell a story of abrupt transition.

In 1953, according to the hospital’s surgical log, fifty-seven patients received surgical procedures. Forty-two of those were performed by the neurologist during a week-long visit in November.

The hospital’s superintendent, in his year-end summary, noted that the operations had “significantly reduced the burden on our most disturbed wards.” He recommended budgeting for another surgical week the following year.

The 1954 report contains the first mention of the compound. A single paragraph in the pharmacy section notes that a trial supply of Thorazine was received in July.

“Initial results on a small cohort of agitated patients are promising,” the superintendent wrote. That year, the surgical tally fell to thirty-four. His visit was shorter. He performed twenty-eight procedures in two days.

By 1955, the shift was categorical. The pharmacy logs show that Western State ordered Thorazine in bulk quantities—five hundred vials in January, another thousand in June.

The medication was added to the standard treatment protocol for all newly admitted patients exhibiting psychosis or severe agitation. The surgical log for 1955 records only nine procedures.

He visited once, in March, and performed seven. The superintendent’s report that year devoted three pages to the “pharmacological management” program.

It noted a sixty percent reduction in the use of physical restraints, a forty percent decrease in injuries to staff, and a twenty percent increase in patient participation in occupational therapy. The section on neurosurgery was two sentences long.

The 1956 report did not mention the operation at all.

The transformation was not merely numerical. It was physical, audible, and visible on the wards. A nurse who worked at Western State from 1952 to 1958, interviewed for an oral history project in the 1970s, described the change.

“Before the compounds,” she said, “the violent ward was a place of constant noise. Shouting, screaming, the sound of restraints being applied. You’d walk through and your shoulders would be tight by the end of the corridor.”

After Thorazine, the noise changed. It became quieter. Not silent, but manageable. You could hear yourself think. “You could talk to a patient without shouting.”

The hospital’s inventory records reflect this shift. In 1953, the hospital ordered fifty new leather restraints and twelve padded seclusion room doors.

In 1956, it ordered ten restraints and no new doors. Instead, it purchased additional chairs for the day rooms and tables for group activities.

The change also altered the rhythm of staff training. According to internal memos, the nursing curriculum in 1953 included a unit on “preoperative and postoperative care for psychosurgery.”

By 1955, that unit was replaced by a new course titled “Pharmacological Management of Acute Psychosis,” which covered dosage calculation, side effect monitoring, and injection technique.

The hospital’s in-house medical journal, The Western State Quarterly, which had featured surgical results in 1952 and 1953, published no papers on psychosurgery after 1954. Its 1955 issue contained three studies on medication efficacy.

The economic calculation was explicit in the budget committee minutes. A memo from April 1955, comparing costs, laid out the figures.

The average surgery, including his fee, operating room costs, and two weeks of postoperative care, totaled approximately $350 per patient. The average monthly cost of the medication for a patient, at the standard dosage, was $18.

The memo concluded: “The pharmacological approach offers not only a reversible intervention but a considerable reduction in per-patient expenditure.” The savings were redirected.

The 1956 budget allocated funds for a new outpatient clinic—a first for the hospital. Patients stabilized on medications could be discharged with prescriptions and follow-up appointments. The surgical procedure had never created an outpatient; it created a dependent.

His relationship with Western State deteriorated. Correspondence between him and the superintendent shows a polite but firm divergence.

In a letter dated August 1955, the superintendent thanked him for his “continued service” but explained that the hospital’s treatment philosophy was evolving in line with new pharmacological advances.

He indicated that future surgical schedules would be “limited to exceptional cases.” He replied with a three-page letter arguing that medications were a “stopgap measure” and that the hospital was abandoning a cure for a temporary convenience.

The superintendent’s next letter was shorter. He informed him that the hospital’s surgical committee had decided to suspend all procedures for the remainder of the fiscal year to assess the long-term outcomes of the drug program. The doctor was not invited back in 1956.

The pattern at Western State was replicated across the country. At Greystone Park in New Jersey, the surgical tally dropped from forty-one in 1954 to six in 1956 after the adoption of the compound.

At Milledgeville State Hospital in Georgia, the number fell from thirty-eight to four. A survey of twelve major state hospitals conducted by the Public Health Service in 1957 found that the introduction of antipsychotic medication had correlated with an average decline in procedures of eighty-seven percent within two years.

The chemical agent did not eliminate psychosurgery. It relegated it. It became a procedure of last resort, reserved for patients who failed every medication regimen, whose violence was unmanageable by any other means.

Its application narrowed from a broad tool for institutional order to a rare intervention for extreme cases. The conveyor belt of the surgeon's roadshow slowed, then stopped at many stations.

The quiet revolution was, in the end, a revolution of accounting and calm. It replaced the irreversible with the adjustable.

It replaced the dramatic ceremony of the operating room with the routine of the medication line. It replaced the emptied bed with the outpatient clinic.

It did not cure mental illness. It managed it. But management, for the state hospitals drowning in overcrowding and violence, was enough.

The needle replaced the pick. The shadow of the lobotomobile grew shorter, until in many places it vanished, leaving only the stacked cardboard cartons in the pharmacy storeroom, and the silent, chemically calmed wards.

The fracture began not with a dramatic confrontation, but with the quiet retraction of professional invitations. His calendar for 1955 shows a gradual erosion.

In January, he was scheduled to speak at seven medical society meetings across the country. By December, he had only two.

The cancellation letters, formulaic and polite, cited “changes in program focus” or “a shift in our annual theme toward pharmacological advances.” One stated simply that the committee had decided to feature emerging research in neurochemistry this year.

He annotated these letters with increasing frustration. On the neurological association’s note, he scribbled, “Chemistry over cure.”

The editorial pages of major journals charted the intellectual pivot. In October 1955, The Journal of the American Medical Association published an unsigned editorial titled “Therapeutic Modalities in Psychiatry: A Time for Reassessment.”

It did not mention him by name. It referenced psychosurgery only in passing. Its focus was the “remarkable efficacy” of the compound and the “promising horizon” of other psychoactive compounds under development.

The editorial concluded that the future of psychiatric treatment lay in “the precise modulation of neural chemistry, not its irreversible ablation.” He wrote a rebuttal.

The journal declined to publish it. A rejection letter from the editor stated that his perspective represented a minority view not supported by the current clinical data.

Grant funding followed the editorial sentiment. The National Institutes of Mental Health’s annual report for 1956 listed its funded research projects.

In 1954, the NIMH had supported three studies on the long-term outcomes of the surgery. In 1956, it supported none.

Instead, it allocated over $2.3 million to research on the “pharmacology, metabolism, and clinical application of neuroleptic agents.” The money flowed toward laboratories studying receptor sites, not surgeons refining orbital picks.

He applied for a grant to conduct a ten-year follow-up study on his transorbital patients. His application was rejected.

The review panel’s notes stated that the proposed research did not align with the institute’s current priorities in dynamic treatment modalities.

The most significant defection came from within his own former partnership. Dr. James Watts, the neurosurgeon who had collaborated with him on the first American procedures, now made his position public.

In a 1956 article for The New England Journal of Medicine, Watts reviewed the emerging data on the compound. He said it measured, surgical.

He described the procedure as “a historical method whose utility has been superseded by less invasive, reversible interventions.” He did not condemn him personally.

He condemned the procedure’s permanence. “The neurosurgeon’s duty,” Watts wrote, “is to restore function, not to destroy it. Where a chemical agent can achieve a therapeutic effect without destruction, it represents a superior ethical choice.”

The article was widely cited. He read it. In a letter to a colleague that November, he referred to Watts as “a traitor to the field.”

Other neurosurgeons joined the chorus. Dr. William Scoville, a prominent figure who had himself performed lobotomies, published a paper in 1957 advocating for a “moratorium on prefrontal leucotomy” pending further chemical research.

Francis Grant, chairman of the American Board of Neurosurgery, issued a statement to his members advising that psychosurgery “should now be considered only in cases of intractable illness where all pharmacological options have been exhausted.”

The phrase “intractable illness” became the new standard. It narrowed the method's application to a tiny fraction of patients.

The institutional machinery of medicine turned slowly, but it turned. The American Medical Association’s Council on Mental Health issued a revised set of guidelines in 1958.

The previous guidelines, from 1950, had listed the procedure as a “recommended intervention for certain chronic psychotic states.” The 1958 revision replaced that language.

It now stated that “pharmacological management should be the first line of treatment for acute psychotic disorders.” Surgery was relegated to a footnote: “Psychosurgery may be considered in cases where medication therapy has proven ineffective after a sustained trial.”

The document was distributed to every state medical board.

The effect on his practice was concrete. State hospitals that had once welcomed him as a cost-saving solution now required proof that a patient had failed on chemical regimens before approving surgery.

His own records show that by 1959, over half of his prospective surgical cases were delayed or canceled because the patient’s institution demanded a six-month trial of chlorpromazine first.

Many of those subjects, once medicated, no longer met the threshold for “intractable” violence. His surgical volume began to drop.

In 1954, he performed 345 procedures. In 1957, he performed 228. In 1959, the number fell to 112.

The professional isolation was compounded by a shift in media coverage. Newspaper articles from the late 1950s, indexed in his own scrapbooks, show a change in tone.

Earlier pieces had often portrayed him as a pioneering, somewhat heroic figure battling the scourge of mental illness. Later articles framed him as a controversial holdout.

A 1958 profile in The Washington Post described him as “a relic of a more invasive age” and contrasted his methods with the “modern, pill-based approach” prevailing at St. Elizabeths. He kept the clipping. He underlined the word “relic.”

The final, formal repudiation came from within his own specialty. In 1960, the American Psychiatric Association released a position paper on treatment standards.

It was the product of a two-year committee study. The paper’s introduction acknowledged the historical role of the surgery.

Its conclusions were unequivocal. “The standard of care for psychotic disorders,” it stated, “is now pharmacological intervention combined with psychosocial support. Psychosurgery is not a standard treatment.”

The paper was endorsed by the American Neurological Association and the Society of Biological Psychiatry. He was a member of all three organizations.

He received the mailed pamphlet. He did not attend the annual meeting where it was presented.

The evidence trail shows not a sudden revolt, but a steady, systemic abandonment. It was not driven by a single scandal or a dramatic expose.

It was driven by data, by cost analysis, and by the quiet accumulation of outcomes on thousands of treated subjects.

The medical establishment did not denounce him; it simply walked away, redirecting its attention, its funding, and its authority toward the pharmacy.

He continued to lecture, to publish, and to tour. But the audience grew smaller. The invitations grew scarce.

The grants dried up. He became a man speaking into a room that was emptying, his words echoing against the walls of a profession that had already turned its back.

The chemical revolution, then, did not end the procedure on ethical grounds. It ended it on practical ones.

Chlorpromazine and its successors were more manageable, more profitable, and reversible. They offered administrators a calculable cost per patient per month instead of a one-time surgical fee.

They offered pharmaceutical companies a recurring revenue stream instead of a single instrument sale. They offered doctors a dial they could turn up or down, instead of a permanent, irrevocable cut.

The shift was not a moral awakening; it was a logistical and economic optimization.

The financial gravity pulled the profession toward the pill.

But this new model carried its own shadow. These compounds did not cure schizophrenia or manic-depression. They suppressed their most disruptive symptoms.

They traded the permanent blankness of the surgical subject for a state of drugged compliance. The side effects were debilitating and often permanent.

Tardive dyskinesia—the involuntary movements of the face and limbs caused by long-term antipsychotic use—began appearing in patients by the late 1950s.

A 1960 study published in The Archives of General Psychiatry documented its onset in approximately twenty percent of patients on chronic regimens.

The condition was irreversible. Yet because it emerged gradually, and because the patient remained quiet and manageable, it was often tolerated.

A memo from the medical director of Greystone Park in 1961, reviewing the trade-offs, stated plainly: “The control of violent behavior achieved with Thorazine outweighs the risk of tardive dyskinesia in our most difficult cases.”

The ethical dilemma was not resolved. It was transferred. The question of therapeutic intent—was the treatment for the patient’s benefit or for the institution’s convenience?—persisted.

A 1959 internal audit at Milledgeville State Hospital showed that seventy percent of prescriptions for the drug were written for “behavioral control” rather than “therapeutic relief.”

The medications were used to maintain ward order, to reduce staffing requirements, to facilitate mass feeding and bathing routines. This was not the rhetoric of healing Freeman had used; it was the rhetoric of efficiency, now dressed in pharmacological language.

The profit motive shifted venues. He had profited, but his was an artisan’s profit—the fee for a procedure, the sale of a book.

The pharmaceutical industry’s profit was industrial. Between 1955 and 1960, sales of the chemical in the United States grew from $2 million annually to over $75 million.

Drug companies invested not in surgical tools, but in marketing. They distributed free samples to hospital pharmacies.

They funded clinical studies that emphasized the medications' advantages over surgery. They sponsored symposia for psychiatrists where the operation was described as “archaic.”

And the patient’s voice remained muffled. The surgical method had erased it. The medications subdued it.

Patient autonomy was not a primary concern in either model. Consent for the surgery was often obtained from family members or guardians, not the subject.

Consent for long-term administration was rarely sought at all; it was considered a medical necessity. The legal framework of “informed consent,” which would emerge in the 1970s, was absent in both eras.

What the chemical revolution revealed, therefore, was not a sudden commitment to patient dignity. It revealed a persistent institutional desire for simple, controlling solutions to complex mental suffering.

The icepick was a crude, dramatic solution. The pill was a subtle, repeatable one. Both were tools of management.

Both served systems overwhelmed by numbers. The difference was that the pill allowed the system to pretend it was offering treatment, not just control.

This was the uncomfortable truth that lingered after the surgeon's roadshow ended. The wards were quieter. The restraints were fewer.

The outpatient clinics grew. But the fundamental calculus—the weighing of a person’s interior life against institutional efficiency—remained unchanged.

The needle was cleaner than the pick. It was less bloody. It was reversible, in theory.

But its widespread, often indiscriminate use created a new population of patients: not surgical subjects, but individuals chemically subdued; not empty, but muted; not surgically altered, but pharmacologically restrained.

The history of psychiatry in the twentieth century is not a linear march from barbarism to enlightenment. It is a series of substitutions, each new method promising to correct the horrors of the last, each carrying its own hidden cost.

The surgery was replaced not because it was deemed unethical by a consensus of conscience, but because a cheaper, less messy, and more scalable alternative appeared.

The ethical reckoning with chemical restraint would come later, in the 1970s and 1980s, with the lawsuits over tardive dyskinesia and the exposes of over-medication in state hospitals.

That reckoning would echo the earlier questions about the method: Who benefited? Who was harmed? And who decided?

The conclusion of the practice, then, was not a victory for medical ethics. It was a victory for medical economics and administrative convenience.

It left the deeper questions unanswered. It left the underlying temptation—the desire for a definitive, controlling solution to the messy, resistant human mind—unexamined.

That temptation would wait, dormant, for the next miracle cure to arrive.
