The synagogue air smelled of old parchment and cedar oil, the benches worn smooth by generations of argument. Saul gripped the edge of the reading desk, his knuckles pressing against the grain. The faces before him were a mosaic of disbelief—furrowed brows, lips pressed thin, fingers drumming against thighs. A man in the front row kept cracking his knuckles. The sound was a tiny hammer on Saul’s nerves.

“Isaiah saw the Lord high and lifted up,” he said. His voice didn’t sound like his own. It was too loud, too sharp, the way it had been in Damascus when he first stood before a crowd like this. “The seraphim cried, ‘Holy, holy, holy.’ But the prophet said, ‘Woe is me, for I am undone.’” He paused. The knuckle-cracker stopped mid-pop. “Why undone? Because he saw the King—and knew himself unclean.”

A woman near the back snorted. “And you’re clean now, Saul of Tarsus?”

The question hung in the air like smoke. Someone coughed. A child kicked the bench legs, the rhythmic thud underscoring the silence.

Saul exhaled through his nose. The scar on his palm itched—the one from the Damascus wall, where the basket’s rope had burned him. “No,” he said. “But the coal touched my lips anyway.”

The room rippled. A man with a silver beard—Rabbi Eliezer, who’d once praised Saul’s zeal—shook his head. “You quote scripture like a man who’s found a new toy. But we remember how you wielded those same words like a lash.”

“I wielded them wrong.”

“Or you wield them wrong now.”

A fly buzzed against the high window, desperate for the fading light. Saul tracked its frantic path. He’d prepared arguments—prophecies about suffering servants, Psalms about pierced hands—but they felt like stones in his mouth. These people didn’t need convincing. They needed to spit in his face.

A young man near the door stood abruptly. “You dragged my cousin to the Sanhedrin for saying less than you’re saying now.”

Saul’s gut tightened. He didn’t ask the cousin’s name. He didn’t want to know. “Then let them drag me.”

The room erupted.

Outside, the street was eerily quiet. The sun had dipped behind the rooftops, stretching the shadows long and thin. Saul’s sandals scuffed the stones. His throat was raw. He’d shouted more than he’d intended.

A figure leaned against a wall ahead—Barnabas, arms crossed, his face unreadable. “They didn’t throw anything this time.”

“They considered it.”

Barnabas pushed off the wall and fell into step beside him. “You can’t keep doing this.”

“I have to.”

“Not like this. Not—” Barnabas cut himself off, rubbing his temple. “You’re not debating. You’re provoking.”

Saul stopped walking. A donkey brayed in the distance, the sound lonely and sharp. “They need to hear it.”

“They need to hear it from someone they don’t want to stone.”

The words landed like a slap. Saul turned away, studying the cracks in the street. A line of ants marched single-file through a fissure, carrying crumbs twice their size.

Barnabas sighed. “There’s a gathering tonight. At Miriam’s.”

“The widow?”

“She asked for you.”

Saul’s chest constricted. The last time he’d seen Miriam, her silence had been a blade. “Why?”

“I don’t know. But she didn’t look like she wanted to kill you.”

“That’s an improvement.”

Barnabas didn’t laugh.

The muttering started as they turned the corner. Saul didn’t need to look back to know who it was—the Hellenists from the synagogue, clustered in a tight knot. Their voices were low, but the tone carried.

…dangerous…

…twisted the Law…

…should’ve stayed blind…

Barnabas tensed beside him. “Don’t turn around.”

Saul didn’t. But the words slithered down his spine anyway, cold and familiar. He’d heard that tone before—in his own voice, in the halls of the Sanhedrin, in the moments before the stones flew.

The chill was the same.

The room smelled of lamp oil and the faint metallic tang of the city’s water. Saul sat on the edge of a low bench, his fingers tracing the rough grain of the wood. The house was too quiet—no voices from the courtyard, no footsteps in the hall. Just the occasional creak of the roof beams settling in the cooling night.

Peter stood by the window, his silhouette cutting a sharp line against the pale rectangle of moonlight. He hadn’t spoken since Saul finished recounting the day. James, seated across from him, picked at a loose thread on his sleeve. The silence was worse than the shouting in the synagogue.

“You’re certain you didn’t provoke them?” James finally said.

Saul’s jaw tightened. “I quoted Isaiah.”

“Isaiah provokes.” Peter’s voice was flat. “You know that.”

A moth fluttered against the lamp, casting frantic shadows. Saul watched it batter itself against the clay rim. “They were provoked before I opened my mouth.”

James exhaled through his nose. “And the Hellenists following you after?”

“They followed Barnabas too.”

“Barnabas didn’t used to hunt them.”

The words landed like a stone in still water. Saul’s fingers stilled on the bench. He could recite every prophecy about the Messiah’s suffering, every Psalm that foretold the piercing of hands and feet. But this wasn’t about scripture. It was about the space between his ribs where their distrust lived, sharp and unyielding.

“You think I’m still that man.”

Peter turned from the window. The moonlight caught the lines around his eyes, the weariness Saul hadn’t noticed before. “I think you don’t know what you look like to them. To us.”

The moth gave up and spiraled to the table, wings twitching. Saul focused on the way its antennae trembled. “Then tell me.”

James leaned forward, elbows on his knees. “You walk into a room and people see the man who dragged their brothers to prison. You open your mouth and they hear the voice that condemned their sisters. You can’t—” He broke off, rubbing his forehead. “You can’t preach like the others. Not yet.”

Saul’s pulse thudded in his throat. He wanted to argue, to list every hour he’d spent in Arabia wrestling with the Law and the Prophets until the pieces fit. But the words wouldn’t come. Instead, he heard Elisheva’s voice from Damascus, soft and terrible: You don’t get to be the hero of this story.

Peter sighed. “We’re not saying stop.”

“Be quieter,” James finished.

The lamp flickered. Outside, a dog barked, the sound echoing down the alley. Saul stood abruptly, the bench scraping against the floor. “I’ll take the roof.”

Neither man objected.

The night air was cooler above the city, the stars sharp through the haze of smoke and dust. Saul lay back on the flat clay tiles, his arms folded behind his head. The ache in his chest was familiar now—not the blinding pain of Damascus, but a dull, persistent weight, like a stone sewn into his tunic.

Footsteps sounded on the stairs. He didn’t turn.

“You’re not subtle,” Lucius Valerius said, leaning against the parapet. The Roman’s armor gleamed dully in the starlight, the leather straps creaking as he shifted. “Half the city knows where you are by now.”

Saul closed his eyes. “Then why haven’t they come?”

“Maybe they’re waiting for you to do their work for them.”

A breeze carried the scent of charred bread from a nearby oven. Lucius had been shadowing him since Jerusalem, a silent observer with no apparent orders. Saul had stopped asking why.

“Your friends don’t trust you,” Lucius said.

“They’re not my friends.”

“No?” Lucius tilted his head. “Then why do you care what they think?”

Saul exhaled through his nose. The stars blurred above him. “You wouldn’t understand.”

“Try me.”

Below, a door creaked open. Voices floated up—James and Peter, speaking in hushed tones. Saul held his breath.

"…too much like before…

"…dangerous for everyone…

"…if he’s wrong again…

Lucius raised an eyebrow. “Sounds like a lively debate.”

Saul rolled onto his side, facing the city. The rooftops stretched like a jagged sea, the Temple Mount looming dark against the sky. He’d stood there once, certain of everything. Now the certainty was gone, but the weight of it remained—the choices he couldn’t unmake, the faces he couldn’t forget.

Lucius pushed off the parapet. “For what it’s worth, I think you’re wasting your time.”

“Why?”

“Because no one believes a convert.” Lucius’s sandals scuffed against the tiles as he walked away. “Especially not one who used to burn their holy books.”

The stairs creaked under his weight. Saul didn’t watch him go.

A cat yowled somewhere in the dark. The voices below had faded. Saul sat up, rubbing his palms against his thighs. The scar from the Damascus rope was rough under his fingers.

He’d thought the blindness was the hard part.

The room below was empty when he descended. The lamp had burned low, the wick drowning in its own wax. A single cup sat on the table, the dregs of wine dark as old blood.

Saul picked it up. The clay was cool against his palm. He could pour it out, smash it against the wall, watch the shards scatter like the pieces of his old life.

Instead, he set it down carefully.

The courtyard was silent. The dog had stopped barking. Even the insects had gone quiet. Saul stood in the doorway, the night pressing against his back.

Somewhere in the city, the Hellenists were whispering. The apostles were doubting. The Romans were watching.

And he was here. Alone.

The weight in his chest shifted, settling deeper.

He stepped into the dark.

The Temple courts swarmed with pilgrims and merchants, the air thick with the smell of roasting meat and crushed herbs. Saul wove through the crowd, his pulse loud in his ears. The morning sun glared off the white stone, sharp enough to make his eyes water. Or maybe it was the lack of sleep. He’d spent the night on the roof, chasing fragments of dreams that dissolved like smoke when he reached for them.

A group of men clustered near the money-changers’ tables. Saul recognized the cut of their robes—Hellenists, the same ones who’d muttered behind his back yesterday. One of them, a broad-shouldered man with a scar across his cheekbone, caught Saul’s eye and held it. The look was a challenge. Saul’s throat went dry. He knew that face. Not the name, but the way the man had flinched when Saul’s men dragged his brother away in Damascus.

Barnabas gripped his elbow. “Not here.”

Saul shook him off. “Where, then?”

The words came out harsher than he’d intended. Barnabas’s mouth tightened, but he didn’t argue.

A woman shoved past them, her basket of doves jostling against Saul’s side. The birds cooed, soft and frantic. He could feel the Hellenists watching. Their stares prickled like heat on the back of his neck.

He turned toward the colonnade, where a handful of people had gathered to listen to a Pharisee debate ritual purity. The man’s voice carried, precise and condescending. Saul had heard this lecture before—had given it, years ago, to wide-eyed students in Tarsus. The memory sat like a pebble in his shoe.

He stepped into the open space beside the speaker. The Pharisee broke off mid-sentence, his brow furrowing. Saul didn’t wait for permission.

“You argue about the outside of the cup,” he said. The words spilled out before he could weigh them. “But what about the hand that holds it?”

The crowd stirred. A few heads turned. The Pharisee’s nostrils flared. “Who are you to interrupt?”

Saul’s pulse hammered. He could still walk away. He could let the moment pass.

But the scarred Hellenist was pushing through the crowd now, his friends at his back. Saul’s breath hitched. He knew what came next.

“I’m the man who used to care more about the cup than the hand,” he said, louder. “Who thought righteousness was a list of rules, not a heart turned toward God.”

The Pharisee’s face darkened. “Blasphemy.”

The word hung in the air. The crowd rippled. Someone gasped.

The scarred man reached the front, his hands clenched at his sides. “This is the one,” he said, voice rough with fury. “The one who arrested my brother in Damascus. Now he stands in the Temple courts and spits on the Law?”

The accusation landed like a torch in dry grass. The crowd surged forward, voices overlapping.

Traitor!

Stone him!

Saul’s vision narrowed. The faces blurred—angry mouths, bared teeth, hands reaching for him. Then, for a heartbeat, he saw Stephen. Not as he’d last seen him, bloody and broken, but as he’d been in that first moment when the stones began to fly—eyes wide, breath ragged, the realization of what was coming crashing over him.

The terror was the same.

A rock grazed Saul’s temple. Warmth trickled down his cheek. He staggered back, the world tilting. Hands grabbed at his tunic, yanking him off balance.

Barnabas’s voice cut through the noise. “Enough!”

A wedge of bodies shoved between Saul and the mob—believers from Miriam’s house, their faces set like flint. A woman with a baker’s strong arms planted herself in front of him, her stance wide. The scarred man lunged, and she caught his wrist, twisting it back with a practiced motion.

“You don’t want this fight,” she said, low and steady.

The man wrenched free, but the momentum had shifted. The crowd hesitated, milling like sheep without a shepherd.

Lucius Valerius leaned against a pillar nearby, his expression bored. He picked at a fig with his thumbnail, not even bothering to watch the scuffle.

Barnabas hauled Saul upright. “Move.”

They stumbled back through the colonnade, the believers forming a tight circle around them. The mob’s shouts faded behind them, replaced by the rhythmic slap of sandals on stone. Saul’s head throbbed. The blood dripped onto his tunic, bright against the wool.

They rounded a corner into a narrow alley. The baker woman pressed a cloth to Saul’s temple, her fingers firm. “Idiot,” she muttered.

Saul didn’t argue. The weight in his chest had turned liquid, seeping into his ribs. He’d thought he understood what he’d done in Damascus. But understanding was one thing. Feeling it—the sheer animal panic of the stones flying, the certainty that this was how it ended—that was something else.

Barnabas gripped his shoulder. “You can’t stay in Jerusalem.”

The words weren’t a suggestion. Saul wiped his face with his sleeve. The cloth came away red.

“Where, then?” he asked again, but this time it was a whisper.

Barnabas exhaled. “Home. Tarsus.”

The name landed like a sentence. Saul closed his eyes. The image of Stephen flickered behind his lids, but this time, it wasn’t fear he saw. It was peace. The kind that came after the stones stopped.

The baker woman tied the cloth around his head, the knot tight. “You’re lucky it wasn’t worse.”

Saul knew better. Luck had nothing to do with it.

Footsteps echoed at the mouth of the alley. Lucius appeared, his armor gleaming dully in the shadowed lane. He took in the scene—the blood, the believers, Saul’s ashen face—and smirked.

“Still wasting your time, I see.”

Barnabas stepped forward. “This doesn’t concern Rome.”

Lucius shrugged. “Not yet.” His gaze slid to Saul. “But it will.”

The threat hung in the air, heavier than the blood still dripping down Saul’s neck.

Barnabas pulled Saul to his feet. “We’re leaving.”

Lucius didn’t move to stop them. He just watched as they shuffled down the alley, his expression unreadable.

The believers formed a tight knot around Saul, their bodies shielding him from view. The baker woman kept a hand on her knife.

Saul’s legs moved without his permission. The city blurred around him—white walls, dark doorways, the occasional flash of a startled face.

Somewhere behind them, the mob’s shouts rose again, distant but undeterred.

And beneath it all, the memory of Stephen’s face, not accusing. Just waiting.

The weight in Saul’s chest settled deeper.

He kept walking.
