The neon sign buzzed like a dying mosquito, its blue light flickering across the waterlogged dock. Jesus stepped onto the warped planks, the dummy's plastic arm slung over his shoulder. It smelled like fried gator and regret.

Inside, the bar was a taxidermist's fever dream. Stuffed alligator heads lined the walls, their glass eyes tracking the newcomers. A ceiling fan spun lazily, stirring the humid air into something resembling a breeze. The jukebox skipped. Waylon Jennings stuttered about Luckenbach, Texas.

Mary nudged a chair out with her boot. "We need to—"

"Not here." Jesus jerked his chin toward a booth in the back, half-shadowed by a broken Budweiser sign. The bartender—a woman with a shotgun and a nametag that read "Marge"—watched them without interest.

Rex dripped onto the floor, his scales clicking. "My internal diagnostics suggest I am now approximately fourteen percent bioluminescent swamp water."

"Congratulations," Mary said. She slid into the booth, her fingers tapping an arrhythmic pattern on the table. The dummy slumped beside her, its painted smile chipped at the corners.

Jesus scanned the room. Two fishermen arguing over a bass tournament. A woman in a sequined dress crying into a piña colada. A kid playing Galaga in the corner. No Eden auditors. Yet.

Mary leaned in. "Judas-9."

The name landed like a gut punch. The preacher-dummy from the shanty boat had broadcast their location before sinking. Static and betrayal, wrapped in a holy suit.

Rex's tail twitched. "The signal originated from an archive. A storage facility. Or a tomb."

The jukebox died mid-chorus. For a second, the only sound was the hum of the swamp through the floorboards. Then the TV above the bar crackled to life.

"—breaking news out of the Everglades," said a too-perfect anchor. The screen showed grainy footage of their airboat fleeing the glowing church. "Authorities are searching for these individuals in connection with tonight's disturbance at the First Church of Perpetual Rebirth."

Mary's knee bounced under the table. "They edited out the gator."

"Of course they did." Jesus watched his own pixelated face flash on screen. The chyron below read: CULT ACTIVISTS SOUGHT IN MIRACLE FRAUD.

The bartender muted the TV. She didn't look at them.

Rex's nostrils flared. "The broadcast is piggybacking on Eden's emergency alert system. Standard protocol for containment breaches."

A phone rang at the bar. Marge answered, listened, then hung up. She reached under the counter and came up with a baseball bat.

Jesus stood. "Time to go."

The front door burst open. SWAT helmets gleamed in the neon.

"Or not," Mary said.

The lead officer's visor reflected the stuffed gator heads as he stepped inside. "We're looking for a dinosaur, a carpenter, and a hacker." He glanced at Rex. "Two out of three ain't bad."

Rex's reactor whined. "I take offense to that categorization."

Jesus grabbed the dummy. Mary kicked the table into the closest officer. The Galaga machine exploded in a shower of sparks as Rex's tail took out the power line.

Darkness. Then the hum returned—louder now, vibrating up through the floor. The sound of something old waking up.

The SWAT team's radios screeched static. One voice broke through: "—containment breach at Archive Nine—"

Mary was already moving. "Judas-9 just sold us out again."

Jesus followed her toward the back door, the dummy's dead weight banging against his hip. The hum followed them, a sound like a thousand whispered betrayals.

Outside, the swamp waited. The water was perfectly still.

Too still.

Somewhere deep in the swamp, the water answered.

The answering ripple spread until it lapped against a stone embankment miles from the village. Here, the swamp gave way to orderly canals. A figure waited in a small, flat-bottomed skiff, hand extended over the water. Her palm felt the ripple’s arrival.

She pulled the hand back. "It’s time."

Her companion, a man with skin the grey-green of waterlogged cypress, didn’t look up from his net mending. "The old woman’s dead?"

"The water says so."

He grunted, a sound like a frog diving. "About time. You’ve waited long enough, Mira."

Mira dipped an oar, turning the skiff toward a channel choked with floating fern. "Waiting’s the easy part. Claiming’s the fight."

In the hut, Lira stared at the empty hammock. The body was gone. Only the indent in the woven fibers remained, a shallow bowl of absence.

"She’s with the water now."

The voice came from the doorway. An elder Lira knew as Bren leaned against the frame, arms crossed. His eyes were on the empty space, not on her.

"Where did they take her?" Lira’s own voice felt thin, borrowed.

"Doesn’t matter. You’re not from here. You don’t follow our dead." Bren shifted his weight. "You’ll be leaving. Today."

It wasn’t a question. Lira’s fingers found the edge of the table, the wood smooth from generations of touch. "She said I had a place."

"Her place. Not ours." Bren finally looked at her. His gaze was flat, like still water over deep mud. "You got her final days. That’s gift enough. Pack your things."

He didn’t wait for a reply. His footsteps squelched away from the hut.

Lira moved to the hearth. The clay pot with the last of the stew was cold. She lifted it. Beneath it, pressed into the packed earth of the hearthstone, was a single, water-smoothed key. It was black stone, warm to the touch. She hadn’t seen the old woman put it there.

She pocketed it. The action felt final.

Her pack was meager: a change of clothes, a water skin, the knife. She slung it over her shoulder and stepped outside. The village seemed different. The children who usually watched her with open curiosity were gone. The women at the drying racks kept their backs turned. The path to the main channel was just mud and roots.

No one stopped her. No one said goodbye.

She pushed her borrowed canoe into the tea-dark water. As she picked up the paddle, a young boy darted from behind a stilt-house. He threw a small, cloth-wrapped bundle. It landed at her feet with a soft thud.

"From my ma," he whispered, then vanished.

Lira unwrapped it. Hard travel bread, a wedge of salty cheese. She tucked it into her pack, her throat tight. She didn’t look back as the current caught the canoe.

Mira’s skiff slid through a tunnel of mangrove roots. The air grew thick, still. Light fractured into green shards.

"She’ll go to the Crossroads," the man said, finally setting his mending aside. "Everyone does."

"I know."

"You want me to meet her there? Explain the situation?"

Mira smiled. It didn’t reach her eyes. "No, Joren. I’ll do the explaining. You just make sure the Crossroads is… receptive."

Joren nodded once. He picked up a pole and pushed against the murky bottom, angling their skiff down a narrower fork. "And if she doesn’t want the key? If she just throws it in the water and walks away?"

"Then the water will bring it to me anyway." Mira trailed her fingers through the surface. "But she won’t throw it away. The old woman chose a keeper, not a courier. You can feel a thing like that."

"I feel moss and rot."

"Exactly."

The tunnel opened into a wide, circular pool. In its center stood a structure on stilts—a waystation, its wood silvered by age. The sign above its door, carved with a stylized river fork, was the only marker for miles: The Crossroads.

Mira’s smile turned genuine. "Let’s get a drink. The waiting’s almost over."

Lira let the current do the work. The swamp unspooled around her, a monotony of green and brown. Her mind was blank, a defense against the hollow feeling in her chest. She focused on the mechanics: dip the paddle, pull, lift. Repeat.

The key was a weight against her thigh.

Ahead, the channel branched. Left, the water flowed swift and clear toward distant hills. Right, it turned sluggish, dark, winding back into deeper swamp. Tied to a half-submerged log at the fork was a rickety signpost. One arrow, pointing left, read "Upland Road." The other, pointing right, had no text, only the same river fork symbol she’d seen on scraps in the old woman’s hut.

She steered toward the right fork.

The way narrowed. Mangrove roots formed a knotted archway. As she passed under it, the light changed. The oppressive green gloom lifted to a soft, grey luminescence. The water here was black glass.

Around a final bend, the waystation appeared. It looked deserted. One shutter hung from a single hinge. A skiff was tied at its dock, empty.

Lira nudged her canoe against the dock. The wood groaned under her weight as she climbed out. Her legs were stiff. The silence here was complete, no birdcalls, no insect hum.

She pushed it open. The room inside was a single space: a low bar made of driftwood, a few tables, shelves mostly bare. A woman stood behind the bar, polishing a glass with a cloth. She looked up.

"You made good time," Mira said.
