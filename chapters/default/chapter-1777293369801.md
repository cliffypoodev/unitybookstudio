Jolene’s heart skipped a beat. Not a metaphorical one. The titanium valve in her left ventricle hissed, caught, and gave a solid thunk against her ribs.

She smacked her own chest. “Knock it off.”

The drone’s cockpit smelled of ozone, burnt wiring, and the faint, persistent scent of existential fuel—something like despair mixed with low-grade diesel. Outside the curved viewport, the interdimensional highway unspooled in ribbons of neon and void. Billboards for soul-realignment services and fast-food salvation flickered past. A holographic map, styled like some medieval monk’s fever dream, glowed on her main screen. It showed her route as a dotted pilgrim’s path winding through constellations labeled with things like “Perdition’s Rest Stop” and “Elysian Fields Truck Plaza.”

The delivery chime rang. Not the standard bing-bong. This was a deep, resonant gong that vibrated through the floor plates.

“Oh, hell,” Jolene muttered. Divine class. Nothing but trouble.

She tapped the screen. The order manifest appeared, glowing with soft, gold-leaf lettering.

RECIPIENT: The Township of Absalom. DESTINATION: Coordinates: [REDACTED BY DIVINE ORDINANCE]. PACKAGE CONTENTS: One (1) Celestial Reminder. DELIVERY WINDOW: Before the sound of the last trumpet. (See Appendix 7-B for estimated local apocalypse timelines.)
SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS: Do not open. Seriously. Soul forfeiture clause 3.2 applies.

Jolene scrolled. The destination field wasn’t just blank. It pulsed with a gentle, assertive nullity. She pulled up her navigation database and typed “Absalom.”

SEARCH RESULTS: 0.

She tried the regional atlas. Zero. The historical settlement logs. She even accessed the Celestial Cartography Guild’s public records, which usually had entries for places that had only been imagined in particularly vivid dreams.

Nothing.

“Hey, Mabel,” Jolene said. “Cross-reference ‘Absalom’ with all known dimensional manifolds, including theoretical and heresy-class.”

The drone’s AI voice was a calm, maternal alto. “Cross-referencing. One moment, dear.” A soft whirring filled the cockpit. “I’m not finding any municipal entity, township, hamlet, or metaphysical confluence registered under that designation. Are you certain you spelled it correctly?”

“It’s on the manifest.”

“The manifest may be in error.”

“It’s a divine manifest, Mabel. They don’t do typos. They do plagues and moral tests.”

“Perhaps it’s a new development,” Mabel offered. “A pop-up suburb. They do that in the seventh quadrant.”

Jolene called up the real-time dimensional overlay. The highway around her was a river of light and shadow, but off the designated exits, she could see the faint gossamer of other realities—suburban neighborhoods where lawn gnomes came alive at night, noir cityscapes where it always rained, one entire plane that seemed to be a never-ending IKEA. No new towns. Especially not one with a name like “Absalom.” It sounded dusty. Full of regrets.

Her chest gave another soft thunk.

She ignored it and punched in a direct query to the Dispatch mainframe. The response was immediate.

QUERY RECEIVED. DESTINATION: ABSALOM. STATUS: CONFIRMED. DELIVERY MANDATORY. FAILURE TO DELIVER CONSTITUTES BREACH OF CONTRACT CLAUSE 12: SOUL FORFEITURE.

“Well, that’s dramatic,” Jolene said aloud.

“The mainframe does not engage in drama,” Mabel replied. “It engages in contractual enforcement. Your soul is listed as collateral on your equipment lease, Jolene. I’ve reminded you to read the fine print.”

“I was busy.” She’d been trying to feel something that day. She’d put her hand on the engine casing of a grounded delivery skiff and waited for a spark. Nothing. Just warm metal.

She stared at the manifest. The “do not open” instruction glowed brighter than the rest. Every delivery driver’s curse was that instruction. It was like putting a single “DO NOT PRESS” button in a room full of toddlers with caffeine addictions.

The cargo hold indicator lit up. Package ready.

Jolene unstrapped herself. The cockpit was so cramped she had to turn sideways to shuffle back to the hold. The door hissed open.

The divine package sat alone in the center of the sterile space. It was a simple, polished wooden box about the size of a loaf of bread. No markings. No latches. It just sat there, projecting an aura of profound importance and incredible inconvenience.

She knelt. The floor grille imprint left a checkerboard pattern on her knee. She didn’t reach for the box. Instead, she leaned forward and pressed her lips to the smooth wood, right above where she guessed a locking mechanism might be.

Her mechanical heart cycled up. A hum built in her ears.

The box didn’t speak in words. Machines never did with her. It communicated in impressions. A feeling of immense age. Long, long lists. The dry scent of parchment and the chill of a deep, administrative vault. And underneath it, a sharp, metallic fear.

She pulled back, wiping her mouth. “It’s scared.”

“Inanimate objects do not experience fear,” Mabel said through the hold’s speaker.

“This one does.” Jolene stood. Her heart valve clicked back into its normal rhythm. “It’s like it knows where it’s going is worse than being lost.”

“That is not a navigational data point I can use.”

“I know.” Jolene returned to the cockpit and dropped into her seat. The interdimensional highway curved ahead, a neon-green ramp spiraling up toward a cluster of reality layers that looked like floating soap bubbles. Her medieval map flickered, the dotted pilgrim’s path now ending abruptly in the middle of a labeled void: HIC SUNT DRACONES.

Here be dragons. Classic cartographer cop-out.

“Plot a course to the last known coordinates before the destination redaction,” Jolene instructed.

“Calculating.” Mabel was silent for a moment. “The coordinates are in a non-traversable buffer zone between the reality of ‘Endless Paperwork’ and the demesne of ‘The Accounting That Waits.’ The dimensional shear would tear the drone apart.”

“What about a soul?”

“Theological constructs are more resilient than aluminum alloy, generally. But I wouldn’t recommend it.”

Jolene leaned her head against the cool viewport. A billboard for a heart-tuner service slid past. Tired of existential arrhythmia? We’ll sync your beat to the cosmic drum! She’d been there. The tuner, a guy named Phil with too many eyes, had tapped her chest and listened with a stethoscope that ended in a tiny galaxy. “You’re fighting it,” he’d said. “This heart wants to count down to something. You keep trying to make it feel.”

She looked at the manifest again. Soul forfeiture.

The chime rang once more, insistent. A new message scrolled across the bottom of the screen.

REMINDER: DELIVERY BEFORE LAST TRUMPET. ESTIMATED TIME TO LOCAL APOCALYPSE (APPENDIX 7-B, SUB-SECTION C): 3 HOURS, 14 MINUTES.

“Great.” Jolene started the drone’s engines. The hum built, a physical vibration in her teeth. “Mabel, give me every scrap of data on ‘Absalom.’ Folklore. Myth. Misheard song lyrics. Anything.”

“Compiling.”

Data began to stream. Fragments.

…a name mentioned in a lost gospel, the ‘Gospel of the Missed Deadline’…
…a theoretical town proposed by 14th-century scholastics as the place where unanswered prayers go to retire…
…a typo in a celestial ledger from 302 B.C.

It was all ghosts. Echoes of a place that had never been.

Her personal comm buzzed. She thumbed it open.

A man’s face filled the small screen—weary, kind, framed by the grease-stained walls of the garage back in the Prime Material plane. Hank. Her sometimes-boss, full-time worrywart.

“Jo. You picking up weird routing?”

“You could say that.”

“Heard a ripple on the dispatch band. Divine class to a non-loc. That’s a soul-snagger. You drop it?”

“Not yet.”

“Drop it.” Hank’s face was serious. “Leave it on the side of the highway. Let some angelic courier pick it up. That’s their mess.”

“My name’s on the manifest.”

“So? They’ll process your damnation before you finish apologizing.” He scratched his chin. “How’s the heart?”

Thunk. “Fine.”

“It’s not fine. It’s trying to tell you something. Listen to it.”

“It tells me to check the oxygen levels and regulate my cholesterol. It’s a mechanic, Hank. Not a poet.”

“Everything mechanical is trying to be something else.” Hank looked off-screen, yelled, “No, don’t touch that! It’s holy!” He turned back. “Just… be smart, Jo. You’re too young to spend eternity filing forms in perdition.”

The call died.

Jolene stared at the box on her hold monitor. It just sat there.

She couldn’t deliver it to a nowhere. She couldn’t open it. She couldn’t abandon it.

Three hours.

“Mabel. Take us to the buffer zone. The edge of it.”

“The shear—”

“Just get us close. I need to see it.”

The drone banked, leaving the neon river of the main highway for a darker, quieter tributary of reality. The billboards faded. The light outside dimmed to a twilight grey. The holographic map fizzed, the medieval illustrations dissolving into static.

They flew in silence for twenty minutes. The only sound was the drone’s engine and the occasional thunk from her chest.

The buffer zone wasn’t a place. It was an absence. A tear in the fabric of everything else, a long, ragged scar of non-existence fringed with crackling energy. It looked like a TV tuned to a dead channel, stretched across infinity.

“There is no town here,” Mabel stated, voice softer. “There is not even a ‘here’ here.”

Jolene’s eyes stung from staring into the nullity. It was the opposite of a thing. Her mechanical heart began to beat faster, a steady, rhythmic thump-thump-thump that had nothing to do with blood and everything to do with panic.

She had a choice. It was stupid, but it was hers.

She could turn around, log a “destination unreachable” exception, and fight the soul forfeiture in the celestial courts. That would take centuries. She’d need a lawyer who specialized in eschatological contract law.

Or.

She unstrapped herself again and went back to the hold.

The wooden box waited.

“I’m not opening you,” she told it. “I’m just… checking the address.”

She knelt. This time, she didn’t kiss it. She ran her fingers over the grain. Smooth. Perfect. No seam, no lid. It was a solid block of purpose.

Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic prisoner in a titanium cage. She wanted to feel real fear, real desperation. All she got was the physiological report: adrenaline spike, respiratory increase, systemic alert.

“Damn it,” she whispered.

She leaned forward and put her mouth to the wood again, closing her eyes.

This time, the impression was a single, clear image.

A scroll. Unfurling. And on it, in a handwriting that hurt to look at, a list.

Item 1: Fix the leak in the firmament. (See: Rainbow Covenant, clause re: flooding.)
Item 2: Re: Soul of Keith. Review petition for reconsideration. (Note: He was genuinely sorry about the hamsters.)
Item 3: Check on the Township of Absalom.

The image cut off.

The box trembled under her lips.

Jolene jerked back. The box was now glowing, a soft, internal light seeping through the grain.

“Oh, you have got to be kidding me.”

The “do not open” instruction on the manifest flared, then winked out.

The box was opening itself.

A seam appeared along the top. With a sound like a sigh, the lid slid back.

Inside, on a bed of what looked like spun starlight, lay a single scroll of parchment. It was tied with a red ribbon that was fraying at the ends.

Jolene didn’t touch it. She stared.

The scroll glowed with the same soft, ominous light. It knew it was seen.

This was the Celestial Reminder. God’s forgotten to-do list.

And item three was a check on a town that didn’t exist. A town that was, apparently, hiding from something.

The town’s nonexistence wasn’t a mistake. It was protection.

And she was supposed to deliver this reminder right to its doorstep.

Her comm buzzed again. Hank.

She didn’t answer. She watched the scroll. The ribbon untied itself, slowly, as if exhausted.

The parchment began to unroll.

Jolene lunged and slapped the lid shut. The glow vanished. The seam resealed.

Her hands shook. Actually shook. A fine, visible tremor.

She stared at them. That was new. That wasn’t a system report. That was a thing her body was doing all on its own.

She crawled back to the cockpit, her knees weak.

“Mabel.”

“Yes, dear?”

“The town isn’t on the maps because it’s hiding. The package… it’s a memo. A check-in. If it gets delivered, whatever they’re hiding from finds them.”

“That is a logical deduction.”

“So I can’t deliver it.”

“Your soul—”

“Is currently beating a hole in my chest trying to get out.” Jolene took the pilot’s seat. The view of the buffer zone, that awful tear of nothing, filled the viewport. “What if I just… hold onto it? Keep it lost?”

“You would be in breach. The forfeiture would be automatic.”

“What if,” Jolene said, the idea forming like a cold knot in her stomach, “the town doesn’t exist, but the address does? The coordinates are in a buffer zone. What if I deliver it… to the buffer zone? To the idea of the address?”

Mabel was silent for a long time. “That is… creatively fraudulent.”

“Is it against the rules?”

“The rules do not account for metaphysical loopholes of this nature. The contract specifies ‘delivery to the designated recipient at the designated location.’ The location is redacted. The recipient is defined by the location.”

“So if the location is a non-place…”

“The recipient is a non-entity.”

Jolene almost smiled. It felt strange on her face. “So I deliver it to a non-entity in a non-place. Technically, mission accomplished.”

“Theological logic is not my domain, Jolene.”

“But contractual logic is. Does it work?”

Another pause. “Possibly. The mainframe would likely flag it for review. Review could take millennia.”

“During which my soul is…?”

“In escrow.”

Jolene let out a breath she hadn’t known she was holding. Damn. That was one of the banned phrases. She didn’t care. It was accurate.

“Okay.” She gripped the controls. “Mabel, plot a course into the buffer zone. Just far enough to drop the package and get out before the shear tears us apart.”

“The odds of survival are—”

“Just do it.”

The engines whined, rising to a pitch that made her teeth ache. The drone turned, nose aimed at the crackling edge of the nullity.

Her heart was a steady, pounding drum now. Thump-thump-thump.

Not fear. Not yet.

But something. Something that was hers.

She pushed the throttle forward.

The drone shot toward the tear in reality.

The drone hit the edge of the buffer zone.

The world didn’t tear. It stuttered.

The viewport flickered—grey nullity, then the garish neon of the highway, then the grey again, like a bad transmission. The engine’s whine chopped into a series of hiccups. Vrrp-vrrp-vrrp. Jolene’s teeth chattered. Her skeleton hummed.

“Dimensional shear is not a binary threshold,” Mabel announced, voice glitching. “It is more of a… gradient of unpleasantness.”

“Noted.” Jolene wrestled with the controls. They felt spongy, distant. The drone listed to port. A warning light she’d never seen before, shaped like a frowning cherub, illuminated on the console. “How deep do we need to go?”

“Theoretical coordinates are approximately two point seven subjective kilometers into the non-space.”

“What’s a kilometer in here?”

“Also theoretical.”

A billboard for a reality-anchoring service flickered past, half-consumed by the grey. Losing Your Place? We’ll Nail Your Existence Down!

The box in the hold began to hum. A low, resonant frequency that made the fillings in Jolene’s molars ache. The scroll knew it was close. It was trying to check itself off the list.

The medieval map on her screen fizzed. The HIC SUNT DRACONES label melted, replaced by a single, blinking word in stark, modern typeface: HERE.

Then the traffic jam appeared.

It wasn’t a traffic jam of cars. It was a traffic jam of concepts.

Ahead, the nullity was clogged with vehicles, but they were half-formed, shimmering in and out of solidity. A chariot made of conflicting opinions, its wheels spinning in philosophical mud. A vast, floating ledger with wings, grounded by a single decimal point entered in the wrong column. A barge carrying the weight of unintended consequences, sunk to its gunwales. They were all stuck, gridlocked around a single point—a pulsing, bureaucratic nexus that looked like a toll booth made of polished brass and red tape.

The booth blocked the only navigable path deeper into the buffer zone. A line of… things… stretched behind it, engines idling with sounds like sighing and the rustle of paper.

“What is that?” Jolene whispered. The sound didn’t travel. It just died in the air.

Mabel’s voice came through the internal speakers, thin and strained. “Cosmic bottleneck. A metaphysical audit point. It should not be manifesting in a buffer zone. The package is causing localized reality crystallization.”

“You mean it’s making a line for itself.”

“Essentially.”

The box’s hum rose to a keen. The lid rattled.

Jolene’s drone drifted to the back of the line, behind a floating palanquin carried by four somber-looking doubts. She watched the toll booth. The clerk was a tall, thin figure in a grey robe, its face a blur of shifting features. It moved with methodical, devastating slowness. It examined a manifest presented by a trembling, ghostly schooner, comparing it line-by-line to a ledger the size of a coffin.

This would take forever. She had two hours and fifty-three minutes.

“Can we go around?”

“Negative. The crystallized path is the only stable route. Venturing into the raw buffer zone would result in immediate existential dissolution. Your lease agreement specifically forbids dissolution.”

Jolene’s chest gave a heavy, final THUNK. A new alert flashed on her internal display: SYSTEM OVERRIDE: SOUL COLLATERAL PROTOCOL ENGAGED. Her vision hazed at the edges, tinted a faint, judicial grey. They were pre-processing her damnation.

Panic, real and chemical and hers, finally arrived. It wasn’t a wave. It was a spike. A hot, sharp thing lodged under her sternum. Her mouth went dry as forgotten parchment. She fumbled for a water bulb, sucked it dry, and tasted dust.

The line didn’t move.

The clerk was now arguing with the chariot of opinions. One of the chariot’s horses—a sleek, agitated concept of ‘Market Optimism’—was insisting it had filed a form 7-B. The clerk demanded the triplicate copy.

“Okay,” Jolene breathed. “Okay. New plan.”

She unstrapped and stumbled back to the hold. The box was vibrating on the floor, glowing like a hot coal. The seam was open a hair’s breadth. A sliver of painful white light cut the dim hold.

She couldn’t deliver it. She couldn’t not deliver it.

The scroll wanted to be delivered. It was a list. Completion was its only function.

But the town was hiding. The scroll’s third item was a check. A check could mean inspection. Inspection could mean discovery.

What if she didn’t deliver the whole list?

The idea was absurd. You couldn’t deliver part of a divine reminder.

Could you?

She knelt. The trembling in her hands was worse now. She watched them shake, fascinated. They were disobeying her. It was the most honest thing they’d ever done.

She didn’t kiss the box this time. She pressed her palm flat against the lid. The wood was hot. She let the impressions come.

The ledger.

The image shifted. Not the scroll. The town. A dusty, quiet main street under a perpetual twilight sky. A saloon with swinging doors. A general store. A town hall with a crooked steeple. And in the basement of the town hall, a ledger. A simple, leather-bound book. It glowed with the same awful light as the scroll.

The scroll was the check-in.

The ledger was what they were hiding.

And the third item wasn’t just a check. It was a warning. Do not let them find… The sentence was a fragment, cut off by divine redaction.

Jolene pulled her hand back. It was blistered.

They expected her to fail. They’d banked on it.

The anger that came then was clean and bright. It didn’t show up on her internal diagnostics. It was just a pure, white-hot certainty in the center of her skull.

“Oh, no,” she said to the box. “You don’t get to damn me for following your stupid rules.”

She stood up. Her legs held. She marched back to the cockpit, her boots loud in the silent ship.

The line had moved forward one vehicle. The toll booth clerk was now weighing a soul on a small brass scale, tutting at the results.

Jolene didn’t sit down. She leaned over the console, her blistered hand hovering over the controls.

“Mabel. That toll booth. What’s behind it?”

“Sensor analysis suggests a localized collapse point. A minor administrative black hole used for the disposal of voided forms and redundant prayers.”

“A trash chute.”

“A filing cabinet for non-existence, yes.”

Jolene smiled. It felt better this time. “Perfect.”

She disengaged the auto-hold. The drone lurched out of line, drifting sideways across the grey. The other vehicles didn’t react. They were too busy existing miserably.

An alarm chirped. The cherub light blinked faster.

“You are deviating from the stable path,” Mabel said.

“I know.”

“The shear forces are increasing.”

“Your soul collateral protocol is nearing its final adjudication phase. The mainframe is preparing a stamp.”

“Let it.”

The drone bucked. A sound like tearing canvas filled the cockpit. A crack split the viewport from top to bottom, not in the glass, but in reality itself. Through it, Jolene saw impossible colors and heard a noise that was almost a voice calling her name.

She ignored it. She aimed the nose of the drone straight for the toll booth.

The clerk looked up from its scales. Its blurry face focused for a second—two deep, endless pits of filing tabs and regret.

Jolene didn’t slow down.

At the last possible second, she slammed the controls forward and down. The drone dipped, skimming under the brass counter of the booth. She hit the external release for the cargo hold.

The glowing box shot out like a cannonball.

It didn’t arc toward the administrative black hole. It flew straight and true, a comet of divine obligation, heading for the coordinates pulsing on her screen.

The clerk shrieked. It was the sound of a million folders being misfiled at once.

Jolene didn’t watch. She wrenched the controls, pulling the drone into a screaming turn back toward the grey. The shear grabbed it. Metal groaned. The viewport crack multiplied.

But she was already past the booth, past the line, back into the unstable murk.

Behind her, a sound like a great intake of breath.

She glanced at the rear display.

The box hung in the space before the black hole. The scroll had fully emerged, unfurled, blazing with holy light. It was trying to complete its mission, to fly to the town it could now sense.

The toll booth clerk stretched out a long, grey arm. It didn’t grab the scroll. It grabbed the idea of the delivery.

With a sound of profound bureaucratic finality, it stamped the air next to the box.

VOID: ADDRESS INSUFFICIENT.

The black hole yawned. It wasn’t a violent suck. It was a polite, inexorable intake. The scroll flared once, a brief, furious sun, and then was gone. The box followed, swallowed without a trace.

The clerk smoothed its robe. It turned its pit-like eyes toward Jolene’s fleeing drone.

Her console lit up like a festival. Messages scrolled.

PACKAGE STATUS: DELIVERY ATTEMPTED. DELIVERY LOCATION: NON-COMPLIANT. RESOLUTION: PACKAGE VOIDED. CONTRACT CLAUSE 12 (SOUL FORFEITURE): UNDER REVIEW. ESTIMATED REVIEW TIME: 5,678,342 YEARS (STANDARD). SOUL STATUS: PLACED IN ESCROW. PENDING.

The grey around her dissolved. The neon highway snapped back into view, loud and garish and beautiful. The crack in the viewport sealed itself. The engine’s whine smoothed to its normal hum.

Jolene slumped into the pilot’s seat. Her heart wasn’t thunking. It was beating a slow, steady, artificial rhythm. A neutral rhythm.

She was damned. But on a technicality. And the appeal would outlast the stars.

Hank’s face popped up on her comm. He was eating a sandwich. “Jo. You alive?”

“Sort of.”

“Heard a ripple. Big one. Something about a divine package getting shredded in a protocol black hole. That you?”

“Might be.”

He chewed, thoughtful. “They’ll be pissed.”

“They’ll be busy. They have to fill out the forms for a lost celestial reminder. That’s a whole other department.”

Hank snorted, spraying crumbs. “So what now?”

Jolene looked at the highway ahead. A billboard for a new heart-model flashed by. Feel Everything! Models from Ecstatic to Morose!

She didn’t want a new heart. This one had gotten her into and out of the worst mess of her life. It had kept beating when she was too stupid to be scared.

“Now,” she said, “I think I’m going to feel the inside of my own skull for a while. It’s… loud in there.”

“Sounds terrible.” Hank grinned. “Got a load of plumbing parts for a water park in the dimension of unchecked glee. Pays crap. You in?”

The drone flew on, a speck of dirt and determination in the river of reality. Somewhere, in a town that didn’t exist, a ledger sat untouched in a dusty basement. And somewhere else, in the belly of a bureaucratic singularity, a to-do list was being cross-referenced with a complaint form.

Jolene adjusted her course. The medieval map on her screen was blank. A fresh start.

“Yeah,” she said. “I’m in.”
