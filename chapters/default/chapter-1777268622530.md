The rain had stopped, but the apartment still smelled of it—damp wool and the faint metallic tang of wet pavement drifting through the cracked kitchen window. Cassius sat at the table, the restraining order spread out in front of him like a map to a place he hadn’t meant to go. The paper was crisp, official. The ink didn’t smudge when he pressed his thumb against it.

He folded it once, then again, until it was small enough to fit in his palm. The marble rolled against his fingers in his other pocket, its weight familiar. He didn’t take it out.

The radiator ticked. Somewhere in the building, a door slammed. The sounds were ordinary, the kind of noise that should have been comforting. Instead, they felt like a performance, like the world was pretending nothing had changed.

Cassius stood. His left hand trembled, just slightly. He pressed it flat against the table. The wood was cool, the grain rough under his skin. He’d sanded this table himself years ago, back when he still believed in the possibility of smooth surfaces.

The knock came just as he was reaching for the kettle. Three sharp raps, the kind that didn’t wait for an answer. Cassius didn’t move. The kettle hissed, the sound building to a shriek before he lifted it off the burner.

Another knock. Then a voice, muffled through the door: “Mr. Ashbourne? Court liaison. You need to sign for this.”

Cassius set the kettle down. The steam curled up, vanishing before it reached the ceiling. He crossed to the door but didn’t open it. “Slide it under.”

A pause. Then the envelope appeared, a white rectangle inching across the threshold. Cassius waited until the footsteps receded before picking it up. The paper was thick, expensive. His name was typed, not handwritten.

Inside, a single sheet. Court-ordered therapy. Twice a week for six months. Dr. Eleanor Voss, PhD. An address downtown.

Cassius exhaled. The sound was closer to a laugh than a sigh. He’d been here before—not with this name, not with this paper, but the shape of it was the same. The concerned voices, the clipboard questions, the slow, patient nods that meant nothing.

He folded the letter and tucked it into his back pocket. The marble was still there, warm from his body. He didn’t need to look at it to know the blue streak would catch the light just so, like a sliver of sky trapped under glass.

The apartment was too quiet. The kind of quiet that pressed against his ears, that made the blood in his veins too loud. Cassius picked up the kettle again, poured the water into a mug that had gone cold. The tea bag floated, limp.

He carried it to the living room, where the notebooks were stacked in uneven towers. The top one was open to a page from last week, his handwriting jagged where the pen had skipped: Lilith’s sheets. Check for sand.

Cassius closed the notebook. The cover was worn, the edges frayed. He’d bought it years ago, back when he still thought the act of writing things down might make them stay.

The knock came again. Different this time—softer, hesitant. Cassius didn’t move.

“Cassius?” Miller’s voice, muffled through the door. “You in there?”

Cassius set the mug down. The tea hadn’t steeped. It was just hot water now, the color of weak sunlight.

He didn’t answer.

Miller knocked again. “Look, I know you’re pissed. But you can’t just—” A sigh. The doorknob rattled, then stilled. “Just call me, okay? Before they drag you in for the psych eval.”

Footsteps, retreating. Cassius waited until they were gone before he let his breath out. His left hand was shaking again. He curled it into a fist, pressed it against his thigh until the tremor stopped.

The apartment settled around him. The radiator hissed. The fridge hummed. The dog bowl in the corner was empty, the water long since evaporated. Cassius hadn’t refilled it.

He reached into his pocket, pulled out the marble. The blue streak caught the dim light, glinting. Proof. Anchor. Useless thing.

Somewhere, in another life, Lilith was shaking out her sheets. Somewhere, Damien was riding a bike down a street that didn’t exist. Somewhere, a version of Cassius was sitting at a different table, in a different kitchen, holding a different object that meant nothing to anyone but him.

Cassius rolled the marble between his fingers. The court order was in his back pocket. The therapist’s name was already fading from his memory.

He didn’t need to explain. Not again.

The marble was warm. The apartment was quiet. The world outside kept turning, oblivious.

Cassius closed his hand around the marble and waited for the next thing to bleed through.

The therapist’s office was too bright. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead, their glare bouncing off the pale blue walls and the framed diplomas that hung just slightly crooked. Cassius sat in the chair closest to the door, his back straight, his hands folded in his lap. The marble was in his pocket. He could feel its weight against his thigh, a silent counterpoint to the clipboard in Dr. Voss’s hands.

She was younger than he’d expected. Mid-forties, maybe, with dark hair pulled into a tight bun and glasses that reflected the light when she tilted her head. She didn’t smile as she read, just flipped through the pages with a practiced efficiency that suggested she’d already made up her mind.

“So,” she said finally, setting the clipboard aside. “Court-mandated sessions. You know how this works.”

Cassius nodded. He knew.

Dr. Voss leaned forward slightly, her elbows resting on her knees. “I’d like to hear it in your words. What brought you here.”

The radiator in the corner hissed. Cassius flexed his fingers, then stilled them. “I remember things that didn’t happen.”

A pause. Dr. Voss’s pen hovered over her notepad. “False memories?”

“No.” The word came out sharper than he’d intended. Cassius took a breath. “They’re real. Just not here.”

Her pen moved, a quick scribble. “Can you give me an example?”

Cassius looked past her, at the window behind her desk. The blinds were half-closed, slats of light cutting across the floor. “A house. Blue Victorian, white trim. Cedar porch. There’s a—” His throat tightened. “A garden in the front. Tomatoes, I think. And a bike leaned against the railing.”

Dr. Voss wrote something else. “And this house doesn’t exist?”

“Not in this timeline.”

The pen stopped. She looked up, her expression carefully neutral. “Timeline.”

Cassius’s left hand twitched. He pressed it flat against his knee. “I’m living multiple lives at once. The memories bleed through. Sometimes it’s just flashes—smells, sounds. Sometimes it’s whole days.”

Another note. Longer this time. “How long has this been happening?”

“Since I was a kid.”

“And you’ve never sought treatment before?”

Cassius almost laughed. “I have. They called it schizophrenia. Then dissociative identity disorder. Then complex PTSD. None of it fit.”

Dr. Voss set her pen down. “What do you think it is?”

The question startled him. No one had ever asked. Cassius hesitated, then reached into his pocket. The marble was warm, the glass smooth under his fingers. He held it up, letting the light catch the blue swirl. “This is the only thing that’s consistent. The only proof I have.”

She studied the marble, her face unreadable. “And what does it prove?”

“That I’m not imagining it.” His voice was quieter now. “That somewhere, this was real.”

Dr. Voss leaned back in her chair. The leather creaked. “Cassius, have you considered that the marble might be a coping mechanism? A tangible object your mind created to anchor you during periods of stress?”

He closed his hand around it. “It’s not like that.”

“You’re describing symptoms consistent with delusional disorder. The fixed false belief in an alternate reality, the—”

“It’s not false.”

“—the inability to accept contradictory evidence,” she continued, as if he hadn’t spoken. “The good news is, with the right combination of therapy and medication, we can help you manage these thoughts.”

Cassius’s grip tightened. The marble dug into his palm. “You’re not listening.”

“I am.” Her voice was calm, measured. “But I need you to meet me halfway. The first step is acknowledging that these memories aren’t real.”

The radiator hissed again. Cassius stared at her, at the way her glasses caught the light, at the faint smudge of ink on her thumb. He’d been here before. Different office, different face, same script.

“What about the sand?” he asked suddenly.

Dr. Voss blinked. “Sand?”

“In the sheets. I wake up with it sometimes. Grit under my nails, in the bed. It’s not from here.”

She picked up her pen. “Have you been sleepwalking?”

Cassius exhaled. The sound was sharp, frustrated. “You’re doing it again. You’re taking what I’m telling you and twisting it into something that fits your checklist.”

“I’m trying to understand.”

“No. You’re trying to diagnose.” He stood abruptly, the chair scraping against the floor. The marble was a hard knot in his fist. “You don’t believe me.”

Dr. Voss didn’t flinch. “It’s not about belief. It’s about evidence.”

“I showed you evidence.”

“A marble isn’t evidence, Cassius. It’s an object.”

The words landed like a blow. Cassius took a step back, his chest tight. He’d known this would happen.

“Sit down,” Dr. Voss said gently. “Let’s talk about other ways we can approach this.”

Cassius didn’t move. The office was too small suddenly, the walls too close. The diplomas on the wall blurred as his vision swam.

“Cassius?”

He turned and walked out.

The hallway was empty, the carpet muffling his footsteps. Cassius didn’t stop until he was outside, the cold air sharp in his lungs. The marble was still in his hand. He uncurled his fingers, staring at the blue streak. Proof. Anchor. Useless thing.

Behind him, the door opened. Dr. Voss stood in the threshold, her expression unreadable. “If you leave now, I have to report it to the court.”

Cassius didn’t look back. “Do what you have to do.”

He walked away, the marble warm in his palm, the therapist’s words already fading like the others. The street was busy, cars rushing past, people talking on phones, laughing, living their single, simple lives. Cassius tightened his grip on the marble and kept walking.

Somewhere, Lilith was shaking out her sheets. Somewhere, Damien was riding his bike. Somewhere, a version of Cassius was sitting in a different office, talking to a different doctor, hearing the same words.

The marble was warm. The world kept turning. Cassius walked until his legs ached, until the noise of the city drowned out everything else.

He didn’t look back.

The apartment smelled of dust and old paper. Cassius had stopped opening windows.

He sat at the table, the notebooks spread before him in a fan of cracked spines and dog-eared pages. His pen moved in quick, precise strokes, filling the margins with dates, times, descriptions of smells that didn’t belong. Salt. Cedar. Cut grass. The words were small, cramped, as if he could press the memories flat between the lines and keep them there.

The marble sat on the table beside his elbow, catching the dim light of the desk lamp. He didn’t touch it. It was enough to know it was there.

Outside, the city hummed. Car horns, distant laughter, the occasional siren. None of it reached him. The sounds were muffled, as if the apartment had sealed itself off from the world, a bubble of silence where only the scratch of his pen and the ticking of the radiator existed.

His left hand trembled. Cassius set the pen down, flexed his fingers, pressed his palm flat against the page. The paper was cool, slightly rough under his skin. He’d bought these notebooks years ago, back when he still thought documentation might make a difference. Now, he knew better. The writing wasn’t for proof. It was for witness.

A knock at the door.

Cassius didn’t look up.

The pen rolled slightly where he’d left it. Cassius watched it, the way the light glinted off the silver clip.

Another knock. “Come on, man. You can’t just disappear.”

Cassius picked up the pen. The knock came again, harder this time, the door rattling in its frame. He didn’t flinch.

A long silence. Then the sound of Miller’s footsteps retreating down the hall. Cassius exhaled, a slow, deliberate breath. His chest ached, a dull pressure behind his ribs. He ignored it.

The notebook was open to a fresh page. He wrote the date at the top, then paused. The ink bled slightly into the paper, a small blue stain.

No more doctors.

The words looked final. Cassius stared at them, then added, No more explanations.

His phone buzzed on the table. A text. He didn’t check it. The screen lit up, then went dark again.

The radiator hissed. Cassius turned the page.

He worked methodically, cross-referencing older entries, marking discrepancies, noting the frequency of the bleed-throughs. The data was useless to anyone but him, but that was the point. He wasn’t trying to convince anyone anymore. He was building an archive.

A sound made him look up.

The dog bowl in the corner rattled, just slightly, as if something had bumped it. Cassius stared at it. The bowl was empty. Had been empty for weeks.

He didn’t move.

The rattling stopped. The apartment was silent again. Cassius waited, his breath held, his fingers curled around the pen.

Then—A smell. Salt. Iron. Sunscreen.

Cassius closed his eyes. The scent was so strong it made his throat tighten. He could feel the grit of sand between his fingers, the warmth of a body next to his in bed, the sound of waves just beyond the window.

Lilith’s sheets.

He opened his eyes. The apartment was unchanged. The notebooks, the pen, the marble. The dog bowl sat still and empty in the corner.

But the smell lingered.

Cassius exhaled shakily. His left hand trembled. He pressed it flat against the table, willing it to stop.

The phone buzzed again. Another text. Cassius ignored it.

He turned back to the notebook. His handwriting was steadier now, the letters dark and even. Bleed-through: olfactory. Coastal timeline. Duration: approximately 8 seconds.

The words were clinical. Detached. It helped.

Outside, the city lights glowed, a constellation of windows and streetlamps stretching into the distance. Cassius didn’t look at them. He kept his eyes on the page, on the careful lines of ink, on the record of a life—lives—that no one else would ever believe.

The marble caught the light. Cassius reached for it without thinking, rolling it between his fingers. The glass was smooth, the blue streak vivid even in the dim room.

Proof. Anchor. Useless thing.

He set it down.

The notebook was nearly full. Cassius flipped to the back, where the pages were still blank. He wrote slowly this time, the letters deliberate.

If no one remembers, did it happen?

The question hung in the air, unanswered. Cassius stared at it, then carefully crossed it out. He turned the page.

The next entry was simpler. I remember.

The radiator ticked. The city hummed. The apartment held its breath.

Cassius wrote until his hand cramped, until the words blurred and the ink smudged against his fingers. He didn’t stop.

Here, in this apartment, in this timeline, Cassius closed the notebook and stacked it with the others. The marble glinted in the lamplight.

The proof was in the pages. The proof was in his hands.

Cassius stood, stretching his stiff shoulders. The apartment was quiet. The city outside was bright.

He crossed to the window, looking down at the streets below. People moved in clusters, talking, laughing, living their single, simple lives. Cassius watched them for a long moment, then turned away.

The notebooks waited on the table. The marble glowed faintly in the lamplight.

Cassius sat down and picked up his pen.
