The light in the office was the kind of tired yellow that seemed to leach the color from everything it touched. Iris stood just inside the door, a stack of mimeographed scripts a dead weight against her ribs. The room smelled of dust and the particular, sweetish decay of old paper, undercut by a sharper scent—pine disinfectant, the kind used in hospital corridors. A single fly bumped against the windowpane with a dull, rhythmic tap. Tap. It was the only sound.

Pauline was at her desk, her back to the door, a silhouette against the grimy window. She didn’t turn. “Put them on the cabinet.

Iris crossed the worn linoleum. She set the scripts down. Next to the pile was a framed photograph, glass dusty. A younger Pauline, hair in a defiant cloud, stood with a group before a boarded-up storefront. Someone had painted “THE PEOPLE’S STAGE” in rough letters on the wood. Iris looked away, but the image stayed—the angle of Pauline’s jaw, the set of her shoulders. Twelve years collapsed into the space between one breath and the next.

“Strauss is coming.” Pauline’s voice was flat, stripped of its usual theatrical resonance. She swiveled in her chair. The light caught the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the tight purse of her mouth. “He wants to see the space. Take measurements, he said. For his production.

“His production.”

“That’s the idea.” Pauline lit a cigarette, the scrape of the match loud in the quiet. She exhaled, the smoke a slow, grey ribbon that caught the light before dissolving. “He’s bringing his lawyer. A Mr. Feld. They’ll be in the hall. I don’t want them in here.

There was a chip in the green paint of the filing cabinet, shaped like a jagged little continent. Iris traced its outline with her gaze. “You could tell him no.

“I did. He’s coming anyway.” Pauline tapped ash into a chipped saucer. “Money has a right to inspect its investment.

The word landed, a cold pebble in the pit of Iris’s stomach. Investment. Cross’s voice in the automat: Any mention of external contacts. Patrons. She turned toward the door, her hand already reaching for the knob. The metal was cool, almost slippery. “I’ll keep an eye on the hall.

“Don’t engage. Just… be a presence.

Iris nodded, a short, sharp movement. She stepped back into the dim corridor, pulling the door shut behind her. The latch clicked with a finality that felt like a mistake.

The hall was empty, just the long stretch of scarred floorboards and the distant, muffled sound of a rehearsal from the main stage—a line repeated, over and over, with different inflections. A chair scraped. She leaned against the wall by the water cooler, the glass bottle half-empty, a few trapped bubbles clinging to its side. She watched them not move.

They arrived ten minutes later. Duke Strauss first, a camel-hair coat draped over his shoulders, his hat in his hands. He moved with a buoyant confidence that seemed to push the air ahead of him. Behind him was a thinner, older man in a severe black overcoat—Feld, presumably. He carried a leather portfolio and a look of distilled impatience.

“Iris, darling! A sight for sore eyes in this…” Duke’s gesture took in the peeling plaster, the exposed conduit running along the ceiling. “This temple of art. This is Benjamin Feld.

Feld offered a curt nod, his eyes already scanning the hallway, assessing. “Miss Finch.

“Mr. Strauss. Mr. Feld. Pauline’s in her office. She asked not to be disturbed.

“Of course, of course.” Duke’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. They were a pale, watery blue, and they kept moving. “We’re just getting the lay of the land. Ben here needs to understand the… logistical challenges.” He clapped a hand on Feld’s shoulder. “The man is a wizard with a budget. Turns lead into gold. Or at least into a decent spotlight.

Feld detached himself from the hand. “I’d like to see the electrical panel. The fire exits.

“Down the hall to the left,” Iris said. “The door under the stairs.

Feld strode off without another word, his footsteps precise and loud. Duke watched him go, the smile slipping from his face like a discarded mask. He rubbed a hand over his jaw. The sound of his fingers against the stubble was a dry, scratchy whisper.

“He’s a good man, Ben. Cautious. You need cautious men when you’re handling other people’s money.” Duke lowered his voice, though they were alone. “Especially this money. The patrons… they’re skittish. Artists with causes, you know the type. All passion until the ledger comes out.

Iris said nothing. She watched a dust mote spiral in a sunbeam that sliced through a high, grimy window.

“This play,” Duke continued, his voice a conspiratorial murmur. “It’s important. More than they know. A statement. But statements need a certain… visibility to be effective. A certain sheen. That’s where you come in, my dear. Your name. Your face. It gives the whole enterprise a… a respectable veneer. Makes the medicine go down.

He was looking at her now, really looking, as if gauging her comprehension. Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It tightened her throat. She thought of the bank draft Pauline had secured, the crisp paper, the institutional name. Leo had mentioned that bank over dinner, in passing, while complaining about the fees for his wife’s charity ball.

“You’re funding it,” she said, the words coming out flat. “Not just producing. You’re the backer.

Duke’s expression shifted, a subtle realignment of features into something harder, more appraising. The avuncular producer vanished. “Let’s say I’m a conduit. A facilitator for a group of individuals who believe in Miss Carter’s work. Who believe art should have a… a social conscience. But conscience is a luxury in 1949. It requires camouflage.” He took a step closer. The scent of his cologne was sharp, citrus over something medicinal. “Your involvement is that camouflage. A celebrated American songbird, rediscovering her passion on the stage. It’s a beautiful story. It draws the eye. And while everyone is looking at you, they can do their work.

They. The word hung in the air between them. The patrons. The skittish artists with causes. The ones already on a list somewhere in a grey filing cabinet in Washington.

Feld’s returning footsteps echoed in the hall. Duke’s face smoothed over instantly, the genial mask slipping back into place. “Ah, Ben! What’s the verdict? Can we light it, or will we be performing by candlelight?

Iris didn’t hear the answer. The fly was still tapping at the office window. Tap. A frantic, stupid rhythm. She saw Pauline’s back, the rigid line of her spine. She saw the photograph of the boarded-up storefront. A statement needing a sheen. A target within a target.

Feld was speaking about amperage, about code violations. Duke was nodding, making a note in a little book with a gold pencil. They moved down the hall toward the stage, their voices fading into a distant murmur.

Iris stayed by the water cooler. The cold knot under her ribs had a new shape now, a specific density. It wasn’t just fear for herself anymore. It was a dreadful, clarifying understanding. The play wasn’t a refuge. It was the heart of the trap. And she, with her famous face, was the decoy. She looked at the closed office door. She didn’t open it. She just stood there, listening to the fly, until the silence in the hall became a thing she could feel against her skin.

She waited until their footsteps faded down the hall. The fly was still at the window. The sound of it, that tiny, persistent thud, seemed to fill the office. She left without looking at Pauline again.

The main theatre was a cavern of shadows at this hour. The work lights were off, only the dim, amber glow of the exit signs painting the edges of the aisles in a sickly red. Iris stood in the wings, stage right, her fingers brushing against the rough hemp of a sandbag rope. The air here was different—cold, still, smelling of sawdust and the ghost of old paint. From the far side of the building, she could hear the muffled, tinny sound of Feld’s voice, then Duke’s laugh, a short, sharp bark that didn’t carry any joy.

They were in the hallway just outside the double doors to the house. The acoustics in this old church were strange, channels and pockets. Words could travel in unexpected ways.

“—not a charity, Ben.” Duke’s voice, clearer now, strained with a forced patience. “It’s an investment. In visibility.

“Visibility for whom?” Feld’s tone was dry as old paper. “The names on your list can’t afford visibility. Not now. Not with the press sniffing around the Waldorf conference. One of them is already on a subcommittee watchlist. You think they want their handwriting on a cheque for a play about a naval mutiny?

A mutiny. The word hung in the cold air. That wasn’t the word they used. It was a disaster. An accident. A failure of command. Never a mutiny.

“Their handwriting is on a bank draft. From a numbered account in Zurich. That’s the beauty of it.” A rustle of fabric, a coat being adjusted. “The money is clean by the time it hits the institute’s ledger. It’s a grant. From the American Foundation for Theatrical Arts. Sounds like the DAR, for Christ’s sake.

“And the Finch woman? She’s the foundation’s idea of artistic oversight?

“She’s the respectable face. The former darling. A little tarnished, sure, but the public remembers the voice, the smile. They see her name on the program, they think it’s a comeback story. A sentimental journey. They don’t think about politics. They don’t look at the fine print.

Iris’s hand stilled on the rope. The fibers were coarse, leaving a faint, dusty grit on her skin. She remembered the feel of a different rope, the thick hawser of a ship’s rail in Southampton, the day she left for New York. The salt spray had stung her eyes. That was the story, anyway. In truth, it had been a calm day. Grey. The memory was wrong.

“She’s a liability.” Feld’s voice cut through the wrong memory. “She has her own problems. Cross talks to her.

“Cross talks to everyone. That’s his job. Her having problems is the point. She needs this. She’ll be grateful. She’ll be compliant. She’ll stand on that stage and say the lines and the people who matter will see a safe bet. A redemption arc. It draws the heat.

“Or it focuses it.”

“That’s a risk.” Duke’s voice dropped, the words coming faster, a low, urgent stream. “Everything’s a risk. The patrons know that. They’re not paying for a sure thing. They’re paying for a statement. A flare in the dark. And if the flare lights up the wrong people… well, the money trail ends at the foundation. At me. I’m the producer. I’m the visible one.

Silence then. The kind that felt heavy, full of things unsaid. Iris heard the click of a lighter, the slow inhale of a cigarette. Duke’s, probably. He smoked a particular brand, something Turkish, that left a sweet, cloying scent in its wake.

“And Carter?” Feld asked.

“What about her?”

“Does she know the provenance?”

“She knows the grant came through channels. She’s not a fool. She knows the play can’t be done with collection plates and bake sales. She takes the money. She does the work. The art is the thing. That’s what she believes.” A pause. The ash being tapped. “It’s what I need her to believe. The art protects the money. The money protects the art. And Finch in the middle makes it all look like… culture.

The art protects the money. The money protects the art. It had a rhythm to it, a cynical little couplet. Iris found her other hand had come up to her throat, her fingers resting lightly on the place where the notes used to form. There was nothing there now but the faint, ever-present ache.

Another memory, this one sharp and clear: a cafe table in Montparnasse. Pauline, leaning forward, her eyes alight. The art isn’t a shield, Iris. It’s the weapon. The truth doesn’t need protection. It needs a delivery system. She’d been so sure. So fierce. That was before the letters stopped coming. Before the silence set in.

From the hallway, the sound of footsteps moving away. “Show me the boiler room,” Feld said, his voice already receding. “I want to see the condition of the pipes.

Their voices faded into the building’s hum. Iris let go of the rope. Her palm was marked with its pattern, a lattice of red lines. She stared at it. A delivery system. That’s what she was now. Not a weapon. A decoy. A respectable face to draw the heat so the money could do its work unseen.

She wasn’t just an informant on a play. She was the glittering bait on a hook that was already in dangerous water. Cross wanted names. He wanted patrons. And Duke was right there, weaving a net of left-wing money around them all, using her to blind the fish.

The house lights suddenly buzzed on, one bank at a time, flooding the auditorium with a harsh, white glare. She flinched, raising a hand against the assault. From the tech booth high at the back, a stagehand waved, apologetic. “Sorry, Miss Finch! Just testing the circuit!

She nodded, lowering her hand. The empty seats stared back, row upon row of worn red velvet, a sea of judgement. Her heart was a dull, heavy knock against her ribs. Not fear, exactly. Something closer to recognition. A flat, cold understanding, like finding the correct key for a lock that had been stubborn for years.

She turned her back on the seats and walked off the stage, her footsteps soundless on the wooden floor. In the corridor, the smell of Duke’s cigarette still lingered, a faint, sweet ghost. She passed Pauline’s closed door without stopping. She didn’t look at the chip in the paint on the filing cabinet. She went straight to the little alcove where the payphone hung, its cord coiled like a tired serpent.

She lifted the receiver. The metal was cool against her ear. She dialed the number Langston had made her memorize. It rang once, twice.

She listened to the empty sound of the line. Then, carefully, quietly, she placed the receiver back in its cradle. She didn’t deposit a coin. She hadn’t said a word.

The walk back to the empty theatre was slower. The fly had finally stopped tapping. It lay on its back on the windowsill, legs curled in the air, a tiny, still comma against the dusty wood. She looked at it for a long moment. Then she pushed open the stage door and stepped out into the grey afternoon, the cold air a slap against her skin. She didn’t have a report to make. Not one she could write down. The knowledge sat inside her, a new, silent weight. It changed the shape of everything.

The fly was still at the window. Tap. Pause. A stupid, persistent little metronome. Iris watched it, her back against the cool plaster of the hallway wall, until the sound of Duke’s and Feld’s departing footsteps faded into the stairwell’s echo. The silence they left behind was different. It had a texture now, a kind of greasy residue.

She pushed off the wall. Her legs felt unsteady, as if the floor had tilted a few degrees while she wasn’t looking. The door to Pauline’s office was ajar. She didn’t knock.

Pauline hadn’t moved. She sat at her desk, the cigarette burned down to a long column of ash in the saucer. The smoke hung in the tired yellow light, a blue-grey cloud that wouldn’t dissipate. She was staring at the framed photograph, the one with the boarded-up storefront.

“They’re gone,” Iris said.

Pauline didn’t look up. “What did he want? Specifically.

“To see the space. His lawyer wanted the electrical panel.

“That’s not what I asked.” Pauline’s voice was a low scrape. “What did he want.

The memory of Duke’s murmured words—makes the medicine go down—clicked into place beside Cross’s flat demand in the automat. Any mention of external contacts. Patrons. The two pieces fit, forming a shape she didn’t want to see. A cold, sharp understanding settled behind her breastbone, not quite fear, not quite anger. A professional recognition of a trap being set.

“He said my name gives the project a respectable veneer.” Iris heard the flatness in her own voice. “He said the patrons are skittish. Artists with causes.

Pauline’s laugh was a short, dry exhale. No humor in it. “Of course.” She finally looked up, her eyes dark and unreadable. “He’s using you as a liberal fig leaf. A famous white face to paste over the radical content. Makes it palatable for the money. And the money, I assume, does not like to see its name in the papers.

“Where does the money come from?”

“Does it matter?” Pauline stubbed the cigarette out with more force than necessary. The ash crumbled. “It’s money. It pays for the lights. The royalties. The damned mimeograph paper.” She gestured at the stack Iris had brought in. “It’s a transaction.

“Transactions have terms.”

“All terms are a variation on the same theme. Don’t embarrass us. Don’t get us subpoenaed. Make it art, but not that kind of art.” Pauline stood abruptly, turning her back to look out the grimy window. Her shoulders were rigid. “He’s not wrong, in his way. Your involvement does change the calculus. It gives them a different story to tell. The Canary Finch, seeking artistic redemption in Harlem. It’s sentimental. It sells.

Iris felt the words like a physical push. She took a step further into the room. The smell of dust and pine was stronger here, almost choking. “And what story are you telling?

Pauline was silent for a long moment. The fly tapped. Outside, a truck ground its gears on the street below. “I’m telling the truth about three hundred and twenty men who got blown to pieces because the Navy told them it was safe to load bombs in the dark. I’m telling it with his money, in his production, with his star.” She turned around. Her face was all angles in the poor light. “The vessel is always compromised. The message is not.

It was a rehearsed line. Iris could hear the cadence of it, the careful rhythm of a credo. But something flickered behind Pauline’s eyes—a fatigue so deep it looked like pain.

“He knows the play is a target,” Iris said quietly.

“Everything here is a target.” Pauline’s gaze held hers. “The question is what you do when you realize you’re standing in the crosshairs. Do you freeze? Do you step aside? Or do you make sure your shadow falls exactly where they’re aiming?

The air between them tightened. Iris’s throat felt dry. She thought of the report she was supposed to write for Cross tonight. The blank lines waiting for names, for meeting times. She thought of Duke’s patrons, their skittish money. A target within a target. A stack of traps.

She didn’t realize she’d moved until she was standing beside the filing cabinet. Her hand reached out, not for the handle, but to touch the chip in the green paint. The edge was rough under her fingertip. A jagged little continent. Somewhere, a country of her own making.

“He asked about your private meetings,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper. She watched her own finger trace the outline. “With the petty officer. The mother of the sailor. He has the times.

Pauline went very still. “Does he.

“He wanted names. Subjects discussed. External contacts.

“And will you give them to him?”

Iris lifted her hand from the cabinet. She looked at the dust on her fingertip, a fine grey powder. She rubbed it away against her skirt. The action felt decisive, final. She didn’t answer the question. She asked one instead. “What do you discuss? In those meetings.

A beat of silence. Then Pauline let out a breath, a slow surrender to something. “Logistics. Where to place a pause so it hurts more. How to make a courtroom feel like a ship’s hold. Which gestures read as guilt to a white audience, and which read as grief.” She leaned back against the windowsill, crossing her arms. “We discuss the work. The dangerous, meticulous, utterly banal work of making truth legible from fifty feet away in the dark.

It was the most direct thing Pauline had said to her since she’d walked back into this building. No armor. No subtext. Just the plain, weary fact of the thing.

Iris nodded. A simple, acknowledging motion. The knot under her ribs, the one that had been there since the automat, since Cross slid that notebook across the table, didn’t loosen. It changed. It became a different kind of weight. A stone she chose to carry, not a chain she was forced to drag.

“I should go,” she said. “They’ll be waiting for me at home.

Pauline didn’t try to stop her. She just watched, her expression unreadable again, the mask back in place. “The script revisions are on the cabinet. Your scenes are marked. We start blocking Act Two tomorrow. Don’t be late.

“I won’t.”

Iris turned and left, pulling the door shut behind her. This time the latch clicked with a softer sound. Almost gentle.

The hallway was empty, the water cooler humming. She walked past it, her footsteps echoing in the cavernous quiet. At the top of the stairs, she stopped. From the main stage below, she heard a voice, clear and strong, riding the tail end of a line: “…and the silence after the blast was the loudest thing I ever heard.

She stood there for a minute, listening to the silence that followed in the real hall. It wasn’t loud. It was deep, and it was waiting. She descended the stairs, each step a measured beat, and pushed through the heavy front doors into the lavender-grey twilight of the street. She didn’t look back.
