The stage lights had cooled to a dull, glassy orange, like the last embers in a grate. Iris’s script lay open on a wooden chair, the pages soft at the edges from handling. She had marked Eleanor’s lines in pencil, a stave of marginalia where a rest meant more than a note. Her throat felt scraped raw, not from use, but from the constant, silent pressure of holding the words in the right shape. A muscle under her left shoulder blade had tightened into a hard, specific knot—the kind that wouldn’t ease with stretching, only with a complete surrender of tension she couldn’t afford.

The others had trickled out an hour ago, voices fading down the corridor, the heavy street door thudding shut. She should have left with them. Langston expected her for a late supper with Duke and some investors, a performance of a different kind. But her feet had remained planted center stage, in the precise spot where Eleanor’s final, silent stare was meant to land. The empty house watched back, a dark cavern of plush shadows and the faint, dusty smell of velvet. High above, a single work light buzzed, a tired insect trapped in the rafters. Its yellow glow caught the swirling motes of dust their movement had disturbed, a miniature galaxy spinning in the void between the proscenium and the first row.

A door clicked open backstage left.

Iris didn’t turn. The footsteps were familiar now—a measured, deliberate tread, the slight scuff of a practical shoe on unfinished wood. Pauline emerged from the wings, a silhouette against the brick wall. She carried two chipped mugs. She didn’t speak, just crossed the stage and held one out.

Iris took it. The porcelain was warm. The liquid inside was black, smelling of chicory and burnt toast. Army coffee.

“It’s all that’s left,” Pauline said. She spoke, the sound stripped of its daytime edge, worn down to something quieter. She leaned against the lip of the stage, looking out into the dark.

“It’s fine.” Iris took a sip. It was terrible, bitter and granular. It spread a familiar, acrid warmth through her chest. She remembered the taste from USO tours, from green rooms in converted hangars. A taste of displacement.

They drank in silence. The buzz of the light, the distant groan of a water pipe in the walls. Iris’s eyes traced the faded mural on the ceiling—a peeling heaven of clouds and dim stars, a forgotten angel with one wing crumbling into plaster dust. Someone had loved this place enough to paint that. Someone else had let it fade.

“You’re working the silence like a beat,” Pauline said, not looking at her.

“It’s the loudest part of the scene.”

“I know.” Pauline fished a cigarette case from her skirt pocket and offered one. Iris took it. The lighter flared, a sudden, intimate sun in the dimness. Pauline lit her own, then snapped the lighter shut. The Zippo’s click echoed in the hollow space. Iris’s own, unused sat heavy in her pocket.

The smoke was a pale thread between them. Iris watched the way Pauline held the cigarette, fingers curled, elbow cradled in her other hand. A defensive posture. Exhaustion did that—broke down the formal architecture of a body.

“Why did you take the job, Iris?”

The question landed softly, almost lost in the smoke. It wasn’t the director’s demand from their first meeting. It was just a question. The air in the room seemed to thin.

Iris traced the rim of the mug with her thumb. The truth was a cold, smooth stone on her tongue. She could give the practiced answer: Langston’s management, a need for steady work, a desire to teach. The lies tasted like ash.

“The club circuit is a funeral parlor,” she said instead, which was also true, just not the reason. “You play the same songs to thinner crowds. They want the ghost of what you were. It’s… embalming.”

Pauline nodded slowly, as if this confirmed something. She took a long drag. “And here?”

“Here it’s alive.” The words were out before she could measure them. They hung there, dangerously honest. “Even when it’s painful. It’s not a museum.”

“It’s a target,” Pauline corrected, her tone flat, factual.

“I know that, too.”

Another silence, thicker now. Pauline tapped ash onto the stage floor, a tiny grey heap on the worn boards. “Sometimes,” she said, her gaze fixed on some middle distance in the dark house, “this building smells of burning paper. Late at night. No one else is here. No fire. Just the smell. Old ledgers maybe. Or letters.”

Iris’s breath caught. She forced it out slowly, hoping the tremor didn’t show. She took a drag of her cigarette. The tobacco was harsh, comforting.

“I’ve never smelled it,” she managed.

“No.” Pauline’s voice was almost a whisper. “You wouldn’t.”

It wasn’t an accusation. It was just a statement of fact, as simple as noting the time. The knot under Iris’s shoulder blade gave a sharp, vicious twinge. The words were a jumble of useless notes, a chord with no resolution.

Pauline straightened, stubbing her cigarette out on the sole of her shoe. She dropped the butt into her empty mug. The moment was over, the fragile thread pulled taut and then released. “Your blocking for the second act is timid. You’re playing the space like you’re afraid of it. Claim it. She owns that room, even if she hates what it represents.”

The director was back. The wall was up. Iris felt a ridiculous surge of gratitude for it.

“I’ll work on it.”

“See that you do.” Pauline picked up both mugs. She paused, her profile sharp in the low light. “Lock up when you leave. The bolt sticks. You have to lift it as you turn the key.”

Then she was gone, her footsteps receding into the shadows of the wings. The door clicked shut.

Iris stood alone again in the pool of yellow light. The smell of their smoke lingered, mixed with dust and old coffee. She finished her cigarette, crushed it under her heel. The angel on the ceiling watched with its one good eye. She should go home. Langston would be pacing. Duke would be charming someone, laying the groundwork for the next strategic move.

She walked to the spot Pauline had vacated. She placed her feet exactly where Pauline’s had been. She looked out at the dark, silent audience, trying to see what Pauline had seen. Not emptiness. Potential. Judgment. A field of fire.

From this angle, she noticed a deep, splintered gouge in the floorboards, dark with age and dirt. Someone had dropped something heavy there, years ago. The scar had never been sanded out. It was just part of the stage now, a flaw in the foundation. You learned to step over it.

The silence stretched became its own entity in the room.

“Feld was here today,” Pauline said, her gaze still fixed on the dark house. “With Duke Strauss.”

Iris started, turning. Pauline stood a few feet away in the half-light, one mug in each hand. Iris hadn’t heard her return. The mention of the producer’s name was a cold finger on the back of her neck. She let the dead cigarette fall from her fingers and crushed it under her heel. “Checking on his investment?”

“Something like that.” Pauline set one mug down on the floorboards with a soft tock, then pushed off from the stage lip and walked a few paces into Eleanor’s light. Her shadow stretched long and thin across the boards, a dark slash. “He asked after you. Wanted to know if you were… settling in.”

“And you said?”

“I said you were a professional.” Pauline turned, her face half in shadow. “Which is true. You hit your marks. You remember your lines. The work gets done.”

It was praise, but it felt like a diagnosis. A professional. A mechanism. Something that functioned.

“He’s funding the play,” Iris said. It wasn’t a question.

“The grant came through his foundation. Yes.”

“Why?”

Pauline’s laugh was short, dry. A single cough of sound. “Why does any rich man fund anything? Guilt. Ego. The desire to see his name in a program next to the word ‘patron.’ He thinks this is a safe bet. A serious play about a serious subject. It makes him look serious.”

Something in the delivery was off. A fraction too glib. Iris remembered the conversation she’d overheard, the low, urgent stream of Duke’s voice in the hall. The money trail ends at the foundation. At me. She looked at Pauline’s profile, the tight line of her jaw. “Is it? A safe bet?”

For a long moment, Pauline didn’t answer. She studied the gouge in the floorboards, as if calculating its depth. “There’s no such thing,” she said finally. Her voice had dropped, gone quiet and rough. “Not anymore. Not here. Safety is an illusion you pay for with silence. I don’t have that kind of currency.”

The words hung between them. Iris’s hand went to her pocket, her fingers finding the familiar, cool shape of the Zippo. She didn’t pull it out. Just pressed the metal into her palm until the pattern left an imprint.

“The money,” Iris said carefully. “Does it… come with conditions?”

Pauline’s head tilted. A director noting an actor’s inflection. “All money comes with conditions. The condition is the play gets produced. That it’s good. That it says the thing it needs to say without saying it so loudly the walls come down.” She paused. “Why? Does that worry you?”

Yes. The answer was a stone in her throat. Because if Duke was a front, if the money was tainted, then her reports to Cross weren’t just about a radical theater. They were about a pipeline. A network. The stakes weren’t just her career, or Pauline’s anger. They were larger, darker, a room whose dimensions she couldn’t see. “I just wonder who else is in the audience,” Iris said, which was as close as she could come.

“The audience is always the question.” Pauline began to pace, a slow, measured circuit at the edge of the light. Her shoes made a soft, gritty sound on the dust. “You craft the thing for the people in the seats. But you never really know who’s out there. A critic. A rival. A man with a notebook.” She stopped, looked directly at Iris. “A ghost.”

The air left Iris’s lungs. She couldn’t tell if Pauline meant it metaphorically. If she meant Paris. If she meant the burning paper.

“Ghosts don’t buy tickets,” Iris managed, her voice thin.

“No. They get in for free.” Pauline’s expression was unreadable, a mask of fatigue and something harder, something like resolve. “You asked why I took the job.”

Iris blinked. “I did.”

“You didn’t answer. Not really.” Pauline folded her arms across her chest, a barrier against the cool air. “But I’ll answer for me. This institute… it’s a ledger. A balance sheet. Every kid who walks out of here with their head a little higher, who learns how to command a space with their voice instead of their fists, that’s a credit. Every play we mount that makes someone in the balcony see their own life reflected back, that’s a credit. The debits are the fear. The men in suits at the door. The smell of burning paper. The days you look at the bank statement and know you can’t make payroll.” She took a slow breath. “The job is to keep the ledger in the black. However you can. However ugly it gets.”

It was the most Pauline had ever said to her about the place. Not as art, not as politics, but as a fragile, desperate economy. Iris felt the weight of it, the sheer exhausting math of survival. Her own calculations—gin measurements, strategic smiles, the careful distance she kept from everyone—seemed petty in comparison. A child’s arithmetic.

“You think I’m a debit,” Iris said. The words were out, stark and plain.

Pauline didn’t flinch. “I think you’re a variable. An unknown quantity. You walked in here with your own ledger. I don’t know what’s written in it.”

Iris wanted to tell her. The want was a physical ache, a cramp in her diaphragm. The truth was right there, behind her teeth. They have me by the throat. They have letters. I’m here because I have to be, because the only other choice is a public unmasking, a slow, professional death. I am the worst kind of debit. I am a poison pill. But the words wouldn’t form. They were notes in a key she could no longer sing.

Her silence was the answer.

Pauline nodded, once. A small, accepting motion. “Right.” She turned and walked toward the wings, her shadow receding. “Lock up when you leave. The deadbolt sticks. You have to lift it as you turn the key.”

“Pauline.”

She stopped, but didn’t turn back.

“The ghost,” Iris said, her voice barely above a whisper. “In the audience. If you knew it was there… would you change the play?”

Pauline stood very still for a moment, a cutout against the dark brick. Then she shook her head, a slow, weary negation. “No. You play to the ghost. You play for it. Because the ghost is the only one who really understands the cost of the ticket.”

And then she was gone, her footsteps fading into the backstage gloom. The echo of the heavy door closing reached Iris a few seconds later, a distant, hollow thud.

Alone again. The work light buzzed. The motes of dust continued their slow, pointless dance. Iris looked down at the gouge in the floor. A flaw in the foundation. You learned to step over it. Or you didn’t. You tripped. You fell. The play went on either way.

She finished the terrible coffee, the dregs gritty on her tongue. Then she gathered her script, her coat. She moved through the dark house, her footsteps loud in the empty rows. At the door, she lifted the bolt as she turned the key. It clicked open with a sound like a bone snapping.

The night air was a cold slap after the close, dusty warmth of the theatre. Iris walked, the script tucked under her arm like a stolen thing. The streets were quiet, the sound of her heels on the pavement too loud, a metronome marking her passage through the sleeping city. She passed a bakery where the first lights were coming on, the smell of yeast and sugar blooming in the dark. It was a smell from childhood, from before microphones and contracts, a smell that meant safety. It made her throat tighten for reasons she couldn’t name.

The apartment was dark when she let herself in. Langston would still be out with Duke, sealing whatever deals men sealed over brandy and cigars. The silence here was different from the theatre’s—a dead, expensive silence, muffled by thick carpets and drapes drawn tight against the city. She dropped her coat on the chair by the door, a small rebellion against his curated order. Her body moved on its own toward the sideboard, her hand finding the cut-glass decanter without needing light. The gin poured with a clear, liquid whisper. She didn’t measure it. She drank it straight from the heavy tumbler, the alcohol a clean, burning line down to her stomach. A second pour, this one measured into the toothbrush glass she kept on the shelf beneath. Two fingers. A ritual of containment.

She carried the small glass to the living room but didn’t sit. She stood at the window, parting the drapes a sliver. The street lamp below cast a sickly yellow cone on the empty sidewalk. The knot under her shoulder blade was still there, a faithful companion. She rotated the glass in her hand, watching the liquid tilt, a tiny, captive sea.

The art protects the money. The money protects the art.

The phrase had a rhythm, a horrible, catchy rhythm. A jingle for damnation. She took a sip, let the gin sit on her tongue until the burn faded to a medicinal warmth. She needed to file her report for Cross. A bland, useless paragraph about blocking and line readings. She hadn’t written it yet. The blank page in the typewriter on Langston’s desk felt like a mouth waiting to be fed.

She finished the gin. The empty glass felt too light. She needed the weight of it full, the small, specific task of holding it steady. She turned from the window, aiming for the sideboard again, and her foot caught on the edge of the Persian rug. She stumbled, her hand shooting out to brace against Langston’s desk. The old wood groaned. A drawer, the thin top one where he kept pens and paper clips, slid open an inch.

Iris righted herself. Her heart did a clumsy, too-loud beat against her ribs. She pushed the drawer shut. It stuck. She pushed again. It jammed on something. Cursing under her breath, she tugged it open fully to reseat it. A sheaf of engraved stationery lay inside, the thick, creamy stock Duke Strauss used. Beneath it, the corner of a different paper peeked out—a pale, government-issue grey.

Her breath stopped. The air in the room grew very still.

She knew that paper. She’d seen it in a manila folder in a dark supper club.

Her fingers were cold. They moved on their own, nudging Duke’s stationery aside. There were three sheets. The top one was a carbon copy of a typed memorandum. The letterhead was the Committee’s. The date was two weeks ago. Her eyes skipped, snagging on phrases.

The words blurred. A high, thin noise started in her ears. She lifted the sheet. Beneath it was a list, handwritten in Langston’s precise, vertical script. Names. She recognized two—actors from the institute. Next to one, he’d written: Spouse in Party. Next to another, seen with Robeson fundraiser?

The third sheet was a bank statement. Not their joint account. A different bank. A deposit, dated four days after her first meeting with Cross. The amount was five thousand dollars. The depositor’s line read: Consulting Retainer. Federal Arts Liaison.

The tumbler was still in her other hand. She looked at it, confused for a second about what it was. She set it down on the desk blotter. The sound it made was too final.

She stood there for a long time. The street lamp’s light crept across the floor. Somewhere in the building, a pipe knocked. An ordinary sound. The world was just going on.

It wasn’t surprise. That was the thing. It was a cold settling recognition, like a stone finding the bottom of a well. The pieces—the new pen, the phone calls, his insistence on names, his lack of fear for himself—clicked into a configuration so obvious she felt stupid for not seeing the shape sooner. He wasn’t managing the threat. He was a part of the transaction. Her past was a commodity. Her present cooperation was his continued fee.

A sound escaped her, not a laugh, not a sob. A short, dry exhale.

She carefully placed the papers back in the order she’d found them. She slid the drawer shut. It closed smoothly this time. She picked up her glass walked to the sink, and washed it. She dried it with a tea towel, her movements methodical, slow. She returned it to its shelf beneath the sideboard.

Then she went to the typewriter. She rolled in a fresh sheet. She typed her report. Three sentences about the emotional pacing of Act Two. She signed it I. Finch. She left it in the center of the blotter.

The bedroom was too large. The bed was a vast, cold plain. She didn’t undress. She lay on top of the coverlet, on her side, staring at the stripe of light under the door. She thought about the smell of burning paper. She wondered if it was a real smell, or just the memory of a thing feared. Her mind kept circling back to the bank deposit. Five thousand dollars. It was almost exactly what they owed in back taxes from ’47. A problem solved. A retainer.

She closed her eyes. Behind her lids, she saw the peeling angel on the theatre ceiling, one wing crumbling into dust. Someone had loved it once. Someone else had let it fade.

A key turned in the front door. The sound was enormous. Footsteps in the hall, heavy, a little unsteady. Langston humming a tune she didn’t recognize. He paused. She pictured him seeing the report, picking it up. She heard the rustle of paper, then the slow satisfied creak of his desk chair.

She didn’t move. She kept her breathing even, deep. She made herself a still thing under the darkness, a closed door. The footsteps approached the bedroom. The door opened onto it. Light from the hall fell across her. She felt his gaze on her back, a physical weight. He stood there for a full minute. Then the door sighed shut, plunging her again into the black.

She listened to him move through the apartment, the sounds of his own ritual—the clink of a glass, the run of water in the bathroom. The ordinary noises of a life shared. They felt like sounds from a play she was watching from a great distance. The knot in her shoulder had begun to ache with a dull, persistent throb. She focused on that. It was a real thing. It was hers.
