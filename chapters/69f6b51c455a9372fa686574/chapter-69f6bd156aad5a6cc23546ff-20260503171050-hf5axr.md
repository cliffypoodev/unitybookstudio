Chapter 16

The ghost light was off. That was the first thing Iris noticed when she pushed open the heavy door to the little dressing room they’d given her—a windowless box off the main rehearsal hall. The single bulb over the mirror was dead too, the room lit only by the grey afternoon light seeping under the door from the corridor. It gave everything a flat, underwater quality. The room smelled of dust and the faint, sweetish tang of old face powder. Someone had left a coffee mug on the vanity, a dark tidemark circling its inside like the memory of a pond.

She didn’t remember leaving it.

Her coat felt heavy, sodden with the damp cold she’d carried in from the street. She shrugged it off and let it slump over the single wooden chair. The fabric made a sound like a sigh. She should go home. Arthur would be waiting would have questions about her day, about the rehearsals, his voice a careful instrument tuning itself to the pitch of her guilt. The thought was a physical pressure behind her eyes. Instead she stood there, listening to the empty building. A radiator ticked. Somewhere, a door closed with a soft, definitive click.

On the scarred surface of the vanity, beside the mug, lay a folded sheet of paper.

It wasn’t hers. She never left papers here. Her scripts, her notes, she took them with her. A habit born from years of hotels and shared green rooms, a distrust of leaving traces. This paper was thick, creamy, the kind used for formal correspondence. It had been folded neatly into quarters.

Her hand moved before her mind could shape a question. The paper crackled as she opened it.

It was a typescript. A carbon copy, the letters a little blurred. No letterhead. No date.

Subject: Finch, Iris “The Canary.” File cross-reference: Operation Nightingale. Wartime asset registry, Paris-London corridor, 1942-1944. Asset designation: LYRIC. Handlers: See attached Annex B. Primary function: Signal relay via commercial radio broadcast and public performance. Material encoded in original musical compositions. Confirmed successful transmissions on seventeen occasions. See signal logs. Risk assessment post-war: HIGH. Asset possesses operational knowledge of network protocols and identities. Personal proclivities (see medical/psych evaluation, 1945) present additional security vulnerabilities. Current disposition: Civilian. Monitoring advised.

The words did not make sense at first. They were blocks of type, arranged in a familiar, bureaucratic pattern. She read them again. Operation Nightingale. Asset designation: LYRIC. The radiator ticked. A bus groaned past on the street outside, a distant, animal sound.

Seventeen occasions.

She saw the cramped studio at the BBC, the microphone like a black insect. The producer’s hand giving the countdown. Her own voice, steady, pouring out a ballad about a bluebird and a willow tree. The specific ache in her jaw from holding the smile in her voice. The engineer, a man named Peter with a kind face, giving her a thumbs-up through the glass. She’d thought he liked the song.

A medical evaluation. 1945. That was the year after Arthur found her. The year she agreed to marry him. She’d been run-down, nerves shot. He’d arranged for a doctor, a discreet man in a Park Avenue office. She’d answered questions. Yes, trouble sleeping. Yes, bad dreams. The doctor had been gentle, paternal. He’d prescribed tonics. She’d signed a form.

Her throat closed. A dry, tight closure, as if a wire had been drawn shut.

This wasn’t Arthur’s file. His was a collection of her past, her loves, her private handwriting. This was something else. This was a ledger. An inventory of a piece of machinery that had once been useful and was now a liability. Personal proclivities. That meant Pauline. That meant the whispers in Paris, the shared bed in the attic room, the way they’d worked on songs together, heads bent over a notebook. It meant the photograph Arthur kept. It meant everything.

The paper went unsteady in her hand. Not a shake, but a fine, constant vibration, as if it were alive.

Who had put it here?

Pauline? A test? A brutal way of saying see, you were never just a singer, you were always this, you can’t hide from it? But no. Pauline wouldn’t have this. This was government paper. Military or intelligence. The phrasing was cold, clean. Additional security vulnerabilities.

Arthur, then. A sharper warning. A reminder that his file was just the pretty frame around a much uglier painting.

But he wouldn’t leave it here. He controlled access. He doled out fear in measured doses, like medicine. This felt like a spill. An accident.

The door to the rehearsal hall was unlocked. Anyone could have come in. A student. A janitor. Cora, the secretary, with a message. Someone who wanted her to see. Someone who wanted her to know she was still being counted.

She looked at the vanity mirror. In the grey light, her reflection was a smudge, a woman-shaped shadow in a cave. Her mother had a saying: You make your bed, you lie in it. A practical, Midwestern verdict. Iris had made a bed of microphones and hotel linen and contract clauses. She had lain in it. She had sung in it. She had, it seemed, transmitted coded messages from it.

A laugh bubbled up, a harsh, dry thing that hurt her chest. She clamped a hand over her mouth. The sound was swallowed by the dusty room.

Seventeen times. What had she passed along? Weather reports? Ship movements? Names? She remembered the lyrics. Bluebird at the break of day, carries my sorrow far away. Willow weeps where the river bends, love on a telegram never ends. Nonsense. Sentimental dross. She’d been proud of the melody.

Had someone died because of her bluebird?

The question landed, a cold stone in the pit of her stomach. She sat down on the chair, her coat bunching beneath her. The wool was scratchy against her neck.

This changed the shape of the trap. It wasn’t just about who she loved. It was about what she had done. Arthur’s threats were personal, theatrical. This paper was historical. It was a fact. It could be entered into a congressional record without a blush. Operational knowledge. She could be charged not with a sin, but with a crime. Espionage. Treason. The words were too large; they belonged in newsreels.

She refolded the paper along its original creases. The action was precise, automatic. She put it in the inside pocket of her coat, over her heart. The cheap fabric did nothing to hide the crackle.

Stand in the light, Pauline had said.

But the light showed this. The light showed a line in a ledger, an asset decommissioned, a vulnerability to be managed. The light showed a woman who had, for years, carried a purpose she didn’t understand, like a radio playing in an empty room.

She got up. Her legs held. She put her coat back on. The paper rustled against her ribs with every breath, a secret second heartbeat. She turned off the grey light by opening the door to the corridor.

The hall was empty. The polished floor reflected the emergency exit signs in long, red smears. She walked toward the front entrance, her heels tapping a steady, ordinary rhythm on the linoleum. As she passed the main theatre doors, she heard a sound—a single, sustained note from a clarinet, pure and lonely, scaling upward, then falling away. A student, practicing in the empty auditorium. The sound followed her out into the chill of the evening, a ghost of a song, until the heavy door swung shut and stopped it whole.

The apartment smelled of lemon polish and the faint metallic scent of the radiator. Arthur had been home for hours. He’d left the lamps on—the good ones, the brass ones with the green glass shades they’d bought when her last record charted. The light pooled on the polished surface of the drinks trolley, glinting off the cut-crystal decanters. Everything was in its place: the magazines fanned on the low table, the ashtray empty and waiting, the sofa cushions plumped. It was a stage set. The air was warm, too warm, and still.

He was in his armchair by the window, a book open on his lap. He didn’t look up when she came in. He turned a page. The sound was crisp in the quiet.

She hung her coat in the closet, her movements slow, deliberate. The wool was still damp. She could feel the folded paper in her pocket, a stiff square against her thigh. It felt like it was generating its own cold.

“You’re late,” Arthur said. He spoke mildly. He still didn’t look at her.

“Rehearsal ran long.”

“I made calls. To the club. They said you left at four.” He closed the book, laid it on the table beside him. It was a biography of Roosevelt. The bookmark was a strip of ivory leather. “I was concerned.”

“I went for a walk.”

“In this weather.” Finally, he looked at her. His face was calm, composed. The lamplight softened the lines around his eyes. He looked like a man at ease in his own home. “You’ll catch a chill, Iris. You need your voice.”

She stood in the middle of the room, her hands at her sides. The radiator ticked. On the wall above the mantelpiece was a painting they’d bought at a gallery on Fifty-Seventh Street—abstract shapes in grey and blue. She’d never liked it. The blue was the wrong shade, too bright, like a robin’s egg. It made her think of a childhood Easter, a dress she’d hated, the way the starch in the collar had chafed her neck.

“Something happen at the institute?” he asked.

“No.”

“Did Pauline give you trouble?”

“No trouble.”

He nodded, as if her answers satisfied him. He stood went to the trolley, and poured two fingers of Scotch into a glass. He didn’t offer her one. He took a slow sip, his back to her. The fabric of his suit jacket stretched taut across his shoulders. It was a new suit. A fine, dark wool. “I had a meeting today. With a man from the committee. The same one you spoke with. He had some questions.”

Her mouth went dry. A familiar, cottony dryness. “What questions?”

“Procedural things. Timelines. Associations.” He turned, leaned against the trolley, glass cradled in his hand. “He’s building a file. You understand. They all build files. It’s what they do.”

“What did you tell him?”

“The truth, of course. That you’re a patriot. That you’re cooperating fully. That your work at the institute is proof of your willingness to contribute.” He took another sip. “He was particularly interested in your wartime work. The broadcasts. The USO tours.”

The room seemed to tilt, just slightly. The blue in the painting swam. “Why?”

“Context, he said. They like to have the full picture.” Arthur’s eyes were on her face, watching. “I told him you were a dedicated performer. That you lifted morale. That you were decorated by the French government for your contributions to Allied radio.”

“I was.”

“You were.” He smiled. It was a small, professional smile. “I may have mentioned your code name, in passing. It came up in the documentation he had. ‘Lyric.’ A fitting name don’t you think? Poetic.”

The word hung in the warm air. Lyric. It wasn’t a code name. It was a designation. An asset designation. The paper in her pocket seemed to burn through the fabric.

“You told him that?”

“It was in his file already, Iris. He knew. I was simply confirming.” He set the glass down. The crystal made a soft click on the trolley’s surface. “He has quite a file. Thicker than I expected. Signal logs. Transmission confirmations. Notes from handlers. A real operational history. It seems you were busier than I ever knew.”

She couldn’t move. Her feet were rooted to the carpet. The pattern was a geometric swirl of burgundy and gold. She’d chosen it. She remembered the showroom, the salesman unrolling the sample, Arthur saying it looked expensive. She’d agreed.

“You never told me the extent of it,” Arthur said. The way he said it was still mild, but there was a new thread in it. Something like reproach. Or curiosity. “All those years. The trips to London. The studio sessions at the BBC. You made it sound like entertainment. Morale. But it was more than that wasn’t it?”

“It was what they asked me to do.”

“They. Who is ‘they,’ Iris?”

“The people who asked.” She lowered the words, thin and distant.

“The people who asked.” He repeated it slowly, as if tasting the words. “The network. The apparatus. You were a part of something. A cog. A rather important one, it seems.” He pushed off from the trolley and took a step toward her. “My file—our file—it’s personal. Letters. Photographs. Sentimental things. His file is cold. It’s a ledger. It lists you as a high-risk asset. Do you know what that means?”

She didn’t answer. She was looking at his hands. He had beautiful hands. Long fingers, clean nails. A pianist’s hands. He’d never played.

“It means you’re not just a woman with unfortunate private tastes. You’re a woman with state secrets in her head. Protocols. Names. The kind of information that doesn’t expire.” He was close now. She could smell the Scotch on his breath, the clean starch of his shirt. “My file protects you from scandal. His file condemns you for treason. Do you see the difference?”

The radiator knocked. A sharp, hollow sound.

“Why are you telling me this?” The question came out a whisper.

“Because you need to understand the stakes. Completely.” He reached out, not touching her, but his hand hovered near her arm. A gesture that could be comfort or a warning. “Your cooperation with the committee isn’t a suggestion anymore. It’s the only thing standing between you and a very different kind of hearing. One that doesn’t care about who you shared a bed with. One that cares about who you passed messages for.”

She thought of the paper in her dressing room. The creamy stock. The blurred carbon letters. An accident. A spill. Or a message. From him. A demonstration.

“You left something for me,” she said. “Today. At the institute.”

His eyebrows lifted, just a fraction. A performance of surprise. “I haven’t been to Harlem in weeks.”

“A page. From a file. It was on my vanity.”

“Perhaps Pauline left it. A dramatic gesture. She always had a flair.”

“It wasn’t Pauline.”

He sighed, a sound of gentle exasperation. He dropped his hand. “Iris. Look at me.” She forced her eyes to his. They were a pale, watery blue. Kind eyes. The eyes of a man who took care of things. “I am your husband. I am your manager. My only interest is your safety. Our safety. The file I keep—our file—it exists to give us options. To give us leverage. To prove your value as a cooperative witness. The other file. That is the abyss. You do not want them looking too closely at that file. And they will, if you are not helpful. If you are not convincingly, publicly, on their side.”

He stepped back, retrieved his glass, finished the Scotch. He grimaced slightly, as if the taste had turned. “The play. This Port Chicago. You will perform it. You will be brilliant. And when it is done, you will provide the committee with a full account of the political discussions at the institute. The sentiments expressed. The individuals involved. You will name names. Starting with Pauline Carter.”

The word was out before she could stop it. Small. Hard.

“No.”

Arthur went very still. He set the glass down again, precisely in the center of a cork coaster. “What did you say?”

“I won’t do that.”

He studied her. The kindness drained from his eyes, leaving something flat and assessing. “You are tired. You’ve had a shock. We’ll discuss this tomorrow.”

“There’s nothing to discuss.”

“Iris.” He said her name like a period. A full stop. “You have a choice. You can be the cooperative witness, the redeemed artist who saw the error of her ways and is now helping her country. Or you can be the exposed asset, the traitorous deviant who conspired against the United States and corrupted the youth of Harlem. The first choice involves a period of difficulty, then a quiet retirement. Perhaps even a return to modest performance. The second choice involves a prison cell. Or a hospital. Do you understand the choice?”

She understood. The warmth of the room was suffocating. The blue in the painting was too bright. It hurt her eyes.

“I understand,” she said.

“Good.” The professional smile returned, a little tighter now. “Get some rest. You look pale.”

He picked up his book and settled back into his chair. The audience was over. She was dismissed.

She walked to the bedroom, each step an effort. In the hallway, the floorboard creaked under the runner, a familiar, mournful sound. She closed the bedroom door behind her. The room was dark. The streetlight outside cast a long, skeletal shadow of the window frame across the bed.

She didn’t turn on the lamp. She took the folded paper from her pocket sat on the edge of the bed, and smoothed it open on the coverlet. She couldn’t read it in the dark, but she didn’t need to. The words were etched behind her eyes.

Asset designation: LYRIC.

Arthur’s file was a cage. But this—this was the lock. And he hadn’t put it there. Someone else had. Someone who wanted her to know that the lock existed, that it could be turned, that her husband was just another man holding a key he didn’t forge.

She folded the paper again, small, smaller, until it was a dense, thick square. She held it in her closed fist. The radiator knocked again, a persistent, rhythmic complaint. Somewhere in the building, a toilet flushed. A door closed. The ordinary sounds of an ordinary evening. She sat there for a long time, listening to them, the paper a hard, cold stone in her hand.

The stage was a vast, dark mouth. Iris stood at its edge, one hand on the rough velvet of the curtain, the other holding the folded paper. She had left the dressing room and walked the length of the empty corridor, her footsteps the only sound. The house lights were off. The ghost light, that single bulb on a stand in the center of the stage was the only source of illumination. It cast a small, lonely circle of yellow on the worn floorboards, leaving the rest of the theatre to the deep blue of near-darkness. The air smelled of old varnish and dust, and underneath that, the faint, clean scent of lemon oil someone had used on the railings.

She stepped into the light.

The paper in her hand was no longer trembling. It felt inert, a dead leaf. She had read the words so many times they had lost their shape, becoming only a cold pressure behind her breastbone. Asset designation: LYRIC. Additional security vulnerabilities.

She let her coat slide from her shoulders. It pooled at her feet, a dark puddle. The air was chilly, raising gooseflesh on her arms. She should be afraid. She knew the script for fear—the dry mouth, the racing heart, the urge to look over her shoulder. This was different. A hollowed-out feeling, as if someone had taken a spoon and scraped her clean. The fear was there, but it was distant, like traffic noise from another street.

Her eyes adjusted. She could make out the first few rows of seats, their plush curves like sleeping animals. A forgotten playbill lay crumpled under a seat, a white smudge in the dark. On the far wall, a fire extinguisher gleamed dully in its glass case.

She had come here to think. That was the story she told herself. To find a quiet place, to decide what to do. But her mind was blank. It kept circling back to the coffee mug in the dressing room, the tidemark. Had it been hers? She couldn’t remember the pattern. Was it blue? The memory wouldn’t come.

A line from an old song surfaced, unbidden. One of her first recordings, a trifle. The willow knows what the river told, in a language made of silver and cold. Nonsense. Pretty, melodic nonsense she’d written to please a producer who wanted something pastoral. She’d sung it into the BBC microphone. Seventeen occasions.

Her feet moved. Not a decision, just a shift of weight. She walked to the center of the ghost light’s circle. The boards creaked under her. She looked out at the empty seats.

“Hello,” she said. She spoke, a scratchy thing, hesitant in the dark.

Silence answered. Not a peaceful silence. An attentive one.

She cleared her throat. The sound was too loud. “My name is Iris Finch.”

That wasn’t right. She tried again, pitching her voice lower, aiming for the back row. “My name is Iris Finch. I am forty-eight years old.”

It sounded like a confession. Perhaps it was.

“I was a singer.” She paused. The past tense sat in the air, wrong and final. “I am a singer. And in the war, I transmitted coded messages for the Allied resistance through my radio broadcasts.”

She waited. The words didn’t change anything. The seats didn’t gasp. The fire extinguisher didn’t fall from its case. They were just sounds, leaving her lips and dissolving into the thirsty dark. She had never said it aloud. Not to Arthur, not to the discreet doctor, not even to herself in the mirror. Saying it here, to no one felt like dropping a stone into a well and hearing no splash.

A memory surfaced, sharp and bright. Not of the broadcasts, but of the time before. Paris, 1937. Pauline at a café table, sketching on a napkin. Every art is a code, she’d said, her finger tracing a line. The question is, who are you trying to talk to, and what do you want to say so badly that you’re willing to hide it? Iris had laughed and called it pretentious. She’d been working on a love song, a simple thing about moonlight. She’d wanted it to be a hit.

She looked down at the paper in her hand. An inventory. They had turned her art into a line in a ledger. Primary function: Signal relay.

A hot, sudden anger flashed through her—a quick, bright spike that tightened her jaw. It wasn’t at the faceless bureaucrat who wrote the report. It was at herself. For letting them. For thinking the code was just in the lyrics, in the agreed-upon phrases about bluebirds and willows. The real code had been in the performance itself. In the act of standing in front of a microphone and lying with her whole heart. They had used her willingness to lie.

The anger faded as fast as it came, leaving a residue of clarity, cold and metallic on her tongue.

She wouldn’t lie tonight.

But she couldn’t tell the plain truth, either. Not here. Not yet.

She closed her eyes. The darkness behind her lids was a deeper blue. She thought of the lines from Port Chicago, her character’s big monologue in the second act. The clerk, trying to explain his silence. The words were good. Pauline’s words always were. But they weren’t hers.

Her right hand came up, fingers loosely curled as if around a microphone that wasn’t there. Her breath steadied. This was the preparation, the second before the downbeat. The world narrowed to the space between her ribs and the back wall of the theatre.

She began to sing.

Not a song. A scale. A simple, ascending major scale, the first thing every vocal student learns. The words came out rough, unused, the notes clear but unadorned. Do, re, mi, fa, sol, la, ti do. She held the last note, let it vibrate in the empty space until her breath ran out.

Silence rushed back in.

Then she sang it again. But this time, on the fourth note—fa—she let it crack. Just a fraction, a deliberate, ugly flatness. On the seventh note—ti—she did it again, a sharp, brittle sound that wasn’t quite the right pitch. She repeated the corrupted scale. Do, re, mi, fa-flat, sol, la, ti-sharp do.

It was a mess. A broken thing.

She opened her eyes. The empty seats stared back.

She sang it a third time, embedding the same two wrong notes. Then a fourth. Each repetition was slower, more deliberate. The wrong notes were no longer mistakes. They were landmarks. A pattern. A simple, musical cipher. Two deviations in an eight-note sequence. If you knew to listen for them, they spelled something. A short, brutal truth.

I am here. I am compromised. I am not what they think.

She didn’t know who would hear it, or when. Maybe no one. Maybe it was just for the dust and the shadows. But it was hers. Not a broadcast for the war effort. Not a hit for the charts. A signal sent from a dark stage into a darker room, with no expectation of reply.

Her throat ached. She stopped.

The quiet that followed was different. It felt occupied. As if the theatre had been listening, and had understood.

From the wings, a soft scrape.

Iris turned, her heart a sudden, hard knock against her ribs.

Pauline stood just outside the circle of light, one shoulder leaning against the proscenium arch. She was still in her day clothes—a dark skirt, a cream-colored blouse. Her arms were crossed. How long had she been there?

Iris’s mouth went dry. She said nothing.

Pauline didn’t speak either. Her gaze was on Iris, but it wasn’t accusatory. It was measuring, the way a director watches a run-through, seeing the shape of the thing, not the performer.

After a moment, Pauline pushed off from the arch and walked into the light. She stopped a few feet away, her eyes dropping to the crumpled paper in Iris’s hand, then lifting back to her face. “I wondered where you’d gone,” she said. She said it quietly, almost conversationally. “Cora said you never collected your messages.”

Iris found her voice. It was hoarse. “I was. Practicing.”

“I heard.” Pauline’s head tilted slightly. “A strange exercise. Unless you’re planning to audition for a tone-deaf choir.”

“It’s a code,” Iris said. The words were out before she could stop them, blunt and foolish.

Pauline didn’t smile. She just nodded, once, as if Iris had confirmed something she already knew. “I gathered.” She looked past Iris, out at the vacant seats. “Who’s it for?”

“I don’t know.”

“That’s the only honest way to do it.” Pauline’s attention returned to her. “If you know who you’re talking to, you start editing. Trying to please. Or protect.” She paused. “It’s harder, talking to no one. It’s the purest form.”

Iris looked at the paper in her hand. The urge to show it to Pauline was a physical pull in her chest. Look. This is what they say I am. But she didn’t move. Showing it would be another kind of editing—a plea for pity, or alliance. She folded the paper once more, pushed it into the pocket of her skirt.

“I should go,” she said. “Arthur will be waiting.”

Pauline didn’t try to stop her. She just took a small step back, clearing the path to the wings. “The light’s better on this side,” she said, not looking at Iris. “For finding your way out.”

Iris bent, picked up her coat. The fabric was cold. She walked past Pauline, through the narrow gap between a flat stack and a ladder, her shoulder brushing against coarse canvas. She didn’t look back.

In the corridor, the institutional lights were blinding after the stage’s dark. Her footsteps echoed. She could still feel the vibration of the wrong notes in her throat, a phantom tremor. She put a hand to her neck and felt the jump of her pulse beneath the skin. It was steady. It was her own.
