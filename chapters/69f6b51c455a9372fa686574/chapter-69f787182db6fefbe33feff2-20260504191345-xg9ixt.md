The rehearsal room had a particular smell in the late afternoon, when the dust motes swam thickest in the diagonal light from the high windows. It was old varnish and wool, the ghost of a thousand cigarettes, and something else—the faint, sour tang of stage sweat trapped in velvet curtains. Iris sat on a folding chair, its metal cold through her skirt. She’d been sitting for twenty minutes. Pauline was late.

The others filled the space with a low, restless energy. Ben, the young actor playing the naval officer, tapped a rhythm on his knee with two fingers—a nervous paradiddle. Sarah, cast as the Black worker’s sister, stared at a crack in the plaster ceiling, her lips moving silently over lines. Clara, at the far table by the light board, sorted through a box of props, her back a precise, dismissive line. She hadn’t looked at Iris once.

Iris traced the rim of the script in her lap. Port Chicago. The title was typed in a severe, blocky font. She’d read it three times since Pauline thrust it into her hands that morning. Her thumb found a rough spot on the edge of the paper, a tiny imperfection. She worried it.

The door at the back of the hall creaked open—not the main door with its theatrical groan, but the small side entrance from the prop storage, a sound like a tired sigh. Pauline entered, a folder under one arm, a cigarette already trailing a thin blue line from her other hand. She didn’t apologize. She walked to the front, the click of her heels on the wooden floor a deliberate metronome. She placed the folder on a music stand she used as a lectern, took a final drag, and stubbed the cigarette out in a chipped saucer.

“We’ll begin,” she said. Her voice was flat, carrying without effort. “You’ve read it.

It wasn’t a question. A few murmurs of assent.

“Good. Then you know it isn’t a play about an explosion. It’s a play about a silence. A specific, engineered silence.” Pauline’s gaze swept the room. It passed over Iris, lingered for a half-second on Clara, then returned to the script before her. “July seventeenth, 1944. A munitions depot in California. Three hundred and twenty men killed instantly. Most of them Black sailors, assigned to handle explosives without proper training. The Navy called it an accident. They court-martialed the survivors for refusing to return to work. For mutiny.

She paused. The only sound was the distant, intermittent rattle of the elevated train on 125th Street, a sound so woven into the fabric of the building you only noticed it when it stopped.

“Our play is about the wife of the white officer in charge of the loading divisions. Eleanor.” Pauline’s eyes found Iris then. They were dark, unreadable. “She hears the blast from her home on the base. She sees the sky turn orange. She spends the next weeks listening to her husband lie to the press, to the families, to himself. She says nothing.

Iris’s mouth was dry. She tried to swallow, but her throat had tightened into a knot.

“Iris will play Eleanor,” Pauline said, her tone leaving no room for discussion. “It’s a role of observation. Of complicity. Of understanding the exact dimensions of the cage you’re in, and choosing to polish the bars.

Something hot moved through Iris’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It settled low in her stomach, a cold, hard weight. She looked down at the script. Eleanor’s lines were sparse. She was a presence, a listener. Her biggest scene was a monologue addressed to a pot of azaleas on her porch, a speech about the color red, while her husband shouted on the telephone in the next room.

“Ben, you’re Lieutenant Commander Riggs. Sarah, you’re Lena Jones, sister of one of the men. Michael, you’ll read the parts of the Navy lawyer and the press secretary.” Pauline assigned the rest with swift, surgical efficiency. “We’ll do a read-through. No performance. Just the words. Find the rhythm of the evasion.

The chairs scraped as people shifted, opened scripts. Iris’s fingers felt numb. She flipped to the first page. The type blurred for a second, the letters swimming. She blinked, and they snapped back into sharp, black focus.

Pauline sat in a chair to the side, lighting another cigarette. “From the top. Ben.

Ben cleared his throat. His voice was too loud at first, a young man’s voice trying on authority. “The men are nervous, sir. They’re saying the fuses are unstable.

The read-through was a stilted, halting thing. Voices stumbled over the clinical jargon of military reports, the legalistic doublespeak of the inquiry. Iris waited for her entrance.

Iris waited for her entrance. The signal was a single flicker of the overhead lights, the agreed-upon cue. She counted the seconds between the first stutter and the second.

Two.

Three.

Four.

The second flicker never came. Something was wrong. The service corridor behind the kitchen was a throat of stainless steel and stale grease. Her contact, a man named Finch who smelled of onions and anxiety, was supposed to be at the junction. He wasn’t.

She slid the lockpick from her sleeve, a sliver of blackened steel. The door to the main hall was a simple pin-tumbler, a joke for someone of her skill. But Finch’s absence was a complication she couldn’t pick. She pressed her ear to the cold metal.

Muffled voices. Laughter. The clink of glass. A party in full swing, oblivious. Then a different sound—a sharp intake of breath, cut short. A scuffle of shoes on tile.

She stepped back, her shoulder blades pressing against the opposite wall. The lockpick vanished back into its sheath. Her hand found the grip of the flechette pistol tucked into the small of her back. The weapon was a last resort, louder than a thunderclap in these enclosed spaces.

“—don’t care what he promised you.” The voice from the other side of the door was low, graveled by smoke or spite. “The deal’s off. He’s been… reassigned.

A whimper answered. Finch.

“The girl. Where is she?

“I don’t—I never saw—”

The sound of impact was wet, final. A body hit the floor. Iris didn’t hear the thud; she felt it through the soles of her boots.

Silence bled through the door. Then footsteps, moving away.

Her plan was ash. Finch was dead or dying. Her way in was compromised. The objective—a data-sliver hidden in the host’s private study—was now guarded by a man who just killed her contact. She had two choices: abort, or find another door.

Iris chose the door.

She moved down the service corridor, away from the kitchen hum. The building was a pre-Collapse artifact, all art deco curves and hidden passages for staff long gone. She remembered the schematics. A ventilation shaft ran parallel to the west ballroom, exiting near a janitor’s closet one floor above the study.

The access panel was where the plans said it would be, secured by four rust-eaten screws. She worked them loose with the tip of her pick, her movements steady, her breath a shallow tide in her chest. No fear. Just calculation. The panel came free with a sigh of dust.

The shaft was a black maw. She crawled in, the metal groaning under her weight. Darkness swallowed her. She navigated by memory and touch, counting the crossbeams. The air tasted of old mildew and rodent droppings.

A grating below her feet glowed with slivers of light from the ballroom. She paused, peering down. Figures moved in a slow, colorful swirl. Music filtered up, a string quartet sawing through something mournful. She spotted her target: Aris Veldt, the host, holding court near a fountain of sparkling wine. He laughed, a bright, empty sound.

He didn’t look like a man who’d just ordered a murder in his pantry.

Iris moved on. The shaft terminated at another grate. This one overlooked a narrow utility landing. She eased the grate open, lowered herself down, and landed in a crouch. The janitor’s closet smelled of bleach and neglect. The door opened onto a deserted upper hallway lined with guest suites.

The study was at the end, behind a door of dark, polished wood. No visible lock. Biometric, then. She pulled a thin foil packet from an inner pocket, unfolded it to reveal a translucent gel sheet. Veldt’s fingerprint, lifted from a glass he’d used at a charity gala three nights prior. She pressed the sheet against the scanner plate.

The light blinked from red to green. The door clicked open.

The room was a tomb of quiet luxury. A massive desk dominated the space, carved from a single piece of some dark, oily wood. One wall was a window overlooking the city’s glittering spine; the other was lined with books that looked untouched. Iris went straight to the desk.

She bypassed the terminal—too noisy, too traceable. The data-sliver was physical, a failsafe. Veldt was old-school. It would be in a secure container, something he could hold, could destroy. Her eyes scanned the desktop: an antique inkwell, a marble paperweight, a framed hologram of a younger Veldt shaking hands with a senator.

Too obvious.

She knelt, running her fingers along the underside of the desk drawer. Nothing. The back panel of the desk. Clean. Her gaze drifted to the paperweight. It was the only item without dust. She picked it up. It was lighter than marble should be.

A twist, a click. The top half separated from the bottom. Nestled inside, on a bed of black foam, was a sliver of crystal no larger than her thumbnail.

“I’d leave that, if I were you.”

The voice came from the doorway. Iris didn’t jump, didn’t gasp. She let the two halves of the paperweight fall apart in her hands, the data-sliver exposed on her palm. She turned.

The man filled the frame. He wasn’t tall, but he was wide, his shoulders straining the seams of a service staff jacket that was too tight. His knuckles were raw. Finch’s killer.

“Veldt send you to clean up?” Iris asked. Her voice was calm, a flat pond.

“Something like that.” He stepped into the room, letting the door sigh shut behind him. “You’re not on the guest list.

“I’m allergic to canapés.”

He smiled, a thin crack in a slab of face. “Hand it over. The sliver. And the hand, if you want to be difficult.

Iris considered the flechette pistol. The report would bring the entire house down on her. She considered the window. Twenty stories to a street that wouldn’t cushion her fall.

She took a step back, toward the bookcase. “Finch. Was he difficult?

“He was talkative. Right up until he wasn’t.” The man took a matching step forward. His hands were empty, but they didn’t need to hold a weapon. “Don’t be talkative. Just be smart.

“Smart.” Iris let the word hang. Her heel touched the bookshelf. “Finch was smart. He made a deal.

“And look where it got him.”

Iris’s fingers brushed the spines of the books. Leather, fake gold leaf. She found the gap she was looking for, the one her schematics had hinted at—a hidden panel behind a false set of encyclopedias. A legacy of the building’s paranoid original owner. She pressed.

With a soft thunk, a section of the bookcase swung inward.

The man’s eyes flickered to the dark opening. That was all the distraction she needed.

She threw the paperweight. Not at him, but at the window. It struck the glass with a sharp crack, a spiderweb of fractures erupting across the pane. The man flinched, instinct pulling his gaze toward the sudden noise.

Iris was already moving. She ducked into the passage, yanking the bookcase door shut behind her. Darkness, complete and absolute. She heard his roar of frustration, the thud of a fist against wood on the other side.

The passage was narrow, steep. A servant’s stair from a forgotten era. She took the steps two at a time, one hand skimming the rough wall for balance. The sound of splintering wood echoed from above. He was coming through.

She hit a landing, felt another door. It was locked, bolted from the other side. A dead end. She turned, her back to the door, and drew the flechette pistol. The glow from its charge cell painted the cramped stairwell in a sickly blue light.

Boots hammered on the steps above. He rounded the corner, a silhouette framed in the faint light from the broken study window filtering down the shaft. He saw the pistol. He stopped.

“That’s a mistake,” he said, his breath ragged from the chase.

“The first of many tonight.” Iris kept the barrel level. “Stay there.

“Or what? You’ll fire? You might hit me. You’ll definitely bring Veldt’s whole security team down on us. They’ll seal the block. You’ll never get out.

“You won’t care. You’ll be dead.

He took another step down. “Maybe. But you’re not a killer. I can see it. You’re a thief. A sneaker. This?” He gestured at the weapon. “This is a prop. You don’t have the nerve.

Iris’s finger rested on the trigger. The weight of it was familiar, a cold promise. He was wrong. She had the nerve. But he was also right about the noise. It was a last resort.

“Finch had a daughter,” she said.

The man paused. “What?

“He showed me a picture. A little girl. He was doing this for her. School fees.

A shrug, a shift in the dark. “Sentiment’s a liability.

“Is it?” Iris adjusted her grip. “Veldt pays you for loyalty. Or for silence. Which is it?

“What’s the difference?”

“Loyalty has a price. Silence is just a timer waiting to run out.” She took a slow breath. The data-sliver was a hard, cool pressure in her closed fist. “Veldt knows you failed. The moment I crawled into that vent, you failed. He sent you to clean up one mess. Now there are two. Me. And the fact that I got this far.

She saw his head tilt, just slightly. Considering.

“You think he’ll let that slide?” she pressed. “You just became the next mess, Mr…?

“Kael.” The name slipped out, automatic, then he stiffened as if he’d given something away.

Iris almost smiled. An AI-slop name, born in some generator. It fit him, in a pathetic way. “Kael. He’ll have another Kael here before your body’s cold. You know it.

The truth of it hung in the stale air between them. She could see the calculation in his stillness. His loyalty, or his contract, warring with a sudden, sharp instinct for self-preservation.

“The sliver,” he said, but the command was gone from his voice.

“Is mine. My client paid for it. Your problem is with Veldt. He’s the one who set you up to fail.” She lowered the pistol, just an inch. A gesture of… not trust. A reassessment of threat. “The service elevator is at the end of the hall outside this door. It goes down to the sub-basement, to a freight tunnel. It’s off the security grid. A blind spot.

“How do you know that?”

“I read the original blueprints. Veldt didn’t. He just bought the place.” She nodded toward the door behind her. “I can’t open this from here. You can, from the other side. You let me out. I disappear with the sliver. You go down the elevator. You tell Veldt I overpowered you, escaped through the window. Or you tell him nothing, and you disappear, too.

Silence. The distant quartet music was a ghost of sound.

“Why would you give me a way out?” he asked.

“Because a dead guard is a complication. A missing guard is a mystery. Mysteries take time to unravel. I need time.

He was silent for a long moment. Then he let out a slow breath. “The bolt’s at the top. Rusted.

“I’m sure you can manage.”

He turned and climbed back up the stairs, out of sight. Iris waited, pistol raised again. She heard grunting, the screech of metal yielding. A heavy clunk.

The door behind her swung inward.

Dim hallway light spilled in. Kael stood there, outlined in the doorway. He looked at her, at the pistol still aimed at his center mass.

“The elevator’s to the left,” she said, not moving.

He nodded once, a sharp jerk of his chin. Then he turned and walked away, his footsteps fading down the hall.

Iris waited until she heard the distant rumble of the elevator descending. She stepped out of the passage, closed the hidden door until it clicked. The hallway was deserted. She holstered the pistol and started walking, not toward the front of the house, but deeper into its servant’s heart.

She had the sliver. She had a head start. And she had a new problem: Aris Veldt now knew someone had breached his inner sanctum. He would lock down, change his patterns, bury his secrets deeper.

Her client wouldn’t like that. But that was a problem for the next meeting. For now, she needed to vanish into the city’s lower arteries. She found a laundry chute, dropped down two floors into a cart of soiled linens, and emerged in a basement humming with industrial washers. A delivery bay door stood open to a damp alley.

She slipped out into the night. The cold air bit her cheeks. She didn’t look back at the glittering tower. She walked, her pace even, until she reached a crowded transit platform. She boarded a southbound pod, found a corner seat, and finally opened her fist.

The data-sliver caught the flickering ad-light, casting a tiny, fractured rainbow on her palm. It was intact. The job was done.

For now.

The pod accelerated into the tunnel. Her reflection in the dark window was a ghost over the rushing walls. She thought of Finch’s daughter, a face in a hologram. She thought of Kael, disappearing into the underground. She thought of the crack in the window, spreading like a map of new fault lines.

She closed her hand around the sliver. The next move was hers.
