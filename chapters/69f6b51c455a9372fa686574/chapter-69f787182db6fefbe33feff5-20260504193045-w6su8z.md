The basement of the church had a particular smell on Monday mornings. Ozone from the old wiring, yes, and the damp concrete, but also the ghost of Sunday’s incense, a cloying sweetness that had seeped into the plaster and now fought a losing battle against the scent of dust and cold coffee. Iris stood in the center of the rehearsal hall, the script for Port Chicago held loosely in her left hand. Her right thumb traced the edge of the paper, back and forth, a silent metronome. The space was empty, the chairs stacked against the brick wall, the work lights off. Only the dim grey morning light filtered through the high, narrow windows, painting bars across the scarred floor.

She had come early, needing the quiet. Needing to stand in the space without Pauline’s gaze, without the other actors’ tentative curiosity, without the weight of the words. She closed her eyes and tried to hear the scene. Eleanor’s first monologue. Not the meaning of the words—the shape of them. The rhythm. A woman setting a table for a husband who would not come home, speaking to the empty chairs. It wasn’t grief. It was the performance of normality, a bright, brittle shell. Iris imagined the sound of it: a high, clear flute line, but with a tremor underneath, a vibrato born of a hand pressed too hard against a wound.

Her own throat felt tight. Not from the character. From the meeting two days prior, in the diner with Cross. The fly against the window. His brother in the library. There is a cost to dwelling on the dark things. The pressure had shifted. It was no longer just about her past. It was about the play itself. The thing she was helping to make. His suggestion had been clear as a razor cut: walk away. Make it easier for everyone.

A door clicked open somewhere above, in the main body of the church. Footsteps, heavy and confident, echoed on the wooden stairs that led down to the basement annex. Not Pauline’s measured tread. Not Clara’s quick, light steps. This was a man’s walk, and it was accompanied by another set, lighter, sharper. Business shoes.

Iris moved without thinking. The instinct was old, from a hundred green rooms and backstage corridors: the need to be unseen before being seen. She slipped behind the tall, wheeled flats stacked against the far wall—a painted interior of a genteel living room, now just a façade of stability leaning at an angle. The space was narrow, dark. She could see a slice of the hall through a gap between the canvases.

Duke Strauss came into view first. He wore a camel-hair coat over a suit the color of expensive bourbon. He carried a silver-tipped cane, though he didn’t need it; it was a prop, a mark of theatricality. Behind him was a thinner, older man in a severe black overcoat, carrying a briefcase. His hair was the colour of old typewriter keys.

“Christ, it’s a crypt,” Strauss said, his voice booming in the empty space. He took off his hat and looked around, his expression one of amused distaste. “You’re sure this is the place, Hal?

“The address is correct, Mr. Strauss,” the other man said. His voice was dry, precise. A lawyer’s voice. “The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. Incorporated 1946. Director: Pauline Carter. Non-profit status pending.

“Pending. I love that. Means they’re begging for it.” Strauss walked to the center of the room, right where Iris had been standing. He tapped his cane on the floor. “Solid enough. They’ll have to re-wire the whole joint. Fire hazard waiting to happen. But the location… the location has cachet. A church in Harlem. The papers’ll eat that up with a spoon.

“The backers will appreciate the symbolism,” Hal said, setting his briefcase on a folded-out chair with a soft click. “It reads as authentic. A grassroots endeavor. That is the primary requirement.

“The primary requirement is that it doesn’t blow back on them. Or on me.” Strauss lit a cigarette, the match flaring in the dim light. He exhaled a plume of smoke that drifted toward the high ceiling. “That’s what the Canary is for. Pretty, damaged wrapper. Everyone looks at her, they see a washed-up chanteuse trying for a second act. A little tragic, a little brave. They don’t look too hard at what’s inside the box.

Iris’s breath stopped. Her thumb, still resting against the script, went cold.

“Her involvement provides plausible deniability,” Hal agreed, his tone clinical. “A mainstream name, however tarnished. It suggests artistic ambition, not political agitation. The committee’s focus will be diluted. They may question her judgment, but they will be less likely to trace the financing to the consortium.

“Exactly. She’s the lightning rod. She takes the hit if the weather turns.” Strauss gestured with his cigarette. “And if the play’s a dud, artistically, we blame her lack of stage experience. If it’s too hot, we say she was led astray by the radical element here. We win both ways. And our friends in the back room get their message staged in a respectable theatre, with a respectable face on the poster.

“The transfer to the Cort Theatre is already being drafted. The contract will stipulate her top billing, of course. It’s essential to the strategy.

“Of course. Let her have her name in lights. It’s all she’s ever wanted, anyway.” Strauss chuckled, a low, unkind sound. “She’s perfect for it. Hungry enough to say yes, scared enough to do as she’s told. And that husband of hers… Langston. He’s already on the phone twice a week, smelling a percentage. He’ll keep her in line. He wants the comeback story almost as much as she does. Almost.

Hal opened his briefcase, the sound of latches snapping back sharp as gunshots in the quiet. “The initial transfer of funds to the institute’s account was completed last Friday. Through the intermediary. Miss Carter appears to have accepted it without undue suspicion. She is pragmatic. The art requires capital.

“She’s a true believer. They’re the easiest to handle. Give them just enough rope to hang their masterpiece.” Strauss began to pace, his cane tapping a slow, irregular rhythm. “The trick is to keep the two elements separate. The money stays clean, filtered. The talent stays focused on the stage. And our little Canary sits in the middle, singing the tune we wrote, thinking it’s her own idea.

“And if the committee does subpoena her? Given her… existing arrangement?

Strauss paused. He took a long drag on his cigarette. The ember glowed. “Then she’s already compromised. She’ll give them whatever they want to hear to save her own skin. Probably name a few junior members of the cast. A stagehand. She won’t touch the money. She won’t know who’s behind it. She’s a useful idiot. The best kind.

The words landed not as a shock, but as a cold, settling fact. It was the taste of bad coffee left on a radiator, bitter and familiar. Iris leaned her forehead against the rough wood of the flat. The painted wallpaper on the other side was a cheerful floral pattern. She stared at a tiny, perfect painted rose.

She was the wrapper. The decoy. Her face on the poster was not a redemption, but a shield for men with briefcases and intermediaries. Her participation didn’t legitimize the art; it laundered the politics. And Strauss knew about her arrangement with Cross. He assumed she’d cave. He was probably right.

The two men continued talking, their voices dropping into technicalities—tax structures, union contracts, the cost of hauling the set downtown. The specifics blurred into a low hum. Iris watched a spider traverse the corner of the canvas, a tiny, deliberate architect in a world of painted gardens and eavesdroppers.

After what felt like an hour, but could only have been minutes, they left. The lawyer’s precise footsteps, Strauss’s confident stride, receded up the stairs. The door clicked shut. The silence that followed was different. It had texture now. It was full of the words they had left behind, hanging in the air like motes of dust in the grey light.

Iris didn’t move. The cold from the brick wall at her back seeped through her coat. She thought of the bank draft Pauline had secured. The bank’s name had been one Strauss mentioned in passing over dinner. She’d noted it, the way she noted a minor key change in an otherwise conventional song, and then forgotten. She remembered now.

She pushed herself away from the flats. Her legs were stiff. She walked back to the center of the room, to the very spot Strauss had occupied. She looked down. There was a cigarette butt, crushed on the floor. A brand she didn’t recognize. She knelt and picked it up, the paper damp from the concrete. A pointless thing to do.

The rehearsal would begin soon. The actors would arrive, full of Monday morning energy and anxiety. Pauline would appear, her eyes scanning the room, assessing, directing. The work would continue. The play would take shape. And now, every line, every gesture, would be shadowed by this new knowledge. It was a target. She was the bullseye painted on its forehead.

She had to report this. It was the exact kind of information Cross wanted. The hidden financing, the political backers, the use of the production as a vehicle. It was more valuable than any gossip about who was sleeping with whom, or who grumbled about capitalism after a few beers. This was the machinery. Telling him could protect her. It might even satisfy him enough that he’d ease the pressure, call off the dogs. She could give him Strauss. She could give him the lawyer, Hal. She could say she’d overheard them planning to use her as cover.

Her hand went to her pocket, where she kept the little notebook she used for her reports. The pages were mostly blank, filled with trivialities. She took it out. The pen was cold in her fingers.

She stood there, in the empty room, and tried to form the sentence. Today, I overheard a conversation between Duke Strauss and his legal counsel. The words were dead on the page before she even wrote them. They were Cross’s words. They were the words of the report.

From the street outside, a truck backfired, a loud, flat crack. She flinched, her whole body jerking. The sound was so much like a gunshot. She thought of Cross’s brother, on the floor of some quiet library, hiding from the noise of a peaceful city. There is a cost to dwelling on the dark things.

But the dark thing wasn’t just the play. The dark thing was the system that made the play necessary, and the same system that wanted to silence it. The dark thing was men in rooms trading lives for safety, trading art for currency. She was a token in that exchange. She had been for years.

She closed the notebook. She didn’t put it back in her pocket. She walked to the stacked chairs, pulled one out, and sat down. She placed the notebook on the floor beside her. Then she picked up her script again. She found Eleanor’s monologue. She began to read it aloud, her voice a bare whisper in the vast, cold room.

“The plates are chipped. Did you know? The good set, the one from my mother. I never told him. He liked things to be perfect. So I set them out, every night, and I turned the chipped side toward me. That was my job. To turn the broken part toward myself, so he could see the whole.

Her voice grew stronger, filling the space. It wasn’t a performance. It was an act of memorization. Of absorption. She was learning the lines not as an actress, but as a confederate. If she was the wrapper, then she would learn every fold, every crease. She would become so convincing a part of the package that no one would think to look past her.

She read until the door opened again, and the first of the actors arrived, a young man named Samuel, blowing on his hands. He nodded to her, shy. “Morning, Miss Finch.

“Good morning, Samuel,” she said. Her voice was steady. She smiled, a small, professional thing. She did not look at the notebook on the floor. When she stood to greet the others, she left it there, a small, black rectangle on the dark wood.

The conference room was a study in grey. Grey walls, grey carpet, a long grey table that reflected the flat light from the ceiling panels. Kaelen took the chair opposite the door, her back to the wall. Voss and Silas filed in, their movements a study in contrast: Voss loose-limbed, dropping into a seat; Silas precise, placing a tablet on the table before sitting.

"Report," Voss said. No greeting.

Kaelen slid a data chip across the smooth surface. It stopped midway. "The schematics were a decoy. The real payload was a tracker. It’s dormant now.

"A decoy." Silas picked up the chip, didn’t look at it. "You’re certain.

"The file architecture was wrong. Too clean. A plant.

Voss leaned back, the chair creaking. "And the source?

"Gone. The apartment was scrubbed. Professional job." She kept her hands flat on the table. The tremor was gone, but her knuckles were white.

Silas tapped the tablet screen. A schematic bloomed in the air above the table—a wireframe of the city’s northern grid. "The tracker pinged twice before you killed it. Here. And here." Two red dots pulsed near the river docks. "They know we’re looking.

"They know I’m looking," Kaelen corrected.

"Semantics." Voss stood, paced to the window. The sky outside was the same dull grey as the walls. "They’ve drawn a line. Question is, do we cross it?

Silas shut off the projection. "The directive is observation. Not engagement.

"Directives change." Kaelen’s voice was lower than she intended. The other two looked at her. She didn’t elaborate.

Voss turned from the window. "You left something at the safehouse." It wasn’t a question.

She saw the black rectangle on the dark wood in her mind. "A burner. Compromised.

"Why leave it?"

"A message." She met his gaze. "Let them know we know it’s compromised.

Silas made a short, sharp sound—almost a laugh, but colder. "Amateur psychology. They’ll read it as a retreat. A dropped weapon.

"Or a challenge," Voss said. He studied Kaelen. "You wanted a challenge.

Her jaw tightened. She said nothing.

The rain started as she left the building. A fine, needling mist that beaded on her synth-leather jacket. She walked east, away from the grey block, into the older sectors where the streetlights flickered and the buildings leaned close.

Her thoughts were a closed loop: the decoy, the tracker, the scrubbed apartment. The clean, professional emptiness of it. It felt like a conversation where only one side was speaking.

A figure detached from a doorway ahead—a man in a long coat, collar up. He fell into step beside her, matching her pace.

"You’re hard to find," he said. His voice was gravel wrapped in silk.

"I’m walking." She didn’t slow.

"Your friends in grey don’t approve of walks."

"You’re watching the office."

"I’m watching you." He lit a cigarette, the flare of the match illuminating a face all angles and shadows. "The apartment. Nice work. Took my team six hours to re-print the wallpaper.

So this was the professional. Kaelen stopped, turned to face him. The rain collected in his hair, darkening it. "You left a gift.

"A calling card. You left one, too. I’m curious—was that pride, or stupidity?

"Neither. It was a period. End of sentence.

He inhaled, the cigarette tip glowing. "Your sentence. My paragraph. See the difference?

"I see a man standing in the rain trying to sound clever." She started walking again.

He kept pace. "The tracker was a courtesy. A way to say ‘hello.’ The next one won’t be so polite.

"There won’t be a next one."

"Confident." He tossed the cigarette into a puddle. It hissed out. "Your grey-room boys play a cautious game. You don’t. I like that. It makes you predictable.

They reached a corner. A neon sign for a noodle shop buzzed, painting the wet pavement in sickly green. Kaelen faced him. "What do you want?

"A conversation. Without the grey room.

"We’re having one."

"A better one." He nodded toward the noodle shop. "You look like you haven’t eaten in a day.

She was hungry. The realization came sharp and sudden. She’d forgotten to eat. Again. "I don’t eat with strangers.

"The name’s Holt." He offered a hand. She didn’t take it. He smiled, dropped it. "See? Not strangers anymore.

The shop was empty, stools overturned on the counter except for two. The air smelled of old broth and ammonia. A tired-looking man with a tattoo creeping up his neck wiped a glass, didn’t look at them.

Holt took a stool. Kaelen took the one next to the wall.

"Two specials," Holt said. The man grunted, disappeared into the back.

Silence, broken by the drip of a leaky faucet and the distant thrum of an airship.

"You work for Merrick," Kaelen said.

Holt’s eyebrow twitched. A minor tell. "I work for myself. Merrick is a client.

"The decoy was his idea."

"The decoy was my idea. Merrick pays for results, not artistry.

The cook slid two bowls across the counter. Steam rose, carrying the scent of garlic and ginger. Kaelen didn’t touch hers.

Holt picked up chopsticks, broke them apart. "You’re not eating.

"You’re not talking."

"I’m talking right now."

"You’re circling." She pushed the bowl away. "Why the tracker? If you wanted me found, you could have just asked for this charming meeting.

"I wanted to see what you’d do. You killed it fast. Clean. No panic, no backup call. Just… click." He snapped his fingers. "That told me more than a dozen dossier files. You’re not a team player. You’re a solo act stuck in a chorus.

Her fingers curled around the edge of the counter. The laminate was cool, sticky. "Get to the point.

"I’m offering a job."

"I have a job."

"You have a leash. I’m offering a knife. You’re good with those, I hear." He ate a mouthful of noodles, watched her. "Merrick has a problem. A internal one. Someone’s skimming data, selling it to a third party. He wants it stopped. The grey room would study the problem, write a report, maybe in six months make a move. I want it cut out. Now.

"And you can’t do it?"

"I’m… recognizable within the organization. You’re a ghost. You walk in, find the leak, plug it. Simple.

"Nothing’s simple."

"This is." He put down his chopsticks. "Merrick’s winter gala. Three days from now. Every lieutenant, every tech, every money man under one roof. The leak will be there. You get an invite, you walk in, you identify the problem. Then we handle it.

"We."

"You and me."

Kaelen stared at the oily surface of her soup. A slice of pork floated, pale and congealing. "Why would I betray my side for yours?

"Who said anything about betrayal?" Holt’s voice dropped. "The data being sold? It’s your grey room’s own operation files. Procurement routes. Safehouse locations. Someone on Merrick’s payroll is selling your secrets. You stop them, you protect your own. Your bosses get security, Merrick gets loyalty, I get a bonus. Everybody wins.

Except the leak. She didn’t say it.

The cook turned on a small radio. Static, then a tinny voice singing about lost love.

"I need to think," she said.

"Think fast. The gala is Friday. Invitation expires at midnight." He placed a small, white card on the counter next to her untouched bowl. It was plain, just a date, a time, an address. No name.

"How do you know I won’t just take this to Voss?"

"Because you didn’t tell him about me. You could have, back in the grey room. You didn’t." Holt stood, threw a handful of credit chits on the counter. They scattered like metallic leaves. "You’re bored, Kaelen. And bored people do interesting things.

He walked out, the bell over the door jangling once.

She sat. The radio played. The cook wiped the same spot on the counter over and over.

After five minutes, she picked up the card. The paper was thick, expensive. She slid it into her inner pocket. Then she pulled the bowl of noodles back and ate. They were cold, the fat solidified. She ate every bite.
