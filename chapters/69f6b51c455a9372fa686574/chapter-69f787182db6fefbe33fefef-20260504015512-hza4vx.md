The smoke in Le Chat Noir had a particular density, a blue-grey haze that hung between the tables like a second curtain. It smelled of Gauloises and spilled Pernod and the faint, damp wool of coats left too long on hooks by the door. Iris sat on the stool, her guitar an anchor across her lap, and waited for the last chatter to die. The piano player, a mournful-looking man with a pencil moustache, gave her a nod. The room settled into a low hum, the clink of a glass, a cough.

She didn’t announce the song. She just began.

Her fingers found the opening arpeggio, a simple, descending pattern in A minor. It was a song she’d written on the train from Marseille, watching the vineyards blur past, thinking of nothing in particular. The words came soft at first, a description of light on wet rails. Then the melody turned, a half-step modulation that always caught in her throat. The station empty, the ticket torn… Her voice wasn’t big in this room. It didn’t need to be. It was a thing for close walls, for the space between one breath and the next. She closed her eyes, not for effect, but to feel the vibration in her chest, the truth of the note.

When she finished, the silence was absolute for a three-count. Then the applause came, not the polite patter of the New York clubs, but a solid, warm sound. A few bravos from the back. She opened her eyes, gave a small, genuine smile, and took a sip of the tepid water on the stool beside her.

That was when she saw her.

A woman at a corner table, alone. She wore a man’s tweed jacket over a dark sweater, her hair a close-cropped cap of black curls. She wasn’t clapping. She was just watching, one elbow on the table, a cigarette burning forgotten between her fingers. Her eyes were dark, unblinking, and they held Iris with a focus that felt like a physical touch. It wasn’t admiration. It was assessment. As if Iris were a line of text to be parsed.

Iris looked away first, heat prickling at the back of her neck. She launched into a Gershwin standard to break the spell, something bright and syncopated. But her rhythm was off, a fraction hurried. She could still feel that gaze like a thumbprint between her shoulder blades.

After the set, she collected the few francs tossed into the open guitar case. The piano player murmured, “Belle voix,” and lit another cigarette. As she was packing up, a shadow fell across the case.

“Your bridge is flat.”

Iris looked up. It was the woman from the corner. Up close, she was younger than Iris had thought, maybe twenty-five. Her skin was the rich, warm brown of strong coffee, and there was a small, pale scar through her left eyebrow. She smelled of tobacco and cheap soap and something else, something clean and sharp like ink.

“I’m sorry?”

“The bridge. On your guitar. It’s tilting forward. The angle is wrong. It will ruin the intonation in another month.” Her voice was low, matter-of-fact, with an accent Iris couldn’t place—American, but filtered through something else. French cadences, maybe. Or just impatience.

Iris glanced down at the old Martin. The bridge did look a little off-kilter. She’d never noticed. “Thank you. I’ll have it seen to.”

“You should.” The woman took a final drag from her cigarette and stubbed it out on the sole of her boot. “It’s a crime to mistreat an instrument that honest.” She turned to leave.

“Wait.”

The woman paused, one hand already pushing the door open a crack. The damp night air rushed in.

“Do you play?” Iris asked, and immediately felt foolish.

“No.” A faint, almost imperceptible smile touched the woman’s mouth. “I listen. I have a journal. L’Étincelle. We publish poets, essayists. Sometimes we note musicians who are not full of shit.” She looked Iris up and down, that assessing look again. “You are not full of shit. Mostly.”

Iris laughed, a short, surprised sound. “Mostly.”

“Pauline Carter.” The woman didn’t offer a hand.

“Iris Finch.”

“I know. The bartender told me. The American girl with the sad songs.” Pauline’s eyes flicked to the guitar case. “Do you have another set?”

“Not tonight.”

“Good. Come have a drink. There’s a place around the corner where the wine doesn’t taste of vinegar.” It wasn’t an invitation. It was a directive, delivered with such casual authority that Iris found herself nodding, snapping the case shut, following her out into the Parisian night without another thought.

The place around the corner was a basement bar, all rough stone walls and candles stuck in bottles. It was louder, full of students arguing in rapid French, a knot of Spaniards singing a mournful folk song in a corner. Pauline secured two glasses of murky red wine and led them to a small table wedged under a staircase.

“So,” Pauline said, leaning back, “you write your own material. Why?”

The question was so blunt it threw Iris. “Why not?”

“Most performers here do covers. American jazz, French pop. It’s safer. What you did tonight, that personal excavation… it’s a vulnerability. A strategic error.”

“It’s not a strategy. It’s just what comes out.”

“Exactly.” Pauline took a sip of her wine, grimaced slightly. “That’s what makes it interesting. And dangerous. Art that ‘just comes out’ is the only kind that matters. The rest is decoration.” She set her glass down with a sharp click. “Your song about the train. The second verse. ‘The fields are sleeping, the wires hum / a song that no one else can hear.’ That’s a political line.”

Iris blinked. “It’s about being lonely on a journey.”

“It’s about isolation as a product of infrastructure. The wires hum. Who controls the wires? Who profits from the song no one hears?” Pauline’s gaze was intense, alight with a fervor that was both intimidating and magnetic. “You wrote a song about the alienation of modern travel and didn’t even know it. That’s fascinating.”

“Or maybe you’re reading into it.”

“Everything is read into. The question is, who is doing the reading?” Pauline fished a packet of cigarettes from her jacket, offered one to Iris. She declined. Pauline lit hers with a swift, practiced motion. “Your music has a truth. But it’s a passive truth. It observes sadness. It doesn’t ask why the sadness is there, who built the room for it.”

The conversation was like walking a narrow ledge. Iris felt off-balance, exhilarated. “And your journal? L’Étincelle. The Spark. Does it ask the questions?”

“It tries. We have a patron. A wealthy boy from Lyon who thinks revolution is fashionable. His money pays for the printing press. We use it to publish pieces that would get you arrested in his father’s house.” She blew a stream of smoke toward the low ceiling. “It’s a compromise. But the fire has to start somewhere. Even with someone else’s matches.”

They talked for two hours. Or rather, Pauline held forth on the Spanish Civil War, the Popular Front, the corrosive poison of fascism wearing a respectable suit, and Iris listened, interjecting only to ask a question, to clarify a point. It wasn’t a lecture. It was a sharing of a world, a desperate, urgent mapping of a landscape Iris had only glimpsed from a distance. Pauline’s intelligence was a scalpel, sharp and unforgiving, but her passion was a live wire. It warmed the space between them.

Iris found herself talking, too. About her stifling middle-class childhood in Ohio, the escape to New York, the dead-end gigs, the decision to book a one-way passage on a cargo ship with her last two hundred dollars. She told her about the cramped room in the 5th arrondissement with the window that wouldn’t shut, about the strange peace of being unknown in a city that prized anonymity.

“You’re hiding,” Pauline said, not unkindly.

“I’m breathing.”

“For now.” Pauline stubbed out her third cigarette. “The world is contracting. The walls are coming in. You can’t just breathe forever. Eventually, you have to choose a side to stand against the wall.”

It should have felt like a threat. Instead, it felt like a promise. A terrible, necessary promise.

They left the bar as it was closing. The streets were slick with a recent rain, the cobblestones gleaming under the gas lamps. The smell of wet stone and baking bread from a boulangerie already starting its dawn work filled the air. They walked without a destination, their shoulders occasionally brushing.

Outside Iris’s building, a narrow, shuttered place on Rue du Pot-de-Fer, they stopped. A single light burned in a top-floor window, casting a yellow trapezoid onto the street below.

“This is me,” Iris said, her voice too loud in the quiet.

Pauline nodded. She looked at the building, then at Iris. In the half-light, her features seemed softer, the severe intelligence tempered by something else. A simple curiosity. A fatigue. “Your bridge,” she said again. “I know a luthier. In the Marais. He’s good, and he doesn’t rob artists. I could take you.”

“I’d like that.”

A pause. The distant clatter of a cart. Pauline reached out, not to touch Iris, but to tap the guitar case with one finger. A gentle, almost proprietary gesture. “Goodnight, Iris Finch. Don’t dream in minor keys.”

She turned and walked away, her hands shoved deep in her jacket pockets, her silhouette dissolving into the mist of the alley. Iris stood there long after she was gone, the place where Pauline’s finger had tapped the case humming as if it were a string she’d plucked.

The luthier’s shop was a cave of wood shavings and glue pots. The old man, Monsieur Renard, clucked over the Martin, fixed the bridge in twenty minutes, and refused payment. “For the voice,” he grunted, waving a gnarled hand. “Next time, you bring her back before the neck separates.”

Afterward, they walked along the Seine. It was a cold, bright afternoon. Pauline talked about a play she was writing, a one-act about a factory strike told from the perspective of the machinery. “The machines are the only honest characters,” she said, her breath making little clouds. “They have no illusions about their purpose.”

“That sounds bleak.”

“It’s not bleak. It’s clear-eyed. Sentiment is a luxury. It’s the candy they give you to make you forget you’re in a cage.”

Iris stopped, leaning on the stone parapet, looking at the grey-green water sliding past. “What if the song is the sentiment? What if that’s its purpose? To name the feeling, so someone else knows they’re not alone in it?”

Pauline leaned beside her, close enough that Iris could smell the wool of her jacket. “That’s a beginning. But it’s not an end. If you show a person the lock on their cage and then just sing them a sad song about it, you’ve done half a job. The other half is handing them the file.”

“Maybe the song is the file.”

Pauline looked at her, a long, considering look. A barge honked on the river, a deep, mournful sound. A sudden, irrelevant memory surfaced for Iris: her grandmother’s kitchen in Ohio, the specific pattern of cracks in the yellow linoleum near the icebox, like a map of a foreign country she’d never visit.

“Maybe,” Pauline conceded, and it felt like a victory. A small, hard-won piece of ground. “We should test the theory. Your music. My words. A collaboration.”

The idea landed between them, bright and fragile as a soap bubble. “What kind of collaboration?”

“I don’t know yet. Something that isn’t a song and isn’t a pamphlet. Something that breathes.” Pauline pushed off from the wall. “Come to my flat tonight. I’ll make terrible coffee. We’ll talk. We’ll see what comes out.”

Iris’s heart performed a slow, heavy roll in her chest. Something hot moved through her—not quite excitement, not quite fear, something between the two that tightened her throat. “Alright.”

Pauline’s flat was a single room near Place d’Italie, dominated by a massive oak table buried under piles of paper, books, and ashtrays. A small stove hissed in one corner. There was no decoration, save for a framed poster for the 1936 Popular Front elections and a single, beautiful Japanese print of a bird caught in mid-flight, pinned to the wall with a thumbtack.

The coffee was, as promised, terrible. Bitter and thick as tar. They drank it anyway, sitting on the floor by the stove, their backs against the edge of the table. Pauline read her snippets of the play. Iris played a new melody, haltingly, searching for chords that fit the rhythm of Pauline’s sentences.

It wasn’t smooth. They argued about a line, about a chord change. Pauline’s critiques were merciless. “That’s pretty. Don’t be pretty. Pretty is the enemy.” Iris pushed back. “It’s not pretty, it’s resolved. There’s a difference.” But the friction was creative, a sparking of flint on steel. It felt like work. Real work.

Hours slipped by. The room grew dark. Pauline lit a kerosene lamp, which cast a warm, wobbly circle of light over their papers. The outside world receded—the political tensions, the nagging worry about money, the future. There was only this: the shared concentration, the occasional brush of a hand reaching for the same sheet of music, the quiet, companionable silence when a problem was solved.

Iris looked up from her guitar to find Pauline watching her, the harsh lines of her face softened by the lamplight. The assessment was gone. In its place was something quieter. A recognition.

“What?” Iris asked, her voice barely a whisper.

Pauline didn’t answer. She leaned forward, slowly, giving Iris every chance to pull away. Iris didn’t move. The kiss, when it came, was not soft. It was deliberate. A punctuation mark. It tasted of bitter coffee and cigarettes and a shocking, uncomplicated want.

When they parted, Pauline rested her forehead against Iris’s. Her eyes were closed. “Well,” she said, the word a soft exhalation.

“Well,” Iris echoed.

Nothing else was said. The collaboration had simply, inevitably, changed form. The papers were pushed aside. The guitar was laid carefully on its back. The lamplight guttered, painting long, dancing shadows on the wall, the bird in the print seeming to tremble in its static flight.

Later, lying on the thin pallet by the stove, listening to the steady rhythm of Pauline’s breathing, Iris stared at a water stain on the ceiling. It spread from a corner in a pattern like a branching river delta, or a crack in dried mud. It was just a stain. It meant nothing. But she memorized its shape anyway, a strange, peaceful anchor in the whirlpool of the night. The world outside was vast and threatening, but here, in this room, with this woman’s heat along her spine, the only thing that mattered was the exact texture of the wool blanket against her cheek, and the profound, silent understanding that she had, without meaning to, walked into the very center of the fire.

The brasserie table was sticky with rings of spilled wine. Iris traced one with her thumb, a perfect, greasy circle. The man from the label—his name was Henderson, she’d already forgotten his first name—slid the contract across the sticky wood. The paper was thick, creamy, expensive. It smelled of ink and something else, a faint, sweet odor like a new magazine.

“Standard terms,” Henderson said. He had a Chicago accent, flat and friendly. “Three-year commitment. We handle the arrangements, the studio time, the promotion. You’ll be based in New York, of course. We’ll need a few covers for the first album—Something familiar for the radio—but the material you played last night, that’s the real draw. That’s the heart of it.”

Pauline was silent. She sat beside Iris, a careful inch of space between their shoulders. She’d been smoking the same cigarette for five minutes, the ash a long, precarious cylinder. Her eyes were on Henderson, but they weren’t seeing him. They were fixed on a point somewhere over his left shoulder, where a fly battered itself against the windowpane.

“It’s a tremendous opportunity, Miss Finch,” Henderson said, filling the silence. “We’re looking for authenticity. That postwar sincerity. Your voice has it. A little ache, a little hope. It’s what people want to hear right now.”

Iris’s mouth was dry. She took a sip of the bitter coffee. It tasted of chicory and burnt sugar. “The travel. The arrangements. You’d handle all of it?”

“Every detail. First-class passage. An apartment in Manhattan, at least for the initial period. We have a relationship with a very good vocal coach, if you need one. Though from what I heard, you don’t.” He smiled, a practiced, reassuring gesture that didn’t reach his eyes. They were the color of dishwater. “This is the break artists dream of, Miss Finch. The door is open.”

The fly gave up. It fell to the sill, twitched once, and lay still. Pauline finally moved. She lifted her cigarette to her lips, inhaled, and the ash fell onto the contract, a grey smudge on the clause about royalty percentages. She didn’t apologize. She didn’t brush it away.

“Pauline?” Iris’s voice came out small.

“You should take it.” Pauline’s tone was flat, analytical. She might have been discussing the weather. “The terms are adequate. The exposure is significant. It is a logical step for a commercial performer.”

Henderson’s smile tightened at the edges. “We prefer the term ‘recording artist.’”

“Do you.” Pauline stubbed out her cigarette in the saucer. It made a tiny, violent hiss. “Iris is a songwriter. Will she retain publishing rights?”

“That’s negotiable. We have a team of excellent—”

“It is not negotiable. It is a prerequisite.” Pauline stood up. The legs of her chair screeched against the tile floor. “If you will excuse me. I have a meeting with a printer.” She looked at Iris, and for a fraction of a second, the flatness cracked. Something moved behind her eyes—a flash of pure, unguarded panic, there and then gone, swallowed by a slow, deliberate blink. “We’ll discuss it tonight.”

Then she was walking away, her boots sharp on the floor, her tweed jacket a dark shape swallowed by the gloom of the doorway.

Henderson watched her go. “Friend of yours?”

“Yes.”

“She’s… protective.” He chuckled, a dry sound. “You’ll want people like that in your corner. But you’ll also want people who know the business.” He tapped the contract. “Think it over. Not too long. The offer is warm right now. These things can cool down.”

He left her with the bill and the ashy contract.

The room above the printer’s shop was cold. The little stove had gone out. Iris stood in the center of the floor, still in her coat, the contract folded in her pocket. It was too dark to see the water stain on the ceiling. The only light came from the streetlamp outside, casting a sickly yellow parallelogram across the floorboards, missing the pallet entirely.

Pauline was at the small desk, her back to the room. She was writing, her pen scratching in the quiet. She didn’t turn around.

“You didn’t come to the set,” Iris said.

No answer. The scratching continued.

“I told him I needed a day. To think.”

Pauline’s hand paused. Then the scratching resumed, faster now.

A hot, prickling sensation started at the base of Iris’s skull. It wasn’t anger. It was something thinner, more brittle—the feeling of a note held too long, the point where beauty frayed into strain. “Are you going to say anything? Or are you just going to write your damn editorial?”

Pauline put the pen down. Very carefully. She didn’t turn. “What would you like me to say, Iris? Congratulations? It is what you came here for, isn’t it? To be discovered. To be packaged and sold.”

“That’s not fair.”

“Fair.” Pauline laughed, a short, hollow sound. “Fair is a concept for children. This is commerce. You have a product. They wish to buy it. The transaction is simple. My opinion is irrelevant.”

“It’s not irrelevant to me.”

Finally, Pauline swiveled in her chair. Her face was in shadow, the streetlight catching only the sharp line of her jaw. “What do you want me to tell you? To follow your dream? This—” she gestured at the room, the cold stove, the stack of poorly printed journals—“this is not a dream. It is work. It is a choice. You made a different one. There is nothing more to discuss.”

“I haven’t made any choice yet.”

“You have. The moment you sat down with that man, you made it. The only question now is whether you are honest enough to admit it to yourself.” Pauline stood up. She walked to the window, looking down at the wet, empty street. “You think I am angry. I am not angry. I am… tired. I am tired of watching talented people mistake a cage for a stage.”

“It doesn’t have to be a cage. I could do both. I could make the records, have the security, and still write what I want. I could send money. For the journal. For you.”

The moment the words left her mouth, she knew they were wrong. They hung in the cold air, clumsy and insulting.

Pauline went very still. When she spoke, her voice was so quiet Iris had to strain to hear it. “I do not need your money, Iris. I need…” She stopped. Shook her head once, a sharp, frustrated motion. “No. That is sentimental. And incorrect. I do not need anything from you. Your path is yours. Take it.”

“That’s it? After everything?”

“What did you expect? Tears? A scene? I am a pragmatist. You are an opportunity. I have always known you would leave.” She turned from the window. Her expression was smooth, closed, a door shut and locked. “The train to Calais leaves at seven-fifteen tomorrow morning. I suggest you be on it. Contracts have a tendency to evaporate.”

She walked past Iris, toward the door. She didn’t touch her. She didn’t look at her.

“Pauline.”

Pause. Hand on the doorknob.

“Is that really all?”

A long silence. The distant sound of a motorbike backfiring two streets over. Pauline’s shoulders rose and fell with a breath that seemed to cost her something. “No,” she said, so softly it was almost a whisper. “It is not all. But it is all I can afford to say.”

She opened the door and left. The click of the latch was precise, final.

Iris didn’t move. She stood in the yellow light, listening to the echo of her own heartbeat in her ears. The room smelled of cold ink and the ghost of their last meal—onions, cheap wine. A button from Pauline’s shirt lay on the floor by the desk, a small, white disc. It must have come off days ago. Iris hadn’t noticed.

She packed her few things in the grey light of dawn. The guitar, two dresses, a notebook half-full of lyrics that suddenly seemed juvenile. She left the wool blanket folded neatly on the pallet. She left the button on the floor.

The station at Gare du Nord was a cathedral of noise and rushing air. Steam from the engines coiled under the high glass roof, smelling of coal and damp wool and anxious humanity. Iris clutched her ticket in one hand, her guitar case in the other. The case felt heavier than it ever had.

She had looked for her. A foolish, desperate scan of the crowded platform, though she knew, she knew, Pauline wouldn’t come. The absence was a physical space beside her, a shape of cold air. People jostled past, laughing, crying, shouting farewells in a dozen languages. A child dropped a red balloon; it soared up into the smoky rafters and was gone.

The porter called for boarding. Her train hissed, a great, weary exhalation.

As she turned toward the third-class carriage, a flash of tweed caught her eye. Across the platform, leaning against a pillar, was Pauline. She wasn’t looking at Iris. She was reading a newspaper, her face impassive. But she was there. She had come.

Iris’s breath caught. She took a step forward, raising a hand.

Pauline turned a page. She didn’t look up. It was a dismissal so complete, so utterly deliberate, that it stole the air from Iris’s lungs. This was the punishment. Not a fight, not a scream. This: to be seen, and then deliberately unseen. To be made a ghost while still standing in the living world.

The final whistle blew, sharp and imperative.

Iris climbed the steps into the carriage. The compartment smelled of stale tobacco and old velvet. She found a window seat, her guitar case propped between her knees. The train gave a lurch, then began to move, slowly at first, the platform sliding past.

Pauline was still there. As Iris’s window drew level with the pillar, Pauline finally looked up. She met Iris’s gaze across the steam and the distance. Her expression didn’t change. No smile, no frown, no wave. She just looked. Then she folded her newspaper, tucked it under her arm, and turned away, walking into the crowd without a backward glance.

The platform vanished. The city began to unspool—soot-blackened buildings, washing lines, a church spire, all blurring into a grey smear. Iris pressed her forehead against the cold glass. Her reflection stared back, pale and indistinct. Something was building in her chest, a pressure with no name. It wasn’t grief, not yet. It was the silence before the note, the terrible, empty moment when you know the sound you are about to make will change everything, and you make it anyway.

She opened the guitar case. Nestled in the velvet beside the Martin was a single sheet of paper, folded small. She hadn’t put it there.

Her hands shook as she unfolded it. It was a page from Pauline’s journal, the mimeograph ink smudged in one corner. A poem. No title.

You speak of fire as if it is only warmth,
forgetting it is also the thing that clears the field. What grows after is never what was burned. Remember that. When the ash settles, and you are planting
in the strange, quiet soil of your new life,
remember what you chose to set alight.

No signature. Just the words, and the faint, clean smell of ink.

Iris read it once. Then again. The pressure in her chest tightened, sharpened into a single, clean point of agony. She folded the paper along its original creases, her movements precise, ritualistic. She slipped it into the pocket of her coat, next to the contract.

The train gathered speed, carrying her north, toward the coast, toward the ship, toward the safe, bright future on the other side of the ocean. She watched France disappear, field by grey field. The only sound was the rhythmic clatter of the wheels on the tracks, a monotonous, endless phrase that sounded like you chose, you chose, you chose.

And she had.
