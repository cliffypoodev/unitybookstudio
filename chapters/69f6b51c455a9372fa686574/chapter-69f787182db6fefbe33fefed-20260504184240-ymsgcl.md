The light over the small stage was the color of weak tea, a dusty amber that pooled around her feet and did nothing for the complexion. Iris stood in its center, a familiar spot worn smooth by a decade of lesser performers. The microphone stand was cool under her palm, a steadiness she didn’t need but accepted anyway. Beyond the footlights, the Blue Parrot’s dining room stretched back into a gloom punctuated by the occasional red tip of a cigarette and the pale oval of a face turned up in polite expectation. Half-empty was generous. A third, perhaps. The tables were islands of white linen in a dark sea, most of them bare.

She began without introduction. The piano player, a young man named Marty who smelled always of hair tonic and nervous sweat, played the opening bars of ‘The Last Goodbye’. The notes were correct, technically. But they were brittle. They had no memory in them.

Her voice, when it came, was not the instrument it had been. It was a thing of careful management now. She navigated around the tender spot on her left cord, a small, persistent betrayal deep in her throat. The high note in the bridge—the last good night, the long, long light—she took an octave down, a smoky descent that felt like surrender. A few of the pale ovals nodded. They remembered the song, if not the woman singing it. It had been everywhere in ’43. In USO halls, on the radio between news bulletins, pressed into shellac that soldiers carried in their kits. A photograph from that time came to her, unbidden: a stack of records on a factory floor, their labels a bright, cheerful blue. She had signed one for a girl with ink-stained fingers. What was her name? The memory dissolved, leaving only the taste of the club’s bad coffee, thick and bitter on the back of her tongue.

She finished. The applause was a soft, brief patter, like rain starting on a roof and then thinking better of it. She offered the room a slight, professional dip of her head, the same one she’d given a crowd of two thousand at the Paramount. The muscles in her neck performed the motion on their own. Her left shoulder ached, a dull throb from standing still under the lights. She’d pulled something reaching for a suitcase last week, a stupid, domestic injury.

Offstage, the air was cooler and smelled of old carpet and gravy from the kitchen. Langston was waiting in the narrow corridor by the service door, a silhouette against the EXIT sign. He held her coat. His face was a study in pleasant concern, the mask he wore for business.

“Lovely, darling,” he said. The word ‘darling’ had no weight anymore. It was a placeholder, like a rest in a measure of music. “A very appreciative house.

She slid her arms into the coat. The lining was silk, chilly against her skin. “Appreciative of the steak Diane, maybe. I was the sorbet between courses.

“Now, Iris.” His tone was mild, chiding. He straightened the collar of her coat with a fussy little tug. His fingers were dry and warm. “A gig’s a gig. It keeps your name in the ledger.

The ledger. His ledger, a vast, leather-bound thing on his office desk where her life was reduced to columns of figures. She said nothing. They moved through the kitchen, past a sweating dishwasher who didn’t look up, and out into the alley. The February night hit her face, a sharp, sooty cold. Above the buildings, the sky was a dull purple, the color of a bruise healing. A trash can lid clattered somewhere, a solitary, hollow sound.

Their car, a black DeSoto, waited at the curb. Langston held the passenger door for her. The gesture was automatic, a relic of a different kind of performance. She got in. The interior held the faint, sweet smell of his pipe tobacco and something else, something chemical and new—his aftershave, probably. He’d started using a different kind.

He drove with a quiet competence, his eyes on the wet streets. The wipers made a soft, rhythmic protest. Thump-squeak. She traced the rim of the window with her thumb, feeling the cool vibration of the glass. They passed a newsstand, its headlines screaming about spies and loyalty oaths in bold, black type. The words blurred into a smear of ink.

“There’s a gentleman who wants to meet you,” Langston said. His voice was casual, the way it was when he was about to deliver bad news wrapped in good opportunity.

“A gentleman.”

“From Washington. A Mr. Cross. He’s with the Committee.

She didn’t need to ask which committee. The word hung in the close air of the car, taking up all the space. The wipers kept their time. Thump-squeak.

“What does he want?”

“To talk. He’s a fan of your work. Wartime contributions and all that.” Langston’s knuckles were white on the steering wheel. He relaxed them, one finger at a time. “He’s at the hotel. I said we’d stop by on the way home. A courtesy.

“At ten-thirty on a Tuesday.”

“He’s a busy man.”

The hotel was one of the older ones near Grand Central, all marble and brass and tired grandeur. The lobby was a vast, hushed space. Their footsteps on the checkerboard floor echoed up into the shadowy heights of the ceiling. A clerk with a pinched face nodded them toward the elevators. Langston pressed the button for the fourth floor. The elevator cage shuddered, then rose with a sigh.

Room 407. Langston knocked, a firm, polite rap.

The door opened almost immediately. The man who stood there was younger than she’d expected. Mid-thirties, with a neat, forgettable face and hair the color of sand. He wore a grey suit, perfectly pressed, and a tie with a small, tight knot. He looked like an accountant. His eyes were a pale, watery blue.

“Mr. Finch. Mrs.” His voice was pleasant, mid-Atlantic, without accent. “Please, come in. I appreciate you making the time.

The room was a standard suite. A sitting area with a sofa and two armchairs, a low table between them. A bottle of Scotch and three glasses sat on the table, untouched. The curtains were drawn. The only light came from a single lamp with a fringed shade, casting a warm, intimate circle that felt entirely false. The air was still and slightly warm, smelling of furniture polish and starch.

“Can I offer you a drink?” Cross asked, gesturing to the bottle.

“No, thank you,” Langston said, answering for them both. He sat on the edge of one armchair. Iris took the other. The upholstery was scratchy under her palms.

Cross sat on the sofa, leaning forward slightly. He clasped his hands together. They were clean, with short, trimmed nails. “First, let me say, it’s a genuine pleasure. ‘The Last Goodbye’ was a favorite of my mother’s. She played it constantly during the war. A real comfort.

Iris nodded. She could feel her own pulse in her throat, a quick, light flutter. She kept her hands still in her lap.

“The Committee,” Cross began, his tone shifting into something smoother, more official, “is engaged in a vital national project. We’re looking into certain… atmospheres. In places where ideas are exchanged. The arts, particularly. We believe artists have a special responsibility to the health of the country.

He paused, as if waiting for agreement. Langston gave a slow, grave nod.

“The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts has come to our attention. A fascinating place. Very… energetic. A lot of new work being developed there. Work with a point of view.” Cross picked up a file folder from the table beside him. He didn’t open it. He just held it. “We’d like a better sense of that atmosphere. The people. The conversations. The themes of these new plays.

Iris’s mouth was very dry. She found her voice. It came out quieter than she intended. “I don’t know the institute.

“But you could,” Cross said. He smiled. It was a small, polite smile that didn’t reach his eyes. “We understand they’re looking for guest instructors. Someone with your professional pedigree, your experience… it would be a natural fit. A community service, really. Helping to guide the next generation.

The flutter in her throat was a hard, cold knot now. She looked at Langston. His face was a careful blank, but a muscle in his jaw ticked once, twice.

“I’m a singer,” she said.

“You’re a performer,” Cross corrected gently. “Storytelling is storytelling. And from what I understand, you’ve been looking for a new… direction. Something to reinvigorate your career.

Langston cleared his throat. “What Mr. Cross is suggesting, Iris, is an opportunity. A chance to give back. And to be seen giving back. In the current climate, that kind of goodwill is… invaluable.

“The current climate,” Iris repeated. The words felt thick.

Cross set the folder down. He leaned back, spreading his hands. “Let’s speak plainly. The Committee has a mandate. We follow leads. Sometimes those leads involve personal matters. Moral matters.” His pale eyes fixed on hers. “We have a file. It contains certain letters. Written to you. From Paris. 1937.

The room seemed to tilt, very slightly. The fringes on the lamp shade blurred. She remembered the stationery. Thick, cream-colored paper. Pauline’s handwriting, bold and sloping, the ink sometimes bleeding where she’d pressed too hard. The smell of Gauloises and cheap perfume on the pages. She had kept them. A foolish, sentimental act. She thought she’d burned them all before she married Langston.

“I don’t know what you’re talking about,” she said. The lie was automatic, flimsy.

“I think you do.” Cross’s voice was still pleasant. “The content is, shall we say, unambiguous. In the wrong hands, such correspondence could be misconstrued. It could be seen as evidence of a certain… propensity. A moral turpitude that would make you unfit for public life. For any life, really. The Lavender Scare isn’t just a headline, Mrs. Finch. It’s a reality. For people like you.

The word ‘lavender’ hung in the air, absurd and deadly. A color. A scent. Now a sentence.

Langston shifted forward. “Surely, there’s a compromise to be made here. My wife is a patriot. She sang for the troops. She raised funds.

“Exactly,” Cross said, turning his polite smile on Langston. “Which is why this isn’t an accusation. It’s an invitation to collaborate. To help us understand a potentially subversive environment. In return, the file remains closed. Your contributions are celebrated, not scrutinized. It’s a simple exchange of perspectives.

“An informant,” Iris said. The word was ugly in the warm, quiet room.

“A consultant,” Cross said. “A cultural liaison. You attend. You observe. You teach, if you like. You send me a weekly report on the general mood, the topics of discussion. Nothing that would betray any confidences. Just… the atmosphere.

“And if I refuse?”

The silence that followed was absolute. The distant hum of the city through the window seemed to cease. Cross’s expression did not change, but something in his eyes went flat and still, like the surface of a pond on a windless day.

“Then the file becomes part of the public record,” he said, each word precise and clean as a scalpel. “Your recording contracts will be voided. You will be unemployable in any venue that holds a liquor license. Your husband’s management company will be associated with a moral degenerate. You will be a name in the papers, Mrs. Finch. And not the kind of name you’re used to being.

Langston’s hand came down on her arm, a firm, warning pressure. “Iris. Think.

She was thinking. She was thinking of the cream-colored paper. She was thinking of the way Pauline laughed, her head thrown back, a sound like breaking glass. She was thinking of the half-empty supper club, the weak tea light, the ache in her shoulder. This was the price of that silence. This was the interest on the cowardice she’d buried for twelve years.

“What would the reports entail?” Langston asked, his voice taking on a businesslike tone. He was negotiating. For her. For them.

“General observations,” Cross said, spreading his hands again. “The political leanings of the leadership. The themes of the plays they choose to produce. The guest speakers they invite. Names, if they’re volunteered in casual conversation. We’re not asking for secret handshakes, Mr. Finch.

“And this would satisfy the Committee’s concerns regarding my wife’s past?”

“It would demonstrate her current loyalty. The past would remain where it belongs. In the past.

Langston looked at her. His eyes were pleading, but beneath that was a hard, practical urgency. This was the deal. The only deal. He gave a slight, almost imperceptible nod.

The cold knot in her throat was a solid thing now, a stone she would have to carry. She looked at Cross. His bland, patient face. The folder on the table beside him, so innocuous. It contained the most precious and dangerous part of her life, reduced to evidence.

“When would I start?” she heard herself ask. Her voice was a stranger’s.

Cross’s smile widened, just a fraction. It was still a polite smile. “The institute’s spring term begins in two weeks. I’ll have my office send the details to Mr. Finch. And Mrs. Finch?” He paused, his pale eyes holding hers. “Thank you for your cooperation. Your country appreciates it.

They didn’t speak in the elevator. They didn’t speak in the car. Langston drove, his profile stern in the dashboard glow. The city slid by, a stream of light and shadow. Iris watched her own reflection in the window, a pale ghost overlaid on the passing dark. She traced the rim of the glass, around and around, until her thumb went numb.

When they reached their apartment building, Langston turned off the engine. The silence expanded, filled the car.

“It’s the only way,” he said, not looking at her. “You see that, don’t you? It’s protection.

“For who?” she asked. The question was quiet.

He turned to her then, his face earnest in the dim light. “For us. For everything we’ve built. This is just… a formality. You go, you listen, you write a few bland paragraphs. And we move on. We’re safe.

Safe. The word echoed in the close space. She thought of the folder. Of Pauline’s handwriting. Of the institute, a place she’d never seen, now a trap she was walking into with her eyes open. A stage of a different kind.

She opened the car door. The cold air was a shock. She walked toward the building, her heels clicking on the pavement in a steady, hollow rhythm. Langston followed, a step behind. The lobby was empty. The elevator took them up to the eighth floor. Their apartment was dark and still, smelling of lemon wax and yesterday’s flowers.

She went to the sideboard and poured a glass of gin. She didn’t measure it. The liquid was clear and cold. She didn’t drink it. She just held the glass, feeling the chill seep into her palm.

Langston stood in the doorway of the living room, watching her. “It will be alright, Iris.

She didn’t answer. She was looking at a crack in the plaster of the far wall, a hairline thing that started at the ceiling and zigzagged down toward the floor. She’d never noticed it before. It looked like a tiny, meandering river on a map of nowhere.

She lifted the glass finally and took a sip. The gin burned, a clean, medicinal fire. It didn’t warm her. Nothing would. She was already somewhere else, standing in a room she’d never seen, waiting for a door to open onto a past she had thought was buried. The silence in the apartment was not peaceful. It was the silence of a held breath, of a wire pulled taut, waiting for the first note to sound and begin the long, inevitable fall.

The frayed thread on the carpet was still there the next afternoon, a pale worm against the burgundy. Iris stared at it from the same chair, the gin glass empty in her hand. Langston had gone out—to the office, he’d said, to make calls. To smooth things. The apartment held his absence like a held note. She’d spent the morning not packing, not deciding, just moving from room to room, picking up objects and putting them down. A porcelain dove from the mantel. Its wing was chipped, a flaw she’d never noticed. She’d bought it in a market in Nice, a lifetime ago, when the sun felt like a different kind of heat.

The doorbell rang.

It was a blunt, electric sound, alien in the quiet. She didn’t move. It rang again, longer this time. She set the dove down, careful, and walked to the foyer. Through the peephole, the world was a fishbowl curve. A man in a grey hat stood on the landing, his face a pale, distorted oval.

She opened the door.

Phillip Cross stood there, hat in hand. He wore a different suit, a charcoal herringbone, but the same expression of polite purpose. He didn’t smile. “Mrs. Finch. I apologize for calling unannounced.

“Mr. Cross.” Her voice worked. That was something. “My husband isn’t here.

“It’s you I’ve come to see. May I?

He was already inside, a subtle shift of weight, before she could answer. He placed his hat on the hall table beside a bowl of keys. He glanced at the living room archway. “A lovely apartment. Quiet.

“It’s a Tuesday.” She closed the door. The click was final. “Would you like a drink?

“No, thank you.” He moved into the living room, not waiting for an invitation. He stood by the window, looking down at the street. His hands clasped behind his back. “I thought our conversation last night might have been… abstract. The setting was wrong. Hotels lack context.

Iris remained in the archway. She folded her arms. The silk of her dressing gown was cool against her skin. “It seemed concrete enough.

“The principle, yes. The mechanics, no.” He turned from the window. The afternoon light cut across his face, highlighting the fine lines around his eyes. He looked tired. “You have questions. Practical ones.

“I have a question. What happens if I say no?

Cross considered this. He walked to the bookshelf, his eyes scanning the titles without seeing them. A clock ticked on the mantelpiece, a steady, cheap sound. “Then the Committee proceeds with its inquiry. Your name is entered into the record. Subpoenas are issued—for you, for your associates from Paris, for anyone who might clarify the nature of those relationships. The press receives certain documents. Your radio contract is voided. Your existing records are pulled from circulation. Your husband’s agency is audited by the IRS. It’s not punitive,” he said, turning back to her. His tone was explanatory, almost gentle. “It’s procedural. A matter of establishing a complete picture.

A complete picture. She saw it: the photographs, the letters, pulled into the light of a hearing room. Pauline’s name in a transcript. Her own voice on the wireless, replaced by static.

“And if I say yes?”

“Then you help us contain a problem before it grows. You take a position at the Harlem Institute. You observe. You report on the atmosphere, the conversations, the political content of the work. You note the names of those who are particularly… fervent. In return, your file remains closed. Your career continues. Your private life remains private.” He took a step toward her. “It’s a compromise, Mrs. Finch. A way to serve your country and protect what you’ve built.

“What I’ve built.” The words tasted like ash.

“This.” He gestured vaguely at the room, the dove on the mantel, the quiet. “A life. A reputation. You’re a wartime icon. That still means something to a great many people. We’d prefer to preserve that.

We. The word hummed in the air. She thought of Langston’s white knuckles on the steering wheel. His new aftershave. The way he’d said a gig’s a gig.

“How would it work?” she asked. Her own voice sounded distant, someone else’s.

Cross reached into his inner jacket pocket. He withdrew a small, leather-bound notebook and a silver pen. He laid them on the coffee table. “You’ll be offered a position as a vocal coach. A courtesy to a respected artist. You’ll attend rehearsals, meetings. You listen. You remember. Once a week, you meet me. You tell me what you’ve seen and heard. I write it down. That’s all.

“That’s all.”

“Your reports are confidential. They inform our understanding. They may prevent others from being drawn into a dangerous ideological space.” He paused. “It’s protective work, in its way.

Iris looked at the notebook. The leather was a rich, oily brown, well-used. There was a small, dark stain on one corner. Coffee, maybe. The pen lay beside it, sleek and cold. Instruments. She imagined his hands writing in it, the quick, efficient script.

“And Pauline Carter?” The name felt dangerous in her mouth, a lit match.

Cross’s expression didn’t change. “A person of interest. Your prior connection is… operationally useful. It provides a natural point of access. Your assessment of her current mindset, her intentions, would be particularly valuable.

Assessment. She saw Pauline’s face, not as it was now, but as it had been: fierce with argument in a café, softened by lamplight on a pillow. A mind like a razor. A heart she had chosen to leave behind for a stack of blue-labeled records.

“She won’t want me there.”

“Then that will be your first piece of intelligence,” Cross said. He picked up his hat from the hall table. The visit was over. “Her resistance. What it reveals.” He stopped at the door, his hand on the knob. “Talk to your husband. He understands the realities. He wants what’s best for you.

“Does he.”

It wasn’t a question. Cross offered a thin, brief smile that didn’t reach his eyes. “The position starts Monday. Nine a.m. The institute is on 135th Street. A converted church. You can’t miss it.

He left. The door sighed shut behind him.

Iris stood in the silent foyer. From the street below, a car horn blared, a short, angry sound. She walked back to the coffee table and looked down at the notebook. She didn’t touch it. He’d forgotten it. Or left it on purpose. A promise. A threat.

She went to the kitchen. The gin bottle was on the counter. She poured two fingers into a water glass, not measuring. The first swallow was a shock, a clarity. She carried the glass to the window. The sky was the color of dishwater, a uniform, hopeless grey. On the building across the way, a fire escape ladder was hanging loose, one hinge broken. It swayed slightly in the wind, tapping against the brick with a faint, irregular click. Tap. Tap-tap.

She thought of the silence in the Blue Parrot, the polite, empty applause. She thought of a rehearsal room she’d never seen, a door opening. She thought of Pauline’s hands, how they moved when she talked, precise and sure.

The phone rang in the hall.

She let it ring. Three times. Four. On the fifth, she set her glass down and went to answer it.

“Iris?” Langston’s voice, tinny on the line. “I just got off the horn with Duke Strauss. You remember Duke? He’s got a ear to the ground. He thinks this institute thing could be a good look. A return to your roots, artistic integrity, all that. Very respectable.” He was talking too fast. “Cross called me. He said he stopped by. To clarify.

“He clarified.”

A pause on the line. She could hear him breathing, the faint crackle of the connection. “It’s the only way, darling. You see that, don’t you? It’s just talking. Observing. It’s not like you’re wearing a wire.

She didn’t answer. She watched the loose fire escape ladder tap against the brick.

“Iris?”

“I see it,” she said.

“Good. That’s good.” Relief flooded his voice. “We’ll make it work. It might even be good for you. Get you out of the apartment. A new challenge.

“A new challenge.”

“I’ll be home by seven. We’ll go to Luigi’s. Celebrate.” He hung up.

She placed the receiver back in its cradle. The plastic was warm. She returned to the living room and stood over the notebook. After a long moment, she picked it up. It was heavier than it looked. She opened it. The pages were blank, a creamy, unlined expanse. The paper smelled faintly of tobacco and something else, something chemical—the ink from a mimeograph machine.

On the inside front cover, in small, neat script, was written: For your thoughts. – P.C.

Her breath caught. Not Cross’s handwriting. Pauline’s. She’d know it anywhere—the angular P, the graceful, looping C. She snapped the book shut, her heart a sudden, frantic drum against her ribs. How? When?

Then she understood. It wasn’t a message. It was a sample. Cross had shown her a page from her file. He had her letters. He had this. He was showing her the depth of the well, the specific, intimate shape of the things he could pull into the light.

She dropped the notebook on the table as if it had burned her. The pen clattered to the floor.

The room was very still. The clock ticked. The fire escape tapped. Tap. Tap-tap.

She walked to the phonograph. Her last album was there, still in its sleeve. She didn’t put it on. She just stood there, her hand on the dusty lid, staring at the pattern of the inlay—a stylized bird, wings spread, caught in a circle of gilt. A canary. She traced the edge of the circle with her thumb, around and around, until the skin felt hot and thin.

Outside, the grey afternoon deepened towards dusk. The tapping stopped. The wind had changed.
