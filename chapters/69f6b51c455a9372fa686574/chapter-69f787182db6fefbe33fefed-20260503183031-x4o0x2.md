The stage lights at The Blue Parrot were a particular kind of yellow, the color of old teeth. Iris stood in the center of that jaundiced halo, her hands loose at her sides. The applause that greeted her introduction was a polite patter, the sound of people being careful not to spill their drinks. She waited for it to subside, a professional pause she could measure in heartbeats. The cigarette smoke hung in layers, a gauzy scrim between her and the half-seen shapes of the audience. It was a Tuesday. Tuesday audiences were always like this: nostalgic, but only up to a point.

She nodded to Teddy at the piano. His hands came down on the opening chords of “The Last Goodbye,” the notes too bright in the quiet room. She drew a breath that tasted of gin and dust, and began.

The voice that came out wasn’t hers. Not the one she remembered. It was thinner, a half-step lower, with a new, unwelcome tremor on the sustained notes. She followed it like a stranger, adjusting the placement in her mouth, leaning away from the old belting style that now scraped like gravel in her throat. She thought of vocal cords, of the doctor’s cold speculum and his colder diagnosis: nodules. She thought of the crack in the ceiling of her dressing room upstairs, a jagged line that forked like a lightning bolt just above the mirror. She’d been staring at it for seven years.

We said we’d meet again, beneath a kinder sky…

The words were automatic. Her mind drifted to the composition of the room itself. The clink of a glass from the bar was a sharp, off-beat percussion. A woman’s laugh from a shadowed booth was a flatted third. The hiss of the espresso machine was a sustained pedal tone. She arranged these sounds into a score in her head, a counterpoint to the tired melody. It was how she got through it. She didn’t feel the ache of the song anymore, the young, stupid hope of it. She felt only the mechanics: breath support, vowel shaping, the slight tilt of the chin to project. Performance was a series of corrected mistakes.

She finished. The silence held for a beat too long before the applause resumed, the same careful patter. A few couples got up to leave, their chairs scraping the floor. She offered the room a small, closed-lipped smile, the one Langston said suggested dignified mystery, and walked off. The steps from the parquet to the worn carpet of the wings felt like a mile.

Her dressing room was a closet that smelled of other people’s powder and old sweat. She sat at the mirror, not looking at her reflection, and traced the rim of a water glass with her thumb. The skin made a faint, squeaking sound. Outside the door, she could hear the murmur of the departing crowd, the sound of money being counted at the register. A good night, by 1949 standards. They hadn’t thrown anything.

Langston came in without knocking. He carried the air of the club with him—cigars, spilled whiskey—and something else, a tightness around his eyes that had nothing to do with the take. He leaned against the doorframe, blocking the light.

“They liked you, darling.”

“They remembered me,” she said. “It’s not the same thing.

He ignored this, moving into the room. He took up space in a way that was both familiar and oppressive. “A gentleman was asking after you. From Washington. A Mr. Cross.

The name meant nothing. A fan, maybe. A reporter from one of the trade papers doing a ‘where are they now’ piece. “Send him my regrets. I’m tired.

“It’s not a request, Iris.” Langston’s voice was low, a new register. He adjusted his cufflink, a precise, gold thing she’d bought him in a better year. “He’s with the House Committee. The Un-American Activities bunch.

The air in the little room went very still. The squeak of her thumb on the glass stopped. She saw, with perfect clarity, a coffee stain on the knee of Langston’s trousers, a dark brown blotch shaped like Italy.

“What does he want with me?”

“A chat. That’s all he said. A friendly chat.” Langston wouldn’t meet her eyes in the mirror. He was studying his own reflection, checking his tie. “He’s in the back booth. The one in the corner.

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It settled as a cold knot just under her ribs. She stood, her performer’s posture locking into place automatically. “And if I don’t want a friendly chat?

“Iris.” He finally looked at her, his expression a practiced blend of concern and condescension. “Let’s not be difficult. It’s just a conversation. I’ll be right there with you.

He said it like a promise. It felt like a sentence.

The club was mostly empty now. The busboy was clearing glasses, the clatter too loud in the hollow space. In the far booth, a man sat alone. He wasn’t what she’d expected. No grey fedora, no trench coat. He wore a neat, brown suit, and he was reading a newspaper folded to the racing forms. He looked like an accountant. As they approached, he glanced up and smiled. It was a pleasant, empty smile.

“Mrs. Finch. A pleasure. Phillip Cross.” He didn’t offer his hand. He gestured to the seat opposite. “Please.

She slid in, Langston settling beside her too close, a wall of warm wool and anxiety. Cross folded his paper away. His eyes were a pale, watery blue. He had the kind of face that was impossible to describe five minutes after he left a room.

“Enjoyed the show,” he said. “A classic. ‘The Last Goodbye.’ Seems appropriate for the times, in a way. A lot of goodbyes being said.

“I just sing it, Mr. Cross. I didn’t write it.

“Of course.” He took a sip of what looked like club soda. “Your husband tells me you’re between engagements at the moment.

“I’m selective.”

“Admirable.” He placed his glass down exactly in the center of a coaster. “The Committee is interested in the cultural health of the nation, Mrs. Finch. We like to keep a pulse on institutions that shape public thought. The theatre, for instance.

She waited. Her mouth had gone dry.

“There’s a place in Harlem,” Cross continued, his tone conversational. “The Institute of Dramatic Arts. Run by a Pauline Carter. You’ve heard of it?

The name was a physical blow, a jolt that traveled up her spine and locked her jaw. She felt Langston’s leg press against hers under the table, a warning. She managed a slight shrug. “Vaguely. A community theatre.

“It’s a bit more than that. Miss Carter has… interesting ideas. About art, about society. The Committee is curious about the atmosphere there. The sort of conversations that happen. The influences.” He paused, letting the words hang. “We’d like you to go there. Take a position. Teaching voice, perhaps. You’d be a perfect fit.

“I’m a singer, not a teacher.”

“You’d be an observer,” Cross corrected gently. “A concerned citizen contributing to the national security. We’d appreciate regular reports. Nothing formal. Just your impressions.

The cold knot under her ribs tightened. “And if I have no impressions to share?

Cross’s pleasant smile didn’t waver. He reached into his inner breast pocket and withdrew a small, leather-bound notebook. He didn’t open it. He simply laid it on the table between them, his fingertips resting on the cover. “We have a file, Mrs. Finch. Everyone has a file these days. Yours has some… gaps. The war years were complex for entertainers. So much travel. So many acquaintances. Paris, 1937, for example. A formative time, I understand.

The lights seemed to dim. The clatter of dishes from the kitchen became a distant, tinny echo. She could smell the lemon oil the busboy used on the tables, a scent suddenly cloying and sickly sweet.

“I was recording. For Liberty Records.

“Of course you were.” Cross tapped the notebook. “But associations can be misconstrued. Moral turpitude is such a broad term, isn’t it? It can cover a multitude of private sins. The Lavender Scare, the papers are calling it. Unfortunate phrasing, but evocative.

Langston leaned forward, his voice a low, soothing murmur. “Now, see here, Mr. Cross. My wife is a patriot. She’s been investigated before, cleared completely. This is harassment.

“This is an opportunity, Mr. Finch.” Cross’s eyes never left Iris. “A chance to clarify the record. Permanently. A few months of your time, a few discreet conversations with me. In return, the file is sealed. Your broadcasting license stays valid. Your name stays clean.” He leaned back, spreading his hands. “Or, you can refuse. And we proceed with a formal inquiry into your activities and associations, both past and present. The choice is, as they say, yours.

There was no choice. They all knew it. The booth felt like it was shrinking, the red vinyl sticky against the backs of her thighs. She looked at Langston. His face was a mask of sympathetic distress, but his eyes were already calculating, moving past the threat to the negotiation.

“There must be a compromise,” Langston said, playing his part. “A teaching position, as you say. But my wife is an artist, not a spy. Reports are out of the question. She observes, she teaches. That’s the limit.

Cross pretended to consider this. He took another sip of his soda. “Very well. A trial period. We’ll call it… cultural outreach. You start next Monday. I’ll be in touch.

He stood, picking up his newspaper. He left the notebook on the table. A prop, she realized. Probably empty. He nodded to them both, that same empty smile in place, and walked out of the club. The door sighed shut behind him.

The silence he left was enormous. Langston let out a long breath. “It’s for the best, darling. Really. A few months in Harlem, keep your head down, and it’s over. They’ll lose interest.

Iris didn’t answer. She was staring at the notebook. It was cheap leather, cracked along the spine. She reached out and turned it over. The pages were blank.

The booth was deep in shadow, the leather worn to a dull shine. Mr. Cross folded his newspaper and laid it aside with a neatness that felt deliberate. He didn’t stand. He simply looked at her, the pleasant smile not wavering.

“Miss Finch. Thank you for coming. Please, sit.

His voice was mild, mid-range, with a slight Midwestern flattening of the vowels. He gestured to the seat opposite. Langston hovered at her elbow, a hand on her arm guiding her down. She sat. The leather was cold.

“My husband says you’re from Washington,” she said. Her voice, the one she used for these situations, was a careful blend of curiosity and detachment. It was the voice she used for producers who promised nothing.

“That’s right. Phillip Cross.” He didn’t offer a title. “I enjoyed your performance. ‘The Last Goodbye.’ A lovely song. Very poignant for the times.

“It was popular during the war.”

“Yes. A certain… sentiment. A longing for a better world.” He took a cigarette from a silver case, tapped it twice on the table. “Sentiment is a powerful thing. It can move people. It can also, in certain contexts, be misinterpreted.

Langston cleared his throat. “Mr. Cross is here in an official capacity, Iris. He’s conducting a review of certain artistic institutions. A sort of… cultural survey.

Cross lit his cigarette. The flame of the match was a brief, orange flower in the dimness. He blew out the smoke in a thin stream. “Your husband is being diplomatic. The Committee has concerns about subversive elements infiltrating our cultural life. Theater, particularly. It’s a potent medium for messaging.

Iris traced the rim of her water glass again. The squeak was gone; her thumb was dry. “I’m a singer. Not an actress.

“But you have connections to the theatrical world. From your time in Paris, I believe. Before the war.

The air in the booth thickened. The coffee stain on Langston’s knee was still Italy. She focused on its southern coastline, the boot shape.

“Paris was a long time ago.”

“1937,” Cross said. He didn’t consult notes. “You were part of a rather vibrant expatriate community. Artists, writers, thinkers. Some of whom held views that were, let’s say, ahead of their time. Or behind ours.

She remembered the smell of rain on the cobbles outside the café where they’d all met. It had nothing to do with this.

“I was there to work,” she said. “I had a contract.

“With Monsieur Renault’s label. Yes.” Cross smiled again. It was the same smile. “You also collaborated with a playwright. Pauline Carter. An American, like yourself. But of a different… persuasion.

The name landed in the silence between them. It didn’t echo. It just sat there, a stone on the table. Langston shifted beside her, a rustle of fabric.

“We worked on a few songs for a review,” Iris said. Her throat was tight. “It was a professional arrangement.

“Of course.” Cross took a slow drag from his cigarette. “Miss Carter has since returned to New York. She runs an institute in Harlem. The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. You’re aware of it?

“I’ve heard the name.”

“It’s become something of a gathering place. For certain types of artists. The Committee is interested in its atmosphere. The ideas being exchanged there.” He leaned forward, just slightly. The pleasantness didn’t leave his face, but his eyes held hers. They were a pale, watery grey, like the sky outside. “We’d like you to help us understand that atmosphere.

“I don’t work there. I don’t know anyone there.

“You could.” Langston’s voice was soft, urgent. “They’re looking for instructors. Voice coaching, performance. I’ve made some inquiries. It could be a position for you. A respectable one.

Something cold settled in her stomach, a hard, definite weight. It wasn’t fear anymore. It was comprehension. “You want me to go there. To watch.

“To participate,” Cross corrected. “To contribute your talents. And to report back, in a friendly, informal way, on the general tenor of the place. The conversations. The themes of the work being developed. Nothing too burdensome. A monthly chat.

“And if I don’t?”

Cross’s smile finally faded. It didn’t vanish; it just relaxed into a neutral, patient expression. He took another drag, let the smoke curl up toward the ceiling. “The Committee has a broad mandate. We look into many things. Loyalty. Patriotism. Moral character.” He paused. “Moral turpitude is a particular concern in the entertainment industry. The influence it has on the public. We’ve found that sometimes, personal histories from overseas can shed light on present associations.

He wasn’t looking at her now. He was looking at Langston. A quiet, shared understanding passed between them, a current in the still air. Langston’s hand, still on her arm, tightened.

“It wouldn’t be an accusation, Iris,” Langston said quickly. “Just a… a review. Of your past associations. It could become public. The papers love these stories. It would be very difficult for your career. For both of our careers.

“My career,” she said, and the words came out flat, drained of all tone. “Is singing in empty clubs on Tuesday nights.

“It could be nothing,” Cross said. He folded his hands on the table. They were clean, nails trimmed. “Or it could be a lot. It’s a matter of perspective. We’re offering you a chance to provide a useful service to your country. And to avoid an unpleasant… perspective.

The busboy passed by their booth, carrying a tray of empty glasses. The clatter was like broken bells. Iris watched him go, the white apron tied crookedly at his back. She thought of the crack in her dressing room ceiling, the forked lightning bolt she’d stared at for seven years. This was just another crack, splitting open in a different direction.

“What exactly would I have to do?”

“Take the position Langston has arranged. Attend the meetings, the rehearsals. Listen. Then meet with me, perhaps once a month, and share your impressions. In confidence.” He shrugged, a small, economical gesture. “It’s really a form of civic engagement. Many artists are eager to assist.

She knew then, with a clarity that was almost physical, that Langston had already agreed. He had brokered this. The meeting, the offer, the threat—it was all a settled transaction, and she was the item being moved from one column to another. The cold weight in her stomach spread, a slow seep into her limbs.

“And my past? Paris?

“Would remain a private matter. A closed chapter.” Cross stood. He was not a tall man, but his posture was erect, filling the space. “I think we understand each other. I’ll leave you to discuss the details with your husband.” He picked up his newspaper, gave her that pleasant smile again. “Thank you for your time, Miss Finch. I look forward to our future conversations.

He walked away, his footsteps silent on the carpet. He didn’t look back.

Langston waited until the door at the far end of the club closed behind him. Then he let out a breath, a long, shaky sigh. He turned to her, his face a mask of relief. “It’s the best outcome, darling. Really. A teaching job. It’s respectable. And you’ll be… protected.

“Protected.” She looked at him. His eyes were bright, eager. He saw a solution, a path forward. He didn’t see the crack.

“From any scrutiny. They’ll leave you alone. They’ll leave us alone.

“And Pauline?”

He blinked. The name, spoken aloud by her, seemed to surprise him. “She’s just… part of the environment. You don’t have to engage with her personally. Just observe. Report on the general… themes.

“You know she’s there. You knew when you made this arrangement.

“I knew the institute was a potential concern. Her presence there is incidental.” He reached for her hand, but she didn’t give it. “This isn’t about her, Iris. It’s about you. About us. Keeping our lives intact.

Our lives. She looked at the booth, the empty space where Cross had sat. The ashtray held his single cigarette butt, neatly crushed. Our lives were now a monthly chat in a shadowy booth, a report on themes, a closed chapter that could be opened at any time. The composition of it was simple, a minor key with no resolution.

She stood. Her legs held her. “I need to go home.

“Of course.” He rose with her, a united front. “We’ll talk more there. It’s all going to be fine.

She walked out of the club ahead of him, through the empty tables, past the bar where the night’s receipts were being stacked. The street outside was cold, the February wind cutting through her coat. Langston’s car was waiting, a dark shape at the curb. He opened the door for her, a gentlemanly gesture from another time.

She got in. The leather seat was cold, just like the booth. She stared at the windshield, at the faint reflection of the streetlights in the glass. Langston got in beside her, started the engine. The heater would take a minute to warm up.

He drove. She watched the city pass by, the blocks of darkened windows, the occasional glow of a late-night diner. She thought of nothing. She let the cold seep into her bones, let the weight in her stomach anchor her to the seat. She was going home. She was going to take a teaching position at the Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. She was going to report on its atmosphere. She was going to see Pauline Carter again.

The car turned onto their street. The apartment building was a grey bulk against the sky. Langston parked. He turned to her, his face soft now, pleading. “It’s for us, Iris. You understand that?

She nodded. It was a performer’s nod, the kind you gave an audience when you had no more songs to sing. She got out of the car, walked to the door, waited for him to unlock it. The hallway inside was quiet, the carpet a dull green. She went up the stairs, her heels making no sound.

In the apartment, she went to the bedroom. She didn’t turn on the light. She stood by the window, looking out at the same skyline she’d looked at for fifteen years. The cold weight was still there. It had a name now. It was the future, already arrived, sitting inside her like a stone.

The apartment on West End Avenue held the day’s chill deep in its bones. Iris shut the door and the silence of the place rushed at her, a dense, woolen thing. Langston went straight to the sideboard, the clink of crystal against glass a familiar punctuation to their homecomings. She didn’t take off her coat. She stood in the foyer, listening to the radiator knock. A single drop of water hung from the tarnished fixture above the hall table, catching the weak light. It trembled but didn’t fall.

He brought her a drink. Gin, neat, in the heavy cut-glass tumbler she hated. She took it. Her thumb found the rim, began its circuit.

“It’s the best possible outcome,” Langston said. He’d already finished half of his. “A position. Respectable. It gets you out of those clubs.

“It gets me into their club.” Her voice sounded flat, tinny to her own ears. The knot under her ribs hadn’t loosened. It had simply become part of her geography.

“Don’t be dramatic. It’s observation. Atmosphere. You’re an artist going to an artists’ institute. You’ll fit right in.” He said it like he was explaining a train schedule. “You go, you teach a little, you come home. You tell Mr. Cross about the…the mood of the place. The topics of conversation. No names need be named if the atmosphere is sufficiently patriotic.

She thought of the racing form left on the booth table. The neat checkmarks next to losing horses. A man who kept his own failures close, a private record. “And if the atmosphere isn’t?

“Then you note the exceptions.” Langston wouldn’t look at her. He studied the amber in his glass. “It’s a formality, Iris. A box to tick so they leave us alone. So we can move on.

We. The word was a splinter. She turned and walked down the hall to the bedroom, the drink heavy in her hand. The room was dark, the bed a vast, unmade plain. She set the glass on the dresser without tasting it. Her fingers found the small, velvet-covered box in the top drawer without her having to look. The hinge gave a soft sigh.

Inside, on a bed of faded silk, lay the Zippo. The chrome was scratched, the emblem on the side worn to a vague ghost of a bird in flight. Paris. 1937. A damp cellar bar and the smell of Gauloises and cheap wine. Pauline’s laugh, a low, rich contralto that seemed to vibrate in the stone walls. It’s a phoenix, Canary. Don’t you know anything? She’d won it in a bet with a Swiss journalist. She’d pressed it into Iris’s palm at the Gare du Nord, her fingers warm, her eyes already distant. For the cold nights.

Iris had never put fluid in it. She carried the emptiness.

The memory was a physical ache, a specific hollow beneath her sternum. It had nothing to do with Committees or institutes or moral turpitude. It was older, simpler. A girl choosing a record contract over a revolution, a future over a feeling. She’d thought it was the grown-up thing. The professional choice. She’d spent twelve years learning the price of that kind of professionalism.

From the living room, the radio clicked on. A swell of strings, some inane commercial jingle for soap. The cheerful noise was an obscenity. She closed the box, the click final.

In the bathroom, she turned on the tap. The pipes groaned. She studied her face in the mirror—the fine lines at her eyes, the tired set of her mouth. A performer’s face, trained to suggest emotion rather than feel it. The face that had smiled for cameras as her records shipped off to boys on troop ships. She thought of the crack in the ceiling at The Blue Parrot, that forked lightning bolt. A flaw in the plaster, unchanging for seven years. She’d measured her decline against it.

She poured the gin from the tumbler down the drain. The astringent smell rose, sharp and medicinal. She filled the glass with water, drank it all. The taste was of pipes and chlorine.

Back in the bedroom, she changed out of the stage dress, the silk whispering as it slid to the floor. She put on a worn wool robe. The fabric smelled of camphor and her own skin. She stood at the window, looking down at the slice of street visible between the buildings. A taxi sped past, its yellow a brief smear in the gloom. A man walked a small, fussy dog. Life, ordinary and unconcerned.

Langston appeared in the doorway, a silhouette. “You should get some sleep. We’ll go over the details in the morning. Strauss knows the director there. He’ll make the introduction smooth.

“The director,” Iris said, the word dry in her mouth.

“Pauline Carter. Quite the rising star, in certain circles. Wrote that play about the sharecroppers’ union. Had a little trouble with it in Boston, I hear.” He said it like a piece of gossip, a bit of shop talk.

The name landed like a stone in still water. The ripples moved out, slow, inevitable, touching every part of her. Pauline. Here. Not a ghost in a Parisian station, but a woman in Harlem, running an institute. The coincidence was too perfect, too cruel. It wasn’t a coincidence. Cross had known. The file folder on the booth table, the calm, pleasant smile. We understand you have a history with certain artistic circles. He hadn’t meant the clubs.

The cold knot under her ribs dissolved into a freefall. Her stomach clenched. Her mouth was dust.

“Iris?” Langston’s voice came from far away. “Did you hear me?

She heard the hiss of the radiator, the drip from the hall fixture finally hitting the wood below with a tiny, definitive tap. She saw the way the streetlight bled a watery halo on the wet pavement. She saw Pauline’s hands, long-fingered, gesturing with a cigarette as she diagrammed a scene on a napkin.

“Yes,” she said. The word was automatic, a note held on a fading breath. “Pauline Carter. I’ve heard of her.

She turned from the window. The room seemed smaller, the air thinner. She had the distinct, absurd thought that she should water the philodendron on the dresser. Its leaves were edged in brown.

“Good. Then you have something to talk about.” Langston sounded relieved. The deal was settling, becoming real. “It’s just a job, darling. A few months. Then we can put all this unpleasantness behind us.

He came forward, meaning to kiss her forehead. She shifted, pretending to adjust her robe, and his lips brushed her hair. He smelled of club smoke and the cloying sweetness of his whiskey. He patted her shoulder, a dry, soundless touch, and left, pulling the door closed behind him.

Iris didn’t move. She stood in the center of the dark room, the robe cinched tight around her. The past wasn’t behind her. It was waiting for her in a converted church on 135th Street, with a script in its hand and a perfect, unforgiving memory. The performance of her life hadn’t been on any stage. It had been the last twelve years. And the curtain was finally coming down.
