The rehearsal hall smelled of dust and old varnish, a scent that always reminded Iris of an instrument case left too long in an attic. Sunlight fell in long, lazy columns through the high, grimy windows, catching the dust motes in a slow, swirling dance. She watched them from her chair by the piano, her fingers tracing the rim of a cold coffee cup. The cup had a hairline crack near the handle, a thin brown line she followed over and over with her thumb. It was a Tuesday. Tuesdays had a certain acoustic quality, a flatness. She’d noticed that years ago.

Pauline stood at the far end of the room, her back to the light. She was examining a page of the script, her pencil tapping against the paper in a rhythm that wasn’t quite jazz, wasn’t quite march time. Something in between. Something unsettled.

The main doors swung open with a theatrical groan.

Duke Strauss didn’t so much enter as occupy. He filled the doorway, then the space around it, a large man in a suit the color of expensive oatmeal. He carried a hat and a smile, both broad and confident.

“Miss Carter!” His voice was a rich baritone, polished for projection. It bounced off the wooden floors. “And the legendary Canary Finch. What a pleasure. This place has character. I can feel it in the bones.

He moved forward, hand extended. Pauline looked at his hand, then at his face. She didn’t take it. The pencil kept tapping. A staccato dot-dot-dot on the paper.

“Mr. Strauss.” Pauline’s voice was dry as the dust in the air. “To what do we owe the intrusion?

“Intrusion? Never.” He clasped his hands, undeterred. His gaze swept the room, the battered chairs, the faded backdrop of a painted forest. He was cataloguing, Iris realized. Assessing square footage, potential. “I was in the neighborhood. Langston mentioned you were workshopping something new. Something with… heft. I’m always interested in heft. If it can be made to sing.

Iris’s stomach tightened. She set the cup down. The crack caught the light.

“Our work tends to speak, not sing,” Pauline said.

“All the better! A spoken-word aria. The possibilities.” Strauss pulled a chair around and sat, straddling it backwards like a man at a campfire. He focused on Iris. “Your husband tells me you’ve found a new calling. From chanteuse to thespian. I admire versatility. The public loves a comeback story. Especially now. Especially after the war. We’re all looking for… renewal.

He said ‘renewal’ as if it were a product he was selling. Iris’s mouth felt dry. She remembered the taste of the coffee, bitter and thin.

“It’s a workshop,” Iris said. Her voice came out quieter than she intended. “Just an exercise.

“Exercises build muscle. Muscle fills seats.” Strauss leaned forward, his eyes bright. “Here’s what I see. A powerful drama. This Port Chicago tragedy—a terrible thing, a national shame, but a story that needs air. It’s timely. It’s got gravity. And you, Iris, at the heart of it. The audience remembers you. They remember ‘The Last Goodbye.’ They remember the voice.” He paused, letting the ghost of her old applause hang in the silence. “Now imagine the finale. The truth is told, the curtains start to close… then a single spotlight finds you. Downstage. The band strikes up—just a simple piano, a clarinet maybe. And you sing. Not a new song. One of the old ones. ‘The Last Goodbye.’ But now, in that context, it’s not just a love song. It’s an elegy. For the lost boys. For the cost of silence. The audience… they’ll weep. They’ll stand. They’ll understand.

The room was very still. The dust motes swirled in their sunlit column, oblivious.

Pauline’s pencil stopped tapping.

“You want to put a cabaret review at the end of a play about three hundred and twenty murdered Black sailors.”

“I want to give it resonance,” Strauss corrected, his smile never wavering. “I want to bridge the gap. Art for the sake of message, that’s a lecture. Art that marries message to memory, to a melody they already carry in their hearts? That’s a phenomenon. That’s a conversation.

“That is a desecration.”

The words were flat, cold, final. Strauss’s smile tightened at the edges. He turned his hands palm-up, a gesture of benevolent reason. “Miss Carter, I admire your purity. Truly. But purity doesn’t pay the light bill. It doesn’t get a second printing in the Times. This play, as is, will play to a hundred people a night who already agree with you. What’s the point of preaching to the choir?

“The point,” Pauline said, “is that the choir knows the hymn. The point is to sing it correctly, so we remember the words. Not to set it to a foxtrot.

Iris watched a fly bump against the windowpane. A faint, persistent buzz. She could feel the two of them pulling at the fabric of the room, of the play, of her. Strauss’s vision was a gilded cage, familiar and safe, its bars made of ticket stubs and familiar chords. Pauline’s was a bare, cold platform under a naked bulb—terrifying and true. Her throat ached, not with nodules, but with a old, familiar silence.

“Iris understands the bridge,” Strauss said, his eyes on her again. Appealing, now. Man-to-artist. “She’s lived in both worlds. The popular and the… profound. She knows an audience needs a hand to hold as they walk into the dark.

Did she? She thought of her reports to Cross, the sterile sentences. A different kind of bridge. A traitor’s footpath.

“The play is the play,” Iris heard herself say. The words felt inadequate, pebbles dropped into a deep well.

“Of course it is!” Strauss stood, clapping his hands once. The sound was like a gunshot in the quiet hall. “And we would not change a word. Just… add a coda. A moment of catharsis. Think about it. That’s all I ask.” He placed his hat back on his head, adjusting the brim with a practiced tug. “The money I’ve facilitated for this workshop… it comes with a hope, not a demand. A hope that the art reaches as many eyes and ears as possible. That’s all any patron wants.

He nodded to Pauline, who did not nod back. He gave Iris a warmer, more conspiratorial smile. “Talk to Langston. He gets it.

And then he was gone, leaving the smell of bay rum cologne and ambition hanging in the air where the dust motes danced.

The fly found a corner of the window and was still.

Pauline didn’t look at Iris. She looked at the space where Strauss had been. “A coda,” she said, the word a dry leaf crumbling. “He doesn’t want a bridge. He wants a drain. To siphon the poison out and replace it with syrup.” She finally turned. Her eyes were dark, unreadable pools in the shadowed planes of her face. “That’s the first pressure. The commercial one. It comes wearing a smile and talking about your heart. Remember the taste of it.

Iris licked her lips. She could still taste the bad coffee. Under it, something metallic. Like a penny on the tongue. She nodded, a short, stiff motion. She didn’t trust her voice.

Pauline’s pencil began to tap again. A new rhythm. Slow, relentless. Like a heartbeat counting down.

The silence in the rehearsal hall after Duke Strauss left had a physical texture—thick, resistant, like the air after a slammed door. Iris’s coffee cup was empty. The crack in the glaze was a tiny brown river on a white plain. She followed it with her thumb again, a pointless pilgrimage.

Pauline hadn’t moved from her spot by the window. The sun had shifted, cutting across her shoulders, leaving her face in shadow. The pencil was still in her hand, but it was quiet now.

“My office,” Pauline said. The words weren’t an invitation. They were a verdict.

Iris stood. Her knees felt stiff, as if she’d been sitting for hours. She followed Pauline’s retreating back down a short, dim corridor lined with framed playbills. The Emperor Jones. Mamba’s Daughters. The glass was dusty. A corner of one frame had come loose, tilting the poster at a slight, permanent angle.

Pauline’s office was a closet of paper. Manuscripts teetered in stacks on a battered desk, filled a bookshelf, colonized the single spare chair. The smell was immediate: mimeograph ink, the sweet-rot scent of old newsprint, and underneath it all, the faint, familiar ghost of Gauloises. Iris’s stomach did a slow, unpleasant turn. She leaned against the doorframe. There was no room to enter.

Pauline went to the window behind the desk—a small, grimy square looking out onto a brick wall—and lit a cigarette. She didn’t offer one. The match flared, died. She exhaled a thin stream of smoke that climbed toward the water-stained ceiling.

“He knew the details,” Pauline said, her back still turned. “The Port Chicago play. The structure. The number of sailors. He knew you were in it.

“Langston talks.”

“Langston sells.” Pauline turned. Her eyes in the grey light were flat, like stones at the bottom of a creek. “He sold you to the record company in ’37. He sold your voice to the war bond tours. Now he’s selling your… rehabilitation. To a man like Strauss. It’s what he does. He finds the price of things.

The words were precise, surgical. They left no blood, only a cold, spreading numbness. Iris looked at a stack of scripts. The top one had a coffee ring on the cover, a perfect brown halo.

“It’s not that simple,” she said, but the protest was weak, automatic. A line from an old script.

“It is exactly that simple.” Pauline tapped ash into a clay dish overflowing with butts. “Strauss didn’t come here to appreciate the art. He came to assess the asset. You. And the play is just the packaging. A serious, political wrapper for a sentimental comeback. He’ll wrap it in a flag, too, if he thinks it’ll move tickets. And your husband will help him do it.

Something hot moved through Iris’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It tightened her throat. “You think I want that?

“I think you’re standing in my doorway waiting for someone to tell you what to do.” Pauline’s voice was quiet, . “You always have. Paris was my vision. The songs were Langston’s. Even this… this informant farce. That’s Cross’s script. When was the last time you chose a direction and walked toward it, Iris? Without someone handing you the map?

The memory arrived, unbidden and specific: the smell of wet wool and diesel on the Gare du Nord platform, 1937. Pauline’s hand letting go of hers. Not a dramatic pull away, just a slow, deliberate release. The train whistle blowing. Iris had chosen the direction of the waiting train car, the contract in her valise, Langston’s impatient smile. She had walked toward it. That was a choice.

“You left first,” Iris heard herself say. The words were out before she could catch them, small and petulant. A child’s grievance, twelve years stale.

Pauline went very still. For a long moment, the only sound was the distant, rhythmic thump of a rehearsal from another room—someone practicing a dance step. Then she gave a short, sharp exhale that wasn’t quite a laugh. “Yes. I left the party. I left the scene. I went back to a country that was on fire in a different way. You left me. You signed the paper. You got on the train. There is a difference.

The dance steps stopped. A sudden silence flooded in, thick and heavy.

Iris’s mouth was dry. She focused on the brick wall outside the window, the pattern of the mortar. One line was crooked. “He’s my husband.

“He’s your manager. The marriage license was a piece of administrative flair. A convenience. You told me that yourself.” Pauline stubbed out the cigarette with more force than necessary. “Don’t hide behind it now. It’s beneath you.

The flush this time was pure shame, hot and prickling across her neck. She looked away, at the tilted playbill in the hall. Mamba’s Daughters. 1939. She’d never seen it.

“Strauss is a vulture,” Pauline said, her tone shifting back to business, cold and clear. “But he’s a useful gauge of pressure. His interest means the play is making noise in the wrong circles. Or the right ones. It means we’re on schedule.” She opened a desk drawer, rummaged, and pulled out a thin manila folder. She held it out. “Your next scene. The monologue for the mother of the witness. It’s new.

Iris took the folder. The paper was crisp, fresh from the typewriter. She didn’t open it. “Cross is meeting me tonight. For a report.

“I know.”

“He asked about our funding. Last time. He mentioned a bank.

Pauline’s gaze sharpened. “Which bank?

“The… I don’t remember. Hamilton Trust?

A flicker in Pauline’s eyes. Something closed off. She turned back to the window. “Tell him it’s a private donor who wishes to remain anonymous. A grieving family member. It’s plausible. It’s what he’d want to hear.

“Is it true?”

“The truth is a luxury.” Pauline’s reflection in the dirty glass was blurred, ghostly. “Give him the plausible answer. It’s what you’re good at.

The dismissal was clear. Iris clutched the folder. The edge was sharp against her finger. She took a breath, the air still thick with smoke and memory. “Pauline.

“What.”

“The finale he described. With the song. It would be a betrayal.

Finally, Pauline looked at her. Not at her reflection, but directly. Her face was tired. “Yes.

“I wouldn’t do it.”

“You wouldn’t mean to.” Pauline’s voice softened, just for a second. It was worse than her anger. “But they’ll ask. And they’ll have a reason. A good one. For your safety. For the institute’s survival. For the greater good of getting the story seen. And you’ll stand in a doorway, holding a script someone else wrote, and you’ll try to find the note you’re supposed to hit.” She shook her head, a small, weary motion. “Go learn your lines, Iris. The real ones.

Iris stood there a moment longer, in the space between the office and the hall. Then she turned. She walked back down the corridor, past the crooked playbill, the sound of her own footsteps the only thing in her ears. The new pages in her hand felt like a verdict, too. Or a test. She couldn’t tell the difference anymore.

The automat’s fluorescent lights hummed at a pitch that made the fillings in Iris’s teeth ache. She sat in a booth by the window, the vinyl seat cracked and sticky. Outside, Times Square bled its neon promise into the dusk, a smear of garish color against the grey sidewalk. The coffee in her chipped porcelain cup had gone cold and oily. She’d been there twenty minutes, watching the door.

He entered without haste. A man in a grey suit, hat in hand, moving through the nearly empty room like a clerk at the end of a long day. He slid into the booth opposite her. He didn’t order anything.

“Miss Finch.”

“Agent Cross.”

He placed his hat on the table between them, aligning it parallel to the edge. His hands were clean, nails trimmed. He had the look of a man who slept exactly six hours a night and never dreamed.

“You’ve been busy,” he said. His voice was flat, without accusation. A simple statement of observed fact.

“Rehearsals.”

“Yes. For The Port Chicago Incident. A working title, I understand. Ambitious subject matter.

The cold in her stomach wasn’t fear. It was something heavier, a lump of lead settling low in her gut. He knew the title. Of course he knew.

“Exercises often reveal character.” He rested his forearms on the table. His eyes were the color of a winter sky through dirty glass. “Tell me about the structure. The scenes.

She traced the rim of her cup. The crack snagged against her thumb. “It’s in development. Scenes are fluid.

“The third scene. The one where the character based on the Navy lawyer confronts the character based on the surviving petty officer. That’s the pivot, isn’t it? Where the cover-up becomes personal.

The air left the room. The hiss of the coffee urn behind the counter grew deafening. He hadn’t read a report. He’d read the script. He’d studied it.

“You have a copy,” she said. Her own voice sounded distant.

“The Committee takes an interest in works that address matters of national security. Especially those based on recent, classified events.” He paused. “The dialogue in that scene is quite specific. The lawyer’s line: ‘The truth is a weapon we cannot afford to fire.’ A powerful sentiment. Is that Miss Carter’s writing? Or did you contribute?

She remembered the afternoon they’d workshopped that line. Pauline pacing, cigarette trailing a blue ribbon in the dusty light. It’s not clever enough, it’s bureaucrat-speak, it needs to taste like ash in the mouth. Iris had suggested the rhythm, the hard stop on ‘fire.’ Pauline had nodded, once, and scribbled it down.

“It’s collaborative,” Iris managed.

“Collaboration requires trust.” Cross’s gaze didn’t waver. “Do you trust Miss Carter?

The question hung in the air between them, next to his hat. A fly, the same stubborn kind from the rehearsal hall, buzzed against the window beside them. A tiny, frantic percussion.

“She’s the director.”

“That’s not what I asked.” He waited. When she didn’t answer, he gave a small, almost imperceptible sigh, as if disappointed by a minor error in a ledger. “She holds meetings after hours. In her office. With certain members of the cast. The young man who plays the petty officer. The woman reading the mother of a deceased sailor. Are these artistic discussions?

“I wouldn’t know. I’m not invited.

“But you’re the lead. The conduit. The famous face.” He said it without inflection. “It would be logical to include you in strategic conversations. Unless the strategy is of a different nature.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: the smell of printer’s ink in that Paris garret, the way it clung to their clothes for days. She could smell it now, a phantom stain in the automat’s air of boiled cabbage and disinfectant.

“What do you want me to do?” The words were ash in her mouth.

“The names of everyone who attends those private meetings. The subjects discussed. Any mention of external contacts. Patrons.” He leaned forward an inch. The fluorescent light caught the fine lines at the corners of his eyes. “My brother served in the Pacific. He was at Leyte. He never spoke about it. Not a word. When he came home, he’d sit in his chair and watch the wall. For hours.” Cross blinked, as if surprised by his own digression. He straightened his cuffs. “What happened at Port Chicago was a tragedy. A failure of command. But airing that failure now, in this climate… it isn’t patriotism. It’s provocation. Miss Carter knows that. Which means her purpose is provocation. I need to know who else shares that purpose.

Iris looked past him, at the long wall of little glass doors where pies and sandwiches sat under dull light. A slice of coconut cream had a single, perfect fingerprint on the glass. Someone’s hunger, paused.

“You asked if I trust her,” Iris said, her eyes on that fingerprint. “I don’t. But that doesn’t mean I think she’s wrong.

It was a dangerous thing to say. She felt the danger like a draft on the back of her neck.

Cross didn’t react. He picked up his hat. He stood. “Your next report is due Friday. Be specific.” He started to turn, then stopped. He looked down at her, his expression unreadable. “That line. ‘The truth is a weapon we cannot afford to fire.’ It’s a good line. It has weight. But it’s incorrect. The truth is a weapon that fires whether you can afford it or not. The only choice is who it hits.

He walked out, leaving her alone with the cold coffee and the buzzing fly. She sat there until the neon outside bled fully into the darkness, until the night shift waitress began wiping down the distant counters with a grey rag. The fingerprint on the pie glass remained. A tiny, smudged monument to a want that had passed by.
