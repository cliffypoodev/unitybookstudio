The smoke in Le Chat Noir had a particular density, a blue-grey haze that hung between the ceiling and the heads of the crowd like a second, low sky. It smelled of Gauloises and spilled wine and the damp wool of coats left to steam by the small, inadequate stove. Iris’s fingers ached. The strings of her guitar were cheap, the wire biting into her skin, but the pain was a clean, sharp thing. It kept her here, in this cellar on the Rue de la Huchette, and not in the tidy, silent room her scholarship money paid for across the river.

She finished the phrase, a descending run of notes that felt like walking downstairs in the dark, and let the last chord vibrate into the murmur. A smattering of applause, the sound of a few dozen hands preoccupied with glasses and conversation. She didn’t mind. Applause here was an afterthought. The listening was the thing. The way the room would sometimes still, a collective held breath in the middle of a verse. That was worth more than any roar from a packed house.

She adjusted the microphone, a clumsy metal blossom on a bent stem. “That was something by Django,” she said, her voice a low scrape in the speakers. “Or near enough.”

A laugh from a table near the front. She took a sip of tepid beer from the bottle beside her stool. The label was peeling. She picked at it, a mindless action, her gaze drifting over the crowd. Students with patched jackets. A couple of older men playing chess in the corner, the pieces clicking like distant gunfire. A woman alone at a small table against the wall, a notebook open, a pen held loosely in her left hand.

Iris looked away, then back.

The woman was watching her. Not with the vague appreciation of the other patrons, but with a fixed, analytical attention. Her skin was the color of strong coffee, her hair cut close to her skull in a cap of tight curls. She wore a man’s white shirt, the sleeves rolled to her elbows, and a dark vest. She didn’t smile. She just looked, her eyes catching the dim light from the candle on her table.

Iris felt a flush creep up her neck. She busied herself with tuning the guitar, though it didn’t need it. When she glanced up again, the woman was writing, her head bent over the page. The moment had passed. Or it hadn’t happened at all.

She started another song, one of her own. A foolish thing to do here, maybe. The room preferred interpretations, the safety of a known name. But the ache in her fingers had spread to her chest, a hollow, yearning sensation that only her own chords seemed to fit. The words were simple, maybe too simple. She could feel the chess players’ concentration break for a second, could sense the drift of attention in the room. But she kept her eyes on the fretboard, on the calluses forming on her fingertips.

When she finished, the silence was different. Not the silence of inattention, but a quiet that felt full, waiting. She risked a look.

A peculiar drop, then. A disappointment so sharp it surprised her. She began packing her guitar into its case, the leather worn soft at the edges. The club owner, Marcel, a man with a permanent cigarette glued to his lower lip, came over and counted a few francs into her palm. “The American songs,” he grunted. “More of those next time, eh? They like the Gershwin.”

She nodded, not trusting herself to speak. The francs were cold and thin. She tucked them into the pocket of her dress.

As she turned to leave, a voice spoke from the shadowed archway that led to the street. “The train whistle.”

Iris stopped. The woman stood there, leaning against the damp stone wall, her notebook tucked under her arm. Up close, she was taller than Iris had realized, and younger. Her eyes were a lighter brown than seemed possible, almost amber.

“I’m sorry?”

“In your song. You sang it a minor third above the root. It’s usually a fourth. Or a fifth.” Her voice was low, melodious. Not French. American, but softened around the edges by something else.

“It sounded wrong as a fourth,” Iris said. The words came out defensively. “Too hopeful.”

A slow blink. A considering look. “Yes. It would have been.” The woman pushed off from the wall. “I’m Pauline. Pauline Carter.”

“Iris Finch.”

“I know. Marcel announces you.” Pauline fell into step beside her as Iris pushed open the heavy door to the alley. The cold night air was a shock, smelling of wet stone and rotting vegetables. “You study at the Conservatoire?”

“For another six months.” Iris shifted the guitar case to her other hand. “Then I suppose I go home.”

“To do what?”

The question was direct, unadorned. Iris had no good answer. “Sing in supper clubs, probably. Try for the radio.”

Pauline made a small sound in her throat. Not quite a dismissal, but close. They walked in silence for a block, their footsteps echoing. Iris was acutely aware of the other woman’s presence, the space her body occupied in the narrow alley. She noticed a small scar, a pale crescent, just above Pauline’s right eyebrow.

“You’re a writer,” Iris said, to say something.

“I try. Poems. Some plays. Nothing produced.” Pauline glanced at her. “You write your own music. That’s rare.”

“It’s not very good.”

“It’s honest. That’s rarer.” Pauline stopped at an intersection where a single gas lamp hissed, painting the cobbles in a weak yellow circle. “My flat is up there. The one with the window box. What’s left of the geraniums.” She pointed to a building across the street, its facade crumbling in the lamplight. “I have a phonograph. And terrible wine. If you’d like to not go home just yet.”

It wasn’t a question, not really. It was a statement of fact, an offering laid bare. Iris felt the decision not as a thought, but as a physical turning, a pivot on the damp cobblestones. “All right,” she said.

The room was smaller than her own, and infinitely more lived-in. Books were stacked on the floor, on the single chair, on the edge of a narrow bed tucked under the eaves. Papers covered a small table by the window—manuscript pages covered in a tight, angular script, leaflets with bold headlines in French and Spanish. The air smelled of ink, of the cheap coal burning in the small grate, of the faint, clean scent of the soap Pauline used.

Pauline poured two glasses of a rough red wine from a bottle without a label. She handed one to Iris, then sat on the floor, her back against the bed frame. Iris took the chair, moving a stack of pamphlets to the floor. One headline read ¡No Pasarán! above a photograph of grim-faced men in trenches.

“You’re political,” Iris said.

“Aren’t you?” Pauline took a sip of wine, her eyes steady over the rim of the glass.

“I don’t know. I don’t think about it much.”

“That’s a privilege.” Pauline said it without accusation. A simple calibration of fact.

Heat rose to Iris’s cheeks. She looked away, her eyes landing on the phonograph, a handsome mahogany box with a brass horn. A record was on the turntable. She leaned closer to read the label. Bessie Smith. “Backwater Blues.”

“You like her?” Pauline asked.

“I’ve never heard her.”

Pauline’s eyebrows lifted. She set her glass down, got up, and wound the phonograph. She lowered the needle with a careful hand. The scratch, then the voice filled the small room—a voice of worn velvet and gravel, of a sorrow so deep it had become a kind of power. Iris sat very still. She had never heard a woman sing like that. It wasn’t pretty. It was true. It was a bone-deep truth that made the hair on her arms stand up.

When the song ended, the silence in the room was different from the silence in the club. This was a shared silence, a thing they held between them.

“That,” Pauline said softly, “is what you do with a minor third.”

Iris looked at her. Pauline was watching her, a faint, challenging smile at the corner of her mouth. Something unlocked in Iris’s chest, a door swinging open on a room she hadn’t known was there. “Play it again,” she said.

Time, in that room, lost its orderly shape. It became measured in records played side by side on the floor, in the slow arc of the sun across the cracked plaster ceiling, in the number of cigarettes smoked down to the filter in a shared ceramic ashtray. Days bled into nights. Iris stopped going to her classes at the Conservatoire. The formal exercises, the rigid scales, felt like a betrayal of the messy, vital thing that was happening to her in Pauline’s garret.

They wrote together. Or rather, Pauline wrote words—fierce, jagged poems about hunger strikes in Barcelona, about lynchings in Alabama, about the quiet violence of a turned back—and Iris found melodies for them. Not pretty melodies. Angular, dissonant things that fought the words before finally settling into an uneasy, powerful harmony. They argued passionately, wonderfully. About whether a line needed another syllable. About whether a chord progression was cliché. About politics, about art, about whether beauty had a moral obligation.

“Art is a weapon or it is decoration,” Pauline said one afternoon, her pencil tapping a frantic rhythm against her knee. She was editing a leaflet for a solidarity fund. “There is no middle ground.”

“Can’t it just be true?” Iris asked. She was sprawled on the rug, trying to find a bridge for a song about a closed border. “Just a true thing, beautifully said?”

“And who decides what’s true? The ones with the money to publish it? The ones with the radios?” Pauline shook her head. “Neutrality is a fairy tale for children. Your silence is a vote for the way things are.”

Iris flinched. The word silence felt like an indictment. She had been so proud of her quiet songs, her personal aches. Now they seemed small, self-indulgent. She wanted to be bigger. She wanted her music to be a weapon, if that’s what Pauline needed it to be.

The idyll was porous. Reality seeped in at the edges. The francs from Marcel grew fewer. The letters from her mother in Connecticut, with their careful, worried questions about her plans, piled up unopened. The news from Spain grew worse. The shadow in the corner of the room, the one they tried to ignore, was the shape of the future. It had two forms: the war coming, and the money running out.

One evening, a man came to the flat. He was young, dressed in a suit that was too fine for the neighborhood, his hair slicked back with brilliantine. He carried a leather portfolio. He introduced himself as Laurent, a representative of a new arts journal, L’Avant-Garde. He was polite, effusive. He had heard, through a friend of a friend, about the remarkable collaboration between Mademoiselle Carter and the American singer.

“We wish to fund you,” he said, placing the portfolio on the table like a surgeon laying out his tools. “A monthly stipend. In return, you grant us first publication of your work. The journal has… generous patrons. They believe in art that speaks to the moment.”

Pauline was wary, her arms crossed. “Who are these patrons?”

Laurent gave a graceful, dismissive wave. “Men of vision. Men who understand that the old world is dying. They wish to midwife the new.” His eyes slid to Iris. “And you, mademoiselle. Your voice is extraordinary. Pure. It could be very popular, with the right… direction.”

Iris felt a thrill, cold and bright. Recognition. Validation. She saw Pauline’s jaw tighten.

They took the money. The first stipend bought coal, and wine, and a proper meal at a brasserie. It bought time. Iris felt a giddy relief. They could work. They could be artists. This was what success looked like.

But the arguments changed. They were no longer just about art. They were about the journal’s edits, which softened Pauline’s most incendiary lines. They were about Laurent’s suggestion that Iris perform at a salon for the journal’s benefactors—a soirée, he called it, all crystal and champagne.

“It’s just a performance,” Iris pleaded one night, after a particularly vicious fight. “It’s money. We need it.”

“It’s performing for the people who own the mines where the strikers are shot,” Pauline said, her voice quiet and terrible. She was at the window, looking out at the rain-slicked street. “You don’t get to take their money and pretend your hands are clean.”

“What’s the alternative? Starving in a garret for purity? Who does that help?”

Pauline turned. The light from the single bulb carved shadows under her eyes. “It helps me sleep at night.”

The words hung between them, a line drawn. Iris understood, then, that for Pauline, this was a religion. A faith with no room for compromise. For Iris, it was a life she was building, brick by precarious brick. The two things were starting to look like they could not occupy the same space.

The letter from the American record label arrived at her old lodgings. Her landlady forwarded it, a note of reproach pinned to the envelope about her unpaid rent. The letter was on thick, creamy paper. It offered a contract. A real one. A six-month recording deal in New York, with a sizable advance. They had heard a recording, a crude thing she’d made on a lark at a studio in Montparnasse. They saw potential. They wanted to cultivate her “unique sound.”

Safety. A path. A way out of the damp and the doubt and the constant, grinding calculation of francs. A way to be a professional, to be heard.

She carried the letter to Pauline’s flat for a week, a secret weight in her pocket. She told herself she was deciding. But she knew, in the way her heart beat a little faster when she thought of a studio, of an orchestra, of her name on a record label, that the decision was already made. She was just waiting for the courage to speak it.

She found it on a Thursday. The room was cold. They had run out of coal. Pauline was at the table, wrapped in a blanket, trying to write by the gray light from the window. Her breath made small ghosts in the air.

Iris sat across from her. She placed the letter on the table between them.

Pauline read it. Her face showed nothing. When she finished, she looked up, not at Iris, but at the wall behind her. At a water stain that spread across the plaster like a brown continent.

“When?” she asked. Her voice was flat.

“The boat leaves in ten days.”

A nod. A long, slow exhalation. Pauline pushed her chair back, the legs scraping on the floor. She walked to the window, her back to Iris. The silence was a physical presence, a cold, dense thing that filled the room and pressed against Iris’s skin. She wanted Pauline to shout. To throw something. To tell her she was a coward, a sellout, a traitor to everything they had believed in together. The anger would have been a bridge she could cross.

But Pauline said nothing. She just stood there, looking out at the Paris street, her shoulders rigid under the blanket.

“Pauli,” Iris whispered.

“You should go,” Pauline said, still not turning. “You’ll miss your last class.”

It was the cruelest thing she could have said. A dismissal so complete it erased the last two years entirely. Iris stood, her legs unsteady. She picked up the letter. She looked at Pauline’s back, at the precise line of her neck, at a single, escaped curl at her nape. She wanted to touch it. She wanted to say a hundred things.

She said nothing. She walked out, closing the door softly behind her. The click of the latch was the loudest sound she had ever heard.

On the day of her departure, a sleeting rain fell. Iris stood on the platform at the Gare Saint-Lazare, her guitar case in one hand, a single suitcase in the other. She wore her best coat, the one she’d bought with the first journal stipend. She scanned the crowd, her heart a frantic bird in her throat. A foolish, desperate hope.

Pauline did not come.

The train whistle blew, a sharp, commanding shriek. The sound was a perfect fourth. Hopeful. Iris flinched. She climbed aboard, found her compartment. She sat by the window, her guitar case propped against her knees. As the train began to pull out of the station, gathering speed, she pressed her forehead to the cold glass. The city blurred into streaks of gray and brown and vanished green. She did not cry. She just held the case tighter, her fingers tracing the familiar scuffs on the leather, as if it were the only true thing left in the world.

The compartment door slid open with a metallic scrape. A man in a conductor’s uniform stood there, holding a small silver tray. “Mademoiselle Finch?”

Iris looked up. The man’s face was a map of fine lines, his eyes tired. He placed a folded note on the tray. “For you.”

She took it. The paper was thick, expensive. The edges were crisp. She didn’t open it. She just held it, watching the conductor retreat, the door sliding shut again. The train’s rhythm was a steady, hypnotic thrum against the tracks. She turned the note over. No seal. No address. Just her name, written in a handwriting she didn’t recognize—blocky, impersonal.

She unfolded it.

The words were typed, not written. A business letter.

Dear Miss Finch,

We have followed your performances at Le Chat Noir and other venues with considerable interest. Your compositional style, while derivative in certain aspects, shows a distinctive melodic sensibility that we believe could find a receptive audience in the American market. We would like to offer you a standard recording contract with our label, including studio time in New York, professional arrangement, and a promotional tour of select radio programs. The terms are enclosed. Please contact our Paris representative at the address below to discuss.

Sincerely,
Robert H. Gaines
Vice President, Apex Records

Below that, a Paris address. A street near the Opéra.

Iris read it again. Then a third time. The paper felt alien in her hands, slick and cold. Derivative in certain aspects. The phrase stuck, a small burr under the skin. But the rest—studio time, New York, a tour. The train’s motion seemed to slow, the world outside the window crystallizing into discrete, sharp objects: a lone tree, a farmhouse with a red roof, a man leading a horse along a dirt road.

She had not told Pauline about the letter. Not that day, nor the next. She carried it in her pocket, the crisp edges softening against the fabric of her dress. It became a physical weight, a counterbalance to the other things she carried: the guitar, the francs, the smell of Gauloises on her coat.

They met, as they often did, at Pauline’s apartment. It was a single room above a printer’s shop, the walls stained with ink-smudged fingerprints, the air thick with the scent of lead and paper. Pauline was at her desk, a cigarette burning in a clay dish beside her, a manuscript spread before her. She didn’t look up when Iris entered.

“You’re late.”

“The tram was slow.”

Pauline made a mark on the page with a red pencil. Her movements were precise, economical. Iris stood by the window, watching the street below. A boy was kicking a stone along the gutter, his shoes scraping against the pavement. The sound was irregular, a syncopated rhythm against the steady hum of the printer’s machine.

“I have something to tell you,” Iris said.

Pauline’s pencil stopped. She didn’t turn. “Tell me.”

Iris took the letter from her pocket. She unfolded it, the creases now permanent. She placed it on the desk beside the manuscript.

Pauline read it. Her eyes moved down the page, then back to the top. She read it once, completely. Then she picked up her cigarette, took a long drag, and set it back in the dish. She did not touch the letter. She did not look at Iris.

“Apex Records,” she said. Her voice was flat. “They have a subsidiary that produces patriotic radio dramas for the army. Did you know that?”

“No.”

“They do.” Pauline turned a page of her manuscript. The rustle of paper was loud in the quiet room. “Their last major release was a series called ‘The Freedom Hour.’ It glorified the colonial police actions in the Philippines.”

Iris felt something tighten in her throat. “It’s a contract. It’s a chance.”

“A chance for what?” Pauline finally looked at her. Her eyes were not angry. They were distant, assessing, like a doctor examining a specimen. “To become a product? To sing their approved songs? To have your ‘distinctive melodic sensibility’ arranged by their professionals until it sounds like everything else on their shelf?”

“It’s security. It’s a way out of… this.” Iris gestured, a vague motion that encompassed the room, the ink-smudged walls, the perpetual smell of poverty and effort.

“This?” Pauline’s smile was a thin, cold line. “This is the work. This is the only thing that matters. You play in a cellar for people who listen. You write songs that have no fourths. That is the work. This—” she flicked the edge of the letter with her pencil—“is decoration.”

The boy outside kicked the stone again. It hit a lamppost with a dull clang.

“I have six months left at the Conservatoire,” Iris said. Her voice sounded small, defensive. “Then I have nothing. No money. No prospects. I go back to Ohio and I sing in hotel lounges for men who want to hear ‘Sweet Georgia Brown.’ That’s my future. This is a different future.”

“It’s a future they own.” Pauline stood up. She walked to the window, her back to Iris. She looked down at the street, at the boy who had now abandoned his stone and was running after a dog. “You understand what they will ask of you, yes? The songs will be vetted. Your appearances will be scheduled. Your image will be managed. You will be photographed with the correct people. You will not mention certain things. You will not be seen in certain places.”

“I can still write my own music. They said—”

Iris’s fingers found the rim of the clay dish. The cigarette had burned down to a long ash, still holding its shape. She traced the dish’s edge. It was rough, unglazed. “What is the alternative? Stay here? Play for pennies until Marcel decides he prefers a real French singer? Wait for you to finish your play and maybe, if it is produced, if it is not banned, if it finds a publisher who is not arrested, I can write the music for it? That is a fantasy.”

“It is a choice.” Pauline’s voice lowered. It lost its analytical edge and became something else, something raw and quiet. “It is the choice between making something true and making something safe. You cannot do both. The world is not built for both.”

Iris looked at the manuscript on the desk. The red pencil marks were like wounds on the page. She thought of the train whistle, that perfect, hopeful fourth. She thought of the compartment, the cold glass, the guitar case against her knees. She thought of a studio in New York, a microphone that did not bite, a paycheck that did not vanish.

“I have to answer them,” she said.

Pauline did not move. She stood by the window, a silhouette against the grey afternoon light. The printer’s machine downstairs stopped abruptly, leaving a sudden vacuum of silence. In that silence, Iris heard the faint, rapid ticking of a clock somewhere in the room. She had never noticed it before.

“Then answer them,” Pauline said.

There was no argument. No pleading. No final, devastating scene. Pauline simply returned to her desk. She picked up her pencil. She began to write again, her hand moving across the page with the same precise, economical motion. The conversation was over.

Iris waited. She waited for a minute, maybe two. The clock ticked. The printer’s machine started again, a low, grinding roar. Pauline did not look up. Iris picked up the letter from the desk. She folded it, put it back in her pocket. She left the apartment without saying another word.

The street was the same. The boy was gone. The dog was gone. A woman pushed a cart full of cabbages past, the wheels squeaking. Iris walked to the river. She stood on the bridge and watched the water move, brown and sluggish under the grey sky. She took the letter out and read it once more. The words did not change.

She went to the address near the Opéra the next day. It was an office building with a marble lobby and a uniformed attendant at a desk. The representative was a man named Laurent. He had a careful mustache and spoke English with a practiced, neutral accent. He explained the terms. The contract was for three years. They would own the rights to her compositions. They would provide a monthly stipend. They would arrange her travel. They would handle publicity. There was a clause about moral turpitude—any behavior deemed damaging to the label’s reputation could void the agreement.

“It is a standard clause,” Laurent said, smiling. “For the protection of both parties.”

Iris signed. The pen he gave her was heavy, gold-plated. It left a thick, black line on the paper.

She did not see Pauline for a week. She did not go to Le Chat Noir. She packed her few things in her room across the river. She bought a new suitcase, a sturdy one with brass fittings. She practiced the songs Laurent had suggested—popular American standards, a few wartime ballads. They felt foreign in her mouth, the phrasing too broad, the emotions too simple.

On the last day, she went to the printer’s shop. The door to Pauline’s apartment was locked. She knocked. No answer. She knocked again, harder. The printer below looked up from his machine, shook his head, and went back to his work.

Iris left a note. She wrote it on a page from her music notebook.

I am leaving tomorrow. On the ten o’clock train. I wish you would come.

She slipped it under the door.

The morning of the train was damp, a fine drizzle misting the station platforms. The Gare du Nord was a cathedral of noise and steam, the air thick with the smell of coal and wet wool and hurried bodies. Iris stood by the carriage, her suitcase and guitar case at her feet. She watched the clock. The hands moved with a slow, inexorable grace.

She did not expect Pauline to come. And yet, she watched the archways, the staircases, the streams of people flowing toward the trains. She watched a man arguing with a porter over a trunk. She watched a woman trying to calm a crying child. She watched the way the light fell through the high glass windows, diffused by the rain into a soft, grey glow.

When the whistle blew, that sharp, commanding shriek—a perfect fourth—she felt it in her teeth. A porter approached, touched his cap. “Mademoiselle? You should board.”

The city blurred into streaks of grey and brown and vanished green. A patch of graffiti on a wall, a phrase she couldn’t read. A line of empty bicycles chained to a railing. The dome of the Sacré-Cœur, a pale ghost against the sky. Then it was gone, replaced by fields, by hedgerows, by the flat, endless landscape of the north.

She did not cry. Her eyes remained dry, her breath even. She just held the case tighter, her fingers tracing the familiar scuffs on the leather, the worn spot near the handle where her thumb always rested. She traced it over and over, as if it were a map, as if it could tell her where she was going, or where she had been.

The compartment door opened again. The same conductor, his face still tired. “We will be at the border in one hour, mademoiselle. Your documents, please.”

Iris handed him her passport. He examined it, nodded, handed it back. “Thank you.”

As he left, she saw something on the floor by the door. A small, folded square of paper, different from the thick business letter. It must have fallen from his tray, or been dropped by a previous passenger. She bent and picked it up.

It was a page from a notebook. The paper was cheap, thin. The handwriting was familiar—the sharp, angular script, the red pencil she used for editing.

There were only three words.

The cage is open.

No signature. No explanation. Just that.

Iris stared at the words. The train rocked slightly, a gentle sway. The fields outside were a monochrome green under the rain. She folded the paper and put it in the pocket of her coat, beside the Apex contract. The two pieces of paper lay against her hip, one crisp and official, one soft and worn.

She did not know what the words meant. A condemnation? A blessing? A simple statement of fact? Pauline’s mind worked in metaphors that were often opaque, lines that connected in ways Iris could not always follow. The cage is open. Was she the cage? Was Paris the cage? Was the contract the cage, and now she was free of it? Or was it the opposite—the cage of their life was open, and she had flown out, into something else?

The ambiguity was a knot in her stomach. It did not loosen. It tightened with each mile, each click of the track. She took out the page again, looked at the red pencil marks. There was a smudge at the edge, a fingerprint in ink. Pauline’s fingerprint, from the printer’s shop. Iris touched it. The ink was dry, but it left a faint, dark trace on her own thumb.

She put the page away. She leaned her head against the window. The glass was cold. The world outside was a blur of motion, of leaving. She closed her eyes. In the darkness, she heard the train whistle again, that perfect, hopeful fourth. But now, in her memory, it sounded wrong. It sounded like a door closing.
