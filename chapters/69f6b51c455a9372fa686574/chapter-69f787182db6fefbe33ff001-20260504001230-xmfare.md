The air in the theatre was a dense, woolly thing, thick with the scent of old velvet, face powder, and the faint metallic tang of a hundred held breaths. From her dressing room, a converted broom closet with a mirror and a single bare bulb, Iris heard the muffled roar of the house settling. A cough. The rustle of programs. The collective shift of bodies on wooden seats, a sound like distant surf. She had performed for crowds ten times this size, but this silence before the curtain was different. It wasn’t anticipation. It was scrutiny.

She laid out her tools on the chipped vanity. Not makeup. A stub of charcoal pencil. A clean handkerchief. The small, pewter ashtray from the windowsill. Her hands were steady, which was its own kind of strange. She’d expected tremors. Her fingers moved with a deliberate, almost ceremonial calm. The ache in her throat was a familiar companion now, a low hum beneath the skin.

Her gaze caught on a hairline crack in the mirror’s silvering. It ran from the top left corner down to the center, splitting her reflection into two slightly misaligned women. One looked tired, her face a map of fine lines under the harsh light. The other looked like a stranger, eyes too dark, mouth set in a line that wasn’t quite hers. She touched the glass. The crack felt like a ridge of ice.

She reached into the pocket of her dressing gown. The Zippo lighter was there, its metal worn smooth and warm from twelve years of pointless carrying. She placed it in the center of the ashtray. It looked small. Insignificant. Just a cheap lighter from a Parisian tobacconist, the engraving of a sparrow on its side nearly rubbed away. She’d never once put fluid in it. It was a souvenir of a life she thought she’d chosen to leave. Now she knew she’d been pushed. The difference was everything, and it was nothing.

A knock at the door, two sharp raps. Clara didn’t wait for an answer. She slipped inside, closing the door softly behind her. She wore black from head to toe, the uniform of the stage manager, a headset dangling from one hand. Her eyes went to the ashtray, then to Iris’s face.

“House is full,” Clara said. She spoke, the sound flat, professional. “Strauss is in the front, holding court. The Times critic is here. So is the man from the Tribune. And your. Husband. Third row, aisle. He’s with the government man.”

Iris nodded. She’d known they would be together. Knowing and hearing it were different. A cold knot tightened low in her stomach. Not fear. Something cleaner. Resolve had a taste, she realized—like the air after a lightning strike, sharp and empty.

“Did you give Sam the cue?”

“He took it. Didn’t ask.” Clara shifted her weight. “He’s scared. They all are. Pauline’s pacing backstage like a caged panther. She hasn’t said a word to anyone for an hour.”

“She’s in the zone.”

“She’s terrified,” Clara corrected, and there was no malice in it, just a statement of fact. “It’s a different look on her. I’ve never seen it.”

Iris picked up a matchbook from the vanity. The cover advertised a bar she’d never visited. She struck a match. The flare was sudden, a tiny, violent sun in the dim room. The smell of sulfur was brief and acrid.

“What are you doing?” Clara asked.

Iris didn’t answer. She held the match to the corner of the handkerchief. The cheap cotton caught slowly, the flame blooming orange and lazy. She let it burn for a three-count, then dropped the cloth into the ashtray, on top of the lighter. The fabric blackened, curled. A thin tendril of smoke, grey and bitter, spiraled upward. The lighter itself wouldn’t burn, of course. But the act was the thing. Letting the fire touch it. Letting the soot coat the sparrow.

Clara watched, her expression unreadable. “A ritual?”

“An eviction,” Iris said, her voice rough. She watched the flame die, leaving a blackened scrap and the lighter, now dusted with ash. The ghost of Paris, finally smoked out. A ridiculous sentimental gesture. It made her feel lighter anyway.

From beyond the door, the murmur of the house changed timbre. A hush fell, deeper than before. Then the faint, electric buzz of the house lights beginning to dim. A shiver went through the building itself, a vibration in the floorboards.

“That’s the five-minute call,” Clara said. She turned to go, then stopped, her hand on the doorknob. She didn’t look back. “Sam also said. There are two new men. In the back. Standing against the wall. Not sitting. They’ve been there since the doors opened it. They’re not press.”

Iris’s breath caught. She saw them immediately in her mind’s eye. Grey suits. Hats in hand. Watching. Not the play. The audience. “Cross’s friends.”

“Or Langston’s,” Clara said, and opened the door. The noise from the corridor swelled—a whispered line, the creak of a flat being shifted, a hissed “Places!” —then cut off as the door clicked shut.

Langston’s. The word hung in the smoky air. He wasn’t just with Cross. He had brought his own reinforcements. A show of force, or a guarantee? The transaction made visible. Her husband and her handler, side by side in the third row, a shared investment watching the stage. And now his own men in the back, ensuring the product performed as contracted. The cold knot in her stomach dissolved, replaced by a hollow, airy feeling. It was almost relief. The mask was off. The pretense of protection, of a difficult partnership was gone. What remained was the simple, brutal architecture of the deal.

She stood. The dressing gown whispered to the floor. Her costume—a simple dove-grey dress of no particular decade—felt like a second skin. She looked at the mirror one last time. The crack no longer split her face. She was whole on one side, fractured on the other. It seemed right.

She left the dressing room without looking back at the ashtray. The backstage darkness was a living thing, punctuated by the faint glow of tape marks on the floor and the slivers of light around the edges of the heavy velvet curtain. She could feel the presence of the other actors, their silent forms like statues in the gloom. Pauline was a darker shadow at stage left, her profile a sharp cutout against the deep red of the curtain. She was absolutely still.

Iris took her place in the wings, her designated spot. From here, she could see a narrow slice of the audience through the gap between curtain and proscenium. The lights were down, but the exit signs cast a faint, bloody glow on the first few rows. She found them. Third row, aisle. Langston sat straight, his profile aristocratic, a faint smile on his lips as he listened to something Cross was saying. Cross nodded, his face a pale oval in the dark. He looked relaxed. Avuncular. A man at the theatre.

Then Langston turned his head, just slightly, and his gaze swept the curtain. Not searching for her. Surveying. Assessing the property. His eyes, even at this distance held the flat, evaluative look she’d seen him use on club owners and bandleaders. The look that said I own this. It lasted only a second before he turned back to Cross, leaning in to murmur something that made the agent give a small, quiet chuckle.

The hollow feeling inside Iris filled with a substance that was both heavy and sharp. It wasn’t hatred. It was colder. A final, absolute recognition. The love she’d never really felt for him had curdled years ago into a kind of numb tolerance. Now even that was gone. What remained was the clean, empty space where a person should have been. It was a relief so deep it felt like grief.

From the light booth, a single, clear cue light glowed red.

Pauline’s voice, low and steady cut through the darkness. “Stand by.”

A breath from the company. The rustle of fabric.

The curtain began to rise, its heavy heave a sound like a world turning over. The stage lights came up, warm and golden, painting the set—a spartan living room, a table, two chairs—in a false, forgiving daylight. The audience vanished into a black void beyond the glare.

Iris stepped into the light. The heat of it was a physical shock on her skin. For a moment, she was blind. Then her vision cleared. She saw only the empty chair where her character’s husband would sit. She didn’t look at the third row. She didn’t look at the back wall. She sat, arranged her hands in her lap, and waited for the first line. The silence in the house was absolute. It was the most honest sound she’d heard in years.

The first line came from the wings, delivered with a brittle precision by the actor playing her sister-in-law. Iris responded on cue, her own voice a measured, weary thing. The words were about a misplaced grocery order, a trivial domestic complaint. But the subtext, the space between each syllable, hummed with a different frequency—the exhaustion of a woman who had seen the official report and knew every line was a lie.

She moved through the blocking without thought. Poured a cup of imaginary tea. Smoothed the fabric of her skirt. Her body remembered the rehearsals, the muscle memory of fiction. Her mind was elsewhere, floating somewhere near the ceiling, watching the delicate, dangerous machinery of the scene click into place.

The dialogue turned to the husband, a sailor home from the port. The fictional wife spoke of his silence, his distant eyes. Iris felt the words land in the hushed theatre not as character exposition, but as a direct transmission. She allowed her gaze, just once, to drift past the footlights. Not to the third row. To the back wall. Two dark shapes against the faded crimson plaster. Standing. Watching. The heat of the spotlight on her face was a brand.

Her scene partner’s hand trembled slightly as he lifted his prop newspaper. A bead of sweat traced a path from his temple to his jawline. He was a young actor from the institute, brilliant and terrified. He mispronounced a word, a minor harbor’s name, corrected himself with a flush. The mistake was human. Perfect. It made the whole artifice feel more real.

A line about safe homecomings. Iris delivered her reply, a simple, “We can only hope.” The scripted meaning was wifely concern. The truth in her mouth was for the three hundred souls lost in the munitions explosion, for the Navy’s denial, for the two men in the back who knew exactly why this play was dangerous. The air in the house seemed to thicken, to hold the double meaning aloft for a second before letting it settle.

She noticed a moth. A small, dusty brown thing caught in the cone of a follow-spot offstage. It fluttered in frantic circles, a tiny asteroid trapped in a sunbeam. Its silent struggle was more compelling than any of her lines. She had to wrench her attention back.

The act built toward its first quiet climax: the wife finding the hidden letter. Iris crossed to the writing desk, her footsteps the only sound in the world. The prop letter was where it was meant to be, tucked under a blotter. Her fingers touched the paper. A real shudder went through her then, involuntary. It wasn’t in the script. Her throat tightened. For a terrible second, she saw not the prop, but the crisp bond of a government subpoena, the elegant slant of Pauline’s handwriting on a Parisian envelope. The past and the present and the fiction collapsed into a single point of pressure behind her eyes.

She took a breath. The air tasted of dust and ozone. She unfolded the letter.

Her next line was a whisper. “It says they called it an accident.”

On the page, the line was followed by a stage direction: (She sits, stunned.)

Iris didn’t sit. She stood very still, the letter hanging from her hand. She looked out, not at her scene partner, but straight into the dark heart of the audience. Her vision had tunneled. She could see only the vague, pale ovals of faces in the first few rows. “They listed the cause as ‘handling error.’ By the men. The colored men.”

The silence was a living entity. It had weight, texture. It pressed against her eardrums. From the corner of her eye, she saw her scene partner freeze, unsure. This wasn’t the blocking. She was supposed to break down, to weep. Not to state it like a fact to a jury.

She let the silence stretch a beat too long. Two beats. Then she crumpled the letter slowly, the sound of the paper loud as a gunshot in the quiet, and let it fall to the floor. Only then did she sink into the chair, her movements stiff, mechanical. The curtain began its slow, merciful close for the intermission.

The moment the heavy velvet touched the stage, the spell broke. A muffled roar of conversation swelled from the house. The backstage world snapped back into focus—snapped instructions, the scuff of shoes, a stagehand wheeling a flat past her.

Iris didn’t move. She stared at the crumpled paper on the boards.

Pauline was there suddenly, a dark presence at her shoulder. She didn’t speak at first. She lit a cigarette, the match flare a tiny, violent star in the dim wing space. She exhaled a plume of smoke that wreathed Iris’s head.

“You changed the rhythm,” Pauline said finally. The way she said it was low, devoid of any readable emotion.

“It felt right.”

“It wasn’t the rhythm we rehearsed.”

“No.” Iris looked up at her. Pauline’s face was in shadow, the sharp planes of her cheeks lit from below by the ghost light on the empty stage. “It was better.”

Pauline took another drag. Her eyes were on the vibrating curtain, listening to the rumble of the audience. “The two men in the back. They left.”

A cold finger traced Iris’s spine. “Together?”

“No. One went left, toward the lobby. The other went right, toward the fire exit.” Pauline’s gaze shifted to Iris. “Splitting up. That’s procedure.”

“You sound like you know.”

“I’ve read stories.” Pauline dropped the cigarette, ground it out under her heel. “The first act is done. They’ve heard the heresy. Now they’re reporting in.”

“Let them.” The words came out with a force that surprised her.

Pauline studied her for a long moment. Something in her expression softened, a fraction. Not warmth. A kind of recognition. “Your daughter is at the booth. She says the cue is ready.”

“I know.”

“Iris.” Pauline’s hand came up, not quite touching her arm. An aborted gesture. “The silence you asked for. Ten seconds. It’s a long time.”

“I’m counting on it.”

A voice called for Pauline from the opposite wing. She turned, then paused. “Don’t wait for the cue light. You’ll know when.” Then she was gone, went still by the backstage gloom.

Iris stood. Her legs held. She walked off the stage, past the whispering clusters of actors, toward the sanctuary of her closet dressing room. Halfway there, Langston stepped out from behind a towering flat of a painted parlor wall.

He looked out of place, his tailored overcoat too clean, his face too pale amidst the sweat and sawdust. He held a program, rolled tight in his fist.

“A compelling interpretation,” he said. His smile was a thin, professional curve. “A bit. Direct, perhaps.”

Iris stopped. She could smell his cologne, something with sandalwood, overlaying the scent of theatre. “It’s called acting, Langston. Sometimes you have to mean it.”

The smile vanished. “Iris. Be careful. You’re not just performing for the critics.”

“I know exactly who I’m performing for.” She started to move past him.

His hand shot out, fingers closing around her wrist. The grip was tight, proprietary. “This isn’t a game. Cross is. Concerned. The deviation. He sees it as provocation.”

She looked down at his hand, then up at his face. The fear in his eyes was real, but it wasn’t for her. It was for the deal, the arrangement, his own fragile safety. She pulled her arm free. The contact left a cold ring on her skin.

“Tell Mr. Cross,” she said, her voice barely a whisper, “to watch the second act. The provocation hasn’t started yet.”

She left him standing there, the rolled program trembling slightly in his hand. As she reached her door, she heard the distant, tinny voice of the stage manager calling places for Act Two. The sound was like a bell tolling. She went inside, closed the door, and leaned against it. In the dark, with only the slit of light from under the door, she finally let the tremor come. It started in her knees, a fine, constant vibration, as if the floor itself was humming. She pressed her palms flat against the wood until it stopped.

The tremor in her knees subsided, leaving a hollow, airy feeling, as if her bones had been replaced with balsa wood. She pushed away from the door. The dressing room was a tomb. The single bulb above the mirror cast a hard, unforgiving circle of light on the vanity, leaving the corners in deep shadow. The ashtray sat in the center of the light, the blackened handkerchief a crumpled fist around the lighter. She should clean it. She didn’t move.

She looked at her reflection in the cracked glass. The woman looking back was a stranger she was beginning to recognize. Not the Canary. Not Langston’s wife. Not even Pauline’s lost lover. Someone pared down to the essential nerve. The recognition was quiet, an internal click like a lock sliding home.

A floorboard creaked in the corridor outside. Not the hurried step of a stagehand. A measured, hesitant tread. It stopped outside her door.

She knew who it was before the knock came. Three soft, tentative raps. The sound of a man who had lost his script.

“Iris?” Langston’s voice, muffled by the wood was stripped of its usual polished confidence. It sounded thin. Pleading.

She didn’t answer. She watched the door, her breath shallow in her chest. Let him stand there. Let him sweat in the silence he had helped to create.

The knob turned. He didn’t wait for permission. He slipped inside, closing the door quickly behind him as if he were the one being pursued. He looked smaller in the cramped room. His tuxedo, usually a second skin of authority seemed to hang on him. His face was pale, a fine sheen of sweat on his upper lip. He held his silk top hat in both hands, turning it in a slow, nervous rotation.

“You shouldn’t be here,” she said. She spoke — flat. A statement of fact.

“I had to see you.” He took a step forward, then stopped, his eyes darting from her face to the ashtray and back. “Before the second act. We need to talk.”

“We don’t.”

“Iris, please.” The hat completed another revolution. “What you did out there. That little speech. It was unnecessary. Reckless. Strauss is livid. He thinks you’ve poisoned the well.”

“Let him think it.”

“This isn’t a game!” The words burst from him, sharp with a panic he couldn’t contain. He lowered his voice, a harsh whisper. “Those men in the back. They aren’t just observers. Cross brought them as. Insurance. In case the performance deviated from the approved material. Do you understand what that means?”

She understood perfectly. It meant Langston had known the contingency plan. He’d been briefed. The cold knot in her stomach hardened into something like marble. “And what was your role in that? To give the signal? A nod? A lifted program?”

He flinched. A genuine, physical recoil. “No. God, no.”

“You already have.” She picked up the pewter ashtray. The soot came off on her fingers, a gritty black dust. She held it between them. “You traded the letters. My past. For what, exactly? A guarantee they’d look the other way while you peddled patriotic dross to the networks? A promise your name stayed off a list?”

His mouth opened, closed. The denial died before it reached the air. He looked at the floor, at a water stain shaped like Italy near the baseboard. “It wasn’t like that. It was. Damage control. For both of us. They had the leverage. I used it to get us the best possible terms.”

“Terms.” The word tasted of ash and metal. “You brokered a deal. With my life as the currency.”

“I protected you!” Now his eyes met hers, blazing with a desperate, wounded righteousness. “For twelve years, I have protected you. I gave you a career when yours was over. A name when yours was mud. A roof. Respectability. Do you think any of that was easy? Do you think the world was just waiting to embrace a has-been chanteuse with a. A complicated personal history?”

The room seemed to tilt. Not with anger, but with a dizzying, vertiginous clarity. She saw it all, laid out like the neat columns of his account books. Her love for Pauline, her fear, her silence—all of it had been a commodity in his ledger. A liability to be managed, then leveraged. The protection had never been for her. It was for his investment.

“The letters,” she said slowly. “You kept them. All these years. Not as a memento. As collateral.”

He didn’t deny it. His shoulders slumped, the fight draining out of him. “I knew a day like this might come. I had to have something they wanted more than they wanted to crucify you. I thought. I thought if it ever came to it, I could trade them for your safety.” He gestured weakly toward the door, toward the unseen audience. “This was never the plan. You were supposed to do the play, file the reports, and we’d walk away. Your name cleared. A modest comeback. A quiet life. That was the deal.”

“And Pauline? And her institute? What was the deal for them?”

He looked away again, his jaw working. “They were never my concern.”

There it was. The naked truth, simple and ugly. It between them, sharper than any accusation. The silence that followed was different from before. It wasn’t tense. It was empty. A space where a marriage had been.

From the corridor, the stage manager’s voice called out, “Places for Act Two! Five minutes, people!” The sound was muffled, distant, like news from another country.

Iris set the ashtray down. She wiped her sooty fingers on a clean corner of the handkerchief. The action was methodical, final. “You should go back to your seat. Your friends will be wondering where you are.”

“Iris.” He took a step closer. He smelled of his usual bay rum cologne and, beneath it, the sour tang of fear. “We can still fix this. After the show, I’ll talk to Cross. We’ll renegotiate.”

“There’s nothing to fix.” She cut him off, her voice quiet. “The deal is done. You made your trade. I’m making mine.”

He stared at her, uncomprehending. “What trade?”

She didn’t answer. She turned to the mirror, pretending to check her hair, her face a mask of calm in the broken glass. She could see his reflection behind her, a ghost of a man clutching a hat.

After a long moment, he turned. His hand paused on the doorknob. “You’ll ruin yourself,” he said, not looking at her.

“Probably.”

He left, closing the door with a soft, definitive click.

The silence rushed back in. It was a cleaner silence now, scoured of his presence. She looked at the Italy-shaped stain on the floor, then at the crack in the mirror. A hairline fracture, from corner to center. It didn’t matter which reflection was the real one anymore. They were both hers.

She took a deep breath. The air still held the faint, acrid scent of burned cloth. Underneath it, she caught the smell of the theatre itself—dust, glue, the faint, sweet decay of old wood. It was the smell of waiting. Of empty stages and full houses. Of breath held before a line.

She picked up the charcoal pencil from the vanity. Her hand was steady. In the margin of her script, she wrote a single word, not for the play, but for herself. A note to remember.

Fly.

Then she stood, smoothed her costume, and walked out into the humming darkness of the wings.
