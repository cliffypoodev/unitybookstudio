The rehearsal room was cold. A damp, persistent cold that seeped up from the floorboards and settled in the bones. Iris stood in the center of the empty space, the script for Port Chicago limp in her hand. The words had blurred into a gray smear. She knew them. She’d written them in the margins of her own confession, then folded that page and burned it in the ashtray that morning. The ash had smelled like cheap paper and regret.

Pauline sat in a folding chair by the piano, her posture a study in contained patience. The students were a loose semicircle on the floor, legs crossed, watching. A boy in a striped sweater kept cracking his knuckles. The sound was like small twigs snapping. Iris focused on a water stain on the far wall, a brownish map of some unknown continent. She could not remember the first line.

“From the top, Iris.” Pauline’s voice was neutral, a tool.

The scene was simple. Her character, a clerk in the naval office, was to receive the official notification of the explosion. A messenger brought the telegram. That was all. No tears in the stage directions. No wailing. Just the act of taking the paper, reading it, looking up. The silence after the news was the point.

She took a breath. The air tasted of dust and old varnish. She could do silence. She’d built a career on the pause between notes, the look away from the spotlight. This was different. This silence had to be full of a specific, terrible knowledge. Not the generic melancholy of a torch song. Something precise. A stone dropped into a deep well, waiting for the sound that never comes.

The boy playing the messenger, a serious young man named Charles, approached. He held out a folded piece of blank paper. “Ma’am,” he said. His voice was too loud. A performance.

Iris took the paper. Her fingers were steady. Good. Steady was professional. She unfolded it, her eyes scanning the invisible type. She arranged her face. A slight parting of the lips. A tiny tremor in the hand holding the paper. She let her gaze drift up from the page, over Charles’s shoulder, into the middle distance where tragedy supposedly lived. It was a good look. She’d used a variation of it for the cover of Nightfall in Montmartre.

The room was quiet.

Pauline didn’t speak. She simply watched. The knuckle-cracker stopped.

“I’m sorry,” Iris said, still holding the pose. “Should I go again?

“What did you just feel?” Pauline asked. Not to the class. To her.

Iris lowered the paper. “Feel? She’s just gotten the news. It’s… shock. Numbness.

“No.” Pauline stood, the chair legs scraping against the floor. The sound made Iris’s shoulders tighten. “You performed shock. You indicated numbness. You gave me a beautifully composed picture of a woman receiving terrible news. What did you feel?

A hot flush crept up her neck. This was the old argument, the one from Paris studios smelling of turpentine and garlic. Stop illustrating the feeling, Iris. Be the room the feeling is in. She’d never understood it then. She didn’t understand it now. “I felt the scene,” she said, a defensive edge in her voice she hadn’t meant to show.

Pauline walked toward her, her footsteps measured on the hollow floor. “Let’s try an exercise. Charles, thank you. Everyone, watch the floor. Not her. The floor.

The students dropped their gazes obediently. Charles retreated. Iris was alone in the circle again, the blank paper a stupid weight in her hand.

“The telegram isn’t about an accident,” Pauline said, stopping a few feet away. Her voice dropped, became almost intimate. A trick. “It’s a reply. To a letter you sent. A letter where you told them the loading procedures were unsafe. You used the exact words your brother, who works on the docks, told you. You signed your name. You mailed it three weeks ago.

Iris stared at her. This wasn’t in the script.

“This,” Pauline said, tapping the paper in Iris’s hand, “is their answer. Read it.

Iris looked down at the blank page. Her mouth had gone dry. A familiar panic, thin and metallic, began to pool under her tongue. This was a trap. A director’s game. She was supposed to conjure something from nothing.

“Read it aloud,” Pauline said.

The words wouldn’t come. There were no words. She shook her head slightly.

“Then just stand there,” Pauline said, her voice softening into something worse than impatience. “Stand there with the answer in your hand.

Iris stood. She held the paper. She tried to think about Port Chicago, the munitions ship, the light a white tear. But her mind skittered away, landing on the memory of Arthur’s face last night, bland and expectant over his steak. Any progress, darling? The way he’d cut the meat, precise surgical slices. The silence in their apartment after, a different kind of void. She looked at the blank paper and saw the chaotic field of ink from her own nighttime writing. I knew. I helped. I sang. The paper in her hand was no longer a telegram. It was Arthur’s file. It was the agent’s bland smile. It was every unsigned contract, every coded lyric that had been a coward’s compromise.

Her breath hitched. A stupid, small sound. She tried to swallow it back.

“Don’t fix it,” Pauline murmured, so low only Iris could hear. “Let it be ugly.

But it was ugly. A pressure was building behind her eyes, a sharp, physical ache. This wasn’t the character’s shame. This was hers. The paper trembled. Not a staged tremor. A real one, starting in her wrist, a loose vibration she couldn’t control. She wasn’t looking into a middle distance anymore. She was staring at the grain of the floorboard right in front of her scuffed shoes, seeing the long scratch that looked like a chalk line. A body outline. Her own.

She made another sound. A kind of gasp. The pressure behind her eyes broke, not into tears, but into a searing heat that made her vision swim. She didn’t cry. She stood there, rigid, holding the stupid blank paper, her face contorting into something she knew wasn’t pretty, wasn’t composed, was just a raw, open wound of a feeling with no name. It was anger at herself. It was fear so deep it felt like vertigo. It was the crushing weight of every answer she’d ever received to a risk she’d never taken.

The room was utterly still. No one cracked their knuckles.

After a long moment, Pauline said, “Thank you, Iris.” Then, to the class, “That is the difference between performing a state and inhabiting a consequence. You may look up now.

Iris couldn’t move. The heat in her face receded, leaving a cold, hollow nausea. She carefully refolded the blank paper, her fingers clumsy. She didn’t know where to put it. In her pocket? Hand it back? It was just a prop.

“We’ll break for ten minutes,” Pauline said. The students began to stir, murmuring, getting up, flowing toward the door for cigarettes. Their voices were a low, respectful buzz. They’d seen something. A crack in the famous finish.

Pauline walked to the piano and leaned against it, waiting.

Iris finally moved, walking stiffly to a chair and sitting down. Her legs felt unreliable. She placed the folded paper on the seat beside her, aligning its edges with the wood grain. A pointless bit of order.

“That was good,” Pauline said.

“It was a mess.”

“Yes.”

Iris looked at her then. Pauline’s expression was unreadable, but her eyes were bright, focused. Not triumphant. Alert. “You ambushed me.

“I gave you a truth to stand in,” Pauline corrected. “The facts were fiction. The consequence was real. You found it.

“I found my own goddamn panic,” Iris said, the profanity coming out flat.

“That’s the only panic that matters.” Pauline pushed off from the piano. “The character’s terror is an abstraction. Yours is a tool. Remember that. When we run it again with the actual lines, you won’t be performing the news. You’ll be standing in the wreckage of the letter you sent. The words will just be… what’s left.

Iris looked at the water stain on the wall again. It looked less like a continent now and more like a spill. Something that needed cleaning up. “I don’t know if I can do that on purpose.

“You just did.” Pauline turned to go, then paused. “The ugliness is the point, Iris. The pretty pain is what they sell you. The ugly truth is what you own.

She left then, joining the last of the students in the hallway. The door swung shut behind her, cutting off the murmur of voices.

Iris sat alone in the cold room. From the hallway, she caught a fragment of a student’s laugh, quickly suppressed. The radiator in the corner let out a long, sighing hiss, a breath it had been holding for years. She looked at her hands. They were still. The tremor was gone. In its place was a faint, persistent hum, a tuning fork struck and left to vibrate in the empty air.

The students had filed out, their whispers and the scrape of chairs fading down the corridor. The silence they left behind had a different texture than the one from the exercise. This was an old silence, a waiting silence. Iris remained in the center of the floor. The blank paper was still in her hand. She folded it once, precisely, then again, making a small, tight square she could hide in her palm.

Pauline didn’t move from her chair by the piano. She lit a cigarette, the match flare a brief, sulfurous star in the dimming room. The blue evening light through the high windows made the dust motes look like suspended ash.

“You can go,” Pauline said, exhaling smoke. “If you want.

“Do you want me to go?”

“I want you to make a choice. That’s all I’ve ever wanted.

Iris felt the words land, a dull thud in her stomach. Not a new accusation. An old one, dug up and brushed off. She walked to the row of chairs, pulled one out. It screeched on the floor, a sound that made her jaw tighten. She sat, not facing Pauline directly, but at an angle, so she could see the window and the slice of Pauline’s profile. The water stain on the wall was now a dark bruise in the twilight.

“The scene wasn’t working,” Iris said. A fact. A defense.

“I’m trying.”

“No. You’re performing ‘trying.’ It’s a different skill set. One you’ve mastered.” Pauline’s voice was flat, observational. That was worse than anger. “You did it in Paris. When the Comet Records man came to the café. You remember.

Iris remembered. The smell of spilled wine and Gauloises. The man’s cufflinks, gold and too large. Pauline’s hand on her knee under the table, a hard, warning pressure. And her own voice, bright and slippery, talking about artistic evolution, about reaching a broader audience. Performing her own betrayal while it was happening. Standing beside it.

“That’s not fair,” Iris said, but the words were thin, a habit.

“Fair.” Pauline almost smiled. It didn’t reach her eyes. “You stood beside us then, too. Me. Marta. The whole damn collective. You gave a lovely little speech about pragmatism. You indicated regret beautifully. And then you left.

The memory was a physical thing, a sudden, sour taste at the back of her throat. She could see the pattern on the café floor, black and white tiles, one of them chipped. A detail that served nothing. She’d stared at it the whole time she was lying.

“I wrote the checks,” Iris said, the old counter-argument emerging automatically. “From the first royalty payment. I sent the money.

“Oh, the money.” Pauline stubbed out the cigarette, grinding it with more force than necessary. “The conscience money. Yes, we got it. It bought paper. It paid for Marta’s sister’s passage, in the end. It was useful. It was also a way for you to keep standing beside it. To be the generous benefactor from a distance. Never in the room where the decisions were made. Never getting your hands dirty with the actual work.

Iris’s face grew hot. Her hands, in her lap, were perfectly still. A trick she’d learned. “You think I wanted that life? The supper clubs, the… the husband? You think that was a prize?

“I think you were afraid of the alternative. I think you still are.” Pauline turned her head now, looked directly at her. The fading light carved shadows under her cheekbones. “You’re afraid of this room. Right now. You’re afraid of what I might say next. You’re afraid of that blank paper. Because it’s not a telegram. It’s a mirror. And you have spent twelve years learning how not to look into mirrors.

The accuracy was a knife twist. A clean, professional cut. Iris found herself staring at a chip in the paint on her chair leg, a tiny white crescent. She focused on it. “The work is different here. It’s not singing. It’s… louder.

“That’s not true.” The protest was automatic, hollow.

“Isn’t it?” Pauline’s gaze didn’t waver. “What did you tell him? After our little meeting last week. Did you give him a name? A tidbit about the institute’s funding? Something to keep him happy?

Iris’s mouth was dry. She saw Arthur’s expectant face. The steak. She had given him nothing. But she had also given him nothing of substance, just vague assurances, performances of cooperation. She had stood beside the act of informing, neither committing nor refusing. Pauline saw it all. She always had.

“I haven’t named anyone,” Iris said, the truth sounding feeble.

“But you haven’t told him to go to hell, either. You’re keeping your options open. That’s your specialty. Keeping a door cracked, just in case the room gets too hot.” Pauline stood up abruptly, the chair legs scraping. She walked to the window, her back to Iris. Her silhouette was a cut-out against the deep blue. “I can’t direct that. I can’t work with a ghost. Port Chicago isn’t about ghosts. It’s about the living. The ones who signed the manifests. The ones who stood on the dock and watched the ship load. The ones who knew. You have to be in the room, Iris. You have to be the clerk who filed the order. Not someone who just happened to be passing by.

The words hung in the cold air. Iris uncrumpled the paper square in her hand, smoothed it against her thigh. It was soft from handling, the creases white.

“What do you want from me?” The question was a whisper.

Pauline didn’t turn around. “I want you to choose a side of the door. And close it. Even if it locks behind you.” She paused. “The play can’t be your latest alibi. Art isn’t something you hide behind. It’s the thing you show. Even when it’s ugly. Especially then.

Outside, a streetlamp flickered on, casting a sickly yellow trapezoid on the far wall. It sliced across the water-stain continent, rendering it unfamiliar. Iris felt a sudden, absurd memory of the lime tree outside their Paris flat, how the leaves used to slap against the window when the wind blew from the west. A useless detail. It ached.

“I don’t know how,” she said. Not to Pauline. To the room.

“Then learn.” Pauline finally turned. Her face was in shadow, unreadable. “Or leave. But stop taking up space in the middle. It’s cowardly. And I’m tired of watching it.

She walked to the door, took her coat from the hook. The sound of wool sliding against wood was terribly final.

“Pauline.”

She stopped, one arm in her coat sleeve.

The words piled up behind Iris’s teeth: apologies, explanations, justifications. A lifetime of standing beside. They curdled into silence. All she managed was, “The rehearsal tomorrow. Same time?

Pauline looked at her for a long moment. Then she gave a single, slow nod. It wasn’t forgiveness. It was a schedule. “Same time. Come in the room. Or don’t come at all.

She left. The door sighed shut behind her, the click of the latch a soft, precise period.

Iris sat in the gathering dark. The paper in her hand was warm now from her skin. She looked at it, a blank square in the gloom. A reply she hadn’t written. An answer she’d have to invent for herself. The streetlamp hummed. Somewhere, a pipe knocked. She closed her eyes and saw not the stage, not the script, but the chipped tile on a café floor twelve years gone. A small, irrevocable flaw in the pattern.

The office was a closet with a window. Iris shut the door, leaned her forehead against the cool wood, and listened to the building settle. A pipe knocked somewhere deep in the walls. The sound was hollow, a question no one answered.

She’d meant to work. To review the blocking for the next scene, the one where her character argued with her brother. The script lay on the desk, a neat rectangle under the lamplight. She couldn’t touch it. Her hands felt wrong—too light, as if the bones had been replaced with air. The rehearsal had ended hours ago. The students had filed out, their chatter a bright, distant stream she couldn’t enter. Pauline had given her a final look, not angry, not even disappointed. Just a quiet assessment, like a doctor noting a symptom. Then she’d turned and left, the echo of her heels swallowed by the corridor.

Iris walked to the window. The glass was cold against her palm. Harlem at night was a quilt of shadows and scattered gold. A lone taxi crawled down the street, its headlights cutting two frail cones in the dark. She’d performed for rooms this color. Bottle-green darkness pinpricked by cigarette cherries and the gleam of a sequined gown at a back table. She’d known how to hold that kind of attention. You gave them a shape to admire—the curve of a shoulder, the tilt of a chin, the sorrow in a minor key. It was a transaction. They took the beautiful sadness; you took the check.

This was different. Pauline wanted the thing behind the shape. The emptiness the shape contained.

Her throat tightened. Not with tears. It was a drier thing, a constriction. She went to the desk, opened the bottom drawer. The bottle of Gilbey’s was there, half-full, the gin catching the lamplight like pale oil. She poured two fingers into a cloudy glass she’d scavenged from the faculty lounge. The first swallow was a cold line of fire. The second didn’t burn at all. It just spread, a familiar numbness starting in her stomach.

It wasn’t working. The usual fog refused to rise. The edges of the room stayed sharp. The water stain on the ceiling, the one she’d been ignoring for weeks, resolved itself into the profile of a hawk, beak open in a silent shriek.

She sat in the wooden chair. It creaked. The sound was human, a complaint.

Her gaze drifted to the top drawer, the locked one. Inside were her teaching notes, a few old reviews clipped from newspapers, a dried-up fountain pen. And the key. A small, brass Yale key on a thin ring. It belonged to the filing cabinet in Arthur’s study. She’d pocketed it three months ago, during one of their stilted Sunday dinners. He’d been in the kitchen, mixing martinis. The key had been in the lock of the cabinet, left there like a dare. She’d taken it without a thought, slipping it into her dress pocket. A petty theft. A tiny act of rebellion that had made her heart hammer against her ribs for the rest of the evening.

She’d never used it. The thought of what was in that cabinet—the orderly files, the photographs, the letters—was a cold stone in her gut. It was one thing to know the stone was there. Another to lift it and look underneath.

The gin sat in her, inert. The numbness she wanted was on the other side of that drawer. Not in the bottle. In the knowing.

Her hand moved. It didn’t feel like a decision. It felt like something giving way. She pulled the top drawer open, rummaged past the paper clips and rubber bands, and her fingers closed on the key. It was warm from the confined dark.

She stood. The office walls seemed to lean in. The hawk on the ceiling watched.

It wasn’t about Arthur’s file, not really. It was about the blank paper in the rehearsal room. The silence Pauline had asked her to fill. The silence she’d built her life around, polished and presented as art. She’d thought she was an expert in silence. Now she understood she’d only been its curator. She’d framed it, lit it, put it on a stage. She had never lived inside it.

The key bit into her palm. The metal had a taste, coppery and old, though she wasn’t licking it.

A memory surfaced, unbidden. Not Paris. Later. 1942. The back room of The Morocco. The air thick with smoke and fear. A man she knew only as Leo—not the Broadway producer, another Leo, thinner, with ink stains on his fingers—handing her sheet music. His touch was brief, impersonal. For the boys overseas, he’d said, his voice a low murmur under the band’s brassy rendition of “Chattanooga Choo Choo.” She’d taken the papers, slipped them into her folio. The lyrics were innocuous. Moonlight, longing, a train whistle in the distance. But the chord progressions he’d scribbled in the margins told a different story. Locations. Times. A route.

She’d sung those songs. In a sequined dress, under a pink spotlight, she’d sung the locations of safe houses. She’d turned troop movements into a bluesy bridge. She’d encrypted the war into three-minute pop songs, and the audiences had swayed and sighed, hearing only romance.

She’d been so clever. So terribly, dangerously clever.

And she’d kept every piece of sheet music. A record of her cleverness. Souvenirs.

Arthur had found them. During one of his periodic “archiving” sessions in her old apartment. He’d been delighted. Your patriotic period, he’d called it, his eyes alight with a new kind of calculation. He hadn’t understood the code. He hadn’t needed to. He understood that she had secrets, and that she’d written them down. That was the leverage. The specifics were irrelevant. The fact of the hidden text was enough.

The key in her hand was suddenly heavy. A ridiculous weight for such a small thing.

Her artistic failure today wasn’t about technique. It was about language. She had spent years speaking in a code only the initiated could understand. A code that could be denied. A code that kept her safe. Pauline was asking her to speak in plain English. To stand in a room and say, with her body, with her silence: This is what I did. This is what we did.

The telegram in the play wasn’t a reply to a safety complaint. It was a reply to every coded song she’d ever sung. It said: We heard you. We know.

Iris put the key on the desk. It made a faint click on the wood.

She wouldn’t go to Arthur’s study. She didn’t need to see the files to know what was in them. Her own handwriting. Her own careful, cowardly poetry.

The bottle of gin was still there. She looked at it, then pushed it aside. The numbness wasn’t the answer. The answer was in the cold clarity that was currently turning her stomach to water. The answer was the hawk on the ceiling, screaming without sound.

She picked up the Port Chicago script. The words were still a gray smear. She turned to a blank page at the back. She took her dried-up fountain pen, the one that hadn’t worked in years, and held it like a stylus. She didn’t write. She just pressed the point into the paper, making a small, deliberate dent. Then another. She wasn’t writing words. She was making a record of pressure. A braille of guilt.

Outside, a siren warbled, distant and fading. The city went on with its night. In her closet of an office, under the silent hawk, Iris Finch sat and made marks that no one would ever read.
