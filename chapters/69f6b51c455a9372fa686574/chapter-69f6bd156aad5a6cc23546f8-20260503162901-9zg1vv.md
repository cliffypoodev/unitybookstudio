The pages felt slick in her hands. Not like the heavy bond of a contract, but cheap and thin, already curling at the corners from the damp in the air. Iris stood in the middle of the living room, the script for Port Chicago held away from her body as if it might stain her dress. A drop of rain traced a slow, cold path from her temple to her jaw. She’d forgotten to close the window in the bedroom. The smell of wet pavement and distant gasoline seeped in, a gray smell. On the arm of the sofa, a single brown feather lay curled—from a pillow maybe, or dragged in on a shoe. It had no business being there.

She couldn’t start reading. Not yet. The words Pauline had spoken in the rehearsal hall kept circling, a flat, declarative loop. A cryptographer. It was the kind of word that should have felt like an accusation. It didn’t. It felt like a diagnosis. Precise. Terminal. She looked down at the top page. The character’s name was Evelyn. Not Iris. Evelyn.

The key turned in the apartment door.

Her body moved before her mind did. A smooth, practiced pivot toward the sideboard, the script pressed flat against the polished wood as she reached for the cut-glass decanter. The gin poured with a sound like a slow sigh. Her hands were steady. That was the first lesson wasn’t it? The body tells the story. The mind just follows along, chattering nonsense.

Arthur came in with the weather still clinging to his overcoat. He shrugged it off hung it on the stand by the door with a precise, familiar motion. His eyes went to the glass in her hand, then to her face, then past her to the room. Taking inventory.

“You’re home early.” He said it neutral, a statement of fact.

“The class ended.” She took a sip. The gin was cheap, bitter. It left a chemical warmth at the back of her throat. “Rain.”

He nodded, moving to his desk—a heavy, dark thing that dominated the far corner. He opened a drawer, withdrew a ledger, placed it just so on the green blotter. Every movement had a specific, weightless efficiency. It was the efficiency of a man who never doubted the purpose of an object, or his right to use it.

“How is the institute?” he asked, not looking up as he unscrewed the cap of his fountain pen.

“It’s a school.” She traced the rim of her glass. A tiny, hairline chip near the base caught her fingertip. She’d never noticed it before. “Children learning to pretend. It’s fine.”

“And Carter?”

The name hung between them. Iris watched a bead of condensation slide down the side of her glass, aiming for the chip. “Professional.”

Arthur made a note in the ledger. The scratch of the nib was very loud. “Good. Professional is manageable.” He looked up then, his gaze cool and assessing. “I had lunch with Henderson.”

Something in her stomach tightened. Not a clench, not a flutter. A slow, cold hardening, like plaster setting. She didn’t say anything. She watched the rain smear the window into a blur of gray and brown.

“He’s pleased with your… compliance thus far. The teaching position is seen as a positive step. A return to respectable society.” Arthur leaned back in his chair, the leather sighing under him. “But they need a demonstration, Iris. Good faith. The committee thrives on names.”

She turned from the window. The feather on the sofa arm seemed to tremble in a draft she couldn’t feel. “I gave them names. From the Paris days. You had the list. They were already in exile or dead.”

“Those were appetizers.” He set his pen down, aligning it perfectly parallel to the ledger’s edge. “They want a main course. Someone current. Someone with connections that matter now.”

The room was too warm. The radiator in the corner ticked, a persistent metallic heartbeat. She thought of the students in Pauline’s rehearsal hall—the young man doing the Othello speech, his voice shaking with a fury too big for his body. The sharp, intelligent eyes of the girl playing Emilia. Their faces were a blur, but their energy wasn’t. It was a thick thing in that room, reckless and alive.

“There’s no one,” she said. She lowered the words to something thin, airy. A performance of calm. “I sing in clubs. I teach a class. My connections are to bartenders and has-beens.”

Arthur smiled. It was a small, dry movement of his lips. “You’re rehearsing a new play. Pauline Carter’s play. A political piece, from what I understand. About a naval disaster. A racial incident.” He let the words sit. “That’s a room full of connections, Iris. Actors. Designers. The people who fund that sort of thing. The committee is very interested in the cultural networks. The flow of ideas. And money.”

The script on the sideboard seemed to glow, a white rectangle in the dim room. He’d known. Of course he’d known. He had someone watching, or Pauline had a leak in her company, or he’d simply had her followed. The method didn’t matter. The fact did. Her new cage had bars he’d already measured.

“I’ve agreed to be in it,” she said. A strange, defiant truth. “It’s a small part. The wife of an officer. Complicit.”

“Perfect.” Arthur didn’t miss a beat. “Then you’re perfectly placed. You attend rehearsals. You listen. You note who is passionate. Who makes speeches during breaks. Who seems… ideologically driven. Carter herself, certainly. But others. The backers. Anyone from the Institute’s board who takes a special interest.” He stood up came around the desk. He didn’t approach her, just stood there, a silhouette against the rainy window. “It’s not a betrayal, Iris. It’s an inoculation. You give them a name or two—peripheral people, perhaps—and you secure your own safety. You prove your loyalty is here, now. To your country. Not to some romanticized past.”

Loyalty. The word tasted like the gin. Chemical. False. Loyalty was a quiet hand on her back in a Left Bank café, a shared notebook, a harmony sung soft into the dark. Loyalty was what she’d shattered on a producer’s desk in 1937. What she was being asked to shatter again felt like its own separate thing. Not betrayal. Erosion.

“And if I can’t?” The question left her mouth before she could shape it into something less revealing.

Arthur’s expression didn’t change. He walked to the sideboard, picked up the script she’d left there. He didn’t open it. He just held it, weighing it in his hand. “Then we have a different conversation. We can talk about the sustainability of this teaching position. We can talk about the renewal of your work visa. We can talk about the interest certain editors might have in a series of letters from the war years. Not just love letters, Iris. The other ones. The careful, clever ones.” He met her eyes. His were a pale washed-out blue. The color of a winter sky just before snow. “We protect what’s ours. That’s the transaction. You’ve always understood transactions.”

He placed the script back on the sideboard, exactly where it had been, but now it looked different. Tainted. He returned to his ledger, his back to her. The conversation was over.

Iris finished her gin. The heat was gone, leaving only the bitterness. She looked at the script, then at Arthur’s straight, unyielding back. She thought of Evelyn, the officer’s wife. A woman who knew the weights were wrong and served the lemonade anyway. A process. The daily, quiet mechanics of looking away.

She walked to the sofa, picked up the stray brown feather. It was insubstantial, almost nothing. She carried it to the window, opened the sash a little wider, and let it go. The wet wind snatched it, whirling it down into the gray canyon of the street, out of sight immediately. A useless, irrelevant detail. Gone.

She closed the window. The lock clicked with a final, solid sound. Then she went back to the sideboard, picked up the pages, and this time, she began to read.

The radiator in Pauline’s office hissed, a thin, constant complaint. Iris stood just inside the door, the script for Port Chicago rolled tight in her fist like a baton. The room was small dominated by a scarred oak desk buried under teetering stacks of plays. A single green-shaded lamp cast a pool of jaundiced light, leaving the corners in deep shadow. On the wall behind the desk, someone had pinned a flyer for a Paul Robeson concert at Peekskill. The edges were frayed, the red lettering faded to pink by the sun.

Pauline didn’t look up from the page she was marking with a red pencil. She wore a simple black turtleneck, the sleeves pushed to her elbows. The line of her jaw was very clean in the low light. “Close the door.”

Iris pushed it shut with her hip. The click of the latch was final. She remained standing.

“Sit,” Pauline said, still not looking up.

“I’d rather stand.”

“Suit yourself.” The red pencil moved, a quick, decisive slash. “Rehearsals start Monday. Seven PM. Don’t be late. We don’t have the luxury of star temperament here.” She spoke, the sound flat, administrative. “You’ll need to be off-book by the fifteenth. Evelyn is a quiet role, but she’s the linchpin. If she doesn’t work, the third act falls apart.”

The pencil stopped. Pauline leaned back in her chair, the wood groaning. She finally looked at Iris. Her expression was unreadable, a closed door. “Of course he does.”

“He says the committee is interested. In the networks. The people involved.”

“I’m sure they are.” Pauline set the pencil down, aligning it neatly beside a tin of paperclips. “Let them be interested. It’s a play. Not a manifesto.”

“Is there a difference?”

A muscle flexed in Pauline’s temple. “Not to them. But there should be to you.” She gestured to the script in Iris’s hand. “Have you read it?”

“Parts.”

“All of it. You need to understand the whole mechanism to play your cog.” She folded her hands on the desk. Her fingers were long, the knuckles pronounced. A silver ring, a simple band, gleamed dully on her right hand. Iris had never seen it before. “Why did you say yes?”

The question hung in the stale, paper-scented air. Iris’s mouth was dry. She thought of the gin, the cheap warmth of it. “You offered.”

“I’ve offered you things before. You had a talent for refusal.” Pauline’s gaze was steady, a physical weight. “This isn’t a supper club gig, Iris. This isn’t a three-minute torch song where you can hide behind a pretty melody. This is a room. A live audience. A story about real men who died because the navy decided their black skin made them expendable. There’s no encryption thick enough for that. You either tell the truth, or you get off my stage.”

“I know what it’s about.”

“Do you?” Pauline stood up, turning to look out the small, grimy window that overlooked the fire escape. Her reflection was a ghost in the glass. “You know the facts. July 1944. Port Chicago, California. Munitions explosion. Three hundred and twenty men killed. Two hundred and twenty of them black. The white officers got leave. The black survivors were ordered back to work, then court-martialed for mutiny when they refused.” She recited it like a police report. “But this,” she tapped the script on her desk, “isn’t about facts. It’s about the wife who packs her husband’s lunch while he signs the orders. It’s about the woman who hears the news on the radio and turns the volume down because the dinner party is about to start. It’s about the silence that isn’t empty. It’s full. It’s heavy. It has a shape.” She turned back. “You’ve lived in that silence for twelve years. That’s why I asked you. Not despite it. Because of it.”

The words landed, a series of small, precise blows. Iris felt them in her throat, a constriction. She looked away, her eyes catching on a crack in the plaster ceiling. It branched like a lightning bolt, stopping just short of the light fixture. “He wants names. From the company.”

Pauline was quiet for a long moment. The radiator hissed. Somewhere in the building, a pipe clanged. “And what will you give him?”

“I don’t know.”

“You’ll have to choose.” Pauline’s voice was softer now, almost tired. “That’s the thing about complicity, Iris. It’s a series of choices. Small ones. Do I look away? Do I change the subject? Do I hand over a name to keep my own head down? Each one feels like nothing. A grain of sand. But they accumulate. They become a wall you build around yourself. And one day you wake up and you’re inside it, and you can’t remember what the sky looks like.”

Iris unrolled the script, looked at the first page. EVELYN, 34, wears a floral apron. She is humming. The stage direction was a knife. “You think I’m already inside.”

“I think you have the blueprints.” Pauline came around the desk, leaning against the front of it. She was close now. Iris could smell her soap—lemongrass, something clean and astringent. “This play is a chance to dismantle it. Brick by brick. In public. But it will cost you. Arthur. The committee. Whatever fragile peace you’ve bargained for. It will cost you that.”

“And if I can’t pay?”

“Then you shouldn’t have walked in here.” Pauline pushed off the desk, moving to the door. She opened it, a clear dismissal. The sounds of the building filtered in—a distant piano, a burst of laughter from down the hall. “Monday. Seven PM. Read the whole thing. Know what you’re agreeing to.”

Iris didn’t move. She stared at the script, the neat columns of dialogue. A strange, hollow calm had settled in her chest. It wasn’t peace. It was the silence before a note is sung, the air in the lungs, the potential for sound. “You still wear that ring.”

Pauline’s hand went to the silver band, a brief, unconscious touch. Her face closed. “It’s just a ring.”

“It’s the one I bought you. At the flea market on Rue de Seine. It cost ten francs. You said it would turn your finger green.”

“It did.” Pauline’s voice was barely audible. “For a week.”

A memory, sharp and unbidden: Pauline laughing, holding her hand up to the Paris sun, the cheap silver gleaming against her skin. The smell of roasted chestnuts from a vendor’s cart. The way the light had caught the dust motes in the attic room they shared. It was a stupid, irrelevant detail. It had nothing to do with munitions or committees or choices. It was just a moment lost.

“Why keep it?” Iris asked.

Pauline looked at her, and for a second, the mask of weary professionalism slipped. Something raw and unguarded flickered in her eyes—not anger, not forgiveness. A kind of recognition, old and worn smooth by time. “To remember,” she said simply. “That some things do leave a mark.”

She held the door open, waiting. The hallway beyond was bright, alive with the chaotic energy of the institute. Iris looked from the script in her hands to Pauline’s face, then stepped across the threshold. The door shut softly behind her, the click of the latch like a period at the end of a sentence.

The stage was a vast, dark mouth. Iris stood at its lip, the script for Port Chicago rolled tight in one fist like a baton. Her other hand traced the rough velvet of the main curtain, a texture like dried moss. The house lights were off, but the ghost light—a single bare bulb on a stand at center stage—cast a frail, yellow pool. It was enough to see the dust motes swimming in the air, a slow, silent snowfall in reverse.

She’d left the apartment without a word. Arthur was still at his desk, bent over columns of figures that meant security, or danger, or both. The click of the door behind her had been a small, clean sound. The walk through the rain-slick streets had been long, her heels tapping a rhythm that said nothing at all.

Now, the emptiness was a physical presence. It smelled of old wood, of dust and sweat and the faint, sweet tang of lemon oil from the floorboards. A forgotten coffee cup sat on a prop table stage left, a brown tidemark halfway down its side. She walked toward the ghost light, her footsteps echoing in the cavernous dark. The sound was wrong—too loud, too singular. On a stage, you were never supposed to be alone. You were always part of an echo.

She stopped in the circle of light. Unrolled the script. The pages were already soft at the edges from her handling. Evelyn’s lines were underlined in pencil. Eight pages. One monologue. I didn’t know. The weight allocations are off. She knows, and she says nothing.

Pauline’s voice in her head, flat and final. A cryptographer.

Iris let the script fall to the floor. It landed with a whisper. She looked out at the black void of the auditorium. Five hundred empty seats, watching. A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: the smell of a specific brand of hair tonic her father used, a sharp, medicinal scent that always meant he was going out. It had nothing to do with anything here.

Her throat was tight. Not with tears—with something drier, more granular. A residue.

She began to move. Not as Evelyn. Not as anyone. She just walked the perimeter of the light, her arms loose at her sides. The stage directions were simple. Evelyn enters the kitchen. She picks up the letter from the table. She reads it. She does not sit. She does not cry. The simplicity was the trap. Every silence in those directions was a question. What filled it?

Iris stopped. Picked up an imaginary letter. The paper was crisp in her mind. Official letterhead. She looked down at it. Her face felt like a mask, cold and stiff.

Nothing came. No feeling, no understanding. Just the words on a page she’d memorized. A perfect, empty vessel.

A sound cracked the silence—a distant door closing, far off in the building’s depths. A janitor maybe. The sound should have startled her. It didn’t. It was a pinprick in a vast numbness. She lowered her hand. The imaginary letter drifted away.

This was the failure. This was what Arthur bought and sold. A woman who could stand in a light and feel nothing at all.

She turned from the audience, facing upstage into the deeper dark. The back wall was a confusion of ropes and pulleys, dim shapes in the gloom. One of the ropes, a heavy hemp line for a sandbag was fraying at the end, little blond fibers sticking out like untidy hair.

Her breath hitched. A stupid, physical thing. A crack in the plaster.

Then her body moved without permission. A slow curl forward from the waist, hands coming to rest on her knees. The position was inelegant, undignified. A stitch in her side from the walk. She stayed there, head down, staring at the dust between her shoes. A long, slow breath in. It tasted of dust and defeat.

When she straightened, her eyes were hot and dry. The void of the auditorium seemed to pulse.

She spoke to the dark. Not Evelyn’s words. Her own. A raw, quiet thing.

“There was a club on Rue de la Huchette. The walls were painted like a forest. Badly. The green was too bright.” The way she said it was a stranger’s. Hoarse from disuse. “You could smell the river through the floorboards when the wind changed. Damp stone. Rot.”

She took a step. Another.

“I sang there three nights a week. For meals. Sometimes for francs if the owner was drunk enough to feel generous. Pauline wrote the arrangements on napkins. She had a way of tapping her pencil when she was thinking—a quick, light staccato, like Morse code.” Iris brought her own hand up, mimed the tapping against her thigh. The rhythm was off. She’d forgotten it.

“The song they liked was ‘Les Feuilles Mortes.’ I hated it. Sentimental. But the way she wrote the bridge… it wasn’t about dead leaves. It was about burning them. The chord change was a match strike.” She hummed a few notes, low in her throat. They dissolved into the silence, meaningless.

“He came backstage. Arthur. Clean suit. American shoes. He said he could get me a real recording. A proper studio. He said the song could mean something else entirely. Something safer. Something that sold.” She looked down at her empty hands. “He had a contract in his briefcase. The paper was so thick. I remember the sound it made when he laid it on the dressing table. Not a rustle. A thump.”

She was circling the ghost light now, a planet in a tiny, yellow orbit.

“I didn’t sign it that night. I took it to her. To Pauline. We were in that attic room with the window that never closed. She read it under the single bulb. She didn’t tap her pencil. She was very still. Then she looked at me and said, ‘It’s a translation. They’re going to translate you into a language you don’t speak.’” Iris stopped. The words, more real than the dust. “I told her it was just a song. I told her we could be careful. I told her a lot of things that weren’t true.”

The silence that followed was different. It was listening.

“I signed it the next morning. At a café. The ink was blue. I used his pen.” She let out a short, sharp breath that wasn’t a laugh. “Twelve years of translations.”

From the dark house, a new sound. Not a door. A soft, deliberate scrape. A shoe on concrete.

Iris froze. Her heart was a sudden, hard fist in her chest. She peered into the blackness, seeing nothing but shapes.

“Who’s there?”

No answer. The silence was absolute again. Had she imagined it? The building settling? Her own pulse in her ears?

Then, a voice. Low, male. From the shadows near the exit door. “Don’t let me interrupt. It’s a compelling audition.”

The agent. Henderson. He stepped forward, just to the edge of the light’s reach. He was in a trench coat, dry. He must have been there for some time. His hands were in his pockets. His expression was unreadable in the gloom.

Cold spread from Iris’s core to her fingertips. She didn’t move. “This is a private rehearsal.”

“Is it?” He took another step. Now she could see the bland curiosity on his face. “Sounded like a confession. Historical context. Very helpful for the committee’s understanding.” He tilted his head. “The ‘translation’ metaphor is especially apt. We’re in the business of ensuring accurate translations ourselves. Making sure the public hears the right version of events.”

He was close enough now that she could see the fine wrinkles at the corners of his eyes. They didn’t look like laugh lines.

“You followed me.”

“I was in the neighborhood. Wanted to see the famous institute.” His gaze swept the dark balcony. “The play you’re rehearsing. This Port Chicago. It’s a tragedy, I understand. A lot of young lives lost. A narrative of… negligence. Maybe malice.” He looked back at her. “Stories like that need careful handling. The wrong emphasis, and they become incendiary. We wouldn’t want a worthy artistic effort to be misunderstood as agitation.”

The threat was velvet-wrapped. Perfect.

Iris said nothing. Her mouth was dust.

He smiled, a thin curve of his lips. “But you know all about managing narratives, Miss Finch. Your husband tells me you’re eager to cooperate. To provide the committee with a clearer picture of the artistic community here.” He paused, letting the word community linger. “I look forward to our next conversation. After you’ve had more time to… rehearse.”

He gave a slight, almost courteous nod. Then he turned and walked back into the shadows. The exit door opened with a sigh of compressed air, spilling a brief rectangle of hall light onto the floor. Then it closed, and the dark was complete again.

Iris stood alone in the circle of light. The imaginary letter, the memory of the attic, the taste of cheap gin—all of it was gone. Replaced by a new, precise cold.

He hadn’t come for names. Not yet. He’d come for the play. To see it. To mark it.

She bent down, slowly, and picked up the script from the floor. She smoothed the pages against her leg. A corner was bent. She folded it back, a sharp, neat crease.

Then she walked off the stage, leaving the ghost light burning in the empty dark.
