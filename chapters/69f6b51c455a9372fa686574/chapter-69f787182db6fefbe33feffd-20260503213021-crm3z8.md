The rain tapped a steady, irregular rhythm against the windowpane, a syncopated counterpoint to the metronome in her head. Iris stood at the living room window, her thumbnail tracing the cold, smooth wood of the sill where the water ring had been. She’d polished it out that morning with a paste of baking soda, the circular ghost erased. The action felt significant in a way she couldn’t name—a small, futile correction. Outside, the brownstones across the street were bleary smudges in the grey afternoon light. A man with an umbrella hurried past, his shoulders hunched against the drizzle, a dark shape dissolving into the gloom. She watched until he was gone. There was nothing else to look at.

The doorbell rang, a sharp, two-toned chime that made her flinch.

Langston was at the office, “doing damage control,” he’d said. She was alone. She smoothed the front of her wool skirt, a nervous habit, and went to answer it.

The man on the landing was a stranger shaped like a memory. He was older, of course—they all were—with a neat grey beard and wire-rimmed glasses that magnified his eyes. He wore a dark overcoat spotted with rain, and he held a brown paper parcel tied with string. He smelled of damp wool and, faintly, of old paper.

“Miss Finch,” he said. His voice was softer than she remembered, with a Parisian accent worn smooth at the edges by time. “You may not recall. Henri. From the bookstall by the Seine. The one with the cat who slept in the philosophy section.

The cat. A ginger tom with a chewed ear. The memory arrived not as an image but as a sensation: the rough weave of a wicker chair in the sun, the taste of bitter coffee, the weight of a book in her lap, and that cat purring against her ankle. A perfect, pointless afternoon.

“Henri,” she said. The name felt strange in her mouth. “Of course. Please, come in.

She took his coat, heavy with moisture, and hung it on the stand. The parcel he placed carefully on the hall table. He followed her into the living room, his gaze taking in the room with a slow, appraising sweep—the upright piano, the shelf of untouched sheet music, the too-perfect arrangement of chrysanthemums in a vase. A room staged for a life.

“You have a lovely home,” he said. It was the polite, empty thing anyone would say.

“Thank you. Can I get you some tea? It’s a miserable day.

“That would be kind.”

In the kitchen, her hands moved automatically: kettle to tap, gas ring lit, the quiet clink of china. The familiar ritual calmed the quick, shallow beat of her heart. An old contact. From Paris. Her mind snagged on the preposition. Not a friend. A contact. Someone who moved in the same circles, who sold the pamphlets and journals, who knew the faces. Why was he here? Langston hadn’t mentioned a visitor. Cross certainly hadn’t. This was something else, then. Something off-book.

She carried the tray back in. Henri was standing by the piano, not touching it, just looking at the framed photograph on top. It was a studio portrait of Iris from ’44, in a sequined gown, microphone in hand, mouth open in mid-song. The Canary.

“You look very different here,” he said, turning. “Not worse. Just… a different person.

“It was a different time.” She set the tray down. “Please, sit.

They settled in the armchairs by the cold fireplace. The tea steamed between them, a fragile barrier of civility. He sipped his, black, no sugar. She held her cup just for the warmth, the heat seeping into her palms.

“I was passing through the city,” he began, setting his cup back on its saucer with a precise click. “A bookseller’s conference, if you can believe such a thing exists. I remembered Langston’s name from the old days. I found his office in the telephone directory. He gave me this address.” He smiled, a thin curve of the lips. “He said you would be at home. That you were… between engagements.

The phrase was a polite shiv. She felt it slide in. “Yes. I’m teaching now. At a theatre institute in Harlem.

“Ah.” He nodded slowly, as if this confirmed something. “Pauline’s place.

The sound of her name in his mouth, so casual, so knowing, sent a current through Iris’s nerves. Her throat tightened. She took a sip of tea to disguise it. The liquid was too hot; it scalded the tip of her tongue. “You know Pauline.

“Everyone knew Pauline. Or wanted to.” He leaned back, his fingers steepled. “A force of nature. Is she still so… uncompromising?

“More so.”

“I can imagine. The war hardened everyone. Or broke them.” He looked past her, toward the rainy window. “I heard about her work here. The institute. A bold project. Dangerous, perhaps.

“It’s a school,” Iris said, too quickly. The defensiveness in her voice was a flare. She tried to soften it. “It’s important work.

“I am sure it is.” His magnified eyes found hers again. They were a pale, watery blue. “It is good you are there. After everything. A kind of homecoming.

There was no judgment in his tone. Only a flat, observational quality that was worse. She put her cup down. The silence stretched, filled only by the rain and the slow tick of the clock on the mantel. She counted three ticks. Four.

“Why are you really here, Henri?”

He didn’t seem offended by the bluntness. He reached for the parcel from the hall and placed it on the low table between them. “A gift. I was clearing some old stock. I found these. I thought you should have them.

With careful fingers, he untied the string and folded back the brown paper. Inside were three slender volumes, their bindings faded. Not books. Journals. Le Phare. Pauline’s journal. The radical little paper she’d published from that cramped apartment, the one that argued aesthetics were politics, that a blues progression could be a manifesto. Iris hadn’t seen one in twelve years.

Her hand hovered over them. She didn’t touch. “You kept these.

“A bookseller keeps many things. Sometimes they are just paper. Sometimes they are evidence.” He said it lightly, but the word hung in the air. Evidence. “This run is quite rare now. Most were confiscated. Or burned.

“Burned?”

“When the trouble started. After you left.” He took another sip of tea, his gaze steady over the rim of the cup. “You were very lucky, you know. The timing of that American contract. Like a door closing just as the storm hit.

A cold prickle started at the base of her spine. “What trouble?

“The roundups. The suspicions. ’37 was a fever dream, and ’38 was the waking hangover. The Spanish war going badly, the Popular Front crumbling. The police took a new interest in certain circles. Foreigners. Artists. People with the wrong pamphlets on their shelves.” He nodded toward the journals. “Pauline’s crowd was of particular interest. Her patron, the Swiss gentleman… his money was said to have questionable origins. A taint of Moscow. You know how these things go.

She did know. She knew exactly how these things went. A slow, creeping chill was settling in her stomach, unrelated to the weather.

“They scattered,” Henri continued, his voice a low, even monotone. “Some to Spain, a futile gesture. Some to the countryside. Some just… disappeared into the system. Pauline stayed longest, of course. Stubborn. But even she had to go to ground eventually. I heard she was in a camp for a time. Gurs. A miserable place.

The words landed like stones in still water. Ripples spread out, touching facts she thought were fixed. She had left for a record contract. She had abandoned Pauline for safety and fame. That was the story. The only story. A clean, selfish betrayal.

But what if the door had been held open for her? What if the contract, arriving like a miracle, had been a ticket out someone had made sure she took?

“You never wondered?” Henri asked, his head tilted. “Why the offer came so suddenly? Why the terms were so generous for an unknown singer in a left-bank club?

“I was talented,” she whispered. The words sounded pathetic, a child’s defense.

“You were. But talent is cheap. Connections are currency.” He leaned forward, his voice dropping further. “Pauline had a gift for strategy. She saw the weather changing before any of us. She had… protectors. People who believed her work was vital. And they believed you were vital to her. A vulnerability. A lovely, singing liability.

The room tilted slightly. Iris gripped the arms of her chair. The patterned fabric was rough under her fingertips. She focused on that sensation—the tangible weave of the upholstery—to keep from floating away.

“What are you saying?”

“I am saying you were lucky,” he repeated, but the word had changed shape. It was no longer about fortune. It was about design. “The roundups started not a month after your train pulled out of the Gare du Nord. Pauline’s apartment was raided the following week. They took everything. The press, the manuscripts, the subscription lists. If you had been there…” He spread his hands, a small, eloquent gesture. “You would not have sung for very much longer, I think.

The silence that followed was absolute. The rain had stopped. The clock ticked, each second a hammer blow on the hollow inside her. She saw it not as a memory, but as a new, terrible projection: herself, bewildered, in a stark police station. Pauline, grim-faced, refusing to look at her. The two of them tangled in the same net. No contract. No escape. An end, not a beginning.

All these years, she had carried the guilt of leaving. The weight of the choice she believed she’d made for her own shallow ambition.

What if it hadn’t been her choice at all?

“She never said,” Iris managed, her voice rough.

“Of course she never said.” Henri’s smile was thin, sad. “What would have been the point? To make you a martyr to gratitude? To tie you to her with a debt? That was not her way. Her way was to let you go, to let you hate her for it, if that’s what it took to get you clear.” He stood up, brushing a speck of lint from his trousers. “I have overstayed. The tea was excellent.

He moved toward the hall. Iris stood, her legs unsteady. She followed him, a sleepwalker.

At the door, he shrugged on his still-damp overcoat. He looked at her, his magnified eyes soft. “Keep the journals. Or burn them. They are yours. A piece of the story you perhaps did not have.

He let himself out. The door closed with a soft, final click.

Iris stood in the quiet hall. From the living room, the three journals lay exposed on the table, their faded covers a dull red, like old blood. She didn’t go back in. She walked to the kitchen instead, her movements precise. She filled a glass with water from the tap and drank it all, though she wasn’t thirsty. She set the glass in the sink. Then she looked at her hands. They were perfectly still. Not a tremor.

That was the strangest thing of all.

The silence pooled between them again, thick as the steam from their cups. Henri’s words—a kind of homecoming—hung in the air, an accusation dressed as sentiment. Iris focused on the pale ring the base of her cup left on the polished surface of the side table. She would have to move it. In a moment.

“It was a long time ago,” she said. Her voice sounded thin, unconvincing even to herself.

“Not so long.” He reached for the brown paper parcel on the floor beside his chair. The string made a soft, rasping sound as he untied it. “I brought you something. A small thing. I thought you might appreciate it.

He withdrew a slim, cloth-bound journal. The cover was a faded burgundy, the edges of the pages foxed with age. He placed it on the table between them. It lay there like a sleeping animal.

“What is it?”

“Open it.”

She didn’t want to touch it. Her hands felt clumsy, disconnected. She picked it up. The cloth was soft, worn. She opened to the first page. It was a program, handwritten in elegant, sloping script. Soirée Littéraire. 12 Juin, 1937. Readings: P. Carter, A. Breton (invité), I. Finch. Her own name, there in that ink. Below it, a list of songs. ‘Strange Fruit.’ ‘Les Feuilles Mortes.’ Her throat closed. She remembered the smoky back room of the café, the close heat of bodies, the dangerous, thrilling silence after the last note of ‘Strange Fruit’ died away. Pauline’s hand on her shoulder afterward, a brief, solid pressure.

“Where did you get this?”

“It was in a box of ephemera I purchased from an estate. The widow of a gendarme, if you can believe it. He had quite the collection.” Henri sipped his tea. “I remembered you were on the bill. It seemed… significant.

Significant. A piece of evidence. She turned the pages. They were blank after the program. Empty. Waiting. She closed the cover. The rain beat a little harder against the glass.

“Thank you,” she said, because it was the thing one said.

“It is nothing.” He waved a hand. “A fragment. We are all fragments now, I think. Scattered.” He looked at her, his gaze steady behind the magnifying lenses. “You were lucky, you know. With your timing.

“My timing.”

“The American contract. The exit visa. It all came through so neatly at the end of that summer.” He said it lightly, as if commenting on a fortunate turn in the weather. “You left in… September, was it?

“Late August.”

“Ah. Even better.” He nodded. “The roundups started in October. The Prefecture had new lists. Names from the journals, the salons, the backroom readings. Anyone with connections to the Spanish groups, the anti-fascist committees. Pauline’s crowd… they were on several lists. They scattered to the winds. Or worse.

The word roundups landed in the room with a physical weight. It had a sound to it—the slam of a van door, the echo of boots on cobblestones. Iris’s fingers tightened on the journal. The rough cloth grain bit into her skin.

“I didn’t know.”

“Of course not. How could you? You were already on the ship, singing for your supper.” He didn’t say it cruelly. It was a simple statement of fact. “It was a chaotic time. Communications broke down. People disappeared into the countryside, or over borders. A few went to the camps. We did not know then what the camps were. We thought they were… holding pens.

The ice in her gin last night had made a specific cracking sound, like a tiny bone breaking. She heard it now, inside her head. She set the journal down, aligning its edge perfectly with the table’s corner.

“Pauline had resources. The Swiss patron, remember? And her own cleverness. She got out. To London first, I heard, then Lisbon. A long, difficult route. She lost everything, of course. The journal, the press, her flat. All of it.” He leaned forward, his voice dropping, though they were alone. “It changed her, I am told. The war changes everyone, but that kind of flight… it strips you. It makes you hard. Or it breaks you completely. Pauline, I think, chose hardness.

Iris saw the woman in the rehearsal hall, a pillar of contained fury. The sharp angles of her, the eyes that missed nothing. Not a warmth lost, but a warmth excised. Surgically. She felt a sudden, vertiginous lurch in her stomach, as if the floor had tilted. All these years, the story she told herself was simple: she had chosen career over love. She had been selfish, cowardly, ambitious. She had broken Pauline’s heart for a record deal and a first-class cabin.

What if it wasn’t that? What if her cowardice had been her salvation? What if Langston’s slick deal-making, his rushed contracts and pressured signatures, hadn’t just stolen her from a lover, but pulled her from a burning building a moment before the roof fell in?

The thought was monstrous. It inverted the moral universe. It made her betrayal a deliverance. It made Langston not a thief, but a rescuer. She couldn’t hold the idea; it slipped away, too slippery and awful to grasp.

“Why are you telling me this?” The question came out raw.

Henri sat back, his expression unreadable. “History is not a single story. It is a mosaic. Sometimes we look at our piece and think we understand the picture. Sometimes it is useful to see another piece.” He finished his tea. “And because you are back in her orbit. It seemed… prudent for you to have the context.

Prudent. A warning, then. Wrapped in nostalgia and tied with string.

He stood, smoothing the front of his waistcoat. “I have taken too much of your time.

She rose on unsteady legs. The room seemed too bright, the details too sharp—the pattern in the rug, the dust motes swirling in a grey beam of window light. She fetched his coat. It was still damp, heavy in her hands.

At the door, he turned. The parcel string was looped around his fingers. “The journal is yours. Keep it. Or burn it. As you see fit.

Then he was gone, descending the stairs with quiet, measured steps. She closed the door and locked it. The click of the bolt was absurdly loud.

She walked back to the living room but didn’t sit. She stood in the center of the space, arms wrapped around herself. The burgundy journal sat on the table. A fragment. She picked it up again, turned to the page with her name. I. Finch. The ‘I’ was a proud, lonely capital. She had signed the contract with Langston two weeks after this soirée. He’d arrived in Paris like a deus ex machina, all smiles and promises and talk of destiny. She’d been flattered, terrified, tempted. Pauline had argued, her voice low and fierce in their cramped bedroom. This is a choice, Iris. Not between me and a career. Between truth and a performance of truth. They will put you in a cage and call it a stage.

She had called Pauline jealous. She had packed her suitcase while Pauline smoked on the fire escape, her back a rigid line against the Paris sky.

Now, another sentence formed in her mind, cold and clear: He got me out before the arrest warrants.

She dropped the journal as if it had burned her. It fell to the rug with a soft thump.

The rain whispered against the window. Somewhere in the building, a pipe groaned. She went to the piano, not to play, but to look at the photograph. The Canary, mouth open forever in a silent, sparkling cry. A different person. A person who didn’t know what was coming. A person who might have been saved, for all the wrong reasons, by the worst possible man.

She traced the curve of the silver frame. It left a smudge on the glass. She would have to polish that, too.

The rain had stopped. The street outside was a black mirror, reflecting the amber smears of the streetlights. Iris stood in the center of the living room, the silence after the door closed a physical presence. It pressed against her eardrums. Henri’s teacup sat on the side table, a half-moon of pale liquid in the bottom. A single stray tea leaf clung to the inside curve, a dark, delicate comma.

She picked up the cup. The china was cold now. She carried it to the kitchen, ran the tap until the water steamed, washed it with methodical care. Her hands moved, but her mind was elsewhere—a bookstall by the Seine, the ginger cat, the smell of damp stone and river mud. A different silence then, a comfortable one, broken by the rustle of pages and Pauline’s low laugh from the next stall over. She’d been looking at a volume of Rimbaud. Or had it been Baudelaire? The memory wouldn’t settle. It kept sliding away, replaced by Henri’s mild, magnified eyes. The roundups started not a month after you left.

She dried the cup, placed it back in the cupboard. The action was absurd. The cup was clean. The man was gone. The words remained.

She walked back to the living room, but the space felt alien. The perfect chrysanthemums were a parody of life. The piano was a sealed coffin. She needed to move. She went to the bedroom, not turning on the light. The grey dusk filtered through the window, painting the room in shades of charcoal and dust. She sat on the edge of the bed, her hands flat on the chenille spread. The texture was nubbled, familiar. She focused on it.

A month. She’d signed the American contract in late August ’37. Sailed in September. The crisp official letter from the record label had arrived like a pardon. Langston had handled the negotiations, his telegrams full of triumphant exclamation points. Your ticket home, darling! The big time! She’d been giddy with it, a clean, bright fear. Escape. A real career. A life that made sense on paper.

Pauline had called it a surrender. They’d fought on the Pont Neuf, the water below the colour of tarnished silver. You’re trading a voice for an echo, she’d said. Iris had shouted something back, something about practicality, about reality. She couldn’t remember the words now. Only the set of Pauline’s jaw, the way she’d turned and walked away without another sound, her coat collar turned up against the evening chill.

She’d left Paris with a trunk full of new clothes and a hollow place under her ribs where something vital had been carved out. She told herself it was the price of ambition. The necessary sacrifice. She’d been twenty-three. She thought she understood cost.

A cold, sick understanding began to uncoil in her stomach. It wasn’t a feeling with a name. It was a slow, internal tilt, like the floorboards slanting under her feet.

Had he known?

Langston.

He’d been her manager then, too. Hungry, charming, full of schemes. He had contacts in New York, he’d said. People who were interested in a ‘continental’ sound. The deal came together with a speed that felt like fate. She’d been so grateful. So relieved.

She stared at the pattern of shadows the window frame cast on the opposite wall. A grid of rectangles. A cage of light and dark.

What if it wasn’t fate? What if it was a transaction?

The thought was ugly, a black stain seeping at the edges of a clean page. She tried to push it away. Paranoia. The stress of the present infecting the past. But the pieces fit together with a quiet, terrible click. Langston’s pragmatism had always been his most reliable quality. He read the weather. He always had. In 1937, the weather in Paris was turning stormy for Pauline’s crowd—the radicals, the pamphleteers, the artists who talked too loudly about politics in cafes. The Spanish Civil War was a bleeding wound just south of the border. Fascism wasn’t a theoretical debate anymore; it was a train you could hear coming in the night.

And she, Iris Finch, the American singer with the promising voice, was entangled with the most visible radical among them. A liability. Or an opportunity.

Get her out. Secure the contract. Remove her from a dangerous equation before the authorities started asking questions that could tarnish a future investment. It was the kind of clean, ruthless calculation Langston excelled at. It might even have felt like protection at the time.

She stood up, her legs unsteady. The room was too warm, the air too still. She went to the window, pushed it open a few inches. The damp night air flowed in, smelling of wet pavement and distant exhaust. A bus groaned on the avenue, its headlights cutting yellow tunnels through the gloom.

If he had known—if he had engineered her extraction not just for a record deal, but to sever a compromising tie—then what did that make her departure? Not a betrayal of love, but a managed evacuation. A piece moved off a board before the sweep began. She had felt like a coward for twelve years. The shame of it had been a stone in her pocket, its weight a familiar punishment. But this… this was different. This turned her grief into something colder, more clinical. A side effect of good business.

And if he had done that then, what was he doing now?

The connection sparked, bright and terrifying. The letters. Her Paris letters, which he’d so casually taken for ‘safekeeping.’ He’d framed it as a necessity, a move in their current game with Cross. But what if the game was older? What if those letters weren’t just insurance against the present; what if they were the original currency? Proof of a connection he had once paid to sever, now repurposed as a token to buy his own continued safety.

Her breath hitched. A sharp, dry pain lanced behind her sternum. She pressed the heel of her hand against it.

No. It was too neat. Too conspiracy-minded. Langston was an opportunist, not a mastermind. He reacted to pressure; he didn’t weave webs that spanned twelve years.

But he kept things. He reused assets. The fountain pen. The way he’d mentioned the bank’s name when talking about Leo Strauss’s funding, a detail that seemed to stick in his mind. He was a man who understood the value of information, who held onto anything that might be leveraged later. The letters were information. The oldest, most potent kind he had on her.

From the street below, a couple walked by, their voices a murmur, then a laugh. The sound was painfully ordinary. She closed the window, shutting it out.

She walked to her dressing table, sat on the little velvet stool. In the dim mirror, her face was a stranger’s—pale, eyes too dark. She looked like a woman waiting for bad news. The dressing table was tidy. A hairbrush, a pot of cold cream, a tray for jewelry. In the top drawer, tucked under a stack of handkerchiefs, was the Zippo lighter from Paris. She didn’t take it out. She didn’t need to. She knew its weight, the precise pattern of scratches on its steel case.

Henri’s visit hadn’t been an accident. He was a man who dealt in stories, in out-of-print truths. He had come to give her a missing page. Not out of kindness. Because a narrative with a hole in it was less valuable. He wanted her to have the whole book.

She understood now. The question wasn’t whether Langston had known. The question was what he had bought with her ignorance, and what he was buying with it now. The suspicion settled over her, not as a shock, but as a dreadful, deepening chill—the slow understanding that the cage she lived in had not been built yesterday. Its foundations were twelve years old, and she had helped carry the bricks.
