The taxi dropped her at the corner of 135th and Lenox. The building was a church, or had been. The steeple was still there, a dark spike against the grey wool of the sky, but the cross was gone. In its place, fixed to the stone above the double doors, a simple wooden sign: HARLEM INSTITUTE OF DRAMATIC ARTS. The paint was chipped. Someone had tried to scrub away graffiti on the right-hand pillar; a ghost of white paint lingered, the shape of a word she couldn’t make out.

She paid the driver. The door of the cab slammed, a sound like a gunshot in the quiet street. She stood on the sidewalk, her gloved hands clenched around her purse. The air here smelled different from the Upper West Side—hot pretzels and bus exhaust, the damp wool of passing coats, a sharp undercurrent of something chemical, maybe from the laundry on the next block. A memory surfaced, pointless: a laundry in Paris, steam billowing from a basement grate, the smell of lye soap and wet stone. She’d walked past it every day to the café where she waited for Pauline. The memory had no edges, just that smell, and a feeling of her own younger body, light with anticipation.

She climbed the steps. Her heels tapped a nervous rhythm on the worn stone. The left door was propped open with a brick. Inside, the vestibule was a shock of warmth and noise.

The ceiling soared into shadows. Light fell in dusty shafts from high, arched windows, catching on floating motes. The air vibrated with a dozen different sounds—a piano stumbling through scales in a distant room, the percussive thump of feet on a wooden floor, a young voice projecting, “To be, or not to be,” with more volume than conviction. A group of students clustered by a bulletin board papered with flyers and cast lists, their laughter a bright, sharp counterpoint. They were all Black. She was the only white person in the room.

A peculiar stillness came over her. It was the stillness of a wrong note held too long. She had prepared for many things—coldness, suspicion, a bureaucratic reception. She had not prepared for this: a living, breathing organism of art, so entirely separate from the world she inhabited. She felt like a specimen under glass. An interloper. Which, of course, she was.

“Can I help you?”

The woman at the makeshift reception desk—a door laid across two filing cabinets—had a voice like smoked honey. She wore a green headwrap and glasses with thick frames. She looked up from a ledger, her expression polite, neutral, waiting.

“I’m Iris Finch. I have an appointment with Pauline Carter.”

The woman’s eyes flickered. Something shifted behind the polite mask, a shutter clicking closed. “Miss Carter is in rehearsal. The main hall. Through those doors, down the corridor to your right. You can wait in the back.”

“Thank you.”

The corridor was narrow, the walls a faded mustard yellow. Scuffed floorboards groaned under a thin runner of carpet. The sound of the piano grew louder, then resolved into a familiar, melancholy tune—a Gershwin prelude. The playing was competent, but with a strange, rigid quality, as if the pianist were reading the notes from a sheet but had never heard the music before. A door stood ajar at the end of the hall. A rectangle of brighter light spilled out.

She stopped just outside. The hall was vast, the nave of the old church. Rows of wooden chairs faced a raised platform that served as a stage. On the stage, two young men rehearsed a scene. One of them held a script, his finger tracing the lines.

“Again,” a voice said. “From ‘I saw the strangest woman.’ And don’t tell me the words. Be the man who saw her.”

That voice.

It was lower than Iris remembered. Rougher. It had lost the playful, melodic lift of Parisian cafés, the conspiratorial whisper in dark bedrooms. This voice was a tool, precise and unyielding. It cut through the dusty air and went straight into Iris’s chest, a cold wire.

She stepped into the doorway.

Pauline stood in the center of the empty chairs, her back to the door. She wore a man’s white shirt, the sleeves rolled to her elbows, and dark, wide-legged trousers. Her hair was shorter than it had been, cropped close to her skull in tight, elegant curls. She held a clipboard loose in one hand. A cigarette smoldered between the fingers of the other. The set of her shoulders was a study in contained energy, like a spring coiled tight.

One of the actors began his line. “I saw the strangest woman yesterday.”

“Stop.” Pauline didn’t turn. “You’re illustrating. You’re showing me ‘look how strange she was.’ I don’t care. I want you to be strange yourself. The strangeness is in you. It’s a stone in your shoe. Try it again.”

The actor shifted his weight. He closed his eyes, took a breath. When he spoke again, his voice was quieter, more internal. “I saw the strangest woman yesterday.”

“Better.” Pauline took a drag of her cigarette. The smoke curled up into a shaft of light. “Now walk with it. The stone is in your shoe. Cross the stage.”

The actor began to move, a slow, limping walk.

Iris’s throat closed. She couldn’t have spoken if she tried. She watched the line of Pauline’s neck, the familiar slope where it met her shoulder. A tiny scar, pale against her skin, just above the collar of her shirt—a burn from a splatter of hot wax, a night in a Montparnasse garret, laughter, the smell of cheap wine. The memory was so vivid it was physical, a hand pressing against her sternum.

Pauline lifted her head, as if catching a scent. She turned.

Time did not stop. It fractured. The present moment splintered into a thousand shards—the dusty hall, the hesitant actor, the grey light, the taste of her own panic, metallic on her tongue. And layered over it, a ghost-image: Pauline, younger, her hair longer, laughing into a glass of absinthe, her foot nudging Iris’s under a tiny table.

Now, Pauline’s eyes found hers. Dark, intelligent, impossible to read. There was no shock in them. No surprise. Only a slow, deliberate recognition, like someone identifying a specimen they had long expected to see.

The cigarette hovered near her lips. She didn’t move.

The actor faltered, his line trailing off. The pianist missed a note. The world narrowed to the space between the doorway and the center of the room, a distance of maybe thirty feet that felt like a chasm.

Pauline’s gaze held hers for three heartbeats. Then it swept away, dismissing her, returning to the stage. “We’ll take five,” she said, her voice unchanged. “Get some water. Think about the stone.”

She stubbed her cigarette out on the sole of her shoe and dropped the butt into a pocket. She didn’t look at Iris again. She walked to the stage, spoke quietly to the actor, her hand gesturing at the script. A professional at work.

Iris stood frozen in the doorway. The cold wire in her chest was now a live current, humming. She had imagined this moment a thousand ways in the cab ride over. She had prepared sentences, explanations, a mask of professional courtesy. None of it remained. There was only this: the absolute, glacial indifference in Pauline’s eyes. It was worse than hatred. Hatred would have been an acknowledgment. This was an erasure.

A student brushed past her, carrying a toolbox. The spell broke. Iris took a step into the hall, her legs unsteady.

Pauline finished with the actor and turned. She walked toward Iris, not with purpose, but with the inevitable, measured pace of a tide coming in. She stopped a few feet away. Up close, Iris saw the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the tired set of her mouth. She smelled of cigarette smoke and lemony soap and, faintly, the same sandalwood perfume she’d worn twelve years ago.

“Miss Finch.” Pauline’s tone was flat, administrative. “You’re early.”

“The traffic was light.”

“I see.” Pauline looked past her, toward the corridor. “My office is upstairs. We can speak there.”

She moved to walk past. Iris found her voice. It came out thin, strained. “Pauline.”

Pauline stopped. She turned her head, just enough. Her profile was a clean, sharp cut against the dark wood of the stage. “It’s Miss Carter,” she said, without looking at her. “Follow me, please.”

Iris followed her up a narrow, iron spiral staircase at the side of the hall. The steps groaned under their weight, a different pitch for each one—a discordant scale. Pauline’s shoes, scuffed brown oxfords, moved with a steady, unhurried rhythm. Iris fixed her eyes on the back of Pauline’s left ankle, the pale sock, a small darned patch just above the heel. The air grew warmer, closer, smelling of old paper and the particular, sweetish dust that settles in places never properly cleaned.

The office was a converted choir loft. One entire wall was an arched window overlooking the rehearsal hall below, the actors now tiny, distant figures. The other walls were buried under bookshelves crammed with scripts, their spines a riot of faded colors and cracked lettering. Political posters were tacked up between the shelves: a WPA theatre project notice, a flyer for a Paul Robeson concert at Peekskill, a stark graphic of a raised fist with the words ‘ART IS A WEAPON.’ A scarred wooden desk faced the window, piled with more papers, an ashtray overflowing with cigarette ends, and a chipped mug holding a bouquet of pencils.

Pauline did not sit. She went to the window, her back to Iris, and lit a fresh cigarette from the stub of the old one. The match flared, a tiny, angry star. She shook it out, dropped it into the ashtray. The silence stretched, filled only by the muffled sounds from below—a line of dialogue, the scrape of a chair.

“You’ll assist on The Glass Menagerie,” Pauline said, still looking down. Her voice was flat, administrative. “The Williams play. Rehearsals are Tuesday and Thursday evenings, Sunday afternoons. You’ll take notes for the director. Blocking, line flubs, pacing. You know the drill.”

“I do.” Iris’s own voice sounded thin, a stranger’s.

“The script is there.” Pauline gestured with her cigarette toward a stack of papers on a chair. “Familiarize yourself. We open in five weeks.”

Iris moved to take the script. The top page was mimeographed, the purple ink slightly blurred. She picked it up. Underneath it was another script, its cover page stark white. The title was typed in bold black letters: THE CHILDREN’S HOUR by Lillian Hellman.

Her hand froze. The air left the room.

Pauline turned. She leaned back against the window ledge, one ankle crossed over the other. She took a slow drag, her eyes on Iris’s face. They were the same dark, impossible brown, but the warmth Iris remembered—the warmth that had felt like a physical place, a room you could step into—was gone. These eyes were assessing, cool. They noted the minute tremor in Iris’s fingers, the way her breath caught.

“That one is for you to study as well,” Pauline said. Her tone was conversational, almost pleasant. “Separately. I’d like your thoughts on Martha’s final monologue. The psychology of it. The… mechanics of confession.”

The radiator under the window hissed, a long, wet exhalation. Somewhere in the building, a pipe clanged. Iris’s mouth was dry, a desert taste. She could smell the nicotine on Pauline’s clothes, a smell that was once familiar as her own skin. Now it was just smoke.

“Why that play?” The question came out before she could stop it, a stupid, transparent thing.

Pauline’s smile was a brief, cold flicker. “It’s about a lie. A lie that destroys lives. I thought it might resonate.” She pushed off from the ledge and moved to the desk. She opened a drawer, took out a ledger. “Your first rehearsal is tomorrow. Seven p. m. Don’t be late. The students’ time is valuable.”

It was a dismissal. Clean, surgical, devoid of anything but a brutal professionalism. The assignment was a broom closet. The Hellman script was a knife left on the table between them, its edge catching the grey light from the window.

Iris clutched the Menagerie script to her chest. The paper was cool against her palms. “Pauline,” she said, the name feeling illegal in her mouth.

“It’s Miss Carter.” Pauline did not look up from her ledger. “We’ll keep it professional. It’s simpler.”

There was nothing else to say. Iris turned. The floorboards creaked under her feet, a different pattern from the stairs. She was at the top of the spiral when Pauline’s voice stopped her, not loud, but clear.

“One more thing, Finch.”

Iris looked back. Pauline was watching her, the ledger forgotten in her hand. Her expression was unreadable, a closed door.

“The report,” Pauline said. “The one you’ll file for your friends in Washington. I’d appreciate it if you got the details right. We’re doing The Glass Menagerie. A family play. A memory play. Nothing subversive about memories, is there?”

The world tilted, just a degree. The dust motes in the shaft of light seemed to freeze in their dance. Iris’s heart did something complicated in her chest—a hard, syncopated thump against her ribs. She hadn’t said a word about Cross, about the arrangement. She had barely breathed it to herself.

Pauline gave her another of those faint, icy smiles. “The door downstairs sticks. Give it a good shove.”

The cold outside was a shock, a return to reality. Iris walked for two blocks without seeing anything, the two scripts held rigidly at her side. Her mind was a blank, white screen. Then, all at once, it filled with a single, absurdly vivid image: the pattern of frost on a windowpane in a cheap hotel in Lyon, 1938. She and Pauline had watched it form, their bodies warm under a thin blanket. It had looked like ferns, like delicate, branching bones. The memory had no place here, on a Harlem street corner in 1949. It was just a piece of mental debris, surfacing for no reason.

She found a taxi. The driver had the radio on low, a baseball game. The announcer’s voice was a soothing drone of statistics. She leaned her head against the cold glass of the window, watching the city blur past. The Menagerie script lay on the seat beside her. The Children’s Hour script was in her purse, a hidden weight.

At home, the apartment was silent. Langston was out. A note on the hall table, in his precise hand: Dinner with Duke Strauss. Don’t wait up. She stood in the center of the living room, still in her coat. The clock on the mantel ticked. A shaft of late-afternoon sun cut across the carpet, illuminating a thousand swirling dust particles. She focused on one, trying to track its meaningless, random path.

She poured a gin, measured it in the toothbrush glass. She did not drink it. She set it on the desk in the study, next to the typewriter Langston had bought her last Christmas, a sleek, modern thing she never used.

She sat. She rolled a sheet of paper into the machine. The blankness yawned.

Her fingers found the keys.

Report #1. February 14, 1949. Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. Initial observations.

She stopped. The words were inadequate, ridiculous. She saw Pauline’s face, the cold precision in her eyes. Get the details right.

She backspaced, the keys clacking like teeth.

The Institute is housed in a former church. Approximately thirty students observed. Activities appear focused on standard theatrical training. Current production: “The Glass Menagerie” by Tennessee Williams. Director: Unknown. Head of the institute, Pauline Carter, is organizing rehearsals. No overt political activity witnessed.

She read it over. It was sterile. Harmless. It said nothing and everything. It was the kind of report a guilty person would write.

She thought of the Hellman script in her purse. The mechanics of confession.

Her thumb traced the rim of the gin glass. A thin, high sound. She took a sip. The alcohol burned, a clean, clarifying fire.

She typed one more line.

Further observation required to assess full scope of operations.

She pulled the paper from the typewriter. The words sat there, black and final. A lie that was not a lie. A confession that confessed nothing. She folded the paper once, then again, until it was a small, thick square. She would give it to Cross. It was what he wanted. It was what she had to do.

The sun had moved. The dust in the air was invisible now. The room was just a room, waiting for the dark.

The study was too warm. The radiator under the window knocked, a hollow, persistent complaint. Iris sat at Langston’s desk, his fountain pen heavy in her hand. The sheet of stationery was blank except for the letterhead: Langston Finch Management. The creamy paper seemed to absorb the lamplight, refusing any mark.

She had written the date. February 14, 1949. A Valentine. That was all.

The words wouldn’t come. Not the right ones. She could write a report. She knew the shape of it. A list of observations: the converted church, the number of students (approximately twenty), the rehearsal in progress, the director’s methods. Sterile. Safe. The kind of thing Cross would want—a catalogue of facts that meant nothing and everything.

But her mind kept snagging on the irrelevant. The chipped green paint on the baseboard in the hallway. The specific squeak of a floorboard near the stage, a B-flat above middle C. The way the light from the high window had cut across Pauline’s cheekbone, sharp as a blade. The smell of the place—not just dust and old wood, but a faint, sweet trace of cocoa butter from the students’ hair.

None of that belonged in a report.

She put the pen down. Her thumb found the rim of the inkwell, traced the cool glass. A habit. Langston hated it. Said it left smudges.

The door opened it. He didn’t knock. He carried two glasses, the good crystal, and a bottle of gin. His face was a careful composition of concern.

“Making progress?” He set a glass beside her, poured two fingers.

“It’s difficult to know what to say.”

“The truth. Just the facts. Don’t editorialize.” He took the chair opposite the desk, swirling his own drink. He looked tired. A small, vertical line had etched itself between his eyebrows, a new detail. Or one she’d stopped noticing.

“The facts.” She picked up the pen again, watched the nib hover. “The institute is housed in a deconsecrated church. The student body appears to be exclusively Negro. They were rehearsing a scene. I couldn’t identify the play.”

“See? That’s a start.” His voice was encouraging, the way he spoke to a nervous vocalist. “And Carter?”

Iris took a sip. The gin was a cold fire, familiar. “She was directing. She gave me a script to study. The Children’s Hour.”

Langston’s glass stopped halfway to his mouth. He set it down, the crystal clicking against the wood. “Hell of a choice.”

“I imagine it was deliberate.”

“Of course it was.” He leaned forward, elbows on his knees. The manager now, assessing damage. “What did she say to you? Exactly.”

“She said hello.” The memory was a physical pressure behind Iris’s eyes. That first look. The ice in it. “She said she’d see me tomorrow. That was all.”

“Nothing about Paris? About the past?”

“No.” It was true. The past had been in the room, a third presence, but neither of them had acknowledged it aloud. A strange, silent pact. “It was… professional.”

Langston sat back, unsatisfied. He took a long drink. “You’ll have to do better than that for Cross. He’ll want names. Who was there? Any other faculty? Visitors?”

A flash of the young man at the piano, his shoulders tense. The woman with the headwrap and the ledger. The students by the bulletin board. Giving Cross their names felt different from describing the peeling paint. It felt like handing him a stone.

“I don’t know any names,” she said.

“Then you’ll learn them.” His tone was final. He nodded at the paper. “Write what you can. Keep it simple. We’ll review it in the morning.”

He stood, meaning to leave her to it. But he paused by the door, his hand on the frame. “Iris.” He didn’t turn. “This is the only way. You understand that, don’t you? There’s no path back to the microphone without walking through this room first.”

She didn’t answer. The radiator knocked again.

When he was gone, she looked at the blank page. The truth was a knot she couldn’t unpick. The fact was the dust in the air. The fact was the stone in her shoe. The fact was Pauline’s voice, a cold wire in her chest.

She lifted the pen. She wrote a single sentence.

The subject, Pauline Carter, appears fully committed to her pedagogical duties.

It was nothing. It was everything. It was a lie that felt, in its sterile omission, like a deeper truth. She couldn’t say she saw the woman she’d loved, now a stranger made of principle and anger. She couldn’t say the rehearsal hall felt more like a sanctuary than any church she’d ever sung in. She couldn’t say she was afraid not of Cross, but of the part of herself that wanted to stay in that dusty, vibrant room and forget the man waiting in the car.

She signed it. I. Finch.

The ink dried, a small, black scar on the perfect paper. She folded it once, twice, sealed it in the envelope Langston had left for her. It was addressed to a post office box in Washington. The act of licking the glue tasted bitter, chemical.

From the living room, the radio murmured. A news bulletin. Something about tensions in Berlin. The voice was calm, authoritative, describing the world as a series of strategic positions. She envied that clarity.

She stood and went to the window. The street below was quiet, just the occasional car sliding past on wet pavement. In a third-floor apartment across the way, a man stood at his own window, a silhouette against a yellow lamp. He was perfectly still, looking out at the night, or at his own reflection. She wondered what he saw. After a moment, he turned and moved away, swallowed by the light of his room.

Her own reflection ghosted the glass—a pale woman in a dark dress, holding an envelope. A messenger. A courier of nothing.

She left the study, turned off the lamp. In the darkness, the radiator’s knocking seemed louder, more insistent. A code she couldn’t decipher. She placed the envelope on the hall table, where Langston would find it in the morning. Then she walked to the bedroom, the silk of her dress whispering against her legs, a sound like pages turning in an empty room.
