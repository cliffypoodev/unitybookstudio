The light in the dressing room was the color of weak tea, a yellow that didn’t warm anything. It pooled on the scarred surface of the vanity, catching the fine dust of face powder that never seemed to settle. Iris pressed a cotton pad to her cheek. It came away smudged with beige and a faint, rusty streak of sweat. The applause from the Blue Parrot’s main room had been a polite, distant rustle, like leaves skittering across pavement. It was over. Another show. The silence that followed it now was a solid thing, a cork pushed into the neck of a bottle.

Her reflection looked tired. Not the interesting, tragic tiredness of the chanteuse persona, but a plain, forty-eight-year-old exhaustion. The fine lines around her eyes were more pronounced under this bulb. She noticed, with a detachment that felt almost clinical, a single grey hair at her temple she had missed with the dye. It caught the light, a tiny filament of silver defiance. She left it.

The door opened without a knock. Arthur’s reflection appeared behind hers in the glass, a taller, narrower shape in a charcoal suit. He carried the scent of the club with him—stale smoke, spilled whiskey, the faint, sweet rot of carnations from the tables.

“They liked the new arrangement,” he said. He didn’t look at her in the mirror. His eyes were on the clutter of her dressing table: the cold cream jars, the scattered bobby pins, the half-empty glass of gin she’d poured before going on. His gaze lingered there a fraction too long, then moved on, inventorying. “The booker from the Copa was in the back. He left before I could get to him.

Iris nodded, peeling off a false eyelash. The glue tugged at her skin. Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was about the booker, maybe. Or the gin. Or the way Arthur stood there, a manager assessing stock. She remembered the feel of a different tug, the sharp, clean pull of a thread through linen, her mother’s hands showing her how to mend a seam. A useless memory. It arrived without permission.

“He probably had a train,” she said. Her voice was hoarse, the gravel of two shows a night. She cleared her throat. A nervous habit. She did it again.

“Perhaps.” Arthur moved then, coming to stand beside the vanity. He picked up a framed photograph she kept there, tilted it toward the light. It was an old studio shot from ’37, her in a beaded gown, mouth open mid-note, eyes bright with a confidence that felt like a costume she’d borrowed from a stranger. He studied it, his thumb brushing the edge of the frame. “We need to talk about the future, Iris.

The word ‘we’ did it. A cold knot tightened under her ribs. She reached for her cigarettes, lit one. The first drag was a relief, a familiar burn. “The future’s been talked about, Arthur. It’s a smaller room, a later slot, a cheaper orchestra. I know the script.

“That’s not the script anymore.” He set the photograph down with a precise click, aligning it perfectly with the corner of the vanity. “The Parrot can’t renew. They’re changing format. More comedy, less… nostalgia.

Nostalgia. The word hung in the air, cheap and final as crepe paper.

She watched the smoke curl toward the ceiling, where a long, hairline crack ran from the light fixture toward the wall. It looked like a river on a map of nowhere. “So,” she said.

“So.” Arthur’s voice softened, adopting the tone he used for difficult clients. Reasonable. Fatherly. It made her skin prickle. “I’ve been making inquiries. Quietly. There’s an opportunity. A professorship.

A laugh escaped her, short and dry. “I’m not a professor.

“It’s at the Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. Voice and performance. It’s respectable. It’s secure.

Harlem. The word landed differently. It carried a charge, a hum of other memories—not childhood cracks, but the thick, vibrant air of basement clubs, the smell of hot wax and ambition, a different kind of music. And a name. Pauline. The thought of her was a sudden, physical ache, a fist of old grief she’d mistaken for indigestion. She pushed it down, swallowed against the tightness in her throat.

“Teaching kids to warble?” she said, stubbing out the cigarette half-smoked.

“Teaching them discipline. Craft.” Arthur’s eyes met hers in the mirror now. They were a pale, watery blue, impossible to read. “It’s a salary, Iris. A roof. It’s not the Copa, but it’s not the gutter either. Think of it as… a strategic retreat.

Strategic. A word from his ledger. She looked away, her gaze falling on the small, locked briefcase he always carried. It sat by his feet, scuffed black leather. He called it his files. Manager’s records, contracts, correspondence. Insurance, he’d joked once, years ago. The lock was brass, tiny and bright against the worn leather.

“Why would they want me?” The question was genuine. Her kind of fame—the supper club circuit, the torch songs—was a fading echo in places like the Institute. They dealt in protest plays, raw social dramas, the future. She was the past, prettily packaged.

“They have a need. I have an acquaintance on the board.” Arthur adjusted his cufflink, a minute, fastidious gesture. “It’s been agreed, in principle. Pending your meeting with the head of theatre. A formality.

Her stomach dropped. “The head of theatre.

“Pauline Carter.”

The name, spoken aloud in this yellow light, was a violation. It stripped the air from the room. Iris’s mouth went dry. She fumbled for another cigarette, her fingers refusing to cooperate. Arthur watched the struggle, his expression neutral. He’d known. Of course he’d known. He’d pulled the file on Pauline years ago, a clinical dossier of her career, her politics. He’d called it due diligence.

“That’s cruel,” she managed, the words a scrape.

“It’s practical. She runs the department. She’s respected. Her endorsement would be… meaningful.” He paused, letting the implication settle. “It’s been twelve years, Iris. It’s just a job.

Just a job. She finally got the cigarette lit. The smoke tasted of ash and nothing else. The crack in the ceiling seemed to pulse, a faint, jagged vein. She thought of Pauline’s hands, the way they could be gentle tracing a line of poetry on a page or fierce chopping vegetables for a soup that cost nothing to make. Those hands, now shaping plays. Now having to shape an opinion of her.

“What did you tell her?” Iris asked.

“The truth. That you’re a professional. That you’re looking for a… steadier platform.” He leaned forward, his voice dropping to a confidential murmur that felt like a trap closing. “I may have suggested you were seeking a return to more substantive work. A reinvention. It’s what she wants to hear.

A reinvention. As if she were a coat turned inside out to hide the stains. The hot, nameless thing in her chest coiled tighter. She wanted to throw the glass of gin, to see the amber liquid arc and shatter against the wall. She sat very still.

“And if I say no?”

Arthur straightened up. He didn’t smile. He didn’t frown. He simply looked at the locked briefcase, then back at her. “Then we have no script at all. The Parrot is done in two weeks. The other clubs… well. You heard the applause.

It wasn’t a threat. It was a balance sheet. Her value, calculated, depreciating. The silence stretched. In the club beyond the door, a janitor’s bucket clanged against a leg of a chair, a hollow, lonely sound.

“When is the meeting?” she said.

“Tuesday. Ten a.m.” He took a folded sheet of paper from his inner pocket and placed it on the vanity, beside the photograph of the girl in the beaded gown. An address. “It’s not an audition. Just a conversation.

He turned to leave, then stopped, his hand on the doorknob. “Oh. I’ve been sorting through the old storage. Letters, fan mail, that sort of thing. For the archives.” His tone was casual, offhand. “I found that batch from your USO tour. The V-mail. You had quite a correspondence with that pianist—what was his name? Miller? The one who ended up in that trouble with the Legion magazine.

Her blood went still. She kept her eyes on her own reflection. “I hardly remember.

“You wrote such vivid letters. Full of… local color.” He paused. “I’m keeping them separate. They’re a unique record of the time. A historical curiosity.

The door clicked shut behind him.

Iris sat in the yellow light. The ice in her gin had melted into a sad, watery crescent at the bottom of the glass. She picked up the paper with the address. Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. Her hand trembled. Just a tremor, the kind that came with fatigue. She willed it to stop. It didn’t.

She looked at the locked briefcase he’d left behind, forgotten or placed there as a reminder. A historical curiosity. The phrase echoed. She remembered the code, then. A silly thing she and the pianist had used, a substitution cipher based on the chords of “Body and Soul.” A game. Youthful exuberance. She’d written a letter full of it, describing a rainstorm in Naples, the pattern on the café awning, the number of steps to the post office. Nonsense. Unless you had the key.

Arthur had the key. He had all the keys.

She finished the cigarette. The ash dropped onto the vanity, a grey bloom on the polished wood. She did not wipe it away.

The restaurant was the kind of place where silence was a commodity, bought and paid for with thick carpets and heavy drapes. Iris sat in a leather booth that sighed under her weight. The tablecloth was starched white, a blank page. A single red carnation stood in a slim vase, its petals edged with brown, like something left too near a flame.

She’d worn a navy suit, something with shoulders. Armor. Arthur sat beside her, not touching. He’d ordered a club soda. His briefcase rested against his leg, a familiar, dark shape.

The man from the government was late. That was the point, probably. Let her sit with the quiet, let her notice the way the ice in her water glass had already begun to melt, diluting the lemon slice into a pale, sour ghost. She traced a finger along the rim. The crystal sang a faint, high note. A memory surfaced, unbidden: the sound of a wet finger on the edge of a wine glass in a Paris cafe, a table of students making their cheap Beaujolais hum. Pauline, laughing, trying to match the pitch. A useless, stupid detail. Her throat tightened.

“Relax,” Arthur murmured, not looking at her. He was studying the menu as if it contained code.

The agent arrived without fanfare. He was a man of middling everything—height, build, age, the color of his hair—dressed in a grey suit that wanted to be invisible. He slid into the booth opposite them. He did not smile. He placed a plain manila folder on the table between the salt cellar and the dying flower.

“Miss Finch. Mr. Doyle. Thank you for your time.” His voice was dry, uninflected. He had the look of a man who spent his days in rooms without windows.

“Of course,” Arthur said, his manager’s charm switched on like a lamp. “This is my client, Iris Finch. Iris, this is Mr. Henderson.

Henderson nodded once. His eyes were a flat, neutral grey. They moved over her face, not appraising, just recording. “I’m with the House Committee on Un-American Activities. We’re conducting a review of certain artistic circles. Your name came up.

The words landed with a soft, final click, like the lock on Arthur’s briefcase. Iris kept her hands in her lap. One thumb pressed hard into the knuckle of the other. A somatic anchor. “I’m a singer,” she said. Her voice held. “I perform standards. Romantic ballads. There’s nothing un-American about ‘Embraceable You.

“It’s not the songs you sing,” Henderson said. He didn’t open the folder. “It’s the company you kept. The conversations you had. The… affiliations.” He let the word hang. It meant everything and nothing. It meant Paris. It meant basement clubs where the smoke was thick with more than tobacco. It meant Pauline.

Arthur leaned forward, a conciliatory angle. “Iris has had a long career. She’s worked with hundreds of artists, musicians. Many were passionate, young. Ideas were exchanged. It was a different time.

“1937,” Henderson said. He said it like a date on a tombstone. “You were in Paris. On a cultural exchange visa. You performed at the Café le Select. The Blue Note. You shared a flat with…” He finally opened the folder, glanced down. Not because he needed to read it. For effect. “Pauline Carter. A playwright. Known associate of several socialist literary groups. The correspondence between you two was… voluminous.

A cold weight settled in Iris’s stomach. Correspondence. The letters. She’d thought them lost to time, to moves, to Arthur’s discreet pruning of her past. But he hadn’t lost them. He’d filed them. Insurance. The lock on the briefcase gleamed in the low light.

“Pauline was a collaborator,” Iris said. The word felt thin, inadequate. “We worked on material together. That’s all.

“Material with a certain political tenor,” Henderson said. “And then there’s the war years. Your performances for the USO were commendable. But other performances… private fundraisers for the Spanish Refugee Appeal. The Committee for the First Amendment. Your signature on a petition for the Hollywood Ten.” He tapped the folder. “It creates a pattern. A pattern of sympathy for subversive elements.

Arthur’s hand came to rest on the table, near her arm but not touching it. A warning. “Iris has always been a patriot.

“Is not in question,” Henderson interrupted. His politeness was worse than anger. It was a tool. “What’s in question is her judgment. And judgment, in times like these, is a matter of national security.” He looked directly at Iris. “There are rumors, Miss Finch. About your personal life. The Lavender Scare, they’re calling it. A moral weakness is seen as a vulnerability to blackmail. A security risk.

The air left the room. The carnation seemed to wilt further. Iris’s mouth was parchment. She saw, with perfect clarity, a chip in the pale green paint of the wall behind Henderson’s head. It was shaped like a tiny crescent moon.

“What is it you want?” she asked. Her voice was her own, a low, steady thing from some deep, untouchable place.

“Cooperation,” Henderson said. “The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts is a nest of radical thought. Agitators. We know Pauline Carter is at the heart of it. You have a history with her. You’ll have access. You can report back. Who she meets with. What they’re planning. The content of their… plays.

The twist was not in the ask. It was in the casual, offhand way he linked it all together—her past, her present, her future—as if drawing a straight, inevitable line. Arthur had known. This professorship wasn’t a retreat. It was an insertion. A strategic placement. The cold knot under her ribs turned to ice.

“You want me to inform on my friends,” she said.

“I want you to prove your loyalty to your country,” Henderson corrected. “A public gesture isn’t enough. We need a private understanding. Your continued freedom, your ability to work… it depends on this understanding.” He closed the folder. “Refuse, and this pattern of behavior becomes a matter of public record. The rumors become facts. You’ll never sing again. You may well face charges. Conspiracy is a broad term.

Arthur was silent. He studied his club soda, the bubbles clinging to the side of the glass. He had brought her here. He had made the inquiries. This was the opportunity. A roof. Security.

“I need time,” Iris heard herself say.

“You have forty-eight hours,” Henderson said. He stood, adjusting his cuffs. He left the folder on the table. “The details of the position are there. Your first class is Monday. We’ll be in touch with further instructions.” He gave a curt nod, more a tic of dismissal than a courtesy, and walked away. The carpet swallowed the sound of his steps.

The silence he left behind was different. It was charged, brittle. Iris looked at the folder. A plain manila coffin.

Arthur let out a long, slow breath. “It’s not ideal,” he said.

Iris picked up her water glass. Her hand did not shake. She took a sip. The water was tepid, the lemon flavor faint and medicinal. “You set this up.

“I protected you,” he said, his voice low and urgent. “They were going to come for you regardless, Iris. This way, there’s a path. A way through. You teach. You keep your head down. You tell them a few harmless things. And you survive.” He touched the briefcase with his foot, a subconscious gesture. “It’s the only play.

The only play. She looked at him, really looked. The pale eyes, the careful part in his hair, the mouth that could smile so convincingly. He had her past in that case. He had traded it for this. Not just her manager. Her warden. The realization was quiet, a door closing in a distant room of her mind.

She stood. The leather booth released her with another soft sigh. “I need air.

“Iris—”

She walked away from the table, from the folder, from him. The maître d’ gave her a concerned look as she passed. She pushed through the heavy door and out onto the sidewalk.

The afternoon light was sharp, clean. It felt like a lie. A delivery boy whistled as he rode past on a bicycle. The normalcy of it was a physical shock. She leaned against the brick facade, the rough stone solid against her palm. She focused on the sensation, the gritty reality of it. A strategic retreat. An insertion. A private understanding.

The words circled, vultures. She closed her eyes. Behind her lids, she didn’t see Henderson’s grey face. She saw the crack on her dressing room ceiling, the river on a map of nowhere. She saw the single grey hair she’d left in place. A tiny filament of defiance.

It wasn’t enough.

The air outside the Blue Parrot held the city’s particular chill, a dampness that seeped through wool and settled in the joints. Iris walked, the strap of her handbag digging a groove into her shoulder. She didn’t remember deciding to walk. Her feet had simply carried her away from the club, from Arthur’s reasonable voice, from the dressing room with its tea-stained light. The pavement was slick with a recent rain, reflecting the neon of a liquor store in oily streaks of red and green. She passed a diner, the windows steamed, and caught the greasy, comforting smell of fried onions. It made her stomach turn over, a hollow, queasy roll. She hadn’t eaten.

Strategic retreat. The words marched in time with her heels on the concrete. A salary. A roof. A professorship. Each term was a neat, closed box. Arthur dealt in boxes. His briefcase was a box. His files were boxes within boxes. The lock was so small, so bright.

She found herself on a different street, the buildings taller, the sidewalks wider. The noise changed—less traffic, more voices, a snatch of piano from an open window high above, a complex, cascading run that ended abruptly. The air tasted different here, a mix of coal dust and something sweeter, like fried dough. She stopped under the awning of a closed haberdashery, its window displaying a single, elegant grey fedora on a stand. She lit another cigarette, her hands steady now, the tremor gone. Numbness was a kind of steadiness.

Across the street, a sandstone building rose three stories, its upper windows catching the last of the afternoon sun in warm, honeyed squares. A brass plaque beside the double doors read: HARLEM INSTITUTE OF DRAMATIC ARTS. The doors were propped open with a brick. From within came a layered sound—a voice projecting, a sudden burst of collective laughter, the scrape of a chair, a single, plaintive note from a trumpet warming up. Life. Uncurated, messy, simultaneous life.

She stood there smoking, watching. A young man burst out of the doors, script pages fluttering in his hand, calling back to someone inside, “I have it, I have it, just give me a minute!” He collided with a lamppost, laughed, and kept running. He couldn’t have been more than nineteen. The energy coming off him was a physical thing, a charge in the air. Iris felt ancient, a fossil preserved in gin and face powder.

Her eyes traveled up the facade. On the third floor, a window was open. A figure stood there, silhouetted against the light of the room behind. A woman. She was leaning on the sill, looking down at the street, not moving. The height and the shadow blurred the details, but the posture was unmistakable—a certain straightness of the spine, the tilt of the head. It was a posture she had drawn in the margins of notebooks twenty years ago. Pauline.

The cigarette burned close to her fingers. She didn’t feel it. The street noise faded to a hum. There was only the silhouette in the window, a cut-out against the gold. Then the figure turned away, dissolving into the interior light.

The ache returned, not in her chest this time, but lower, a deep, muscular memory of loss. It wasn’t grief. It was the ghost of a specific gravity, the weight of another person’s head on her shoulder, the scent of turpentine and black tea. A ridiculous detail to remember. Turpentine.

She dropped the cigarette, crushed it under her heel. The movement was sharp, final. She wouldn’t go in. Not today. The thought of climbing those steps, of facing that silhouette in the flesh, of seeing the changes time had carved—it was impossible. Arthur’s offer was one thing. This was another. This was the lock on the briefcase, but the lock was inside her now.

She turned and walked away, back toward the subway, back toward the apartment she shared with Arthur. The walk was longer going back. Her feet hurt. She noticed a child’s chalk drawing on the pavement, a lopsided sun with too many rays, half-washed away by the rain. It looked like a dandelion gone to seed.

The apartment was quiet when she let herself in. Arthur was still out, managing something, someone. She poured two fingers of gin, didn’t bother with ice. She carried it to the window that overlooked the airshaft. The room was tidy, everything in its place—the sofa aligned perfectly with the rug, the magazines fanned on the table. Arthur’s order. It had always felt like safety. Now it felt like the stillness before an inventory.

On the bookshelf, between a volume of Keats and a biography of Toscanini, was a small, framed photograph. She hadn’t looked at it in years. It showed two young women on a Parisian bridge, the Seine a grey blur behind them. She was smiling, squinting into the sun. Pauline was looking at the camera, her expression serious, intense, as if challenging the photographer to get it right. Iris had kept it. A sentimental lapse. Arthur had framed it for her one birthday. “To remember your muse,” he’d said. His tone had been light, but his eyes had been on her face, watching.

She took the photograph down now. The glass was cool. She traced the line of Pauline’s jaw with her thumb. The young man’s energy, the open door, the silhouette in the window—it was a current, and she was standing on the bank. To step in would mean getting wet. It would mean the careful order of this room, of this life, would be ruined.

From the street below, a car backfired. She flinched, her hand tightening on the frame. The gin in her other hand sloshed, a small tidal wave against the glass. She set the photograph face down on the shelf. She didn’t put it back in its place.

The gin was bitter on her tongue. She thought of the lock on the briefcase, and then she thought of the brick propping open the Institute’s door. A temporary measure. Anyone could walk in. Or out.
