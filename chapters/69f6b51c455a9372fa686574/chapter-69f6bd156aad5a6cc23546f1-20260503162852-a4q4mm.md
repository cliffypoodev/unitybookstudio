The apartment was too quiet. Iris stood at the window, the empty gin glass cool in her hand. The airshaft outside was a canyon of brick and fire escapes, strung with a single, forgotten clothesline, empty except for one wooden pin that clicked against the metal pole in a faint, irregular rhythm. Click. Pause. It sounded like a metronome with a weak spring, the sort of sound you only noticed when you were trying not to think about something else.

She should have gone into the Institute yesterday. The thought was a dull pressure behind her eyes. Arthur would ask. He would want a report, a strategy. The job was his solution, his neat little package tied with the string of her desperation. A teaching position. The words felt absurd in her mouth, like trying to swallow a spoon.

The front door clicked open. She didn’t turn. She heard the precise, familiar sequence: the key, the latch, the soft thud of his briefcase on the hall table, the rustle of his overcoat being hung on the brass hook. Arthur moved through rooms like a curator, placing himself exactly where he belonged.

“You’re up,” he said from the doorway.

“The sun is up.” The words came out rough.

He came to stand beside her, not too close. He followed her gaze to the empty clothesline. “Not much of a view.”

“It’s honest.”

Arthur made a small sound, not quite a laugh. He held a cream-colored envelope between his first two fingers. The paper was thick, official. The return address was typed in a crisp, black font she didn’t recognize. “This came. Special delivery.”

He didn’t hand it to her. He just held it there, a silent offering. Her throat tightened. It was a specific, physical reaction, a constriction just below her jaw, as if her body had recognized the threat before her mind could name it. She took the envelope. It was heavier than it looked.

“Aren’t you going to open it?” Arthur asked. He moved to the sideboard and poured himself a whiskey, neat. It was ten in the morning.

The paper tore with a sound like fabric ripping. Inside was a single sheet, the same heavy stock. The letterhead was simple, stark. Committee on Un-American Activities. United States House of Representatives. The words were black holes on the page. Her vision narrowed, the edges of the room going soft and indistinct. She could see only the typed paragraphs, the formal salutation to Mrs. Iris Finch, the invitation—no, the summons—to an informal conversation. To clarify certain associations from the war years. It mentioned her professional travels, her artistic collaborations. It used the word “clarify” three times. The signature at the bottom was a scrawl, illegible.

“They want to talk,” she said. Her own voice sounded distant, someone else speaking from the end of a long corridor.

“Everyone wants to talk.” Arthur took a sip. “It’s how they decide if they want to shout.”

She looked up from the page. The light from the window caught the fine lines at the corners of his eyes, the careful gray at his temples. He was watching her, his expression calm, analytical. He was waiting for her to understand the equation. The letter in her hand was one variable. The job at the Institute was another. The locked briefcase in his study was the constant.

“An informal conversation,” she said.

“It’s always informal until it isn’t.” He set his glass down on the windowsill. The click of crystal on wood was terribly loud. “This teaching position. It’s a good look. Stable. Respectable. It provides a. Narrative.”

A narrative. The word hung between them. She thought of the child’s chalk drawing on the pavement, the lopsided sun washed away by rain. You could draw any story you wanted, but the weather always had the final edit.

“And if I don’t provide the right narrative?”

Arthur’s gaze didn’t waver. He picked a piece of invisible lint from the sleeve of his suit jacket. “Then the conversation becomes less informal. They have questions, Iris. About your songs. About the people you knew in Paris. About the kinds of circles you moved in.” He paused, his eyes meeting hers again. “About the nature of those circles.”

The nature of those circles. The phrase was a polished stone, smooth from handling. It meant Pauline. It meant the smoky Left Bank cafes where they’d argued politics and art, where hands had brushed under tables, where a life had seemed possible. It meant the coded lyrics she’d written during the war, the ones that felt clever and brave at the time, little paper airplanes of defiance. It meant the photograph she knew he had, the one of her and Pauline on the Pont des Arts, their heads tilted together, laughing at a joke lost to time. Arthur had the negative. He had everything.

Her hand, the one holding the letter began to tremble. A faint, involuntary quiver in the fingers. She willed it to stop. It didn’t.

“I see,” she said.

“I know you do.” He spoke quieter now, just a shade. It was the voice he used to close deals. “The Institute is a solution. It gets you off the stage, which is what they want. It puts you in a. Supervised environment. It gives you a new story to tell. A professor. A mentor. It’s a good role.”

A role. Of course. It was all a performance. The marriage, the career, the carefully curated sadness of “The Canary.” Now this. Professor Finch. She wondered if the students would see the seams.

“And Pauline?” The name felt dangerous in her mouth, a shard of glass.

Arthur’s expression didn’t change, but something cooled behind his eyes. “Pauline Carter runs the theatre department. She’s a respected figure. A radical, but respected. Your association with her, in this new context could be seen as a positive. A return to serious artistic pursuits. As long as it remains professional.” He let the word professional sit there, heavy with implication. “She can be part of the narrative, if she’s managed.”

Managed. Like a difficult actor or a tricky key change. Iris looked back at the letter. The black type seemed to pulse. Clarify. Associations. Cooperation.

“What do they want me to say?”

“They want you to confirm things. Names, dates, meetings. They want to know who was serious, and who was just playing at revolution. They want you to help them separate the wheat from the chaff.” He finished his whiskey. “Your friend Pauline. She’s made a name for herself with that last play. The one about the dockworkers. It got attention. The wrong kind, perhaps.”

A cold knot formed in Iris’s stomach. It wasn’t fear, not exactly. It was a colder, quieter thing, a sinking sensation of understanding. This wasn’t just about her. They were using her to get to Pauline, to the Institute, to the whole vibrant, dangerous world of artists who asked the wrong questions. Arthur was offering her a bargain: her silence, her performance as a reformed artist, in exchange for a target on someone else’s back.

“I’m to be the reformed sinner,” she said flatly. “Pointing out the other sinners.”

“You’re to be a responsible citizen,” Arthur corrected, his voice mild. “Sharing what you know for the good of the country. It’s a patriotic duty. And it keeps you safe.” He stepped closer took the letter from her numb fingers, and slid it back into its envelope. “Think of the teaching as rehearsal. You learn the part. You perform it flawlessly. And everyone gets to go home when the curtain falls.”

He placed the envelope on the sideboard, next to the empty whiskey glass. “You have an appointment with Miss Carter tomorrow at eleven. I’d suggest wearing the gray suit. It’s authoritative without being intimidating.” He gave her a brief, practiced smile, the one that didn’t reach his eyes. “I have a meeting with a producer about a new patriotic medley for the USO. Broad appeal. Very wholesome. It could be a good next act for you, once this. Academic interlude is settled.”

He left the room then, his footsteps retreating down the hall. A door closed softly. The apartment settled back into its profound quiet.

Iris stared at the envelope. The click of the clothesline pin outside continued its erratic beat. Click. Pause. She became aware of a tiny, sharp pain in her palm. She looked down. Her fingernails had dug into her skin, leaving four crescent moons of white that slowly flushed red. She hadn’t felt herself doing it. She uncurled her fist, watched the blood return.

On the bookshelf, the volume of Keats leaned slightly against the biography of Toscanini. She had read that Keats book in Paris, lying on a thin mattress in a room that smelled of rain and garlic. She had underlined a line about negative capability. Being in uncertainties, mysteries doubts, without any irritable reaching after fact and reason. She had thought it was the most beautiful, impossible thing she’d ever read.

She walked to the bookshelf, pulled the Keats out. The spine cracked. The yellowed page fell open to the underlined passage. In the margin, in her own youthful, looping script, she had written a single word: Yes.

She closed the book. The patriotic medley, the gray suit, the informal conversation, the managed narrative—it was all fact and reason. It was all a terrible, irritable reaching. She slid the book back into its place, aligning it perfectly with the shelf’s edge. The envelope on the sideboard seemed to glow in the flat morning light. A single, cream-colored rectangle containing the next line of her script. She knew her cue. The curtain was already rising.

The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts smelled of old wood and lemon oil, with an undercurrent of sweat and something sweet, like cheap perfume left too long in a bottle. The main hall was a cavern of scuffed floors and high, grimy windows that let in long, dusty columns of afternoon light. Students moved through the space in twos and threes, their voices bouncing off the plaster walls in a low, constant hum. They were all so young. Their clothes were a riot of color and texture—a bright yellow scarf, a pair of trousers with a sharp, confident crease, a sweater darned at the elbow with thread that didn’t quite match. Iris stood just inside the doors, her own navy suit suddenly a uniform, a declaration of a different, more cautious world.

A young man with a stack of sheet music tucked under his arm glanced at her, his gaze lingering a moment too long on the cut of her jacket. Not recognition. Assessment. She was a new variable in his equation. He nodded, a quick, polite dip of the chin, and moved on.

Her instructions were to find the main rehearsal room. She followed the sound of a piano, a stumbling, repetitive phrase of Gershwin that someone was practicing with dogged, joyless determination. The hallway was lined with framed playbills, their edges curling. Porgy and Bess. Mamba's Daughters. Native Son. The names were a history she had watched from a distance, from the plush seats of Broadway houses where she was the entertainment, not the chronicler. One poster was for a production of the Little Foxes. Someone had drawn a tiny, delicate mustache on Regina Giddens in blue ink.

The piano stopped. A door stood ajar just ahead, the number 3 painted in black on the frosted glass. She pushed it open.

The room was large dominated by a wide, empty stage at one end. The floor was marked with tape in various colors, a cryptic geometry of blocked movements. Chairs were stacked haphazardly against one wall. The air was warmer here, thick with the smell of dust and effort. At the far side, near a bank of windows, a woman stood with her back to the door, writing on a large chalkboard. Her posture was straight, her shoulders square under a simple olive-green shirt. Her hair was shorter than Iris remembered, cropped close to her head in dark, precise waves.

Pauline.

The recognition was not a shock, but a slow, deep settling, like a stone finding its bed at the bottom of a pond. Iris’s mouth went dry. She noticed a coffee stain on the sleeve of her own jacket, a faint brown smudge near the cuff. She’d gotten it yesterday at the diner, wiping her mouth with a napkin that had left a trace. A ridiculous thing to notice now.

Pauline finished a word on the board—INTENTION—and turned, the chalk still in her hand. Her eyes found Iris immediately. They were the same dark, liquid brown, but the warmth Iris remembered had been replaced by a cool, appraising sharpness. The lines around them were deeper, etched by time and, Iris suspected, by a different kind of weathering than her own.

For a long moment, neither of them spoke. The only sound was the distant, resumed piano, hitting a wrong note and starting the phrase over.

“You’re late,” Pauline said. She said it lower, smoother. It held no surprise.

“The letter said today. It didn’t specify a time.”

“It said ten o’clock.” Pauline placed the chalk on the ledge of the board and dusted her hands together. A fine white powder drifted to the floor. “It’s nearly eleven. Punctuality is the first courtesy. Especially when you’re asking for a favor.”

“I’m not asking for a favor. I was offered a position.”

“By Arthur.” Pauline said the name like it was a specimen under glass. She took a few steps closer, but stopped well out of reach. Her gaze traveled from Iris’s shoes to her hair, a clinical inventory. “He called. To ‘prepare’ me. He said you needed a quiet place to land. That your. Current narrative was becoming problematic.”

The word “narrative” in Pauline’s mouth was an accusation. It stripped Arthur’s euphemism bare.

“He manages my career,” Iris said. The defense sounded hollow even to her.

“He manages your containment.” Pauline’s smile was thin, without humor. “But that’s not my business. My business is this institute. We train artists here. We don’t provide hiding places for faded celebrities.”

The sting was precise, surgical. Iris felt it not as anger, but as a hot flush across her collarbones. She focused on the chalkboard. Below INTENTION, Pauline had written ACTION and OBSTACLE, with arrows connecting them in a tight, constant loop.

“Then why agree to see me?”

“Because Arthur implied the alternative for you was worse. And because,” Pauline paused, her eyes narrowing slightly, “I was curious. To see what twelve years of the wrong life does to a person.”

She said it plainly, a statement of fact. There was no malice in it, which was worse. It was simply the conclusion of an old experiment.

“You look well,” Iris said, the automatic social script rising to her lips.

“I look forty-six.” Pauline crossed her arms. “You look like you’re dressed for a funeral. Or a board meeting. Neither of which is appropriate for a rehearsal room. This isn’t the Copa. We don’t perform for tips here.”

A student appeared in the doorway then, a young woman with braids piled high on her head. “Miss Carter? They need you in the office about the lighting order for Deep Are the Roots.”

“Tell them I’ll be there in five minutes, Lena.”

The girl nodded, her eyes flicking to Iris with open curiosity before she disappeared.

The interruption broke the spell of the room. The sounds from the hall seeped back in—a laugh, a scrap of sung scales, the practicing piano.

“I have a class in twenty minutes,” Pauline said, her tone shifting into something brisk, professional. “You can observe. What would be expected, if this arrangement proceeds.” She moved to a table and gathered a sheaf of papers into a worn leather folder. “The topic today is subtext. Speaking the unspeakable. It might be a challenge for you. I understand your recent work has been rather. Literal.”

Another precise cut. Iris felt unsteady, as if the floor had tilted a few degrees. She remembered the feel of Pauline’s skin, the exact temperature of it in the morning light of that attic room in Paris. The memory had no place here. It was a ghost in a room full of living, breathing purpose.

“I can handle subtext,” Iris said.

Pauline looked at her, really looked at her, for the first time. Her expression was unreadable. “Can you?” She picked up her folder. “We’ll see. The classroom is down the hall to the left. Don’t be late again. The students notice. They’re always watching.”

She walked out, leaving Iris alone in the rehearsal room. The silence she left behind was different from the apartment’s quiet. This was an active silence, crowded with the echoes of voices and movements, waiting to be filled again. Iris looked at the chalkboard. INTENTION. ACTION. OBSTACLE. The words seemed to pulse on the dark slate.

On the floor near the table, half-hidden by a leg was a single, discarded earring. A small, faux pearl on a cheap gold post. It looked lost. Iris had the sudden, irrational urge to pick it up, to put it in her pocket. She didn’t. She turned and walked toward the door, her heels clicking a measured, solitary rhythm against the marked floor.

The letter lay on the sideboard, a white rectangle against the dark wood. Iris couldn’t look at it. She found herself in the bathroom, running the tap until the water steamed. The mirror was fogged, her reflection a pale smudge. She pressed her fingertips to the glass, watching the condensation bead and run. A pointless gesture. The smell of Arthur’s shaving soap, a sharp, medicinal pine, filled the small room. It was a smell she associated with order, with control. Today it made her stomach turn.

She dressed with deliberate care: a gray wool skirt, a cream blouse, a single strand of pearls. Armor. The pearls felt cool against her throat. She checked her lipstick in the cleared patch of mirror. The Canary looked back, composed, a little tired. It would have to do.

The subway to Harlem was crowded, a press of wool coats and damp umbrellas. She stood, holding a strap, watching the stations blur past in the grimy window. A man across from her was reading a newspaper. The headline shouted about spies. He turned the page with a crisp snap. The sound was like a bone breaking.

She was late. The imposing stone facade of the Institute seemed to lean over the sidewalk. Students flowed around her, a river of young voices and bright scarves. Their energy was a physical thing, a hum in the air. She felt ancient, a fossil washed up on a strange shore. The main doors were heavy, carved oak. They opened into a vast, echoing foyer. The floor was scuffed marble, the walls hung with portraits of severe-looking men and a few women. The air smelled of lemon polish and, faintly, of sweat.

A young woman at a reception desk directed her to the third floor, Studio C. Her heels clicked on the terrazzo stairs, a solitary metronomic sound. From behind a closed door on the second floor, a piano played something complicated and dissonant. The notes chased each other, unresolved.

Studio C was a large, high-ceilinged room. One wall was mirrored. The others were painted a dull, institutional green, scarred with tape marks and forgotten smudges. Sunlight fell in dusty shafts through tall, grimy windows. A group of students—eight or nine of them—were clustered in the center of the wooden floor. They were all Black, dressed in simple rehearsal clothes. And they were utterly still.

Pauline stood before them. She had her back to the door.

Iris stopped on the threshold, unseen. Pauline wore a simple black turtleneck and trousers, her hair cut close to her skull. Her posture was straight, but not rigid. She held a script loosely in one hand. She wasn’t speaking. She was waiting.

One of the students, a young man with a nervous energy, broke first. “I don’t get it. He’s just standing there. What’s the action?”

Pauline didn’t turn. She spoke, the sound calm, low. “He’s waiting for the bus, Samuel. What do you do when you wait for the bus?”

“You wait.”

“Do you?” Pauline finally moved, turning slowly to face the group. Her profile was sharp against the light from the windows. “You check your watch. You shift your weight. You look down the empty street. You feel the coins in your pocket. You remember you’re going to be late. You think about the reason you’re going. Your body is still. Your mind is a riot. That is the action.”

Iris’s breath caught. The timbre of Pauline’s voice was deeper than she remembered, weathered. But the cadence, the way she let a thought hang in the air until it demanded an answer—that was the same. A memory surfaced, unbidden and complete.

Paris, 1937. A basement club so smoky the ceiling was a gray haze. They were squeezed into a corner table, knees touching under the scarred wood. Pauline was arguing with a painter from Lyon about Brecht, her hands cutting the air. Iris was supposed to be networking, charming a producer across the room. Instead, she watched the lamplight catch the sweat on Pauline’s temple, the passionate line of her throat as she spoke. She wasn’t listening to the words. She was listening to the music underneath them, the conviction that vibrated in every syllable. Later, in the cramped bathroom, Pauline had turned to her, eyes bright. “They talk about alienation like it’s a theory. It’s not. It’s in here.” She’d tapped her own sternum. “A hollow space. You have to let the audience feel that hollow space.” Iris had kissed her then, tasting wine and cigarette smoke, wanting to fill that hollow with something warm and real.

The memory was so vivid Iris could smell the damp wool of her own coat, hear the clatter of a dropped glass from the bar. The present room seemed to tilt.

“Let’s try it again,” Pauline said. “From ‘I guess the bus is late.’ Not with your voice. With your spine.”

The students rearranged themselves. The young man, Samuel took his position. He let his shoulders slump, just a fraction. He stared at a point on the floor. His fingers twitched at his side. The silence in the room changed. It became charged, attentive.

Iris saw it. The hollow space. The boy wasn’t acting waiting; he was waiting, freighted with a specific, invisible dread. It was terrifying. It was the most honest thing she’d seen on any stage. Her own performances—the arched eyebrow, the wistful sigh, the carefully placed tear—felt like gaudy costume jewelry in comparison. A flush of shame heated her skin.

Pauline watched, her head tilted. “Better.” A single word of praise that seemed to land on the boy’s shoulders like a tangible weight. He stood a little taller.

Then Pauline’s gaze lifted, past her students, and found Iris in the doorway.

Time didn’t stop. It did something worse: it folded. The girl from the Paris basement and the woman in the gray wool suit existed in the same instant, in the same line of sight. Pauline’s expression didn’t change. No surprise. No anger. No welcome. It was a flat, assessing look, the look a director gives a new piece of stage furniture. She held the look for three heartbeats. Then she gave a small, almost imperceptible nod—not to Iris, but to the room—and turned back to her students.

“Again,” she said, her voice unchanged. “And this time, Samuel, let us see the moment you give up hope that it’s coming at all.”

The dismissal was absolute. Iris was a ghost. She took a step back, then another. Her heel caught on the uneven floorboard at the threshold. She stumbled, catching herself on the doorframe. The sound was horribly loud in the quiet room.

None of the students turned. Pauline didn’t turn.

Iris walked away down the long corridor. The piano from the second floor had stopped. The only sound was the frantic, ragged rhythm of her own breathing, which she tried, and failed, to quiet. She passed a bulletin board thick with flyers. One advertised a lecture: “Art as Weapon: The Federal Theatre Project Reconsidered.” Another, a dance recital. A bright yellow poster, slightly askew, announced auditions for a new play. Port Chicago, it said. By Pauline Carter.

She had come here for a job, for a narrative. Arthur’s plan. What she had found was an excavation. Pauline wasn’t just teaching acting. She was teaching her students how to hold truth in their bodies, how to make it visible. And she had looked at Iris and seen a woman who had spent twenty years learning how to hide it.

The hollow space. It wasn’t in the characters on a stage. It was in her. It had always been in her. Pauline had known it in Paris. She knew it now.

Iris fumbled in her purse for a cigarette. Her hands were unsteady. The first drag tasted of nothing. She looked up at the Institute’s windows wondering which one was Studio C. The glass reflected the flat white sky, giving nothing back.
