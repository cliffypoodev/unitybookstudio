The silence after the last voice fell was not the same as the silence before it began. This one had texture. It held the shape of the words just spoken, the ghost of the explosion they described. Iris sat perfectly still in her folding chair, the metal seat digging a cold line across the backs of her thighs. The script in her lap was a dead weight. She stared at a water stain on the scuffed floorboards a few feet away—a brownish bloom with tendrils like a crude starfish. It was the most interesting thing in the world.

People were getting up, the scrape of chair legs, the soft rustle of coats being gathered. Muttered compliments floated past her. “Powerful stuff.” “That ending.” She didn’t look up. Her throat was a closed pipe. The taste of the institute’s bitter coffee, gulped down hours ago, lingered at the back of her tongue.

A hand touched her shoulder. She flinched.

“You’re still here.” Pauline’s voice came from above and behind her. It wasn’t a question. Iris watched the starfish stain. If she looked at it long enough, it might start to move.

“I was just leaving,” Iris said. Her own voice sounded thin, borrowed.

“No, you weren’t.”

Pauline moved around the chairs and sat in the one next to her, the one Cora had vacated. She didn’t speak. She just sat, her own copy of the script resting on her knees. The rehearsal hall was emptying, the last of the students and faculty drifting out into the Harlem evening, their conversations fading down the corridor. A single work light high above them hummed, a low, steady insect noise. It cast hard shadows, carving Pauline’s profile into something severe and unfamiliar.

Iris’s hands were cold. She rubbed them together, a dry, whispery sound. The call from the agent was a stone in her stomach. Obtain a copy of the script. Note any revisions. She had the script. It was right there. She had made notes in the margin during the reading, little penciled ticks and queries about rhythm. They were innocent. They could be made to look like something else.

“You didn’t speak,” Pauline said finally.

“I read my lines.”

“You read the words. That’s not the same thing.” Pauline turned her head. Her eyes were dark pools in the shadow. “You held back. I heard it. Everyone heard it. You were measuring the distance between you and the part, like you were worried about getting your shoes dirty.

A hot flush climbed Iris’s neck. It wasn’t anger. It was shame, but a complicated kind, laced with a weary defiance. “It’s a first reading. We were sight-reading.

“Don’t.” The word was flat, final. “Don’t give me that club singer excuse. Not here.

The air between them tightened. Iris focused on the starfish stain. One of its tendrils was longer than the others. It pointed toward the stage like an accusation.

“Why did you ask me to do this, Pauline?” The question came out before she could stop it, a raw thing.

Pauline was quiet for a long time. The radiator in the corner knocked twice. “Because you’re the only one who can play a woman who watches,” she said, her voice lower now. “Who stands by and lets it happen. You understand the geometry of complicity.

The words landed like a physical blow. Iris’s breath caught. She thought of Arthur’s file, of the agent’s calm, reasonable voice. She thought of Paris, of walking away from the café table, from Pauline’s stunned silence, the sound of her own heels on the cobblestones the loudest thing in the world. The geometry of complicity. Yes. She knew the angles.

“It’s a good play,” Iris managed.

“It’s a dangerous play. You know that. Or you should.” Pauline leaned forward, elbows on her knees. The script slipped to the floor. She didn’t retrieve it. “Leo Strauss is coming to the next rehearsal.

The name was a jolt. “Strauss? Why?

“Funding. His money comes with strings. Pretty, gilded strings.” Pauline’s smile was a brief, grim flicker. “He’s interested in you. Thinks your ‘redemption arc’ could be good box office. Wants to discuss a patriotic medley for you to perform at a benefit. Something to… contextualize your involvement.

The pieces clicked together with an almost audible sound. The agent’s pressure. Strauss’s sudden interest. It wasn’t a coincidence. It was a pincer movement. They wanted to frame the narrative, to turn her defiance into a product, her danger into a selling point. The realization was a cold trickle down her spine. They weren’t just threatening her; they were trying to use her, to monetize the very fear they were stoking.

“He’s working with them,” Iris said, the words barely a whisper.

Pauline went very still. “With who?

“The committee. The agent. They’re two sides of the same coin. One applies the pressure, the other offers the commercial release valve.” She looked at Pauline then, really looked at her. “He’s not saving me. He’s selling tickets.

Pauline held her gaze. There was no surprise in her face. Only a deep, weary confirmation. “I know.

“You knew?”

“I suspected. His office made inquiries weeks ago, before you even arrived. Asked about the play’s themes, about your suitability. It was too… orchestrated.” Pauline sighed, a long, tired exhale. “He thinks he’s being clever. Using their witch hunt to create buzz. Neutralizing you by making you a headline.

The cynicism of it was so vast it was almost beautiful. Iris felt a strange, hollow laugh bubble up in her chest. She swallowed it. It tasted like metal. “So what do we do?

“We do the play.” Pauline’s voice was iron. “We do it right in front of him. And we do not give him a single note he can sell.

“He’ll want changes. He’ll want the edges sanded down.

“Let him want.”

They sat in the humming silence again. The starfish stain seemed to pulse in the corner of Iris’s vision. A memory surfaced, unbidden and irrelevant: the smell of hot wax and damp wool in a Parisian cathedral, a tourist clutching a guidebook, Pauline whispering a blasphemous joke in her ear that made her snort into her glove. The ache of that lost, easy intimacy was a physical pain.

“I can’t be your canary in this cage, Pauline,” Iris said softly. “Not if it means leading the wolf to your door.

Pauline reached down and picked up her fallen script. She brushed a speck of dust from its cover. “The wolf is already at the door. He has been for years.” She stood up, the chair creaking in relief. “The question isn’t whether he gets in. The question is what he hears when he does. A pretty song he can put on a record? Or a truth that will keep him up at night?

She didn’t wait for an answer. She walked toward the hall’s double doors, her footsteps echoing in the empty space. At the threshold, she paused, a dark silhouette against the dim light of the corridor.

“Be here tomorrow,” she said, not turning around. “And this time, get your shoes dirty.

Then she was gone.

Iris sat alone under the humming light. She looked from the starfish stain to the script in her lap, then to the empty stage where the ghost of an explosion still hung in the air. She picked up her pencil. Slowly, deliberately, she drew a single, dark line through one of her innocuous margin notes. Then she began to write another.

The costume room was a tomb of forgotten gestures. Iris stood just inside the door, her hand still on the light switch. The single bulb overhead did little more than push the shadows into the corners, where they gathered like dust. The air smelled of mothballs and old sweat, a sweet-rotten scent that caught in the back of her throat. Racks of clothing stood in silent rows, shrouded in clear plastic that gleamed dully in the weak light. A headless mannequin leaned against a far wall, one arm raised in a perpetual, unanswered hail.

Pauline had given her the key. “The uniform for Port Chicago is in there somewhere. Navy issue, 1944. See if it fits. If it doesn’t, we’ll make it fit.” She’d said it like an order, but her eyes had held something else—a challenge, or maybe just a tired curiosity to see what Iris would do alone in the dark with a ghost’s clothes.

Iris moved between the racks, her shoes whispering on the concrete floor. The plastic shrouds brushed her arms, cold and slick. She passed gowns from forgotten revues, their sequins dulled to the color of old fish scales. A row of military jackets, arms stiffly at attention. A cluster of peasant blouses from some production of The Cherry Orchard. The past was here, sorted by era and usefulness, waiting to be borrowed.

She found the navy section. It wasn’t large. A few officer’s coats, a pair of bell-bottom trousers hanging like empty legs. And there, on a wire hanger: the woman’s uniform. A navy-blue jacket with brass buttons, a matching skirt, a white cotton blouse. It looked small. Insignificant. She unhooked the hanger and held the jacket up to the light. The wool was coarse under her fingers. A small, fraying patch on one sleeve where a rating badge might have been.

Her own hands looked alien holding it. These were singer’s hands. They knew the weight of a microphone, the cool sweat of a highball glass. They didn’t know this.

On impulse, she shrugged out of her own cardigan and slid her arms into the jacket. The lining was cold, slick against her skin. It was tight across the shoulders—a different build, a different life. She buttoned it. The brass was cool against her chest. She looked down at herself. The jacket ended at her hips. The skirt still hung on the hanger. She didn’t put it on.

Instead, she turned to a full-length mirror propped against a rack of Victorian crinolines. The glass was speckled with age, a galaxy of black spots. Her reflection was fractured, a woman in a too-tight navy jacket standing in a sea of shadows. Her face was pale, her hair a dark shock against the stiff collar. She looked like someone playing dress-up. Or like someone being erased.

A memory surfaced, not as a story but as a sensation: the smell of damp wool and ozone. London, 1943. A different uniform—a Red Cross volunteer’s overcoat, scratchy at the neck. She’d been singing at a canteen for troops shipping out. A young soldier, his face still soft with boyhood, had asked for “Something to remember.” Not a song. A phrase. A code. He’d slipped her a scrap of paper with three words written in pencil. Tell Martha yes. She’d sung “I’ll Be Seeing You,” and during the instrumental break, she’d leaned into the microphone and murmured, “Martha says yes,” as if it were a lover’s secret. The boy’s shoulders had slumped with relief. He’d boarded his transport the next morning. She never learned who Martha was, or what the yes meant. It was just a phrase, passed along. A piece of encryption.

She’d done that a dozen times. Maybe more. Little phrases tucked into songs, into banter between numbers. It had felt like a game. A secret. Arthur had known, of course. He’d called it “patriotic ambiance.” He’d kept a list. She’d seen it once on his desk, a column of initials and dates. He’d covered it with his ledger when she walked in.

The jacket felt heavier now. It wasn’t a costume. It was evidence. The agent wanted the script. Arthur had the list. Strauss wanted to sell the redemption. They all wanted to use the encryption—to turn her little acts of secret mercy into a ledger of guilt, or a product.

A wave of dizziness passed through her, a hollow, swaying feeling behind her eyes. She put a hand out to steady herself, her fingers brushing against the plastic shroud of the next rack. Something clattered to the floor.

She looked down. A prop pistol, a cheap stage piece with a painted wood handle. It had fallen from a shelf above the rack. She bent to pick it up. The handle was dusty. The trigger didn’t move. It was just a shape, an idea of a weapon.

But her fingers curled around it anyway. The shape was familiar. The weight was wrong, but the intention—the intention of the object was clear. She straightened and looked back into the mirror. The woman in the navy jacket now held a gun. The image was wrong, disjointed. A clerk with a weapon. A bystander armed.

A sound made her freeze. A soft scrape, from the doorway.

She turned, the prop pistol still in her hand.

Cora stood just inside the room, a laundry basket piled with towels balanced on her hip. She didn’t look surprised. Her gaze traveled from Iris’s face to the navy jacket, to the gun in her hand. Her expression didn’t change.

“Looking for mutiny, Miss Finch?”

Iris lowered her hand, feeling foolish. “It fell.

“They do that.” Cora set the basket down on a wooden table scarred with paint spills. She began folding towels with quick, efficient motions. “The uniform fit?

“It’s tight.”

“They starved the women’s corps. Smaller rations. The jackets were cut for boys.” Cora didn’t look up from her folding. “Pauline said you’d be in here. Said to leave you to it. But the light was on.

“I was just leaving.”

Cora nodded, folding a towel into a perfect rectangle. “You know, my cousin Bernice wore that uniform. Port Chicago. She was a clerk-typist. Second class. She typed the manifests. The lists of what was in the holds.” She placed the folded towel on the pile. “She never talked about the blast. Talked about the typing. How her fingers would ache from the keys. How the carbon paper would stain her hands blue for days. Said it got under her nails and wouldn’t come out.

Iris looked down at her own hands. No stains.

“When they asked her afterwards, at the inquiry, what she knew, she said she knew how to type. That’s all. She just typed what they gave her.” Cora finally looked at Iris. Her eyes were calm, deep pools in the dim light. “They gave her a medal for her testimony. A little pin. She threw it in the river.

The silence that followed was a living thing. It held the ghost of typewriter keys, the imagined splash of a medal in dark water.

“Why are you telling me this?” Iris asked, her voice barely a whisper.

Cora picked up another towel. “Because you’re standing there holding a fake gun and looking at yourself in the mirror like you’re trying to find a villain. The villain isn’t in the mirror, Miss Finch. The villain is the one who handed you the script. The one who gives you the list to type.” She smoothed the towel flat. “Bernice spent thirty years wondering if the stain was still under her nails. Wondering if she should have looked at the manifests instead of just typing them. Wondering is a heavy thing to carry. Heavier than a gun.

She finished folding, hefted the basket again, and turned to leave. At the door, she paused. “The light switch is on the outside. Don’t let the door lock behind you. It sticks.

Then she was gone, her footsteps fading down the corridor.

Iris stood alone in the quiet tomb. She looked back at the mirror. The woman in the navy jacket stared back, her face all angles and shadows. The prop gun hung limp at her side.

Slowly, she unbuttoned the jacket. The brass buttons were stubborn. She shrugged it off, the cold air hitting her skin, and placed it back on the hanger. She hung it neatly on the rack, between the officer’s coat and the empty trousers.

Her own cardigan was draped over a chair. She put it on. The familiar soft wool was a relief.

She walked to the door, turned off the light, and stepped into the hallway, pulling the door shut behind her. The lock clicked with a solid, final sound. She tested the handle. It didn’t move.

The corridor was empty. The only sound was the distant hum of the building at night. She walked back toward the rehearsal hall, her footsteps echoing. The taste of mothballs was still in her mouth, a bitter ghost. She thought of blue stains under fingernails, of a medal sinking in a river, of a young soldier’s relieved shoulders. A chain of small complicities, stretching back through the years.

She didn’t have a medal to throw away. All she had was a script, and a choice of what to type.

The apartment smelled of lemon polish and stale tobacco. Iris stood just inside the door, her coat still on, keys cold in her hand. The afternoon light slanted through the slats of the blinds, cutting the living room into precise, dust-moted stripes. Arthur sat in his usual armchair by the dormant fireplace, a ledger open on his lap. He didn’t look up.

“You’re late.” His voice was calm, a statement of fact.

“Rehearsal ran long.” She shrugged out of her coat, the wool catching on the rough skin of her knuckles. She hung it on the stand, taking a moment too long to align the sleeves. The silence stretched. A clock ticked in the hallway, a steady, metallic click. She noticed a chip in the plaster above the doorframe, a tiny white scar she’d never seen before.

“The Institute’s accountant called today.” Arthur turned a page. The sound was crisp, like a dry leaf. “There’s a discrepancy in the petty cash ledger for the theatre department. A small one. Twenty dollars.

Iris’s stomach tightened. She walked to the sideboard, poured a finger of gin into a cut-glass tumbler. The liquid was the color of weak sunlight. “And?

“And he thought I should know, as your representative. Given your… involvement.” He finally looked at her. His eyes were pale, the color of winter sky. “He said Miss Carter was quite cavalier about it. Called it a ‘creative expense.’” He let the phrase hang. It wasn’t an accusation. It was a specimen, laid out for examination.

“Pauline handles the theatre budget. I teach voice. I don’t touch the money.” She took a sip. The gin burned a clean, familiar path.

“Of course you don’t.” He closed the ledger, set it aside. “But you’re associated. Your name is on the letterhead now. ‘Visiting Artist.’ Your choices reflect on the institution. And, by extension, on me.

There it was. She leaned against the sideboard, the edge digging into her hip. The room was too perfect. Every book was aligned, every ashtray empty. It was a museum of their arrangement. She thought of the rehearsal hall, the scuffed floors, the starfish stain, the electric hum of a single work light. The chaos of real work.

“I’m doing the job you arranged for me, Arthur.”

“Are you?” He steepled his fingers. “The agent called again. This morning. He says your reports have become… cursory. Vague. He says you’re no longer providing useful context.

The stone in her stomach grew colder, heavier. She didn’t speak.

“He mentioned Leo Strauss’s visit. He seemed pleased. Said it was a positive development, a sign of your reintegration into the mainstream.” Arthur’s gaze was steady, unblinking. “But I wonder. I saw you last night, after you got home. You went straight to the piano. Not to practice. You were writing. I heard you. Three notes, over and over. A descending line. It sounded like a question.

A flush crept up her neck. She’d thought he was asleep. She’d been chasing a phrase, a rhythm for a monologue she’d never get to perform. It was a stupid, private rebellion. “I was working on an exercise. For the students.

“No.” The word was quiet, absolute. “You weren’t. You’ve changed, Iris. Your posture is different. You hold your head like you’re listening for something I can’t hear. You come home with chalk dust on your sleeves.” He paused. “You look at me as if I’m a piece of the furniture you’ve suddenly decided you don’t like.

The accuracy of it stole her breath. She put the glass down. It made a sharp sound on the polished wood.

“This play,” he said. “This Port Chicago. It’s not just a play, is it? It’s a rallying cry. And you’re standing in the middle of it. Letting them use your name, your face, to give it legitimacy.” He stood up, smooth and controlled. He walked to the fireplace, adjusted a porcelain figurine of a shepherdess by a millimeter. “The agent believes Strauss is leveraging the committee’s interest to create a marketable controversy. He’s not a savior. He’s a speculator. And you… you are the commodity being shorted. The danger is the product.

Iris’s mouth went dry. So he knew. He’d pieced it together, or the agent had told him. The two mechanisms of her life—the commercial and the political—were gears in the same grinding machine.

“Because I need you to understand the geometry.” He turned to face her. His expression was not angry. It was analytical. “Your value has always been in your controllability. Your voice, your image, your story. I have spent twenty years building that control. Protecting it.” He took a slow breath. “That file in my study isn’t just insurance, Iris. It’s a blueprint. It details every person you associated with from ’37 to ’45. Every letter with a coded turn of phrase. Every photograph with a face that later turned up on a watchlist. It doesn’t just prove you loved a woman. It suggests you knew exactly what you were doing when you wrote ‘Midnight in Montmartre.’ It suggests you were, at the very least, a sympathetic courier.

The room seemed to tilt. The stripes of light on the carpet blurred. She focused on the shepherdess’s placid, painted smile.

“If you continue down this path—if you lend your name to Pauline’s propaganda, if you withhold from the agent, if you become uncontrollable—I will have no choice.” His voice was gentle, almost regretful. “I will provide the committee with the blueprint. They will connect the dots you’ve been so careful to keep separate. And Leo Strauss’s little redemption story will become a tragedy. You won’t be a canary in a gilded cage. You’ll be the reason the mine collapses on everyone inside it.

He wasn’t shouting. He was reading her a sentence, and the sentence was life without parole. The chill in her stomach spread to her limbs. She felt it in her fingertips.

“You would do that?” The question was a whisper.

“It’s not a question of would.” He came closer. She could smell his cologne, a sharp, clean scent like pine needles. “It’s a question of equilibrium. You are destabilizing the system. My job is to restore balance. By any means the system provides.

He reached out, not to touch her, but to straighten the collar of her blouse. His fingers were cool and dry. She didn’t move.

“Go to rehearsal tomorrow,” he said softly. “Be brilliant. But be careful. Your lines are written. Don’t try to improvise.

He picked up his ledger and walked out of the room. His footsteps were soundless on the carpet. Iris stood by the sideboard, listening to the clock tick. She looked at her glass of gin, the light caught in its facets. It looked like a jewel, or a piece of cut ice. She left it there, untouched. The silence after he left had a new quality. It wasn’t empty. It was full of the precise, terrifying shape of the box she was in. Every wall had just moved an inch closer.
