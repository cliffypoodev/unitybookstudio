The silence after Henderson’s voice was a different quality than the quiet of the empty theatre. That had been a neutral thing, a held breath. This was a vacuum, a sucked-out hollow where even the dust seemed to stop drifting. Iris’s mouth had gone paper-dry. She tasted the ghost of old stage paint and damp wool from his coat.

“A tragedy,” he repeated, as if tasting the word. “But tragedies need villains. Who do you suppose the villain is in this little production?”

She found her voice, but it came out flat, a thing rehearsed. “It’s a historical piece. About an accident.”

“Is it?” He moved another step into the light. The single bulb overhead cast his shadow long and thin across the scarred floorboards, a dark finger pointing at her feet. “Seems to me it’s about blame. About systems that grind men into paste and call it procedure. That’s a dangerous kind of story right now, Miss Finch. It asks the wrong questions.”

He wasn’t looking at her. He was looking at the script pages she’d dropped on the wooden crate. His gaze was a physical weight on the paper.

“It’s just a play,” she said.

“Nothing is just anything.” He finally met her eyes. The bland curiosity was gone, replaced by a weary, professional assessment. Like a mechanic estimating the cost of a repair. “You were talking about translation. About truth hiding in plain sight. I found that instructive. We have a similar problem, you see. We have people who sing one tune in public and whisper another in private. Our job is to ensure the public hears the correct melody. To harmonize the discordant notes.”

Her hands wanted to shake. She pressed them against the rough grain of her skirt. The fabric was a cheap cotton bought off a rack at Macy’s. She noticed a loose thread near the hem, a tiny curl of blue against the navy. She focused on that.

“I don’t know what you want me to say.”

“I want you to think.” He leaned against the proscenium arch, casual as a stagehand on a break. “About the company you’re keeping. This institute. Miss Carter. Her work has a certain political fragrance. Not overt. She’s too clever for that. It’s in the subtext. The choice of subject. Port Chicago. The wronged Negro sailor. It’s a sympathetic narrative, but it’s a narrative that points fingers at the Navy. At the government. That’s a choice.”

He let that hang. Somewhere in the building, a pipe groaned. The sound was human, a sigh of pain.

“And you,” he continued. “You’re here now. In her orbit again. After all these years. You’re either very brave or very foolish. Or you’re here because someone sent you.” He tilted his head. “Which is it?”

The question was a trap with the jaws wide open. Any answer would spring it. Denial would sound like guilt. Admission was impossible. The truth—that she was a cornered animal with nowhere else to go—was the most damning thing of all.

“Arthur sent me,” she said. It was the only safe part of the truth.

“Ah. Arthur.” Henderson nodded, as if this confirmed a private theory. “Your husband. Your manager. A man who understands the value of a good narrative. He’s been very cooperative. Providing context. Helping us understand your historical complexities.”

A cold knot tightened below her ribs. Cooperating. The word was so clean, so bureaucratic. It meant Arthur was handing over pieces of her life, curating the evidence. Making sure the committee’s translation matched his preferred version.

“He’s protective,” she heard herself say.

“Of course. Of your career. Your reputation.” Henderson pushed off the arch and took a slow turn around the edge of the playing space. His shoes made a soft, gritty sound on the boards. “Tell me about the play. Your role.”

The shift was so sudden it left her dizzy. “It’s a small part. A secretary in the naval office. She processes the paperwork. The manifests.”

“A functionary. She sees the numbers, but not the men.”

“Yes.”

“And does she know?” He stopped his pacing, directly opposite her now. “Does she know the ships are overloaded? That the safety protocols are a joke?”

Iris hesitated. Her throat clicked. “The script. It’s ambiguous. She suspects. But her job is to file the forms, not question them.”

“Complicity through procedure.” A faint, unpleasant smile touched his lips. “Interesting. And do you relate to that, Miss Finch? To the woman who follows orders, even when her conscience whispers?”

The rehearsal script was in her bag. The pages she’d been working on, the ones where she’d begun penciling in her own substitutions—a line from an old song here, a rhythm from a Parisian poem there. An encryption. He was standing too close to the bag. She had a sudden, vivid memory of a café on the Rue de Seine, the smell of Gauloises and burnt coffee, Pauline’s hand covering hers on a notebook as they crossed out a clumsy lyric. The truth is in the rhythm, Iris. Not the words. The hesitation.

“It’s acting,” Iris said, her voice firmer than she felt. “I’m trying to understand the character.”

“I’m sure you are.” He didn’t believe her. He didn’t need to. “Rehearsal is one thing. The performance is another. When the lights come up and the audience is waiting, that’s when choices become permanent. That’s when ambiguity becomes statement.” He glanced at his watch, a smooth, silver flicker in the dim light. “I’ve taken enough of your evening. You’ll be hearing from the committee soon. Formal interview. Think about what version of your history you’d like to present. The coherent one. The cooperative one.”

He didn’t say goodbye. He just turned and walked back into the shadows of the wing. His footsteps faded, then were stopped by the maze of backstage corridors. A distant door clicked shut.

Iris didn’t move. The cold knot in her stomach had melted into a low, sick thrum. She looked at the script pages on the crate. Her penciled notes in the margin looked childish now, a secret code a schoolgirl would invent. She thought of Arthur in his study, neat rows of files, each one a shard of her life he could arrange into any pattern he liked. A narrative. A translation.

She should have been afraid. And she was, a thin, wire-like tension running up her spine. But underneath it, something else moved. Something hotter and more defiant. It wasn’t anger, not quite. It was the same feeling she’d get mid-song when she’d deliberately flub a note the bandleader had written, bending it into a blue tone that made the audience sit up. A tiny, silent act of sabotage.

She walked to the crate, picked up the script. Her fingers left damp smudges on the paper. She found her pencil in the bag, her hand steady now. In the margin beside her character’s climactic silence—the moment where the secretary chooses to stamp the manifest without looking up—Iris wrote a single line, not from any song, but from a letter she’d never sent. The canary doesn’t sing for the mine. It sings because the air is gone.

It made no sense in the scene. It was a non sequitur, a fracture. She read it over, and a strange, quiet calm settled over her. It was a bad line. Pauline would hate it. It was hers.

She gathered her things. The theatre was a cavern of echoes again, but the silence felt different. It felt like a held breath waiting for a cue. She switched off the work light, plunging the stage into a darkness so complete it was like stepping off the edge of the world.

The silence after Henderson left was a different texture than the one he’d found. This was a brittle, ringing quiet, like the aftermath of a struck bell. Iris stood on the stage, her eyes on the exit door he’d used. The spot where he’d leaned against the proscenium arch seemed colder than the rest of the wood. She bent, slowly, and picked up the script pages from the crate. The paper was cool. She smoothed a corner that had curled.

A door at the back of the house creaked open. Not the exit. The side door to the wings. Light from the hall spilled in cutting a yellow rectangle across the floorboards. Pauline stood in the frame. She held a stack of student folders against her chest. She didn’t speak.

Iris’s stomach dropped. A simple, physical sensation, as if the floor had tilted a degree. How long had she been there? The question was a dry leaf in her mind, rattling against the others. She had no air to voice it.

Pauline walked down the aisle, her shoes making no sound on the carpet runner. She stopped at the edge of the orchestra pit. Her face was a study in shadow and hard angles cast by the single work light.

“He’s gone,” Iris said. The words were useless, obvious.

“I saw.” Pauline’s voice was flat. “I was in the lighting booth. Checking a circuit. The acoustics in this place are terrible. You can hear a pin drop from the stage.” She placed the folders on a seatback. The movement was precise, controlled. “You can also hear a conversation.”

The cold knot below Iris’s ribs, the one Henderson had tied, tightened another notch. She folded the script pages in half, then in half again, making a small, thick square she could hold in one hand. The action was pointless. She did it anyway.

“He followed me here.”

“Did he.”

It wasn’t a question. Iris looked at the script in her hand. The folded edge was already fraying. She remembered a dress she’d owned in Paris, a green silk thing with a hem that constantly unraveled. She’d spent an entire evening at a party picking at the loose threads, one after another, until Pauline had taken her hand and stilled it. Leave it be. It gives it character.

“He was making a point,” Iris said.

“He succeeded.” Pauline stepped up onto the stage with a practiced ease that Iris envied. She stopped a few feet away, not looking at Iris, but at the space Henderson had occupied. “A functionary. Complicity through procedure. He’s a clever man. He knows how to pick a lock.”

“It was just a rehearsal.”

“Nothing is just anything.” Pauline echoed Henderson’s words without inflection. She finally turned her head. Her eyes were dark, unreadable pools in the poor light. “That’s what he said isn’t it? And he’s right. You were talking about translation. About truth hiding. You gave him the key and then you showed him the door.”

“I was talking to an empty room.”

“You were talking to yourself. Which is worse.” Pauline took a step closer. The scent of her reached Iris then—not perfume, but the smell of mimeograph ink and the faint, sweet odor of the glycerin soap they kept in the institute’s washroom. A clean, sharp smell. It was utterly, painfully familiar. “What did you tell Arthur?”

“Nothing.”

“Don’t lie to me. Not here.” Pauline’s voice was low, a wire pulled taut. “He’s been ‘cooperative.’ Providing context. What context, Iris? What has your husband been providing about my institute?”

The accusation hung between them. Iris felt a heat move up her neck—not quite shame, not quite anger, a sour blend of both. She thought of Arthur’s hands, neat and careful, sorting letters into chronological order. The quiet click of his filing cabinet.

“He has a file,” she said. The admission was a stone dropped into a well. She waited for the splash. “Letters. Photographs. From Paris. From during the war. He’s had it for years.”

Pauline didn’t move. Her expression didn’t change. But something in her posture solidified, as if she were bracing against a wind only she could feel. “Of course he does.”

“He says it’s insurance.”

“It’s a leash.” Pauline said it simply, a statement of fact. “And now he’s sharing it with his new friends. To prove what? That you were a romantic? That you had poor taste in companions?” She gave a short, humorless sound. “Or that the songs you wrote for troop morale had verses you never sang on the radio?”

The air left Iris’s lungs. It was a physical punch, soft and precise. She saw a flash of a cramped studio, a glass of gin sweating on the piano, a man with kind eyes and a press pass asking if she’d consider a special performance for some union organizers. She’d said yes. She’d written a bridge for it, a minor-key progression that suggested a question without ever asking it. Arthur had heard a rehearsal. He’d said it was maudlin. He’d suggested a different key.

“He wouldn’t,” she whispered. But it wasn’t true. He would. He was. Cooperating. Providing context.

“He already has.” Pauline closed the distance between them. She didn’t touch Iris. She stood so close that Iris could see the fine weave of her wool skirt, a small darn near the pocket that had been done with thread a shade too dark. A human detail. A repair. “That man, Henderson. He wasn’t here to scare you. He was here to assess the asset. To see how you’d react under pressure. To see if you’d run to me with a warning, or if you’d stand there and fold your script into a little square.”

Iris looked down at her hand. The paper square was damp from her palm. She forced her fingers to relax. “I didn’t know he was there.”

“But you knew he could be.” Pauline’s gaze was steady. “That’s the world you chose, Iris. The one with managers and files and convenient husbands. You traded a room of your own for a gilded hallway with someone else holding the key. And now the hallway has eyes.”

The old resentment was there, just beneath the surface, but it was colder now. Tempered by a newer, more immediate fear. It wasn’t about Paris anymore. It was about now. About this room.

“What do you want me to do?” The question was raw, stripped of performance.

Pauline was silent for a long moment. She looked past Iris, to the dark rows of seats, as if counting an absent audience. “I want you to understand the cost,” she said finally. Her voice had lost its wire edge, gone quiet and tired. “Not to you. To everyone else in this building. To every student in those folders. This play isn’t a game. It’s a match struck in a room full of old paper. You brought the federal government into my theatre tonight. You may not have meant to. But you did.”

She turned and walked to the edge of the stage, picking up her folders. She held them to her chest again, a shield. “Rehearsal is at ten tomorrow. Don’t be late.”

“Pauline.”

She stopped, but didn’t turn back.

The words crowded Iris’s throat—apologies, explanations, the frantic, stupid desire to explain that she’d been trying to understand, that the encryption was for her, a private rebellion. They all sounded hollow, childish. The silence stretched, thin and fragile.

“The loose thread,” Iris said, the words coming out before she knew she had them. “On your skirt. By the pocket. You used black thread, but the skirt is charcoal.”

Pauline glanced down, then back at Iris. A faint, bewildered line appeared between her brows. It was the wrong thing to say. It made no sense. It served nothing.

“It’s what I had,” Pauline said. Then she was gone, the door to the hall swinging shut behind her with a soft, definitive click.

Iris stood alone under the work light. She unfolded the script, smoothing the creases against her thigh. They wouldn’t come out. The paper was permanently marked. Somewhere in the walls, the building’s pipes groaned again, a long, low note of protest that faded into nothing.

The apartment smelled of lemon polish and the faint, sweet decay of Arthur’s pipe tobacco. He’d been cleaning. The ashtrays were empty, the surfaces wiped to a dull gleam. He sat at the small dining table, his back to her, a neat stack of papers before him. The morning light through the window was a pale, watery thing, catching the motes of dust his polishing had disturbed. They drifted in the still air like ash after a quiet fire.

Iris stood just inside the door, her keys cold in her hand. She’d walked most of the way home from the institute, her mind a blank white noise. The walk had taken over an hour. The heel of her left shoe had developed a click that hadn’t been there before. Click. Silence. It had kept time with her heartbeat.

“You’re late,” Arthur said, without turning. He spoke calmly, conversationally. He selected a paper from the stack, examined it, and placed it in a different pile. The sound was a dry whisper. “Your first class with Miss Carter’s advanced students is in forty minutes. I took the liberty of calling a cab. It will be here in ten.”

She let her bag slide from her shoulder. It thumped against the floor. The sound was too loud in the polished quiet. “Henderson was there last night. In the theatre.”

Arthur’s hand paused for a fraction of a second above the next paper. Then it continued, picking it up, turning it over. “I know.”

“You know.”

“He reported his observations. He’s thorough.” Arthur finally turned in his chair. His face was composed, but there were new lines at the corners of his mouth, tiny fissures in the calm. He looked tired. “He said you were rehearsing. That you gave quite a performance. A soliloquy on truth and translation.”

The cold knot from the theatre was back, a hard, familiar stone below her breastbone. She walked to the kitchenette, her movements stiff. The percolator was on the stove, cold. She picked it up and ran water into it. Her hands were steady. That surprised her. “He threatened me. Again. More clearly this time.”

“He clarified the stakes. There’s a difference.” Arthur watched her measure out the coffee. Two scoops. Three. The spoon clinked against the tin. “He’s not a subtle man, but he’s not incorrect. Your association with that play, with that place. It’s a provocation. It makes his job harder. It makes my job of protecting you considerably harder.”

“Protecting me.” She lit the gas. The blue flame hissed, a tiny, furious sound. “Is that what you call it?”

“What would you call it, Iris?” He leaned back, the chair creaking. “You were adrift. Your career was a memory. The wolves were at the door. I found you a harbor. A paying job. A measure of respectability. And what do you do? You immediately begin weaving a noose with the very rope I threw you.”

She turned. The coffee began to burble softly behind her. “It’s a teaching job. I’m teaching.”

“You’re immersing yourself in a production that is, at best, politically inconvenient. At worst, it’s a flag you’re choosing to wave in front of a bull. Henderson sees it. Pauline Carter, I’m sure, sees it as a glorious rebellion. And you?” He spread his hands, a gesture of pure, frustrated reason. “What do you see? A chance to atone? To win back her approval by playing the martyr?”

The words landed like physical blows. They were too close to the vague, shameful hope she hadn’t even let herself name. Her face grew hot. “It’s a part. A small part.”

“There are no small parts in a play like that. Only small actors who become large targets.” He stood up, smoothing the front of his waistcoat. He walked to the window, looked down at the street. “He wants a report. A simple one. Observations on the institute’s atmosphere. The political leanings of the faculty. The nature of Miss Carter’s conversations with her inner circle.”

“You want me to spy.”

“I want you to survive.” He turned from the window. His eyes were flat, the color of old slate. “This isn’t 1937, Iris. There’s no Left Bank to escape to. The world has shrunk. The walls have ears. Cooperation is the only currency that matters now.”

“And what do I tell him? That they talk about art? About history? That they want to tell a story everyone else wants to forget?”

“Tell him the truth. The harmless truth. That they are artists, concerned with their craft. That Pauline is passionate, perhaps too passionate, about giving voice to the voiceless. Use her own language back to him. It’s what he expects to hear. It confirms his suspicions without providing anything new. It’s a performance.” He took a step toward her. “You’re good at those.”

The coffee boiled over, a dark stream hissing onto the burner. She moved the percolator off the flame. The smell of burnt coffee joined the lemon and tobacco. A domestic tragedy in three scents.

“And if I refuse?”

Arthur was silent for a long moment. He walked back to the table, opened the drawer. He withdrew a plain manila folder, not thick, but substantial. He laid it on the table between them. “Then I cannot protect you from the consequences of your refusal. Henderson will proceed with his investigation. He will subpoena records. Interview acquaintances from the war years. He will find the connections you and I know are there. The file I hold”—he tapped the folder with one finger—“will become a matter of public record. It will no longer be insurance. It will be evidence.”

She stared at the folder. It was just paper. Cardstock and glue. It held photographs she remembered—Pauline laughing on a bridge, her own younger face turned up to a Parisian sun—and letters whose rhythms she could still feel in her bones. It held other things too. Lists of names from benefit concerts. Notes passed backstage at USO shows. Silly, earnest codes about freedom and peace that now read like manifestos.

“You would give that to him.”

“I would have no choice.” The words came out quiet, almost gentle. “It’s the bargain, Iris. Always has been. I manage the narrative. You follow the script. When you deviate, the narrative corrects itself. With or without you.”

The click of the taxi horn sounded from the street below. Two short bursts.

Arthur glanced at his watch. “Your cab. Don’t be late. First impressions matter.” He picked up the folder, slid it back into the drawer. The sound of the wood sliding shut was final.

Iris didn’t move. She looked at the empty space on the table where the folder had been. A memory surfaced, unbidden and pointless: the feel of a specific brand of French stationery, the way the pen nib sometimes caught on the fibrous paper, leaving a tiny tear in the ‘e’ of her signature. She had no idea why she thought of that now.

“What happens after the report?” she asked. She spoke quieter now, distant, someone else’s.

“Then we see. If you are seen to be cooperative, helpful, the pressure eases. The institute might even be left alone. For a while. You could have a life there, Iris. A quiet one. Teach your classes. Do your play. Keep your head down.” He handed her coat from the hook by the door. It was the navy one. He’d brushed it. “It’s not the life you dreamed of. But it is a life. It’s the one that’s left.”

She took the coat. The wool was soft under her fingers. Worn thin at the cuffs.

The horn sounded again, impatient.

She walked out the door without looking back at him, without the coffee she no longer wanted. The hallway smelled of cabbage and floor wax. The stairs creaked under her feet on the way down. Each step was a decision she wasn’t consciously making.

The taxi idled at the curb, exhaust puffing into the cold morning. She got in. The door closed with a solid thunk.

“Where to, lady?”

She gave the address of the institute. Her mouth formed the words automatically.

As the cab pulled away, she looked up at the window of her apartment. Arthur was there, a silhouette against the glass. He was not watching the taxi leave. He was looking down at the table, at his neat, terrible piles of paper, already moving on to the next calculation.
