The air in Pauline’s apartment smelled of turpentine and cigarettes, a smell Iris would later try to recreate with cheap gin and the wrong kind of tobacco. It never worked. The smell was the smell of linseed oil on canvas, of Gauloises bleues, of the particular dust that collected on the windowsills of the rue Delambre. It was the smell of the floorboards creaking underfoot, a sound like a ship’s deck in a gentle swell. It was the smell of wet wool drying on a radiator after a walk along the Seine, of the stew Pauline would make with whatever was left in the market stalls at dusk—potatoes, carrots, a tough bit of meat she’d simmer for hours until it surrendered.

Iris sat on the floor, her guitar across her lap. The floor was bare wood, scrubbed pale in patches. A single rug, woven in some mud-brown color, lay near the stove. She wasn’t playing. She was listening. Pauline stood at the easel, a brush in her hand, her back to Iris. She wasn’t painting. She was talking.

“They want a manifesto,” Pauline said. Her voice was low, steady. It didn’t rise to ask a question. “A statement of principles for the journal. Something to print in the first issue. Something that doesn’t sound like it was translated from German.”

“French. But not Parisian French. Something from the provinces. Something with dirt under its nails.”

Iris plucked a single string. The note hung in the air, a dry ping. “You could write it.”

“I have written it. Three times. It reads like a lecture.”

“So write it like a song.”

Pauline turned then. She held the brush like a conductor holds a baton, loose at the base, the tip pointed at Iris. “A song.”

“Not a love song. A work song. A song you sing while you’re breaking stones.”

Pauline’s eyes narrowed, but not in suspicion. In calculation. She looked at Iris as if she were a new pigment on the palette, something whose properties needed testing. “What would the words be?”

Iris didn’t have words. She had a rhythm. She tapped it out on the body of the guitar—Three quick beats, a pause, two slower ones. A heartbeat after running. A hammer striking, then resting. She hummed a fragment of melody, something she’d heard on the street, something a woman selling oysters had sung. It wasn’t a tune. It was a shape.

Pauline listened. She set the brush down on the lip of the easel. She walked to the small table by the window, picked up a notebook, came back. She sat on the rug, not beside Iris, but facing her. She opened the notebook. Her pencil moved.

Iris watched the pencil. It was a cheap thing, the wood stained dark from Pauline’s fingers. It moved in short, sharp lines. Not writing. Drawing. Iris saw the curve of a shoulder, the angle of a jaw. A face looking down. A hand holding a tool.

“That’s not a manifesto,” Iris said.

“It’s the person who would read one.”

Iris plucked the string again. The same note. “You’re avoiding the words.”

“Words are traps. Pictures are. openings.”

“Pictures can be traps too.”

Pauline looked up from the sketch. Her gaze was direct, unblinking. “Then we make a picture that is also a song. And the song is also an argument. And the argument is also a face.”

Iris smiled. It felt unfamiliar on her lips, a muscle she hadn’t used in years. “That’s a mess.”

“It’s a beginning.”

They worked that way for hours. Iris would find a rhythm, a fragment of melody. Pauline would draw. Sometimes she would write a line of text beneath the drawing—not an explanation, but a caption that tilted the image sideways. The stone does not know it is a wall. The hammer forgets its own hand.

The light changed. The sun slipped below the roofline of the opposite building, and the room filled with a grey, aqueous glow. Pauline lit the lamp. The oil flame made a soft, gulping sound. Iris’s fingers grew sore on the strings. She stopped.

Pauline was still drawing. She had moved to a new page, a fresh sheet. She was sketching Iris now. Not her face, but her hands on the guitar. The way her left hand curved around the neck, the way her right hand lay against the body. She drew the guitar as a box, a container. She drew Iris’s hands as birds resting on its edge.

“You’re making me look gentle,” Iris said.

“You are gentle.”

“I’m not.”

“When you play, you are.”

Iris looked at her own hands. They were not gentle. They were strong. They had calluses. They could grip and twist and pull. They had held other things—the handle of a suitcase, the rim of a glass, the small, cold weight of a railway ticket. “It’s a performance.”

“Everything is.”

The pencil stopped. Pauline studied the drawing. She added a single line, a shadow beneath the guitar’s bridge. Then she closed the notebook. She stood, stretched. Her body made a series of small cracks and pops, like a tree settling in the wind.

“I’m hungry,” she said.

They ate the stew. It was thick now, the meat soft. They ate it from bowls, sitting on the floor again, the lamp between them. Pauline talked about the journal—the printer they had found, the distributor who was sympathetic, the writers who had promised contributions. She talked about the money. There wasn’t much. A patron, a young man from a wealthy family in Lyon, had given them a sum. It was enough for the first issue. After that, they would need more.

“What does he want in return?” Iris asked.

“A revolution.”

“That’s cheap.”

Pauline laughed. It was a short, dry sound. “It’s the only thing he can’t buy himself.”

After the meal, Pauline washed the bowls in the sink. Iris wiped them dry. They put them away on the shelf. The ritual was quiet, precise. It felt like a liturgy. When the last bowl was placed, Pauline turned to Iris. She didn’t speak. She took Iris’s hand, the one that had held the drying cloth, and led her to the bed.

The bed was a narrow cot against the wall. The mattress was thin, stuffed with something that rustled when you moved. The blanket was wool, army grey. Pauline pulled it back. She sat on the edge, still holding Iris’s hand.

Iris sat beside her. The room was cold now. The stove had gone out. The lamp flickered.

“You’re leaving,” Pauline said.

It wasn’t a question. Iris felt the statement enter her body like a chill. She didn’t answer.

“The contract. The American label. You’ve decided.”

“I haven’t.”

“You have. You’re just waiting to say it.”

Iris looked at the lamp. The flame was a yellow tear, trembling in its glass womb. “It’s a chance. A real chance.”

“It’s a factory. They will put you in a machine and stamp you out like a coin.”

“I can make something first. Before they do that.”

“What?”

“Money. Security. A name.”

“You have a name here.”

“I have a room. I have a guitar. I have you.”

Pauline’s hand tightened. Her grip was fierce, almost painful. “That is a name.”

“It’s not a name that travels.”

Pauline released her hand. She stood up, walked to the easel. She picked up the brush she had left there. She held it out to Iris. “Take this.”

Iris took it. The brush was stiff, the hairs clogged with dried paint.

“I know.”

“Then you have already chosen.”

Iris put the brush down on the cot. She stood. She faced Pauline. The space between them was small, just a few feet. It felt vast. “It’s not a choice between you and the contract. It’s a choice between hunger and food. Between cold and warmth. Between being nobody and being somebody.”

“You are somebody.”

“I am nobody who plays for pennies in a basement. You are nobody who paints pictures no one will buy. We are nobodies together.”

Pauline’s expression did not change. It was a mask of calm, but Iris saw the tremor at the corner of her mouth, the slight tightening of the skin around her eyes. “Then go. Be a somebody alone.”

“I don’t want to be alone.”

“You do. You want to be alone with your money and your security and your name. You want to be alone in a warm room, with a full stomach, and no one to argue with about manifestos.”

“That’s not true.”

Iris felt a weight in her chest. It was not sadness. It was not anger. It was a solid thing, a block of stone. It sat behind her ribs, pressing against her breath. She wanted to argue. She wanted to list the reasons—the war coming, the rumors from Germany, the way her father’s voice had sounded in the last letter, tired and afraid. She wanted to say that safety was not a betrayal. It was a necessity.

But the words wouldn’t form. They stayed inside the stone.

She picked up her guitar. She didn’t strap it over her shoulder. She held it by the neck, like a suitcase. She walked to the door. She opened it.

The hallway was dark. The stairwell smelled of cabbage and damp plaster.

“I’ll come back,” she said. The words were thin, almost transparent.

Pauline did not turn. “You won’t.”

Iris stepped into the hallway. She closed the door behind her. The click of the latch was a small, final sound.

She walked down the stairs. Each step echoed. She reached the street. The night air was cold, but not as cold as the room behind her. She walked toward the river. She didn’t know where she was going. She just walked.

A man passed her, whistling a tune she recognized. It was a popular song, one she’d heard on the radio. It was bright and empty. She walked until her feet hurt. She found a bench near the Pont Neuf. She sat.

The guitar lay across her lap again. She didn’t play it. She looked at the river. The water moved slowly, a dark sheet under the bridges. A boat passed, its engine making a low, grinding noise. The sound faded.

She thought of the brush. The stiff hairs. The dried paint. She thought of the drawings in the notebook. The face looking down. The hand holding a tool. The stone does not know it is a wall.

She stood up. She walked back.

The apartment door was still closed. She knocked. There was no answer. She knocked again. She heard movement inside—the rustle of the mattress, the scrape of a chair. The door opened it.

Pauline stood there. She had not been sleeping. Her eyes were clear, dry. She looked at Iris. She looked at the guitar.

“I forgot something,” Iris said.

“The song. For the manifesto.”

Pauline stepped back. She let Iris enter.

The room was the same. The lamp was still burning. The notebook lay open on the table. Pauline went to it. She picked it up, brought it to Iris.

Iris took the notebook. She looked at the last drawing—her hands, the guitar, the shadow. She turned to a blank page. She took the pencil from the table.

She didn’t draw. She wrote. She wrote a line of words, then another. They were not lyrics. They were not a poem. They were a statement. They were simple, direct. They said: Art is not a commodity. Art is a tool. The tool is for breaking walls.

Pauline read the words. She read them twice. Then she looked at Iris. “This is the manifesto?”

“It’s the start.”

Pauline closed the notebook. She put it on the table. She came to Iris. She put her hands on Iris’s shoulders. Her hands were warm. They were strong.

“Then we will break walls,” she said.

They didn’t sleep that night. They worked. Iris wrote more lines. Pauline drew more pictures. They argued about words, about images, about the order of things. They drank cheap wine. They fell onto the cot together, exhausted, their clothes smelling of paint and tobacco and sweat.

In the morning, the patron from Lyon came. He was a young man with soft hands and a careful suit. He read the manifesto. He looked at the drawings. He nodded. He said it was good. He said it was radical. He said it would print.

He left money on the table. A stack of francs. It was more than they had seen in months.

Pauline took the money. She counted it. She put half in a drawer. She gave half to Iris.

“For the guitar,” she said. “For new strings. For whatever you need.”

Iris held the money. It felt heavy, like coins. It felt light, like paper. She didn’t know what to say.

They went out that afternoon. They bought strings. They bought paint. They bought food—not the stew leftovers, but fresh bread, cheese, a bottle of proper wine. They walked through the market, through the streets. They held hands. They didn’t talk about the contract. They didn’t talk about America. They talked about the next issue of the journal. They talked about a painting Pauline wanted to make, a large one, a mural maybe. They talked about a song Iris wanted to write, a long one, a story maybe.

The days passed that way. A rhythm set in. Iris played in the basement clubs at night. Pauline painted during the day. They met in the apartment in the late afternoon, in the evening. They worked on the journal. They ate. They slept.

The contract arrived by mail. It was a thick envelope, American paper. Iris opened it. She read it. She put it on the table.

Pauline saw it. She didn’t touch it. She didn’t ask about it.

They worked that night as usual. But the manifesto pages were slower. The drawings were tighter. The air in the room felt thinner.

A week later, the man from the label came. He was an American, tall, with a hat he didn’t remove. He sat in the apartment and talked about studios, about recordings, about distribution. He talked about money. He named figures. They were numbers Iris could not hold in her mind. They were stacks of francs so tall they would reach the ceiling.

Pauline listened. She sat on the rug, her notebook open, her pencil still. She didn’t draw. She didn’t write. She just listened.

When the man left, he left the contract on the table. He said Iris should sign it. He said there was a train leaving in three days. He said there was a room reserved in a hotel in New York. He said there was a future.

After he left, the room was silent. The lamp guttered. The stove was cold.

“You should go,” Pauline said.

“I don’t know.”

“You do know. You have known since the envelope arrived.”

“It’s not just the money.”

“It is just the money. And the safety. And the name. It is all of that. It is everything I cannot give you.”

Iris felt the stone in her chest again. It had grown. It pressed against her lungs. “I can come back. After I make something. After I have something.”

“You will not come back. You will have something, and it will need protecting. And you will protect it. And you will need more. And you will protect that too. And you will forget this room. You will forget this smell. You will forget my face.”

“I won’t.”

“You will.”

They did not work that night. They did not eat. They sat on the cot, side by side, not touching. The lamp burned down. The room grew dark.

In the dark, Pauline spoke. Her voice was a whisper, almost a sigh. “There is a wall between us now. You have built it. You are on one side. I am on the other.”

“I can break it.”

“You don’t have the tool.”

Iris reached for her hand. Pauline’s hand was cold. It did not grip back. It lay still, like an object.

“I have the tool,” Iris said. “I have you.”

Pauline withdrew her hand. “I am not a tool. I am a person. And you are leaving me.”

The next two days were a slow collapse. They spoke less. They moved around each other like shadows. Iris packed her things. She packed the guitar. She packed the clothes. She did not pack the brush. She did not pack the notebook.

On the last day, Pauline gave her a gift. It was a small box, wrapped in brown paper. Iris opened it. Inside was a Zippo lighter, silver, engraved with a simple pattern—a bird, a single line for a wing.

“For the cigarettes you will smoke in your warm room,” Pauline said.

Iris held the lighter. It was cool, smooth. “Thank you.”

“Don’t thank me. Use it. Or don’t use it. It doesn’t matter.”

They walked to the station together. They did not hold hands. They walked side by side, a foot of space between them. The station was crowded, noisy. The train was on the platform, a long, dark shape, breathing steam.

Iris had her ticket. She had her suitcase. She had her guitar.

Pauline stopped at the edge of the platform. She did not approach the train. She stood still, her hands in the pockets of her coat.

“Goodbye,” she said.

Iris wanted to say something else. She wanted to say she would write. She wanted to say she would return. She wanted to say the wall was not permanent. But the words were stones in her throat. She could not lift them.

She nodded. She turned. She walked to the train. She climbed aboard.

She found her seat. She put her guitar in the rack above. She sat down. The window was dirty, smeared with grime and rain. She looked out.

Pauline was still standing there. She was watching the train. Her face was expressionless, a blank canvas. Then she turned. She walked away. She did not look back.

The train moved. It pulled out of the station. It gathered speed. The city passed by the window—the buildings, the streets, the river. Then the countryside, the fields, the grey sky.

Iris held the lighter in her hand. She did not light a cigarette. She held it until her palm was warm from the metal. She held it until the train was far from Paris, far from the rue Delambre, far from the smell of turpentine and cigarettes.

She put the lighter in her pocket. She felt its weight there, a small, hard shape against her leg. She would carry it for years. She would never use it.
