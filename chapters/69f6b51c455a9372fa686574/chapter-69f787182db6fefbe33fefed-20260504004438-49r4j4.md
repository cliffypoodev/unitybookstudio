The spotlight was a physical weight on her shoulders, a hot, dusty column that pinned her to the center of the stage. Beyond its rim, the room dissolved into a murky blue gloom, punctuated by the occasional red ember of a cigarette and the pale, floating ovals of faces. They were listening. Or at least, they were quiet. Iris couldn’t hear the clink of glassware anymore, or the low murmur of conversation. Just the dry rustle of her own breath in her throat, a sound like paper being crumpled very slowly.

She held the last note of ‘The Last Goodbye’ for a count of three. Not the full four she’d managed in ’43, when her lungs felt like bellows and the air in the USO tent had been thick with hope and cordite. Now, the note thinned, turned brittle at the edge, and she let it go before it could crack. The silence that followed was absolute for a heartbeat. Then the polite, scattered applause began, the sound of two dozen pairs of hands meeting with a kind of dutiful gentleness. It was the applause you gave a museum piece. Respectful, distant, already half-forgotten.

She bowed her head, a shallow, professional dip. The sequins on her gown caught the light and threw it back in a hundred tiny, desperate winks. A man at a front table was staring at his watch, the face of it a tiny green moon in the dark. The strap was brown leather, cracked at the fold. She noticed that. It had nothing to do with anything.

“Thank you,” she murmured into the microphone, her voice a husk of its former broadcast clarity. “You’ve been a lovely audience.”

She turned, the stiff taffeta of her skirt whispering a protest, and walked off into the wing. The moment she cleared the light, the world snapped into a different focus—harsh, unflattering. A single bare bulb hung over a rickety table littered with ashtrays and a glass of water that had gone flat an hour ago. The backstage air smelled of old wood, dust, and the faint, clinging sweetness of gardenia perfume from the girl who’d sung novelty numbers before her. Someone had left a copy of Variety on the table, folded open to the radio listings. A coffee stain bloomed in one corner, shaped vaguely like Italy.

Langston was there, leaning against the brick wall. He’d been waiting. He always waited after a set now, a silent escort. He held her fur wrap, not offering it, just holding it. His face was a study in pleasant neutrality, but a muscle in his jaw ticked, a tiny, metronomic pulse beneath the skin.

“They liked the new arrangement,” he said.

It wasn’t a new arrangement. It was the same one from the record. She didn’t say so. “Did they?”

“Very sophisticated. Mature.” He stepped forward and draped the wrap over her shoulders. His hands lingered for a second, adjusting the collar. They were smooth, manicured hands. She remembered when they’d been thinner, more restless. “There’s a gentleman here to see you. A Mr. Cross. From Washington.”

Washington. The word sat between them, inert and heavy as a stone. She felt a cold knot tighten just below her ribs. Not fear, exactly. Something older. A familiar, weary dread, the kind that came with a knock at the door at an inconvenient hour.

“What does Washington want with a supper club singer?”

“He didn’t say. He’s in my booth. He’s… civil.” Langston’s eyes scanned her face, looking for something. Preparedness, maybe. Or a crack. “I think it’s best if we hear him out.”

We. Always we. She gave a short, dry laugh that hurt her throat. “Is it?”

The booth was at the back of the club, shrouded in deeper shadow. Phillip Cross stood as they approached. He was younger than she’d expected, mid-thirties perhaps, with the kind of neat, forgettable handsomeness of a bank clerk or a junior officer. His suit was a sober grey, his tie a dark blue. Nothing flashy. Nothing that drew the eye. He smiled, a small, polite movement of his lips that didn’t reach his eyes.

“Mrs. Finch. An honor. I enjoyed your performance very much.”

“Thank you, Mr. Cross.”

They slid into the booth, Langston guiding her in with a light touch on her elbow. Cross waited until they were settled before he sat. On the table between them was a thin manila folder. He didn’t open it. He simply rested his fingertips on its cover.

“I’ll come straight to the point,” Cross said. His voice was quiet, measured. A schoolteacher’s voice. “The Committee is interested in certain cultural institutions. Their… atmospheres. The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts has come to our attention. You’re familiar with it?”

A flash of memory—Pauline, in a Paris garret, waving a manuscript, her eyes alight with a fierce, burning certainty. Theatre isn’t decoration, Iris. It’s a weapon. The memory was twelve years old. It felt like it had been etched onto the inside of her skull yesterday.

“I know of it,” Iris said. Her thumb found the edge of the table and began tracing the wood grain. A small, concentric circle.

“We understand the head of the institute, a Miss Pauline Carter, is an old acquaintance of yours.”

The air in the booth grew very still. Langston shifted beside her. She could feel the heat of his leg through the layers of their clothes.

“We moved in similar circles once. A long time ago.”

“I see.” Cross nodded, as if she’d confirmed something. “The Committee believes someone with your background, your… social grace, could provide valuable insight into the institute’s activities. The tone of the place. The conversations one might overhear.”

“You want me to spy on them.”

“We prefer the term ‘consult.’ A cultural liaison.” He tapped the folder once. “Your cooperation would, of course, be appreciated. And remembered.”

“And if my cooperation isn’t forthcoming?”

Cross’s smile didn’t change. He opened the folder. He didn’t push it toward her, just angled it so she could see. It wasn’t a document. It was a photograph. A grainy, black-and-white image of two women walking arm-in-arm along the Seine, their faces turned toward each other, laughing. The taller one had her hat tipped at a rakish angle. The shorter one was her. The date, scrawled in white ink in the margin, read Juillet, 1937.

The cold knot in her stomach turned to liquid. She heard Langston draw a sharp, quiet breath.

“The post-war climate is one of moral renewal, Mrs. Finch,” Cross said, his tone still terribly polite. “The public has a certain expectation of its entertainers. Past… indiscretions can be forgiven. But willful lack of patriotism in the present?” He closed the folder softly. “That’s a different matter. It has consequences. For a career. For a reputation. For a marriage.”

The silence that followed was different from the stage silence. This one was thick, suffocating. She could hear the distant thump of the bass from the club’s trio, playing a jaunty Cole Porter tune. The music sounded obscene.

Langston leaned forward, his voice a low, urgent murmur. “There must be a compromise, Mr. Cross. My wife isn’t… political. She’s an artist.”

“Precisely,” Cross said, turning his calm gaze to Langston. “An artist in need of a new venue, perhaps. The institute, I’m told, is seeking instructors. A voice coach, maybe. Someone with professional experience. A position there would be a natural fit. A way to contribute to the cultural life of the city.” He looked back at Iris. “And to demonstrate one’s loyalty.”

It was laid out so neatly. A path through the minefield. She looked from Cross’s impassive face to Langston’s, which was now pale, strained. Her husband gave a tiny, almost imperceptible nod. Take the path.

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was the feeling of a door clicking shut, a lock engaging. The last possible exit, gone.

“When would I start?” Her own voice sounded strange to her. Flat. Hollow.

Cross’s smile finally reached his eyes. It made him look even younger. “We’ll be in touch with the details. Welcome aboard, Mrs. Finch.”

He stood, gave another small, polite nod, and melted away into the darkness of the club. The photograph was gone, sealed back inside its folder. It might as well have been burned onto her retinas.

Langston let out a long, shaky breath. He reached for her hand under the table. His palm was damp. “It’s for the best, Iris. It’s just… observing. It keeps you safe. It keeps us safe.”

She didn’t pull her hand away. She left it there, limp in his grasp, while she stared at the empty space where Cross had been sitting. On the wall opposite, a sconce flickered, casting a jumpy, anxious light. The bulb was dying. It would go out soon.

The dying bulb in the sconce gave one last, violent flicker and went out. The shadow it left behind felt thicker, a stain spreading across the wall. Langston’s hand was still on hers, a warm, dry weight. She could feel the precise pressure of each finger.

“Well,” he said. The word was a release of breath. He let go of her hand and reached for his cigarette case. The snap of the lid was too loud. “That could have been worse.”

She flexed her fingers, feeling the blood return. “Could it?”

“He’s giving us a path, Iris. A way through. It’s not what I’d have chosen, but it’s what’s on the table.” He lit a cigarette, the flame of his lighter a brief, orange flower in the gloom. The smoke he exhaled hung between them, a gauzy curtain. “A teaching position. It’s respectable. It gets you out of these… rooms.”

These rooms. The Blue Parrot. The Silver Lizard. The Gilded Cage. A circuit of velvet-draped tombs where they played her records before she came on, so the audience would remember what they were supposed to be hearing. The ghost of her voice, trapped in shellac, haunting the living version.

“He mentioned Paris,” she said.

“He mentioned an acquaintance. That’s all.” Langston’s tone was careful, a man walking on frozen pond ice. “Old history. It doesn’t signify.”

But it did. It was the only thing that signified. The manila folder was gone, but its shape seemed imprinted on the table’s surface. She could still see the faint, square outline of it in the grain of the wood. A ghost folder. Pauline’s name was in it. Her own was too, probably. Linked by a typewritten line. A connection from 1937, preserved in some filing cabinet in Washington, waiting to be given weight again.

“What exactly does ‘observing the atmosphere’ entail, Langston?”

He took a long drag. “You attend. You teach. You listen. You write a report. A bland one. Nothing inflammatory. You comment on… morale. The general tone of discussions. The subjects of the plays they choose.” He spoke to the smoke, not to her. “It’s a formality. They want a presence there. A name they can cite. ‘According to Mrs. Finch, the institute is a harmless cultural endeavor.’ It gets them off your back. It gets them off our back.”

Our. The apartment on the West Side. The accounts he managed. The fragile respectability of Mr. and Mrs. Langston Finch, a partnership that was, in its own way, a perfectly rehearsed performance. He played the devoted husband-manager. She played the fading chanteuse, safely, quietly married. It kept the wolves from a different door.

“And if I refuse?”

He looked at her then, his eyes catching the faint light from the club proper. They were the color of weak tea. “Iris. Don’t.”

“It’s a question. If I refuse.”

“Then Mr. Cross files his preliminary report. Which includes, I’m certain, not just speculation about your… Paris friendships, but a thorough accounting of your movements these past few years. The people you’ve been seen with. The benefits you performed for during the war that had certain political affiliations. The lyrics in some of your earlier songs that could be construed as… sympathetic.” He crushed his cigarette into the ashtray with a precise, grinding motion. “They’ll subpoena you. Publicly. The columnists will have a field day. ‘The Canary’s Red Past.’ ‘From Torch Songs to Torchbearers?’ And then, my dear, no club. No radio. No nothing. Just a very quiet, very permanent retirement.”

The cold knot below her ribs hadn’t loosened. It had simply changed shape, become a familiar companion. This was the calculus of their life. The measurement of risk. She was a ledger to him, always had been. Assets and liabilities. Her voice had been an asset. Her past was a liability. Her marriage to him was the column that balanced the two.

“You’ve already agreed to it,” she said. It wasn’t a question.

“I’ve secured the best possible terms. You are not a suspect. You are a consultant. A patriotic citizen lending her expertise. There is no admission of wrongdoing. It’s a graceful exit from one stage and an entrance onto another.” He signaled to a waiter for the check. The movement was crisp, final. “Pauline Carter runs a serious institute. It’s well-regarded. This isn’t a punishment. It could be a… Reinvention.”

Reinvention. The word tasted like tin. She thought of Pauline’s hands, always in motion, shaping ideas in the air. The way she could dismantle a line of dialogue, a look, a gesture, and rebuild it into something true. Something dangerous. Iris hadn’t seen her in twelve years. The last time, in a cramped Left Bank room, Pauline’s face had been a mask of contemptuous hurt. You’re choosing a jingle over a revolution. She hadn’t been wrong.

The waiter brought the check on a small silver tray. Langston placed a bill on it without looking, a gesture so effortless it seemed to erase the transaction entirely. Money, like everything else, was something he managed without visible strain.

“When?” Iris asked. Her voice sounded far away.

“Tomorrow. Ten a. m. I’ll take you. It’s all arranged.”

Of course it was. She looked past him, through the archway to the main room. The band was packing up. The pianist was wiping the keys of the baby grand with a white cloth, a slow, reverential gesture. On the wall above him, a painted parrot, the club’s namesake, was frozen mid-flight against a tropical backdrop. One of its blue feathers was peeling away from the plaster, curling at the edge like a dead leaf.

“I need some air,” she said.

“I’ll get your coat.”

“No. I’ll meet you at the car. I just need a moment.”

He hesitated, the manager in him assessing the risk of letting the talent walk unescorted. But the husband won, or perhaps the part of him that was tired of managing her every breath. He gave a short nod. “Don’t be long. It’s cold.”

She walked through the empty club, her heels clicking on the parquet floor. The tables were littered with the debris of the evening: smudged cocktail glasses, overflowing ashtrays, a single gardenia floating in a melted ice cube. The smell of stale smoke and spilled gin was a presence, a fog she pushed through.

The alley door was heavy, bound in iron. She pushed it open and the February night hit her like a slap. The air was knife-cold and damp, carrying the distant, oily scent of the river and the more immediate stench of garbage. The brick wall was rough against her back as she leaned into it, letting the cold seep through the thin silk of her dress.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and irrelevant: a winter in Chicago, 1941. She’d been waiting for a streetcar, her breath pluming. A man next to her was eating a roasted chestnut from a paper cone, the smell sweet and smoky. He’d offered her one. She’d taken it, burning her fingers. She couldn’t remember his face, only the startling, earthy taste of it, and the way the heat had felt in her frozen hands.

From inside the club, faintly, came the sound of a broom sweeping. A steady, rhythmic swish-swish against the floor. It was the sound of erasure.

She knew what Langston was doing. He was framing it as salvation because the alternative was unthinkable. But as she stood in the alley, the cold tightening the skin on her arms, a different understanding settled over her. This wasn’t a path through. It was a trade. Her silence about one thing for her silence about everything else. Her body, her presence, would become the government’s stamp on Pauline’s work. Her own history, the one true thing she’d ever felt, was now the lever they would use to bury whatever truth Pauline was trying to build.

The door opened behind her, spilling a wedge of yellow light onto the wet asphalt. Langston stood there, holding her coat. His silhouette was neat, contained.

“Car’s here,” he said.

She pushed herself off the wall. Her limbs felt heavy, as if the cold had seeped into the marrow. She walked toward him, toward the light, and let him help her into the coat. The fur collar was soft against her neck. It smelled of camphor and, faintly, of her own perfume from a hundred nights ago.

As he guided her toward the waiting car, its engine a patient murmur in the dark, she knew one thing with perfect, silent clarity. Tomorrow, she would walk into Pauline Carter’s institute. And Pauline would look at her, and she would see not an old lover, not a faded singer, but the thing she had become: a weapon, pointed directly at the heart of everything Pauline held dear. The cage wasn’t being opened it. She was being moved into a new one, with different bars.

The car door shut with a soft, expensive click. The apartment building’s awning was a stripe of damp maroon in the streetlight glow. Langston’s hand stayed on her elbow, a light, proprietary pressure, all the way up in the elevator. The brass panel showed their warped reflections—a tall, greying man in an overcoat, a woman in sequins, a smudge of dark fur. Her own eyes were two pits of shadow.

He unlocked the door. “I’ll make a call,” he said, already moving toward the study. “Just to smooth the way for tomorrow.”

She didn’t answer. She stepped inside and the door sighed shut behind her, sealing out the city’s faint night hum.

The apartment held its breath. The radiator ticked once, then fell silent. She stood in the foyer, the parquet floor cool through her stockings. From the study, the low murmur began, words she couldn’t catch and didn’t try to. She unclasped the fur. It slid from her shoulders to the floor with a sound like a sigh, a heap of lifeless brown. She left it there.

The living room was a stage set waiting for the next act. A single lamp with a fringed shade threw a yellow circle onto the Persian rug. In that circle, a worn patch near the sofa leg glowed faintly, the pattern rubbed away by decades of feet. It looked like a faded stain, something spilled long ago and never fully cleaned. She’d stared at it through a hundred other silences. Tonight, it was just a spot on the rug.

Her throat felt raw. Not from the singing. From the not-screaming.

She went to the sidebar. The cut-glass decanter was there, the gin inside catching the light in cold, hard angles. Next to it, her toothbrush glass sat upturned on a linen coaster, a tiny island of order. She poured. The liquid climbed to just below the rim, a clear, sharp meniscus. She didn’t drink. She held the glass, let the chill bleed into her palm.

The meeting replayed, not as memory but as sensation. The dry texture of the booth’s leather. The perfect, even cadence of his voice laying out the terms. A list of names. A report on atmospheres. Langston’s interjections, calm, reasonable, weaving the bars around her. A position. An observer. It’s the only way.

She took a sip. The gin was a cold blade, then a false, spreading warmth. It didn’t reach the cold place. Nothing did.

The murmur in the study ceased. A drawer slid shut. A lock clicked, a tiny, final sound.

She turned, glass in hand, and walked down the short hall. Her bedroom door was ajar. Inside, it was all pale blues and creams, a curated silence. The sheet music for ‘The Last Goodbye’ hung framed on the wall, the notes like a scattering of black ash. On the dressing table, her silver brush and hand mirror lay parallel, precisely one inch apart. She’d arranged them that morning. A lifetime ago.

She set the glass down, breaking the line. The surface of the dressing table was cool, smooth. A faint film of dust already coated it near the base of the lamp. She opened the top drawer. The scent of cedar and old paper drifted out. Beneath a stack of linen handkerchiefs lay the box.

Tokyo, 1944. Cherry blossom inlay, the wood dark with handling. The colonel had pressed it into her hands after the show, his own hands damp, his eyes not quite meeting hers. A private concert, he’d said. Just for the officers. She’d taken the box and never gone. The clasp was stiff. She worked it open.

The velvet inside was the color of dried blood. Three objects. A gardenia, pressed flat, its white turned the brown of old tea. A train ticket stub, Paris to Nice, the ink blurred. And the lighter.

She picked it up. The chrome was scratched, cloudy in places. The hinge resisted. She didn’t flick the wheel. She never did. Her thumb found the groove on the side, worn smooth. Pauline’s thumb had rested there, that first afternoon. Lighting a Gauloise, the flame a sudden, shocking bloom in the dim garret. The smoke had been thick, bitter. Iris had coughed, her eyes watering. Pauline had laughed, head thrown back, the sound rich and unguarded. You’ll get used to it, Canary. Or you’ll sing with a rasp. More character.

A floorboard creaked in the hall.

Her fingers closed around the lighter. The metal was room-temperature, inert. She placed it back on the velvet, closed the box, slid the drawer shut. The click was loud in the quiet room.

Langston appeared in the doorway. He’d removed his jacket and tie. In his shirtsleeves, he looked softer, more vulnerable. It was a trick. He leaned against the jamb. “All settled. You start at ten. Miss Carter is expecting you.”

“Is she?”

“I framed it as a professional courtesy. A veteran artist offering her expertise.” He came into the room, sat on the edge of the bed. The springs groaned softly under his weight. “It’s a good cover. It gives you access. A reason to be there.”

She picked up her glass, took another swallow. The false warmth was spreading, making her fingers feel slightly detached. “What’s the first report due?”

“Don’t think of them as reports. Think of them as… impressions. Cross will contact you in a week. The people—” He looked at her, his eyes searching her face. “You can do this, Iris. It’s just another performance.”

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It tightened her throat. She turned to the window, looked out at the dark backs of other buildings. A single square of yellow light burned in one, a lonely, late-night vigil. Someone else who couldn’t sleep.

“Pauline will know,” she said, her voice flat.

“Know what? That you’ve been asked to teach? That’s all she needs to know.”

“She’ll look at me and she’ll know.” She could already see it: Pauline’s eyes, that deep, assessing brown, taking her in. Seeing the fear, the calculation, the ghost of the girl who’d packed a single suitcase and left without saying goodbye. Pauline had always seen the spaces between the notes.

Langston sighed, a patient exhalation. “Then act better. You’re good at that.”

The cruelty of it was casual, uninflected. A simple statement of fact. She was good at that. She’d acted the devoted wife, the patriotic songbird, the woman content with fading echoes. She’d acted so well she’d almost forgotten the script was a lie.

He stood. “Get some sleep. Big day tomorrow.” He paused, then crossed to her. He bent and pressed a dry kiss to her temple. His lips were cool. “It’s for the best. You’ll see.”

He left, pulling the door closed behind him.

She stood at the window until the square of yellow light in the distant building went out. The city was a vast, dark murmur. She finished the gin, the glass empty now, just a cold vessel in her hand.

She began to undress. The sequined gown was a difficult, prickly shell. She struggled with the tiny hooks at the back, her fingers clumsy. Finally, it pooled at her feet, a glittering, lifeless skin. She stepped out of it, left it on the floor. Put on a silk robe. The fabric was cool, slippery.

She didn’t get into bed. She sat at the dressing table and looked at her reflection in the oval mirror. The makeup was a mask—the arched brows, the red lipstick bleeding slightly at the edges. The woman staring back was a stranger, someone she’d been rehearsing for years. Under the careful construction, the face was tired. The eyes were old.

Her hand went to the drawer again. She didn’t open it. She just rested her fingertips on the polished wood, right above where the box lay. A strange thought came, unbidden and useless: she wondered if the dried gardenia would crumble to dust if she touched it now. It probably would. Some things only lasted if you never looked at them.

Tomorrow. Ten o’clock. The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. Pauline.

She picked up her hairbrush and began to brush her hair. Long, slow strokes. The bristles scraped against her scalp, a soothing, rhythmic punishment. Fifty strokes. A hundred. The motion was automatic, a lullaby for a mind that would not quiet. In the mirror, her reflection blurred, became a smudge of pale skin and dark hair, a ghost already forming.
