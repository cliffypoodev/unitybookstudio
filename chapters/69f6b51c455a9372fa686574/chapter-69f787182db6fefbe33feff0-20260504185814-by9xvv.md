The rehearsal space was cold in a way that bypassed the skin and settled into the bone. It wasn’t the temperature of the air, which was tepid enough, but the quality of the silence that filled the vast, high-ceilinged nave of the converted church. Dust motes turned in the weak afternoon light that fell through the tall, arched windows, each pane a grid of lead and smudged glass. Iris stood just inside the double doors, her coat still on, and counted the chairs arranged in a loose semicircle on the scuffed wooden floor. Thirteen. One was missing a leg, propped against the wall. The smell was familiar and alien all at once: old wood polish, the ghost of candle wax, and underneath it all, the sharp, clean scent of anxiety sweat.

Pauline was already there, of course. She stood at a makeshift lectern—a music stand with a clipboard clamped to it—her back to the door. She wore a charcoal-gray sweater, the sleeves pushed to her elbows. Her shoulders were motionless. She was writing something, the scratch of her pencil the only sound in the room. Iris had the sudden, absurd thought of a teacher waiting for a tardy student. She unbuttoned her coat, the sound of the buttons popping through their holes obscenely loud.

“You’re early,” Pauline said, without turning.

Iris checked her watch. She was five minutes past the called time. “Am I?

“For the part you’re playing.” Pauline set the pencil down and finally turned. Her expression was one of mild, professional assessment. “The penitent. The reluctant volunteer. They’re meant to arrive flustered, breathless. You look like you’ve been standing in a queue for rations.

“It’s the coat. It’s heavy.” Iris folded it over the back of the nearest chair. Her hands, freed, felt empty. She put them in the pockets of her trousers, then took them out again.

“We’ll begin with a read-through. No blocking today. Just the words.” Pauline picked up a stack of scripts from beside the lectern. They were mimeographed sheets, bound with brass fasteners, the purple ink already smudging at the edges from handling. She held one out. “You’ll read Martha.

Iris crossed the floor. The boards creaked under her weight in a specific sequence—a low groan, a higher protest, a sigh. She took the script. The title was handwritten across the top in Pauline’s precise, angular script: The Children’s Hour. The paper was cool and slightly damp to the touch. She didn’t open it. “I’m to direct. That was the arrangement.

“The arrangement,” Pauline repeated, as if tasting the word. “You’ll direct by understanding what you’re asking of others. You can’t ask for a truth you haven’t faced yourself. Martha.

“I’m not an actress.”

“No. You’re a performer. There’s a difference. Today, you’ll learn it.” Pauline’s gaze was flat, dispassionate. “Sit. The others will be here shortly.

The others were two young women and a young man, students from the institute whose names Iris immediately forgot. They filed in with a collective quietness, nodding to Pauline, casting curious, sidelong glances at Iris. They took their scripts without a word and settled into chairs. One of the girls had a small, healed scar on her chin, a pale crescent moon. Iris found herself staring at it, wondering how she got it—a fall from a bicycle, perhaps, or the corner of a childhood table. Something ordinary. The girl noticed her looking and touched the spot self-consciously, then tucked her chin down.

“We’ll start at the top of Act Three,” Pauline said, taking her own seat in the circle. She lit a cigarette. The smoke curled up, a pale gray thread in the column of light from the window. “Martha’s confession. Iris, begin when you’re ready.

Iris looked down at the script. The pages blurred for a second, the purple lines of dialogue swimming. She found her place. The words were a tight, dense block of text. She cleared her throat. The sound was dry, a rustle of paper.

“I don’t know how to say this.” Her voice came out thin, pitched too high. She tried to drop it, to find the resonance she used for singing. “I’ve thought about it for so long. All the time, it seems. Since I first knew you, I guess.

It was wrong. All of it. She sounded like she was reading a grocery list. She heard the flatness, the complete absence of the agony the words were supposed to carry. She pushed on.

“I’ve loved you the way they said. The way they said it was true. I have.

Silence. The dust motes danced. The boy across the circle examined a hangnail on his thumb. The girl with the scar studied her script as if the answer to a difficult exam question were hidden in the margins.

Pauline took a long drag from her cigarette. She exhaled slowly, watching Iris over the plume of smoke. “Stop.

Iris stopped. Her heart was a clumsy fist knocking against her ribs.

“You’re singing it,” Pauline said. “You’re putting a melody to it. A little tremolo on ‘loved,’ a descending minor third on ‘true.’ This isn’t a torch song. It’s a suicide note. Try again. From ‘I’ve thought about it.

Iris looked at the words. They were just words. Black marks on a page. She couldn’t find the person who was supposed to say them. “I’ve thought about it for so long. All the time, it seems.

“No.” Pauline’s voice was calm, surgical. “You’re telling us she’s thought about it. You’re narrating. You need to be the woman who has thought of nothing else for years. The thought has worn a groove in her mind. It’s the only thought she has. It’s the air she breathes. Again.

Iris’s mouth was parchment. She tried to wet her lips. “I’ve thought about it for so long.

“Where is the thought? In her head? In her chest? In her hands? Find it. Put it somewhere in your body. Then speak from there.

This was ridiculous. Cruel. Iris felt a hot prickling at the back of her neck. She wasn’t here for this. She was here to watch, to note names, to file a report about the atmosphere. This was the atmosphere—a cold room, a colder woman, a script that was a landmine. She took a breath, a singer’s breath, deep into the diaphragm. She let it out.

“I’ve thought about it,” she said, and this time she didn’t try to act. She let the exhaustion she felt seep into the words. The profound, daily exhaustion of carrying a secret that was no longer even a secret, just a fact everyone politely ignored. “All the time, it seems.

Something flickered in Pauline’s eyes. Not approval. Recognition. “Better. Now the next line. ‘Since I first knew you.

“Since I first knew you.” The words were ash.

“What does she mean, ‘knew you’?” Pauline asked the circle, but her eyes stayed on Iris. “Not intellectually. Not ‘I made your acquaintance.’ She means she knew. In the biblical sense. In the marrow sense. The moment her life split into a before and an after. That knowing. Deliver that.

The girl with the scar shifted in her chair. The boy had stopped picking his thumb.

Iris looked at Pauline. Really looked. She saw the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, deeper than they’d been in Paris. The way she held her cigarette, between her thumb and two fingers, like a painter holding a brush. A memory surfaced, unbidden and utterly irrelevant: Pauline in a bathtub in a rented flat in Montmartre, steam curling, humming a Bessie Smith song off-key. The smell of cheap lavender soap. The way the light from a single bare bulb had glinted on the wet skin of her shoulder.

“Since I first knew you,” Iris said, and the memory was in her voice, a thickness that hadn’t been there before.

Pauline was very still. The cigarette burned, forgotten, in her hand. “Go on.

Iris didn’t look at the script. She knew the next line. It was waiting in her mouth. “I guess.

A beat of silence. Then Pauline gave a single, slight nod. “Continue.

She continued. The words began to flow, not as a performance, but as a slow, painful exhalation of something that had been trapped. She didn’t try to act Martha’s despair. She borrowed her own. The quiet panic of the hotel room with Cross. The sick dread of typing the first report. The hollow space where her music used to live. She fed it all into Martha’s confession. It wasn’t good acting. It was barely acting at all. It was a transfusion.

When she reached the end of the scene—Martha’s bleak, quiet “I don’t want to live anymore.”—the silence in the room was different. Heavier. The dust motes seemed to hang suspended. The girl with the scar was staring at the floor, her arms crossed tightly over her chest.

Pauline stubbed out her cigarette in a tin ashtray on the floor beside her chair. The gesture was final. “Thank you, Iris.” She turned to the others, her demeanor shifting back to that of the instructor. “That is the difference between performing a feeling and inhabiting a truth. The text is a cipher. Your life is the key. You cannot lie to the audience if you are using a real key. They will sense the lock turning.” She stood. “We’ll pick up there tomorrow. Dismissed.

The students gathered their things and left with soft, murmured goodbyes. They didn’t look at Iris. She sat in her chair, the script heavy in her lap. The aftertaste of the words was in her mouth, metallic and sour.

Pauline busied herself at the lectern, straightening papers. The practical, mundane action was a wall.

“That was the point, wasn’t it?” Iris’s voice sounded scraped raw. “Choosing this play. Giving me that part.

Pauline didn’t look up. “It’s a relevant text. The damage a lie can do. The cost of a truth.

“It’s a play about a woman destroyed because people think she’s like me.”

Now Pauline looked at her. Her expression was unreadable. “Is that what you think it’s about?

“Isn’t it?”

“It’s about a woman who believes the lie they tell about her. She internalizes their condemnation and lets it become her verdict. That’s the tragedy. Not the accusation. The surrender.” Pauline gathered her clipboard and her cigarettes. “You grasped the surrender quite well.

The words were a slap. Iris felt the sting high on her cheek, though nothing had touched her. “You wanted to see me squirm.

“I wanted to see if you could still access an honest emotion. However ugly.” Pauline started toward the doors, then paused. She looked back, her profile sharp against the dark wood of the wall. “Your report will be easy to write tonight. Atmosphere: tense. Political undercurrents: present. Reading material: subversive. You can tell your Mr. Cross we’re doing Hellman. She’s already on their list. It won’t surprise anyone.

She left then, her footsteps echoing in the empty nave until the side door clicked shut behind her.

Iris didn’t move. The light from the windows was failing, the bright squares on the floor deepening to a dull gold, then a gray-blue. The room grew cold. She looked down at the script in her hands. Martha’s lines. A woman speaking a terrible truth aloud for the first and last time.

She thought of the report she had to type. The sterile sentences. Observed a rehearsal of Lillian Hellman’s ‘The Children’s Hour.’ Students attentive. Director Carter focused on textual analysis. She could write that. It was true. It was also nothing at all.

From the street outside came the distant, rhythmic clatter of the El train, a sound like bones shaking in a box. It faded. In the new silence, Iris became aware of a faint, high hum. It might have been the old electrical wiring in the walls, or the water in the pipes. It was a single, sustained note, just at the edge of hearing. She listened to it until her ears ached. Then she stood, her joints stiff, and placed the script neatly on the seat of her chair. She put on her heavy coat, buttoned it slowly, and walked out into the gathering February dusk.

Her apartment was a twenty-block walk, but her body turned east after ten, toward the river. The air grew colder, sharper, carrying the wet-stone smell of the docks. She found a bench overlooking the water, the wood dark and swollen with damp. She sat. The river was the color of old lead, moving with a slow, heavy insistence. A barge chugged past, its engine a low, bronchial rumble.

The humiliation was a cold stone in her throat. Not just the failure—she’d failed before, missed notes, forgotten lyrics in a packed house. This was different. Pauline hadn’t been angry. Anger would have been a fire, something to push against. This was dissection. A clinical removal of the performance to reveal the empty cavity beneath. You’re singing it. And she had been. She’d turned a confession of ruin into a cabaret turn, all technique and no truth. The worst part was the flicker of pride she’d felt at the start, the professional’s confidence that she could handle a few lines of dialogue. Pauline had stripped that away in three minutes.

A tugboat sounded its horn, a long, mournful blast that seemed to hang in the air. Iris fumbled in her coat pocket for her cigarettes. Her fingers brushed against the Zippo. The metal was smooth, worn to a dull sheen along the edges from a thousand unconscious thumb-strokes. She didn’t take it out. She lit a match instead, the flare of sulphur brief and acrid. The smoke was harsh, comforting in its familiarity.

What was the truth of Martha’s line? It wasn’t about love, not really. It was about the accusation becoming the identity. About having the thing they feared named, and in the naming, made real. It was about the world forcing a shape onto you, and you, in your terror, agreeing to live inside it. A cage of someone else’s making, with a lock on the inside.

Her coffee that morning had been bitter, over-brewed. Langston had made it, leaving the grounds in too long. He’d been humming, a tuneless, anxious sound, as he moved around the kitchen. He’d asked, again, about the institute. Anything of note, darling? Just the general atmosphere. She’d given him nothing. The report for Cross was due tomorrow evening. A paragraph. Sterile observations. The institute operates a workshop theater model. The current production is a revival of Lillian Hellman’s ‘The Children’s Hour.’ Morale appears high. It was a nothing-burger, as the agents liked to say. It wouldn’t satisfy them. They wanted names. Tendencies. Political affiliations whispered in the green room.

Pauline’s face floated back into her mind. Not as she was today, all sharp angles and colder light, but as she’d been in Paris, leaning against the doorframe of that tiny flat on Rue du Cherche-Midi, a smudge of blue paint on her temple from the set she’d been helping to build. She’d been laughing at something Iris said, her head thrown back, the sound rich and unguarded. That was the memory that came, not the fights, not the final, silent breakfast before Iris left for the boat train. Just that laugh, and the late afternoon sun turning the dust in the air to gold.

The mist thickened into a proper drizzle. Iris stood, her muscles stiff. She walked back toward the streets, the river’s murmur fading behind her. She needed a drink, but not at home. Not with Langston’s concerned, calculating eyes on her. She found a bar on a side street, a narrow place with a neon Schlitz sign sputtering in the window. Inside, it was warm and dim, smelling of beer and fried onions. Three men in work clothes hunched at the bar, not talking. The radio was on low, a baseball game from some other city.

She took a stool at the far end. The bartender, a woman with tired eyes and a permanent kink in her apron string, nodded at her. “Gin. Rocks.

The gin arrived in a thick, cloudy glass. She took a sip. It was cheap, sharp as a razor, but it did its job. A warmth spread through her chest, a false courage. She traced the rim of the glass with her thumb, the same automatic motion. You’re singing it.

The door opened, letting in a gust of wet air and the sound of traffic. A man walked in, shaking water from his hat. He was in his fifties, with a careful, nondescript face and a grey raincoat. He didn’t look at the other men. He didn’t look at the bartender. His eyes found Iris, held for a fraction of a second too long, and then moved on as he took a seat two stools down.

Her stomach tightened. It wasn’t Cross. This man was older, with a quiet, settled weariness about him that Cross hadn’t yet earned. He ordered a bourbon, neat. He drank it slowly, staring at the bottles lined up behind the bar as if reading a familiar, depressing text.

Iris kept her eyes on her glass. The hum was back, the high, faint note from the rehearsal hall. It was in her own head. She took another swallow of gin. The man finished his drink, placed a bill on the bar, and stood. As he passed behind her stool, his coat brushed against the back of her chair. A small, white rectangle of paper fluttered down, landing on the bar next to her elbow.

Her hand closed over the paper. It was a business card, thick stock. No name. Just ten digits typed in a clean, official-looking font. A telephone number. On the back, in neat, handwritten pencil: 9 p.m. The usual place.

The usual place. Which meant this wasn’t the first contact. Which meant Langston had given them another way to reach her, another thread to pull.

The gin turned to acid in her throat. She left money on the bar, crumpling the card deep into her coat pocket as she stood. The walk home was a blur of wet pavement and shifting shadows. She kept her head down, her hands in her pockets, one fist clenched around the card until the edges cut into her palm.

Langston was in the living room when she let herself in, reading the Times in his armchair. A single lamp cast a pool of yellow light. He looked up, his reading glasses perched on the end of his nose. “You’re soaked.

“It’s raining.”

“I see that.” He folded the paper. “How was the institute?

“Educational.” She shrugged out of her coat, hung it on the rack by the door. The card was a weight in the pocket. She could feel it from here.

“Learn anything useful?”

She turned. He was watching her, his expression a careful blend of husbandly concern and managerial interest. The two things had become indistinguishable. “Pauline’s having me act in the play. To understand direction, she says.

Langston’s eyebrows lifted. “Act? You?

“That was my reaction.”

He took off his glasses, polished them on his shirtfront. “Well. It’s a foot in the door. Gets you closer to the daily workings. The people.” He put his glasses back on. “Might provide better material for your report.

There it was. The gentle nudge. The reminder of the leash.

“I suppose it might.” Her voice was flat.

He heard the tone. He leaned forward, his hands clasped. “Iris. This isn’t a game. Cross called today. He was… impatient. He expects something substantive. Not just set decoration.

“What did you tell him?”

“That you were acclimating. That these things take time.” He spread his hands. “But time is a commodity we’re running short of. You need to give them something. A name. An observation with a little more… color.

A name. The girl with the scar on her chin. The boy with the hangnail. Pauline.

“I’ll see what I can do,” she said.

He nodded, satisfied. He picked up his paper again, a signal the conversation was over. She went to the kitchen, poured herself a glass of water. Her hands were steady. That was something. Through the doorway, she saw the curve of his shoulder, the thinning spot at the crown of his head. The man who had booked her first real gig at the Waldorf. The man who had stood beside her at a city clerk’s office five years ago, a marriage of convenience that had become a cage of mutual need. The man who had, just this afternoon, given a government agent the location of a bar where she could be found alone.

She drank the water. It was tasteless, lukewarm.

In her bedroom, she took the card from her pocket. She didn’t turn on the light. The streetlamp outside cast a long, pale rectangle across the floor. She sat on the edge of the bed, the card in her hand. The usual place. Where was that? Some other anonymous hotel room? A phone booth in Grand Central? A part of her wanted to tear it into pieces, flush it down the toilet. A louder, colder part knew she wouldn’t.

Pauline hadn’t been teaching her to act. She’d been showing her the mechanism. How to hide a truth in plain sight. The irony was a knot in her chest, tight and painful. The very skill she needed to survive as an informant—the art of layered performance, of meaning beneath meaning—was the one Pauline was forcing her to learn. And she would have to use it to betray the teacher.

She placed the business card on her nightstand. The numbers seemed to glow in the dim light. 9 p.m. Tomorrow.

She lay back on the bed, still in her clothes. The ceiling above her was smooth, white. A long, hairline crack ran from the corner of the room toward the center, forking once like a slender, barren tree. She followed its path with her eyes until they grew heavy. Outside, the city murmured on, a vast, indifferent machine. Somewhere in it, Pauline was smoking a cigarette, tapping a pencil in a complex rhythm. Somewhere, Cross was filing a report. And here she was, in the quiet dark, learning the grammar of a language she never wanted to speak.
