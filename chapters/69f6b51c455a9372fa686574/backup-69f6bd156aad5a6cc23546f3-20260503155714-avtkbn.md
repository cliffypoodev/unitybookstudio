The hallway was empty and smelled of wax and old wood, a school smell that tightened something in Iris’s chest. She stood there a moment, the cheap script in her hand, the quiet click of Pauline’s door still echoing in her ears. A transaction. Of course. Everything was. The floorboards were a dark, scarred maple, and a single, long scratch ran the length of the hall like a chalk line. She followed it with her eyes until it disappeared under a door marked ‘Costumes.

Her own footsteps were too loud. She passed classrooms where voices rose and fell in exercises—a man’s booming baritone reciting something about a seashore, a chorus of younger voices repeating a phrase in unison. The sounds were muffled, layered, a hive of earnest work. She didn’t belong here. She was a cut flower in a greenhouse, already wilting, propped up for a legitimacy she didn’t possess.

The main rehearsal hall was at the end of the corridor, its double doors propped open with two battered sandbags. The light was different here—pale, diffuse winter light from high, grimy windows, cutting through the dust motes that swirled in the vast, open space. The floor was sprung, marked with tape in various colors, a cryptic geometry. A grand piano sat in one corner, its lid closed, a forgotten sweater draped over the bench.

A group of students clustered at the far end, around a woman demonstrating a physical exercise. They moved in slow, exaggerated unison, reaching for an invisible ceiling, then folding forward. Iris recognized the woman from a photograph in Pauline’s office—Martha, the movement instructor. She had a calm, focused energy, her instructions clear and low. None of them looked her way.

She found an empty chair near the wall, one of a dozen stacked haphazardly. The metal was cold through her skirt. She set the script on her lap, ran a thumb over the smudged title. It was still just a block of text. Words. She could do words. She’d built a career on knowing which ones to stress, which to let fall away. But this was different. This required a kind of nakedness she hadn’t practiced in years. If ever.

It had nothing to do with anything. She blinked it away.

“You’re early.”

Pauline’s voice came from behind her left shoulder. Iris hadn’t heard her approach. She turned, a stiff motion. Pauline stood a few feet away, arms crossed, watching the students. She’d changed into a dark turtleneck and trousers, her hair pulled back more severely. She looked like a foreman surveying a worksite.

“I didn’t know where else to go,” Iris said. It was the truth, and it sounded pathetic.

“Observation is a start.” Pauline nodded toward the students. “Watch them. They’re not performing. They’re preparing the instrument. The body. Something you’ve perhaps treated as merely a vehicle for a gown and a spotlight.

The criticism was so direct it felt like a shove. Iris’s mouth went dry. She said nothing.

One of the students, a young man with close-cropped hair, broke from the exercise, frustration clear on his face. “I can’t find the rhythm of it,” he said to Martha, his voice carrying. “It keeps feeling false.

“Because you’re judging it as you do it,” Martha replied, her tone even. “Stop judging. Just do the thing. The truth is in the repetition, not the result.

The truth is in the repetition. Iris turned the phrase over. It was the opposite of everything she knew. A song was a result, polished and sealed. You delivered the result, night after night, until it wore a groove in the air. The idea of truth hiding in the boring, repeated attempt made her feel tired.

“You disagree?” Pauline had been watching her face.

“I don’t understand it.”

“A singer who doesn’t understand practice.” Pauline almost smiled, a thin, humorless thing. “But then, you were always a natural, weren’t you? The rest of us had to grind.

This was the old resentment, polished to a fine point. Iris looked down at her hands, at the faint tremor in her right thumb. She pressed it against the script to still it. “It wasn’t like that.

“No? From where I stood, it looked exactly like that. Talent as a birthright. Conviction as a fashion.” Pauline’s gaze was back on the students. “We don’t traffic in fashion here, Iris. We’re not in the business of producing pleasant distractions. This institute exists because there are stories that need telling in a language they can’t yet censor. Every gesture here is political. Even the silent ones.

A young woman with braids coiled tightly against her skull detached from the group and walked toward the piano. She moved with a loose-limbed grace, ignoring the sweater on the bench, and sat. She didn’t open the lid. She just placed her hands on the closed wood, her head bowed, as if listening for something trapped inside.

“That’s Elara,” Pauline said, her voice dropping, losing its edge for a moment. “Our best. She’s working on a piece about the Port Chicago munitions explosion. The Navy’s refusal to properly investigate, the court-martials of the Black sailors who refused to load after the blast. She’s playing a mother of one of the accused.

Iris watched the girl’s still back. “A play?

“A chorus piece. Documentary theatre. We use testimony, court records.” Pauline’s eyes were fixed on Elara with a fierce, protective intensity. “It’s dangerous work. The wrong people take notice. But it’s necessary. It’s the reason we have these walls.” Her tone shifted again, hardening. “Which is why the board’s insistence on bringing in outside ‘prestige’ is so particularly galling. It dilutes the mission. It invites the very scrutiny we’re trying to withstand.

The meaning landed, cold and heavy. Outside prestige. Iris was the dilution. The invitation.

“Then why agree?” The question left her before she could stop it.

Pauline was silent for a long time. The only sounds were the murmur of the students and the distant rumble of a truck on the street outside. “Because Arthur’s recommendation came with certain… assurances. Financial ones. The institute is perpetually on the brink. And because…” She paused, choosing her words with visible care. “Henderson, the man you met, has made inquiries. Polite, bureaucratic inquiries. About our funding sources. About the content of our season. Having a name like yours on the letterhead, even temporarily, creates a layer of insulation. A celebrated American songstress on the faculty? It suggests we’re patriotic. Mainstream. It’s a shield, however flimsy.

So that was the transaction. Her faded name for their protection. Her presence as a piece of camouflage. Iris felt a hot flush climb her neck—not quite shame, not quite anger, a sour blend of both.

“You’re using me as a decoy.”

“We’re using each other,” Pauline corrected, her gaze finally swinging back to Iris. It was utterly frank. “You get a job, a veneer of respectability to show your government friends. We get a buffer. And maybe, if you’re capable of it, you might actually learn something. You might understand what it means to put your body on the line for a truth that isn’t your own personal lament.

The young woman at the piano, Elara, finally moved. She lifted her hands from the lid and placed them in her lap, her shoulders slumping in a single, silent exhalation of defeat or completion. It was a private moment, unbearably intimate in the public space. Iris looked away, feeling like a trespasser.

“Thursday,” Pauline said. It was a dismissal. She began to walk toward the students, then stopped, half-turning. “And Iris? Don’t wear heels. The floor marks.

Then she was gone, absorbed into the group, her voice blending with Martha’s, giving a low instruction. Iris sat alone on the cold metal chair. The script was a weight on her legs. Outside, the light had shifted, the sun disappearing behind a cloud, plunging the vast room into a uniform, gray dullness. The scratched floor, the silent piano, the students moving like shadows in the distance—it all felt miles away from the spotlight’s warm circle. This was the before. She didn’t yet know the shape of the after, only that a bargain had been struck in plain, unlovely terms, and the rehearsal was about to begin.

The practice room was a closet. Bare walls, a single high window filmed with city grime, a floor of scarred linoleum that gave off a faint smell of lemon polish and dust. A wooden chair stood in the center, an island. Iris shut the door. The latch clicked with a finality that made the space shrink.

She’d come here straight from the rehearsal hall, the script a stiff rectangle in her hand. The corridor had been empty. She’d heard a piano, a few bars of something complex and angry from behind another door, then silence. Now there was only the hum of a radiator pipe, a sporadic knocking deep in the walls.

She sat in the chair. The wood was cold, unforgiving. She opened the script to the marked scene. It was from The Children’s Hour. Martha and Karen. The scene where it begins to unravel. She knew the play, of course. Everyone did. A weapon disguised as art. Pauline’s choice was no accident.

Her lines were in blue ink, a tight, angular hand. Karen’s. The reasonable one. The one who tries to hold the dam together. Iris read them silently, her lips shaping the words. They were simple, declarative. “We have to be calm.” “We must think this through.” The kind of lines that looked easy until you had to mean them.

She stood up. The linoleum was slick under her heels. She faced the blank wall, imagined another woman there—Pauline, younger, her hair a dark cloud, her eyes bright with a faith Iris had once mirrored. She took a breath, the way she did before a song, a slow expansion of the diaphragm.

“We have to be calm,” she said.

Her voice hit the wall and fell flat. It was her Canary voice, the one with the bruised velvet at the edges, the one that promised a shared, sophisticated sorrow. It was wrong. It coated the words in a performance of concern, a beautiful lie.

She tried again, pitching it lower, stripping the vibrato. “We have to be calm.

Better. But it was still a choice, a technique. A manipulation of the larynx and breath. Where was the fear? The real, dry-mouthed terror of a life about to be smashed to pieces? She’d felt that. In Paris, packing her single suitcase while Pauline slept, the taste of cheap wine and cheaper betrayal like a penny on her tongue. She’d been calm then, too. A dreadful, hollow calm. That was the truth. But this script wanted a different flavor of panic.

She ran the scene. She moved through the blocking penciled in the margin—a step forward, a hand half-raised, a turn to the window that wasn’t there. She spoke Karen’s pleas, her denials. Her body executed the shapes. Her voice found the rhythms. It was competent. It was a recitation. A stranger watching would have seen a woman acting. They would not have seen a woman drowning.

The radiator knocked again, a hollow thump-thump-thump like a sluggish heart. A fly, somehow alive in this winter cell, buzzed once against the windowpane and was still.

Iris stopped. She was breathing too fast, a shallow, pointless exertion. Her palms were damp. She looked at her hands, the familiar landscape of veins and bones, the tremor now a constant, faint current. These were the tools she had. This body, this voice. They had carried a tune for thirty years. They had smiled for photographers, accepted glasses of champagne, endured Arthur’s proprietary touches. They had been a vehicle for a product. Now they were being asked to become a conduit for a truth, and they had forgotten how.

She started the scene again. This time, she tried to force it. She pushed the emotion up from her stomach, a hot, ugly knot of it. “You can’t believe this,” she said, and her voice cracked, but it was a theatrical crack, a calculated break. She winced. It was worse than before. It was pathetic.

She sat down heavily in the chair. The script slid from her lap to the floor, pages fanning out. She didn’t pick it up. She stared at the wall, at a long, hairline crack that started at the baseboard and zigzagged upward, disappearing into the shadow near the ceiling. It looked like a bolt of lightning frozen in plaster.

What was the difference? A song was a mask, too. You put on the sadness of the lyric, you felt an echo of it, you sold it. But you always knew where the door was. You could step out of the spotlight, wipe off the makeup, and be someone else—someone empty, maybe, but safe. This acting, what Pauline demanded, seemed to require you to lock the door from the inside and throw away the key. To become the room.

A memory surfaced, irrelevant and sharp. The smell of a specific brand of French soap, a green, herbal scent Pauline had used. It had filled their tiny bathroom in the Left Bank flat, clinging to the damp towels. Iris hadn’t thought of that smell in a decade. Now it was there, vivid as a photograph, carrying with it a wave of something that was not quite regret, not quite grief, but a solid, physical ache behind her breastbone. It had nothing to do with Martha and Karen. It had everything to do with the fact that she was here, in this closet, failing.

The door opened.

Pauline stood there, leaning against the jamb. She’d been there long enough to see. Iris knew it from the settled, assessing look on her face, the lack of surprise.

Iris didn’t move. A hot flush crept up her neck. “I’m working on it.

“Are you?” Pauline stepped inside, letting the door close. She wore no expression at all. “It sounds like you’re working on being good. On getting it right. That’s not the job.” She walked over, bent, and picked up the scattered script pages. She didn’t hand them back. She tapped them against her palm. “Karen is terrified. But she’s also furious. She’s built a life, a school, a partnership with Martha. It’s a good life. An honest one. And a child’s lie is about to burn it down for no reason. The injustice of it is a white-hot coal in her gut. Where’s that fire, Iris?

“I don’t know.” The words were ash in her mouth.

“You should. You’ve had things burned down for no reason.” Pauline’s gaze was relentless. “Or did you think you were the only one who paid a price?

The air left the room. The fly buzzed again, a frantic, trapped sound. Iris’s throat closed. She looked at Pauline, really looked, past the armor of the turtleneck, the severe hair. She saw the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, deeper than they should have been. She saw a weariness that had nothing to do with today’s rehearsals.

“You think I didn’t pay?” The question was out before she could stop it, raw and stripped.

“I think you got a record contract,” Pauline said, her tone flat, factual. “I think you got a new last name and a handsome manager and a spot at the Stork Club. I got a letter. Then I got silence. Then I got a war. Then I got to build this place from scratch while looking over my shoulder. So forgive me if your performance of persecuted innocence rings a little hollow.

It wasn’t an accusation screamed in anger. It was a balance sheet, read aloud in a monotone. It was worse. Iris had no ledger to counter with, only the hollowed-out feeling in her own chest, the knowledge that Pauline’s accounting was, in its brutal way, correct.

Pauline held out the script. “The fire isn’t in the words. It’s in the cost of saying them. Find the cost.

She left. The door clicked shut again, a softer sound this time.

Iris sat in the silence. The radiator knocked. She looked at the script in her hand, then at the lightning-bolt crack in the wall. Find the cost. She knew the cost. It was the green scent of soap in a Parisian bathroom. It was the look on Pauline’s face just now, a look that said the transaction was forever unbalanced, and no amount of good acting could ever make it right.

She didn’t pick up the scene again. She just sat there, in the failing light, and paid.

The hallway outside the practice room was a different kind of quiet now, the earnest hive-noise of the day drained away. Only the occasional thump of a dropped prop or a burst of laughter from a distant classroom echoed in the cavernous space. The light from the high windows had faded to a deep, bruised blue, leaving the corridor in shadow. One bare bulb buzzed over a water fountain halfway down, its porcelain stained with a rust-colored crescent that looked like a dried leaf. Iris leaned against the cool plaster wall, the cheap script curled in her hand. She’d read the first three pages four times. The words remained flat, inert.

Pauline emerged from the practice room, closing the door with a soft, definitive click. She carried a leather satchel slung over one shoulder and a stack of folders under her arm. She looked tired, the sharp planes of her face softened by the poor light. For a moment, she didn’t see Iris standing there in the gloom.

“Pauline.”

Pauline stopped. Her eyes found Iris, adjusted. A flicker of something—annoyance, maybe weariness—passed over her features. “You’re still here.

“I had a question.” Iris’s voice sounded thin. “About the script.

“The read-through is Thursday. Bring it then.

“It’s not about the lines. It’s about…” Iris gestured vaguely with the rolled pages. “The boy. In your office. The one waiting. You said that was honesty. I don’t know how to do that.

Pauline shifted the folders, considering. The buzzing bulb seemed to grow louder. “Come with me.

She turned and walked down the hall, not toward the main entrance, but deeper into the building. Iris pushed off the wall and followed. They passed closed doors, a janitor’s closet with a mop bucket parked outside, a flight of narrow stairs leading down into darkness. Pauline stopped at a nondescript door marked ‘Archives – Faculty Only.’ She produced a key, unlocked it, and stepped inside.

The room was small, windowless, smelling of dust and old paper. Shelves crammed with cardboard boxes and bound scripts lined the walls. A single desk held a green-shaded lamp, a typewriter under a cover, and a precarious stack of playbills. Pauline dropped her satchel on the desk and turned on the lamp. A warm, yellow pool of light pushed back the dark, illuminating motes that danced like static.

“Sit.”

Iris sat in the wooden chair opposite the desk. Pauline remained standing, leaning against a shelf. She took a cigarette case from her pocket, offered one to Iris. A truce, or a delay. Iris took it. The flare of the match was startlingly bright in the close room. The first drag was harsh, familiar. It steadied her hands.

“The boy’s name is David,” Pauline said, exhaling smoke that curled into the lamplight. “He’s seventeen. His brother was one of the Port Chicago sailors. His mother works as a cleaner for a state senator who thinks the investigation is ‘closed satisfactorily.’ David comes here because he doesn’t know where else to put his anger. So he puts it into scenes. Badly, at first. All rage, no shape.” She tapped ash into a ceramic dish. “But the shape is what matters. The rage is the fuel. The shape is what makes it visible. And therefore dangerous.

“I don’t have that kind of fuel,” Iris said. The admission cost her something. “Not anymore.

“Don’t you?” Pauline’s gaze was direct, unsparing. “You’re here, aren’t you? Because someone has a file. Because you sang some songs during the war that maybe meant a little more than the generals thought they did. Because you look at a man from the government and you know, in your stomach, what he wants from you. That’s a kind of fuel.

The words landed like stones. Iris stared at the glowing end of her cigarette. The taste of the smoke was suddenly metallic. She thought of Arthur’s briefcase, the neat, locked compartment. The specific weight of it in his hand.

“They want names,” she said quietly.

“They always do.”

“You’re a name they’d like.”

Pauline gave a short, dry laugh. “I’m at the top of their list, darling. They don’t need you for me. They need you for the others. The ones still hiding. The ones who think a carefully worded denial is a shield.” She leaned forward, the lamp casting her shadow large against the shelves of forgotten plays. “That’s what you need to understand. What we do here isn’t therapy. It isn’t self-expression. It’s encryption.

Iris looked up. “Encryption.

“You take the raw, dangerous thing—the truth, the anger, the love they forbid—and you encode it. You hide it inside a character, inside a line written by someone else, inside a gesture that could mean two things. You deliver it in plain sight, on a stage, under lights. And the people who need to hear it, they have the key. They understand. The ones who don’t… see a play. A good performance. Nothing to report.” Pauline took a final drag, crushed the cigarette. “That boy, David? He’s learning to encrypt his grief so he can carry it out of this room without it destroying him. So he can show it to his mother, and maybe she’ll finally see it.

The concept unfolded in Iris’s mind, cold and brilliant. It was what she’d done, in her way, with the songs. A lyric about a lonely blue train that wasn’t about a train at all. A melody that borrowed a phrase from a Communist anthem, slipped into a love ballad. She’d done it instinctively, for survival. She’d never given it a name.

“You want me to encrypt,” she said.

“I want you to understand what you’re already good at,” Pauline corrected. Her voice lost its lecture-hall edge, dropped into something quieter, more intimate. “You spent a career singing other people’s words, making audiences feel like they were yours. That’s a skill. A powerful one. I’m not asking you to become David. I’m asking you to use your skill for something that isn’t a transaction. To hide a real message in the performance. For once.

The air in the little room felt charged, thin. Iris noticed a crack in the plaster above Pauline’s head, a fine, hairline thing that branched like a river delta on a map. It meant nothing. It was just a crack. But she fixed on it, her mind scrambling for purchase.

“Why?” The word was out before she could stop it. “After everything. Why show me this?

Pauline was silent for a long time. She looked down at her hands, at the faint smudge of ink on her thumb. “Because Arthur called me this morning,” she said, her tone flat, factual. “Before you arrived. To ‘check in.’ To remind me of the Institute’s precarious funding. To suggest that having you here, cooperating with the board’s wishes, would be seen favorably by certain… patrons. He was very clear. Your presence is part of a deal. His deal.

A cold knot tightened in Iris’s stomach. She’d known it, of course. Pauline had said it was a transaction. But hearing the mechanics of it, Arthur’s voice maneuvering behind the scenes, made it feel dirty in a new way.

“So this,” Iris gestured between them, at the hidden room, the shared smoke, “is just part of the deal? Getting me to cooperate?

“No.” Pauline met her eyes. The anger was back, but it was colder now, directed elsewhere. “This is me showing you the lock. Arthur thinks he’s handing you a script. I’m handing you the key. What you do with it…” She shrugged, a slow, weary motion. “That’s the only thing that won’t belong to him.

She pushed off the shelf, gathered her satchel. The conversation was over. Iris stood, her legs stiff. She felt unmoored, the script in her hand suddenly heavy with new potential.

Pauline paused at the door, her hand on the knob. She didn’t look back. “Thursday, ten a.m. Don’t be late. And Iris?” She finally glanced over her shoulder, her profile sharp in the lamplight. “Learn your lines. Then forget them. The truth isn’t in the words. It’s in what you manage to say between them.

She opened the door and stepped into the dark hall, leaving Iris alone in the pool of yellow light, with the dust and the silent archives and the first, faint glimmer of a terrible, necessary idea.
