The silence after the door shut seemed to thicken, to take on a texture. Iris could hear the faint, tinny echo of a radio playing somewhere else in the building, a distant swing tune. It felt obscene.

Pauline walked to her desk, a heavy oak thing scarred with cigarette burns and ink stains. She didn’t sit. She stood behind it, her hands resting on the edge, knuckles pale. The late afternoon light from the high, narrow window cut across the floor, illuminating a long rectangle of dusty floorboards and missing a stack of unbound scripts by an inch. It was a useless detail, that inch of shadow. Iris’s mind fixated on it. The light was so precise, so indifferent.

“You can go,” Pauline said, not looking at her.

“I think I’ll stay.”

“Suit yourself.”

Pauline opened a drawer, took out a pack of Chesterfields, tapped one loose. The rasp of the match was loud. She exhaled a plume of smoke that the sunlight turned to solid blue. Iris remained by the empty chairs where Duke Strauss and Langston had just been. The air still held the ghost of Duke’s cologne, something cloying and expensive, layered over Langston’s more familiar bay rum. The two scents had mingled into a third, unpleasant thing. A negotiation in the air.

The meeting had lasted forty minutes. It felt like a surgery performed without anesthetic.

Duke had arrived with Langston in tow, a bouquet of lilies in his arms for Pauline—a gesture so transparently false it had sucked the air from the room. He’d called the institute ‘charming,’ the play ‘potent,’ and Iris ‘a national treasure waiting for her second act.’ Every sentence was a velvet glove around a fist of steel. His proposal was simple, elegant, and utterly corrosive: he would produce Port Chicago on a proper Broadway stage, with a proper budget, if the third act were replaced with a patriotic tableau. Iris’s old hits woven in. A tribute to the home front. A celebration of American resilience.

“It’s not a censorship, darling,” he’d said, spreading his hands. The light caught the heavy gold of his signet ring. “It’s an enhancement. You give them the tragedy, yes, but then you lift them up. You send them home feeling proud, not… unsettled. That’s what theatre is for. A shared feeling.

Pauline had listened, her face a mask of polite attention. She’d even smiled once, a thin, tight curve of the lips that didn’t touch her eyes. When he finished, she’d asked a single question.

“And the royalties?”

“Standard Dramatists Guild contract. With a bonus for you, of course, given the… collaborative nature of the revisions.

“I see.”

Langston had leaned forward then, his voice the careful, reasonable one he used when explaining a bad review to her. “It’s a lifeline, Iris. For you. A Broadway credit, your music revived. It neutralizes the… other concerns.” He didn’t look at her when he said it. He looked at the lilies, already drooping in their vase.

Iris had said nothing. The words piled up behind her teeth, a jumble of chords with no key.

It was Pauline who refused. Not with a shout, but with a quiet, final clarity that was worse.

“The play ends with the silence,” she said. “There is no lift. The hole is the point. You don’t send them home proud. You send them home ashamed, or thoughtful, or angry. You send them home with a question they can’t shake. If you change that, you don’t have my play. You have a decoration.

Duke’s smile hadn’t faltered. It had just cooled, like something removed from an oven and left to sit. “Art is one thing, Mrs. Carter. Commerce is another. I’m offering you commerce that doesn’t entirely strangle your art. That’s more than most get.

“I’m aware of what most get.”

“Then you’ll consider it.”

“I have. The answer is no.

The silence that followed was different from this one. That had been a tactical silence, a waiting game. Langston had flushed, a blotchy red creeping up from his collar. He’d started to speak, but Duke placed a hand on his arm, a gentle, proprietorial touch. “No need,” he’d murmured. “The lady has a vision. We must respect that.” He’d stood, buttoned his suit jacket with a smooth, practiced motion. “The offer stands, should the vision… expand. The theatre is the Ethel Barrymore. We begin rehearsals in six weeks. The clock, as they say, is ticking.

And then they were gone, leaving the lilies and the smell of cologne and the ticking clock.

Now, in the aftermath, Iris’s throat was tight. Not with unshed tears, but with a kind of furious helplessness. She was the prize in a tug-of-war, and the rope was cutting into her hands. Duke and Langston on one end, Pauline on the other, and Cross somewhere in the shadows, holding the knife.

“He’ll find another way,” Iris said. Her voice sounded strange in the quiet, too high. “If he wants the theatre, he’ll get it. He has the money. He’ll produce a rival. Something patriotic. He’ll drown you out.

Pauline took a long drag on her cigarette. “Probably.

“You could have negotiated. You could have kept some of it.

“Some of what?” Pauline finally looked at her. “The truth? There’s no percentage of the truth. It’s a binary. You tell it or you don’t.

“That’s a luxury.”

“Is it?” Pauline stubbed out the cigarette, half-smoked, in a heavy glass ashtray. The movement was quick, violent. “Or is it the only thing that isn’t a luxury? Everything else is a negotiation. Safety. Comfort. Reputation. Those are the luxuries. Truth is the baseline. You give that up, you have nothing left to trade.

Iris felt a hot, prickling sensation across her scalp. It wasn’t anger. It was closer to shame, but sharper, more specific. “You think I don’t know that?

“I think you know the price of everything and the value of nothing.” Pauline quoted it flatly, without inflection. It wasn’t an accusation. It was an observation, and that made it worse. She came out from behind the desk, leaning against it, crossing her arms. She wore a simple black sweater, the sleeves pushed up to her elbows. A thin silver watch on her wrist, the face tiny and elegant. Iris remembered that watch. She’d won it in a writing competition in Lyon. 1936. “This producer. He’s Langston’s idea.

“Yes.”

“To save you. Or to save his investment?

“Is there a difference, in his mind?”

Pauline didn’t answer. She studied Iris, her gaze steady, appraising. “And the other one? The government man. What does he want from this… collaboration?

The question landed like a stone in still water. Iris could feel the ripple move through her, a cold tightening in her stomach. She had filed two reports. Bland, empty things. Atmosphere is serious. Discussions are artistic. No overt political speech noted. Cross had received them with polite thanks, but his eyes had been dissatisfied at their last meeting in the diner. He’d asked about ‘thematic content.’ He’d asked about ‘personal connections among the company.

“I don’t know,” Iris said. It was almost true.

“You must have a theory. You’re good at theories. At systems.” Pauline’s voice was dry, but the edge from the stage was gone. This was something else. Exhaustion, maybe. “Why would a committee care about a play in a Harlem church basement? It’s not Broadway. It won’t make the papers.

“Unless it does,” Iris said. The thought arrived fully formed, unbidden. “Unless it gets attention. Then it’s a platform. Then it’s a problem.

Pauline gave a short, humorless laugh. “So we’re to stay obscure to stay safe. A fine ambition for art.

“It’s not about art. It’s about survival.

“Again with the luxuries.”

Iris turned away, walking to the window. She couldn’t look at Pauline’s face anymore. The view was of the brick wall of the adjacent building, close enough to touch if the window opened. A fire escape ladder rusted against the bricks. On the sill, a dead fly lay on its back, legs curled inward. She focused on the delicate, desiccated husk. “He asked me about the play. Cross. He knew the title. Port Chicago. He asked if it was about the Navy.

The silence behind her changed. It became attentive.

“What did you tell him?”

“I told him it was a domestic drama. A marriage under strain.

“Clever.”

“It’s what’s on the page, if you don’t read between the lines.”

Iris placed a fingertip on the cold glass. “It’s all I do anymore.” She took a breath. The air in the office was stale, tinged with old smoke and dust. “He mentioned his brother served in the Pacific. He said it like it was nothing. A casual detail.

Pauline was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was low, almost meditative. “So. It’s personal for him, too. Not just a job. That’s interesting.

“Is it?” Iris turned. “It just makes him more dangerous.

“It makes him human. Humans have cracks. Principles have none.” Pauline pushed off from the desk. She moved to a filing cabinet, pulled out a thin folder. “I need you to do something for me.

“What?”

“Take this to the printer. On Seventh Avenue. It’s the revised second act. The one you… scored.” She held out the folder. “I don’t want the carbon here. And I don’t want to send Clara.

Iris didn’t move. “Why?

“Because I’m asking you.” Pauline’s arm remained extended. The folder was a plain manila thing, unmarked. “And because you’re the only one who can explain the notations if he has questions. The printer is an old lefty from the Federal Theatre days. He speaks music. He’ll understand your marginalia.

It was a test. It had to be. A task of trust, of complicity. Taking the script off-site, to a printer with a history. It was a small step, but it was a step. Iris felt the weight of the decision, absurdly heavy for such a simple errand. If Cross asked where she’d been, she’d have to lie. A specific, concrete lie. If Langston asked, another. Each lie was a thread, and she was slowly weaving a net that would eventually hold her.

She took the folder. The paper inside was crisp, thick. “What if I’m followed?

Pauline’s eyebrows lifted, just a fraction. “Then you’re followed. You’re going to a print shop. It’s not a crime.

“It might be. Depending on what’s printed.

“It’s a play, Iris. It’s fiction.” Pauline’s mouth quirked, a ghost of her old, ironic smile. “Even the true parts.

The printer’s shop was in the basement of a tenement on Seventh Avenue, between a barbershop and a storefront church. The stairs down were steep, lit by a single, flyspecked bulb. The smell was glorious—ink, hot metal, and the dry, sweet scent of paper stock. It was a smell from another life, from the print shops in Paris that produced Pauline’s radical pamphlets on tissue-thin paper.

The printer was a man named Sol, with forearms thick as hams and a permanent smudge of black along his left cheekbone. He took the folder, peered at the notations through wire-rimmed glasses perched on the end of his nose.

“This is Pauline’s?”

“She doing okay?”

“As well as can be expected.”

Sol grunted, flipping through the pages. He paused at a page Iris had marked, where she’d drawn a small staff and noted a dissonant chord progression for Eleanor’s monologue. “This is you?

“I’m… assisting.”

“Huh.” He looked up at her, his eyes magnified behind the lenses. “You’re the singer. The Canary.

“Not anymore.”

“Once a canary, always a canary. They’re the ones that sense the gas.” He said it without malice, a simple statement of fact. He went to a large, cast-iron machine, began arranging metal type with a quick, practiced clatter. “Tell her I’ll have it by Thursday. The usual drop.

“The usual?”

“The bookstore. On 131st. She knows.

Iris nodded, feeling like a courier in a film. The whole exchange had taken less than five minutes. As she turned to leave, Sol spoke again, his voice lowered though they were alone.

“You watch your back, girl. Pauline’s got principles. Principles make shadows. And people watch shadows.

She climbed the stairs back into the grey afternoon light, the warning humming in her ears. The street was busy, mothers with prams, men in work clothes heading home. Normal life. She felt utterly separate from it, a ghost moving through a solid world. She decided to walk, to clear the smell of ink from her nose. She passed a newsstand, the headlines shouting about Truman and the Soviets. She didn’t stop.

She was two blocks from the institute when she saw him.

Phillip Cross stood beside a dark sedan parked at the curb, looking at his watch. He wore a grey overcoat, his hat pulled low. He wasn’t looking for her; he seemed to be waiting for someone. Or something. The coincidence was too perfect, too sharp. Her footsteps faltered. She could cross the street, duck into the five-and-dime. But he looked up.

Their eyes met across twenty feet of sidewalk.

There was no surprise in his face. Only a mild, polite recognition. He gave a small nod.

Iris’s heart was a frantic drum against her ribs. She forced her legs to move, to carry her toward him. Running would be an admission. Ignoring him would be suspicious. She had to pass him to get back to the institute.

As she drew near, he touched the brim of his hat. “Mrs. Finch.

“Mr. Cross.

“A pleasant afternoon for a walk.”

“I had an errand.” The folder felt like a beacon in her hand. She considered tucking it under her arm, but the movement would be too deliberate.

“Ah.” His gaze drifted to it, then back to her face. “The institute keeps you busy.

“It does.”

“Good.” He smiled. It was a thin, professional expression. “I was just in the neighborhood myself. A follow-up with another… contact. It’s a small world.

The implication hung in the air, ugly and clear. I have other informants here. You are not my only set of eyes. Was it a bluff? A pressure tactic? Iris’s mouth was dry. She remembered the taste of the diner coffee from their last meeting, bitter and burnt.

“I imagine it is,” she said.

He leaned back against the car, casual. “How is the play coming? The one about the Navy incident.

“It’s… progressing.”

“I’d be interested to read it, when it’s complete. For context. My brother, as I mentioned, was at Leyte Gulf. The Navy’s culture is… particular. I’m sure Mrs. Carter captures it well.

He was asking for the script. He was asking her to steal it for him. The request was veiled in professional interest, but it was a demand. Iris felt a cold knot solidify in her gut.

“I’m not sure I could get a copy. They’re rather protective.

“Of course. Artistic temperament.” He nodded, as if he understood completely. “Well. Perhaps you could summarize the themes. The character motivations. In your next report. That would be nearly as good.

“I’ll try.”

“Please do.” He pushed off from the car, opened the driver’s door. “My regards to your husband. He must be pleased with the Broadway interest. A fortunate development.

He knew about Duke. Of course he knew. He knew everything. He was a spider at the center of a web, feeling every vibration.

“I’ll tell him,” Iris said, her voice barely her own.

He tipped his hat again, slid into the sedan. The engine purred to life, a quiet, powerful sound. He pulled away from the curb, merging into the traffic without a glance back.

Iris stood on the sidewalk, the cold of the afternoon seeping through her coat. The folder in her hand was no longer just paper. It was a token. A thing that could be traded. To Cross, for a shred of mercy. To Langston and Duke, for a Broadway stage. To Pauline, for… what? A sliver of forgiveness? A shared silence?

She looked down at her hands. They were trembling. Not much, just a fine, constant shake, like a wire plucked and left to hum. She made a fist, nails digging into her palm. The sharp pain helped. It was a sensation she could name.

When she finally moved, her steps were slow, deliberate. She wasn’t heading back to the institute. She couldn’t face Pauline, not with Cross’s words echoing in her head, not with the tremor in her hands. She turned west, toward the park.

She walked until she found an empty bench, away from the paths. The trees were bare, skeletal against the grey sky. A few stubborn brown leaves clung to the branches, rattling in the wind. She sat, placing the folder beside her. She took out her Zippo lighter, the one from Paris. The metal was warm from her pocket. She flipped it open, closed it. Snick.

The rhythm was steady, hypnotic.

She thought about systems. About codes. Her life had become a series of overlapping, conflicting ciphers. The script was a code for the truth. Her reports were a code for survival. Her marriage was a code for respectability. Her music had been a code for feeling. And Pauline—Pauline was the original cipher, the one she had never fully broken, because to break it would have meant admitting she was not brave enough to live in the clear text.

A squirrel approached, stopped a few feet away, watching her with black, shiny eyes. It twitched its nose, then scampered off, disappointed she had no food.

Principles make shadows. Sol’s words. And people watch shadows.

Cross was watching the shadow of the play. Duke wanted to erase the shadow with a spotlight. Langston wanted to profit from the shadow’s silhouette. And Pauline stood in the shadow, refusing to step into the light because the light was a lie.

Iris was standing in the middle, in the half-light, where everything was a grey, shapeless murk. Where every choice was a betrayal of someone.

She opened the folder. The top page was the revised second act, her notations in the margin. She’d written ‘cello, sul ponticello – brittle, glassy’ next to a stage direction about Eleanor setting the table. She’d drawn a small, sharp treble clef where the character was to force a smile.

It was a good system. It worked. It made the emotion precise, transmissible. It was also, as Pauline said, a way to avoid feeling it. To treat the feeling as a problem of orchestration, not a fact of the heart.

She closed the folder. The wind picked up, scattering a handful of dried leaves across the path. They skittered like tiny, frantic animals.

She knew what she had to do. It was the only move left that wasn’t a surrender. She had to write a report for Cross. A real one. One that contained truth, but a truth so carefully framed it would seem like compliance while giving nothing away. She would summarize the themes of Port Chicago as a domestic tragedy, a study of a marriage fractured by secrecy. She would write about Eleanor’s guilt, her silence, her complicity in a system she couldn’t name. She would make it sound like a soap opera. She would give him exactly what he asked for, and he would be satisfied, because it would confirm his belief that art was just psychology, that politics was always personal.

And in doing so, she would tell him the truth. Because the play was about that. It was about her. It was about every silent woman who polished the silver while the world burned. He just wouldn’t know he was reading her confession.

It was a new code. A code within a code. A lie that was true.

The irony was almost beautiful. It was the most artistic thing she’d done in years.

She stood, tucking the folder under her arm. The lighter went back into her pocket, a familiar weight against her thigh. She started the long walk home, to the apartment where Langston would be waiting, full of questions about Duke, about the play, about her loyalties.

The sky was darkening, the grey deepening to charcoal. In a window across the street, a light winked on, yellow and warm. A room where someone was living a simple, un-coded life. She watched it until she passed, a faint ache behind her eyes that wasn’t quite envy, wasn’t quite grief. It was just the distance between her and that light, measured in blocks, in years, in choices. A distance that had no name.
