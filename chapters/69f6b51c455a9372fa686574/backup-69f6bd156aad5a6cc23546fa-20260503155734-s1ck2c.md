The taxi left her at the curb with a shudder of its chassis. The Institute’s stone facade looked different in the flat afternoon light—not the imposing edifice of her interview, but a tired building, its windows reflecting the gray-white sky. A stray piece of newspaper skittered across the empty steps, catching on the iron railing. She watched it flutter for a moment, a pointless little dance. Her mouth tasted of the gin she’d swallowed too quickly in the cab, a thin, medicinal burn at the back of her throat. Not enough.

Pauline’s office was on the third floor, at the end of a corridor that smelled of old wood and lemon polish. The door stood ajar. Iris knocked once, the sound swallowed by the thick silence.

“It’s open.” Pauline’s voice came from within, clipped, without warmth.

The office was a cave of books. They lined every wall, spilled from shelves onto the floor in leaning towers, covered the single broad desk in uneven strata. The only clear space was the window behind the desk, which offered a view of a brick wall and a sliver of alley. Pauline sat in a worn leather chair, a script open before her. She didn’t look up.

Iris stepped inside, her heels sinking into a thin, faded rug. The air was close, thick with the scent of paper and dust and, faintly, the lavender soap Pauline had always used. A memory surfaced, sharp and unwelcome: Pauline’s hair, still damp from a bath, smelling of that same soap as she leaned over a café table in Paris, arguing about Brecht. Iris pushed it down.

“You found it,” Pauline said, still not looking up. She made a small mark on the script with a pencil.

“Your directions were adequate.”

“They usually are.” Pauline closed the script. She finally looked at Iris. Her gaze was a physical assessment, taking in the navy suit, the careful makeup, the gloved hands clutching a purse. “Sit. If you can find a chair.

A wooden chair was wedged between a bookshelf and a stack of bound periodicals. Iris moved a heavy volume titled The Federal Theatre Project: A Critical History and pulled the chair out. It scraped loudly on the floorboards.

“I read your notes on the first act,” Pauline said. She leaned back, lacing her fingers over her stomach. The posture was deceptively casual. “They’re surprisingly good.

“Surprisingly?”

“You were always a lyricist. Not a dramatist. The skills don’t always translate.” Pauline’s tone was matter-of-fact, a professor delivering a verdict. “But you have an ear for the subtextual rhythm of a scene. The things people don’t say. I suppose a lifetime of singing other people’s torch songs teaches you that.

It wasn’t a compliment. It was an excavation. Iris felt a hot prickle at the base of her skull. “I made some suggestions. That’s what you asked for.

“I did.” Pauline pushed the script across the desk. Iris’s notes were in the margins, her handwriting—once loose and flowing, now more controlled—a dark blue contrast to the typed text. “Your idea for Mrs. Delaney’s monologue in scene two. Cutting the exposition about the union meeting. Having her just… look at the shipping manifest. Let the numbers do the talking.” She paused. “It’s more brutal. More honest. Why?

The question hung there. Why? Because the numbers were the truth. Because words about solidarity were just noise next to the cold tally of lives considered expendable. Because she’d spent years singing pretty lies over ugly math. She cleared her throat. “It felt stronger.

“It is stronger.” Pauline watched her. A shaft of pale light from the window caught the silver streaks in her close-cropped hair. “It’s also the kind of choice that gets a play labeled ‘agitprop’ and closed down. You understand that?

“I understand the risk.”

“Do you?” Pauline’s voice dropped, became almost conversational. “Arthur’s wife, the respectable professor, advising a radical playwright on how to make a play about a naval disaster more incendiary. He’d have a fit.

Iris’s stomach tightened. She kept her face still. “My professional opinion is separate from my personal circumstances.

Pauline let out a short, dry sound that wasn’t a laugh. “Nothing is separate, Iris. Not here. Not now.” She opened a drawer and took out two glasses and a bottle of bourbon. No label. She poured two fingers into each glass, pushed one across the desk. “Your professional opinion is interesting. It’s also a liability. For you. Possibly for this production.

Iris took the glass. The bourbon was dark, the color of old honey. She didn’t drink. “Are you rejecting my notes?

“I’m trying to understand your angle.” Pauline took a sip, her eyes never leaving Iris’s face. “You vanish for twelve years. You become a… a national mood. A voice on the radio for lonely housewives. Then you show up here, fresh from a summons to Washington, with a husband who practically has ‘government stooge’ stamped on his forehead. And you give me notes that would make Clifford Odets blush. So. What’s the play, Iris? What’s your role?

The directness was a slap. Iris had expected the old resentment, the cold courtesy. Not this surgical unmasking. The heat in her skull spread down her neck. She took a swallow of bourbon. It burned, a different, deeper fire than the gin. “There’s no angle. I’m trying to do the job you offered.

“The job Arthur arranged.”

“Yes.”

“And he’s comfortable with you spending your afternoons in my office, helping me sharpen the knives on Port Chicago?” Pauline’s smile was thin, razor-edged. “He must be more progressive than he looks.

A crack in the plaster above Pauline’s head caught Iris’s eye. It ran from the ceiling corner down toward the window frame, a jagged little bolt of lightning frozen in the white paint. She focused on it, a pointless anchor. “Arthur’s concerns are his own. My work here is mine.

“Is it.” Pauline set her glass down. “Let me be clear. I want this play to be a weapon. A precise, devastating weapon. I don’t have time for dilettantes or for spies. If you’re here because Arthur thinks you can keep an eye on me, or because Washington told you to cozy up to the dangerous negro playwright, you should leave now. Save us both the trouble.

The word ‘spy’ landed in the quiet room like a stone dropped in still water. Iris felt the ripple of it in her chest, a cold vibration. She made herself meet Pauline’s gaze. “I’m not a spy.

“Then what are you?”

What was she? A coward. A performer. A woman with a file drawer full of evidence against her. The bourbon glass was cool and smooth in her hand. She turned it slightly, watching the light catch the rim. “I’m someone who knows what it’s like to put the wrong words in your mouth because the right ones are too dangerous. I know how it feels to stand in a spotlight and feel completely invisible.” Her voice was quiet, almost toneless. “I don’t know what my angle is, Pauline. But the notes I gave you… those are the right words. However dangerous they are.

Silence filled the room again, thicker now. Pauline studied her, the professional assessment gone, replaced by something older, more weary. She finally nodded, once. “Alright.

“Alright?”

“We’ll try it your way. The monologue.” Pauline picked up her pencil again, tapped it lightly on the script. “But we do it properly. You’ll work with the actor. Eleanor. She’s talented, but green. She needs to understand the weight of what she’s not saying. You’ll coach her.

Iris blinked. “I’m not a director.

“No. You’re the person who understood the scene. So you’ll teach her.” Pauline’s tone brooked no argument. It was the voice of the head of theatre, allocating a resource. “Starting tomorrow. Four PM. Rehearsal room B.

It was a concession and a test, bundled together. A step deeper into the machine of the play. Iris felt a strange, fluttering sensation behind her ribs—not quite fear, not quite anticipation, a nervous quickening like a bird trapped in a chimney. “Alright.

Pauline looked back at her script, the dismissal clear. “Don’t be late.

Iris stood, her legs unsteady. She placed the untouched bourbon glass back on the desk. As she turned to go, her eye caught a small, framed photograph on a shelf, half-hidden behind a stack of books. It was a black-and-white shot of a group of people on a makeshift stage somewhere, arms linked. Pauline was in the center, younger, her head thrown back in laughter. Iris couldn’t make out the others. She looked away, a sudden, sharp guilt needling her. It was none of her business.

She was at the door when Pauline spoke again, her voice low.

“The funding came through. For the festival slot.

Iris stopped, her hand on the doorframe. The wood was smooth, worn down by countless hands.

“Leo Strauss,” Pauline continued, as if commenting on the weather. “Broadway money. He’s attaching his name as a producer. Wants a ‘prestige piece with social relevance.’ His words.” She looked up, her expression unreadable. “He specifically asked if you were still involved. Said your name ‘lent a certain gravity.’ I thought you should know.

The fluttering behind Iris’s ribs stilled, replaced by a cold, solid weight. Gravity. Leo Strauss, who traded in reputations, who polished tarnished stars for a percentage. Arthur would know him. Of course Arthur would know him. This wasn’t an opportunity. It was a transaction, and she was a token on the board, being moved by hands she couldn’t see.

“I see,” Iris said, because there was nothing else to say.

“Do you?” Pauline asked again, but this time it sounded less like an accusation and more like a genuine question. She held Iris’s gaze for a long moment, then dropped her eyes back to the script. “Four PM, Finch. Don’t forget.

Iris stepped into the hall, pulling the door shut behind her. The click of the latch was soft, final. She stood there in the quiet corridor, the cold weight settling in her stomach, thinking not about the play or the monologue or the dangerous words, but about the specific, lemony scent of the polish on the floor and how it almost, but not quite, covered the smell of old dust.

The street was a canyon of noise. A delivery truck backfired, the sound like a fist hitting sheet metal. Iris flinched, her shoulder hitting the glass wall of the phone booth. The receiver was cold and heavy in her hand, the cord a greasy spiral. She dropped a nickel into the slot, the clatter too loud in the cramped space. The dial tone was a flat, accusatory hum.

She dialed the apartment. One ring. Two. Arthur’s voice, smooth as poured cream. “Finch residence.

“It’s me.”

A pause, just a beat too long. “Iris. You’re late. I expected a call an hour ago.

“The meeting ran long.” She watched a man across the street adjust the awning of a hat shop. He moved with a slow, deliberate patience that felt like an accusation. “Pauline has questions.

“I’m sure she does.” Paper rustled on his end. “And your answers?

“Vague. Professional.” Her throat was tight. She remembered the bourbon, left untouched on Pauline’s desk. The taste of cheap gin was still there, a sour ghost. “She thinks I’m playing an angle.

“You are. We are.” Another rustle. “It’s called survival. Did you secure the commitment to the play?

“Not yet. She’s wary. The notes I gave her… they were too good. She doesn’t trust why I’d give them.

Arthur’s sigh traveled down the line, a whisper of static. “Iris. The point is to be useful enough to be included. Not so useful you become a liability. You’ve always had a problem with moderation.

A bus rumbled past, its diesel fumes seeping into the booth. The smell was thick, chemical. It reminded her of the backstage area at a USO show in Norfolk, 1943. Greasepaint and engine oil. She pushed the memory away. It had no business here. “She offered me a drink. I didn’t take it.

“Wise.” The approval in his voice was worse than criticism. “Maintain the distance. Remember the narrative. Respectable professor. Dedicated wife. Gradually, cautiously, supportive of a worthy artistic cause. That’s the through-line.

The through-line. A script. She leaned her forehead against the glass. It was cool, smudged with fingerprints. “And the file? The insurance?

“Safe. Where it belongs.” His tone shifted, a fractional cooling. “Why?

“I need to know what’s in it. Exactly.

Silence. Then a soft, dry chuckle. “No, you don’t. You really don’t. Knowing the inventory doesn’t change the premium, darling. It just gives you nightmares.

“It’s my life.”

“It’s our collateral.” The words were precise, surgical. “Let me manage it. You focus on Pauline. On being convincingly reformed. The festival committee meets next Thursday. I need you firmly embedded by then.

Something in his voice—a thread of urgency she hadn’t heard before. It wasn’t about her. It was about a timeline. “Why Thursday?

“That’s not your concern.”

“It is if my performance has a deadline.”

A longer silence. She could picture him at the desk in the study, the green-shaded lamp pooling light on the blotter, his fingers steepled. “There are… other considerations. Broader assessments. My position with certain clients is being evaluated. Your cooperation, your visible stability in this role, reflects well on my management. It demonstrates I can handle… delicate situations.

A cold knot formed low in her stomach. Not fear. Something colder, drier. The realization that she was not the only one being traded. Arthur was using her compliance as a currency with his other, unnamed clients. She was a ledger entry in a larger, darker book. “I see.

“I knew you would.” His voice warmed again, a professional fondness. “Now. I have the first draft of your statement for the Institute’s newsletter. A few bland paragraphs about the joy of mentoring young talent. I’ll leave it on the desk for your signature. We’ll need it by tomorrow.

“Arthur.” Her hand was sweating around the receiver. “The letters. From Paris. The photographs. Are they… are they all there?

He didn’t answer right away. When he did, his voice was so quiet she had to press the receiver hard against her ear. “Every one. Even the silly little sketches you did in the margins. The dove. The broken chain. It’s all there, Iris. Every beautiful, dangerous thought you ever put on paper.” He paused. “It’s a complete set. Now. I have a call. We’ll speak tonight.

The line went dead.

She held the buzzing receiver for a long moment before hanging it up. The nickel was gone. Consumed. Outside, the hat-shop man had finished his task and stood wiping his hands on his apron, looking at nothing.

Her gloves were in her purse. She fumbled for them, her fingers clumsy. The kid leather was soft, worn thin at the fingertips. As she pulled the right one on, she felt a crack in the seam near the thumb. A tiny, failing stitch. She stared at it. A thing of no consequence.

The walk to the subway was four blocks. Her heels clicked a steady, hollow rhythm on the pavement. She passed a diner, the window steamy, the smell of fried onions and coffee leaking out. A man inside laughed, his head thrown back, a pure, unguarded sound. She looked away.

She bought a paper from the stand at the top of the subway stairs. The headline was about baseball. She didn’t read it. The train came, a hot wind preceding it, smelling of ozone and wet concrete. She took a seat in the half-empty car. The woman across from her had a shopping bag with a loaf of bread sticking out, the end of the wrapper twisted into a neat, tight knot.

It’s a complete set.

The words circled. They didn’t mean what she’d asked. She’d asked about Paris. He’d answered about everything. A complete set implied curation. It implied he had added to it, after.

The train jolted into motion. The lights flickered. In the dark window, her reflection was a pale smudge against the rushing tunnel. A complete set would include the later things. The notes from the Stage Door Canteen in ’44. The guest list for the benefit for Spanish refugees—a list she’d typed herself, two carbon copies. The telegram from a producer in ’46, asking in veiled terms if she’d consider a concert for a certain progressive council, a telegram she’d kept because the phrasing was clever, not because she’d agreed.

He would have the carbon copies. He would have the telegram. He kept everything. A magpie of her indiscretions.

The train screeched into a station. More people got on. A boy in a newsboy cap sat next to her, smelling of hair tonic and wintergreen. He bounced his knee, a rapid, nervous tremor she felt through the bench.

Arthur’s other clients. The broader assessment. Her cooperation as proof he could handle delicate situations. The pieces clicked, a lock turning over. She wasn’t just informing to save herself. She was a demonstration. A sample of his work. He was showing someone—the government man, Henderson, or someone above him—that he could deliver a compromised asset, keep her productive and quiet. Her teaching, her involvement with Pauline’s play, it was all part of the presentation. See? She’s useful. She’s contained. I can do this with others.

The cold knot in her stomach tightened, a hard, definite fist. She’d known he was trading her. She hadn’t understood she was the prototype.

The boy got off two stops later. The train emptied again. She looked at the paper in her lap. Below the baseball headline was a smaller item: a city councilman had denounced a proposed community center, calling it a front for un-American activity. The words blurred.

She thought of the broken stitch in her glove. A tiny thing. But once it went, the whole seam would unravel. You could try to mend it, but the mend would always be a weak point, a thickening in the line.

The train reached her stop. She stood, her legs stiff. She climbed the stairs to the street. The afternoon light was failing, a thin yellow wash over the brownstones. Her apartment building looked the same. The same potted geraniums, wilted now, on the stoop. The same brass knob, polished weekly.

Inside the foyer, the mail slot. Among the circulars and bills, a long, cream-colored envelope. No stamp. Hand-delivered. Her name was typed on the front.

She picked it up. The paper was thick, expensive. She tore it open standing there under the weak foyer light.

A single sheet. Also typed.

Mrs. Finch,

A mutual friend suggests a meeting might be beneficial. Your insights into certain artistic networks could be of great service to your country. Your husband has outlined your willingness to cooperate. To discuss the scope of this service, please be at the counter of the Oyster Bar at Grand Central Terminal this Friday at 1 p.m. Ask for the daily special. Come alone.

No reply is necessary. Your attendance will be confirmation.

There was no signature.

The words swam. Your husband has outlined your willingness to cooperate. He hadn’t just shown them the sample. He’d booked the order.

She folded the letter. She folded it again, and again, until it was a thick, small square. She put it in her coat pocket. Her hand brushed against the crack in her glove.

In the apartment, the silence was a held breath. On the desk in the study, as promised, lay a single sheet of paper—her statement for the newsletter. Next to it, a pen.

She didn’t go in. She walked to the kitchen, filled a glass with water, and drank it slowly. The water tasted of pipes. Outside the window, on the fire escape of the building opposite, a gray cat sat licking one paw with fastidious care. It took no notice of her.

She put the glass down. The sound was too sharp.

From the study, the telephone rang. She didn’t move. It rang six times, then stopped. The silence after was deeper, waiting.

She took the square of paper from her pocket, unfolded it, and held it over the sink. She struck a match. The flame was a small, eager bloom. She held it to the corner until the paper caught, then dropped it into the white porcelain basin. It blackened, curled, dissolved into fragile ash. She turned the tap on, a thin stream, and washed it all away.

The cat on the fire escape finished its toilet and stood, stretching its back into a high arch. Then it leapt, soundless, to a higher landing and was gone.

The bourbon sat untouched on the desk between them, a dark, accusatory pool. Iris’s fingers had gone cold around the glass. The question—What’s your role?—echoed in the quiet of the book-crowded room. It wasn’t a question. It was a verdict waiting for her to confirm it.

“My role is to teach voice,” she said. The words sounded thin, unconvincing even to her. “To offer notes on a script. That’s the arrangement.

“Arrangement.” Pauline tasted the word. She set her glass down with a soft click. “You always had a gift for euphemism. It was a ‘professional disagreement’ in Paris. Not a betrayal. This is an ‘arrangement.’ Not collaboration.” She leaned forward, the leather of her chair sighing. “Arthur’s arrangement. Henderson’s arrangement. Where does it end, Iris? When do you get to stop arranging your life and start living it?

Something hot and sour rose in Iris’s throat. Not anger. Something closer to shame, but sharper, more metallic. Her vision narrowed to the grain of the desk between them, a swirling pattern in the old oak like a frozen storm. She noticed, absurdly, a single carved initial—a child’s clumsy ‘J’—near the corner. Someone’s long-ago mischief.

“You think it’s that simple.” Iris’s voice was low. “You always did.

“I think it’s that hard.” Pauline’s gaze didn’t waver. “And you’ve spent twelve years pretending it isn’t. You traded a hard truth for an easy lie. Now the lie is cracking. And you’re sitting in my office, giving me good notes, wondering why it doesn’t feel like redemption.” She pushed back from the desk, the chair rolling a few inches. “The notes are good. I’ll use some of them. But I don’t need a co-writer. I need to know if the woman giving them is going to be in the audience on opening night with her husband and his government friends, pointing out which lines are a little too true.

Iris stood up. The movement was too quick. Her knee bumped the desk, sloshing the bourbon in her glass. A drop landed on the script, darkening a typed word: complicit. She stared at it. The liquid spread, blurring the ink.

“I should go.”

“You should.” Pauline didn’t stand. She picked up her pencil again, turning her attention back to the script as if Iris were already a memory. “Rehearsals for Port Chicago start Thursday. Ten a.m. My classroom. If you’re coming, be ready to work. If you’re not, send a note. I’d prefer the note.

The dismissal was absolute. Iris set the glass down, the bourbon still untouched. She walked to the door, her heels too loud on the floorboards. The smell of lavender and dust followed her into the hall.

The apartment was a cell. Three rooms on the Upper West Side, paid for by Arthur, furnished by a decorator who believed in beige. It held nothing of her. No photographs. No sheet music left on the piano—she hadn’t touched it in months. Just furniture, precise and disapproving, like a museum diorama of a normal life.

She shrugged off her suit jacket, dropped it over the back of a chair. The single lamp by the writing desk cast a tight circle of yellow light, leaving the rest of the room in a deep, velvety gloom. Outside, a siren waned into the distance, a dying animal sound.

She sat. From her purse she took the script, the pages now slightly curled from her grip. She laid it on the blotter. Next to it, a fresh sheet of stationery. The paper was thick, expensive, monogrammed with an elegant ‘I’ that felt like a forgery.

Her hand trembled. Just a faint quiver in the fingers of her right hand. She pressed them flat against the cool wood of the desk until the shaking stopped.

What was her role?

She opened the script to the second act. Mrs. Delaney’s monologue. The one she’d suggested cutting back to the bare numbers. Pauline had seen the brutality in it. She’d seen the truth. Iris read the lines again, hearing not the character’s voice, but the rhythm of it. The spaces between the words. It was a song without melody. A lament built on ledgers.

Her pencil moved. Not on the script, but on the blank sheet.

She didn’t write words at first. She drew a series of marks. Vertical lines, like tally marks. Then she began to group them. Five lines, crossed with a sixth. Five and one. A system from another life. A code she and Pauline had invented in a Paris café, giggling and serious, for passing notes during tedious lectures. One mark for ‘agree.’ Two for ‘disagree.’ Three for ‘meet me after.’ Five and one… that had been their private signal for danger. For ‘say the opposite of what you mean.

Her breath caught. A stupid, sentimental cipher. Childish. The kind of thing Arthur’s file would catalogue as evidence of a subversive mind.

She looked at the tally groups on the page. Then at Mrs. Delaney’s lines.

She began to write, not lyrics, not dialogue, but a hybrid thing. Phrases from the monologue broken apart and stitched back together with the cadence of her old songs. The manifest shows seventy-three. Seventy-three souls signed away with a clerk’s flourish. The ink was blue. I remember the blue. She let the pencil lead her, following the rhythm down the page. It wasn’t good. It was raw, awkward, the seams showing. But it felt like a translation. A message sent from one locked room to another, using a frequency only she could hear.

A floorboard creaked in the hallway outside her apartment door.

Iris froze. The pencil tip snapped against the paper.

Silence. Then the faint, unmistakable sound of someone moving away, footsteps receding down the hall carpet. Not a neighbor. Too hesitant. Too quiet.

Arthur didn’t trust her. Of course he didn’t. He had the file, but files were static. People were not. He would want to know what she did in the silence of her beige cage.

A cold knot tightened under her ribs. It wasn’t fear, exactly. It was a colder, quieter cousin. Recognition.

She looked down at the coded page, at the broken pencil lead. Proof. Of nothing, and of everything.

Slowly, deliberately, she struck a match. The flame was a tiny, violent bloom in the dim room. She held it to the corner of the stationery. The paper blackened, curled, caught. She dropped it into the empty metal wastebasket and watched it burn, the light dancing on the walls, until there was nothing left but fragile gray ash.

The role was silence. The role was compliance.

But the ash held the shape of the words for a second before it collapsed. And she had memorized the rhythm.
