The lobby of the institute held a particular quality of quiet in the late morning, the kind that felt thick and expectant, like the pause before the downbeat in a slow ballad. Dust motes swam in a slant of pale light from the high, arched windows. Iris stood just inside the heavy double doors, the smell of old wood and floor wax wrapping around her. A fly buzzed against a windowpane, a persistent, tinny drone. She’d meant to go straight to the rehearsal room, but her feet stopped. The place was different when empty. The rows of mismatched chairs, the scuffed podium, the faded poster for a long-ago production of Porgy and Bess—it all seemed less like a fortress and more like a waiting room. A place where people sat, hoping for something.

From the administrative office down the short hall, a voice carried. Not Pauline’s. Deeper, smoother, a voice used to filling rooms without effort. Duke Strauss.

“—a matter of optics, Jerry. That’s all I’m saying.

Iris didn’t move. The fly gave up, landed on the windowsill, and rubbed its forelegs together.

Another voice, tinny and distant through a telephone receiver. “And the visibility?

“The visibility is the point. It’s the perfect cover. A has-been chanteuse finding redemption in serious theatre? The papers will eat it up. It’s sentimental. It’s safe. It draws the eye right where we want it.

A long pause. Iris’s throat tightened. She watched the dust motes, her mind a blank, white static.

The tinny voice said something she couldn’t catch. Strauss laughed, a short, dry sound. “Of course they’re watching. Let them watch the show. The real work is in the receipts. The provenance is clean, I told you. The money’s been through three different accounts. It looks like a standard angel investment from a bored widow in Connecticut. Her politics are suitably… opaque.

Another murmur from the phone.

“I am insulating them,” Strauss said, a new edge in his voice. “That’s what this whole production is. A very expensive, very public insulator. You think I’d risk the others? This is the buffer. If the committee gets curious, they see a Broadway producer trying to make a buck off a tragedy and a faded star. They see commerce. They understand commerce. They don’t look past the marquee.

Iris’s hand found the back of a wooden chair. The varnish was sticky under her fingertips. A bored widow in Connecticut. Three different accounts. The words arranged themselves in her head without her permission, forming a shape she didn’t want to recognize. The funding. Pauline’s secured bank draft. The play wasn’t just a play; it was a front. A buffer. A distraction. And she was the marquee.

The fly took off, buzzing back toward the window.

“The Finch woman is the key,” Strauss said, his voice dropping, becoming almost confidential. “She’s the plausible deniability. Her history, her… proclivities, they’re just spicy backstory now. A comeback tale. Nobody suspects the package. They’re all looking at the pretty, damaged wrapper.

A hot, sour taste flooded the back of her tongue. She was the wrapper. The distraction. The thing you looked at so you didn’t see the hands moving behind the curtain.

The office door clicked open.

Iris didn’t jump. She let her hand fall from the chair and turned, as if she had just that moment arrived. Strauss emerged, pulling on a pair of fawn-colored gloves. He saw her and his face opened into a practiced, brilliant smile. It didn’t reach his eyes, which remained the cool, assessing blue of a swimming pool in winter.

“Iris! Just the person. I was hoping to catch you.” He finished with the gloves, smoothing the leather over his knuckles. “We missed you at dinner the other night. Langston said you were under the weather.

“A headache,” she said. Her own voice sounded thin, far away.

“Dreadful things. You’re well now, I hope? We need you in fighting form.” He stepped closer, bringing with him a cloud of citrus cologne and the faint, acrid smell of cigar smoke embedded in wool. “I’ve been speaking with Pauline. A force of nature, that one. I admire her passion, even if it’s a bit… unpolished. We’ve reached an understanding.

“Have you.”

“A temporary détente.” He winked, a grotesque, conspiratorial gesture. “She gets her play. I get my production. And you, my dear, get your stage. Everyone wins.

The real work is in the receipts. She looked past him, at the closed office door. Pauline was in there. Had she heard any of it? Did she know the money in her hand was laundered ideology, that her art was being used as a shield for other, nameless patrons? Or was she a part of it, too? The questions stacked up, a precarious tower in her mind.

“You’re very quiet,” Strauss observed.

“I’m listening.” She made herself meet his gaze. “You said everyone wins. What do the people who gave you the money win?

His smile froze for a fraction of a second. Just a flicker. Then it warmed again, broader, more avuncular. “My investors? They win a modest return on a worthy piece of theatre. And the satisfaction of supporting the arts. It’s not always a complex equation, Iris.

But it was. It was a labyrinth. She was standing at the entrance, and every path led into deeper shadow. Cross wanted her to spy on the institute. Strauss was using the institute as a screen. She was the pivot point between them, a silent, ornamental gear.

“I should get to rehearsal,” she said.

“Of course, of course. Break a leg, as they say.” He touched the brim of his hat. “We’ll speak soon. You and I and Langston. We have plans to make.

He walked past her, his footsteps echoing on the hardwood. The front door opened, admitting a brief gust of cold, metallic city air, then sighed shut. The lobby returned to its thick, dusty silence.

Iris stood there. The fly had found its way to the poster of Porgy and Bess, crawling slowly across Bess’s painted cheek. She thought of the report she owed Cross. A bland update on morale, on rehearsal progress. She could write it tonight. She could mention Strauss’s visit, frame it as producer interference. That would be useful.

But the words from the office clung to her. A buffer. The plausible deniability.

If she told Cross about Strauss’s interest, the committee might look closer. They might follow the money. And if they followed the money, they wouldn’t stop at Strauss. They’d find the bored widow, and the accounts, and the people who needed a buffer. They’d tear through the insulation and find the real house on fire. And the play—Pauline’s play—would be ashes before it ever opened.

Her stomach was a hard, cold knot. She hadn’t made a decision. Not consciously. But the thought of typing those sentences, of handing that particular thread to Cross, made her skin prickle with a sudden, clammy sweat. It was one thing to betray Pauline. That was personal, a old, festering debt. This was different. This was throwing a match into a room she couldn’t see.

She walked toward the rehearsal hall, her heels tapping a slow, irregular rhythm on the floor. The sound was hollow in the empty space. A dead note.

The hall outside the rehearsal room was dark, the door ajar just enough to spill a thin line of yellow light onto the floorboards. Inside, someone was moving. Not the sharp, purposeful strides of Pauline, but a slower, heavier pacing. A man’s shadow crossed the light.

Iris stopped. The instinct to turn around, to walk back down the stairs and out into the February air, was a physical pull in her shoulders. Instead, she leaned against the cool plaster of the wall. The smell here was different—dust and old sweat and the ghost of oranges, maybe from someone’s lunch. A button was missing from her cuff. She’d noticed it that morning, a tiny circle of frayed thread, and the irrelevance of it now was almost comforting.

The pacing stopped. A chair scraped.

“No, I understand the concern.” It was Leo Strauss’s voice, but stripped of its public smoothness. It was tired, frayed at the edges. “But the timeline is fixed. The money is in the account. The Hartford transfer cleared yesterday.

A pause. He must be on the telephone, the one in the corner by the prop cabinet.

“I am not questioning the commitment,” he said, lower. “I am stating a logistical reality. The play opens in six weeks. The budget is locked. If there is a… a hesitation now, it doesn’t just collapse the production. It creates a paper trail of canceled checks that people are paid to notice.

Another, longer silence. Iris watched the motes in the strip of light. They drifted aimlessly.

“The Finch woman is the insulation,” Strauss said, his voice dropping to a murmur she had to strain to hear. “That was the point. Her name, her story—it’s the perfect diversion. A sentimental comeback. A rehabilitation narrative. The committee looks at that and sees a commercial calculation. They understand greed. They don’t look for the other signatures on the check.

Her breath felt shallow. She focused on the missing button.

“I know they’re vulnerable.” A sharp sigh. “That’s why we’re using a third-party conduit. That’s why the money looks like it’s from a literary society. For God’s sake, the return address is a boarding house in Pasadena. It’s clean.

The floorboard under her right foot creaked, a soft, groaning sound. She froze. The pacing inside stopped.

“I have to go,” Strauss said, his voice abruptly normal, louder. “We’ll speak Thursday.

The receiver clicked into its cradle. The shadow moved toward the door.

Iris pushed off the wall and walked forward, making her footsteps audible. She pushed the door open as if she’d just arrived.

The rehearsal room was cavernous and cold. Strauss stood by the old upright piano, one hand resting on its scarred lid. He’d taken his jacket off. His shirtsleeves were rolled up, and he looked older without the armor of his suit. He watched her enter, his face carefully blank.

“Iris.” He didn’t smile. “Rehearsal’s not for another hour.

“I know. I left my script.” It was a lie. Her script was in her bag over her shoulder. She walked to the table where they’d worked the previous day. It was empty save for a single, bent paperclip.

“Find it?”

“Must be somewhere else.” She turned. He was still watching her, his eyes doing that cold assessment. The swimming pool in winter. “You’re here early.

“Meetings,” he said. He picked up his jacket from the back of a chair, shook it out. “Always meetings. The business of art is decidedly un-artistic.” He slid one arm into a sleeve. “You heard from Langston today?

“No.”

“He’s a busy man. Leveraging your participation into quite a few interesting conversations.” He got the other arm in, shrugged the jacket onto his shoulders. It was a performance, this dressing, a reclaiming of his persona. “It’s good. It’s what we want. Noise around the package. Buzz.

“The wrapper,” she said.

He paused, one hand on a button. “What’s that?

“Nothing.”

He finished with the button, adjusted his cuffs. “The money’s secure. For the production. I wanted you to know that. Sometimes these backers get skittish. Artistic temperaments. But it’s handled. You can tell Pauline her budget is safe.

“Why would I tell her?”

“Because you see her more than I do.” He picked an invisible piece of lint from his lapel. “And because she trusts you about as far as she can throw you, which means she might actually believe it if it comes from you. Reassurance. It helps the process.

The audacity of it was almost admirable. Using her to calm the very person his patrons were hiding behind. A closed loop of deception.

“Who are they?” The question was out before she could shape it differently.

“Who?”

His gaze sharpened. “A collection of theatre enthusiasts. A private society. They prefer anonymity. It’s not uncommon.

The temperature in the room dropped several degrees. The only sound was the distant, intermittent rumble of the furnace kicking on in the basement. Strauss’s expression didn’t change, but something behind his eyes shut down. A door closing.

“You have good hearing,” he said finally.

“The hall echoes.”

“It does.” He took a step toward her, not threatening, just closing the space. His citrus cologne was faint now, overwhelmed by the smell of his woolens and the cold room. “Let me offer you some advice, Iris. A lesson from an old showman. There are two kinds of people in a production. Those on the stage, and those in the dark, moving the sets. The audience only pays to see the first kind. They’re happier that way. Safer. If you start peeking into the wings, you see the ropes. The pulleys. The sweat. It ruins the magic. And more importantly, it puts everyone at risk.

“Including the people in the dark.”

“Especially them.” He held her look. “You wanted a stage. You have one. You wanted a chance. This is it. What happens behind the curtain is not your business. It’s mine. My risk. My problem. Your only job is to stand in the light and say the words they wrote for you. Can you do that?

She thought of the report she hadn’t filed for Cross. The empty page in her notebook. She thought of Pauline’s flat, stone-creek eyes. He sells.

“I can say the words,” Iris said.

“Good.” He nodded, a single, curt dip of his chin. The transaction was complete. “Then we understand each other. Rehearsal at two. Don’t be late. Pauline dislikes lateness almost as much as she dislikes capitalists.” He gave her a final, empty smile and walked out, his footsteps echoing down the wooden hall until they were swallowed by the building’s silence.

Iris stood alone in the room. She walked to the piano and pressed a single key. Middle C. The note was faint, slightly flat, the felt on the hammer worn thin. It hung in the air and died.

She was not just an informant on a play. She was the decoy for the play’s own secret heart. A target painted on a target. The realization didn’t arrive as a shock, but as a cold, settling certainty, like a stone sinking to the bottom of a still pond. She traced the chipped ivory of the piano key. There was a hairline crack in it, brown with age. She pressed it again. The same dead note.

From the pocket of her coat, she took out the Zippo lighter. It was cold in her hand. She didn’t flick it. She just turned it over, feeling the familiar ridges of the engraved design, long ago worn smooth by her own thumb. A thing from another life, carried like a stone in her pocket.

She put it back. The button on her cuff would need to be replaced. She made a mental note to look for a match in her sewing box, though she knew she wouldn’t. The frayed threads would just get a little longer, a little more unraveled, until one day the whole cuff would come apart.

The rehearsal room was empty. The chairs sat in their uneven rows, facing the ghost of a stage marked with tape on the floor. A single work light burned on a stand, casting a hard, white circle on the dusty boards. Iris stood just inside the door, the silence a physical thing, a dense wool packed into her ears after the sharp, transactional clarity of Strauss’s voice in the lobby.

She walked to the center of the taped square. Her footsteps echoed, a hollow, lonely sound. The fly from the lobby had followed her, or perhaps it was a different one. It orbited the hot bulb of the work light with a desperate, buzzing futility.

She let the sentence sit in her mind. It had the clean, brutal shape of a truth. Not about art, but about logistics. Money moved through accounts. It left a trail, but you could blur it. You could wrap it in something pretty and sad, like a comeback story, and no one would look at the numbers. They’d look at the face. Her face.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and irrelevant: the smell of the glue they used to paste labels on 78 rpm records in the pressing plant. A sweet, chemical stink that clung to your clothes for days. She’d visited once, early on, with Langston. He’d pointed to the machines stamping out black disks with a proprietorial pride. That’s the product, he’d said. The song is just the sauce.

She was the sauce now. The plausible deniability.

From the pocket of her skirt, her fingers found the Zippo. The metal was warm from her body. She didn’t take it out, just pressed her thumb against its ridged side through the fabric. The conversation in the lobby hadn’t been for her. It had been an accident, a door left ajar. Strauss hadn’t known she was there. That made it real in a way his polished speeches never were.

He was laundering more than money. He was laundering risk. Using her, the play, this whole fragile enterprise, as a buffer. If the committee looked, they would see Duke Strauss, Broadway hustler, trying to make a sentimental dime. They wouldn’t see the bored widow in Connecticut, or the three accounts, or the other, nameless patrons whose politics were apparently so volatile they needed a human shield.

Pauline knew. She had to. The secured bank draft, the specific terms. Pauline didn’t take money without knowing its smell. So she was part of it. Or she was using Strauss as much as he was using her. A temporary détente, he’d called it. A mutual arrangement of crossed fingers and held noses.

Iris looked at the taped outline on the floor. Her mark for the first act climax. The moment her character, the Navy clerk, understood the numbers she’d filed were men, and the men were gone. A simple, terrible arithmetic.

Her weekly report to Cross was due tomorrow. A paragraph of nothing. Rehearsals proceeding. Tensions normal. Carter focused on text. Sterile, safe words that tasted of chalk. She could add a new sentence. Producer Duke Strauss’s funding originates with left-wing patrons. He is using the production as a shield for their activities.

That was the sentence. Five dollars worth of words, maybe less. It would change the color of the file on Cross’s desk. It would make the play a target, not just a subject of observation. It would give them a reason to shut it down, to dig deeper, to subpoena the bored widow. It would pull the buffer away and expose whatever—whoever—was behind it.

The fly hit the bulb with a faint tink. It fell onto the metal reflector, legs twitching.

Iris’s throat felt tight, a familiar constriction. She swallowed. The action did nothing. She thought of the button on her cuff, the frayed threads. A small, unraveling thing.

She wouldn’t write the sentence.

The decision arrived not as a thunderclap, but as a quiet subtraction. The option simply ceased to exist in the space ahead of her. It wasn’t loyalty to Pauline. It wasn’t political conviction—she had none, not really, just a deep, animal aversion to the grey suits and their manila folders. It was something simpler, more selfish. She was part of the buffer now. An integral component of the distraction. To report the mechanism was to report herself. To draw the committee’s eye directly to the very machinery she was supposed to be hiding.

But that wasn’t all of it.

There was the other thing. The hot, sour taste in her mouth when Strauss called her the wrapper. The pretty, damaged thing you looked at so you didn’t see the hands. For twelve years, she’d been the product. The sauce. The thing to be sold. This, here, was different. This time, the wrapper had a function. It wasn’t just decoration; it was camouflage. It had a job. To protect something else.

She didn’t know what that something else was. She didn’t want to know. Knowing was a liability. But being useful, even as a decoy, even for reasons she despised… it was a different kind of cage. One with a door she couldn’t yet see.

The door to the rehearsal room creaked open. Iris didn’t turn. The footsteps were light, familiar.

Clara stood at the edge of the light circle, holding a clipboard. Her daughter’s face was in shadow, unreadable. “You’re early.

“I was in the neighborhood.”

Clara didn’t smile. She never did. “Pauline’s running late. A meeting with the set designer. She said to start without her if you wanted.

“Start what?”

“Blocking the courtroom scene. It’s on the schedule.” Clara’s voice was flat, efficient. She held out the clipboard like a nurse offering a thermometer.

Iris took it. The page was a neatly typed scene breakdown, with marginal notes in Pauline’s tight, angular script. Less indignation. More exhaustion. The truth is tired.

“She wants truth from a woman playing a liar playing a clerk,” Iris said, more to herself than to Clara.

“It’s not about truth. It’s about accuracy.” Clara had moved to the light board, flicking switches. Other lights hummed to life, softening the starkness of the room. “The difference matters.

“Since when are you an expert?”

Clara turned. The light caught her profile, the stubborn set of her jaw that was so like Langston’s. “Since I started paying attention.” She nodded at the clipboard. “She’s got you working on the hard part. The part where the character knows she’s complicit but has to testify anyway. Where the performance is the only confession she gets.

The words hung between them, precise and heavy. Iris looked at her daughter. Really looked. The careful neutrality of her expression, the way her eyes didn’t quite meet Iris’s but didn’t look away either. It wasn’t just resentment. It was assessment.

“You’ve read the script,” Iris said.

“I type the notes. I hear the conversations.” Clara shrugged, a small, defensive motion. “It’s a job.

It was more than a job. Iris felt it suddenly, a prickling along her scalp. The way Clara stood, the specific, unflinching quality of her gaze. It wasn’t the bored detachment of a stage manager. It was the watchfulness of a sentry.

“Clara,” Iris began, but the question wouldn’t form. What would she even ask? Are you part of the buffer, too?

Her daughter glanced toward the door, a quick, habitual check. “You should run the lines. She’ll want to see progress.” And then, softer, almost lost under the hum of the lights: “Don’t think about it so much. Just say the words they gave you. The meaning is in the gaps.

Clara left, the door sighing shut behind her.

Iris stood alone in the suddenly brighter room. The fly was gone. She looked down at the clipboard, at Pauline’s note. The truth is tired.

The rehearsal room was no longer empty. It was full of unnamed things, of hands moving behind curtains, of money on a journey, of a protection she hadn’t asked for. She was the wrapper. The distraction. The marquee.

She took a breath that shuddered on the way in. Then she began to say the lines they gave her, her voice a dry whisper in the quiet, attentive room.
