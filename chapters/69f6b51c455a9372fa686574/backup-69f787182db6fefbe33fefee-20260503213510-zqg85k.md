The cold had a quality to it up here that was different from the cold downtown. It wasn’t the damp chill that seeped from the river; this was a dry, brittle cold that settled in the back of the throat and made the old brick buildings seem to contract against the pale February sky. Iris stood on the opposite sidewalk, looking up at the Institute. It was a former church, just as Langston had said. The stone steps were worn concave in the middle from a century of feet. The stained glass windows were still there, though someone had covered them from the inside with what looked like dark fabric, turning the biblical scenes into opaque, colorful blurs. A simple wooden sign hung beside the heavy oak door: HARLEM INSTITUTE OF DRAMATIC ARTS. EST. 1946.

She’d taken the A train. The rhythmic clatter had been a kind of anesthetic. She’d watched the stations change, the faces in the car shifting from pale to brown, her own reflection a ghost in the dark window. A man across from her had been reading a newspaper folded to the racing forms, his pencil moving down the columns with a tiny, precise tap. She’d focused on that sound, the tap-tap-tap, until it became the metronome for a song that didn’t exist. Now, the silence was absolute. Even the traffic on Lenox Avenue seemed muted, a distant hum.

Her gloved hands were deep in the pockets of her wool coat. In the right pocket, her fingers found the familiar shape of the Zippo. The metal was smooth, cool. She didn’t take it out. She just pressed her thumb against its ridged wheel, a silent, pointless strike. The gesture was twelve years old.

This was a mistake. A profound and possibly final mistake. She could turn around. Walk back to the subway. Tell Langston the position had been filled. Tell Cross she’d been denied entry. There were a hundred lies she could fashion, each one flimsier than the last. He would know. Langston would know. The cold knot under her ribs, which had been her constant companion since the booth at The Blue Parrot, tightened another degree. It was a specific sensation, not quite nausea, not quite pain. A held dissonance.

She crossed the street. Her footsteps on the pavement were too loud. The door had a cast-iron knocker, a lion’s head worn smooth by time. She used it. The sound echoed inside, a hollow, booming report that made her think of a judge’s gavel. For a long moment, nothing. Then the scrape of a bolt, and the door swung inward.

A young woman stood there, perhaps nineteen or twenty. She had a sharp, intelligent face and wore a man’s corduroy shirt with the sleeves rolled up. A pencil was tucked behind her ear. She looked Iris up and down, her expression not hostile, but appraising. Thorough.

“Yes?”

“I’m Iris Finch. I have an appointment with Miss Carter.

The girl’s eyes changed. A flicker of something—recognition, then a swift veiling. “Oh. Right. She’s expecting you. Come in.

Iris stepped over the threshold into a vestibule. The air was different. It smelled of dust and old wood and something else—the faint, sweet tang of rosin. A rehearsal smell. To the left, a staircase curved up into shadow. Ahead, the original church nave opened up, but it was no longer a place of worship. The pews were gone. The space was a vast, open hall. Sunlight streamed in from high, clear windows, catching motes of dust that swirled like slow gold. At the far end, a stage had been constructed, bare except for a few wooden chairs and a battered piano. Someone had painted a flat, a forest scene, and left it leaning against a wall. The paint was still wet in one corner; a drip of green sap color had trailed down to the floorboards.

But her eye caught on the ceiling. The original church ceiling remained, a high, vaulted space of dark beams. And someone, for some reason, had suspended dozens of paper birds from the beams with nearly invisible thread. They were all white, cut from what looked like old manuscript pages, and they turned in the faint thermal currents of the room with a slow, silent flutter. It was beautiful and utterly pointless. A waste of time and paper. Her throat tightened.

“This way,” the girl said. “She’s in the office. Back of the hall.

They walked across the wide floor. The girl’s boots made a solid, confident sound. Iris’s heels clicked, a foreign, too-precise rhythm. From somewhere behind the stage, she heard the sudden, bright run of piano scales, then a curse, then laughter. A deep male voice called out, “Again, from the top of the chorus, and this time, listen to each other!

The office was a former sacristy, a small room with a single tall, narrow window. The door was open. The girl rapped her knuckles on the frame. “Pauline? Miss Finch is here.

Iris’s heart did a thing then. It didn’t race. It seemed to stop entirely, to become a cold, heavy stone in her chest. She had a sudden, vivid memory of the smell of Gauloises cigarettes and cheap wine, of rain on a zinc roof, of a hand brushing a strand of hair from her forehead. The memory had no business here, in this clean, cold light.

Pauline Carter was seated behind a plain wooden desk, her head bent over a ledger. She wore a simple black turtleneck. Her hair, which Iris remembered as a wild, magnificent cloud, was now pulled back into a severe knot at the nape of her neck. She did not look up immediately. She finished writing a line, the scratch of her pen a deliberate, unhurried sound. Then she set the pen down, aligned it perfectly with the edge of the desk blotter, and looked up.

Time did not collapse. It did not fold. It simply emptied out, leaving the two of them in a silent, airless room.

Pauline’s face was older. Of course it was. Fine lines framed her eyes, which were the same deep, unsettling brown, the color of dark earth. But the warmth Iris remembered in them was gone, replaced by a flat, polished neutrality. She looked at Iris as she might look at a piece of furniture that had been delivered to the wrong address.

“Iris,” she said. Her voice was lower than it had been, with a new huskiness. A smoker’s voice. It held no inflection at all.

“Pauline.” The name felt strange in her mouth, a relic she had no right to utter.

Pauline’s gaze shifted to the girl in the doorway. “Thank you, Clara. You can get back to the fly system. The counterweights are still wrong.

Clara. The girl gave a short nod and left, pulling the door almost closed behind her. Not shut. Almost.

The silence stretched. Pauline did not invite her to sit. She leaned back in her chair, the old wood creaking. Her eyes traveled over Iris’s coat, her gloves, her careful, understated hat—the uniform of a downtown woman slumming it uptown. A muscle ticked once, high in Pauline’s jaw.

“Langston Finch’s letter was very persuasive,” Pauline said finally. “A noted performer wishes to contribute her expertise to our little community. A generous offer. One might even call it charitable.

Iris said nothing. The knot under her ribs was a fist.

“We are mounting a production of The Glass Menagerie,” Pauline continued, her tone that of a bored administrator. “The director is a talented young man, but inexperienced with actors who have… professional habits. You will assist him. Mainly with the actress playing Amanda. She has a tendency to indicate. It’s tiresome.

“I understand.”

“Do you.” It wasn’t a question. Pauline opened a drawer and took out two slim volumes. She slid them across the desk. “Your script. And this.” She tapped the second book. It was an older edition of Lillian Hellman’s The Children’s Hour. “Read it. We’ll discuss it next week. Consider it a… baseline text for the atmosphere here.

The cruelty of the choice was so precise, so surgical, that for a moment Iris couldn’t breathe. A play about a lie, about the ruinous accusation of lesbianism in a girls’ school. A play that had been banned in several cities. A play they had seen together in a tiny, smoky theatre in Montmartre, Pauline gripping her hand so tightly it ached afterward, both of them silent and shaken on the walk home.

Iris reached for the books. Her hand did not shake. She willed it not to. “As you like.

Pauline watched her take them. Then she looked down at her ledger again, a clear dismissal. “Rehearsals for Menagerie are weekdays at two. Clara will show you out.

Iris turned. The paper birds in the great hall still turned in their silent, endless circles. As she passed the piano, the same run of scales erupted, this time flawless, a cascade of bright, mocking notes. She did not look back. She walked out into the brittle cold, the two books held against her chest like a shield. The door closed behind her with a solid, final thud. On the top step, partly obscured by a shriveled leaf, was a single white feather. She stared at it. Then she walked down the steps, her heels catching on the worn stone, and did not stop until she reached the corner, where the noise of the city rushed back in to fill the perfect, ringing silence she carried inside.

The girl stepped aside, and there she was.

Pauline stood behind a plain wooden desk, her back to the narrow window. The light from behind her was grey and flat, turning her into a silhouette, but Iris would have known the shape of her anywhere. The set of the shoulders. The way her head was held, as if listening to a distant, difficult piece of music. She was taller than Iris remembered, or perhaps memory had diminished her. She wore a simple black turtleneck and trousers, her hands resting on a stack of papers. She didn’t move.

The silence stretched. It wasn’t an empty silence. It was a dense, loaded thing, humming with the ghosts of a thousand unsaid words. The girl—Clara, Iris would learn her name was Clara—cleared her throat softly and slipped away, her footsteps receding across the hall.

Iris’s mouth was dry. The cold knot under her ribs was a solid, frozen mass now. She could feel the erratic thump of her own pulse in her temples. She should say something. A greeting. An apology. A lie. Nothing came. The air in the small room smelled of ink and the faint, acrid scent of the coal furnace struggling somewhere below.

Pauline moved first. She didn’t come forward. She simply turned her head, just enough for the light to catch the planes of her face. Time had been careful with her. It had sharpened what was soft, etched fine lines at the corners of her eyes and mouth, but it hadn’t softened her. Her gaze found Iris and held it. There was no shock in it. No surprise. Only a deep, weary recognition, and beneath that, a banked fire of something colder.

“Miss Finch.” Her voice was lower than it had been in Paris. Smoother. It had lost the rough, eager edges of youth and gained the controlled resonance of a woman who used her voice as a tool. “You’re punctual.

Two words. They were a dismissal of twelve years. A reduction of memory to a matter of scheduling. Iris felt something hot move through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. She swallowed. “Miss Carter.

Pauline’s eyes traveled over her, a slow, assessing sweep that took in the wool coat, the gloves, the careful makeup. It was the look a director gives a piece of set dressing that doesn’t quite fit the scene. “Langston’s letter was very thorough. A cultural outreach. How civic-minded of you.” She picked up a pencil from the desk and tapped its eraser once, twice, against the wood. A syncopated rhythm. “We’re mounting The Glass Menagerie. You’ll observe rehearsals. Take notes for the actors on vocal delivery. Given your… expertise.

The pause was infinitesimal, but it hung in the air between them, charged.

“I’m not a teacher,” Iris said. Her own voice sounded thin, reedy to her ears.

“No.” Pauline set the pencil down. “You’re not.” She reached into a drawer and pulled out a thin, bound script. She held it out, not coming any closer. “Start with this. Read it. Understand what we’re doing here.

Iris forced her legs to move. Three steps across the worn floorboards. She took the script. The cover was mimeographed, the title stamped in faded purple ink: THE CHILDREN’S HOUR by Lillian Hellman.

A joke. A brutal, pointed joke. A play about lies, about the destruction wrought by a whispered accusation. Her fingers tightened on the paper. The edges dug into her palm.

“I’m familiar with it.”

“I’m sure you are.” Pauline’s gaze didn’t waver. “Familiarity isn’t understanding. Read it again. Then we’ll discuss your role.

From the hall came the sudden, bright clash of a chord on the piano, followed by a burst of collective laughter. The sound was jarring, a splash of normal life against the silent tension in the room. Iris noticed a crack in the plaster above Pauline’s head. It ran from the corner of the window frame up toward the ceiling, a fine, hairline fracture like a vein in marble. She focused on it, a pointless detail, a place to rest her eyes that wasn’t Pauline’s face.

“Is there a schedule?” she asked, her voice barely more than a murmur.

“Clara will provide you with one. Rehearsals are daily. Two until six. We don’t break for latecomers.” Pauline finally moved from behind the desk. She walked to the window, her back to Iris again, and looked out at the bleak afternoon. The space where she had been standing seemed to vibrate. “Your first report to your government friends is due when?

The question landed like a physical blow. Direct. Unflinching. Iris’s breath caught. She hadn’t expected it, not so soon, not with such clean, surgical precision. The hot thing in her chest turned to ice.

“Please.” Pauline didn’t turn around. Her reflection in the dark glass was a smudged, grey impression. “Let’s not waste time with denials. You are here because they sent you. I know it. You know I know it. The only question is what you choose to write down.” She turned then, leaning back against the windowsill. Her expression was unreadable, a mask of polite inquiry. “Will you tell them about the paper birds? They serve no purpose. They’re just… something I wanted to see. A frivolity. Does that sound subversive to you?

Iris couldn’t speak. She could only stare, the script a dead weight in her hand.

“Or perhaps,” Pauline continued, her voice dropping to a conversational tone, “you’ll mention the Port Chicago piece we’re workshopping. The one about the munitions explosion that killed three hundred and twenty Black sailors. The Navy called it an accident. The play suggests it was negligence. Maybe murder. That might be of interest to them. You should probably lead with that. It has the ring of truth they so admire.

She pushed off from the sill and walked back to the desk. She didn’t sit. She just stood there, waiting. The message was clear: the audience was over.

Iris turned. The walk to the door felt like crossing a vast, empty stage. Her heels clicked, a stupid, frivolous sound. She pulled the door open.

“Iris.”

The sound of her name, spoken in that voice after so long, stopped her as surely as a hand on her arm. She didn’t look back.

“The script,” Pauline said. Her voice was quiet now, stripped of its cutting edge. It was just tired. “Read the scene between Martha and Karen. The final one. Tell me what you think it’s really about.

Iris stepped into the hall and closed the door behind her. The echo of the latch clicking into place was final. She stood there, breathing in the dusty, rosin-scented air. From the stage, the piano began again, a slow, bluesy progression she didn’t recognize. She looked down at the script in her hands. The purple ink of the title seemed to bleed at the edges.

Clara was waiting by the forest flat, watching her. Her expression was no longer appraising. It was something closer to pity.

“The schedule’s on the board by the entrance,” she said, not unkindly. “See you tomorrow.

Iris nodded, a stiff, mechanical motion. She walked back across the wide floor, past the silent, turning paper birds. She didn’t look up at them. Outside, the brittle cold hit her face like a slap. She descended the worn stone steps, her legs unsteady. At the bottom, she stopped and leaned against the rough brick wall, closing her eyes. A bus rumbled past on Lenox, its windows glowing yellow in the gathering dusk.

In her pocket, her fingers found the Zippo again. This time, she took it out. She turned it over in her gloved hand. The metal was worn smooth on one side from a habit she’d broken years ago. She didn’t strike it. She just held it, feeling its weight, its dumb, solid reality. Then she put it back. She had a script to read. A report to write. A scene to understand.

The apartment smelled of lemons and stale cigarette smoke. Langston had polished the furniture again, a nervous habit. Iris stood just inside the door, her coat still on, her gloves in her hands. The radiator ticked. She could hear the faint, tinny sound of a radio from the apartment below, a soap opera. A woman was crying with theatrical abandon.

She hung her coat in the closet. Her fingers were stiff. The walk from the subway had been long, the cold drilling deeper. She’d stopped at a drugstore on Broadway and bought a new notebook, a cheap thing with a green cardboard cover. It felt like a prop. Now it sat on the hall table, innocent and damning.

Langston was in the living room. She could see the top of his head over the back of the wingchair. He was reading the paper, or pretending to. He’d heard her come in. He always did.

“How was it?” he called out, his voice carefully casual.

She didn’t answer right away. She went to the kitchenette, filled a kettle at the sink. The faucet had a drip; it left a dark, rusty stain on the porcelain. She watched a single drop form, swell, fall. The rhythm was irregular. She’d meant to fix it last year.

“It was fine,” she said finally. The words came out flat, a monotone.

“Fine?” He appeared in the doorway, the newspaper folded under his arm. He’d changed into his smoking jacket, a maroon thing she’d always hated. “Just fine? What’s she like? Carter?

Iris lit a burner on the stove. The blue flame hissed to life. “Formidable. As you’d expect.

“And the place?”

“A converted church. They’ve hung paper birds from the ceiling.” She turned to look at him. “Why would anyone do that?

He blinked, thrown by the question. “I don’t know. Art, I suppose.

“It’s a waste.” The kettle began to hum. She took down a cup, the one with the chipped handle. The tea was in a tin on the shelf, a blend he liked. She measured a spoonful into the strainer. Her hands performed the ritual without her. “She’s having me assist with The Glass Menagerie. Student production. She also gave me The Children’s Hour to read. For context.

Langston was quiet for a moment. “The Children’s Hour. That’s the one about the teachers, the accusation.

“Yes.”

“Interesting choice.”

“Isn’t it.”

He leaned against the doorframe. She could feel his gaze on her back, a physical pressure. “Darling. You’re being very cryptic. Did something happen?

Something happened. The air in the room had changed composition. The past had walked into the present wearing a corduroy shirt and a look of pure, polished ice. Her life had split into a before and an after, right there on the worn floorboards of a Harlem church. But she couldn’t say that. The words weren’t available to her. They were locked behind a door she’d sealed twelve years ago.

“Nothing happened,” she said. “It was a first meeting. She was professional. Distant.” She poured the boiling water. The steam bloomed up, warm and fragrant. It fogged the window over the sink. “I start properly on Thursday.

“Good. That’s good.” He sounded relieved. He came closer, put a hand on her shoulder. His touch was light, proprietary. “And Cross? When do you see him?

“I don’t. I write a report. He gave me a post office box number.” She shrugged his hand off, gently, under the pretense of reaching for the milk. “It’s all very civil.

“It’s for the best. Keep it orderly. Impersonal.

Impersonal. She stirred the tea, watching the liquid darken. She could write an impersonal report. She could describe the dimensions of the hall, the estimated number of students, the play selections. She could omit the paper birds. She could omit the way the light had caught the dust motes, turning them into a slow, golden snowfall. She could omit the exact timbre of Pauline’s voice, lower now, worn at the edges like stone steps. She could omit the memory of rain on a zinc roof.

She could do that.

She took her tea and the green notebook into the bedroom. Closed the door. The room was neat, sterile. The bed was made with military precision, the pillows plumped. A single lamp on the nightstand cast a small pool of light. She sat at her dressing table, moved a silver hairbrush an inch to the left to make space. The mirror showed her a pale face, her features blurred by the dim light. She looked like a stranger waiting in a green room.

She opened the notebook. The first page was blank, a field of cheap, lined paper. She took her fountain pen from the drawer. It was a good pen, a gift from Langston after her first hit. For the words that will come, he’d said. The words had stopped coming in 1946.

She unscrewed the cap. The smell of ink was sharp, mineral.

Report: First Visit. February 14, 1949.

The date was wrong. It was the 15th. She stared at the error. A simple mistake. She didn’t cross it out. Let it stand.

The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts is housed in a former church on 135th Street. The building is in adequate repair. The interior has been converted to a single large rehearsal hall with a rudimentary stage. Lighting is natural during the day, from high windows. There is a piano.

Her handwriting was steady, a performer’s script. Each letter formed with care.

The director, Pauline Carter, appears to be in her late thirties. She runs the institute with a disciplined approach. The current production is Tennessee Williams’s “The Glass Menagerie,” with a student cast. Miss Carter has assigned me to assist with blocking and vocal coaching for this production. She also suggested I read Lillian Hellman’s “The Children’s Hour” to understand the thematic concerns of modern drama.

She paused. The pen nib hovered over the paper. A drop of ink gathered, a tiny black planet. She lowered the pen, absorbed it into the downstroke of the ‘h’ in ‘thematic.

The student body appears to be approximately twenty individuals, predominantly Negro. Morale seems adequate. There were no overt political discussions observed during my visit. The focus was on theatrical technique.

A lie. The focus had been on the silent, turning birds. On the green drip of paint. On the space between two women where twelve years hung, invisible and thick as velvet rope.

No further observations at this time.

She signed it: I. Finch.

It was done. Three paragraphs. A sterile, harmless thing. A nothing-burger, as the radio comedians said. It could have been written by a tourist. She read it over. The prose was dead. It had no rhythm, no melody. It was the opposite of a song.

She closed the notebook. The green cover was already curling at the corners.

From the living room, the radio soap opera reached a crescendo. A door slammed. A man shouted. Then a commercial jingle for laundry soap, bright and false. Langston laughed at something.

Iris put the notebook in the top drawer of the dressing table, under a stack of silk scarves she never wore. She looked at her reflection again. The woman in the glass looked back, her eyes dark holes. A phrase surfaced, unbidden, from a long-ago conversation in a Paris garret. The truth doesn’t care if you speak it, Pauline had said, tracing a pattern on Iris’s bare knee. It just waits. It has all the time in the world.

The truth was waiting now. It was sitting in a drawer under silk scarves. It was hanging from a church ceiling in the shape of paper birds. It was walking the streets of Harlem in a corduroy shirt.

Iris finished her tea. It had gone cold.
