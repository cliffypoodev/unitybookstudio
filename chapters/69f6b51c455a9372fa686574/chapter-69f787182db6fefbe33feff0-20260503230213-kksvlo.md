The light in the main hall was the color of weak tea, thick with dust motes that swirled in the slants from the high windows. Iris stood just inside the double doors, her fingers tracing the seam of her notebook. The air smelled of old wood, floor polish, and something else—a faint, sweetish odor of mildew from the velvet curtains stacked against the far wall. A grand piano, its lid closed like a coffin lid, occupied a corner. On the stage, a circle of mismatched chairs faced two wooden ones placed in the center. The empty space hummed, a low, expectant note.

Pauline was already there, perched on the edge of the stage, her legs swinging slightly. She held a script, her thumb moving in a slow, rhythmic tap against its spine. She didn’t look up. “You’re late.”

“The train was—”

“Sit.” Pauline gestured with her chin to the circle of chairs. Three young people were already there, watching with the careful neutrality of students who understood a storm was brewing. Iris recognized them from around the institute: a tall young man with a serious face, a girl with her hair in tight, neat plaits, and another young woman who chewed on the end of her pencil. Iris took a chair. The wood creaked. She set her notebook on her lap, a useless shield.

“We’re reading the Children’s Hour today,” Pauline said, her voice carrying in the hollow room. “Lillian Hellman. You’ve all read it. We’re looking at Act Three. Martha’s confession.” She finally lifted her gaze, and it landed on Iris like a physical weight. “Finch. You’ll read Martha.”

A cold knot tightened in Iris’s stomach. She knew the play. Everyone knew the play. A lie about two teachers, a life destroyed by the suggestion of something unspeakable. Martha, admitting it was true. The script was a grenade, and Pauline had just pulled the pin. “I’m not an actress.”

“You’re a performer. Same muscles. Different application.” Pauline stood, walking to the center of the stage. She dropped the script into one of the wooden chairs. “Come up. James, you’ll read Karen. The rest, observe.”

The young man—James—nodded, picking up his own script. He had a soft, careful voice. Iris’s legs felt unsteady as she climbed the three steps to the stage. The boards groaned under her weight. The script in the chair was open to the page. The lines swam for a second, a blur of black type. She picked it up. The paper was cool.

“From the top of the scene,” Pauline said. She leaned against the piano, arms crossed. “No staging. Just the words. Find the truth in them.”

Iris cleared her throat. It was dry. She began, her voice coming out thin, reedy, the careful enunciation of a supper club singer trying to project. “I’ve thought about it for a long time. I’ve thought about all of it. It’s been a long time.”

“Stop.” Pauline didn’t move. “You’re singing it. This isn’t a ballad. It’s a suicide note. Try again. From ‘I’ve thought about it.’”

Iris took a breath. She tried to roughen the edges of her voice, to push some air into it. “I’ve thought about it for a long time.”

“No. You’re thinking about how you sound. Think about what you’re saying. You have loved your friend, your colleague, for years. The world has just told you that love is a monstrous lie. And you are about to agree with them. What does that do to a voice?”

The question hung in the dusty air. Iris stared at the page. The words were a minefield. I have loved you the way they said. She felt a prickling heat at the back of her neck. This wasn’t criticism. It was an autopsy. She tried again, forcing the words out. “It’s funny. It’s all so funny. I used to tell myself that it wasn’t true.”

Her voice faltered. The next line was a cliff. I used to tell myself we were just good friends. The silence stretched. James waited, his script lowered. In the corner of her eye, Iris saw the girl with the plaits studying her own fingernails, a tiny, merciful distraction.

“The line is ‘I used to tell myself we were just good friends,’” Pauline said, her own voice flat. “Why can’t you say it?”

“I know the line.”

“Then say it. Unless it’s too close to something else.”

The air left the room. The dust motes seemed to freeze in their amber light. Iris’s mouth was a desert. She could feel the students’ attention now, sharp and uncomfortable. They knew. They might not know the specifics, but they knew this was about more than a bad read. She looked at Pauline. Her face was a mask of weary patience, but her eyes were chips of flint.

“Louder. She’s confessing. She wants to be heard.”

“I used to tell myself we were just good friends!” The shout echoed off the rafters, ugly and raw. Something cracked open in her chest—not anger, not shame, a sour mix of both that made her throat ache. She gripped the script, the paper crumpling in her fist. “But it wasn’t true. It wasn’t true at all.”

She rushed through the rest, a frantic, stumbling torrent. The terrible, wrenching confession. The plea for forgiveness. The final, quiet devastation. When she finished, the last word—goodbye—dropped into a silence so complete she could hear the faint tick of a pipe somewhere in the walls.

Pauline pushed off the piano. She walked to the center of the stage, stopping a few feet away. She didn’t look at the script. “You played the emotion. The big, tragic feeling. That’s not what the scene is. The scene is about the failure of language. The words are inadequate. They’re the wrong words for what she feels. The pain is in the gap between what she means and what she can possibly say.” She tilted her head. “You, of all people should understand that.”

The dismissal was absolute. Iris stood there, the crumpled script a dead weight in her hand. The students were gathering their things, the lesson clearly over. James gave her a small, sympathetic nod before slipping out. The girl with the plaits followed, then the other. Their footsteps faded down the hall.

Iris didn’t move. The dusty light had shifted, climbing higher on the opposite wall, leaving the stage in deeper shadow. Pauline had returned to her perch on the stage edge, lighting a cigarette. The match flared, a brief, sulfurous bloom in the gloom.

“You did that on purpose,” Iris said. The words shifted, strange to her own ears, scraped hollow.

“I did my job.” Pauline exhaled a plume of smoke. “You’re here to learn. That was the lesson.”

“It was cruelty.”

“It was text.” Pauline tapped ash onto the floor. “The text doesn’t care about your feelings. It only cares about truth. If you can’t separate the two, you’re useless here.”

Iris looked down at the script. The crumpled page. A line caught her eye, one she’d raced past. It was a kind of sickness. The playwright’s words, not Martha’s. A stage direction, an interpretation. A code. The playwright provides the cipher. Hellman wrote the lie the world would believe, and in doing so gave the actor the exact shape of the real pain to fill it with. The script wasn’t a mirror. It was a blueprint for a specific kind of wound.

She let the script fall onto the wooden chair. It landed with a soft whisper. Her hands were trembling. She pressed them flat against her skirt.

“I need some air,” she said, to no one.

Pauline just smoked, staring at the empty chairs across from her. Iris walked off the stage, her footsteps too loud in the empty hall. The double doors groaned as she pushed through them into the corridor. The air was cooler there, smelling of cabbage and disinfectant from a distant kitchen. She leaned against the wall, the plaster cool through her blouse. A framed photograph hung crookedly beside her—a production shot from some forgotten play, the faces smiling, frozen in a moment of applause that had died decades ago. One of the actors had a smudge of dark makeup under his eye, a tiny flaw in the perfect tableau.

She closed her eyes. The words echoed. I used to tell myself. Not Pauline’s voice. Her own. From a different time, a different city. A different kind of lie.

When she opened them, the corridor was empty. The only sound was the distant, rhythmic squeak of a janitor’s mop bucket wheel from around a corner. A mundane, lonely sound. She pushed away from the wall and walked toward the exit, not sure where she was going, only that she had to move. The lesson, it seemed was not over. It was just beginning to settle in her bones.

The exit door led not to the street, but to a narrow, tiled hallway that smelled of bleach and wet wool. A row of metal lockers stood against one wall, their green paint chipped and bubbled. Iris leaned her back against the cool tiles, the rough grout lines pressing into her spine through her blouse. Her breath came in shallow hitches. She focused on a crack in the linoleum floor, a jagged black line that forked like a lightning bolt under the third locker. It was just a crack. It meant nothing. It didn’t care about Hellman or confessions or the way Pauline’s voice could turn a stage direction into an indictment.

The squeak of the mop bucket wheel faded. Silence pooled in the corridor, thick and granular. She could still feel the script in her hands, the phantom weight of it. The words had left a residue, a bitter paste on her tongue. I have loved you the way they said. A code. That was the insight, slippery and half-formed. The playwright provided the cipher. The actor brought the lived pain to make it real. But what happened when the pain wasn’t performed? When it was just… there, a stone in your shoe you’d walked on for twelve years?

She pushed off the wall. Her legs held. The hallway ended at a heavy fire door with a push-bar. She shoved it open, emerging into a small, brick-paved courtyard tucked behind the institute. The afternoon light was grey and thin, the sky the color of a dirty pigeon. A single, spindly tree grew from a square of dirt, its bare branches like nerve endings against the building’s red brick. The air was colder here, sharp with the promise of a later rain.

She lit a cigarette. The first drag was a chemical burn, familiar and anchoring. She leaned against the bricks, watching the smoke dissolve into the still air. From an open window above, a piano started up—scales, clumsy and repetitive, a student practicing. The notes were a child’s earnest plodding, C-D-E-F-G, over and over. There was a comfort in its monotony. No subtext. No hidden meaning. Just the next note, and the next.

The courtyard door opened it. James, the young man who had read Karen, stepped out. He had a packet of cigarettes of his own, tapping one free. He saw her and gave a small, awkward nod.

“Miss Finch.”

“James.” The words came out hoarse. She took another drag.

He lit his cigarette, cupping the match against a non-existent breeze. He was tall, with a quiet, watchful face. He smoked for a moment, looking at the tree. “That was… intense,” he said finally.

Iris gave a short, dry sound that wasn’t a laugh. “You could say that.”

“She does that. Picks the thing that’ll get under your skin. To see what’s there.” He glanced at her. “It’s not personal.”

“It felt personal.”

“Yeah.” He acknowledged the truth of it without argument. “It always does. But it’s about the work. She says the best tool an actor has is a truth they’re afraid of. Makes the fiction real.”

“And if the truth ruins the fiction?” Iris asked, the question out before she could stop it.

James considered. The piano scales above stumbled started again. “Then maybe it wasn’t fiction to begin with.” He took a last drag and dropped the cigarette, grinding it under his heel. “She wants to see you. In her office. Before you go.”

The stone in her shoe became a boulder. “Now?”

“That’s what she said.” He offered another tentative nod and went back inside, leaving her with the cold and the clumsy scales.

Pauline’s office was on the second floor, at the end of a hall where the carpet was worn down to the threads in a narrow path. The door was ajar. Iris knocked once on the frame.

“Come in.”

The office was small dominated by a massive, scarred oak desk buried under leaning towers of books and manuscripts. A gooseneck lamp cast a yellow circle of light on a blotter stained with ink and coffee rings. The walls were bare except for a single framed poster for the Federal Theatre Project’s Macbeth—the Harlem version, from years ago. The air smelled of tobacco and the faint, sweet dust of aging paper.

Pauline sat behind the desk reading a typescript. She didn’t look up. Iris stood just inside the door, her hands clasped in front of her like a supplicant. The silence stretched, filled only by the soft rustle of a page turning.

“Close the door,” Pauline said, still reading.

Iris did. The click of the latch was loud.

Finally, Pauline set the pages down. She removed her glasses, pinching the bridge of her nose. In the lamplight, she looked tired. The fine lines around her eyes were more pronounced. “Sit.”

There was only one other chair, a wooden straight-back piled with books. Iris moved them carefully to the floor—a biography of Eugene O’Neill, a bound copy of the Communist Manifesto, a French dictionary—and sat. The chair was hard.

“Your report,” Pauline said, her voice flat. “To your government friends. What will it say?”

The question was so direct, so devoid of preamble, that for a moment Iris just stared. Her mouth opened, but no sound came out. She’d prepared for more criticism, for another lesson in theatrical cruelty. Not this.

“I don’t—”

“Don’t.” Pauline leaned forward, the chair creaking. Her eyes were dark, unblinking. “We’re past that. You’re here because they sent you. We both know it. The only question is what you tell them. So. What will your report say about today?”

A hot flush crawled up Iris’s neck. The rehearsed lies, the sterile phrases Cross had suggested turned to ash. “I haven’t… I haven’t written anything yet.”

“But you will.” It wasn’t a question. “They’ll expect something. So give me the headline. The summary. What did you observe at the Harlem Institute today?”

Iris’s thumb found the seam of her skirt, tracing it. A nervous habit. She made herself stop. “A reading. Of Hellman.”

“And?”

“And it was… Difficult.”

Pauline’s smile was a thin, bitter curve. “‘Difficult.’ Good. Vague. Non-committal. They’ll like that. It suggests something to be investigated without stating it.” She picked up a pencil began tapping its eraser against the desk in a slow, syncopated rhythm. “But they’ll want names. Did anyone say anything subversive? Did the discussion turn to politics? To… un-American themes?”

The tapping was like a metronome, measuring her guilt. “No,” Iris whispered.

“Louder.”

“No. The discussion was about the text. About performance.”

“Truth in performance,” Pauline corrected. She stopped tapping. “That’s the subversion, Iris. Not the politics of the play. The act of truth-telling itself. That’s what they’re afraid of. That’s what you’re here to monitor.” She set the pencil down with a precise click. “So here is what you will report. You will say the institute is focused on classic American drama. That the students are earnest but untrained. That the director is rigorous, perhaps overly academic. That you saw nothing of concern.” She paused. “You will also say that Pauline Carter is difficult, uncooperative, and shows no particular interest in you or your opinions. That she is a disciplinarian, not a radical. Can you remember that?”

Iris felt a dizzying disconnect. This was a script, but she hadn’t been given her lines until now. “Why are you telling me what to say?”

“Because I need you to stay.” Pauline’s gaze didn’t waver. “And you will only stay if you give them harmless, useless information. If you report anything of actual value, Cross will pull you out. Or worse, he’ll send someone less… conflicted. I can work with a conflicted informant. I can’t work with a good one.”

The admission hung between them, stark and terrifying. I can work with a conflicted informant. It wasn’t forgiveness. It was a tactical assessment. A cold, practical use of her position.

“You knew,” Iris said, the words barely audible. “From the beginning.”

“I suspected. Your arrival was too neat. Your husband’s influence too convenient. And then you walked in, looking like you’d seen a ghost.” Pauline leaned back, the shadows of the room gathering around her. “The past is a luxury, Iris. I don’t have time for it. But your present… Your present is a tool. We’re going to use it.”

“To do what?”

“To put on a play.” Pauline gestured to the typescript on her desk. “A new one. It is about the Port Chicago disaster. You’re going to help me. You’re going to coach the actors. You’re going to be my… musical consultant. And in return, you will feed Cross the blandest, most tedious reports imaginable.”

It was a bargain. A monstrous, pragmatic bargain. No mention of Paris. No mention of the letters. No mention of the way Martha’s confession had felt like a stripping of bone. Just a transaction. Her presence for her silence.

“What if I refuse?” Iris asked, though she already knew the answer.

Pauline’s expression didn’t change. “Then you walk out. You tell Cross whatever you like. And you spend the rest of your life wondering what we built here without you.” She picked up the typescript again, a clear dismissal. “The rehearsal for the Port Chicago piece is Thursday. Seven PM. Be early.”

Iris stood. Her knees were weak. She turned to the door, her hand on the cold brass knob.

“Iris.”

She stopped didn’t look back.

“The truth you’re afraid of,” Pauline said, her voice softer now, almost lost in the rustle of paper. “That’s the only thing you have left that’s any good. Don’t waste it on them.”

Iris stepped into the hall, pulling the door shut behind her. The worn carpet path stretched toward the stairs. The building was quiet now, the piano silent. She walked slowly, each step an effort. The meeting had lasted ten minutes. It had changed everything. She wasn’t a spy, not really. She was an asset turned and now being used by the very target she was meant to betray. The irony was a cold metallic taste in her mouth.

At the end of the hall, a window looked out over the courtyard. The spindly tree was a dark scribble against the gathering dusk. She watched a single, brown leaf detach and spiral down, a slow, pointless descent. It meant nothing. It was just a leaf. And she was just a woman in a hallway, holding a new script she didn’t yet know how to read.

The single work light cast a hard, white circle on the stage floor, leaving the rest of the hall in a deep, velvety gloom. The students had gone, their chairs pushed back into crooked lines. The silence was different now. Not the tense, listening quiet of the rehearsal, but a hollow, exhausted stillness. It had a texture—like the air inside a bell after it had been struck.

Iris stood at the edge of the light. Her script lay on the wooden chair where she’d dropped it, pages fanned like a dead bird. She should leave. Go back to the apartment. Pour a drink and listen to Langston talk in circles about exposure and optics. The thought was a physical weight in her shoes, pinning her to the dusty boards.

Instead, she walked into the light.

The circle was warmer than she expected. The bulb hummed, a low, steady note. She looked out at the empty chairs. From here, the hall seemed vast, a dark mouth waiting to be fed. This was where Pauline stood. This was the angle she saw. The power of it was a cold shock. You could say anything from here. The dark would swallow it, or throw it back.

She picked up the script. The crumpled page where Martha’s confession lay was soft at the edges from her grip. I have loved you the way they said. The words didn’t swim now. They sat there, black and final. A code.

That was the thing. Hellman hadn’t written a play about lesbians. She’d written a play about a lie. The lie was the engine. The love was just the fuel it burned. You could perform the love—the ache, the desperation—and the audience would feel a tragedy. But if you performed the lie, the machinery of it, the cold gears of the accusation… that was something else. That was the truth they couldn’t say out loud. The play was the cipher. Martha’s pain was the key.

Pauline knew that. Of course she knew. This wasn’t just cruelty. It was a lesson. A brutal, unforgiving lesson in encryption.

Iris’s throat tightened. Not with tears. With a kind of furious clarity. She had spent a lifetime singing other people’s coded messages—torch songs about lost men that could be about anyone, wartime ballads about waiting that could mean anything. She’d built a career on the safe, profitable distance between the lyric and the life. She’d thought she was being clever. She’d just been a better lockpick than most.

This was different. This was standing in the light and handing the audience the lock, the pick, and the blueprint, all while smiling and singing the national anthem. The risk was absolute. The art was in the audacity.

A door clicked shut somewhere in the building’s depths. The sound was small, definitive. Footsteps echoed in a corridor, then faded. She was truly alone.

She let the script fall back to the chair. Her hands felt empty. She flexed her fingers, studying the lines of her palms in the stark light. A childhood memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: her mother, standing at a kitchen sink, peeling potatoes with a paring knife. The long, continuous curl of skin, pale and damp, coiling into the white enamel bowl. The quiet methodical sound of the blade against the vegetable. A task that served nothing but the next meal. It had bored her to agony, watching. Now, the memory felt like an artifact from a lost civilization. A world where danger was a dull knife, and the only thing you had to hide was your boredom.

The back of her neck prickled. She turned.

Pauline stood in the shadows just beyond the edge of the light, leaning against the proscenium arch. How long had she been there? She held a cigarette, the tip a dull orange eye in the dark. She didn’t speak.

“You left your script,” Iris said. She pulled the words back, strange in the empty hall, too bright.

“I came back for it.” Pauline took a drag, the ember flaring. She didn’t move.

“It’s a good play.”

“It’s a weapon.”

“Same thing.”

Pauline exhaled, a slow stream of smoke that drifted into the light and dissolved. “You finally heard it.”

“I heard you.”

A faint, almost imperceptible shift in the darkness. A tilt of the head. “What did you hear?”

Iris looked down at the script, then out at the waiting dark. “That it doesn’t matter what the words are. It matters what room you say them in. And who you say them for.”

Silence. Then the soft sound of Pauline pushing off from the wall. She stepped into the fringe of the light, just enough to see the sharp line of her cheekbone, the set of her mouth. She studied Iris with a detached, analytical focus. “My office. Tomorrow. Ten o’clock. We start work on the new piece.”

“What new piece?”

“The one you’re going to help me write.” Pauline dropped the cigarette to the floor, grinding it out with the toe of her shoe. A final, decisive motion. “Don’t be late.”

She turned and walked away, her footsteps echoing until they were swallowed by the dark at the back of the hall. A moment later, the distant sound of a door opening and closing.

The instruction, a hook set deep. We start work. Not you will help me. You’re going to. A presumption of compliance that was its own kind of trap. Or an offer. It was impossible to tell the difference.

Iris became aware of a pain in her hand. She’d been clenching her fist, her nails biting half-moons into her palm. She relaxed her fingers, watching the white impressions fade to red. A physical record of the moment, temporary and telling.

She left the stage, crossing through the pool of light and into the cool dark of the hall. The grand piano was a hulking shape. She trailed her fingers along its closed lid as she passed, feeling the layer of fine dust. At the double doors, she stopped and looked back. The stage was empty again. Just a chair, a script, a circle of light. A perfect, silent diorama of a choice not yet made.

The night air outside was a slap—cold and smelling of distant rain and city grit. She walked to the subway, her mind not on the script, or Pauline, or Cross, but on the memory of that potato peel, curling, endless, into a white bowl. A thing of no consequence. A detail that meant nothing to anyone alive. It felt, somehow, like the most important thing she’d remembered in years.
