Chapter 24

The apartment smelled of Arthur’s cigar, a sweet, cloying scent that clung to the velvet drapes. It was the smell of transactions, of deals made in quiet rooms. Iris stood in the center of the living room, her fingers worrying the edge of the cheap playbill in her coat pocket. The paper had gone soft from handling. She had read it a dozen times on the subway ride home, the mimeographed ink smudging under her thumb. Workshop Presentation: ‘Port Chicago’. Pauline’s name was listed as director. Her own name was not there. It didn’t matter. The fact of its existence in her pocket did.

Arthur was in his study. She could hear the soft click of his lighter, the rustle of paper. He liked to work late, surrounded by the artifacts of other people’s lives. She walked to the doorway and leaned against the frame. He didn’t look up from his desk, which was a landscape of manila folders and carbon paper. A green glass ashtray held the smoldering end of his cigar. The desk lamp cast a perfect yellow circle on the blotter, leaving the rest of the room in a deep, velvety gloom. One wall was nothing but bookshelves, but they weren’t filled with books. They held box files, each labeled in his neat, vertical script. Years. Names.

“You’re back late,” he said, still not looking up. He made a notation in the margin of a contract. His fountain pen scratched. “How was the institute? Another thrilling day molding young minds?”

He said it with the usual blend of amusement and dismissal. It used to make her want to break something. Now it just felt like a distant radio broadcast, a play she’d memorized. She watched his hands. They were clean, the nails perfectly trimmed. A banker’s hands. A warden’s hands.

“I need the file, Arthur.”

The scratching stopped. He placed the pen down carefully, parallel to the edge of the desk. He finally looked at her. His expression was one of mild curiosity, the look he gave a waiter who’d brought the wrong wine. “Which file would that be, darling? You have so many. Tax returns. Royalty statements. The lease.”

“The insurance file.”

The words sat between them. The radiator knocked twice, a hollow sound. Outside, a car backfired, and for a wild second Iris thought it was a gunshot. Her heart did a stupid, painful leap against her ribs. Arthur’s face didn’t change. He leaned back in his leather chair, the springs groaning under his weight. He steepled his fingers.

“That’s not a thing you ask for,” he said. He spoke — quiet, reasonable. “That’s the point of insurance. You don’t get to hold the policy. The company holds it. For your protection.”

“It’s my life.”

“It’s our livelihood. There’s a distinction.” He picked up his cigar, examined the ash. “A critical one. Why this sudden interest in actuarial documents? Has our friend from the Committee been asking pointed questions? I told him you were cooperating. That you were settled at the institute, making connections. He was pleased.”

A cold knot tightened in her stomach. It wasn’t fear. It was colder than fear, more solid. A lump of river ice. “I want it. All of it. The letters. The photographs. Everything you’ve collected.”

He took a long pull on the cigar, let the smoke drift from his nostrils. He looked like a dragon in a tailored suit. “You’re upset. It’s been a long few months. The change of pace, the artistic stimulation. It’s stirred up old ghosts. I understand.” He waved the cigar, a dismissive gesture. “Go to bed. Have a drink. We’ll forget this conversation.”

She didn’t move. The playbill corner was wearing through in her pocket. She could feel the individual fibers giving way. “I’m not asking.”

Arthur’s eyes hardened. The pleasant mask slipped, just for an instant, and she saw the calculation beneath. The constant, ruthless arithmetic. “You are. And the answer is no. That file is the only thing keeping you out of a congressional hearing room, my dear. It’s the only thing keeping us solvent. Without it, you are a middle-aged chanteuse with a suspicious past and a fading voice. With it, you are a valuable asset with a manageable past. I am not in the habit of handing control of assets to the assets themselves. It’s poor business.”

“It’s blackmail.”

“It’s contingency planning.” He corrected her without heat. “A necessary evil in an evil world. You, of all people should understand the performance of necessity.”

The air in the room grew thicker. She could taste the cigar smoke on the back of her tongue, a foul, sweet coating. She noticed a flaw in the plaster of the ceiling above his head, a hairline crack that forked like a lightning bolt. It had probably been there for years. She’d never seen it before.

“What’s in Paris, Arthur?” The question came out flat. “The ’37 correspondence. What exactly did you keep?”

A flicker in his eyes. Surprise, then swift reassessment. He hadn’t expected her to be specific. He’d thought she was operating from a general sentimental dread. “The usual youthful indiscretions. Bad poetry. Worse politics. Photographs that suggest a certain bohemian flexibility. Nothing a jury would convict on. But enough for the papers. Enough for the committee to make an example. You know how they love a parable. The fallen songbird, seduced by radical ideas and unnatural passions.” He tapped the ash from his cigar. “It’s a compelling narrative. Clean. Moral. They’d eat it up.”

“There’s more than that.” She took a step into the room. The Persian rug was soft under her heels. “You have more than love letters and snapshots. You have the other correspondence. The wartime stuff.”

His stillness was absolute. The only movement was the slow curl of smoke from the cigar in his hand. “What wartime stuff?”

“Don’t.” She spoke low, a scrape of sound. “I remember now. I remember what I did. The messages. The codes in the set lists. The people who came backstage. You have records of that. You kept them.”

He was silent for a long moment. Then he gave a slow, almost imperceptible nod. “I kept everything. A good manager does. One never knows when context might become currency.”

The admission should have felt like a blow. It was a relief. The hidden thing was named. It was out in the yellow lamplight between them. “So it’s not just about my reputation. It’s about prison.”

“It’s about options,” he corrected. “Your options are: continue your charming little teaching performance, provide the Committee with harmless, curated information about your radical colleagues, and live a comfortable, quiet life. Or become a cause célèbre in a federal conspiracy trial. The choice is simple. It has always been simple.” He leaned forward, the chair creaking. “This is not a game of truth, Iris. It is a game of survival. I have kept you alive. I have kept you in silk stockings and gin. Show a little gratitude.”

She thought of the playbill in her pocket. She thought of Pauline’s face in the dark office. You didn’t disappoint me. The memory was a physical ache, a bruise on her heart.

“I want the file,” she said again. Her hands were steady. She was glad for that.

Arthur sighed, a sound of deep exasperation. He opened the top drawer of his desk. For a wild, soaring moment, Iris thought he was going to take it out, hand it to her. He removed a single sheet of paper. He slid it across the polished wood toward her.

“This is your future,” Arthur said softly. “A managed comeback. Respectable. Unobjectionable. The Canary, a symbol of American resilience. The file is what guarantees that future. Without the leverage, Strauss has no incentive. The Committee has no patience. You have nothing.” He paused, letting the words settle. “Ask me again, and I make a phone call. The institute receives an anonymous tip about your deeper history. Pauline Carter’s pet project becomes toxic. Her funding evaporates. Her reputation is stained by association. Is that what you want? To burn down the last good thing you touched?”

The ice in her stomach melted, flooding her with a nausea so sudden she had to brace a hand on the doorframe. Her mouth filled with a metallic taste. He hadn’t threatened her. He’d threatened Pauline. He’d known exactly where to press.

He saw it in her face. The mask of benign concern returned. “You see? It’s for the best. We protect each other. That’s the arrangement.” He stood up and came around the desk. He stopped a few feet from her, close enough that she could see the fine weave of his suit, the faint trace of powder on his chin. He didn’t touch her. “Go to bed, Iris. You’re tired. We’ll talk about the Strauss offer in the morning. It’s a good deal. You’ll like the numbers.”

She looked past him, at the wall of box files. Her life, sorted and catalogued. A museum of her own compromises. She felt a terrible, clear exhaustion, the kind that follows a fever.

She turned and walked out of the study down the hall to her bedroom. She closed the door but didn’t lock it. The lock had never worked. She stood in the dark, listening to the faint sounds of him moving in his study. The scrape of a drawer closing. The click of the lighter.

She took the playbill from her pocket and smoothed it out on the dresser. She couldn’t read the words in the dark, but she didn’t need to. She knew what it represented. A door. One he had just shown her was locked from the outside.

For a long time, she just stood there, her hands flat on the dresser top. The streetlight outside cast the faint, elongated shadow of the window frame across the floor. It looked like the bars of a cage, laid down in stripes of weak, yellow light.

The stage was empty. A single work light burned at the foot of the proscenium, casting a stark, lonely circle on the worn boards. The rest of the theatre hung in a vast, velvet dark. Iris stood in the wings, the smell of dust and old paint thick in her throat. She’d come straight from the apartment, the playbill a damp square in her fist. She didn’t know if Pauline would be here. It was past midnight. But the side door had been unlocked.

She stepped into the light. Her footsteps echoed, a hollow sound that seemed to climb into the flies and get lost. The set for Port Chicago was skeletal—a few platforms, a suggestion of a ship’s railing, some crates painted a dull gray. It looked like the bones of a shipwreck. She ran a hand along a splintered edge of one crate. The wood was cool, rough. A fleck of paint came away under her nail, the color of ash.

“You’re supposed to be memorizing blocking, not vandalizing the set.”

Pauline’s voice came from the shadows at the back of the house. Iris’s heart gave a single, hard knock against her ribs. She hadn’t heard her come in. She turned, squinting past the blinding work light. She could just make out a shape in the center aisle, sitting in a seat about ten rows back.

“The door was open.”

“It’s always open,” Pauline said. The words came out flat, tired. “For someone.”

Iris walked to the edge of the stage. The drop was about four feet. She sat, her legs dangling over the side. The darkness between them felt like a physical substance. She could hear a faint, steady drip from somewhere backstage—a leaky faucet maybe. It marked time.

“Arthur has a file,” she said. The words fell into the quiet and were held there. She tried again. “Not just letters. Not just photographs. He has everything. From Paris. The names. The places we went. The things we said. He’s cataloged it. Dated it.”

Silence from the house. The drip kept its rhythm.

“He showed you?”

“He told me. He didn’t have to show me. I saw his face.” Iris pressed her palms into the rough wood of the stage. A tiny splinter caught in her skin. She didn’t pull it out. “It’s not insurance. It’s a ledger. He knows what everything meant. The code. He knows what the songs were for.”

A seat creaked. Pauline shifted. “And you’re telling me this why?”

The question was a blade. Clean, sharp. Iris had expected anger maybe. Or fear. Not this cold, surgical curiosity. It left her exposed, dangling over the edge.

“Because I have to tell someone.” She lowered her voice on the last word. It sounded pathetic. She hated it. “Because if I don’t say it out loud, to you, it stays his. A thing in a box. A transaction. I need it to be real. I need you to know what he has.”

“I already know what he has.” Pauline’s voice was closer. She had moved forward maybe to the edge of the stage light’s spill. Iris could see the faint outline of her now, a darker shape in the dark. “I knew the moment he walked you back into my office. A man like that doesn’t keep souvenirs. He keeps ammunition. The only question was the caliber.”

Iris stared. The drip behind her seemed to grow louder. “You knew?”

“I assumed. It’s what I would have done.” A dry, humorless sound that might have been a laugh. “If I were a coward. Which I’m not.”

The air grew thin. Iris’s chest felt tight. She focused on the splinter in her palm, the specific, pinpoint sting. “The agent from the Committee. He wasn’t just offering me a deal. He was confirming inventory. Arthur’s been talking to them. Trading pieces of my life for—I don’t know. Leverage for his other clients. Safe passage. My teaching job, this play. It’s all part of the performance. For them. To prove I’m manageable. To prove I’ll sing the song they want, on their stage.”

She waited for Pauline to say something. To tell her she was a fool, a narcissist, that the world didn’t revolve around her paranoia. The silence stretched. A car passed outside, its headlights sweeping across the high, vaulted ceiling in a brief, ghostly arc. For a second, the gilded curlicues of the proscenium lit up, garish and beautiful, then faded back to black.

“So don’t sing it.”

Iris blinked. “What?”

“The song they want. Don’t sing it.” Pauline’s voice was clear now, matter-of-fact. “You have a stage. You have a part. You have lines. Use them.”

“It’s not that simple. He’ll release it. All of it. He’ll give them the file.”

“Then he will.” A rustle of fabric. Pauline was standing now. Iris could see the hem of her skirt at the edge of the light. “And you will be a middle-aged chanteuse with a suspicious past and a fading voice. And I will be a radical playwright who hired you. And the institute will be a nest of subversives. And the sun will come up the next day.” She took a step forward, into the fringe of the light. Her face was all shadows and angles. “What did you think was going to happen, Iris? That you could walk through this and come out the other side with your career intact? That you could have both? The truth and the gilding?”

Iris’s mouth was dry. She tasted copper. “No.”

“Then what are you asking me for? Permission to be ruined? You have it. It’s yours. You earned it twelve years ago.” Pauline’s words weren’t cruel. They were heavy, like stones being laid one by one. “But ruin is a direction. Not an ending. You can be ruined quietly, in a hearing room, on their terms. Or you can be ruined loudly, on my stage, on yours. Those are your choices. The third one—the one where you keep everything—that’s gone. It was never real.”

The truth of it was a cold flood. It filled her up, chilled her from the inside. She had known, of course. Some part of her had always known. But hearing it laid out like that, stripped of all her careful, desperate nuance left her hollow. She thought of the crack in the ceiling of Arthur’s study. A fork in a road that led nowhere.

“I’m scared,” she whispered.

“I know.” Pauline’s voice softened, just a fraction. “So am I. This place. It’s all I have. They’ll come for it. They’ve been looking for an excuse. You might be that excuse.” She paused. “But I’d rather burn for something real than slowly suffocate under a mountain of their paperwork. I made that choice a long time ago. You’re just catching up.”

Iris looked down at her hands. The splinter was a tiny dark line. She pulled it out. A bead of blood welled, black in the low light. She pressed her thumb against it. The pain was clean, specific.

“What do I do?” she asked, not to Pauline, not to anyone.

“You perform.” Pauline’s answer came back, immediate. “You perform the hell out of that part. You take every line I wrote, every silence I built into it, and you fill it with everything you’ve been hiding. You let them see it. You let Arthur see it. You let the man from the Committee see it. You make it so true that their file, their photographs, their little notes, look like cheap forgeries next to it.” She took a final step, now fully in the light. Her eyes were dark pools. “You turn their trap into your confession. It’s the only way out that’s worth taking.”

Iris looked at her. The woman she had loved. The woman she had left. The woman who had built a life from the rubble of that leaving. There was no forgiveness in her face. No softness. Just a fierce, unyielding clarity. It was the most honest thing Iris had seen in years.

“Okay,” Iris said. The word was small. It vanished into the dark theatre.

Pauline held her gaze for a long moment. Then she nodded, once. She turned and walked back up the aisle, her footsteps silent on the carpet. The door at the back of the house opened, a rectangle of faint hallway light. She didn’t look back. The door sighed shut.

Iris was alone again with the empty stage and the single, burning light. The drip from backstage had stopped. The silence was absolute. She looked at the crude set, the painted crates, the ghost of a ship. It wasn’t much. But it was hers. For now. She slid off the stage, her feet hitting the floor with a soft thud. She walked to the work light, reached down, and twisted the switch.

Darkness paused everything.

The dressing room was too small for two people, and it smelled of cold cream and old wood. A single bulb in a wire cage cast hard shadows. Iris sat before the mirror, her reflection a stranger’s face painted with the lines of a grieving mother. The makeup felt thick, a second skin. Pauline stood behind her, a silhouette against the closed door, holding the script. She wasn’t looking at it. She was looking at Iris in the glass.

“It’s not right,” Iris said. She said it rough, scraped raw from a week of rehearsals that felt more like exorcisms.

“It’s the script.”

“The script is a cage.”

Pauline didn’t move. A muscle in her jaw tightened. The silence wasn’t empty. It was full of the hum from the stage lights, the distant murmur of the gathering audience out front, the ghost of every line they’d ever spoken to each other. Iris watched a moth beat itself against the hot glass of the bulb. A tiny, frantic ticking.

“You know what happens if you step outside it,” Pauline said finally. The sound was low. “Arthur is in the third row. Left aisle. The agent is with him.”

Iris’s stomach did a slow, cold roll. She hadn’t known that. She’d assumed they’d be there, but the specificity—third row left aisle—made it real. They were landmarks. She could find them in the dark. She picked up a grease pencil from the littered table. The end was worn to a blunt nub. She’d used it to mark her script, her changes a secret map in the margins. The paper was soft at the edges, fragile.

“He asked for the file,” Iris said. She didn’t look away from her own eyes in the mirror. “Last night. I asked him for it.”

Pauline’s reflection went very still. “And?”

“He said no. He called it poor business.” She let out a breath that was almost a laugh. It hurt her throat. “He’s right. It is.”

“Iris.”

“I’m not going to say their lines, Pauline.” She turned on the stool. The wood creaked. The face paint made her feel like she was wearing a mask, but underneath, her skin was hot. “I can’t. It’s a confession written by the prosecution. It asks me to beg for a forgiveness I don’t want from people who don’t matter.”

“It’s the play we agreed to do.”

“It’s the play you wrote to get produced.” Iris stood. The cramped space forced them close. She could smell the starch in Pauline’s shirt, the faint, familiar scent of her skin beneath. “It’s brilliant. And it’s a trap. You built a beautiful, righteous trap and you walked me into it. That’s fine. I walked. But I’m not saying the words at the end.”

Pauline’s eyes searched her face, looking for the act, the performance. Finding none. “What will you say?”

“I don’t know.”

“You have to know.”

“I’ll know when I’m out there.” Iris’s hands were steady. That was the strange part. The tremor that usually lived in her fingers was gone, absorbed into a deeper, more fundamental stillness. “It has to be that way. If I plan it, it becomes another script. Another code they can break.”

A sound came from Pauline then, not quite a sigh, not quite a gasp. A surrender of air. She looked down at the script in her hands, then let it drop onto the cluttered table. It landed with a soft slap. “They’ll destroy you.”

“They already have.”

“No.” Pauline shook her head, a sharp, frustrated motion. “They’ll take what’s left. Your name. Any chance of work. They’ll make sure you never stand in a spotlight again.”

“What spotlight?” Iris gestured around the tiny, shabby room. A crack in the plaster above the door ran diagonally, a pale scar. She’d never noticed the pattern of water stains on the ceiling tiles before. They looked like continents on a map of a forgotten world. “This isn’t a spotlight. It’s a bare bulb in a basement. It’s the only honest light I’ve had in twelve years.”

Pauline reached out. Her hand didn’t touch Iris, but hovered near her arm, a moth drawn to a different kind of heat. “Why now? After all this time. Why this?”

The question hung between them. Iris looked at the hand, at the fine bones of the wrist, at a small, pale scar near the thumb she remembered from Paris—a cut from a broken wine glass, a night of too much laughter. The memory was a physical ache, a sweet, sharp pain behind her ribs.

“Because you gave me the playbill,” Iris said, her voice barely a whisper. “You handed it to me. You put it in my pocket. That wasn’t direction. That was an invitation.”

For a long moment, Pauline didn’t speak. The noises from beyond the door seemed to recede, the world narrowing to this room, this air, this suspended second. Then her hand completed its journey, her fingers closing around Iris’s wrist. The grip was firm, almost painful. “Then do it,” she said. Her eyes were fierce, glistening in the bad light. “Don’t just break the script. Break the stage. Break the whole damn theater. Make them hear you.”

She let go turned, and picked up a small leather case from a chair. She opened it. Inside, nestled in faded velvet was a single piece of costume jewelry—a brooch shaped like a bird in flight, its wings set with chips of blue glass. It was tawdry. The kind of thing a character would wear. Pauline unpinned it, then stepped close again. Her breath was warm on Iris’s cheek. She fastened the brooch to the lapel of Iris’s plain black dress, her fingers deft. The touch was brief, professional.

“A canary,” Pauline said softly, smoothing the fabric. “One last time.”

Iris looked down at the cheap, glittering thing. It was ridiculous. It was perfect. Her throat tightened, not with fear, but with a gratitude so vast it had no name. “Thank you.”

“Don’t.” Pauline’s voice was rough. She took a step back, putting distance between them again. The director returned to her face. “It’s not a gift. It’s a weapon. Use it.” She checked the watch on her wrist, a man’s watch with a cracked leather band. “Fifteen minutes. I need to check the houselights.”

She moved to the door, her hand on the knob. She paused, her back to Iris. “The line about the sealed train car,” she said, to the peeling paint on the door. “The one you rewrote. It’s good. It’s better than good. If you—if you need something to hold onto out there, start with that.”

Then she was gone. The door clicked shut.

Iris was alone with the stranger in the mirror. She touched the brooch, the cold metal of the bird’s wing. The grease pencil was still in her other hand. She looked at her script, open on the table to the final monologue. The words were a foreign language. Her own handwriting crowded the margins, a chaotic, inky vine strangling the printed text.

She picked up the script. She didn’t reread it. She simply tore the final pages out along the seam, the sound a sharp satisfying rip in the quiet room. She folded the pages once, then again, and tucked them into the waistband of her skirt, against her skin. The paper was a whisper, a secret.

What was left in her hands was the empty shell of the booklet, the title page, the cast list. She dropped it into the metal wastebasket by the table.

From the corridor, a voice called, “Five minutes, Miss Finch!”

Iris didn’t answer. She looked at her reflection, at the painted lines of grief, at the blue glass bird on her chest. She took a deep breath, and the air tasted of dust and possibility. Somewhere, a stage manager’s bell rang, a tinny, distant sound. It was time.

She turned off the bare bulb, plunging the room into a darkness that was not quite complete. Light seeped under the door. She stood in the dimness, waiting for the shake in her hands to return. It didn’t. There was only the steady, solid beat of her own heart, a drum counting down to zero.
