The silence in the rehearsal hall had a texture now. It wasn’t empty. It was a held note that had gone on so long it had become the air itself. Iris stood in the cone of light, the taste of unspoken ash still on her tongue. Her throat ached, a raw, physical rebuke. From the darkness, Pauline hadn’t spoken again. The quiet was its own verdict.

Then the heavy double doors at the back of the hall banged open, shattering the note.

A wedge of flat, grey afternoon light cut across the dusty floorboards. A man stood silhouetted in it, his shape broad and solid in an overcoat the color of expensive caramel. He brought the city in with him—the smell of wet wool and cigar smoke, the distant grind of traffic. He clapped his hands together once, a sharp, dry sound.

“Now this is more like it! A real theatre. You can feel the ghosts.” He spoke in a rich baritone, a radio announcer’s instrument, and it filled the space without effort.

Iris blinked, the light from the doors making dark spots swim in her vision. She saw Clara, still seated in the front row with the script, stiffen. She saw the other actors, frozen in their places on the stage periphery, glance toward the wings where Pauline would be.

Pauline emerged from the darkness stage left, not from the wings but from the shadows beside the proscenium. She held a pencil, tapping its end against her thumb in a rapid, staccato rhythm. She didn’t walk toward the man. She waited.

“Mr. Strauss,” she said. The two words were flat, devoid of the welcome his tone had assumed.

Duke Strauss moved down the center aisle with a proprietor’s confidence. He shed his coat, draping it over a seatback as he passed, revealing a suit of fine, muted tweed. His hair was silver at the temples, swept back from a face that was handsome in a well-maintained way, like a polished piece of furniture. His eyes found Iris on the stage and lit with a practiced, genuine-seeming warmth.

“Iris Finch. My God. Still stopping hearts from fifty feet.” He gave a short, appreciative shake of his head, then turned the warmth toward Pauline. “And you must be the formidable Miss Carter. Langston’s told me so much. All good, all good.”

He was now at the lip of the stage, looking up at Iris. He smelled of bay rum and something else, something clean and surgical, like a dentist’s office. A tiny thread had come loose from the collar of his white shirt. Iris’s fingers twitched with an old, useless instinct to fix it, to smooth the imperfection. Her mother’s hands.

“Duke,” Iris said. The word came out a croak. She cleared her throat. “This is a surprise.”

“Life’s full of them, darling. That’s what keeps the blood moving.” He hoisted himself onto the stage with an easy grunt, his leather soles scraping the boards. He stood close enough for her to see the fine web of lines around his eyes, the pores on his nose. He surveyed the empty hall, the single work light, the battered chairs. His gaze was appraising, not unkind. “Langston said you were involved in something… authentic. He undersold it. This place has bones.”

Pauline finally moved, walking to the edge of the light pool. She didn’t join them on the stage. She stood below, looking up, the pencil now still in her fist. “We’re in the middle of a scene, Mr. Strauss. Was there something you needed?”

“Need? No, no. I was in the neighborhood. Meeting with the backers for the new revue at the St. Regis. Iris thought I’d pop by, see the artist at work.” He winked at Iris, a conspiratorial flicker. “And to float an idea. A little synergy.”

The word, oily and wrong. Iris felt a cold knot tighten under her ribs.

“Synergy,” Pauline repeated. She didn’t blink.

“Between your project here and Iris’s return to the spotlight.” Duke spread his hands, taking in the hall. “What you’re doing here, it’s important. Brave. But let’s be practical—it’s a church basement. The message deserves a bigger pulpit. My revue is slated for the Broadhurst in the fall. Patriotic themes, unity, the American spirit. We close the first act with a tribute to homefront heroes. Now.” He turned fully to Iris, his enthusiasm a physical pressure. “Imagine this. We take the core of your play here, the emotional heart, and we… distill it. Twenty, twenty-five minutes of powerful drama. Then, as the curtain falls on that, the lights come up on Iris Finch, center stage. She sings ‘The Last Goodbye.’ A segue from sacrifice to remembrance. From tragedy to hope. It’s seamless.”

The air left the room. Iris heard the faint, perpetual hiss of the steam radiator in the corner, a sound she’d tuned out hours ago. She saw the dust motes swirling in the light beam, stupid and directionless. A crack in the plaster above Duke’s head ran down and then jagged sharply left, like a river on a map changing its mind.

Pauline’s laugh was a short, dry exhalation, more shock than humor. “You want to use a play about a military cover-up that killed three hundred and twenty Black sailors as a… an overture for a torch song.”

“As a bridge,” Duke corrected, his smile unwavering. “An emotional bridge. The song isn’t just a song, it’s a symbol. It’s what every woman felt seeing her boy off. The universal in the specific. Your play provides the gravity. Iris provides the catharsis. Together, it’s not just theatre. It’s an event.”

Iris’s mouth was dry. She thought of the silent leitmotif she’d written for Eleanor, the cello note of complicity. She imagined it swelling, then fading into the familiar, lush orchestration of her hit. The betrayal would be so complete it became meaningless. A wash of sound. She could feel the shape of it, the terrible, slick professionalism of the idea. Langston must have loved it.

“The play isn’t finished,” Iris said. The words were ash again.

“All the better! We can workshop it together. My writers are tops. They can help you… sharpen the narrative. Focus on the communal grief, the shared loss. The politics are a distraction.” Duke leaned in, his voice dropping to a confidential murmur meant only for her, but which carried perfectly in the quiet hall. “This is your way back, Iris. A dignified return. On your terms. This…” He gestured vaguely at the dusty space. “… this is a worthy effort. But let’s be honest, it’s a dead end. This synthesis, though? This is a future.”

From below, Pauline’s voice was quiet, precise, and colder than the February air outside. “A synthesis. You propose to take a work about the specific, violent erasure of Black men by their own government, sand down its edges until it’s a vague tragedy, and then use it as an emotional warm-up act for a white woman to sing a pop song about missing her sweetheart.” She took a single step forward. “That isn’t a bridge, Mr. Strauss. That’s a burial.”

Duke’s smile didn’t falter, but it settled, becoming something harder. He turned his head slowly to look at her. “Art serves the audience, Miss Carter. Not the other way around. I’m offering you a national platform.”

“You’re offering to make a digestible paste out of a broken bone.” Pauline’s eyes flicked to Iris, just for a second. Something hot moved through Iris’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was the feeling of being measured for a coffin while you were still breathing.

“I think,” Iris said, and then pulled the words back, far away, “the play might lose something in the translation.”

“Translation is what we do,” Duke said, spreading his hands again. He was a man used to having space, to his presence being the answer. “That’s show business, darling. Not show art. The business part is what keeps the lights on in places like this.” He patted the proscenium arch as if it were a faithful dog. “Think about it. Talk to Langston. He gets it.”

He gave Iris’s arm a brief, familiar squeeze, then turned and jumped lightly down from the stage. He retrieved his coat, shrugging it back on. He tipped an imaginary hat to Pauline. “A pleasure, Miss Carter. The work you do here is vital. Truly.” He said it like he was confirming a minor fact.

He walked back up the aisle, his footsteps echoing. At the doors, he paused and turned. The grey light framed him once more. “The offer stands, Iris. For both of you. Don’t let purity be the enemy of relevance.”

The doors swung shut behind him, swallowing the light and the smell of bay rum. The held note of silence rushed back in, but it was different now. Fractured. Contaminated.

Iris stared at the spot where he’d been. The ghost of his hand on her arm felt like a brand.

From the darkness, Pauline didn’t speak. The only sound was the deliberate, slow rip of paper. Iris looked down. Pauline was tearing a page from her script. She folded the strip once, then again, her movements exact. She didn’t look up.

“The line, Iris,” she said, her voice stripped of everything but a weary exhaustion. “We have a scene to finish.”

But the scene was gone. All Iris could hear was the slick, plausible echo of Duke Strauss’s voice, weaving a future where her silence wouldn’t be a chokehold, but a selling point. A bridge. She looked at her hands. They were perfectly still.

The coffee shop was called the Gold Cup. It was two blocks from the institute, a narrow slot between a shoe repair and a newsstand. The window was fogged with steam. A radio behind the counter played a tinny big band number, the trumpet solo thin and desperate against the clatter of dishes.

Iris took the booth at the back. The vinyl seat was cracked, the stuffing leaking out in a grey tuft. She ordered coffee from a waitress with a tired mouth and didn’t touch it when it came. The cup was thick, white, a brown stain circling the rim like a tidemark. She traced it with her thumb. The heat from the mug seeped into her skin.

Cross was five minutes late. She watched the door, the bell jangling each time it opened it. A delivery boy with a canvas sack. An old man in a hat that was too big for him. The light from the street was the color of weak tea.

He came in without the bell sounding. He must have caught the door. He wore a different suit today, a charcoal grey, and he carried a folded newspaper under one arm. He saw her gave a small, polite nod, and slid into the booth opposite. He didn’t remove his coat.

“Miss Finch. Thank you for meeting me.” The words came out lower here, quieter. It didn’t carry. He set the newspaper on the table between them. The headline was about a baseball trade.

The waitress appeared. Cross ordered coffee. Black. He waited until she was gone before he spoke again. His eyes were on the window, on the blur of passing legs. “How is the work progressing?”

“It’s a play.” Her own voice sounded strange to her, flat and hollow. “We rehearse.”

“Yes.” He turned his gaze to her. His eyes were a pale, watery blue. The kind of eyes that seemed to take in light but not reflect it. “Port Chicago. A challenging choice of material.”

The air in the booth thickened. The trumpet on the radio hit a sour note. Iris’s stomach clenched, a tight, involuntary fist. She hadn’t told him the name of the play. She was certain. In the club, it had been ‘the project,’ ‘the work.’ She’d given him nothing but dates and times, the sterile log of an informant.

“It’s a historical piece,” she said. The words were ash.

“So I understand.” He accepted his coffee from the waitress with a soft thanks. He didn’t drink it. He stirred it with a spoon, the metal scraping the porcelain in a slow, even circle. “Three hundred and twenty men. A tragedy. The Navy’s report was… thorough.”

He knew. He’d read it. The thought was a cold stone dropping through her. He hadn’t just asked her to watch. He was studying the script himself.

“The director,” he said, not looking up from his stirring. “Miss Carter. She has a particular interpretation, I assume. An angle.”

“Plays are never just about the event.”

He stopped stirring. Laid the spoon neatly on the saucer. “They are about the argument. The grievance. The thing the writer wants you to see that isn’t in the history books. What is Miss Carter’s argument?”

Iris’s mouth was dry. She picked up her own cup, just to have something to do with her hands. The coffee was lukewarm, bitter. A film of oil floated on the surface. She set it down. “The play follows the wives. The waiting. The official lies.”

“Ah.” Cross nodded, as if she’d confirmed something. “The homefront perspective. The betrayal of trust. Not the mutiny, then. Not the disobedience of the surviving sailors who refused to load more munitions.”

“It’s not about that.”

“Isn’t it?” He leaned forward, just an inch. He lowered his voice further. “The refusal of orders is a subversive act, Miss Finch. Even—perhaps especially—when the orders are criminal negligence. To dramatize that refusal, to make heroes of men who turned their backs on their duty… that’s a political statement. Wouldn’t you agree?”

On the counter, the radio announcer’s voice cut in, cheerful and booming, extolling the virtues of a new laundry soap. The disconnect was dizzying. Iris focused on a fly buzzing against the window, its body a frantic black speck in the steam.

“It’s a drama,” she said again, the word useless.

“Everything is a drama.” Cross finally took a sip of his coffee. He didn’t react to the taste. “The question is who is directing it. And for what audience.” He set the cup down. “You met with Duke Strauss today.”

It wasn’t a question. The cold stone in her gut turned to ice. He’d been watching the institute. Or he had someone else watching. Or Langston had told him. The possibilities spiderwebbed, each one worse.

“He visited. Yes.”

“A talented producer. A patriot.” Cross’s tone was neutral, assessing. “His revue at the Broadhurst will be a celebration of American unity. He sees a place for you in it. A bridge, I believe he said.”

He’d heard that, too. The specific word. Iris’s skin prickled, a fine, cold sweat at the back of her neck. She remembered the feeling of being on stage as a girl, the terror of forgetting a line, the blinding light that hid the faces but not the expectation. This was the opposite. The light was on her, and he was in the dark, watching, knowing every line before she spoke it.

“It was a proposal,” she managed.

“A compelling one. Commercial viability. Mainstream appeal. A return to the light for you.” He folded his hands on the table. His nails were clean, trimmed short. “It would be a simpler path. A cleaner narrative. The country is weary of conflict, Miss Finch. It wants reassurance. It wants to remember the war as a victory of spirit, not a catalog of errors. Mr. Strauss offers that. Miss Carter…” He let the sentence hang. The fly hit the window again, a tiny, persistent tap.

“My brother served in the Pacific,” Cross said, so quietly she almost missed it. He was looking past her, at the fogged window, at nothing. “Twenty-two months on a destroyer. He wrote letters about the sunsets. He never wrote about the fighting. When he came home, he couldn’t bear loud noises. The sound of a car backfiring would send him to the floor. He took a job in a library. He needs the quiet.” He blinked, slowly, and his eyes came back to her. They were unchanged. “There is a cost to dwelling on the dark things. To dragging them into the light and insisting everyone look. It serves no one. Not the men who died. Not the ones who came home broken. And certainly not the artists who make a spectacle of the pain.”

The words were measured, reasonable. They coiled around her ribs and squeezed. This was the pressure, applied with surgical precision. Not a threat about her past, not today. A threat about the future. The threat was about the play itself. It was about the choice she was making by staying in that church basement.

“I’m just an actress in it,” she said, but the protest was feeble.

“You are the name attached to it. The face. Your participation legitimizes it. Your departure…” He left it open. “Would be noted. Understood.”

He wanted her to quit. To walk away from Pauline’s play and into Duke’s revue. To let the Port Chicago story fade back into the church basement, untroubled by spotlights or subpoenas. It was the same choice, she realized with a dull thud of recognition. Paris, again. The radical, difficult truth, or the safe, polished lie. The woman she loved, or the crowd that loved her.

Cross reached into his inner coat pocket. He didn’t pull out a folder this time. It was a single, crisp white envelope. He placed it on the table, aligned perfectly with the edge of the newspaper. “A list of names. Individuals associated with the institute’s funding board. We would like to know which of them have visited rehearsals. Who speaks with Miss Carter after hours. The nature of their conversations, if you can ascertain it.”

He didn’t push it toward her. He just left it there, a white rectangle on the Formica.

“This isn’t about the political tenor anymore,” she said.

He walked out, the door closing silently behind him. The bell didn’t ring.

Iris sat. The big band number ended, and a woman’s voice, smooth as honey began to sing a love song. The fly had found a crumb on the next table and was crawling over it. She looked at the envelope. It was blank. No name. No marking.

Her coffee was cold. She drank it anyway, the bitterness coating her tongue, a taste that would linger for hours. When she finally picked up the envelope, it felt weightless. She slipped it into her purse without opening it. Her fingers brushed the cold metal of the Zippo lighter at the bottom. She hadn’t carried it for weeks. She didn’t know why it was there today.

She left money on the table and stepped out into the fading afternoon. The sky was the color of a dirty slate. A few blocks east, the institute waited, dark and silent. A few blocks west, her apartment, where Langston would be mixing a drink, already composing the narrative of her comeback. The envelope in her purse was a third destination, a place that existed nowhere on the map.

She started walking. She didn’t know which direction.

The streetlights were coming on, each one buzzing to life with a soft, electrical complaint. She’d walked blocks without seeing them. The cold had seeped through her coat, a damp, persistent chill that settled in the joints of her fingers. She stopped under one of the lights, its yellow glow making the wet pavement look like old, veined marble. A delivery boy on a bicycle swerved around her, whistling a tune she almost recognized—a few bars of something popular last year. The melody dissolved into the traffic noise.

Her building loomed, a slab of limestone with darkened windows. The lobby smelled of floor wax and yesterday’s boiled cabbage. The elevator operator, an old man named Eddie who had known her when she played the Copacabana gave her a tired nod. The cage rattled upward.

The apartment was silent, but not empty. A different silence from the theatre—domestic, expectant. The scent of Langston’s pipe tobacco, a sweet, cloying blanket over the rooms. A single lamp was lit in the living room, pooling light on the Persian rug he’d bought to impress a critic. One of her sheet music portfolios lay open on the piano bench, a fan of yellowed pages. She hadn’t touched it in months.

She shed her coat, let it fall over the back of a chair. Her body felt heavy, as if she’d been carrying something up the stairs.

“You’re late.”

Langston’s voice came from the doorway to his study. He held a highball glass, the ice mostly melted. He was still in his shirt and tie, but his sleeves were rolled up, a sign he’d been working. Or thinking.

“Rehearsal ran long.” She spoke, quieter now, flat, even to herself.

“Duke called.” Langston took a sip, his eyes on her over the rim of the glass. “He said you were… less than receptive to his proposal.”

“He wants to make a medley out of a munitions explosion.”

“He wants to give you a Broadway stage.” Langston stepped into the room. The light caught the silver in his hair and made it look like frost. “He wants to pay you. A considerable sum, Iris. Enough to… ease some pressures.”

“What pressures?” The question came out before she could shape it. A stupid question.

He didn’t answer it. He walked to the sideboard, poured another finger of bourbon into his glass. He didn’t offer her one. “He said Carter was hostile. That she made a scene.”

“She stated a fact. He didn’t like the fact.”

“Her facts are not our facts.” He turned, leaning against the sideboard. “Our fact is that you have an agreement with the government. Our fact is that you are in that building on sufferance, to observe and report. Not to become a true believer in some… racial grievance play.”

The words, precise and ugly. A racial grievance play. She saw the faces of the actors on that stage. The young man playing the navy clerk, his hands trembling as he mimed typing the manifest. The way he’d looked at her after her silence, a question in his eyes she couldn’t answer.

“It’s a good play,” she said, the words weak.

“It’s a dangerous play.” He put his glass down with a soft click. “And Duke’s offer is a lifeline. It gets you out of that church basement and back into a legitimate venue. It puts you in front of the right people, singing the right song. It provides a… Patriotic context. A counter-narrative.”

“A cover.”

“A solution.” He sighed, a long exhalation of patience wearing thin. “Iris. This isn’t about art. It’s about survival. Cross was here.”

The cold in her fingers became a sharp, sudden ache. “Here?”

“This afternoon. He wanted an update. I told him you were settling in, that the report would be forthcoming.” Langston picked up his pipe from an ashtray, tamped down the dead ash with his thumb. A habitual, fussy motion. “He asked specific questions. He asked about the play’s content. About Carter’s sources. He knew the name of the ship. The Quinault Victory. He knew the tonnage of the explosives.”

Iris’s mouth was dry. She remembered Cross’s offhand comment in the club. My brother served in the Pacific. “He’s done his homework.”

“He’s setting the examination.” Langston struck a match, the flare bright in the dim room. He sucked the pipe to life, wreathing his head in a cloud of blue smoke. “Duke’s revue is the answer key. You perform a segment of the play, then you sing ‘The Last Goodbye.’ You transition the audience from… whatever anger Carter is stirring up, to sentiment. To nostalgia. It’s a narrative correction. And it demonstrates cooperation. Willingness to… align.”

“To betray it.”

“To sanitize it.” He said it, calm, reasonable. The voice that had negotiated her contracts, soothed offended sponsors, explained why a certain lyric needed to change. “There is no version of this where that play is produced as Carter intends. Not in this climate. You know that. Either it gets shut down, and everyone involved is flagged. Or it gets… repurposed. This way, the message is softened. The institute gets some attention, some legitimacy by association. You get your career back. It’s the only outcome where anyone gets anything.”

She walked to the window, looked down at the street. The lights of a taxi streaked by, a smear of yellow in the dark. A man walked a small, prancing dog. Life, ordinary and unconcerned.

“What did you tell Cross?” she asked, her forehead against the cool glass.

“That you were making progress. That the play was indeed political, but that commercial interests were moving to co-opt it. That the situation was contained.” He paused. “I may have implied Duke’s involvement was at my suggestion. A show of good faith.”

So that was it. Langston was brokering a three-way deal. Her compliance for Cross. Her performance for Duke. His own role as the indispensable middleman, securing his slice of safety from all sides.

“He wants names,” Langston said, quieter now. “For the report. Not just observations. Names of the most vocal. The ones driving the political interpretation.”

She closed her eyes. Saw Pauline in the dark hall, a still, watchful shape. Heard her dry, contemptuous laugh. An overture for a torch song.

“I can’t.”

“You can.” He was beside her now. She could smell the bourbon on his breath, the sweet tobacco. He didn’t touch her. “You write down the names of the actors. The ones reciting the lines Carter wrote. It’s not a moral choice, Iris. It’s paperwork. They’re already in the play. They’re already on a list. You’re just… confirming.”

The logic was a trap, clean and inescapable. She felt the weight of the Zippo in her pocket, a small, hard rectangle against her thigh. A useless artifact.

“What if the play is the truth?” The words were a whisper.

Langston was silent for a long moment. Then he made a soft, dismissive sound in his throat. “Truth is a luxury. We’re in the business of perception.” He turned away, back to his drink. “Think about Duke’s offer. It’s the exit ramp. Take it.”

He left her there at the window. She heard the study door close, the faint click of the latch.

The apartment settled around her. The radiator ticked. On the piano, the sheet music for “The Last Goodbye” lay open. The notes were a familiar pattern of dots and lines. She could sing it in her sleep. A song about a goodbye so final it felt like a physical amputation. She’d sung it a thousand times and felt the audience’s yearning swell toward her. They’d thought it was about boys going off to war. It had never been about that.

She traced the rim of a crystal vase on the sideboard, a wedding gift from a producer. Her thumb found a tiny chip in the glass, a rough edge in the smoothness. She pressed her skin against it, just shy of breaking.

Outside, the city hummed its endless, indifferent song. Inside, the silence was not empty. It was filling up with names.
