Chapter 17

The restaurant smelled of garlic and old money. A low, amber light pooled in the centers of tables, leaving faces in a soft, forgiving gloom. Iris’s chair was too close to the wall—she could feel the cold plaster through the thin silk of her dress, a persistent chill against her shoulder blade. Arthur had chosen the place, of course. He sat across from her, his face a study in pleasant expectation, his fingers steepled before him as if in prayer. The third setting at their small table remained empty, the silverware gleaming with a sterile promise.

“He’s a serious patron,” Arthur said, his voice a confidential murmur that carried anyway. “A friend of the arts. And a friend to those who navigate our current… climate with discretion.”

Iris watched a waiter move between tables, his tray balanced high on one palm, a feat of quiet equilibrium. The man’s white jacket had a faint yellow stain near the second button, a tiny, imperfect sun. She focused on that. It was easier than looking at the empty chair.

“You didn’t mention he was bringing a guest,” she said.

“I didn’t know he was.” Arthur’s smile didn’t shift. “But it’s a good sign. It means he’s invested in the institution, not just the idea of it. He wants to see the… Collaboration.”

The word hung between them, a delicate, poisonous thing. Collaboration. The door to the restaurant opened, letting in a brief gust of wet pavement and car exhaust. Two figures were silhouetted against the streetlight.

Iris’s stomach dropped. Not a vague dread, but a specific, physical plummet, like missing a step in the dark.

The man was broad, expensively coated, his hat already in his hands. The woman beside him was taller, her posture erect, her own coat a simple, dark wool. Pauline.

Arthur made a soft satisfied sound. “Ah. Excellent.”

Pauline’s gaze found theirs across the room. She gave no sign of recognition, no flicker of surprise. Her face was a calm, composed mask. She said something to her companion, who nodded and began weaving toward their table, his smile wide and ready.

Iris’s mouth was parchment. She reached for her water glass, her fingers leaving damp prints on the cold surface. The ice cubes had melted into strange, pitted shapes.

“Miss Finch,” the man boomed, arriving at the table. He took her hand before she could offer it. His grip was firm, slightly damp. “An honor. Charles Denton. I’ve followed your career for years. ‘Midnight in Montmartre’—a classic.”

“Mr. Denton.” Her voice worked, a minor miracle.

“And this,” Denton said, turning with a flourish, “is the formidable Pauline Carter. The engine of your little Harlem experiment, I understand.”

Pauline offered a nod. “Mr. Denton is too kind.” Her eyes met Iris’s for a fraction of a second—a flat, neutral glance that conveyed nothing and everything. She took the empty seat, the one between Denton and Arthur, directly across from Iris. The arrangement felt like a tribunal.

A waiter materialized. Denton ordered a bottle of Chianti without consulting the menu. Arthur agreed it was an excellent choice. The ritual of the wine, the tasting, the pouring, filled a cavernous silence. Iris watched the ruby liquid swirl into her glass. A memory surfaced, unbidden and useless: a cheap bottle of vin ordinaire on a scarred table in Paris, the cork floating in the dregs. Pauline laughing, wiping her mouth with the back of her hand.

“To new partnerships,” Denton said, raising his glass.

They drank. The wine was tannic, leaving a dry fur on Iris’s tongue.

The conversation unspooled. Denton loved the theatre. He believed in its power to uplift, to educate. He spoke of the Institute’s potential as a beacon. Arthur echoed him, his interjections smooth and supportive. They were building something in the air between them, a castle of mutual regard and shared purpose.

Pauline spoke only when addressed directly. Her answers were precise, professional. She outlined the curriculum, the upcoming student production, the need for new lighting equipment. She did not look at Iris.

Iris tried to listen. She nodded at the appropriate moments. But her attention snagged on irrelevant details: the way the candle flame reflected twin pinpricks of light in Denton’s spectacles. The rhythmic tap of Pauline’s index finger against the stem of her wine glass—not a nervous tap, but a slow, deliberate beat. One, two. Like a muted metronome.

“And you, Miss Finch?” Denton turned his benefactor’s smile on her. “The transition from the stage to the classroom. It must be a fascinating challenge. Teaching the next generation to… feel.”

All eyes were on her. Arthur’s gaze held a gentle warning. Pauline’s was a wall.

“It’s less about teaching them to feel,” Iris heard herself say. The words felt distant, as if someone else were speaking them. “They already feel. It’s about teaching them the craft to contain it. To shape it. So it can be heard.”

A beat of silence. Denton’s smile widened. “Beautifully put. The vessel for the emotion!”

Pauline’s tapping finger stopped. She took a sip of wine, her eyes on the tablecloth. “Containment is one approach,” she said, her voice quiet but clear. “Some of us believe the craft is in the risk. In allowing the vessel to crack, if that’s where the truth leaks out.”

The air at the table changed. It wasn’t a chill, but a tightening, like a wire drawn taut.

Arthur laughed, a light, dismissive sound. “Two sides of the same artistic coin, I’m sure. Discipline and passion. Both are necessary.”

“Of course,” Denton agreed, though he looked momentarily puzzled, as if he’d missed a step in a dance he knew. “Balance. Everything in balance.”

The first course arrived—a pallid soup in shallow bowls. Iris picked up her spoon. The conversation drifted to safer waters: real estate, the new television sets, the weather. Pauline engaged politely, her earlier comment sinking beneath the surface like a stone.

Halfway through the soup, Denton excused himself to visit the gentlemen’s lounge. Arthur, seizing the moment rose with him. “I’ll point you the way, Charles. This place is a rabbit warren.”

They were gone. The silence they left behind was absolute. The clatter from other tables, the low din of conversation, all of it receded. There was only the table, the two of them, and the uneaten soup.

Iris put her spoon down. It clinked against the china, a shockingly loud sound.

Pauline did not look up. She traced the rim of her water glass with one fingertip. “You handled that well,” she said finally. She spoke with neither compliment nor criticism. It was an observation.

“I didn’t know you’d be here.”

“I gathered.” Pauline’s gaze lifted. In the candlelight, her eyes were dark, unreadable pools. “Denton requested it. He wanted to see the ‘creative dynamic.’ He’s considering a significant donation. For the Port Chicago production.”

The twist was a cold knot tightening under Iris’s ribs. A donation. From a man Arthur had brought to her. The wires of her life were crossing, tangling.

“Arthur didn’t mention that.”

“Arthur,” Pauline said, the name a dry scrap of paper, “doesn’t know I applied for funding separately. He thinks this is his coup. His way of keeping you… useful.” She leaned forward, just slightly. The candle flame danced in the sudden movement. “He doesn’t know I’ve been to three other potential donors this month. He doesn’t know I have a list of names from Cora, people who’ve been waiting for a project like this. People who don’t need to be wined and dined in Midtown.”

The second twist, landing right after the first left Iris breathless. Pauline had her own plan. A separate play. She wasn’t just enduring Arthur’s machinations; she was weaving around them, using them as cover.

“Why are you telling me this?” Iris’s voice was a whisper.

“Because you’re sitting there looking like a woman waiting for the trapdoor to open.” Pauline’s expression didn’t soften. “And because Denton’s money comes with expectations. He’ll want a say. He’ll want a certain… tone. Patriotic. Uplifting. Safe.” She paused. “Arthur will want that, too. It’s the same thing they always want.”

“And you don’t.”

“I want the truth of that dockyard explosion. I want the mothers’ faces. I want the silence of the Navy’s report.” Pauline’s finger tapped the table once, a definitive period. “If Denton’s money means watering that down to a pleasant lesson about unity, then his money is a poison. And I need to know if you understand that.”

It wasn’t a question about art. It was a test. The test was here, in this restaurant, with the smell of garlic and the yellow stain on the waiter’s jacket.

Before Iris could find an answer, the sound of returning laughter preceded the men. Arthur’s hand was on Denton’s shoulder, a picture of camaraderie.

Pauline leaned back, her face smoothing once more into its polite, professional mask. The moment of raw confession vanished, sealed away as if it had never happened.

“My apologies,” Denton said, resuming his seat. “Now, where were we? Ah, yes. The future.” He beamed at them both, a man pleased with the world and his place in it. “I must say, the synergy I see here is remarkable. Truly remarkable.”

Iris picked up her soup spoon again. The metal was warm from her hand. She looked at Pauline, but Pauline was already smiling at Denton, a careful, convincing performance. The crack in the vessel had been shown, and then deliberately, expertly, sealed. Iris understood, with a clarity that was almost painful, that she was the only one at the table who had seen it. And that, too was part of the test.

The theatre held its breath. Not a silence of absence, but a thick, listening quiet, the kind that gathered in the hollows of the empty seats and pooled in the dust motes drifting through the single work-light’s beam. Iris stood at the edge of the stage, her back to the darkness of the house. She could still taste the Chianti, that dry fur on her tongue, and beneath it, the ghost of garlic. A thread had come loose from the hem of her sleeve. She worried it between her thumb and forefinger, feeling the slight rasp of the silk.

Footsteps echoed in the wings, a measured, familiar tread. She didn’t turn.

Pauline stopped a few yards away, just outside the circle of light. She’d shed her coat. Her cream blouse was a pale smudge in the gloom.

“An engine,” Pauline said. She spoke, the sound flat, stripped of the dinner’s professional veneer. “That’s what he called me. The engine of the experiment.”

Iris kept her eyes on the loose thread. It was a darker blue than the rest. “He meant it as a compliment.”

“Did he.” It wasn’t a question. “He also called it a collaboration. That’s the word Arthur used isn’t it? At the table. ‘The collaboration.’” Pauline took a step forward, into the light. Her expression was calm, too calm, the stillness of water over a deep drop. “It’s a specific word. Has a certain weight to it, these days.”

Something hot moved through Iris’s chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. “It was a dinner. He’s a patron. You know the dance.”

“I know the music.” Pauline’s gaze was a physical pressure. “I’ve heard it before. The same key. The same rhythm. The gracious benefactor. The grateful artist. The useful intermediary. It’s a very popular tune. It plays in every country that’s ever decided to start sorting its citizens into boxes.” She tilted her head. “Which box have they put you in, Iris? The redeemable one? The one that says ‘Can be salvaged with proper guidance’?”

“Stop it.”

“Or is it the other one? The canary. The little bird that sings so prettily in the cage, so long as you keep it fed and don’t shake the bars too hard.”

Iris’s hand tightened on the silk thread. It snapped. “You think I wanted that? Any of it? You think I enjoyed sitting there, drinking his wine, listening to him call you an engine?”

“I think you were very good at it.” Pauline’s voice dropped, low and cold. “I think you’ve had years of practice. Smiling at the right men. Laughing at the right volume. Choosing the right compromises. It’s what you do. It’s what you’ve always done.”

The words landed like blows, precise and bruising. Iris’s throat closed. She looked past Pauline, at the fire curtain with its faded mural of a rising sun. A long crack ran through the center of it, a black vein. “You don’t know what you’re talking about.”

“Don’t I?” A short, mirthless sound escaped Pauline. “Paris. 1937. A record contract. A ticket home. A choice. You made it. You packaged it up as pragmatism. Survival. You told yourself it was the only way. And now here we are, twelve years later, and you’re still making the same choice. Just with different table settings.”

The memory rose, unbidden: the cheap linoleum of that Left Bank office, the smell of Gauloises and damp wool, the contract a crisp, terrifying rectangle on the desk. The way her hand had shaken when she picked up the pen. Not from fear of poverty, not entirely. From fear of the life she was saying no to. The one with Pauline. The one that demanded a different, harder courage.

“It wasn’t just about the money,” Iris said, the words scraping out. “In Paris.”

“No.” Pauline’s agreement was swift, brutal. “It was about the door. The one that opened onto the stage lights and the nice reviews and the respectable life. The one that closed on everything else. On us. On the work we talked about. The songs that were supposed to mean something.” She took another step closer. The light caught the silver at her temples, the fine lines at the corners of her eyes. “You sold the key, Iris. You sold it for a spotlight. And now you’re surprised that the man who owns the theatre decides what you sing?”

The accusation hung in the dusty air. Iris’s palms were damp. She wiped them on her dress, a useless gesture. “You think it was that simple.”

“I think you’re a collaborator. Maybe not with a capital C. Not yet. But you’re at the table. You’re drinking the wine. You’re letting them call you a partner.” Pauline’s voice broke, just a fraction. “And you brought them to my door.”

That was it. The core of it. Not the politics, not the past. The present, personal betrayal. Iris saw it then—the real fear in Pauline’s eyes, not for herself, but for the Institute. For the students. For the fragile, defiant thing she’d built here in this crumbling building.

“It wasn’t my choice,” Iris whispered.

“There’s always a choice.”

“No.” The word was louder than she intended. It echoed off the back wall of the house, a small, sharp report. “You don’t understand. You never had to understand.” She turned fully to face Pauline, the heat in her chest solidifying into a hard, reckless knot. “You think my songs were just torch songs? Pretty little nothings for the supper club crowd?”

Pauline went very still.

“During the war,” Iris said, the sentences coming in ragged bursts, “the broadcasts. The ones from London. The coded messages. You heard them.”

A flicker in Pauline’s eyes. A memory. They’d listened together, huddled over a shortwave radio in a borrowed flat, the static like a second language.

“I didn’t just hear them.” Iris’s mouth was dry as sand. “Some of them. The ones with the musical cues. The specific intervals. The pauses in the weather reports.” She held the thought. “They were mine. The lyrics were mine. The patterns. I’d get a phrase, a sequence, a time. I’d work it into a bridge. A verse. ‘Midnight in Montmartre’ wasn’t about a love affair. It was a drop point. A recognition signal.” She let out a breath that shuddered. “Arthur has the letters. Not just ours. The others. The ones that explain it. He’s had them for years. He calls it insurance.”

The silence that followed was absolute. Pauline didn’t move. She seemed to be processing the words, turning them over, examining their edges.

“He knows,” Iris said, the truth of it a cold weight in her stomach. “He’s always known. And now Denton, and the Committee… They suspect. They don’t have proof. But Arthur does. And he’ll trade it. For his other clients. For his own neck. He’ll trade me.” She looked down at her hands, at the broken thread still caught on her finger. “So you see? There wasn’t a choice. There was a lever. And he’s had his foot on it since 1942.”

A moth battered itself against the hot glass of the work-light. The tiny, frantic ticking filled the space between them.

Pauline’s expression had changed. The cold anger was still there, but it had fractured, revealing something underneath—a dawning, horrified comprehension. “The canary in the cage,” she said, almost to herself. “They didn’t put you there. You were already in it. You just didn’t tell anyone the door was locked.”

Iris said nothing. The confession had left her hollowed out, light-headed.

“Why tell me this now?” Pauline asked. The way she said it was low, stripped of accusation.

“Because you called me a collaborator.” Iris met her gaze. “And maybe I am. But not with them. Never with them. The collaboration… it was a long time ago. With people who didn’t make it. The ones who taught me how to hide a truth in a major seventh chord.” She gave a weak, hopeless gesture toward the dark theatre. “This. Acting. It’s the same thing isn’t it? You put on the mask so you can say the thing you’d be arrested for saying in the street.”

Pauline studied her for a long moment. The professional mask was gone. In its place was a weary, deep sadness. “It’s a dangerous game. Using their tools.”

“They’re the only tools I have left.”

From the distant street, a car horn blared, a harsh, alien sound. Pauline looked toward the sound, then back at Iris. The calculation had returned to her eyes, but it was different now. Sharper. “Denton’s money comes with strings. He’ll want oversight. He’ll want to see the script for Port Chicago.”

“I know.”

“He’ll want changes. Soften the edges. Make it more… palatable. A lesson in tragedy, not an accusation.”

“I know that, too.”

Pauline nodded, once. A decision made. “Then we give him a script to read. A very good, very palatable script.” A faint, grim smile touched her lips. “And we rehearse a different one.”

The meaning settled over Iris, heavy and clear. The risk of it was astronomical. It was also the only path forward that wasn’t surrender.

“He’ll be watching,” Iris said.

“Let him watch.” Pauline turned to leave, then paused. She didn’t look back. “The code you were practicing earlier. The one for a tone-deaf choir. Keep working on it. A good cipher needs a convincing key.” She walked into the wings, her footsteps fading into the waiting dark.

Iris was alone again with the quiet and the dust and the single, burning light. Her hands had stopped shaking.

The phone rang.

It sat on the small table by the window, a black, heavy thing. The sound was not a bell, but a mechanical drill, two short bursts, then a pause, then two more. Insistent. The streetlamp outside cast a jaundiced rectangle of light across the floorboards, stopping just short of the table’s leg. Iris watched from the armchair. She hadn’t moved since coming in, still in her dinner dress, the silk now cold against her skin. The smell of the restaurant—garlic, wax, other people’s perfume—clung to her. A single ice cube, forgotten in her water glass from hours ago had melted into a sad, translucent wafer.

The ringing stopped.

The silence felt heavier for the interruption. She could hear the faint, constant hum of the city, the distant whine of a streetcar on its rails. Then the phone began again. Same pattern. Two rings. Pause.

She stood. The movement was stiff, her muscles protesting. She crossed the room, her stockinged feet silent on the wood. Her hand hovered over the receiver. It was warm, she realized, from the last call. Someone had been trying for a while. She picked it up.

“Yes.”

“Miss Finch.” The voice was familiar. A dry, Midwestern baritone, stripped of any identifying warmth. It was the agent. He never gave a name. “I hope I’m not disturbing you.”

“It’s late.”

“It is.” A pause, the faint crackle of the line. “I understand you had a dinner engagement this evening. With a Mr. Charles Denton. And Miss Carter.”

The chill that moved through her wasn’t a wave. It was a slow seep, starting at the base of her spine and spreading outward, like ink in water. He knew. Of course he knew. Arthur would have told him. Or Denton himself, playing all sides. The thought was a flat, dull fact. She said nothing.

“A productive meeting, I trust?” he asked. The question hung there, a hook baited with nothing.

“It was a dinner.”

“Mr. Denton is a patriot. A great supporter of the arts. And of institutions that promote American values.” The agent’s tone was conversational, almost bored. “The Harlem Institute is such an institution wouldn’t you say?”

Iris looked at the window. A moth was beating itself against the glass, a tiny, frantic shadow in the lamplight. Its wings made a soft, papery sound. Tick, tick, tick.

“I teach acting there,” she said.

“You do.” The affirmation was a brick laid on her chest. “And in that capacity, you have a unique perspective. You see the… currents. The discussions. The political education that happens outside the curriculum.”

“They’re students. They talk about plays.”

“They talk about more than plays, Miss Finch.” The agent’s voice lost its veneer of idleness. It became a blade, thin and precise. “Miss Carter ensures they do. Her influence is considerable. Her philosophy. Her… connections.”

Iris’s throat tightened. She thought of Pauline’s finger tapping the wine glass. One, two. A silent beat. A signal to no one.

“I don’t know what you want me to say.”

“I want you to do the job we agreed upon.” The words were delivered without heat, a simple reminder of a transaction. “You are in a position to observe. To listen. Mr. Denton’s patronage is a valuable shield for the Institute. We need to understand what, exactly, it is shielding. Is it a genuine educational effort? Or is it a front for the dissemination of un-American, subversive thought?”

“You want me to inform on my colleagues.” The sentence tasted of ash.

“I want you to provide context. Clarity. For the good of the country.” He let that sit for a moment. The moth had stopped moving. It clung to the glass, wings splayed. “Your cooperation ensures your own situation remains… manageable. You understand the alternative.”

The photograph. Arthur’s file. The coded letters. The public ruin. The gilded cage door slamming shut forever. The images came not as a flood, but one by one, laid out like cards on a table.

“What specifically are you looking for?” she spoke—a stranger’s voice, cool and detached.

“Names. Of visitors to the Institute. Of guest speakers. Topics of discussion in Miss Carter’s private meetings. Any indication of ties to known communist fronts. The content of the play she is producing. Is it merely a historical drama, or is it propaganda?” He listed them off, a grocery list of betrayal. “Start with the play. Port Chicago. Obtain a copy of the script. Note any revisions, any speeches that may have been… augmented.”

Iris closed her eyes. The afterimage of the moth burned against her lids. She saw Pauline in the empty theatre, watching her from the wings. I heard.

“And if I can’t?”

The silence on the line was different now. It was a vacuum.

“Then you become part of the context we must clarify, Miss Finch.” The agent’s tone was final. “Your past is not a secret. It is a fact. We can treat it as a regrettable youthful indiscretion, a path corrected. Or we can treat it as evidence of an enduring character flaw. The choice, as always is yours.”

He didn’t say goodbye. The line went dead with a soft, definitive click.

Iris held the receiver for a long time, listening to the hollow silence of the disconnected line. It had its own sound, a faint, oceanic hiss. She finally placed it back in its cradle. The click echoed in the quiet room.

She walked to the window. The moth was gone. Only a smudge of grey dust on the glass remained, a ghost of its struggle. Outside, the street was empty. A newspaper skated down the pavement caught by a gust of wind, tumbling end over end until it wrapped itself around a lamppost and went still.

Her hand went to her throat. A habit. The canary, checking her instrument. The skin was smooth, warm. No tremor. She felt nothing at all. Just a vast, hollow quiet, as if the phone call had scooped her out from the inside, leaving only a shell of silk and careful posture.

She would have to ask Pauline for the script.

The thought arrived clean and simple, a knife slipped between her ribs. It wasn’t a decision. It was the next action in a sequence she had set in motion years ago, in a different city, with a different choice. She turned from the window. The rectangle of light lay across the floor where she had been standing, a pale, empty stage.
