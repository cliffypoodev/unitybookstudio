The rehearsal hall held the afternoon light at an angle, a solid bar of gold cutting across the dust. Iris stood in the center of it, the script limp in her hand. She wasn’t reading. The words were in her now, a cold sediment settled at the base of her throat. Mrs. Delaney’s lines. The manifest shows seventy-three. A clerk’s flourish. The ink was blue. She had written that last night, the pencil moving of its own accord. A hybrid thing. Now it was in her mouth, a taste like old pennies.

Across the room, Pauline leaned against the edge of the piano, arms folded. She wore a gray work shirt, sleeves rolled to the elbows. A button was missing near the collar. Iris focused on that small, dark gap in the fabric. It was easier than meeting her eyes.

“From the top of the monologue,” Pauline said. She spoke — flat, a conductor’s baton tapping a stand. “Just walk it. Don’t perform it.”

Iris nodded. The dust motes swirled in the slanted light, a miniature galaxy with no purpose. She closed her eyes. Not to remember the lines, but to find the silence between them. The silence where the tally marks lived. Five and one. Say the opposite of what you mean.

She began. Not with the script’s opening, but with her own inserted line. “The ink was blue.”

Her own voice sounded strange in the empty hall. Husked out. Not singing, not speaking. Something in between.

“I remember the blue. It wasn’t navy. It was a cheap blue, the kind that fades. They handed us the pens. The manifest was already typed. Our names in black. We signed in blue.” She took a step, her shoe scuffing the worn floorboards. A long scratch there, like a chalk line from some forgotten game. “Seventy-three. A prime number. You can’t divide it evenly. It just sits there, a lump. A fact.”

From the corner of her eye, she saw Pauline shift. A slight straightening of the spine.

“They talked about tonnage. They talked about capacity. They used words like ‘throughput’ and ‘logistical yield.’” Iris’s hand came up, not a gesture, but a slow press against her own sternum. “They never said ‘boys.’ They were loading munitions. The boys were loading the munitions. The manifest showed seventy-three souls. But the form, the official form, it had a different heading. ‘Units for transport.’”

She was off-script now. Deeply off. This wasn’t Mrs. Delaney. This was the cold knot in her own stomach, the one that had been there since Arthur slid the committee’s letter across the breakfast table. The same kind of bureaucratic blue ink. The same conversion of a life into a unit for processing.

“I checked the math,” Iris said, the words coming faster, a rhythmic patter like the bridge of an old torch song. “I always checked the math. The weight of the shells, the capacity of the hold. The numbers didn’t balance. There was a surplus. A weight unaccounted for. I brought it to my supervisor. He had a mustache, thin, like it was drawn on with that same blue ink. He said, ‘Delaney, your job is to record what goes in the hold. Not what comes out of it.’”

Her throat tightened. Not with staged emotion. With a memory that wasn’t hers and yet was entirely hers. The smell of Arthur’s hair tonic, sharp and medicinal. Your job is to give them a name, Iris. Just a name. Not a life.

“So I signed,” she whispered. The hall paused the sound. “I used the blue pen. I made the flourish. And that night, I went home and I washed my hands. I washed them until the skin cracked. But I could still see the blue. Under the nails. In the creases. A cheap blue that doesn’t fade.”

The silence that followed was different from the silence before. It was a held thing. A glass filled to the brim.

Iris opened her eyes. The bar of gold light had moved, climbing the far wall. It illuminated a water stain shaped like Italy. She stared at it, her breath coming in shallow pulls. Her hands were steady. That was the strange part.

Pauline hadn’t moved. Her face was in shadow, unreadable. Then she pushed off from the piano. The legs squeaked against the floor, a short, high protest. She walked toward Iris, her steps slow and deliberate on the old boards. She stopped a few feet away, just outside the bar of light.

“Where did that come from?” Pauline asked. The words came out low, stripped of its usual conductor’s edge.

“The script,” Iris said automatically, then shook her head. “No. It’s not. Not all of it.”

“I know it’s not all of it.” Pauline took another step, entering the light. Her eyes were dark, searching Iris’s face not for the character, but for the cracks in the performer. “The ink. The supervisor’s mustache. The washing of the hands. That’s not in the pages I gave you.”

“I wrote it down last night. It just… came out.”

“It came out where? From what?”

Iris looked away, back at the water-stain Italy. “From the same place the songs used to come from. A dark room. You just fumble around in it until your hand closes on something.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the only one I have.” The defiance in her own voice surprised her. It felt clean, a sharp edge after years of soft contours.

Pauline was silent for a long moment. She studied Iris as if she were a revised manuscript, puzzling over a marginal note. “It’s good,” she said finally, the words grudging. “It’s dangerous, but it’s good. It makes her complicit. It makes her human.”

“I thought that was the point.”

“The point is the story of Port Chicago. Not the story of Iris Finch.”

The air left Iris’s lungs. It was a direct hit, precisely aimed. “You think that’s what that was?”

“I don’t know what it was.” Pauline’s gaze dropped to the script in Iris’s hand, then back to her face. A new wariness there, a calculation. “It sounded like a confession looking for a crime.”

A cold laugh escaped Iris. It echoed thinly. “Maybe all acting is.”

“No.” Pauline’s reply was swift, absolute. “Acting is craft. It’s technique. It’s choosing what to reveal. What you just did… that wasn’t a choice. That was a leak.”

The word hung between them. A leak. As if her interior life was a pressurized system, corroding at the seams.

“You’re afraid,” Iris said, the realization dawning as she spoke. “You’re not unnerved. You’re afraid. Of what I might say when I stop choosing.”

Pauline’s jaw tightened. She didn’t deny it. She took a half-step back, out of the light again, retreating into the cooler shadow of the hall. “This play is a shield for this institute. For every artist in this building. It is not a platform for your personal catharsis.”

“And what if the two are the same thing?” Iris pressed. “What if the only way I can tell her truth is by telling a piece of mine?”

“Then you become the story.” Pauline’s voice was ice now. “And the story of Port Chicago, the story of those boys, becomes your backdrop. Your scenery. That’s not collaboration. That’s a takeover.”

Iris felt the truth of it, a sucker punch to the gut. That had always been her sin hadn’t it? Putting herself at the center. Her career, her safety, her survival. The Canary, always singing her own coded song. The urge to apologize rose, bitter in her throat. She hesitated it.

“You think I’d do that? Use their tragedy?”

“I think,” Pauline said, spacing the words with deliberate care, “that a woman who has spent a lifetime building a career on subtext might not know how to handle the plain text of someone else’s pain. She might try to fit it into her own key.”

It was the most Pauline had said to her in years that wasn’t purely logistical. It was also the cruelest. Because it was professional, not personal. A critique of her artistry, not her heart. And it was right.

From the hallway came the sound of the janitor’s cart, a wheel squeaking in a persistent, irregular rhythm. The mundane world, pushing back in. Pauline glanced toward the sound, then back at Iris. The moment broke.

“We’ll keep the new lines,” Pauline said, her tone shifting back to the director’s. “The ink, the hands. It’s strong. But from now on, you show me any revisions. Before you embody them. This isn’t your club. This is my theatre.”

She didn’t wait for an answer. She turned and walked toward the piano, gathering her own notes, a stack of papers clipped to a board. The conversation was over.

Iris stood in the bar of light, now warm and orange with the lowering sun. She looked at her hands. No blue ink. Just the faint tremor that was always there now, a tiny vibration beneath the skin, like a wire humming a note too low to hear. She had shown Pauline the leak. And in doing so, she had finally understood the source of the pressure. It wasn’t just the past. It was the fear that she was, at her core, incapable of telling any story but her own. A selfish, surviving thing. The canary, once a warning for others, now just a bird in a cage, singing to itself.

The silence stretched, thin and brittle. Iris kept her eyes on the water-stain Italy. The defiance that had sharpened her voice a moment ago now felt like a shard of glass in her palm—she didn’t know whether to clutch it or drop it.

Pauline didn’t move. “You’re rewriting the play.”

“I’m clarifying the subtext.” The words sounded hollow, a performer’s glib reply. She winced at her own tone.

“No. You’re grafting your own subtext onto it. That’s different.” Pauline’s voice was quiet, almost conversational. She turned and walked toward the hallway door, her footsteps echoing in the hollow space. She didn’t look back. “Come with me.”

It wasn’t an invitation. It was an instruction. Iris followed, the script dangling from her hand like a dead thing. The rehearsal hall door sighed shut behind them cutting off the bar of gold light. The institute corridor was long and dim, lit only by a single frosted sconce halfway down. The bulb buzzed, a low, steady insect hum. The floor was scuffed linoleum, the color of dried mustard. She focused on a black heel mark, a comma someone had dragged and forgotten.

Pauline stopped beneath the sconce, her face washed in its sickly yellow glow. She leaned against the wall, her shoulder pressing into a framed lithograph of some Greek tragedy. The glass was dusty. “That monologue. The ink, the hands. That’s not research. That’s memory.”

“It’s a character’s memory.”

“Whose?”

“Mrs. Delaney’s.”

“Iris.” Pauline’s voice held a flat, exhausted patience. “Don’t. Not here. These walls have ears, but they’re my ears. And I’m asking you. Where did it come from?”

The buzzing of the light seemed to grow louder. It vibrated in Iris’s back teeth. She thought of Arthur’s file, of the blue committee letterhead on her kitchen table. The conversion of a life into a unit. She saw the thin mustache of a man she’d never met. She saw Arthur’s hands, neatly stacking pages. “It’s the same process,” she said, the words leaving her before she could catch them. “Just different paperwork.”

Pauline’s eyes narrowed. something—not understanding, but recognition. A dark shape seen through fog. “What process?”

“The process of making a person into a problem. Then into a number. Then into a signature.” Iris looked down the empty corridor. At the far end, a mop and bucket stood sentinel by a fire door. “You asked me why I took this job. Arthur’s arrangement. You think it’s about money. Or saving face.”

“Isn’t it?”

“It’s about the paperwork.” The admission was a stone dropped into a well. She waited for the splash, but there was only the buzz of the light. “They have a file. He has a file. They want a name from me. A signature. In exchange, my file stays closed.”

Pauline went very still. The only movement was the slow tightening of her fingers around her own forearm. “A name.”

“One name. To start.”

“Mine.”

Iris didn’t answer. The heel mark on the floor seemed to pulse.

“Of course.” Pauline let out a short breath, not quite a laugh. It was a sound of cold, unsurprised finality. “The white woman with the government mandate. The perfect plant. Come to the radical Black institute, befriend the radical Black playwright, and deliver her up. Neat. Efficient. Good bureaucratic throughput.”

The words landed like blows. Iris flinched. “It’s not like that.”

“How is it not exactly like that?” Pauline pushed off from the wall. The Greek tragedy lithograph rattled. “You show up here, a ghost from the worst mistake of my life, handed a job by your husband-agent who’s in bed with the very people who want to burn this place to the ground. You talk about art, about truth. You rewrite my play with your own clever, coded pain. And all the while, you’re here to hand them a reason to shut our doors. To dismantle everything we’ve built. Because that’s what you do, Iris. You survive. You sing whatever song keeps the roof over your head.”

The truth of it was a physical weight, pressing down on Iris’s collarbone. She couldn’t breathe against it. “I didn’t know it was you. When Arthur set it up. I didn’t know.”

“But you know now.” Pauline’s voice dropped, low and fierce. “And you’re still here. Rehearsing. Rewriting. Playing your part.”

“I’m not playing a part.” The protest was weak, childlike.

“Aren’t you? What’s this, then?” Pauline gestured back toward the rehearsal hall. “More encryption? Another pretty, sad song where the real meaning is tucked between the notes for the clever people to find? That’s your specialty. Hiding in plain sight.”

Iris’s mouth was dry. The taste of old pennies was back. “It’s how I stayed alive.”

“It’s how you stayed safe. There’s a difference.” Pauline took a step closer. The yellow light caught the silver threads at her temples, the fine lines at the corners of her eyes that hadn’t been there in Paris. “This institute isn’t a supper club. We’re not hiding meaning in the bridge of a torch song for a few lonely queers to nod along to. We’re speaking it. Plain. Direct. Knowing full well it might get us shut down, or worse. Because some truths are too heavy for code. They need to be held in your bare hands, even if they burn you.”

She was right. Iris felt the rightness of it in her bones, a deep, shameful ache. She had always chosen the cipher over the cry. The metaphor over the fact. It had felt like art. It had been camouflage.

“So what’s your move, Canary?” Pauline asked, the old nickname a deliberate barb. “Do you give them my name? Save your own skin? Or do you walk out of here right now and tell your handler the target is too suspicious, the operation failed?”

“They already have everything they need on you,” Iris said, the realization forming as she spoke. “Your plays. Your speeches. Your associations. My testimony would just be… garnish. A personal touch. What they really want from me isn’t you.”

Pauline went still. “What do they want?”

“They want me to confirm what they already suspect about this place. That it’s a hub. A network. That the art is just the surface.” Iris’s mind was moving now, cold and clear. “They don’t need me to name you. They need me to validate the entire ecosystem. To stand in front of their committee and say, yes, that place is exactly what you fear it is. A single, credible, white voice to legitimize the purge. That’s the signature they want.”

The buzzing light filled the silence that followed. Pauline’s expression shifted, the anger draining away, replaced by a weary, calculating comprehension. She saw it, too. The larger machinery. Iris was not the knife. She was the seal of authenticity on the warrant.

“Then why,” Pauline asked slowly, “are you still rewriting my play? Why are you putting your own confession into Mrs. Delaney’s mouth?”

Iris had no answer. Or she had too many. Because the dark room of creation was the only place she felt real. Because the lines between her compromise and Mrs. Delaney’s were blurring. Because if she was going to be used as a seal, she wanted, in some small, stolen way, to press her own design into the wax.

“I don’t know,” she said, and it was the truest thing she’d said all day.

Pauline studied her for a long moment. The institutional hallway smelled of lemon polish and mildew. Somewhere, a pipe clanked. “The festival is in six weeks,” she said finally. She said it, all business now, the moment of raw confrontation sealed over. “The board is nervous. Funding is conditional. They hear whispers about HUAC interest. They want a patriotic medley for the finale. Something to prove we’re good Americans.”

“A medley.”

“Leo Strauss’s idea. The Broadway mogul. He’s on the funding committee. He suggested you could perform it. A redemption arc. The fallen star finding her voice in patriotic song.” Pauline’s lip curled. “It’s repulsive. But it’s the price of the stage for Port Chicago.”

Iris saw the trap, elegant and brutal. To secure the platform for Pauline’s dangerous truth, she would have to first perform a lie. A pretty, popular, patriotic lie. The irony was perfect. “And if I refuse?”

“Then Port Chicago doesn’t happen. The institute plays it safe with a comedy. And you…” Pauline shrugged. “You go back to your arrangement. You find another way to give them what they want.”

The two paths lay before her, both terrible. One required a public betrayal of everything she was just beginning to remember she valued. The other required a private one.

“I need to think,” Iris said.

“You don’t have the luxury of thinking.” Pauline turned to walk away, then paused. She didn’t look back. “The rehearsal tomorrow. Two o’clock. Be off-book. And Iris?”

“Yes?”

“Bring the version you wrote. The one with the blue ink.”

The apartment smelled of lemon polish and stale tobacco. Iris closed the door behind her, the click of the latch a final, soft sound. She leaned her forehead against the cool wood, eyes closed, the ghost of the rehearsal hall’s dust-mote light still swimming under her lids. Her hands smelled of pencil lead and the faint metallic tang of the old piano keys. A small, irrelevant splinter of memory: the scent of a specific brand of French cigarettes, Gauloises, on a rainy afternoon in a Left Bank bookshop. Nothing to do with anything. The memory evaporated, leaving only the lemon polish.

The living room was dim, the heavy damask drapes already drawn against the evening. A single lamp burned on the desk by the window, casting a tight pool of yellow light. Arthur sat within it, his back to her. He didn’t turn. The slow, deliberate sound of a page turning cut the silence.

She hung her coat on the stand by the door, the movements slow methodical. The wool was still damp from the walk from the subway. She could feel the weight of his attention, a physical pressure between her shoulder blades, though he hadn’t yet acknowledged her.

“You’re late,” he said, his voice even, conversational. Another page turned.

“Rehearsal ran long.” Her own voice sounded thin in the thick quiet. She walked to the sideboard, poured two fingers of gin into a cut-crystal glass. She didn’t add ice. The first swallow was a cold fire that did nothing to warm the chill in her stomach.

“Pauline working you hard is she?” The question was idle. Too idle.

Iris turned, glass in hand. He was leaning back in the leather desk chair now, facing her. The manila folder was open on the blotter before him. She could see the edges of photographs, the creamy corner of an envelope. Her file. Her insurance.

“It’s a challenging part.” She took another drink. The gin tasted like pine and regret.

“I should think so.” Arthur picked up a photograph held it to the light. He studied it as if it were a stock report. “A complicit bureaucrat. A woman who signs papers knowing they’ll lead to death. Not exactly the ‘Canary’s’ usual fare.”

He laid the photo down. From where she stood, Iris couldn’t see the image, only the white border. She didn’t need to see it. She knew them all. The one of her and Pauline on the Pont Neuf, wind in their hair. The one of her at a fundraiser, laughing with a union organizer who later vanished. The letters. The coded, silly letters.

“It’s just a play, Arthur.”

“Is it?” He tapped a finger on the open folder. “Your notes are interesting. The additions you’ve made. The ‘blue ink.’ The ‘washing of hands.’ Quite raw.” He said the word as if it were a clinical term. “It reads less like character motivation and more like… confession.”

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It settled as a tightness behind her ribs. “It’s called acting. You find parallels. You use them.”

“Parallels.” Arthur smiled, a thin stretching of lips that didn’t touch his eyes. He picked up a sheet of paper, not a photograph this time. It was a letter, the handwriting a familiar, looping scrawl. Pauline’s hand. 1937. “This one is particularly rich in parallels. Your little code. The tally marks. Five and one. ‘Danger.’ ‘Say the opposite.’ Quite inventive for two love-struck art students.”

The air left the room. The taste of gin turned to ash in her mouth. She hadn’t known he had that one. She’d thought those notes were lost, burned left in some Parisian gutter. Her hand tightened on the glass. “Where did you get that?”

“I’ve had it.” He set it down gently, aligning its edge with the blotter. “I’ve had all of it, Iris. Every scrap. Precisely organized. Date, sender, context. Cross-referenced.” He looked up at her, his gaze flat and assessing. “Do you understand what this represents? It’s not a pile of nostalgic trash. It’s a ledger. A record of every subversive thought, every questionable association, every deviation from the acceptable narrative. In the hands of the committee, this isn’t evidence of a personal life. It’s a roadmap of sedition.”

The lamp hummed, a low, electrical drone. In its light, she could see the individual pores on Arthur’s nose, the precise part in his thinning hair. A tiny thread had come loose from the arm of his suit jacket. She stared at it.

“You gave me to them,” she said. The words came out quiet, almost wondering. “The teaching job. It wasn’t a stopgap. It was the delivery. You handed them a name—Pauline, the institute—and you handed them me, to go and fetch it for them. And you kept this…” She gestured with her glass toward the folder. A drop of gin sloshed over the rim fell on the Persian rug. “… as the guarantee I’d behave.”

Arthur didn’t deny it. He leaned forward, elbows on the desk, steepling his fingers. “I protected you. I built the cage, yes. A gilded one, but a cage still. It kept you safe. It kept you working. It kept you from the gutter your… instincts… would have landed you in.” He nodded toward the folder. “That is the lock on the door. Without it, you’d have flown straight into the fire years ago. You should be thanking me.”

The hot, nameless thing in her chest solidified into a cold, hard knot. It sat just below her throat, making it difficult to breathe. She thought of Pauline’s missing button, the dark little gap in the gray shirt. A detail that served nothing. A flaw.

“What do you want?” She spoke, the sound a stranger’s.

“The play opens in three weeks. You will perform your part. Exactly as written. No more improvisations. No more ‘blue ink.’ You will be the prestigious name on the program, drawing the right kind of attention, providing the shield the institute needs to survive. And when the committee calls you again—and they will, after the premiere, when the spotlight is nice and bright—you will give them a name. One name. Someone peripheral at the institute. Someone expendable.”

He said it all in the same tone he might use to outline a touring schedule. Calm. Reasonable.

“And if I don’t?”

Arthur closed the folder. The sound was soft, final. He ran his hand over its beige surface, smoothing it. Then he looked at her, and for the first time that evening, his expression held something like genuine emotion. It was pity. “Then I open the cage door, Iris. And I throw this ledger into the wind. You will be unemployable. Unhousable. You will be a headline. ‘The Canary’s Communist Crotch.’ ‘The Red Songbird.’ They’ll dig up every boy you ever smiled at for the papers and call him a beard. Pauline’s institute will be shuttered within a month, her entire company investigated as a nest of subversives and deviants. And you…” He shrugged, a small, elegant movement. “You will have the freedom you seem to crave so much. The freedom of nothing left to lose.”

The silence that followed was not a held breath. It was the silence of a vault being sealed. The hum of the lamp was the only sound. Iris looked past him, out the gap in the drapes to the dark window. Her own reflection looked back, a pale smudge in the glass, holding a glass of gin in a room that smelled of lemons.

She finished the drink. The cold fire was gone; it was just a bitter liquid now. She set the empty glass on the sideboard with a precise click.

“I understand,” she said.

Arthur nodded satisfied. He opened a desk drawer, slid the folder inside, and turned the small brass key. The lock engaged with a solid, oiled click.

“Good,” he said. “Now. I’ve taken the liberty of scheduling a fitting for your costume tomorrow at ten. The designer is expecting you. Try to be on time.”
