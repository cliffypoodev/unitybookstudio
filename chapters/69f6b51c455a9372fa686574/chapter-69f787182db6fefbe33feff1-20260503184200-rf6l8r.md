The diner near Columbus Circle smelled of burnt coffee and fried onions, a greasy, familiar scent that clung to the back of the throat. Iris sat in a booth by the window, the afternoon light a weak, watery yellow on the Formica. She watched a pigeon on the sidewalk outside, its head jerking in that frantic, mechanical rhythm. It pecked at nothing, just a crack in the cement. Her thumb traced the rim of the water glass in front of her. The glass was cool, smudged.

Agent Cross slid into the booth opposite her. He didn’t remove his overcoat. He placed a small notebook on the table, identical to the one he’d given her, and a fountain pen beside it. The pen was black, expensive-looking. He nodded at her, a pleasant, empty gesture. “Miss Finch. I trust you’re well.

“I’m fine.” Her voice came out flat. She took a sip of the water. It tasted of chlorine.

“And the institute? You’ve settled in?

“I’ve attended. Settled is a different thing.

“Of course.” He smiled. It didn’t reach his eyes, which were the color of dishwater. “Your first report. I’ve read it.

He waited. The waitress shuffled past, her shoes squeaking on the linoleum. The sound set Iris’s teeth on edge. She remembered a pair of shoes she’d owned in ’42, black patent leather with a strap. They’d pinched her left little toe, always. She’d worn them anyway, for a photograph in Life magazine. A useless memory. It floated to the surface now, for no reason.

“It was… thorough in its way,” Cross said. He opened his notebook. He didn’t look at it. “A list of play titles. Rehearsal schedules. The dimensions of the stage. Very precise.

“You asked for observations.”

“I did.” He tapped the page with a fingernail. Clean, trimmed. “These are observations of a stage manager. Or a janitor. I need observations of a… witness.

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It tightened her throat. She made herself look at him. “I don’t know what you want me to say.

“Names, Miss Finch. Associations. Who speaks to whom. What they speak about. The mood. Is there… discontent? Against the country that provides their stage?” His voice was low, reasonable. A teacher explaining a simple concept to a slow child. “You’re an artist. You understand subtext. Listen for it.

“They’re putting on a play. The Glass Menagerie.

“A play about delusion and abandonment. Arguably unpatriotic in its pessimism. But that’s not the one that interests me.” He leaned forward, just an inch. The smell of his hair tonic, something sharp and medicinal, cut through the grease. “Pauline Carter is working on something new. Isn’t she.

It wasn’t a question. Iris’s mouth went dry. She took another sip of water. The chlorine taste was stronger. “She’s always working on something.

“This is specific. A new work. You must have heard whispers.

She hadn’t. Not a whisper. Pauline had given her the two scripts and then retreated behind a wall of professional ice. The only words between them had been about Martha, about lies, about cost. “I’m not in her confidence.

“But you see her. You’re in the room.” Cross picked up his pen. He didn’t write. He just held it, balanced between his fingers. “Find out. That’s your assignment. The nature of this new project. Its themes. Its intended audience. And the names of everyone involved in its development. Especially any… external collaborators.

External collaborators. The phrase hung in the air, a grey, shapeless cloud. It meant donors. It meant people with money who might have the wrong kind of politics. Iris felt a cold knot form under her ribs. She thought of the bank draft clue she was supposed to plant, the mundane detail. It felt like swallowing glass.

“She received a bank draft,” Iris said. The words felt thick. “For the institute. I saw it on her desk. The Federal Mercantile Trust. It was just lying there.

Cross’s eyes flickered. A tiny, almost imperceptible reaction. He wrote something in his notebook. A single line. “Federal Mercantile. Good. That’s the kind of detail we need. A thread to pull.” He looked up. “Who else uses that bank? In your circles.

“I don’t know. It’s a bank.

“Think, Miss Finch. Your husband’s producer. Leo Strauss. Does he use Federal Mercantile?

The twist landed quietly, a stone dropped into deep water. Leo Strauss. The Broadway producer Langston was courting for her comeback. Cross knew his name. Knew it and connected it to a bank in the same breath. The world narrowed to the grain of the Formica table, a tiny chip near her elbow shaped like a crescent moon.

“I have no idea where Leo banks,” she said. Her voice sounded distant, someone else’s.

“Find that out, too. Indirectly. A casual question to Langston.” Cross closed his notebook. The conversation was over. He stood, buttoned his coat. “Your next report. More than set design. We’re not interested in the furniture. We’re interested in the foundation.” He placed a dollar bill under his unused water glass. “Good afternoon.

He walked out. The bell above the door jingled, a cheap, tinny sound. Iris sat there. The pigeon was gone. A man in a hat walked past the window, his reflection a blur. She finished her water, the chlorine now a permanent taste on her tongue. She counted the squares in the ceiling tile above her head. Thirty-six. One was stained brown, an old water leak. It looked like a map of a country she’d never visit.

Langston was on the telephone when she let herself into the apartment. His voice, usually so polished and projecting, was a low, unfamiliar murmur from the study. She stopped in the hallway, her coat still on. The radiator hissed. She heard the soft thump of her own heart in her ears.

“…understand the position, but the assurances need to be concrete…” A pause. “The Carter material is the leverage, yes, but I need to know my other clients are… insulated. That’s the term.

Iris’s breath caught. She stood very still, a statue in the dim hall. The floorboard under her right foot creaked, a tiny, betraying sound.

The murmuring stopped. A beat of silence. Then the click of the receiver being cradled, too careful, too soft.

She took off her coat, hung it up. She made noise doing it. When she walked into the study, Langston was at his desk, writing in a ledger. He looked up, his face a smooth mask of mild concern. “Darling. How did it go?

“It went.” She walked to the sideboard, poured a gin. She didn’t measure it. The first swallow was a fire that burned away the taste of chlorine, the smell of onions. “He wants names. Moods. Subtext.

“Of course he does. That’s his job.” Langston set down his pen. He had a new one, she noticed. A sleek, silver fountain pen. It hadn’t been there yesterday. “You’ll have to give him something. Nothing damning. Just enough to keep him satisfied. The color of the walls, the temperature of the room. That sort of thing.

“He asked about Leo Strauss.”

A flicker. Just a flicker, behind his eyes. Then it was gone. “Leo? Why on earth?

“He mentioned a bank. Federal Mercantile Trust. He wondered if Leo used it.

Langston leaned back in his chair. The leather sighed. “A odd question. I bank with Chase. Leo uses… I believe it’s Chemical Bank. Or maybe Manufacturers Hanover. I’d have to check.” He studied her. “Why would Cross care about Leo’s bank?

“I don’t know.” She took another drink. The gin was doing its work, spreading a warm, false calm through her limbs. “He said ‘external collaborators.’ He’s looking for the money behind Pauline’s new play.

“Ah.” Langston nodded, as if this explained everything. “Well. That’s not your concern. Your concern is your own position. And our dinner with Leo tonight. He’s excited. He has an idea for a vehicle for you. A patriotic revue. Songs of American spirit. It could be a lifeline, Iris. A real one.

A patriotic revue. The words were like stones in her stomach. She saw it instantly—the flags, the smiling chorus, her own face smiling back, singing empty words about heartland and home. A performance of belonging. The ultimate lie.

“I see,” she said.

“It’s not art,” Langston said, reading her silence. “It’s commerce. And commerce is what pays for this apartment. For the gin in your glass. Remember that.

He went back to his ledger. The silver pen flashed in the lamplight. Iris finished her drink. The glass was empty. She traced its rim, around and around, until the pad of her thumb went numb.

The apartment held a different kind of quiet than the institute. It wasn’t the silence of a held breath, but the dense, muffled quiet of good wool carpets and thick drapes, a quiet money could buy. Iris stood in the living room, her coat still on. The radiator ticked. A faint scent of lemon oil and last night’s gin hung in the air.

Langston was at the sideboard, pouring two glasses. The clink of crystal was precise, a sound he’d practiced. He didn’t turn. “How was the meeting?

“It was a meeting.” She shrugged out of her coat, let it drape over the back of the sofa. The fabric was cold against her neck.

“Helpful?”

“He wants names.”

Langston turned then, a glass in each hand. His smile was a thin, professional curve. “Well. That’s the job, isn’t it? The whole point of the exercise.” He handed her a glass. The gin was the good stuff, crisp and clean. It tasted like nothing. “Did you give him any?

“I gave him a bank.”

“A bank.” He took a sip, watching her over the rim. His eyes were the color of weak tea. “That’s not a name, Iris. That’s an institution. Institutions don’t have political affiliations. People do.

She walked to the window. The streetlights were coming on, casting long, distorted shadows from the fire escapes across the way. Someone had left a pot of dead geraniums on a windowsill three floors down. The brown, curled leaves rattled in a faint breeze. A stupid thing to notice. “He mentioned Leo Strauss. Asked if Leo used that bank.

A pause. She heard the soft swallow of his drink behind her. “Why would he ask that?

“I don’t know, Langston. You tell me. You’re the one bringing him into our living room.

“He’s a producer. He’s opportunity. That’s all.” His voice was smooth, unperturbed. She heard him set his glass down on the marble top of the sideboard. The sound was a period at the end of a sentence. “What names did he want, specifically?

“Anyone working on Pauline’s new project. External collaborators.” The gin was a cold line in her stomach. “He called them that.

“Then give him some.”

She turned. He was looking at her, his head tilted just so, the way he looked at a balance sheet that didn’t quite add up. “I don’t know any. She hasn’t told me a damn thing.

“You’re in the room. Listen. You have ears.” He picked up his glass again, swirled the liquid. The ice cubes clicked, a brittle, lonely sound. “It doesn’t have to be verified. A whisper. A suspicion. Something for him to chase. That’s what he wants—a place to start digging. You give him a name, he’s occupied. He leaves you alone.

“And what happens to the name?”

“That’s not your concern.” He said it gently, as if explaining a basic fact to a child. “Your concern is keeping your head down and your name out of the newspapers. Our concern.

Something hot and sour rose in her throat. Not anger. Something more like shame, but colder, metallic. “You sound like him.

“I sound like a realist.” He finished his drink. “We have dinner with Leo tomorrow. At the Stork Club. Be charming. Be the Canary. He’s talking about a revue. Something patriotic, uplifting. It could be a lifeline, Iris. A very public, very clean lifeline.

“A revue.” The word felt silly in her mouth, a bauble.

“Songs. Sketches. A celebration of the American spirit. It’s what people want to hear right now. It’s safe.” He came to stand beside her at the window, not touching. His reflection in the dark glass was a ghost over the geraniums. “Give Cross a name. Any name. The stage manager. The boy who sweeps the floor. It gets him off your back. It lets you focus on the work that actually matters.

“The sweeping patriotic revue.”

“The work that keeps a roof over our heads.” His voice had an edge now, fine as a razor. “You think I enjoy this? This… maneuvering? It’s necessary. It’s hygiene. You clean the wound so it doesn’t fester.

The telephone rang.

The sound was a shock in the quiet room, a sharp, insistent jangle. Langston’s reflection went very still. Then he moved, a quick, efficient turn. “I’ll get it.

He crossed the room, picked up the receiver on the fourth ring. “Langston Finch.” His voice was different. Lower, flatter. The pleasant, empty charm was gone. “Yes. Go ahead.

Iris stayed at the window. She watched the dead flowers tremble. She took a slow sip of gin, let it burn.

“Understood,” Langston said. A long pause. She could hear the faint, tinny rasp of a voice on the other end, but no words. “No, she hasn’t. Not yet. She’s being… cautious.” Another pause. His back was to her, rigid. “The Carter letters will suffice for now. They establish the pattern. The connection.

The Carter letters.

The words dropped into the silence of the room and sank. Iris felt them hit the bottom of something inside her. Her hand tightened around the glass. The cold seeped into her palm.

“I’ll have more after the dinner tomorrow,” Langston said. His voice was a confidential murmur now. “Strauss is eager. I think we can… align incentives.” He listened. “Of course. Goodbye.

He hung up. The click was final. He stood there for a moment, his hand on the receiver. Then he turned. His face was a pleasant mask again, but his eyes were bright, alert. “That was Leo’s assistant. Confirming tomorrow. Eight o’clock.

“Who were you talking to?”

“Leo’s assistant. I just said.

“The first part. Before that.” Her own voice sounded strange to her, calm and very far away. “You said ‘the Carter letters will suffice.

Langston’s smile didn’t waver. It just looked painted on. “A figure of speech. Regarding some old contracts. Nothing for you to worry about.” He walked back to the sideboard, picked up the gin bottle. “Top you up?

“No.”

“Suit yourself.” He poured himself another two fingers. He didn’t look at her. “Get some rest, Iris. Tomorrow’s important. We need you sharp.

She set her half-finished glass on the windowsill. The gin caught the faint light, a pool of amber against the darkening glass. She walked past him, toward the hall. Her footsteps were soundless on the carpet. At the doorway, she stopped. She didn’t turn.

“The boy who sweeps the floor,” she said. “His name is Elijah.

A beat of silence.

“Good,” Langston said. His voice was warm again, approving. “That’s a start.

The steakhouse was all dark wood and brass, the air thick with the smell of seared meat and expensive cigars. A low murmur of conversation filled the space, the clink of cutlery a soft, constant percussion. Iris sat with her back to the wall, a habit from her club days. It let her see the room. Langston was to her left, leaning forward, his face animated in a way it never was at home. Across the table, Duke Strauss dominated the space.

He was a large man, not fat, but solid, with a head of silver hair combed straight back. His hands were broad, a gold signet ring on his pinkie. He gestured with his fork as he spoke, a piece of steak speared on the end. “The appetite is for reassurance, Langston. For pageantry. The war’s over, but the boys are still jumpy. The girls want to see a future that sparkles.” He took the bite, chewed thoughtfully. “A revue. Patriotic, but with legs. A celebration of the American spirit.

Langston nodded, a quick, eager motion. “A unifying theme. Iris’s voice, of course, as the anchor. Something familiar, but refreshed.

“Exactly.” Strauss pointed the fork at Iris. “You’ve got the quality. That wounded bird thing. It worked during the war. Now we sand off the rough edges, make it shine. Give ‘em hope wrapped in a pretty melody.

Iris took a sip of her martini. The gin was good, crisp, but it left a medicinal aftertaste on her tongue. She watched a waiter across the room balance three plates up his arm, his face a mask of concentrated calm. The buttons on his jacket were brass, like everything else. One was loose, dangling by a thread. She focused on that tiny, swaying disc of metal.

“I’m not sure my voice is suited to pageantry,” she said. Her thumb found the stem of her glass. “It’s not a brass band.

“It’s what we make it,” Strauss said, not unkindly. It was a statement of fact. “Arrangements. Orchestration. We’ll get the best. You stand in the spotlight, you sing ‘God Bless America’ with a tear in your eye, and every mother in the audience sees her son coming home. Every father feels his sacrifice was worth it. It’s not about your range, darling. It’s about the symbol.

The word hung in the air. Symbol. She was to become a signpost, pointing backward to a feeling that may never have existed. A flat third of nostalgia, missing the resolution. She felt a familiar, cold hollowness open up beneath her ribs. It wasn’t new. It was the same space that had been there for years, since the charts started slipping, since the nods became polite instead of rapturous. They were just proposing to fill it with a different kind of sawdust.

Langston touched her wrist under the table. A brief, proprietary press of fingers. “Iris has a remarkable ability to connect with an audience’s emotional core,” he said to Strauss. “That’s the asset. We contextualize it.

“Context.” Strauss smiled, showing good teeth. “I like that. We’re selling context.” He signaled the waiter for another bourbon. “Now, the institute. This teaching gig. How’s that factoring in? Good PR? The chanteuse giving back?

The shift was so smooth it took a second to register. Iris’s skin prickled. She kept her eyes on the dangling button.

“It’s early days,” Langston said. His voice was easy, but his foot brushed hers under the table—a warning tap. “Exposure to new material. A different discipline. It keeps her sharp.

“Pauline Carter runs the place, doesn’t she?” Strauss took his fresh drink, swirled the ice. “Brilliant woman. Radical, but brilliant. Did a piece on her in the Times last year. All about ‘theatre as a weapon.’” He chuckled, a dry sound. “Dangerous talk these days. But it gets attention.

“You know her work?” Iris asked. The question was out before she could stop it.

“I know of it. We move in different circles. Her crowd is… ideological. Mine is commercial.” He took a sip. “But money is money. I hear she’s got a new project brewing. Something big. Controversial, maybe. That’s the rumor.

The knot under Iris’s ribs tightened. She saw Cross’s dishwater eyes, heard his reasonable voice. Find out. And here it was, served with a bourbon chaser. A rumor at a steakhouse.

“I haven’t heard,” she said.

“No? Well, you’re in the room, like they say.” Strauss studied her, his gaze assessing, not personal. It was the look a man gave a racehorse. “If it’s got legs, maybe there’s a conversation. After the revue, of course. One thing at a time. But a property with heat… that’s always worth knowing about.

Langston was watching Strauss, a calculating light in his eyes. “Is there heat?

“Potential heat. The right—or wrong—kind. Depends on the angle.” Strauss leaned back, patted his stomach. “Let’s just say certain parties are watching. A play about a munitions explosion that killed a bunch of Negro sailors? In this climate? It’s either a masterstroke or professional suicide.” He smiled again. “I’d bet on Pauline for the former. She’s never been afraid of a flame.

The twist arrived not with a bang, but with the quiet click of a puzzle piece sliding into a place she hadn’t known was empty. Port Chicago. The munitions explosion. July ‘44. She’d read a snippet in the paper, backstage somewhere. A vague report about an accident. She’d been learning the lyrics to ‘The Last Goodbye’ that week. The memory was a sudden, sharp smell of hairspray and nervous sweat.

And Duke Strauss knew about it. Knew it was Pauline’s project. Knew it was controversial. He wasn’t just a commercial producer. He was a man who made it his business to know which way the political winds blew, to gauge the temperature of a flame. His interest wasn’t artistic. It was intelligence.

“Sounds like the kind of thing the Committee would love to dissect,” Langston said lightly, but his knuckles were white around his whiskey glass.

“Indeed.” Strauss’s eyes met Langston’s, and something passed between them, a silent transaction Iris couldn’t decipher. “All the more reason for our project to be unimpeachably bright, shiny, and red, white, and blue. A counter-narrative. A safe harbor.” He turned his smile back to Iris. It was warm, professional, utterly vacant. “You’ll be the harbor, darling. What do you say?

The waiter with the dangling button passed by again. This time, the thread snapped. The brass button hit the carpet with a soft, almost inaudible tink. It rolled in a tiny, wobbly circle before coming to rest under a neighboring table. No one else noticed.

Iris felt the cold hollowness fill with something else—a slow, heavy dread, the consistency of cold oil. She was not just an informant. She was to be the foil. The patriotic revue wasn’t just a comeback. It was a strategic move in a game she was only beginning to see the edges of. Langston and Strauss weren’t just planning a show. They were constructing an alibi.

“It sounds like a very clear concept,” she heard herself say. Her voice was smooth, untroubled. The voice of the Canary.

“Good.” Strauss raised his glass. “To clear concepts. And full houses.

They drank. The gin tasted like nothing at all.

Later, in the taxi heading uptown, Langston was quiet. The city lights slid across his face in yellow streaks. He stared out the window, his profile sharp against the dark.

“He knows about the play,” Iris said. Her own voice sounded strange in the close space.

“He knows everything. That’s his job.” Langston didn’t look at her.

“You set that up. You wanted him to bring it up.

Finally, he turned. His expression was weary, stripped of its dinner-party animation. “I wanted to know where he stood. Now I do. He’s watching. Which means we need to be careful. You need to be careful. Your reports to Cross… they need to be bland. Uninteresting. Pauline’s play is a dead end. You tell him it’s gossip, nothing concrete.

“And if it’s not?”

“Then you didn’t hear it from me.” He looked back out the window. “Strauss is a potential investor in the revue, Iris. A major one. That’s the priority. That’s our way out of this. You give Cross his pablum, you do your job at that institute, and you stand on that stage and you shine. That’s the deal.

That’s the deal. Not the one with Cross. The other one. The one between him and Strauss, with her as the commodity being traded. The taxi hit a pothole, jolting them both. A loose coin rattled in the door well.

She didn’t answer. She watched the numbered streets climb, each block a step further from the dark wood and brass, closer to their silent apartment. She thought of the button, lost under the table. A small, useless thing, severed from its purpose. She wondered if the waiter would ever find it, or if it would just be swept away in the morning with the crumbs and the ash.
