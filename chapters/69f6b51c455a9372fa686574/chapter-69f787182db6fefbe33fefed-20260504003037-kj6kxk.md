The gin in the toothbrush glass was warm, a mistake she’d made before leaving the apartment. It had seemed a practical measure, a way to smooth the edges of the evening before they could even form. Now, backstage, with the taste of stale smoke and cold cream in her mouth, the warmth felt medicinal, a dose for a condition without a name.

Applause drifted through the velvet curtain, a polite patter like distant rain. It was for the pianist, not for her. She could tell by its rhythm—three distinct clusters, then a trailing off. A courtesy. Iris set the glass down on the dressing table, careful not to let it click against the wood. The mirror showed her a woman in a beaded dress the color of a day-old bruise, the careful architecture of her hair beginning to list slightly to port. A hairpin, sharp as a nail, pressed against her scalp. She left it. The minor pain was a coordinate, a way to remember where her body was in the room.

Out front, the supper club was a study in polite decline. She’d seen the house from the wing—the empty tables like missing teeth, the way the candlelight pooled in the centers of the white cloths but left the corners in a woolly grey. They’d come for the ghost of ‘The Canary,’ for the memory of a voice that had crackled through wartime radios, a voice that promised a sorrow you could dance to. What they got was Iris Finch, forty-seven, her breath a little short on the sustained notes, her smile a practiced maneuver. She’d sung ‘The Last Goodbye.’ It had felt less like a performance and more like an exhumation.

A draft, cold and smelling of the alley, snaked under the door. It carried the distant clatter of the El train, a sound like shaking a box of cutlery. For a moment, it wasn’t New York at all, but the rattle of a Paris métro car in ’37, the smell of wet wool and Gauloises. The memory had no point. It was just there, then gone, leaving a hollow behind her ribs.

Langston’s footsteps were obvious—a measured, heel-to-toe cadence that managed to sound both brisk and weary. He appeared in the mirror behind her, a tall man in a perfectly tailored overcoat, his face a mask of benign concern that had long ago etched itself into his features.

“They loved you, darling,” he said. His voice was smooth, a broadcaster’s baritone. He didn’t meet her eyes in the glass. Instead, he looked at the array of creams and powders, his gaze touching each item as if taking inventory.

“Did they.” It wasn’t a question. She watched her own thumb trace the rim of the empty glass. Around and around. The gin’s warmth had sunk into a low, dull buzz behind her eyes.

“The sentiment is what they pay for. Nostalgia’s a durable commodity.” He produced a silver case and offered her a cigarette. She shook her head. He lit one for himself, the match flare bleaching his knuckles white for an instant. “A gentleman waited for me after the set. A Mr. Cross. From Washington.”

The name meant nothing. Washington was a place of monuments and memos, irrelevant to the small ecosystem of keys and minor chords she inhabited. She picked up a pot of rouge, studied its cracked porcelain lid. A hairline fracture ran from the edge to the little gilt rose in the center. She’d had it for a decade.

“He’s with the House Committee on Un-American Activities. He’d like a word.”

The air in the room changed. It wasn’t that it got colder. It got thinner, as if someone had opened a valve. The hollow behind her ribs expanded. She set the rouge pot down, perfectly aligned with the edge of the table.

“What could I possibly have to say to him?”

“I think it’s more about what he has to say to you.” Langston exhaled a plume of smoke that curled like a question mark between them. “He’s in the booth at the back. The dark one. It’s a courtesy, Iris. A private conversation is always preferable to a public summons.”

There was a logic to it, a terrible, seamless logic. She stood. The beaded dress whispered, a sound like falling sand. She didn’t look at Langston as she passed him, but she caught his scent—bay rum, the faint, sweet odor of the hair tonic he used, the wool of his coat. For a dizzying second, she wanted to lean into that familiarity, to let the ordinary smells anchor her. She walked to the door, her shoulders held in the careful, upright posture of a performer exiting a stage.

The club was darker now. A busboy moved between the tables, clearing glasses with a muffled clink. In the rear booth, a man sat alone. The candle on his table was unlit. He was neither old nor young, with a face that seemed assembled from neutral parts—brown hair receding neatly, a grey suit that was neither cheap nor expensive. He stood as she approached, a gesture of old-fashioned politeness.

“Miss Finch. Phillip Cross. Thank you for your time.” His handshake was dry, brief. His voice was quiet, devoid of regional accent, a voice designed to be forgotten.

She slid into the booth opposite him. The leather was cold through the silk of her dress. He resumed his seat, his hands folded on the table. They were clean, the nails trimmed short. No wedding band.

“Your husband explained the nature of my work?”

“He said Washington.”

“The Committee takes an interest in the cultural health of the nation. In these times, the arts are a frontline. Ideas are contagious.” He said it like a doctor discussing germ theory. “We’re aware of your. Historical associations. Paris. The late thirties. A rather bohemian scene.”

A laugh, dry and brittle, escaped her. “I sang in cafés. I was twenty-two.”

“You were acquainted with a number of individuals who have since been identified as subversive. Writers. Artists. Pauline Carter, for instance.”

The name hung in the air between them. He’d said it so casually, as if mentioning a mutual acquaintance from a party. Iris’s mouth was very dry. The taste of the gin was gone, replaced by something metallic.

“That was a long time ago.”

“Miss Carter is currently the director of the Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts. A workshop theater. It receives certain. Sympathies. The Committee is interested in the atmosphere there. The ideas being rehearsed, so to speak.” He paused, his eyes on her face. They were a pale, watery blue. “You have a unique opportunity. A chance to be of service. To put past. Ambiguities to rest.”

“I’m a singer.”

“Your career has seen better seasons.” There was no cruelty in the statement. It was an observation of weather. “The management here, the radio sponsors—They’re nervous. A whisper of ‘moral turpitude’ can be remarkably persistent. It closes doors. Permanently.”

The busboy passed by again, his tray laden with empty highball glasses. One of them had a smudge of lipstick on its rim, a perfect crescent of faded red. Iris focused on that. The specific, meaningless detail of it.

“What is it you’re asking me to do?”

“Spend time at the institute. Observe. A teaching position has been arranged. You’ll report on the discussions, the readings, the political tenor of the place. Nothing onerous. A monthly conversation.”

“You want me to inform on them.”

“I want you to help your country clarify its cultural landscape.” He leaned forward, just an inch. The candle between them remained unlit. “The alternative is a formal hearing. Your name in the papers. The details of your personal life, your past affiliations, become a matter of public record. The Lavender Scare, they’re calling it. It has a certain ring. It ruins lives, Miss Finch. This? This is a compromise.”

A compromise. The word sat there, ugly and final. She saw Langston then, reflected in the dark mirror of the club’s window. He stood at the bar, nursing a drink, watching them. Not with worry, but with the tense posture of a broker awaiting the closing of a difficult deal. He’d known. Of course he’d known. He’d probably brokered the terms.

Something moved through her chest then—not quite fear, not quite anger. It was colder than both, a slow, heavy seep, like groundwater finding a crack in a foundation. She looked back at Cross. His expression was patient, expectant. He was a man used to waiting for the inevitable.

“I don’t know anything about theater.”

“You know about performance,” he said. “That’s all this is.” He shifted in his seat, the leather sighing under his weight. He placed a manila folder on the table between them but didn’t open it. His hands rested on either side of it, fingers laced. They were clean, unremarkable hands. “You had a good set tonight.”

“It was a set.” She lowered the words, distant, someone else’s.

“I’m a fan of your work. ‘The Last Goodbye’ was a comfort to a lot of people. My sister played it constantly. She lost her husband at Anzio.” He said this with a flat, factual tone, as if reading from an index card. There was no grief in it, just a statement of utility. “Art serves a purpose. It can unify. It can also. Confuse.”

The busboy passed again, a shadow with a tray. The smell of stale beer and lemon polish rose from the damp cloth he carried. Iris focused on a water stain on the ceiling above Cross’s head. It was shaped like Italy, the boot kicking at Sicily. She’d noticed it during a soundcheck in ’44. Some things didn’t change.

“Confusion isn’t my specialty, Mr. Cross.”

“No. Your specialty is communication. A particular talent for it.” He tilted his head slightly. “We’re interested in communication. In clarity. There are institutions in this city that foster confusion. That trade in ambiguity. They become echo chambers for un-American sentiment.”

He waited. She said nothing. Her throat had tightened, a slow, involuntary cinching. She could still breathe, but each breath felt shallow, insufficient.

“The Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts,” he said. The words lay there, neutral. “You’re familiar.”

A memory surfaced, unbidden: a review in the Amsterdam News last fall, tucked beside Langston’s coffee cup. He’d pointed to it with his butter knife. Carter’s new workshop a haven for radical verse, or something like that. She’d only registered the name. Pauline. The butter had been cold, congealing in yellow beads on the knife. She’d pushed her plate away.

“I know of it.”

“Its director, Pauline Carter is a person of interest. Her associations are problematic. Her art is. Polemical.” He unlaced his fingers, tapped the folder once. “The atmosphere there is what concerns us. The conversations. The loyalties. We need someone with your ear. Someone who understands subtext.”

The cold from the leather seeped deeper. It reached her bones. “You want me to spy.”

“We want you to observe. And report. A cultural liaison.” His voice never changed pitch. “You have a unique opportunity. The institute is seeking a voice coach. A temporary position. Your husband has already made the necessary introductions. They’re eager to have you.”

Langston. Of course. The brisk footsteps, the inventory of her makeup. He hadn’t been assessing her state. He’d been assessing the assets. Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It settled behind her sternum, a hard, indigestible lump.

“And if I’m not eager?”

Cross leaned forward, just an inch. The candlelight from the distant tables finally caught his eyes. They were a pale, watery grey, the color of a winter sky an hour before snow. “Miss Finch, your career has entered a twilight phase. That’s not a criticism. It’s a lifecycle. The supper club circuit is. Genteel. But your security depends on certain licenses. Broadcast licenses. Cabaret cards. The goodwill of networks who are themselves under scrutiny.” He paused, letting the hum of the club’s failing refrigeration unit fill the space. “There are also personal matters. Your time in Paris in the thirties. The company you kept. It was a different time. People were. Experimental. The records are fragmentary, of course. But fragments can be arranged to tell a story. A story of moral turpitude.”

The word hung in the air. Turpitude. It sounded like something that would stain velvet.

“It was twelve years ago.”

“Time doesn’t erase fact. It just makes people less careful with the evidence.” He opened the folder. He didn’t turn it toward her. He looked down at it, his expression one of mild administrative focus. “You have a choice. You can help us foster clarity at the institute. A few reports, your professional impressions. A service to your country. In return, your various licenses are renewed without issue. Your past remains a private matter. Or,” he said, and closed the folder with a soft, final click, “you can decline. And we will pursue clarity through other, more public, avenues. The choice is yours. But it is,” he added, almost gently, “a choice that requires an answer.”

The door to the kitchen swung open. A laugh, bright and sharp, sliced through the smoky air, then was cut off as the door sighed shut. Iris watched a thread come loose from the cuff of her dress. A single gold bead trembled at its end, catching the light for a second before going dark.

Langston materialized at the edge of the booth. He’d been waiting, a discreet distance away. He smiled, a professional curve of the lips. “Phillip. Iris. I trust we’ve reached an understanding?”

Cross stood. He gave a small, neat nod. “I believe we have. The details are arranged. Miss Finch begins at the institute on Monday. Ten a. M.” He looked at her, and for a fraction of a second, his gaze wasn’t administrative at all. It was weary, almost apologetic. Then it was gone. “Thank you for your time. And for the music.”

He walked away, his footsteps silent on the carpet. He didn’t look back.

Langston slid into the booth, into the warmth Cross had left behind. He reached for her hand. His skin was warm, dry. She let her fingers lie limp in his grasp. “It’s the best possible outcome, darling. A teaching position. Respectable. And it keeps the wolves from the door. We’ve turned a threat into an opportunity.”

She looked at him. Really looked. She saw the tiny web of lines at the corners of his eyes that hadn’t been there five years ago. She saw the way his collar was ever so slightly frayed, a secret he’d never allow to show during daylight hours. He was a man negotiating the terms of his own obsolescence, and he’d just brokered a deal that included her as a condition of sale.

“An opportunity,” she repeated. The words tasted of nothing.

“You’ll see. It might be good for you. A change of scene.” He squeezed her hand, then let go, already moving on. “We should go. The car’s waiting.”

He stood, holding out a hand to help her up. She ignored it. She pushed herself up, the beaded dress whispering its dry protest. Her legs held. That was something. She took a step, then another. The club stretched before her, a tunnel of empty tables and drowned candles. She walked through it, Langston a half-step behind, his presence at her back like a gentle, insistent pressure. The alley air hit her face, cold and greasy. The car idled at the curb, exhaust pluming white in the February dark.

She got in. The door closed with a solid, expensive thunk.

The car moved through the city with a quiet purpose, the tires whispering over wet pavement. Iris watched the streetlights slide past the window, each one a brief, sterile sun that illuminated the rain-slicked hood before plunging them back into darkness. Cross had not spoken again. He’d given a quiet address to the driver, then folded his hands in his lap and watched the city go by as if it were a film he’d seen before. The silence was a third presence in the car, dense and patient.

They crossed into the Upper West Side. The buildings grew taller, the canopies more ornate. The car pulled to the curb with a gentle sigh of hydraulics. Cross turned to her. His face in the shadowed interior was all planes and hollows.

“Your husband will have the details. The institute expects you Monday morning, ten o’clock.” He didn’t wait for a reply. The driver was already out, opening her door. The cold night air rushed in, carrying the smell of damp wool and distant garbage. She stepped onto the sidewalk. The door thudded shut behind her, and the car slid away into the traffic, leaving her standing under the awning of her own building.

The elevator groaned its way to the seventh floor. The brass panel inside was smudged at the button for seven, a cloudy fingerprint worn by repetition. She focused on that smudge, the meaningless archaeology of domestic habit. Her key turned in the lock with a sound like a bone snapping.

The apartment was dark except for a slash of light from the half-open door of Langston’s study. The faint, acrid smell of his cigar lingered in the hall. She didn’t call out. She set her clutch on the sideboard, her fingers brushing against the cold marble. A stack of mail lay there, untouched. On top was a circular for a record club, a smiling couple leaning over a phonograph. She left it.

She went to the living room. Without turning on the lamp, she walked to the liquor cabinet. The crystal decanter of gin was half-full, catching a sliver of light from the street. She poured two fingers into a lowball glass, no ice. The first sip was a cold line of fire. The second settled into a familiar, medicinal warmth.

From the study, she heard the scratch of a pen, the rustle of paper. He was working. Of course he was working. The machinery had to be maintained.

She sat in the wingback chair by the window. The street below was a canyon of shadows and occasional moving light. A man walked a small dog, the animal trotting in quick, fussy circles. The sight was so ordinary it felt like a rebuke. Her thumb found the rim of the glass and began its circuit.

Twelve years. The number arrived without context, then attached itself to a memory: Pauline’s face, lit by the guttering candle on a table in that cramped flat near the Place de la Contrescarpe. They’d been arguing about something stupid, something about a chord progression in a song Iris was writing. Pauline had been trying to explain Brecht, the alienation effect. Iris had just wanted to write a pretty tune. The argument had dissolved into laughter, the kind that left your ribs sore. Pauline had reached across the table, touched the back of her hand. Her fingers were stained with ink. You hear the melody, she’d said. I hear the machinery underneath. Both are true.

The memory had no business here. It was a fossil, suddenly exposed by the erosion of the evening. It served nothing. It led nowhere. But it sat in the center of her mind, heavy and specific.

The study door opened it. Langston’s silhouette filled the frame, backlit.

“Iris?”

She didn’t turn. “I’m here.”

He came into the room, a glass of something amber in his own hand. He didn’t turn on the light. He stood near the sofa, a dark shape against the lighter grey of the wall.

“Well,” he said. The single word was freighted with a terrible, managerial finality.

“Well,” she echoed, her voice flat.

“It’s not ideal. But it’s the best possible outcome under the circumstances.” He took a sip. Ice cubes clinked. “A teaching position. It’s respectable. It gets you out of the clubs. And it. Satisfies the requirement.”

“The requirement.” She let the phrase hang in the dark air between them.

“You heard the man. A periodic report on the atmosphere. Not names. Not specifics. Just. The general tone of the place.” He sounded like he was reading from a memo he’d already composed in his head. “You’re an observer. That’s all. You observe the theatre. It’s what you do.”

Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It tightened her throat. She took another drink to push it down.

“And if the atmosphere is seditious?”

“Then you note that it is. In general terms.” He moved closer, his shape resolving. She could see the neat part in his hair, the gleam of his watch. “Iris, look at me. This is survival. It’s not about morality. It’s about physics. A force is applied. You adapt to the pressure, or you break. We are adapting.”

We. The word was a lie so vast it made the room tilt. There was no we. There was Langston, preserving his ecosystem of clients and commissions. And there was her, a piece of that ecosystem that had suddenly become a liability, now repurposed as a listening device.

“You negotiated this,” she said.

“I protected you.”

“You brokered a deal.”

A pause. The distant whine of a siren Dopplered down a cross street. “Semantics,” he said quietly. “The result is the same. You are not subpoenaed. You are not exposed. You have a path forward.”

She looked at him then. His face was all careful concern, but his eyes were fixed on a point just over her shoulder, assessing some invisible ledger. She thought of the way Cross had sat in the booth, the absolute economy of his movement. They were the same species, these men. They dealt in the quiet transfer of liabilities.

“What did you trade?” The question came out before she could stop it.

His expression didn’t change. “I ensured our continued security. That’s all you need to know.”

A crack in the ceiling, a long, meandering line she’d never noticed before caught a faint glow from the window. It looked like a river on a very old map. She stared at it. It meant nothing.

“I’ll need something to wear,” she said, her voice distant to her own ears. “For Monday. Something. Pedagogical.”

The shift was so abrupt it left him blinking. He recovered, nodding, falling gratefully into the practical. “Yes. Of course. Something modest. Authoritative, but not severe. I’ll have Margaret arrange something.”

Margaret was his secretary. Iris imagined the woman selecting a dress, a blouse, a costume for an informer. Would she choose wool? Gabardine? Something that wouldn’t show dust from the wings?

Langston was talking about logistics, about how he’d arranged for a car to take her, how he’d contacted the institute’s board chairman—an old acquaintance—to smooth the way. He spoke, the words a stream flowing past her. She let them go.

When he finally finished, he stood there, waiting for some acknowledgment. She gave none. After a moment, he sighed, a sound of profound, put-upon endurance. “Get some sleep, Iris. It’s been a long night.”

He left the room. The study door closed softly. A minute later, she heard the faint click of his record player, the opening strains of a Debussy étude. His music for thinking.

She finished the gin. The glass was slick in her hand. She stood, her legs stiff, and walked to the bedroom. She didn’t turn on the light. She undressed in the dark, letting the beaded dress pool on the floor like a shed skin. The room was cold. She put on a silk slip, the fabric whispering against her thighs, and got into bed.

The sheets were cold, too. She lay on her back, staring at the darker rectangle of the ceiling. The city’s murmur was a constant, low-grade hum. Somewhere, a pipe knocked.

Her hand went to the nightstand drawer. She didn’t open it. Her fingers rested on the brass pull. Inside, in a small lacquer box was the Zippo lighter from Paris. The enamel was chipped. It had never held fluid. She hadn’t touched it in years.

She didn’t take it out. She just left her hand there, feeling the cool metal of the drawer pull against her palm. Monday was forty-eight hours away. Harlem was a train ride north. Pauline was a ghost in a place she’d never been.

The pipe knocked again. A rhythm. One-two. One-two-three. Then silence. She closed her eyes. The darkness behind her lids was not empty. It was full of the particular, forgotten grey of a Paris dawn, the smell of baking bread and yesterday’s rain, and the precise, aching geometry of a hand reaching across a table, fingers stained with ink.
