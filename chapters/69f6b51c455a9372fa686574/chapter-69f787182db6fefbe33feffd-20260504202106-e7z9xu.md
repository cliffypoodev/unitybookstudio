The afternoon light in Pauline’s office was the color of weak tea, slanting through the high, dusty window and catching the motes that drifted above the desk. Iris stood just inside the door, her hand still on the knob. The room smelled of old paper, the particular dry scent of scripts handled by many hands, and beneath it, the faint, ghostly trace of Pauline’s perfume. Iris had come straight from the coffee shop, the old man’s words—the roundups started not a month after you left—echoing in the hollow place behind her breastbone. She hadn’t gone home. She hadn’t typed a report. She had walked the twenty blocks north, the February air needling her cheeks, and now here she was.

Pauline didn’t look up from the page she was marking with a red pencil. She sat behind the broad oak desk, a fortress of scattered manuscripts. The single desk lamp carved her face into planes of light and shadow, highlighting the sharp line of her cheekbone, deepening the darkness around her eyes. Her pencil tapped once, twice, a staccato beat against the wood.

“Rehearsal’s at seven,” she said, her voice flat. “You’re early.

Iris closed the door. The click of the latch was too loud. She crossed the room, her heels sinking into the worn Persian rug. A thread was loose near the desk leg, a curl of crimson against the faded background. She fixed her eyes on it. “I saw Max Geller today. The bookseller. From the Rue de Seine.

The pencil stopped. Pauline set it down, aligning it perfectly parallel to the edge of the manuscript. She still didn’t look up. “Max. I heard he made it out. Lisbon, then Buenos Aires. I didn’t know he was here.

“He’s on MacDougal. He has a stall.” Iris’s mouth was dry. She hadn’t had a drink since the tepid coffee. “He said I was lucky. To get the Zenith contract when I did.

“You were.”

“He said the roundups started a month after I was on the boat.” The words came out in a rush, airless. “He said your crowd scattered. Or worse.

Finally, Pauline leaned back in her chair. The leather sighed. She looked at Iris then, her gaze level, unreadable. The light caught the silver at her temples, the fine lines at the corners of her eyes that hadn’t been there in Paris. “Is there a question in there, Iris? Or are you just narrating history?

“You never told me.” It wasn’t an accusation. It was a statement of fact, empty of tone. “You let me think I’d walked out on a debate society. A literary salon. You let me carry that.

“Carry what, exactly?” Pauline’s voice was quiet, too quiet. “The weight of your choice? You made it. The information wouldn’t have changed it.

“How do you know?”

“Because you told me.” Pauline picked up the pencil again, rolled it between her fingers. “On the platform. You said, ‘It’s safety, Pauline. It’s a real career.’ You had the contract in your bag. You’d already signed it.

Iris remembered the smell of the Gare du Nord—coal smoke and damp wool and overripe bananas from a vendor’s cart. She remembered the way Pauline had stood, hands in her coat pockets, not touching her. Not even a handshake. The train whistle had been a sharp, painful note in the heavy air. “You could have said something. ‘Stay, it’s getting dangerous.’ You could have given me a reason to tear up the paper.

“A reason?” Pauline’s laugh was a short, dry exhalation. “What, my personal safety? Since when did that ever factor into your calculations? You wanted the stage. The recording studio. The bright lights. A warning about police raids wouldn’t have been a reason. It would have been an inconvenience. Another messy complication.

The truth of it landed, a dull, sickening weight in Iris’s stomach. Not quite anger, not quite shame—a sour amalgam of both, settling deep. She looked away, her eyes skittering over the bookshelves. One volume was still upside down. She had the absurd, overpowering urge to walk over and right it. To fix that one small, wrong thing.

“Max said they took Henri Lefevre,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper. “He said they pulled him from the print shop. He died in a camp in the east. He published your poem. The one about the ash trees.

Pauline was silent for a long moment. The only sound was the distant, rhythmic thump of a janitor’s mop bucket being rolled down the hall outside. Then she said, “I know.

“You never wrote about that.”

“What would have been the point? To make you sorry? You were in New York by then. Singing on the radio.” Pauline stood up abruptly. She walked to the window, her back to Iris. Her silhouette against the grey sky was sharp, contained. “You want a new story, Iris? Is that it? You want to believe you were ushered to safety by a noble lover, that you were spared some terrible fate so you could have your fame? It wasn’t like that. It was messy and ugly and you left because you wanted to. The danger was just… background noise. For you.

Iris’s throat tightened. She watched the line of Pauline’s shoulders, the tension in the set of her neck. “Did you know? Before I left? Did you know what was coming?

Pauline didn’t turn. “We all knew what was coming. The whispers were everywhere. The broken windows. The friends who stopped answering their doors. We didn’t need a calendar.” She paused. “The week you got the offer from Zenith, a man from the Sûreté came to the café. He asked questions about the journal. About the funding. He had a notebook. He wrote down names.

A cold knot formed under Iris’s ribs. “You didn’t tell me.

“You were practicing your scales for the recording test. You were choosing which dress to wear to the meeting with the label man. You were…” Pauline shook her head, a small, frustrated motion. “You were somewhere else. Already.

The memory surfaced, unbidden and precise: Iris at the tiny upright piano in their rented room, working through the bridge of “The Last Goodbye” for the hundredth time. Pauline at the table, surrounded by galleys for La Flamme, a cigarette burning unattended in the ashtray. The silence between them had been different that day—not peaceful, but charged, like the air before a storm breaks. Iris had asked if the print run was settled. Pauline had said yes, her voice distant. Iris had thought she was annoyed by the noise. She’d played softer, thinking she was being considerate.

She had been an idiot.

“You let me go,” Iris said. The words felt thick. “You could have used it. The danger. You could have said, ‘They’re watching us, stay, we need to be careful.’ You could have made it about us. Instead you made it about art. About selling out.

Pauline turned from the window. Her face was in shadow now, her expression impossible to read. “Would that have been better? To manipulate you with fear? To make your choice about my politics, my safety? That would have been a lie, too. Your choice was about your art. Your ambition. Let it be what it was.

“It was a betrayal.”

“Yes.” The word was simple, final. “It was. But it was yours. Not mine. I didn’t betray you, Iris. I just didn’t provide a better alibi for your conscience.

The directness was a slap. Iris’s hand went to the back of the wooden chair in front of the desk, her fingers gripping the carved scrollwork. The varnish was sticky in one spot, a place where someone had spilled something long ago and never properly cleaned it. She focused on that tacky patch, the way her thumb caught on it.

“All these years,” she said, “I thought you were punishing me for choosing a different path. A commercial path. I thought your anger was about purity. Artistic integrity.

“It was.”

“But it wasn’t just that, was it?” Iris looked up, met the darkness where Pauline’s eyes were. “You were angry because I chose a path that led away from the fire. And you were walking straight into it. You were angry because I didn’t even see the flames.

Pauline didn’t deny it. She walked back to the desk, but she didn’t sit. She leaned against it, arms crossed. The light from the lamp now caught the side of her face, the tired curve of her mouth. “You asked Max about the roundups. Why?

“I wanted to know.”

“No. You wanted a reason. A justification. You’re looking for a way to rearrange the past so your leaving wasn’t a abandonment. It was a tactical retreat. A necessary survival.” Pauline’s voice was weary, stripped of its earlier edge. “You want the narrative of the play, Iris. The clear cause and effect. The heroic sacrifice or the cowardly betrayal. Life isn’t a play. It’s a mess of coincidences and selfishness and people trying their best with the limited vision they have. You left because you wanted the contract. I let you go because I was tired of arguing. And because a part of me thought you’d be safer. That’s all. No grand design. No hidden nobility. Just two people making the choices they could live with at the time.

Iris released the chair. Her palm left a faint, damp print on the wood. She felt unmoored, the solid ground of her own guilt turning to sand. For twelve years, she had built a story: she was the coward, Pauline the betrayed idealist. It was a clean story. A story with a moral. This new version was murkier, full of ambiguous shadows and unspoken protections. It was harder to hold.

“Henri Lefevre died,” she said again, stupidly.

“Yes.”

“And you stayed.”

“For a while. Then I left, too. Different direction. Different boat.” Pauline reached for a packet of cigarettes on the desk, shook one loose. She didn’t light it. Just held it between her fingers. “We all have our contracts, Iris. Mine just came with different small print.

The reference to Cross, to the committee, hung in the air between them, unspoken but palpable. Iris’s report was due tomorrow. She hadn’t written a word. She thought of the Zippo lighter in her coat pocket, the cold metal against her thigh. A token from a time before contracts, before small print.

“Max asked after you,” Iris said, shifting the subject to safer, more painful ground. “He said to tell you the ash tree poem is still being passed around. In translation. In Argentina.

A flicker of something—pain, maybe, or a faint, grim pride—crossed Pauline’s face. She nodded once. “Good.

Silence settled over the room, softer now. The frantic energy of Iris’s arrival had dissipated, leaving a strange, exhausted calm. She noticed the pattern of the rug in detail for the first time—medallions and twisting vines, worn almost smooth in a path from the door to the desk. Thousands of footsteps had taken that same route.

“The play,” Iris said. “Port Chicago. Is it… is it a roundup?

Pauline looked at her, really looked at her, for the first time since she’d entered. Her eyes searched Iris’s face, as if looking for a crack, a place where the truth might seep in. “It’s a play about an explosion. About negligence and racism and silence. You know what it is.

“But why that? Why now? With everything… with the committee sniffing around.” Iris took a step closer. The desk was between them, a barrier of oak and paper. “Is it a trench? Like I said? Or is it something else?

“What else would it be?”

“A warning.” The idea formed as she said it, feeling both ridiculous and inevitable. “A signal. To the people who know how to read it.

Pauline’s expression didn’t change. She placed the unlit cigarette on the desk. “You’re reading scripts again. Looking for ciphers where there are only words.

“You taught me to.”

“I taught you to use your life as the key. Not to go hunting for ghosts in the text.” Pauline pushed off from the desk. “The play is what it is. A story that needs telling. The rest… the rest is just context. The world it’s being told in.

It was a non-answer. A director’s answer. Iris felt a familiar frustration rise, but it was muted, tempered by the dizzying conversation that had come before. Nothing was solid. Her own past was shifting under her feet. The present was a minefield. And Pauline, this woman who had once been the axis of her world, was now a closed door, offering only the faintest glimpse of the light inside.

“I should go,” Iris said, though she made no move to leave. “Let you prepare.

“You are prepared.” Pauline’s voice was matter-of-fact. “You know the score. Literally. Your notations are better than any actor’s notes I’ve ever seen. You don’t need to run lines. You need to stop thinking and let it happen.

It was the closest thing to praise she’d given her. Iris felt a stupid, vulnerable warmth in her chest. She crushed it. “And what happens when it does? When the curtain goes up and all those people are out there? The producers. The… others.

“We perform the play.” Pauline’s gaze was steady. “That’s the job. That’s the only thing we can control.

But it isn’t, Iris thought. You’re not that naïve. You know what’s coming. She thought of Cross in his grey suit, taking notes. Of Langston and his new fountain pen, his phone calls in the other room. Of Duke Strauss and his shadowy patrons. The play wasn’t just a play. It was a focal point, a convergence. And she was standing at the very center, pulled in every direction.

She turned to go, her body moving on autopilot. Her hand found the doorknob again.

“Iris.”

She stopped, didn’t turn.

“The reason I didn’t tell you about the Sûreté,” Pauline said, her voice low, “wasn’t just because you were practicing scales. It was because I knew you’d worry. And your worry would have been… performative. You would have fretted, and agonized, and then you would have gone anyway. And the fretting would have made it harder for you to leave. It would have left a residue. I didn’t want that for you. I wanted your choice to be clean. For better or worse.

Iris stood frozen, her forehead nearly touching the cool wood of the door. The confession, if that’s what it was, felt more devastating than any accusation. It was a kindness, a brutal, clear-eyed kindness that assumed the worst of her and acted accordingly. It assumed she needed her hands clean. It assumed she couldn’t bear a messy, complicated goodbye.

She had been right.

Iris opened the door. The hallway was empty, dim, the mop bucket long gone. She stepped out and closed the door softly behind her, leaving Pauline in the pool of lamplight, surrounded by her scripts and her history and her impossible, dangerous play.

She walked down the hall, her footsteps echoing in the empty space. Near the stairwell, a janitor’s cart was parked, a wet mop leaning against it. The smell of pine disinfectant was overwhelming, chemical and sweet. On the wall beside the fire extinguisher, someone had drawn a small, childish smiley face in pencil. It was lopsided, one eye higher than the other.

Iris stared at it for a full minute, her mind perfectly, mercifully blank. Then she descended the stairs, out into the gathering dusk, carrying the new, unbearable weight of a past she had never truly known.
