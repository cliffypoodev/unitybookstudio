The cellar smelled of damp stone and spilled wine, a sour sweetness that clung to the back of the throat. Iris’s fingers found the chords on the borrowed guitar without looking, a twelve-bar blues in a minor key she’d twisted just enough to sound like her own. The spotlight was a single bulb in a tin shade, a yellow puddle that ended at the edge of the tiny stage. Beyond it, shadows and cigarette smoke moved as one. A man coughed, a wet, rattling sound. Someone dropped a glass. The sound didn’t shatter but thudded, muted by a carpet sticky with beer.

She sang low, for herself mostly, the words about a train and a woman left standing on the platform. The old clichés felt new here, in this damp box under the Paris streets. The piano player, a Tunisian named Jacques with a scar through his eyebrow, followed her lead with a light, walking bass in the left hand. They’d played three nights together. He didn’t talk much. That was fine.

In the middle of the second verse, she saw her.

A woman at a small table near the wall, half in shadow. She wasn’t watching the stage so much as absorbing it, her head tilted, a notebook open but untouched, a pencil held loosely between long fingers. The light from a candle on the table caught the angle of her cheekbone, the severe line of her jaw. She wore a man’s white shirt, sleeves rolled to the elbows. Her skin was the rich, warm brown of walnut wood. Iris’s voice caught, just a fraction, on the word “gone.” She covered it by bending the note, letting it fray at the edges. Jacques glanced up, his fingers never slowing.

The song ended. The applause was sparse, a few scattered claps like rain on a tin roof. Iris nodded, placed the guitar carefully against the stool. Her palms were damp. She wiped them on her trousers, a nervous gesture from her girlhood in Ohio she thought she’d unlearned.

At the bar, she ordered a vin rouge. The barman poured it without looking at her. She took the glass, turned, and the woman was there, leaning against the bar a few feet away.

“You flatten the third,” the woman said. Her voice was deeper than Iris expected, textured like crushed velvet. American, but with an accent she couldn’t place—Northern, maybe, educated.

Iris took a sip. The wine was sharp, cheap. “It sounds better flat.

“It sounds resigned. The original is angry.

“Maybe I’m not an angry person.”

“Aren’t you?” The woman’s eyes were dark, impossible to read in the low light. “You sing like you are. Then you soften the note. As if you’re apologizing for the feeling.

No one had ever listened that closely. Iris felt exposed, as if the woman had seen the clumsy seam where the performance ended and the person began. She traced the rim of her glass with her thumb. “It’s just a song.

“It’s never just a song.” The woman extended a hand. “Pauline Carter.

“Iris. Finch.

Her hand was cool, her grip firm and brief. A writer’s callus on her middle finger. “You’re from the States.

“A while ago.” Iris found herself wanting to explain, to list the cities, the failed auditions, the slow drift east. She said nothing.

Pauline lit a cigarette. The match flared, illuminating the strong planes of her face for an instant. “I publish a journal. Le Phare. The Lighthouse. Poetry, essays, some fiction. We’re printing a series on the music of dissent. Spirituals, work songs, the blues. Not as anthropology. As strategy.” She exhaled smoke, watched it curl toward the low ceiling. “I’d like to interview you. If you’re interested.

“I don’t know anything about strategy.”

“You do. You just don’t have the language for it yet.” Pauline’s gaze was direct, unnerving. “That flattening of the third. That’s a choice. It says the pain is permanent, accepted. That’s a political statement, whether you intend it or not. In America, they want your pain to be cathartic. Temporary. A story with an ending. You’re refusing the ending.

Iris looked down at her wine. A faint lipstick mark, not hers, was smudged on the rim of the glass. She turned the glass so the mark faced away. “I just think it sounds better.

A slow smile touched Pauline’s mouth. It changed her face completely, a sudden, unexpected warmth. “Alright. We’ll start there.

They walked along the Seine hours later, the sky a deep indigo bleeding into dawn. The air was cold, clean after the cellar’s haze. Pauline talked about her journal, the wealthy Swiss patron who funded it—“a man who collects radical causes like stamps, but his money spends the same,”—about the Popular Front, about the war in Spain, her sentences long and complex, punctuated by pauses where she would look at Iris as if checking to see if she was still following. Iris mostly listened. The city was quiet, just the slap of water against stone and the distant sound of a cart rolling over cobblestones.

They stopped on a bridge. Pauline leaned against the stone parapet, looking out at the water. “You have a good ear. But you’re listening to the wrong things. You’re listening for the melody. The pretty part. You need to listen for the silence between the notes. That’s where the truth hides.

“You sound like a composer.”

“I sound like someone who has seen what happens when people only hear the melody.” Pauline flicked her cigarette stub into the dark water. A tiny, dying spark. “Come by the print shop tomorrow. Rue du Sommerard. We can talk properly. I’ll show you the galleys for the next issue.

“I have a gig. In the afternoon.

“After, then.” It wasn’t a question.

Iris nodded. The silence that followed wasn’t awkward. It was full, like the held breath before a chord resolves. A barge slipped beneath the bridge, its lantern a single, winking eye in the gloom. She noticed a crack in the stone of the bridge, shaped like a long, thin bird in flight. She pointed to it. “Look.

Pauline glanced at it, then back at Iris. “A swallow. They say if a swallow nests on your house, you’ll never want for love.

“Is that true?”

“No. But it’s a good story.” Pauline pushed off from the wall. “Walk me home. It’s on your way.

It wasn’t on her way. Iris’s rented room was in the opposite direction, a cramped attic near Place de la Contrescarpe. She said, “Alright.

The apartment was small, a fourth-floor walk-up that smelled of ink, paper, and Gauloises. Manuscripts were stacked on every surface. A printing press, a small, hand-operated thing, stood like a sculptural centerpiece in the main room. Books lined makeshift shelves of planks and bricks. Pauline moved through the space with a proprietary ease, clearing a pile of pamphlets from a chair so Iris could sit.

“I’ll make coffee. It will be terrible.

“That’s fine.”

While Pauline worked in the tiny kitchen alcove, Iris looked around. On the wall above the desk was a map of Europe, dotted with colored pins. A photograph of a woman with Pauline’s eyes and a gentle smile, framed in simple wood. A dried sprig of lavender, brittle and grey, tucked into the edge of the mirror. The room was a portrait of a mind, ordered and fervent.

The coffee was, as promised, terrible—bitter and thick as mud. Iris drank it anyway.

Pauline sat on the floor, leaning against the leg of her desk. She watched Iris. “Why Paris?

The question was so simple it felt like a trap. Iris shrugged, a useless gesture. “It was far away.

“From what?”

“From… the version of me that was supposed to happen.” She put the cup down. The taste of the coffee lingered, a dark grit on her tongue. “The marriage. The house. The singing in the church choir on Sundays. All of it.

“And what version is happening here?”

“I don’t know yet.” The admission felt dangerous. “I’m just playing in cellars.

“That’s a version.” Pauline’s expression was unreadable again, but her voice was softer. “It’s a valid one.

Something hot moved through Iris’s chest—not quite hope, not quite fear, a liquid feeling with no name. The dawn light was beginning to seep through the window, a pale grey wash that slowly defined the shapes in the room. It caught the dust motes swirling in the air, turning them into a slow, silent dance.

Pauline stood up in one fluid motion. She crossed the room, took Iris’s empty cup from her hands, and placed it on the desk. Her fingers, for a second, brushed against Iris’s. A static shock, tiny and sharp.

“Stay,” Pauline said. It wasn’t a command. It was an offer, laid bare between them with no ornament.

Iris looked at her, at the serious mouth, the intelligent eyes waiting for an answer. The city outside was waking up. A distant church bell rang once, a clear, cold sound. She thought of her empty attic, the narrow bed, the silence that waited for her there.

“Alright,” she said.

The garret was one long room under the eaves, the ceiling sloping so sharply on one side that Pauline had to duck to reach her desk. Books were the architecture—stacks formed walls, towers served as tables, a precarious fort of ideas. The air was a permanent haze of Gauloises smoke and the smell of strong coffee, burnt at the bottom of the pot. A thin grey cat, all ribs and disdain, watched from the top of a wardrobe, its tail twitching at the precise tempo of a metronome.

Iris learned the rhythm of the place. Mornings were for Pauline’s own writing, the furious scratch of pen on paper a sound as familiar as her own breathing. Iris would sit on the floor by the small, grimy window, tuning her guitar, trying out fragments of melody. She watched dust motes dance in a single shaft of sunlight that cut across the room, a bright blade on the worn floorboards. The light always died at the edge of the largest book pile, never reaching the far wall.

“Read this,” Pauline said one afternoon, not looking up from her typewriter. She slid a sheaf of papers across the desk without breaking her typing rhythm.

It was an essay. On the Use of Folk Idiom in Subversive Narrative. Iris’s name appeared in the third paragraph. The American singer Iris Finch exemplifies this conscious flattening, a musical refusal of catharsis that transforms personal lament into political statement.

“You make me sound clever,” Iris said.

“You are clever. You just dress it up as instinct.” Pauline finished a line, yanked the page from the roller. “Instinct is a luxury. Strategy is work.

Iris traced the words with her finger. She had never thought of her voice as a statement. It was just her voice, the only thing she’d ever owned that felt entirely hers. To hear it parsed, categorized, given purpose—it was like seeing her own face in a distorted mirror. Recognizable, but warped into something more significant. Something dangerous.

They argued about everything. The merits of Brecht. The Spanish Republicans. The proper way to brew coffee. Pauline’s arguments were structured, fortified with references. Iris’s were all feeling, a gut reaction she often couldn’t articulate until the heat of the moment had passed, leaving her with only the ghost of her conviction.

“You’re arguing from sentiment,” Pauline would say, lighting another cigarette.

“And you’re arguing from a book!”

“The book has better citations.”

One evening, the argument was about a poem Pauline had written, a dense, thorny thing about fire. Iris found it cold. “Where’s the person in it? It’s all… ideas.

“The person is irrelevant. The idea is the weapon.

“A weapon to do what? To who?

“To remind,” Pauline said, her voice dropping, losing its lecturer’s edge. “To remind people that some fires are meant to cleanse. To burn away the rot so something new can grow. You can’t do that with a sigh and a flattened third. You need a torch.

She looked at Iris then, a long, measuring look. The cat jumped down from the wardrobe, landing with a soft thump. It stalked over to Iris and rubbed its bony head against her knee, a sudden, inexplicable affection. Iris scratched behind its ears, her throat tight. She didn’t know what to do with this version of Pauline—the one who spoke of fire with a quiet, terrifying certainty.

Collaboration was a series of small surrenders. Iris would hum a phrase. Pauline would scribble words in the margin of a manuscript, her handwriting a furious spike. They fit lines to melody on the threadbare rug, the cat a disinterested umpire between them. Pauline’s touch was different during these hours—not the deliberate contact of a lover, but the casual, focused brushing of hands as she passed a sheet of music, the press of her shoulder against Iris’s as they both peered at a notebook. It was a closeness that felt deeper, somehow. It lived in the space between the notes and the words, in the silent agreement that they were building something that belonged to neither of them alone.

Iris began to hear the world as Pauline did—not as a sequence of events, but as a composition of power and resistance. The rumble of a delivery truck was a bass note of industry. The shrill whistle of a street sweeper was a discordant piccolo. Her own past in Ohio, the strict silence of her parents’ house, the narrow expectations—they weren’t just memories. They were the opening movement, the establishing key. This, now, the smell of typing ribbon and cheap wine, the sound of Pauline’s voice in the dark explaining Gramsci—this was the development. The place where the theme transformed.

She told Pauline about the record contract on a Thursday. The letter had arrived that morning, forwarded from her dingy hotel. A New York label. A real studio. An advance that seemed a fortune. She laid the crisp paper on the desk, over a draft of Pauline’s latest editorial.

Pauline read it. She read it again. Her expression didn’t change. She picked up her cigarette from the ashtray, took a slow drag, set it down. The silence was a physical presence, swelling to fill the garret, pressing against the books, muffling the distant sounds of the street below.

“It’s safety,” Iris heard herself say. The words sounded pathetic, small. “I could work. I could be heard.

“You are heard here.” Pauline’s voice was flat, stripped of all its texture.

“By fifty people in a cellar. This is… a broadcast.

“It is a product. They will package your flattening third. They will sell your refusal as a novelty. Then they will ask you to smile for the camera and sing something patriotic when the war comes.” She finally looked up. Her eyes were dark, hollow. “And you will do it. Because it will be the price of the safety you bought.

“That’s not fair.”

“No. It is accurate.” Pauline stood up. She walked to the window, her back to Iris. The line of her shoulders was rigid. “You have a choice. You can stay here and help build the lighthouse. Or you can go be a decorative lamp in a well-appointed sitting room. A pleasant glow. No fire.

Iris’s chest ached. Something hot moved through it—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. “It doesn’t have to be one or the other. I could go, do this, and come back. With resources.

Pauline gave a short, sharp laugh, a dry crack of sound. “You won’t come back. The path is a slope. You take one step onto it for a good reason. The next step is easier. Soon you are running, and you cannot even see the place you started from.” She turned around. Her face was a mask of calm, but a muscle in her jaw pulsed. “So go. Be a success. But do not pretend you are coming back. And do not pretend this,” she gestured between them, a flick of her fingers that took in the books, the cat, the shared coffee cups, “was anything less than what it was. A diversion. For both of us.

The cruelty of it was surgical. Iris felt the words slice, clean and deep. She had no defense. She looked down at the contract. The official letterhead was a deep, respectable blue. She thought of the yellow puddle of the spotlight in the cellar, of the way Pauline’s cheekbone caught the candlelight that first night. One was a sun. The other was a match, already burning down.

She didn’t remember leaving. There was a blur of motion—picking up her coat, her guitar case. The door of the garret closing with a soft, final click. The sound of her own footsteps on the winding stairs, too loud in the sudden quiet. She realized, on the street, that she was still clutching the contract. The paper was damp from her palm. She had left everything else behind.

The apartment was quiet in a way that felt deliberate, as if the dust motes drifting in the grey morning light were holding their breath. Iris finished packing her suitcase. She folded a blue sweater Pauline had left on a chair the week before, her fingers lingering on the coarse wool. The scent of her—cigarettes and the vetiver cologne she bought from a stall on the Rue de Rivoli—rose from the fabric. Iris placed the sweater in the case. Then she took it out again. She left it on the chair.

The contract from Paramount was on the kitchen table, a crisp rectangle of possibility. She had read it six times. The advance was more money than she’d ever held. The guarantee was for two records, with an option for four more. There was a clause about moral turpitude. She hadn’t understood that clause when she signed it in the producer’s hotel suite, her hand shaking with a cheap, thrilling fear. She understood it now. It meant be quiet. It meant be good. It meant the money came with a lock, and she was to swallow the key.

Pauline was at the print shop. Le Phare went to press on Tuesdays. Iris had calculated this. She would be gone by the time Pauline returned. She had written a note. She tore it up. She wrote another. Pauline—the American offer came through. I have to take it. It’s safety. You understand. She stared at the word ‘understand’. It was a small, brutal piece of theatre. She left the note on the table, weighted with the empty ink bottle.

The taxi to the Gare du Nord took too long. The city passed outside the window—the familiar, grimy beauty of it, the balconies with their iron curls, a woman shaking a rug from an upstairs window, a momentary storm of dust. Iris’s guitar case pressed against her leg. She counted her breaths. In, two three. Out, two three. A singer’s exercise. It did nothing.

The station was a cavern of noise and echoing footsteps, of steam and shouted destinations. The smell of coal and damp wool was so thick it was a taste. She bought her ticket. The clerk’s fingers were stained with newsprint. He did not look at her. She found her platform. The train was a long, dark serpent, breathing heat.

And then she was there.

Pauline stood at the edge of the platform, not moving, her coat buttoned wrong. She must have run. Her hair was coming loose from its knot. She held a proof sheet from the journal, the paper fluttering in the damp draft from the train. She didn’t approach. She just looked.

Iris’s stomach dropped. A clean, physical sensation, like missing a step in the dark. She hadn’t expected this. The note, the clean cut—that was the arrangement. This was a messy, terrible revision.

Pauline walked forward. Her heels clicked on the stone, a slow, deliberate meter. She stopped a few feet away. She didn’t touch Iris. Her eyes were red-rimmed but dry. “Safety,” she said. The word was flat, stripped of tone.

“It’s a contract. A real one.

“I read the note.” Pauline looked past her, at the train. “You didn’t even say it to my face.

“I thought it would be easier.”

“For who?” Now Pauline’s gaze snapped back. It was like being struck. “For you. To slink out like a thief. Is that what we were? A thing to be stolen from?

A porter pushed a cart of trunks between them, breaking the line of sight. For a second, Iris saw only the stacked luggage, a wall of cracked leather. When he passed, Pauline was closer. “You said you hated what they did to music there. You said it was a factory for lies.

“I can change it from the inside.” The line sounded thin, rehearsed. She had practiced it in the mirror.

Pauline let out a sharp breath, almost a laugh. “No, you can’t. You’ll stand in their studio and you’ll sing their words in their arrangements and you’ll flatten every third until there’s no edge left at all. And you’ll call it success.” She took a final step, so close Iris could see the faint pulse at the base of her throat. “Look at me and tell me I’m wrong.

Iris looked. She saw the proof sheet crushed in Pauline’s hand, the ink smearing. She saw the buttonhole strained where the coat was fastened incorrectly. A wave of something hot and shameful moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that tightened her throat. She said nothing.

Pauline’s expression changed. The anger bled out, leaving something worse: a cold, clear comprehension. “You’re not even choosing the music,” she said, her voice low. “You’re choosing the cage. Because someone else is paying for it. Because someone else has the key.

“Fair?” Pauline shook her head. A strand of hair fell across her cheek. She didn’t brush it away. “This was never about fair. This was about building something that didn’t exist. You and me. The journal. Our work. We were building.” Her voice cracked on the word. She looked down at the ruined proof sheet, then back at Iris. “But you need a blueprint someone else drew. You need walls you didn’t make.

A whistle blew, sharp and final. The last call. The sound seemed to pull something essential from the air.

Pauline didn’t move. “If you get on that train, Iris, you don’t get to come back. You don’t get to write me letters about how empty it feels. You don’t get to send for me when you’re lonely. You choose their world. You live in it.

Iris felt the weight of the guitar case in her hand. The Paramount contract was in her purse, a hard corner digging into her ribs through the leather. She thought of the studio, the microphone, her voice finally pressed into wax. She thought of the apartment, now empty, the blue sweater on the chair. The space between her and Pauline was only a few feet. It felt continental.

She turned. She walked to the train carriage, the sound of her own footsteps lost in the station’s din. She handed her ticket to the conductor. She did not look back.

The compartment was cold. She sat by the window, her guitar case on the seat beside her like a silent companion. The train jerked, then began to slide forward. The platform started to move, a slow unspooling of grey stone and posters and receding figures.

Pauline was still there. A solitary, straight-backed shape, growing smaller. She didn’t wave. She just watched the train leave. Then she turned, a precise, military pivot, and walked away, disappearing into the crowd long before the platform curved out of sight.

Iris sat perfectly still. Her hands were cold. She realized she was tracing the rim of the window with her thumb, around and around on the cold glass. Outside, the outskirts of Paris streamed past—soot-blackened walls, lines of washing, a child chasing a dog in a yard. The world was continuing. The train gathered speed, the rhythm of the wheels on the tracks settling into a steady, hypnotic beat. It sounded like a count. It sounded like goodbye.
