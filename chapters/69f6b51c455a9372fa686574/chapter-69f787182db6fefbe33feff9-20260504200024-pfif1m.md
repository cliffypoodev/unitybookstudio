The rehearsal hall at night was a different creature. The daylight, with its hopeful spill from the high windows, had drained away. Now the space was defined by shadows and the single, green-shaded desk lamp on Pauline’s worktable at the far end. Iris had come back for her notebook, the one where she scored Eleanor’s silences. She’d left it on a chair after the afternoon’s run-through, a stupid oversight. The walk from the subway had been long and cold, her breath a thin plume in the February air. The church’s front door, unlocked for the janitor, gave with a familiar groan.

Inside, the vast room held a breath of its own—dust, old wood, the faint, sweet ghost of lemon polish. The chairs were stacked against the walls, the stage a dark platform. Only that lamp, a small island of yellow light in the oceanic dark. Iris moved toward her chair, her footsteps a soft crunch on the grit of the floor. She saw the notebook, a dark rectangle on the seat. And then she saw Pauline.

Pauline was there, at the desk, but she wasn’t working. Her head rested on her folded arms, atop a scattered pile of script pages. She was asleep. Her shoulders rose and fell with a slow, deep rhythm. The light pooled around her, casting the loose curls of her hair into a soft halo against the dark wood. Iris stopped. She shouldn’t be here. This was a private moment, unguarded. She should take her notebook and leave, let the door groan again on her way out. But her feet didn’t turn. She watched. The silence was not empty. It had a texture, like felt, thick and absorbent. It swallowed the distant hum of the streetlights outside, the faint ticking of the old radiator in the corner.

Then Pauline stirred. Not a waking stir. A twitch, a small shudder that traveled from her shoulders down her spine. Her head shifted on her arms. A low sound escaped her, not a word but a murmur, a vowel dragged through sleep. Iris held her breath. She shouldn’t be listening.

“The smoke,” Pauline whispered into the script pages. Her voice was blurred, distant, the voice of someone talking to a room inside their own head. “It has to be everything. Everything.

Iris’s stomach tightened. Not with fear, but with a recognition that felt like a cold hand placed on her neck. This wasn’t a director dreaming of stagecraft. This was a person dreaming of fire.

Pauline’s face, half-hidden, contorted. A line of tension pulled her mouth taut. “No,” she murmured, sharper. “Not the letters. The… the drafts. The drafts first.

Iris remembered the smell of burning paper. Pauline had mentioned it, just once, that night sharing a cigarette. A haunted smell. Now it had a source. Drafts. Not final copies, but the messy, evolving things. The things that showed your thinking. You burned those to erase the trail of your mind, not just your words. The cold hand on Iris’s neck pressed harder. This was a memory, not a dream. A practiced procedure.

Pauline’s breath hitched. She mumbled something else, a jumble of syllables that might have been a name, or a place. Then she settled, the tension draining from her face. She sank back into a deeper sleep, her breathing evening out.

Iris stood there for a full minute, maybe two. The radiator ticked. A car passed outside, its engine noise a brief intrusion that faded. She looked at Pauline’s coat, draped over the back of a nearby chair. It was a heavy wool thing, charcoal grey, the collar slightly frayed. Iris moved then, not toward the exit, but toward the chair. She picked up the coat. It was heavy in her hands, smelling of cigarette smoke and the faint, clean scent of the cold outside. She approached the desk, stepping into the circle of lamplight. Her own shadow fell across the script pages.

She laid the coat over Pauline’s shoulders, gently, trying not to touch her. The wool settled with a soft rustle. Pauline did not wake. Her face, now partly shaded by the coat’s collar, looked younger. Unlined by the day’s concentration, free of the sharp, assessing gaze she wielded like a tool. Iris looked at the script pages under Pauline’s head. They were covered in her handwriting—tight, angular notes in the margins, arrows pointing from one block of text to another, whole paragraphs crossed out with vigorous lines. At the top of one page, she had written: Eleanor’s lie is not in her words. It is in her breath. The pause before she speaks.

Iris’s own breath caught. She looked away, at the dark stage. She took her notebook from the chair. The cover was cool, slightly damp from the room’s chill. She turned and walked back across the vast floor, her steps quicker now, a deliberate rhythm. She didn’t look back. The door groaned shut behind her, sealing the hall and its single, sleeping occupant back into their separate night.

The walk home felt longer. The cold bit through her coat. She kept thinking about drafts. The things you burn first. Not because they were the most dangerous, but because they were the most honest. They showed your process, your doubts, your changes. Final copies were clean. They could be explained. Drafts were the evidence of thought. She thought of her own reports, the ones she filed in the white envelopes. They were final copies. Sterile, approved. The draft of the real report—the one she’d crumpled and hidden under the sheet music—that was the dangerous thing. That showed her thinking. She hadn’t burned it. She’d saved it. A stupid, sentimental risk.

When she reached her apartment building, the lobby was warm and smelled of cabbage soup from somewhere. She climbed the stairs, each step a small effort. Inside, the apartment was silent. Langston was out, or asleep in the bedroom. She went to the desk. She opened the bottom drawer, the one that stuck, and felt beneath the stack of sheet music. The square of paper was there. She didn’t pull it out. She just touched it, the sharp corners against her fingertips. Then she closed the drawer.

She poured a drink, not measuring it this time. The gin was a sharp, clarifying heat in her throat. She sat in the living room chair, the one that faced the blank wall where they’d never hung a picture. The taste of the gin lingered, a medicinal ghost. She thought of Pauline’s face in the lamplight, the murmured words about smoke. Something hot moved through her chest—not quite anger, not quite panic, something between the two that didn’t have a name. It was the feeling of standing too close to a furnace. You could feel the destructive heat, but also the terrible, drawing light.

The gin’s heat faded, leaving a bitter aftertaste like chewing on a pine needle. Iris sat in the chair until the streetlights outside the window buzzed on, casting a jaundiced glow across the empty wall. She went to bed without turning on any other lamps. Langston was out, at some meeting or another. The sheets were cold.

Sleep was a shallow, fitful thing. She dreamed of a furnace door opening, not of the fire inside, but of the cool, dark space just before the flames caught. She woke before dawn, her mouth dry.

The rehearsal hall the next afternoon felt alien. The daylight was a harsh, flat scrutiny through the high windows, bleaching the colour from the wooden floor, exposing every scuff and scrape. The chairs were arranged in a semicircle again, the other actors already murmuring their lines, a low, anxious hum. Pauline stood at the centre, her back to the door as Iris entered. She wore the same charcoal coat, now buttoned, its collar neat. There was no sign of the woman who had slept at the desk.

“You’re late,” Pauline said, not turning. Her voice carried across the space, clear and dry.

Iris checked her wristwatch. She was two minutes past the call time. “The train was held.

“The train is always held. Anticipate it.” Pauline finally turned. Her eyes were sharp, focused, the softness of the night before incinerated. “We’re starting at the top of Act Two. Eleanor’s testimony. Places.

The other actors—Sam, who played the Navy lawyer, and a young woman named Jeanette playing the court stenographer—took their positions on the makeshift stage. Iris climbed the steps, her notebook feeling like a foreign object in her hand. The pages where she’d scored Eleanor’s silences now seemed childish, a map of a country that no longer existed.

“From ‘I saw the loading procedures,’” Pauline called, settling into a folding chair directly before the stage. She didn’t open her script. She just watched.

Sam began his questioning, his voice a practised, reasonable baritone. Iris responded, delivering Eleanor’s lines about the faulty winches, the rushed orders. The words came out flat, technical. A recitation.

“Stop.” Pauline’s voice cut through. She didn’t raise it. “Iris. What are you doing?

“The lines.”

“You’re reading a manual. You’re a witness. You saw three hundred men die. You are trying to explain the inexplicable to a man who has already decided not to believe you. Again. From ‘I saw the loading procedures.’ And this time, look at him. Not at the floor.

They began again. Iris forced herself to meet Sam’s eyes. She saw the actor’s polite concentration, the slight flicker of impatience. She pushed her voice, trying to inject a tremor.

“Stop.” Pauline stood up. She walked to the edge of the stage, close enough that Iris could see the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the faint smudge of fatigue beneath them. “You’re indicating. You’re showing me ‘this is where the character feels sad.’ I don’t want to see the feeling. I want to see the fight against it. The cost of holding it in. Your voice is too clean. It hurts, doesn’t it? To push it that way?

A cold prickle ran over Iris’s scalp. She gave a slight, stiff nod. Her throat did ache, a raw, familiar burn.

“Good. Use that. The pain is the truth your character can’t say. The winches and the procedures are the lie she hides behind. So hide. But let us see you hiding. Again. From the top.

The third time was worse. Iris felt exposed, every syllable scraping. She got to the line, “The lieutenant said it was standard,” and her voice cracked, a genuine, ugly break.

Pauline was silent for a long moment. The hall was quiet save for the distant rumble of a truck on the street below. “Closer,” she said, finally. Her gaze was relentless. “But you’re still protecting her. Protecting yourself. Eleanor isn’t you. She’s braver than you. She has to say the thing she can never take back.” She stepped onto the stage, ignoring Sam and Jeanette. She stood directly in front of Iris, too close for comfort. “What is the thing you can never take back, Iris?

The air left the room. Iris’s mouth went dry. She could smell the faint, clean scent of the cold on Pauline’s coat, the same scent from last night. This was no longer about the play. This was a trapdoor opening beneath her feet.

“I don’t know what you mean.”

“Yes, you do.” Pauline’s voice dropped, a confidential pitch that felt more dangerous than a shout. “It’s not in the script. It’s in the breath before the line. It’s in the space between what you say and what you mean. You’re an expert in that space. Use it. For once, use it for something true.

The hot, nameless thing from the night before moved through Iris’s chest again. It tightened her throat, made her hands feel cold. She looked past Pauline, at the high window where a pigeon had landed on the sill, pecking at something invisible.

“The line is, ‘I knew it was wrong,’” Iris said, her voice barely above a whisper.

“Then say it as if you’ve just discovered the meaning of the word.” Pauline didn’t move. “Say it to me.

Iris looked at her. She saw the challenge, the old, unforgiving scrutiny. She saw the faint shadow of the woman who had murmured about smoke in her sleep. The furnace door was open. The drawing light was terrible.

“I knew it was wrong,” Iris said. The words didn’t feel like lines. They felt like stones dropped into a still pool. The ache in her throat was real, a physical anchor.

Pauline held her gaze for a beat too long. Then she gave a single, slow nod. Something in her own expression shifted, a slight thaw at the edges, a recognition that felt like a wound. “Yes,” she said, quietly. “That’s it. Now keep that. Don’t let it go.” She turned and walked back to her chair. “Sam, from your last line. Let’s run the scene.

The rest of the rehearsal passed in a blur. Iris moved through the motions, but the core of her felt raw, scraped clean. Pauline’s direction became technical again, notes about pacing, about volume. The brutal intimacy was over, locked away as if it had never happened. But the charge of it remained in the air, a vibration just below the audible.

During a break, Iris went to the water fountain in the hall. The metal was cold against her lips. She heard footsteps and turned. It was Clara, Pauline’s daughter, carrying a box of light gels. The young woman’s expression was its usual careful neutral, but her eyes lingered on Iris’s face a moment too long.

“You okay?” Clara asked. “You look pale.

“Just thirsty,” Iris said.

Clara nodded, hefting the box. “She’s in a mood today. Pushing everyone.” She paused, as if weighing her words. “It usually means she thinks they can take it. For what that’s worth.” Then she walked off, her shoes making soft, decisive sounds on the floor.

Iris stood by the fountain, the taste of tinny water in her mouth. She thought of Clara’s words. She thinks they can take it. A strange, thin thread of something like gratitude pulled tight in her, followed immediately by a wave of crushing guilt. She was not being forged into something brave. She was being hollowed out to serve as a container for a lie, a performance that would be watched by men in grey suits. Pauline’s brutal direction was a sculptor’s hand, but the material was rotten. It would not hold the shape.

When she returned, Pauline was discussing lighting cues with the student technician, her voice all business. She didn’t glance at Iris. The coat was draped over the back of her chair. On the floor beside it, partly tucked under the seat, was a single, crumpled page. It wasn’t a script page. It was a bank draft, the kind used for large transfers. Iris caught only a glimpse as she passed—the ornate letterhead of a financial institution, the amount in dense black numbers, and in the memo line, a single typed word: Patronage.

She took her place on stage without a word. The pigeon at the window had flown away. The sill was empty.

The dressing room smelled of damp wool and the cold cream she’d used to remove her makeup. A single bare bulb hung from a cord, casting a harsh, unforgiving light on the chipped porcelain sink, the mirror with its silvering peeling away at the edges like old skin. Iris leaned against the closed door, the wood solid against her back. The silence here was different from the hall’s—a small, tight silence, a held breath in a cramped space. She could still feel the phantom pressure of Pauline’s gaze, that new, surgical intensity that had carved into her all afternoon. Eleanor’s lie is in her breath.

She pushed off the door and went to the sink. The water ran rusty for a moment before clearing. She splashed her face, the cold a shock that didn’t reach the deeper fatigue. In the mottled mirror, her face looked unfamiliar. Hollows under the eyes. A tightness around the mouth that hadn’t been there a month ago. She was scoring a character who was disintegrating, and some of the dust was getting under her own skin. She dried her face on a rough towel that smelled of other people’s sweat.

The scrape of a chair leg on the floorboards made her freeze. The towel stayed pressed to her cheeks.

She hadn’t heard the door open. She would have heard the groan of the hinges. She lowered the towel.

Langston sat in the room’s only armchair, a battered thing with springs poking through the upholstery. He must have been waiting in the dark corner behind the door. He crossed one leg over the other, smoothing the crease of his trousers. His overcoat was folded neatly over the chair’s arm. He looked like he’d been there for hours.

“You work late,” he said. His voice was pleasant. Empty.

Iris’s heart did a strange, lurching thing—not a jump, but a slow, heavy slide against her ribs. She draped the towel over the sink’s edge. The damp spot spread. “The rehearsal ran long.

“So I gathered.” He didn’t smile. His eyes, a flat grey in the bad light, traveled over her—the plain work shirt, the slacks, the absence of lipstick. “You look tired.

“It’s demanding work.”

“I’m sure it is.” He let the silence sit between them. It wasn’t the felt-like silence of the hall. This was a thin, brittle thing, like ice over a puddle. He reached into his jacket’s inner pocket and withdrew a silver cigarette case. It was new. She’d never seen it before. The lid snapped open with a crisp, expensive sound. He offered her one.

She shook her head.

He took one for himself, tapped it twice on the case, and lit it with a matching silver lighter. The first exhale was a slow, deliberate stream that fogged the space between them. The smoke smelled of good Turkish tobacco, not the cheap stuff he usually smoked. “How is the play coming along?

“It’s coming.”

“Pauline must be pleased. She’s driving you hard.

“She’s a thorough director.”

“I watched for a bit. From the back.” He took another drag. “You were quite good. A different kind of performance for you. Less… melodic. More architectural.

The words were a compliment. The tone was an audit. Iris’s throat felt dry. She wanted a drink. There was nothing in the room but a tin cup by the sink. “Thank you.

“She trusts you,” Langston said. It wasn’t a question.

“She trusts the work.”

“Is there a difference?” He leaned forward, elbows on his knees, the cigarette held loosely between his fingers. Ash drifted to the floor. “I need your report.

“I mailed it this morning.”

“The official one, yes. The four-sentence ghost.” He smiled, a thin movement of his lips. “I mean the real one. The progress report.

A cold knot tightened low in her stomach. This was new. The specificity of it. He’d always asked for ‘the report,’ for ‘anything of interest.’ Never ‘progress.’ “I don’t know what you mean.

“Don’t you?” He studied the burning tip of his cigarette. “Cross is getting impatient. Vague assurances about morale and rehearsals aren’t what he’s paying for. He needs something he can use. Something that demonstrates your… access.

“My access is to the script. To blocking. To character motivation.

“Your access,” Langston said, his voice dropping into a lower, more confidential register, though there was no one to hear, “is to Pauline Carter. To her habits. Her associates. The people who come and go from this place after hours. The sources of her funding.” He looked up at her. “The things that worry her.

The coat. The whisper in the dark. The drafts first. Iris’s fingers found the edge of the sink, the cool porcelain. She traced a hairline crack with her thumb. “I’m an actress in her play. Not her confidante.

“You were once.”

“That was twelve years ago.”

“And yet.” He let the word hang. He took a final drag and stubbed the cigarette out on the sole of his shoe, then deposited the butt in the tin cup by the sink. A petty, precise act of defilement. “She’s working on something new. Not the Port Chicago play. Something else. Notes, fragments. I’ve heard rumors.

“What kind of rumors?”

“The kind that make Cross’s superiors very interested.” He stood, brushing a speck of ash from his lapel. He was close now, close enough for her to smell the tobacco on his breath, the faint, clean scent of his shaving soap. “Find out what it is. What she’s drafting. Who’s involved. Where the money originates.

“I’m not a detective.”

“You’re whatever we need you to be.” He said it gently, almost sadly. He reached out and straightened the collar of her shirt, his fingers brushing her neck. A familiar, marital gesture that felt like a violation. “This isn’t a punishment, Iris. It’s an opportunity. Give them something substantive, something that proves your value, and the pressure lifts. For both of us. We could be done with this.

We. The word was a trap. It always had been. She looked past his shoulder at the water stain on the ceiling. It had a new branch, a dark tendril creeping toward the light fixture. “And if I can’t?

He sighed, a sound of genuine exasperation. He picked up his folded coat. “Then you’re not trying. And Cross will conclude you’re compromised. That your loyalties are… conflicted.” He slid his arms into the coat, settling it on his shoulders with a practiced shrug. “He’s not a patient man. And his options for leverage extend beyond your career.

He didn’t elaborate. He didn’t need to. The apartment. The bank account. The ghost of a life they’d built on a foundation of quiet compromises. Clara.

Langston moved to the door. His hand rested on the knob. “You have a week. Bring me something useful. Names. Documents. The distant sounds of the building filtered in—a pipe clanging, a door shutting somewhere down the hall. “You know how to listen. You know how to watch. Use the skills you have.

He left, pulling the door shut behind him with a soft, final click.

Iris stood in the center of the room. The smoke from his cigarette still hung in the air, a slow, coiling grey in the lamplight. She could taste it at the back of her throat. She walked to the tin cup, picked it up, and looked at the crumpled, damp filter inside. A luxury cigarette. A silver case. A new pen in his breast pocket last week. He was spending money. Or someone was giving it to him.

She poured the butt into the palm of her hand. It was still warm. She carried it to the small, high window, cranked it open a few inches, and tossed the fragment out into the night. A gust of cold air rushed in, sharp and clean, cutting through the smoke. She left the window open.

Back at the sink, she ran the water again, scrubbed her hands with the sliver of soap until the smell of tobacco was gone. Her own face in the mirror looked no clearer. The hollows were still there. The tightness.

He’d watched from the back. He’d seen Pauline direct her. He’d seen the charge in the space between them, the old current forced into a new circuit. And he’d decided that was the lever. Not the play’s content, not the institute’s roster. Her. Her history. Her unresolved grief. He was asking her to weaponize the last fragile thread still connecting her to Pauline.

The silence in the room was no longer tight. It was vast. An empty theatre after the audience had fled. She turned off the light and stood in the dark, listening to the wind sigh through the cracked window, feeling the cold seep into her bones.
