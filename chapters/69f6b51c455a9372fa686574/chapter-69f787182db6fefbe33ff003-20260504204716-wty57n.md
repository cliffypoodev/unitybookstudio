The apartment was a museum of silence. Iris stood in the center of the living room, the parquet floor cool under her stockinged feet, and the quiet had a new quality. Not the woolen hush of before, but something brittle and sharp, like the air after a pane of glass shatters. She had come home from the theatre not in a taxi, but on foot, walking the fifty blocks north through the gathering dark until her heels blistered and the cold air scraped her throat raw. Langston was not there. His absence was a tangible object in the room—a suit-shaped void by the liquor cabinet, a missing cigarette smoldering in the cut-glass ashtray. He had left, or he had been called away. It didn’t matter. The result was the same: this profound, echoing stillness.

On the writing desk by the window, the typewriter waited. A fresh sheet of paper, impossibly white, was rolled into the carriage. Cross’s final instruction, delivered in a low monotone backstage as the chaos of the walkout swirled around them, echoed in the hollow of her ear. A statement. For the press. Your retraction. By tomorrow morning. He hadn’t looked at her when he said it. He had watched the stagehands strike the set, his profile a clean, indifferent line. Make it convincing.

Her hands were cold. She rubbed them together, a dry, papery sound. The gesture felt borrowed from someone else. She walked to the window. The streetlamps below were haloes of mist in the February drizzle. A delivery truck rumbled past, its tires hissing on the wet asphalt. A perfectly ordinary sound. The world continued. That was the worst of it—the sheer, insulting normality of a Tuesday evening.

She went to the liquor cabinet. The gin bottle was nearly full. Langston’s scotch, the expensive single malt, stood beside it like a sentinel. She poured two fingers of gin into a water glass, not measuring, not performing the ritual. She drank it in one go. The heat was a blunt instrument, a shock that traveled down to her stomach and settled there, a small, defiant fire. It didn’t warm her hands.

The typewriter keys were metal teeth. She sat before it, her fingers hovering over the home row. My name is Iris Finch. She had said that on the stage. It had felt, for one dizzying moment, like the truest sentence she had ever spoken. Now, it was just a name to be disavowed.

She typed the date. February 22, 1949. The clack of the key was obscenely loud.

She began.

To whom it may concern,

Wrong. She yanked the paper out, crumpled it, tossed it toward the wastebasket. It missed, a white blossom on the floor.

Last evening, during a performance at the Harlem Institute of Dramatic Arts, I suffered a regrettable lapse in professional conduct.

A lapse. A tiny, insignificant crack. Not a chasm. Not a deliberate leap. She stared at the words. Her throat tightened, a specific, familiar constriction just below her jaw. The ghost of her singing voice, the one that had failed her years ago, seemed to ache in sympathy. She took another sip of gin, straight from the bottle this time. The taste was of juniper and regret, a flavor she knew as well as her own breath.

On the wall opposite the desk, a hairline crack in the plaster meandered from the ceiling to the picture rail. She had never noticed it before. It forked near the bottom, one branch veering toward the light switch, the other dying out in the smooth beige plane. It looked like a river on a map of a barren country. She focused on it, letting the crack fill her vision, a welcome escape from the white page. Where did it start? Probably the building settling. Or a pipe had burst in the apartment above, sometime last spring. Langston would have called the super. He handled those things. He would have done it with efficient, faintly annoyed phone calls, his back to her as he spoke into the receiver. Yes, a crack. In the living room. It’s unsightly.

She typed again.

Under the strain of personal circumstances, I inadvertently conflated my own history with the fictional narrative of the play. I offer my deepest apologies to the audience, to the Institute, and to the Committee for the Un-American Activities, whose vital work I have always respected.

The lie was so smooth. So professional. It had the greasy feel of Langston’s hair tonic. She could almost hear him dictating it, pacing behind her chair. That’s the tone, darling. Contrite, but not groveling. A touch of feminine fragility. They’ll accept it.

But Cross hadn’t asked for Langston. He had asked for her. Your retraction. He wanted her voice. Her specific, broken instrument.

A memory surfaced, unbidden and utterly useless. Not of Paris, not of Pauline. Of her mother’s kitchen in Ohio, 1928. The smell of burning toast. Her mother at the stove, swearing softly, a rare, vivid word that hung in the steam. Iris, maybe ten years old, was practicing scales at the upright piano in the parlour. She hit a wrong note, a flat, sour G. Her mother didn’t turn around. “You can’t un-sing it, Iris,” she had said, her voice tired. “Once it’s out, it’s out in the world. All you can do is sing the next note true.

What a ridiculous thing to remember. Her mother had been talking about toast, probably. Not art. Not truth. Just burnt bread.

Iris put her forehead in her hands. The pressure behind her eyes was a slow, steady throb. She hadn’t cried backstage. Not when the grey suits walked out. Not when the other actors stared at her, a mixture of horror and a kind of giddy awe. Not when Pauline, finally, had emerged from the wings. Pauline had stopped three feet away. She hadn’t touched her. She had just looked, her dark eyes taking in Iris’s shaking hands, the sweat at her temples, the borrowed dress. “Well,” Pauline had said, the word a neutral observation. Then she had turned and addressed the stage manager, her voice crisp, all business. “Strike the set. The performance is concluded.” That was all. No condemnation. No absolution. Just the work.

The work was over now. All that remained was the paperwork.

She typed faster, the keys jamming in their haste.

I wish to clarify that my association with certain elements at the Institute was purely professional. Any impression of deeper sympathy for radical or un-American viewpoints was a tragic error of perception, compounded by my own emotional state.

Elements. A sterile, bureaucratic word. It could mean a draft under a door. It could mean carbon and hydrogen. It erased faces. It erased Clara’s skilled hands mending a prop, the fierce concentration on her daughter’s face. It erased the young actor who played the doomed sailor, whose voice had broken every night in rehearsal on the line “I just want it to be accounted for.” It erased Pauline’s tapping pencil, the syncopated rhythm of her thought.

Iris’s fingers stilled. A hot, sour taste flooded her mouth. She swallowed it down. This wasn’t anger. It was something more specific, more shameful: the visceral disgust of a butcher washing his hands after a long day’s work. The water runs red, then pink, then clear. The job is done.

She couldn’t do it.

The realization was quiet. It didn’t arrive with a bang or a flash of insight. It simply was, as if it had been waiting in the room all along, patient as the crack in the wall. She could not put her name to this. She could not sing this next note.

The door to the apartment clicked open.

She didn’t turn. Footsteps in the foyer, deliberate, familiar. The rustle of a wool coat being shed. The clink of keys dropped into the ceramic bowl on the hall table. Langston’s rituals. He entered the living room, stopping just behind her chair. She could smell the cold on his overcoat, the faint, expensive scent of his shaving soap. And underneath it, something else—the acrid, smoky smell of a government building.

“Iris.”

She stared at the half-written lie on the page.

“I’ve been with Cross,” he said. His voice was calm, drained. “And his superiors. It’s a mess. A goddamn historic mess.” He moved around the desk, leaning against it, facing her. He looked older. A new tension pulled at the corners of his mouth, etched fine lines around his eyes that the dim light carved into shadows. “But it’s containable. This,” he gestured at the typewriter, “is the first step. It needs to be good. It needs to be perfect.

“Where were you?” Her own voice sounded strange to her, raspy from disuse.

“Where do you think? Putting out the fire you started.” He pinched the bridge of his nose. “Do you have any concept of what you did? The reporters were already calling the Times by the time I got to the lobby. Strauss is apoplectic. He’s talking lawsuits, breach of contract.

He trailed off, his gaze landing on the gin bottle beside her. A flicker of disapproval, quickly suppressed. He needed her compliant now, not sodden.

“You had to what, Langston?”

“I had to re-negotiate. Our position. Everything.” He straightened his tie, a nervous habit. “The letters are public knowledge now. Thanks to your little soliloquy. They can’t be used as leverage anymore. That’s something. But in return, Cross wants a full, signed statement. A repudiation of the entire Institute project. And names. Specific names of anyone who encouraged your… breakdown.

There it was. The price. Not just her humiliation, but theirs. She was to be the vector, the carrier of the poison. She would get the quiet life, and in exchange, she would hand over the coordinates for the next targets.

“He already has the letters,” Iris said, slowly. “You gave them to him. What more does he want?

Langston’s face went very still. “It’s not about the letters anymore. It’s about the narrative. You created a narrative of coercion. Of persecution. He needs to dismantle it, brick by brick. He needs you to say you were confused. Unwell. That you were influenced by…” He waved a hand, searching for the phrase. “By malign influences.

“Pauline.”

“Among others.” He wouldn’t meet her eyes. He studied his own perfectly manicured nails. “It’s the only way forward, Iris. The only way we survive this.

We. The word was a trap, baited with twenty years of shared habit, of tax returns and dinner parties and silent car rides. She looked at him—really looked. At the fine grey threading his temples, at the slight paunch beneath his vest, at the elegant, helpless hands that had never built anything, only brokered. He was a man who understood cost and value. He had assessed her, twelve years ago, as a promising asset. He had assessed her tonight, and found her value catastrophically depreciated. His re-negotiation wasn’t for her sake. It was salvage. It was saving what he could of his own portfolio.

A profound tiredness washed through her, so complete it felt like gravity increasing. She wanted to lay her head on the typewriter keys and sleep for a century.

“What happens if I don’t?” she asked.

He blinked, as if the question were in a foreign language. “Don’t what?

“Don’t give him the statement. Don’t give him the names.

A long silence filled the room. The refrigerator kicked on in the kitchen, a low, metallic hum. Langston pushed himself off the desk and walked to the window, his back to her. He looked out at the drizzle. “Then they’ll subpoena you. Publicly. The hearing will be a circus. They’ll have the letters entered into the official record. They’ll call Pauline. They’ll call everyone from the Institute. They’ll tear it all apart, piece by piece, and you’ll be at the center of it, until there’s nothing left. Of you. Of them.” He turned. His expression was not cruel. It was clinical. “You won’t win, Iris. You’ll just take everyone down with you. Is that what you want? After everything Pauline did to protect you back then? To throw her to them now?

It was a masterstroke. He had listened, all those nights she had whispered her guilty, broken version of Paris. He had absorbed her story and now he weaponized its twisted logic. He was offering her a chance to be the coward she had always believed herself to be, and framing it as a perverse form of protection.

The crack in the wall seemed to pulse in her peripheral vision. A river on a map. Leading nowhere.

“I need some air,” she said, standing up. Her legs held.

“Iris, we need to finish this.”

“I’ll finish it.” The lie came easily. “I just need to walk. To clear my head.

He studied her, his manager’s eye assessing her stability. He saw the exhaustion, the defeat. He misread it entirely. He nodded, a curt, satisfied gesture. “One hour. I’ll wait here. We’ll draft it together when you get back.

She didn’t take her coat. She just walked out, closing the apartment door softly behind her, leaving him in the museum of their life. The hallway carpet was a faded maroon, worn thin in a path to the elevator. She took the stairs instead, her footsteps echoing in the concrete well. The cold air in the lobby was a shock. The night dozier, an old man named Frank, nodded from behind his desk. “Evening, Mrs. Finch.

She pushed through the heavy brass doors into the drizzle. It was a fine, needling rain now. She turned left, walking without direction. The streets were glossy black, reflecting the neon signs of a diner, a drugstore. A few people hurried under umbrellas. She felt invisible, a ghost in her own city.

Her hand slipped into the pocket of her dress. Her fingers closed not around the Zippo lighter—that was ash in a theatre ashtray—but around a small, folded square of paper. She had almost forgotten it. Clara’s note. The one she had found weeks ago, with the Brooklyn address and the single word: Printer.

She stopped under the awning of a closed haberdashery. She unfolded the paper, the creases soft and worn. The address was in Red Hook. A place for documents, Clara had said. For exits.

Iris looked back toward her building, a fortress of light and warmth and terrible compromise. She saw the window of her living room, a yellow rectangle in the gloom. Langston would be pacing. He would be pouring a scotch, planning the phrasing of her surrender.

She looked down at the address. Then she looked up, searching for a taxi. Her hand, of its own volition, went up. A cab slid to the curb, its tires whispering on the wet street.

“Where to, lady?”

She gave him the address. Her voice didn’t shake.

As the cab pulled away from the curb, she watched her apartment window shrink in the rear window, a single, bright eye that winked once and was swallowed by the night.

The cab left her at the corner of 135th, not at the institute’s steps. She paid the driver, the coins cold and slick in her palm, and watched the taillights recede down the wet street. The walk was three blocks. She took them slowly, her borrowed dress—a simple wool shift from the costume rack—scratching at her neck with every step. It smelled of other people’s sweat and mothballs, a communal scent. Her own clothes, the careful navy suit she’d worn that afternoon, were folded in a department store bag backstage. She had changed after Langston left the apartment. A small, superstitious ritual: shedding one skin for another.

The institute’s facade was dark except for the single bulb above the arched doorway. It cast a jaundiced circle on the worn stone steps. From inside, the low murmur of an audience settling, the rustle of programs, the distant squeak of a seat. A performance night. Just another show. She pushed the heavy door open.

The lobby was empty. The ticket table, draped in black cloth, held a neat stack of unused programs. Clara usually manned it. She was nowhere. Iris walked past the closed doors to the house, toward the backstage corridor. Her heels clicked on the linoleum, a metronome marking time.

Backstage was a pocket of organized chaos. A stagehand she didn’t know—a young man with a pencil behind his ear—rushed past with a coil of rope. He didn’t look at her. From the green room, she heard the low, tense warm-up of an actor running lines, the words a familiar, meaningless rhythm. My life, my life, what have you done with my life? Eleanor’s first line.

Pauline stood in the wings, stage left, a silhouette against the heavy black curtain. She held a clipboard, but she wasn’t looking at it. She was looking at the narrow gap where the curtain didn’t quite meet, a sliver of view into the house. The light from the stage washed her profile in a pale gold, sharpening the angle of her cheekbone, the line of her jaw. She didn’t turn as Iris approached.

“They’re all here,” Pauline said, her voice a flat statement. “The review from the Times. Strauss and his party in the fourth row. And your friends in the third.

Iris stopped a few feet away. She could see the sliver now. The house lights were dimming. Rows of faces, pale ovals in the dark, all turned toward the empty stage. In the third row, three grey suits sat in a neat, grim line. Cross was in the middle. He was looking at his program.

“Langston?” Iris asked.

“Fifth row, aisle. With a blonde. I don’t recognize her.

Iris’s stomach did a slow, cold roll. Not jealousy. Something emptier. A confirmation of a fact she already knew, like learning the date of a historical event you’d lived through.

The stage manager, a wiry woman named Gwen, appeared at Pauline’s elbow. “House lights at half,” she whispered, though the audience couldn’t possibly hear. “Places in one.

Pauline nodded. She finally turned. Her eyes moved over Iris, taking in the dress, the lack of makeup, the raw look of her. There was no criticism in the glance. No approval either. It was an assessment, purely technical. “You remember the blocking for the final monologue?

“Yes.”

“Good.” Pauline looked back toward the stage. The curtain was still closed. “The microphone is live. It feeds to a recorder in the booth. Strauss insisted. For the ‘historic moment.’” She said the last two words with a dry, bitter twist. “They’ll have a record of whatever happens.

Iris’s mouth was dry. She tried to swallow, but her throat refused. “Pauline.

Pauline held up a hand, a quiet, final gesture. “Don’t. Whatever you’re about to say. Don’t say it to me. Say it to them.” She nodded toward the sliver of darkness beyond the curtain. “That’s the deal, isn’t it? You have something to say. So say it.

The house lights went to black. A collective intake of breath from the audience, a sound like wind through trees. The curtain began to rise, its heavy mechanism groaning. The stage lights came up, a warm, forgiving amber that transformed the sparse set—a wooden chair, a plain table, a window frame with no glass—into something solemn, significant.

Gwen touched Iris’s arm. “You’re on.

Iris walked onto the stage.

The light was a physical thing. It pressed against her skin, warm and heavy. She took her position in the chair, as rehearsed. The other actors entered, spoke their lines. The scene unfolded with a terrible, practiced inevitability. Eleanor’s confession. The damning letters. The collapse of a life built on a single, buried truth. Iris spoke her lines, but the words felt like stones in her mouth. She could hear the emptiness in her own voice, a hollow echo where emotion should have been. She was going through the motions. A performance of a performance.

From the corner of her eye, she saw Cross. He had set his program aside. He was watching her, his hands folded in his lap. His expression was unreadable from this distance, just a pale, attentive mask.

The scene built to its climax. Eleanor, cornered, desperate, was meant to break down weeping, to beg for a forgiveness the play would never grant. The actor opposite her, a young man named David, delivered his final accusing line with perfect, brittle clarity. “You have poisoned everything you touched.

Silence. A beat. This was her cue. The weeping. The collapse.

Iris stood up from the chair. The movement was wrong. Not in the blocking. She walked away from David, away from the table, toward the very edge of the stage. The apron. The lights hit her full here, bleaching her vision into a white haze. She could see nothing but the glare, and beneath it, the vague, dark soup of the audience.

She turned her head, not toward David, but out. Toward the third row.

“My name is Iris Finch,” she said. Her voice was quiet, conversational. The microphone above would catch it. “I am an informant for the House Committee on Un-American Activities. I was sent here to watch you. To report on you.

A mutter rose from the darkness. A rustle of fabric, a creak of seats.

She gestured vaguely, a sweep of her hand. “I file reports on what plays you read. What poems you whisper. Who you love.” She paused. Her own breath was loud in her ears. “They have letters. Letters about love. They treat them like evidence of a crime. They are not evidence. They are just… letters. The last true thing I ever wrote.

From the wings, a choked sound. Gwen, maybe. Or one of the actors. She didn’t look.

“I traded them,” Iris continued. The words were heavy, each one a distinct weight she had to lift and set down. “For a quiet life. That was the deal.

In the third row, one of the grey suits stood up. A dark shape against the gloom. Then another. They didn’t rush. They turned, with a terrible, deliberate dignity, and began to walk up the aisle. Their exit was a silent, spreading stain. Others followed. A rustling exodus from the fourth row—Strauss and his party. The scrape of chairs, the whisper of expensive cloth.

Iris kept talking. She talked about the silence. The cost of a song not sung. She talked about Paris, not by name, but by the smell of rain on cobblestones, the taste of cheap red wine, the specific blue of a morning sky over the Seine. She talked until the words ran out. Until she was just standing there, in the blinding light, in the echoing quiet.

The curtain came down. Not with a smooth, mechanical grace, but in a jerky, hurried fall, as if someone had panicked and pulled the lever. It cut off the light, the view, leaving her in sudden, velvety darkness backstage.

For a moment, there was no sound but her own breathing. Then chaos erupted. Muffled shouts from the house. Gwen, somewhere, saying “Oh my God, oh my God.” The sound of a door slamming.

Iris didn’t move. The afterimage of the lights pulsed in her vision, green and purple blotches swimming in the black. She felt a hand on her elbow. Pauline.

“Come.” The word was short, urgent.

Pauline pulled her through the darkness, past the stumbling shapes of other actors, into the narrow passage that led to the dressing rooms. She shoved Iris into the small, shared room and closed the door. The only light came from a single bulb above the mirror, surrounded by cracked, star-shaped shades.

The room smelled of greasepaint and fear.

Pauline leaned against the closed door, her eyes closed. She took a long, slow breath. When she opened her eyes, they were on Iris. There was no anger there. No triumph. Just a deep, profound exhaustion. “Well,” she said. Her voice was hoarse. “You said it.

From the corridor outside, a man’s voice, sharp and official. “Where is she?

Pauline’s gaze flickered to the door, then back to Iris. A decision passed over her face, swift and final. She pushed away from the door and walked to the costume rack. She pulled down a heavy, dark coat—a man’s overcoat, from some forgotten production. “Put this on. The back alley. Clara’s waiting with a car.

Iris stared at the coat. “What?

“They’re not here for a debate, Iris. They’re here to make an example. You just handed them the script.” Pauline thrust the coat at her. “Move.

The knob on the dressing room door rattled. A fist pounded, once, twice. “Open up. Federal agent.

Pauline’s hand went to the bolt lock, old and brass. She slid it home with a soft, solid click. The sound was absurdly loud.

“The window,” she said, nodding toward the small, grimy pane high on the wall. It led to the airshaft, and from there, to the alley. “Go.

Iris shrugged into the coat. It swallowed her, the wool rough and smelling of dust. She dragged a wooden chair under the window, climbed up. The window stuck. She shoved at the sash with the heel of her hand. It gave with a screech of protesting wood.

She looked down. Pauline stood by the chair, her face tilted up. In the bad light, she looked twelve years younger. She looked like she had in Paris, in that cramped room above the printer’s shop, the night before Iris left. That same unreadable expression, caught between a plea and a farewell.

“The cage is open,” Pauline said, quiet, so quiet Iris almost didn’t hear it over the renewed pounding on the door. “Now fly.

Iris hauled herself through the window. The airshaft was a narrow canyon of brick, smelling of wet stone and garbage. A metal fire escape ladder was bolted to the wall a few feet down. She jumped, her hands grabbing cold, wet iron. She climbed down, the rungs biting into her palms.

The alley was dark, pooled with oily water. A car idled at the mouth, its headlights off. The passenger door opened. Clara’s face, pale and tense, appeared in the dim light from the street. “Get in.

Iris ran the last few steps, slid into the passenger seat. The car—a nondescript black sedan—pulled away before her door was fully closed. Clara drove, her knuckles white on the wheel. She didn’t speak. She turned onto a broader avenue, joining the sparse, late-night traffic.

After five blocks, Clara let out a shaky breath. “She’s not coming.

Iris knew. The click of the bolt. The look on Pauline’s face. She had stayed to hold the door. To give them time. “No.

Clara drove in silence for another ten minutes, weaving through side streets. Finally, she pulled over next to a small, shuttered diner. The sign read ‘Joe’s’ in flickering neon. She cut the engine. The only sound was the tick of the cooling metal.

“There’s a train,” Clara said, still looking straight ahead through the windshield. “Penn Station. The midnight to Chicago. From there, you can get to the coast. There are people who can get you on a boat to Canada. Or to Mexico. The network has routes.

Iris looked at her. Clara’s profile was sharp, young. She had Pauline’s jawline. “The network.

“It’s what I do.” Clara finally turned. Her eyes were red-rimmed, but dry. “I help people disappear. I’ve been helping them for two years. Since I understood what they were doing to us.” She reached into the glove compartment, pulled out a small envelope. “Ticket. Fake ID. Some money. It’s not much.

Iris took the envelope. It was thin, light. “You knew. This whole time.

“I knew you were afraid. I knew you were lying. I didn’t know about the letters until tonight.” Clara’s voice cracked, just once. “She loved you. Even after. It made her so angry, but she never stopped.

Iris looked down at the envelope in her hands. The paper was cheap, rough. “What will they do to her?

Clara was silent for a long time. A truck rumbled past on the cross street, its headlights sweeping the interior of the car. “I don’t know,” she said, her voice small. “I’ll find out. I’ll do what I can.” She nodded toward the diner. “You should go in. Use the washroom. The train leaves in forty minutes. You can walk from here.

Iris opened the car door. The cold night air rushed in. She hesitated, one foot on the curb. “Thank you, Clara.

Clara didn’t look at her. She stared at her hands on the wheel. “Just go.

Iris got out. She closed the door gently. The car didn’t move. She walked toward the diner, the heavy coat dragging on her shoulders. She pushed the door open. The diner was empty except for a tired-looking counterman wiping down the Formica. The air smelled of old coffee and bleach. She went past the stools, down a short corridor to the washroom.

The room was tiny, lit by a flickering fluorescent tube. She locked the door and leaned against it. Her reflection in the smudged mirror was a stranger’s—hair wild, eyes too bright, drowning in a man’s coat. She set the envelope on the edge of the sink and turned on the tap. The water ran cold. She cupped her hands under it, splashed her face. The shock was clarifying.

She looked at the envelope. Her way out. A clean end. A disappearance.

From the coat pocket, her fingers found the familiar shape of the Zippo lighter from Paris. She hadn’t even known she’d grabbed it when she left the apartment. A reflex. She took it out, turned it over in her hand. The metal was worn smooth in places, the engraving—a tiny, stylized bird—almost faded away.

She flicked the wheel. It sparked, but no flame came. It was empty. Had been for years.

A dry laugh escaped her, a short, harsh sound in the tiled room. She put the lighter back in her pocket. She picked up the envelope, tore it open along the seam. The train ticket. A slip of paper with a name that wasn’t hers: Margaret Doyle. A photograph of a woman who looked nothing like her. Fifty dollars in worn bills.

She could do it. Walk to the station. Become Margaret Doyle. Vanish into the vast, anonymous country. It was what she had wanted for twelve years. To be left alone. To be safe.

The counterman had a radio on, low. The tinny sound of an orchestra filtered down the hall, a sweet, sentimental tune. A commercial break. A man’s voice, smooth and reassuring, began to advertise a new brand of refrigerator. Keep your family’s future fresh and secure.

Iris looked at her reflection again. At the woman in the borrowed dress, the stolen coat, holding a ticket to nowhere. The fear was still there, a cold stone in her gut. But underneath it, something else had taken root. A quiet, stubborn certainty. She had spoken the truth. It was done. There was no unsinging it.

She folded the ticket, the money, the fake ID back into the envelope. She didn’t put it in her pocket. She left it on the edge of the sink.

She walked out of the washroom, past the counterman who gave her a incurious nod, and back out into the night. She turned not toward Penn Station, but north, back the way she had come. The coat was too big. It didn’t matter. She walked, her footsteps steady on the empty sidewalk. The city around her was a map of dark windows and distant lights, a landscape she knew by heart. She knew where she was going.
