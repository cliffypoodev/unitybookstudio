The light in the theatre had a different quality in the late afternoon. It came in low through the high, grimy windows, slicing the dust into solid, golden bars before it dissolved into the general gloom of the stage. Iris stood just inside the double doors, letting them swing shut behind her with a soft, cushioned thud. The air smelled of old wood, dust, and the faint, sweet-sharp ghost of oranges from someone’s lunch. A single work light burned above the stage, a bare bulb in a wire cage that threw the set into stark relief—a suggestion of a living room, two armchairs angled like teeth, a sideboard that listed slightly to the left. The emptiness of the house was a physical pressure against her eardrums.

She’d meant to arrive early, to find a corner, to prepare. But her feet had dragged on the walk from the subway, each step a separate negotiation. The memory of the piano’s dissonant cluster still vibrated in the bones of her hands. A cold knot, distinct and heavy, had taken up residence just below her ribs. Not fear, exactly. Something more precise—the taut, specific dread of a musician walking onto a stage knowing the instrument is out of tune and the audience hostile.

Pauline was already there. She sat in the front row of the empty house, her back to the doors, a silhouette against the dusty light. She didn’t turn. A script lay open on her lap, but she wasn’t reading it. Her head was tilted back, looking at the stage. One hand rested on the armrest, the fingers tapping out a slow, complex rhythm. A syncopated five-beat pattern. It was the same rhythm she’d tapped on a café table in Paris, arguing about Brecht.

Iris’s throat tightened. She walked down the side aisle, her heels clicking on the linoleum then muffling as she hit the worn runner. The sound seemed obscenely loud.

“You’re late,” Pauline said. She still didn’t turn.

“The train.”

“They all say that.” Pauline closed the script. The tap of her fingers stopped. “Clara’s running an errand. Arthur is in the prop closet looking for a serviceable ashtray. It’s just us for the top of the scene.

Just us. The words hung in the air, simple and lethal. Iris reached the edge of the stage, her hand brushing against the rough velvet of the curtain. A thread came loose, coiling around her finger like a tiny, dark vine. She didn’t pull it free.

“Where do you want me?”

“Where you’re meant to be.” Pauline finally turned her head. The work light caught the planes of her face, deepening the shadows under her eyes. She looked exhausted. “On the stage. In the chair stage left. Eleanor is waiting for her husband to come home. She’s been waiting for twenty-three minutes.

Iris climbed the three wooden steps. The stage floor creaked under her weight, a familiar, mournful sound. She walked to the armchair—a heavy, ugly thing upholstered in a faded cabbage-rose print. She sat. The springs sighed. From here, the house was a dark cavern, the rows of seats receding into blackness. Pauline was a shape in the front, her white blouse a pale smudge.

“You know the lines.”

“I know the lines.”

“Then don’t say them. Sit there. Be her. For twenty-three minutes.

Iris looked at her hands in her lap. Eleanor’s hands. They were supposed to be still. Composed. Waiting. Her own wanted to trace the rim of a glass, to find the Zippo in her pocket. She folded them together. The silence stretched. It wasn’t a peaceful silence. It was the silence of a held breath, of a room where a terrible thing has just been said and no one knows what comes next. She could hear the distant, metallic groan of a pipe in the walls. A truck rumbled past on the street outside, making the windowpanes buzz.

Pauline’s voice came from the dark, quiet and clear. “What does she hear?

Iris didn’t look up. “The clock.

“What kind of clock?”

“A mantel clock. With a brass pendulum. It ticks… it ticks with a little hitch, like it’s catching on something every second.

“Good. What else?

“The ice melting in her own drink. She hasn’t touched it.

“What does she see?”

“The pattern in the rug. It’s Persian. Blues and reds. Worn thin by the door. She’s counted the medallions. Seventeen. The fringe is tangled.

“What does she smell?”

Iris closed her eyes. The orange scent was gone. “Floor polish. The faint, sour smell of the drink. Her own perfume. It’s too sweet. She put on too much.

“Why?”

A simple question. A trapdoor. “Because he likes it.” The answer came automatically, the character’s lie. But it felt thin, a sheet over something sharper. She opened her eyes, stared at the dark hollow where Pauline sat. “No. Because she hopes he’ll notice she wore it. That he’ll say something. Anything.

The dark shape shifted. “And if he doesn’t?

“Then she’ll know.”

“Know what?”

“That it’s over. The pretending.

The words fell into the quiet theatre. They weren’t in the script. Eleanor wouldn’t think that, not so plainly. That was someone else’s thought, bleeding through.

Pauline was silent for a long moment. Then she stood. Her movements were fluid, unhurried. She walked down the center aisle, her steps soundless on the runner, and ascended the steps at the center of the stage. She stopped a few feet from Iris’s chair, looking down at her. The light from above carved her features into something stark, monumental. “You understand the silence.

“It’s not a difficult silence to understand.”

“No. It isn’t.” Pauline took a cigarette case from her pocket, tapped one out, but didn’t light it. She rolled it between her fingers. “Langston called the institute this morning. Spoke to Clara. He was looking for you. He sounded… insistent.

The cold knot below Iris’s ribs tightened. She kept her eyes on the worn roses of the chair arm. One petal was frayed, the threads a pale yellow. “He’s my manager. It’s his job to be insistent.

“He asked if we’d finalized the revisions for the Broadway transfer.” Pauline’s voice was flat, observational. “Clara told him we were still in rehearsal. He said that was unacceptable. That timelines were firm.

Iris’s mouth went dry. The taste was metallic, like old pennies. “He’s under pressure from Duke.

“Is he?” Pauline took a single step closer. The scent of her—tobacco, clean starch, a hint of bergamot—cut through the dust. “Or is the pressure coming from somewhere else? Somewhere with grey suits and very clean fingernails?

The air left the room. Iris’s vision narrowed to the unlit cigarette in Pauline’s hand, the delicate, almost brutal precision of her fingers. She couldn’t breathe. The confession sat on her tongue, a solid, bitter thing. They have me. They have letters. I have to tell them something. But her throat closed around it. She said nothing.

“Pauline.”

She didn’t turn. “Yes?

The word hung there. Iris didn’t know what she meant to say. An apology was a pebble against a fortress wall. An explanation was a lie she no longer had the energy to shape. “Nothing.

Pauline nodded, once. A sharp, downward cut of her chin. “Then we’re done for now. Clara will be back in ten minutes. Don’t be late after lunch.” She stepped off the stage and walked back up the aisle, a receding figure swallowed by the dark. The theatre doors opened, spilling a brief trapezoid of harsh hallway light onto the floor, then closed.

The thread from the curtain was still wrapped around her finger. Iris stared at it, a thin, dark line against her skin. She didn’t move. The trapezoid of light from the hallway was gone, but its afterimage pulsed on the backs of her eyelids. Don’t be late after lunch. An order. A dismissal. The kind you gave a stagehand, or a child.

She finally stood. The armchair released her with a soft, reluctant sound. She walked to the edge of the stage and looked down at the front row seat, empty now. The ghost of Pauline’s presence lingered there—a slight depression in the velvet, a faint, familiar scent of cigarette smoke and lemony soap cutting through the dust. Iris’s throat did that thing again, the tightness that had nothing to do with nodules and everything to do with the sheer, stupid fact of memory. She remembered the soap. Pauline had bought a bar of it from a vendor on the Seine, complaining about the price. That was the day before the letter from Zenith arrived.

A door banged open somewhere in the building’s depths, followed by the distant, cheerful clatter of dishes. Lunch. The spell broke. Iris turned and made her way off the stage, down the side aisle. The green room was just a former Sunday school classroom, its walls still adorned with a faint, painted mural of Noah’s Ark, the animals blurred by time and cheap paint into smudges of color. Clara and Arthur were there, along with two younger actors Iris didn’t know by name. They were unwrapping wax paper from sandwiches. The air smelled of rye bread and boiled ham.

“There’s one for you,” Arthur said, nodding toward a paper-wrapped parcel on the scarred table. “Cheese. No mustard.

“Thank you.” She took it but didn’t unwrap it. The thought of food was a physical impossibility. The cold knot beneath her ribs had solidified.

Clara watched her, chewing slowly. “She’s in her office. On the phone. Sounded… intense.

Iris just nodded. She leaned against the doorframe, the brick cold through her dress. Through the open classroom door, she could see a slice of the hallway and the dark, varnished wood of Pauline’s office door. It was shut. She imagined the conversation behind it—low, urgent, about funds, about permits, about the dangerous play taking shape in the next room. She wondered if Cross had a man on the other end of the line. The thought was slick and ugly.

The office door opened. Pauline stepped out, not seeing her at first. She was pinching the bridge of her nose, her eyes closed. A gesture of pure fatigue. Then she dropped her hand, opened her eyes, and her gaze landed directly on Iris in the doorway. The fatigue vanished, replaced by that familiar, impenetrable focus. A director’s focus. A warden’s focus.

“Finch. My office. Now.

The two young actors stopped talking. Clara looked down at her sandwich. Arthur busied himself with folding his wax paper into a neat, tiny square.

Iris pushed off the doorframe. She walked the ten feet down the hall, the cheese sandwich a dead weight in her hand. She stepped into the office and Pauline closed the door behind them.

The room was small, dominated by a huge, battered desk buried under ledgers, scripts, and ashtrays. The only window looked out onto a brick wall. Pauline didn’t go to the desk. She stood with her back to the door, arms crossed. The air between them was charged, brittle.

“It felt true to the moment.”

“It felt self-indulgent. It was your voice, not Eleanor’s. You stepped out of the scene to make a personal announcement.” She uncrossed her arms, began to pace the narrow space between the desk and the filing cabinets. Two steps, turn. “This play isn’t a confessional booth, Iris. It’s a precise instrument. You are a component. You do not get to ad-lib your guilt into my work.

The words were so sharp, so exactly aimed, that for a second Iris just felt the clean cut of them. Then the heat came. It started in her stomach, a quick, liquid rush that climbed her throat. “My guilt.

“And you’re the expert on screams, are you?” The words were out before she could catch them, her voice lower, rougher than she intended.

Pauline stopped pacing. She turned fully to face her. “In this room, on that stage, yes. I am.

“This isn’t about the stage. This hasn’t been about the stage since I walked in here.” Iris heard her own voice as if from a distance, calm and terrible. “You’ve been carving me apart since day one. Every note. Every correction. It’s not direction. It’s retribution. You’re using this play to punish me for Paris.

The silence that followed was absolute. The only sound was the faint, rhythmic drip of a leaky tap in the corner sink. Pauline’s expression didn’t change. It hardened, like plaster setting.

“You think this is about you.” She said it softly, almost wonderingly. “You think I would craft this, risk this, for something as small as you?” She took a step closer. The lemony soap smell was stronger now, mixed with the sharpness of her sweat. “This play is about three hundred and twenty men, most of them Black, who were blown to nothing because the Navy worked them like mules and called it patriotism. It’s about the lies that get printed in the newspaper and the truths that get buried in unmarked graves. It is about something so much larger than your precious, aching conscience.

“But you gave me the part.” Iris’s hand was shaking. She set the sandwich down on a stack of ledgers before she dropped it. “You could have given it to anyone. You chose me. You put me in that chair and made me wait. You made me listen to the clock. Why? If I’m so small. If my guilt is so muddy. Why am I here?

“Because you can do it!” The control in Pauline’s voice cracked, just for an instant. A flash of something raw and scorching beneath. “Because you have the technique. Because you know how to hollow yourself out and let something else in. I saw it in Paris. I see it now. That’s the only reason. The only one.

“That’s a lie.”

“Is it?” Pauline’s laugh was short, hollow. “You think I wanted you here? You think I wanted to stand in this room and smell that damn gardenia perfume you still wear and remember what it felt like to be twenty-three and stupid enough to believe in forever?” She was close now, too close. Iris could see the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the pulse beating in her throat. “You left. You took the contract and you got on the boat. You made your choice. Do you have any idea what happened after? The patron who funded La Flamme was arrested. The printer vanished. The police came to my flat every week for questions. It was a firetrap, Iris. A beautiful, idealistic firetrap. And you think I’ve spent twelve years nursing a grudge because you didn’t choose to burn in it with me?

The word firetrap hung in the air. It changed the shape of the room. Iris’s mouth was dry. She remembered the patron—a quiet, older woman with kind eyes. She remembered the printer’s shop, the smell of ink and damp paper. She had never asked what happened after. She had boarded the Normandie and watched the coastline disappear and told herself it was for the best, for her art, for her safety. She had never let herself picture the aftermath.

“You never said,” she whispered. The sentence was inadequate, a child’s excuse.

“What was I going to say? In a letter? ‘Wish you were here, the gendarmes are charming this time of year’?” Pauline turned away, leaning both hands on the cluttered desk, her head bowed. The anger seemed to drain out of her, leaving something worse—a profound, weary resignation. “This isn’t punishment. It’s a job. A hard one. You’re a tool for the job. That’s all. Now, can you do it? Or is your guilt too precious to set aside for three acts?

The question was a dismissal. A real one this time. Iris looked at the back of Pauline’s head, at the tight, dark curls at her nape. She thought of the telegram she’d never sent, the letters she’d stopped writing. She thought of the cold knot in her own stomach, and the three hundred and twenty men, and the ugly green ashtray on the stage. The dissonance wasn’t a chord anymore. It was a single, sustained note, and she was trapped inside it.

“I can do the job,” she said. Her voice sounded strange in her own ears.

Pauline didn’t turn around. “Then be on stage in five minutes. We’re running the confrontation from the top. And this time, try for fury. The generalized regret is starting to bore me.

The walk back to the stage felt longer. The light through the high windows had shifted, the golden bars now slanting across the floorboards at a sharper angle, illuminating millions of dancing dust motes. Iris climbed the steps. She walked to the armchair with the faded roses but didn’t sit. She stood behind it, her hands resting on its upholstered back. The fabric was rough under her palms.

She heard the doors open at the back of the house. Heels on the linoleum. Then the soft, sure tread on the runner. Pauline took her seat in the front row. She didn’t speak. She lit a cigarette. The match flared, a tiny, brief sun in the dark.

From the wings, Clara’s voice called, prompt and clear: “Places.”

Iris kept her hands on the chair. She watched the smoke from Pauline’s cigarette rise in a thin, grey ribbon, catching the light before it vanished into the dark above.

“You think this is about Paris?” Pauline’s voice was low, stripped of its director’s precision. It was just a voice in the dark, frayed at the edges. “You think I’m staging some elaborate, twelve-year revenge? That I’ve nothing better to do?

Iris said nothing. Her hands were cold on the worn velvet of the chair arms.

The cigarette tip glowed, a tiny, furious eye. Pauline took a drag, the ember flaring. She exhaled a slow, deliberate stream of smoke that cut through the bar of light. “You left. You took the contract. I expected that. I’d have done the same. The chance to be heard? To have a platform? Of course. I told you to go.

A lie. It had to be a lie. Iris remembered the station, the wet Paris morning, the smell of coal and damp wool. Pauline’s face had been a closed door. She’d said, ‘Don’t write.’ That was all.

“You told me not to write.”

“I told you not to write to me.” Pauline crushed the cigarette against the sole of her shoe, a sharp, grinding twist. “There’s a difference. Writing to me was a risk. For both of us. A letter could be opened. A sentiment could be evidence. I had a journal to run. A network. People who depended on the work. I couldn’t afford a trail of ink leading from my desk to some singer in New York.” She stood up. The movement was abrupt, a shadow detaching itself from the deeper dark. She took two steps toward the stage, stopped at the edge of the light. “You think your betrayal was personal. A romantic wound. It was administrative.

The word landed like a slap. Administrative. A filing error. A logistical complication.

“That’s not true,” Iris heard herself say. The sound was thin, a child’s protest.

“Isn’t it?” Pauline’s laugh was short, airless. “What did you think would happen? You’d become famous singing their songs, and I’d stay in my garret, publishing my little pamphlets, and we’d have a lovely, secret correspondence across the ocean? A romance for the ages?” She shook her head, a quick, dismissive jerk. “The world was burning, Iris. It was 1937. Then it was 1938. Then it was war. You got on your ship and sailed into a spotlight. I got on a different ship. A slower one. With a different cargo.

Iris’s mouth was dry. The cold knot below her ribs had tightened, a hard, frozen fist. “What cargo?

Pauline was silent for so long Iris thought she wouldn’t answer. The theatre seemed to hold its breath. A bus rumbled past on the street, its vibration a dull tremor through the floorboards.

“People,” Pauline said finally. The word was flat, heavy. “Artists. Writers. Jews. Anyone with the wrong papers, the wrong politics, the wrong face. Getting them out. Forging documents. Finding berths on anything that floated. The money from La Flamme didn’t go to printing. Not after ‘38. It went to bribes. To captains. To border guards with greedy hands.

Iris stared at the shape of her in the light. The straight line of her shoulders. The way her hands hung at her sides, fingers curled slightly inward. She saw, for the first time, not the formidable director, not the ghost of a lover, but a woman who had moved through a world of shadows while Iris had stood under bright lights.

“You never said.”

“Why would I?” Pauline’s tone was weary, almost bored. “You had your part to play. The innocent abroad, the rising star. The less you knew, the safer you were. For you. For them. The whole point was that you were clean.” She looked up at the stage, her eyes finding Iris’s in the gloom. “And then you came back. Not with a song. With a subpoena in your pocket. You walked in here wearing their leash, and you had the nerve to think I was punishing you for Paris.

The accusation hung between them, ugly and final. Iris’s mind scrabbled for purchase, for a detail that didn’t fit. “The letters. You stopped writing. After a year.

“I was arrested.”

The statement fell into the silence like a stone into a well. No drama. Just fact.

Iris’s breath caught. The air in the theatre turned thick, difficult to pull in. “What?

“Spring of ‘39. They held me for three days. Questioned me about the journal. About the people who wrote for it. They had a list.” Pauline’s voice was matter-of-fact, recounting a minor inconvenience. “They didn’t have enough to charge me. Someone talked, but not enough. They let me go. With a warning. And with a file. My name was in a file, Iris. In an office in Paris. Then the Germans came, and do you know what they did with the files in those offices? They read them. Very carefully.

Iris could see it. The stark, terrible logic of it. A chain of consequences, each link forged by her own ignorance. Her letters, full of foolish, youthful passion, sitting in a drawer in Pauline’s room. A drawer the police would have opened. Her name, in her own handwriting, linked to a subversive. A file.

“You think I wanted you to stay?” Pauline’s voice cracked, just once, on the last word. She recovered instantly, her tone flattening back to steel. “In that firetrap? With a war coming? I wanted you gone. I wanted you so far away that when they came for me, your name wouldn’t be in the room. That was the sacrifice. Not some grand romantic gesture. A practical, dirty, necessary erasure.

The revelation wasn’t a flood. It was a slow seep, a cold truth seeping through the cracks in the story Iris had told herself for twelve years. It wasn’t a lovers’ quarrel. It was a triage. She hadn’t been cast aside. She’d been scrubbed from the manifest of a sinking ship.

Something broke loose in Iris’s chest. Not a sob. A soundless, inward collapse. The carefully tended narrative of her own cowardice, the guilt she’d worn like a hairshirt—it was the wrong guilt. She’d been guilty of the wrong thing.

“I didn’t know,” she whispered. The words were ash.

“Of course you didn’t.” Pauline turned away, looking out over the empty seats. Her profile was sharp against the dark. “You were never supposed to know. That was the whole point. You played your part. You were the beautiful, apolitical distraction. The perfect cover. And you were good at it.” She paused. “You still are.

The insult, this time, carried no heat. It was an assessment. A director’s note.

Iris stood up. The chair groaned behind her. She felt unsteady, as if the stage floor had tilted. She walked to the edge, looked down at Pauline from the height of the boards. The distance felt vast. “And now? What’s my part now? The informant? The broken canary they send into the mine?

Pauline looked up at her. The work light caught the glint of moisture in her eyes, but her expression was unyielding. “I don’t know what you are now. That’s the problem.” She took a step back, merging with the shadow of the first row. “The rehearsal’s over. Clara will have the notes. We’ll pick it up tomorrow.

She didn’t wait for a reply. She turned and walked up the aisle, her footsteps a soft, receding rhythm on the runner. The double doors at the back opened, a brief rectangle of greyish hallway light, then sighed shut.

Iris was alone on the stage.

The silence was different. It wasn’t the silence of a held breath anymore. It was the silence after an explosion, when the ringing in your ears is all that’s left. She looked at the empty chair, the ugly cabbage roses. Eleanor’s chair. A prop. She looked at the sideboard, listing to the left. A piece of scenery.

Nothing in the room was real. It was all a set. She had been performing on a set for twelve years, following a script she’d never read.

Her hand went to her pocket, her fingers closing around the cold metal of the Zippo. The familiar ridges of the eagle. She didn’t pull it out. She just held it, feeling its weight, its solid, stupid persistence. A souvenir from a trip that never happened. A love story that was a cover story.

A fly, the same one or another, buzzed against a windowpane high up in the wall. A frantic, trapped sound. She watched the bar of golden light. The dust motes churned within it, endless, pointless motion. She counted them until her eyes blurred. Seventeen. Thirty-four. A number with no meaning.

She would have to walk home. She would have to unlock the door to the apartment. She would have to face Langston’s questions, his anxious, proprietary concern. She would have to pour a drink she didn’t want and trace the rim of the glass with her thumb. She would have to do all of it. But the woman who would do those things felt like a stranger, a understudy shoved into the role at the last minute, clutching the wrong script.

The only thing she knew, with a cold, cellular certainty, was that the part she’d been playing was over. The performance had ended, and no one had clapped. They’d just turned out the lights and gone home.
