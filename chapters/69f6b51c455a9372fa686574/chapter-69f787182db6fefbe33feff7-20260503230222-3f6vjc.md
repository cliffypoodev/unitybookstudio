The apartment smelled of lemon polish and the ghost of Langston’s cigar. Iris stood just inside the door, her coat still on, and listened to the silence. It was a different texture from the theatre’s—thinner, more brittle, like old varnish over cheap wood. Her throat ached, a low, familiar burn just below her larynx. She’d pushed a note too far in the afternoon’s rehearsal, a simple line of dialogue she’d tried to color with a singer’s vibrato. The sound had frayed at the edges. Pauline had said nothing, just made a tiny checkmark on her script, the pencil a soft scratch in the quiet.

She hung her coat on the bentwood rack. The hallway mirror showed her a woman blurred at the edges, the light from the sconce casting her face in uneven planes. A strand of hair had escaped her chignon. It made her look unkempt, or perhaps just tired. She left it.

Langston was in the living room, in his usual chair by the radio. He wasn’t listening to it. He was writing. The scratch of his pen was the only sound, a determined, rhythmic dig into paper. She paused in the doorway, watching the top of his head, the careful part in his thinning grey hair. He’d had that part for twenty years. It was a landmark.

“You’re late,” he said, without looking up.

“Rehearsal ran long.”

“With Miss Carter.”

It wasn’t a question. Iris moved into the room, her heels loud on the parquet. She went to the sideboard, poured two fingers of gin into a water glass. The liquid was the color of nothing. She didn’t add ice. The first sip was a cold line of fire that met the ache in her throat and did nothing to soothe it.

“It’s a complicated scene,” she said.

“I’m sure.” He finished a line with a flourish. She saw the pen then. It was new. A deep maroon barrel, gold clip catching the lamplight. Not the sensible black Parker he used for contracts. This was a thing of weight and ceremony. He’d always said expensive pens were for bankers and fools.

She took another drink. “Where’d you get the new instrument?”

His hand stilled for a fraction of a second. Then he capped the pen with a precise click. “A gift. From Duke. A token of his… appreciation for our cooperation.”

Duke Strauss. The producer’s name lay between them like a dropped coin. Iris traced the rim of her glass with her thumb. The gin had left a faint, oily residue. “Generous of him.”

“He’s a generous man. He sees the potential.” Langston finally looked at her. His eyes were the same pale blue they’d always been, but the skin around them seemed tighter, as if he were perpetually squinting into a mild glare. “He asked after you today. Wanted to know how the… immersion was progressing.”

“The immersion.”

“Into the material. The institute’s atmosphere.” Langston set the pen down on the leather blotter, aligning it perfectly parallel to the edge. “He’s keen to understand the artistic process. For the revue.”

The patriotic revue. The one that would sand the edges off Pauline’s play and paste Iris’s old hits on the end like a cheerful postscript. Iris felt something move in her chest—a slow, thick curl of resentment, or maybe just fatigue. It wasn’t hot. It was lukewarm, like dishwater.

“The atmosphere is one of people trying to put on a play,” she said. The words shifted, out flatter than she intended. The vocal cords grated.

“People have names, Iris.” Langston leaned back, steepling his fingers. The pose was one of patient reason. “Duke is compiling a list of associates. For the program notes. A ‘who’s who’ of the Harlem theatre scene. He thought you could provide some color.”

The request, polite and lethal. She saw the shape of it. Not Cross in a diner, not a direct order. This was Langston, her husband, asking for help with a colleague’s project. A commercial project. The kind they understood.

Her thumb kept circling the glass. “I don’t know everyone. I’m just the vocal coach.”

“You’re in the room. You hear things.” He smiled, a thin stretching of lips. “Who argues with Carter? Who seems… overly devoted to the political message? The young ones, the fiery ones. They’d be good for a quote. Duke loves a fiery quote.”

The fire in her throat intensified. She hesitated against it. “I’ll see what I can remember.”

“Don’t just remember. Make a note.” He nudged the maroon pen an inch toward her. “Use this. It’s a pleasure to write with. A smooth flow. Important thoughts deserve a proper instrument.”

She didn’t touch it. She looked past him, to the bookshelf. Her eyes went to the third shelf, to the gap between a volume of Chekhov and a biography of Toscanini. A small, lacquered box should have been there. A souvenir from a Paris flea market, its surface chipped to show cheap wood beneath red paint. It held nothing of value. A broken guitar string. A train ticket stub. A dried flower so brittle it would turn to dust at a touch.

The box was gone.

Not obviously missing. The space wasn’t empty. The books had been shifted, nudged together to close the gap. But she knew the rhythm of that shelf. The Chekhov had a tear in its dust jacket, a white slash across the spine. It now sat half an inch to the left.

“You moved my box,” she said.

Langston followed her gaze. His expression didn’t change. “I was looking for the ’37 tax documents. The ones from the Paris engagement. The lawyer needs them for the audit.”

“They’re in the filing cabinet. Where they’ve always been.”

“I must have gotten turned around.” He shrugged, a minute lift of one shoulder. “It was late. I was tired. That box was in the way.”

“Where is it?”

“On your dressing table, I think. I put it somewhere safe.” He picked up his pen again, uncapped it, examined the nib. “It’s just junk, Iris. You should throw it out. It gathers dust.”

The air in the room felt thick, difficult to draw in. She finished the gin. The alcohol didn’t warm her; it just left a chemical trail. She set the glass down on the sideboard with a soft tap. The sound was absorbed by the Persian rug.

“It’s my junk,” she said.

“Of course it is.” He was writing again, his head bent. The subject was closed. “Don’t forget about the names. Duke will call tomorrow.”

She left him there, scratching his important thoughts onto paper with his smooth, flowing gift. In the bedroom, on her dressing table, the box sat exactly in the center of the lace runner. Someone had wiped the dust from its lid. The red lacquer shone under the lamp, a garish, fake cheer. She didn’t open it. She placed her hand on the lid. The wood was cool.

From the living room, the radio clicked on. A swell of orchestra music, something brassy and optimistic. The theme for the Lucky Strike Hit Parade. Langston’s small, nightly ritual. She listened to the announcer’s booming voice, all false bonhomie, and counted the cracks in the ceiling above her bed. There were seven. One of them, near the light fixture, looked like a clumsy drawing of a bird in flight. She’d never noticed that before.

The bird-shaped crack in the ceiling was still there in the grey morning light. Iris stared at it, listening to the percussive sounds of Langston in the kitchen. The clink of a spoon against a china cup. The scrape of a chair leg on linoleum. A morning suite in three movements. Her throat was a raw, closed fist.

She dressed in the half-light, choosing a high-necked blouse. The fabric brushed the tender skin and she flinched. In the mirror, her face looked pale, the skin under her eyes the color of weak tea. She pinched her cheeks, a trick from the old days. It brought no convincing color, only a brief, sharp sting.

The kitchen smelled of coffee and burnt toast. Langston sat at the small table, the morning paper open beside his plate. He wore a dressing gown of dark green silk. Another new item. It pooled around him like spilled oil.

“Coffee’s fresh,” he said, not looking up from the financial section.

She poured a cup. The liquid was black and bitter. She took it to the table sat opposite him. The silence stretched, filled only by the rustle of newsprint. A crumb of toast clung to the corner of his mouth. She watched it move as he chewed.

“Your report,” he said, turning a page. “For Duke. Did you make any headway last night?”

The coffee was too hot. It scalded her tongue. “I was tired.”

“We’re all tired.” He folded the paper with a crisp, surgical crease. “The revue’s first planning meeting is Thursday. Duke wants a draft list of potential… collaborators from the institute. People who could lend authenticity.”

“Authenticity.” The word tasted like the coffee.

“Local color. It’s important.” He picked up the maroon pen from beside his plate. It had been lying there, next to the butter knife, as if it were cutlery. He rolled it between his fingers. “A name or two. That’s all. To show we’re engaged with the community.”

She thought of the young stagehand, Jimmy, who’d argued with Pauline about the placement of a prop table. He’d been passionate, his voice cracking on the word “integrity.” She thought of Lena, the seamstress, humming a blues tune under her breath as she worked, her fingers flying. She saw the careful, expectant tilt of Langston’s head.

“There’s a boy,” she said. The words felt thick. “Jimmy. He handles props. He’s… enthusiastic.”

Langston made a note on the margin of the newspaper. The pen moved without a sound. “Jimmy. Last name?”

“I don’t know.”

“Find out.” He didn’t look up. “And the others? The ones who seem most devoted to Carter’s vision? The political ones.”

A pain, sharp and specific, lanced through the base of her skull. She pressed two fingers there. “They’re actors. They’re devoted to the play.”

“A play about a munitions explosion that killed hundreds of Negro sailors.” He set the pen down. “Let’s not pretend it’s Charley’s Aunt. Duke needs to know which of them might be… problematic. For a patriotic program.”

The toast turned to paste in her mouth. She forced herself to swallow. “You want me to point out the troublemakers.”

“I want you to help us avoid embarrassment.” The way he said it was calm, reasonable. A teacher explaining a simple sum. “For everyone. For you. If one of Carter’s radicals says something inflammatory at the wrong moment, and it comes out you worked with them closely… the Committee’s patience isn’t infinite, Iris. This revue is a lifeline. It shows you’re rehabilitating. Working with acceptable material.”

The ghost of last night’s gin rose in the back of her throat. She took another gulp of coffee to drown it. “And if I can’t remember any names?”

He smiled. It didn’t reach his eyes. “You have a performer’s memory. You can remember the lyrics to every song you ever sang. You can remember the third-act blocking from a play you saw in 1938. You’ll remember.” He pushed his chair back, the legs squealing against the floor. The sound was a nail down a slate. “I have an appointment with Duke at ten. Use the pen. It’s on the desk.”

He left the room. She heard the shower start, the pipes groaning in the walls. A distant thunder.

She sat until the coffee went cold. A fly buzzed against the windowpane, a frantic, trapped drone. It bounced against the glass fell, buzzed again. She watched its pointless struggle. Her thumb found the rim of her cup and began its circular pilgrimage.

After a while, she stood. Her legs felt stiff. She walked to the living room, to the desk. The pen lay on the green leather blotter, perfectly centered. It was heavier than it looked. She uncapped it. The nib was gold, intricately carved. She touched the tip. It was sharp enough to prick skin.

She didn’t sit. She looked at the bookshelf. She walked over and knelt, her knees cracking. She ran a finger along the spines until she reached the gap. She nudged the Chekhov aside. Behind it, pushed to the back of the shelf was the red lacquered box.

She lifted it out. It was lighter than she remembered. The familiar chip on the lid was there, a moon-shaped flaw. She opened it.

The contents were as she’d left them. The brittle flower. The ticket stub. The coiled, broken string. But the order was wrong. She always placed the flower on the left, the string on the right. Now the string lay across the flower. A small thing. A nothing.

The shower stopped. The silence that followed was absolute.

She closed the box, slid it back into the dark space behind the books. She stood, brushing dust from her skirt. When Langston came out, dressed, his hair damp and neatly combed, she was at the desk, the maroon pen in her hand. A sheet of blank paper before her.

“Any progress?” he asked, checking his watch.

“I’m making a list,” she said. She spoke — clear, a professional alto. It didn’t hurt at all.

“Good.” He adjusted his tie in the mirror over the mantel. “Leave it on the desk. I’ll review it tonight.”

He left without saying goodbye. The front door clicked shut. A final, gentle punctuation.

Iris looked down at the paper. She’d written nothing. The pen’s sharp nib hovered just above the surface. She pressed down. A blot of rich, blue-black ink spread from the point, a tiny, dark star blooming in the white field. She watched it grow, the edges feathering out like capillaries. It consumed the empty space.

She recapped the pen. The blot stared up at her. A single, imperfect mark.

From the street below, a car horn sounded, two short, impatient bleats. The fly at the kitchen window had stopped buzzing. She wondered if it was dead, or if it had just given up.

The silence in the apartment thickened, a sediment settling over everything. Iris’s thumb was still on the rim of the glass, a pointless orbit. She set the glass down on the sideboard. The click was too loud.

She walked past Langston, past the radio with its dark, vacant eye, and into the bedroom. The door didn’t latch properly; it sighed against the frame. She flicked the lamp on. The light was a weak, yellowy pool that made the shadows in the corners seem denser.

She went to the bookshelf. Not to look, but to confirm. Her hand went to the space between the Chekhov and the Toscanini. The air there felt different. Colder. She pulled the Chekhov volume out. A small cloud of dust motes swirled into the lamplight, dancing in a frantic, silent jig before disappearing. Behind the book, the wall was bare. A pale rectangle on the darker wood where the lacquered box had sat for a decade.

She didn’t call out. She walked back to the living room doorway. Langston had uncapped the maroon pen again and was writing, his head bent. The tip glided across the paper without a sound. A smooth flow.

“Where is it?” The words came out a dry leaf.

He didn’t look up. “Where is what, dear?”

“The red box. From the shelf.”

“I haven’t the faintest.” A flourish at the end of a line. “Perhaps you moved it.”

“I didn’t.” She stayed in the doorway, her body a bar across the frame. “It’s gone.”

He sighed, a patient exhalation. He capped the pen, laid it aside. “I may have… tidied. This afternoon. The shelf was a disgrace. All that dust. I consolidated.”

“You consolidated my box into the trash.”

“Don’t be dramatic. I put some things in storage. The attic.” He finally met her eyes. His expression was one of mild, baffled irritation. The look he gave a waiter who brought the wrong soup. “It was full of rubbish, Iris. A broken string. A dead flower. Junk.”

Her breath hitched, a small, stupid sound. Junk. The guitar string had snapped during a rehearsal in a cramped Montparnasse studio, the ping lost in the sound of rain on the skylight. The flower was a sprig of lavender bought from a girl on the Pont Neuf. It had smelled of dust and sugar.

“It was mine,” she said. The words were inadequate, childlike.

“And now it’s in a trunk in the attic. Safe and sound.” He picked up the pen again, tapped it once against the blotter. A conductor’s downbeat. “Now, about those names for Duke. I’d like a list by Thursday.”

Something in her chest tightened, a cord pulled taut. It wasn’t anger. It was colder. A clarity. She saw the sequence: the new pen, the missing box, the request for names. They were notes in the same chord, a dissonant triad. She walked toward him, her steps measured on the parquet. She stopped beside his chair. He looked up, his pale eyes reflecting the lamplight like flat discs.

“You went through my things,” she said.

“I cleaned.”

“You went through them. You opened the box.”

“I glanced. To see if it was worth keeping. It wasn’t.” A faint pink flush crept up his neck. Not shame. Annoyance. “This is about the project, Iris. Our project. Duke’s revue is the lifeline. That… institute is a dead end. A dangerous one. I’m trying to protect our investment. Our future.”

“By stealing my past.”

“By discarding sentiment that has no practical value!” His voice sharpened lost its reasonable veneer. He stood up, suddenly, putting the chair between them. He was taller than her, but he seemed to occupy less space. A man made of angles and irritation. “Those letters, that… detritus. It’s evidence of a life you chose to leave. A life that could bury us if the wrong person saw it. Do you understand? Cross isn’t some blustering politician. He’s a bureaucrat with a file. And he’s looking for things like that red box.”

The air left the room. The faint traffic noise from the street vanished. There was only the hum in her ears and Langston’s face, rigid with a fear he was calling prudence.

“Letters?” The word was a whisper.

He blinked. A tiny stutter. “Figure of speech. The things in the box. They’re like letters from a ghost.”

He was lying. She could see the lie in the way his eyes darted to the desk drawer, then back to her face. A quick, furtive glance. The desk drawer where he kept contracts, stamps, the Parker pen. The drawer was slightly ajar, a dark slash against the polished wood.

She didn’t think. She moved.

Her hand was on the brass pull before he could react. She yanked the drawer open. It juddered on its runners. Inside, neat stacks of paper, rubber bands, a bottle of ink. And there, tucked beside a ledger was a bundle tied with a faded blue ribbon. Seven envelopes, their corners soft with age. Pauline’s handwriting on the top one—a sharp, sloping script that seemed to cut into the paper.

Iris’s fingers went numb. She picked the bundle up. The paper was cool, dry. The ribbon was the one she’d saved from a hatbox. A stupid, pretty thing.

“Those are private,” Langston said. He said it, low, dangerous.

“They’re mine.” She turned. He was close now, his hand half-raised as if to snatch them back. “You had no right.”

“I have every right! I am your husband. Your manager. Your protection.” The words were hissed. “Do you think I enjoy this? Playing nursemaid to your… Your regrets? Keeping them safe from the vultures?”

“Keeping them safe?” She held the letters to her chest. The edges pressed against her sternum. “Or keeping them handy?”

His face changed. The anger didn’t fade, but it solidified, calcified into something colder. More professional. He took a step back, adjusted his cufflink. A tiny, precise motion. “You need to understand the reality of our position, Iris. We are not artists in a garret. We are a business. A brand. And brands require maintenance. Sometimes that means… inventory management. Sometimes it means providing assurances to our partners.”

“What did you do, Langston?”

He looked at her for a long moment. The silence stretched, thin and sharp. Downstairs, a door slammed. A muffled shout. A slice of ordinary life.

“Cross wanted leverage,” Langston said finally. He said it flat, explanatory. A doctor describing a procedure. “Something concrete, beyond your… testimony. Something that would demonstrate your commitment. And my own. The letters are a historical curiosity. They prove a relationship. A relationship with a known radical. In the current climate, they have a certain… value.”

“You gave them to him.”

“I showed them to him. As a gesture of good faith. To prove we have nothing to hide, because we are willingly revealing everything.” He gestured toward the letters. “He photographed them. They’re in the file now. It’s done. It’s insurance.”

Insurance. The word between them, ugly and final. Iris felt the weight of the letters in her hands. They were no longer hers. They were evidence. Catalogued. Photographed. A gesture of good faith.

She looked at the maroon pen on the blotter. A gift from Duke. A smooth flow for important thoughts. She understood now. The pen, the letters, the request for names—They were all part of the same transaction. Langston wasn’t just brokering her cooperation. He was trading in her history, piece by piece, to purchase his own security.

“You sold them,” she said. It wasn’t a question.

“I secured our future.” He sat down again, heavily. The performance of reason was over. Only the weary calculation remained. “Now. The names. Thursday.”

Iris didn’t answer. She turned and walked back to the bedroom, the letters held tight against her. She closed the door. This time, she turned the old brass key in the lock. The click was a soft, definitive sound.

She stood in the center of the room, the weak light pooling around her feet. She untied the blue ribbon. It slithered to the floor. She didn’t read the letters. She didn’t need to. She knew the opening line of the first one by heart. My dearest Canary— She could feel the ghost of Pauline’s voice in the curves of the ink.

From the window, she could see the dark bulk of the city, a thousand lit windows like scattered stars. Somewhere out there, in Harlem, a theatre was dark and empty. A single work lamp buzzed over a bare stage. The thought was a physical ache, a hollow below her ribs.

She placed the bundle on the dresser, beside her hairbrush and a pot of cold cream. They looked alien there. Artifacts in a museum of someone else’s life.

The key was still in the lock. A tiny, brass tooth. She left it there.
