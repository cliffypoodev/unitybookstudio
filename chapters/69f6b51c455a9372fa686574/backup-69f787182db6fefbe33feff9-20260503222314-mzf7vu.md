The rehearsal hall held the day’s heat long after the last body had left. It pooled under the high ceiling, a stagnant warmth that smelled of old wood and the faint, sweetish tang of dried sweat. Iris sat on the edge of the stage, legs dangling into the orchestra pit’s darkness. Her script lay open beside her, the margins dense with a private notation of sharps and flats, rests and crescendos. Eleanor’s silence in Act Two wasn’t a pause. It was a sustained low C, a vibration felt in the teeth, the kind of note that could shatter glass if you held it long enough.

She’d been trying to find the breath for it for an hour. The right breath. Not the shallow sip of air she used for singing now, but the old breath, the one that came from the floor and filled the back of the skull. It eluded her. A fly buzzed against a windowpane across the room, a persistent, irregular rhythm. Thump. Pause. Thump-thump. She found herself counting the spaces between its impacts. Three seconds. Seven. Two. The randomness of it was more distracting than a steady beat.

From the pocket of her cardigan she pulled the Zippo lighter. The metal was smooth, worn to a dull silver along the edges from twelve years of restless turning in her palm. She didn’t flick it. She just pressed her thumb into the grooved wheel, feeling the minute teeth bite into her skin. Nothing to do with anything. Her throat ached, a dull throb beneath her jaw. She swallowed, and the pain sharpened, a precise little stitch.

A sound cut through the fly’s futile campaign.

Not a creak or a sigh of the building settling. A voice. Muffled, distorted by distance and wood. It came from the direction of the offices.

Iris went still, the lighter a cold weight in her fist. The building was supposed to be empty. The janitor, a man named Henry with a pronounced limp, had shuffled through two hours ago, nodding at her silent presence before locking the front doors behind him. He’d left her the key, a gesture of trust that felt like a lead coin in her pocket. She listened. Nothing. Maybe the pipes. Maybe she’d imagined it.

Then it came again. A low, urgent stream of words she couldn’t decipher. Followed by a thump.

She slid off the stage, her shoes hitting the floor with a soft tap that echoed in the vast quiet. She padded across the hall, the polished boards cool through her stockings. The corridor to the offices was dark, save for a sliver of yellow light spilling from under Pauline’s door.

The voice was clearer now. Pauline’s. But stripped of its usual controlled precision. The words ran together, slurred by sleep or distress. “…not the drafts… the final copies. Everything. The smoke… it has to be everything.

Iris stopped outside the door. Her hand rose, knuckles an inch from the painted wood. She should knock. Announce herself. That was the correct, the human thing to do.

She didn’t.

She pushed the door open just enough to see.

Pauline was at her desk, but she wasn’t working. Her head rested on her crossed arms, face turned toward the door. In the pool of light from the green-shaded lamp, she looked younger. The stern lines around her mouth had softened into something vulnerable. Her eyelids fluttered. A sheen of sweat glistened at her temples. Her lips moved, shaping silent words, then, “The smell. God, the smell.

A nightmare. Not a quiet one.

On the coat stand in the corner hung Pauline’s heavy winter coat, a man’s style in a charcoal wool. Iris moved without conscious decision. She took the coat. It was heavier than it looked, smelling faintly of cigarette smoke and the peculiar, dry scent of the institute’s chalk dust. She approached the desk, her own breath shallow in her chest. The floorboard by the wastebasket squeaked, a high, mouse-like protest.

Pauline stirred, a frown knotting her brow. “Qui est là?” she murmured, the French automatic, blurred by sleep.

“It’s nothing,” Iris whispered, the words out before she could think. “Go back to sleep.

She draped the coat over Pauline’s shoulders, tucking the edges around her. Her fingers, for a second, brushed the warm wool of Pauline’s sweater. A jolt went through her, sharp and electric, a live wire touching skin. She snatched her hand back.

Pauline sighed, a deep, settling exhalation. The tension in her shoulders eased under the weight of the coat. Her breathing deepened, slowed. The murmuring ceased.

Iris stood there, a trespasser in the intimate country of another person’s sleep. The desk was a landscape of organized chaos: neat stacks of manuscript pages, a coffee mug holding a bouquet of sharpened pencils, an ashtray overflowing with the pale ghosts of Gauloises. A single sheet had escaped a pile, drifting to the floor. Iris bent and retrieved it. It was a page from the Port Chicago script, but heavily annotated in Pauline’s tight, angular hand. In the margin, next to a line of Eleanor’s dialogue, Pauline had written: Not grief. Calculation. The cost of the lie versus the cost of the truth. She’s doing math.

Iris placed the page back on the correct pile. Her eyes caught on a photograph, half-tucked under a ledger. Black and white, curled at the edges. A group of people on a sun-drenched stone step, squinting at the camera. A younger Pauline, her hair longer, smiled with a ease Iris had never seen on her. A man had his arm around her shoulders. In the background, a sign was partially visible: Librairie… The rest was cut off.

She looked away. The air in the room felt thick, charged with the heat of the lamp and the secret of this vulnerability. A hairline crack in the plaster above the door caught the light, branching like a tiny, frozen river delta. She stared at it until her eyes blurred.

Then she turned and left, pulling the door closed until the latch clicked with a sound as soft as a sigh. The corridor was dark again. She leaned against the wall, the cool plaster against her back. The fly had stopped buzzing. The silence now was absolute, a solid thing she had to push through to get back to the stage. She didn’t go back. She walked to the fire exit at the end of the hall, pushed the bar, and stepped out into the alley.

The night air was a slap, cold and smelling of wet brick and distant garbage. She fumbled for a cigarette, lit it with the Zippo. The flame trembled. She inhaled, the smoke scratching the tender ache in her throat. Above the rooftops, a thin slice of moon hung in a smear of cloud. Somewhere, a radio played a tinny, cheerful swing tune. It felt like a message from another planet.

She had come here to understand a silence. She had found, instead, a woman dreaming of fire.

And she had performed an act of tenderness she would never, ever mention. A stolen kindness. The most dangerous kind. It settled in her stomach, not as warmth, but as a kind of dread—the dread of a door opened that could not be fully closed again.

The cigarette burned down to her fingers. She dropped it, ground it out under her heel. The action felt final. When she went back inside, locking the heavy door behind her, the rehearsal hall felt different. Not empty, but waiting. The page with her musical notations sat on the stage where she’d left it. She looked at it, then folded it once, twice, and put it in her pocket. The score was wrong. She’d have to start again.

The fly had given up. The windowpane was silent. Iris held the annotated page, the paper warm from the lamplight. Calculation. The cost of the lie versus the cost of the truth. The sentence ended mid-thought. Pauline’s pen had left a small, deliberate dot, a full stop where none was meant to be.

She should leave. Put the page back on the desk and retreat into the hall’s darkness. This was a theft, this witnessing. More intimate than anything in Paris.

Pauline shifted under the coat, a small, restless movement. A lock of hair had come loose from its pin and lay across her cheekbone, a dark curve against her skin. Iris remembered the precise weight of that hair in her palm, the coarse, vital texture of it. A memory with no purpose here, a stupid, sensory ghost. It arrived anyway, dragging the scent of rain-washed gutters and hot asphalt after a summer storm. Paris, again. Always Paris.

She laid the page gently atop the nearest pile. Her fingers lingered for a second on the paper’s edge. Then she turned to go.

“You always did have a thief’s footsteps.”

The voice was sleep-roughened, but clear. Iris froze, her back to the desk. The squeaky floorboard. Of course.

“I wasn’t stealing anything.” The lie was automatic, thin as tissue.

“I know.” A rustle of wool. Pauline was sitting up, pushing the coat from her shoulders. The sound of her smoothing her hair, a quick, efficient gesture Iris didn’t need to see to know. “You were returning something. A misplaced thought.

Iris turned. Pauline was looking at her, eyes still soft with sleep but sharpening by the second. The vulnerability was gone, sealed away behind a familiar, weary composure. But a faint sheen remained at her temples. The nightmare clung to her like a smell.

“You were talking in your sleep,” Iris said.

“Was I.”

“Something about smoke. About final copies.

Pauline’s gaze flicked to the annotated page, then back to Iris. A calculation, swift and invisible. “A dream about a printing press. A fire. The two got confused.” She reached for the pack of Gauloises on the desk, tapped one out. Her hands were steady as she lit it. The flame from the match jumped, then settled. She inhaled, the smoke a grey veil between them. “A waste of good paper. That’s all.

It wasn’t all. The air in the office had changed. It was thicker now, charged with the static of a secret almost grasped. Iris’s throat tightened, that old, familiar constriction. She swallowed against it. “You should go home. It’s late.

“So should you.” Pauline exhaled. “But we’re both here. Why is that, Iris?

The question hung there. Because the empty apartment held only Langston’s silent disapproval and the ghost of her own voice. Because the rehearsal hall was the only place where the shape of Eleanor felt real, a suit of skin she could almost step into. Because here, in this building humming with someone else’s dangerous purpose, she felt less like a ghost.

She said none of it. “The breath wasn’t right. For the silence.

“And is it now?”

“No.”

Pauline considered her through the smoke. “Sit.

It wasn’t an invitation. It was a direction. Iris remained standing, a pointless defiance. Pauline didn’t repeat herself. She just waited. The clock on the wall ticked, a loud, metallic sound in the quiet. It was missing a beat every third tick—tick, tock, silence, tick, tock, silence. Iris found herself waiting for the skip.

She sat. In the wooden chair facing the desk. It was still warm from where Henry must have sat earlier, delivering the mail.

“You covered me with my coat,” Pauline said.

“You were cold.”

“I was.” She took another drag. “That was kind. An act of kindness from an informant. It complicates the narrative.

The word landed like a stone in still water. Informant. It rippled through the room, distorting everything. Iris’s stomach clenched, a hard, involuntary fist. She looked at her own hands, folded in her lap. The cuticle on her left thumb was ragged. She’d been worrying it. “I don’t know what you mean.

“Don’t you.” It was flat. Not a question. “Phillip Cross. Grey suit. Smells of bay rum and ambition. He has an office in the Federal Building. He interviewed Michael Fromley from the Daily Worker last Tuesday. He subpoenaed the bookseller on Bleecker Street on Thursday. And he met with you, in the back booth of the Blue Note, three weeks ago tomorrow.” Pauline tapped ash into the tray. “He thinks I don’t know my own stage. He’s wrong.

The world tilted. The careful fiction of her presence here, the fragile performance of a second chance, collapsed into dust. Iris’s mouth was dry. She tried to form a word, any word. Nothing came.

“You look surprised,” Pauline observed. “Did you think you were the only one who could be told to watch?

“Who told you?”

“It doesn’t matter. What matters is that I know. And you know that I know.” She leaned forward, elbows on the desk. “So we can stop the performance. It’s exhausting, isn’t it? The double think. Writing your little reports. Translating art into suspicion.

The humiliation was a hot, sick wave. It burned her cheeks, her ears. “It wasn’t a choice.

“Everything is a choice.”

“They had photographs. From Paris. They had… other things.” She couldn’t say the words. Lavender Scare. Moral turpitude.

“I know what they had.” Pauline’s voice softened, just a fraction. It was worse than her anger. “They have the same for half the people in this building. For me. The difference is, I expected it. You thought your fame was a shield. It was always a target.

Iris stared at the overflowing ashtray. The crushed filters were like tiny, dead things. “What do you want me to do?

“I want you to act.” Pauline stubbed out her cigarette. “Really act. Not this pantomime. Eleanor isn’t a martyr. She’s a mathematician. She’s weighed the cost of telling the truth about that ship exploding against the cost of her family’s safety. She’s chosen the lie. Your job isn’t to play her grief. It’s to play the moment she realizes the lie is more expensive. That the numbers no longer add up. Find that moment. The crack in the arithmetic.

“Why?” The word was a whisper.

“Because the play is going on. Because Cross will be in the audience on opening night. Because every word you speak will be a report he cannot file.” Pauline’s eyes held hers. There was no forgiveness in them. No softness. Only a fierce, relentless clarity. “You wanted to atone? This is how. Not with secret coats. With spoken words in a lit room, in front of the men who own your silence.

A strange, cold calm settled over Iris. The panic receded, leaving something clean and sharp in its wake. The truth was out. The worst had happened, and she was still here, breathing. “He’ll ruin you. If I do what you’re saying.

“He’ll try.” A faint, grim smile touched Pauline’s lips. “But this play has friends in places he cannot see. Patrons who understand fire. Who know that sometimes you must burn a field to save the harvest.

The cryptic phrase from the nightmare. The smoke… it has to be everything. It wasn’t about a printing press. It was about a strategy. A controlled burn.

“Leo Strauss,” Iris said, the connection clicking into place with a quiet, mental snick. “He’s not just a producer. He’s a patron. Your patron.

Pauline didn’t confirm it. She didn’t deny it. She simply looked at Iris, her expression unreadable. “You see? You’re beginning to calculate.

Outside, a truck rumbled down the street, its headlights sweeping across the office window, throwing transient, moving shadows over the walls. For a second, Pauline’s face was in stark relief, all angles and resolve. Then the light passed, and she was just a woman at a cluttered desk, looking tired.

“Go home, Iris,” she said, her voice final. “Find the breath. Find the crack. We rehearse it tomorrow.

Iris stood. Her legs felt steady. She walked to the door. Her hand was on the knob when Pauline spoke again, her voice lower.

“And Iris?”

She turned.

“Thank you. For the coat.

It was nothing. It was everything. Iris nodded, a short, stiff movement, and stepped into the dark hall. She closed the door softly behind her. The echo of the latch was the only sound in the empty building. She stood there for a long moment, her forehead resting against the cool wood. The fly, resurrected, began its futile assault on the windowpane once more. Thump. Pause. Thump-thump.

She listened to its irregular rhythm all the way back to the stage, where her script waited, open to a silence that was no longer empty.

The afternoon light cut across the rehearsal hall in a long, dusty blade, exposing every scuff on the floorboards. Iris stood in the center of the open space, the script a dead weight in her hand. The coat from last night was back on its hook. The desk had been tidy when she’d glanced in this morning. No trace of the woman who’d murmured about smoke.

Pauline entered not from the office corridor, but from the side door that led to the scene shop. She carried the smell of sawdust and turpentine with her, a sharp, resinous cloud. She didn’t look at Iris. She walked to the front row of chairs and sat, placing a notebook on her knees. She lit a cigarette. The scratch of the match was loud in the waiting quiet.

“Act Two. Eleanor’s monologue. The one after the telegram.” Pauline’s voice was flat, stripped of all color. “From ‘I knew the moment I saw the envelope.’ To the end.

Iris’s mouth was dry. She found the place in the script. The words were familiar, a series of black marks she’d scored like a piece of music. A slow, descending line. She cleared her throat. The ache was still there, a familiar tenant.

She began. She tried to find the breath, the low C. She aimed for the technical precision she’d mapped in the margins: a slight tremolo on ‘envelope,’ a rest before ‘the paper was so light.

“Stop.”

Pauline hadn’t moved. Smoke curled from the cigarette held loosely between her fingers. “You’re singing it.

“I’m not singing anything. It’s spoken.

“You’re singing it. The melody is in your head. I can hear it. A pretty little tune of regret. It’s maudlin. It’s safe.” Pauline took a drag, exhaled slowly. “Eleanor isn’t sorry. She’s calculating. The telegram is data. She’s adding a column. Start again.

A hot flush climbed Iris’s neck. She looked down at the page. The words blurred. She blinked, and they resolved. She started again, hardening her voice, clipping the ends of the sentences.

“Stop.” Pauline stood now. She ground the cigarette under her heel on the floorboard, a small, violent twist. “Now you’re reading a ledger. Dry. Bloodless. Where is the calculation? Show me the gears turning. She’s deciding whether to lie to her sister for the rest of their lives. That’s not an emotional moment. It’s a tactical one. Do it again.

The fly from yesterday was back, or another one. It circled the light fixture above with a faint, buzzing whine. Iris focused on that sound, let it fill her head. She dropped the script. It hit the floor with a slap. She closed her eyes.

“I knew the moment I saw the envelope.” Her own voice sounded strange to her. Not singing. Not reading. Something else. Something stripped. “The paper was so light. Like it wasn’t real. I thought, that’s it, then. The weight is gone. But it wasn’t gone. It was just… transferred. To me.

“Better.” Pauline’s voice was closer. She had moved down the row. “Now keep going. Don’t feel it. Think it. What’s the next move?

Iris kept her eyes shut. The darkness was a relief. “The next move is to burn the telegram. Before my sister sees it.

“Why?”

“Because the truth is… inefficient. It causes mess. Delay.

“And what does the lie get you?”

“A clear path. No questions. The story can continue.” The words were coming faster now, not from memory, but from a cold, clear place behind her breastbone. “The lie is clean. The truth has jagged edges. It catches on things.

“What does it cost?”

Iris opened her eyes. Pauline was standing three feet away, her arms crossed. Her expression was one of pure, clinical focus. No tenderness. No recognition of the night before. Just the work.

“It costs a piece of your voice,” Iris said, the words out before she could shape them. “Every time you tell it, the part of you that knew the truth gets quieter. Until one day you can’t hear it at all. You just hear the lie. And it sounds like your own voice.

Silence.

Pauline didn’t blink. She studied Iris’s face as if it were a difficult text. The only sound was the fly, and the distant, irregular thump of a steam pipe coming to life in the wall.

“Good,” Pauline said, finally. The word was not praise. It was an assessment. “That’s the calculation. The cost of the lie versus the cost of the truth. Now. Do the rest of the monologue from there. From the inside of that cost.

Iris looked at the script on the floor. She didn’t pick it up. She spoke to the empty chairs, to the dust motes dancing in the blade of light.

“So I’ll tell her he’s coming home. I’ll describe the train. The uniform. The way he’ll smile. I’ll make it so real she’ll hear the whistle. And every day after that, I’ll add a detail. A letter he sent. A promotion. A posting overseas that delays him. I’ll build him a whole life out of words. A ghost life. And mine will be the voice that sustains it. Mine will be the only voice left.

Her own words chilled her. They hung in the air, precise and terrible.

Pauline took a step closer. “And what does Eleanor want? Right then, in that room, with that paper in her hand. Not in the grand scheme. Not for the future. What does she want in her gut?

Iris’s throat tightened. The answer rose, unbidden, from a place she never visited. “She wants someone to walk in. Right then. To see her with the telegram. To take the choice away from her. To make her tell the truth.

“Yes.” The word was a soft exhalation. Pauline’s gaze didn’t waver. “The desire to be caught. The desire for the lie to become impossible. That’s the crack. That’s where the humanity leaks out. Not in the tears. In the wish for intervention.” She paused. Her eyes flickered, just for an instant, to the office door down the hall. Then back. “Now. From the top. And this time, I want to see that wish. I want to see you hoping for the door to open.

The rehearsal became a kind of surgery. Pauline dissected each line, each pause, each shift in posture. She was . She offered no comfort, only demand. The vulnerability of the night before had been sealed away, transformed into a ruthless artistic standard. Iris felt flayed open. Every instinct to prettify, to soften, to make palatable, was excised.

At one point, Clara slipped in from the wings, a clipboard in hand. She watched for a few minutes, her face unreadable. She made a note, then left as quietly as she’d come. The interruption was like a stone dropped into a still pond. The ripples faded, but the water was never quite the same.

They worked until the light faded from gold to grey. Iris’s legs trembled with fatigue. Her voice was a ragged thing.

Pauline finally nodded, a single, sharp dip of her chin. “Enough. You’ve found the shape of it. The architecture. Now you have to live in it.” She gathered her notebook. “We’ll block it with the other actors tomorrow.

She turned to leave.

“Pauline.”

The name felt dangerous in the empty space.

Pauline stopped. She didn’t turn around. Her shoulders were a straight, dark line against the fading window.

“Last night,” Iris said. The words felt thick. “You were… I brought you the coat.

A long silence. Pauline’s head tilted slightly, as if listening to a distant frequency. “I know,” she said, her voice low. “I woke up. Later. I knew.

“You were talking in your sleep. About smoke.

Pauline went very still. Then she turned. Her face was in shadow, her expression impossible to read. “Dreams are irrelevant. The work is what matters. The work is the only thing that’s real.” She said it like a catechism. A wall. “Don’t be late tomorrow.

She walked out, her footsteps echoing down the corridor until they were swallowed by the building’s silence.

Iris stood alone in the gathering dark. The wish for the door to open. The desire to be caught. It hummed inside her, a new and terrible vibration. She looked at the chair where Pauline had sat. A single, crushed cigarette butt lay on the floor beside it. A faint, final wisp of smoke rose from it, or perhaps it was just a trick of the dim light. It spiraled upward once, twice, and vanished into nothing.
