The rooftop smelled of damp stone and the sea. Cornelius stood at the parapet, the first light catching the salt crusted along the tiles. The city below was still half-shadowed, the streets empty except for the occasional slave hurrying to the markets. He ran his thumb along the worn leather edge of his belt, tracing the imprint of the eagle buckle from the back. The habit had always steadied him before inspections, before battles. Now it anchored him in this new, uncharted morning.

A sound behind him—bare feet on the steps. Lydia emerged, her hair still loose from sleep, a clay jug balanced on her hip. She hesitated when she saw him, then dipped her head. “Dominus.”

“Call me Cornelius.” The correction came automatically. He had given it every day since the baptisms.

She pressed her lips together, the old habit of deference warring with the new reality. The jug shifted in her grip. “The water’s fresh. From the cistern.”

He nodded toward the low table where they had shared bread with Peter. “Set it there.”

She did, then lingered, fingers twisting in the fabric of her tunic. “Will he come back? The fisherman?”

Cornelius watched a gull wheel over the harbor. “Not soon.”

Silence stretched. Lydia exhaled through her nose, a soft, frustrated sound. “Then how do we—” She cut herself off, shaking her head.

He knew the shape of the question. How do we pray? How do we gather? How do we keep this fragile thing alive when the man who brought it has gone? The same questions had circled his mind all night, tightening with each hour.

Below, the courtyard gate creaked open. Gaius’s voice, crisp with command, ordering the morning watch to their posts. The ordinary rhythm of the house, unchanged. Everything had changed.

Lydia touched the jug. “The others are waiting. To break fast.”

“Tell them to begin.”

She hesitated. “They want you there.”

Of course they did. He was the centurion. The paterfamilias. The one who had called them to this. He flexed his hands, the scars along his knuckles pale in the dawn light. “I’ll come.”

She left, her footsteps light on the stairs.

The rooftop was quiet again. The sea stretched to the horizon, flat and endless. He thought of the Germanian woman’s face—not the grief this time, but the way she had looked at him just before the torch fell. Not with hatred. With a terrible, exhausted knowing.

He had spent a decade trying to scrub that look away. With discipline. With ritual. With the exacting precision of a soldier’s piety.

A child’s laughter rose from the courtyard, bright and unburdened. His daughter’s, he thought. Or perhaps the kitchen boy’s. The sound of it lodged in his chest, sharp as a splinter.

He turned from the parapet.

The triclinium was too large for their numbers. The slaves and soldiers clustered at one end of the table, the bread and olives passing hand to hand in a silence that was neither comfortable nor tense, but something raw in between. Drusilla sat at the center, her back straight, her fingers laced in her lap. She had not eaten.

Cornelius took his place beside her. The room still smelled of the fish stew from last night, undercut by the faint metallic tang of the baptismal water that had seeped into the mosaic cracks.

Gaius cleared his throat. “The men are asking—”

“Let them ask me directly.”

A muscle jumped in Gaius’s jaw. He was a good soldier. He knew the chain of command. But this was not the legion.

Lydia broke off a piece of bread, her movements deliberate. “We could sing. Like they did in Jerusalem.”

The kitchen boy—Tertius—flinched. “Not the Roman hymns.”

“No. The other ones.”

A pause. Then, softly, Lydia began. It was not a song Cornelius recognized. The words were Aramaic, the melody uneven, as if she were piecing it together from fragments heard only once. But the others leaned in. One of the soldiers hummed along, finding the rhythm.

Drusilla’s hands tightened in her lap.

Cornelius reached for the jug. The water was cool against his fingers. He poured a cup, the sound loud in the half-song.

Gaius watched him. “What do we do when the prefect calls you to muster?”

“Obey.”

“And if he orders you to make sacrifice?”

The singing faltered. Lydia’s voice trailed off.

Cornelius set the cup down. The water trembled, catching the light. “Then we will decide.”

A murmur around the table. Not dissent. Not quite.

Drusilla stood abruptly. “The bread is stale.” She walked out, her sandals clicking against the tile.

The silence she left behind was heavier. The kitchen boy stared at his hands. One soldier shifted, his training itching under his skin.

Gaius exhaled. “We need rules.”

Cornelius looked at him. “We have them.”

“Not Rome’s.”

The words hung there. Not a challenge. A plea.

Outside, a cart rattled past the house, the driver calling prices for his wares. The ordinary world, moving on.

Cornelius stood. “We keep the watch. We break bread. We tell what we saw.” He touched the cup. “The rest will come.”

Gaius held his gaze a moment, then nodded.

The kitchen boy reached for another olive. The soldiers resumed eating. Lydia wiped her hands on her tunic, her face turned toward the window, where the light was growing stronger.

Cornelius left the triclinium. He found Drusilla in the courtyard, her arms wrapped around herself, though the morning was warm.

She didn’t turn. “They’re waiting for you to lead.”

“I am leading.”

“Not as a centurion.”

A bird fluttered down to the fountain, dipping its beak into the water. The same fountain where they had baptized the child.

Cornelius touched his wife’s shoulder. She stiffened, but didn’t pull away.

“We’ll learn,” he said.

The bird took flight, its wings beating against the brightening sky.

The courtyard tiles burned underfoot by midday. Cornelius paused in the colonnade’s shade, watching the household move through their altered rhythms. Lydia knelt by the herb garden, plucking mint leaves with more care than the task required. Two of his soldiers—men who had stood guard at the baptisms—were repairing a cracked water channel, their usual efficiency replaced by a slower, almost deliberate pace. Even the work had changed.

Drusilla emerged from their chamber, a wax tablet in hand. Her stylus hovered over the surface, marking nothing. She stopped when she saw him, her mouth tightening. “The prefect’s wife has invited us to the Lupercalia rites.”

He knew the shape of those festivities—the blood smeared on foreheads, the feasting, the bawdy songs. A year ago, he would have ordered the household to prepare their best tunics. Now the thought turned his stomach.

“Send our regrets.”

Her fingers whitened around the tablet. “On what grounds?”

“The child is feverish.” A half-truth—Tertius had coughed twice at breakfast.

She breathed out sharply through her nose. “Next month is the Matronalia. Then the Liberalia.”

“I know the calendar.”

“Do you?” She stepped closer, her voice dropping. “Because you still wear the uniform. You still draw pay from the treasury.”

A crash from the kitchen cut her off. They both turned.

Lydia stood over a shattered amphora, olive oil pooling at her feet. Her face went slack with horror. A week’s wages wasted.

Before Cornelius could speak, Gaius was there, crouching to gather the shards. “We’ll salvage what we can.” He said it calmly, but his shoulders were rigid.

The kitchen boy darted forward with a rag. One of the soldiers fetched a bucket. No one shouted. No one demanded punishment.

Drusilla made a small, strangled sound. “This is what I mean.”

Cornelius rubbed his thumb along his belt. The leather was warm from the sun.

They gathered at dusk, as they had each evening since Peter left. Not in the triclinium with its couches and proper order, but in the courtyard, seated on stools or the low garden wall. The kitchen boy passed cups of watered wine. Lydia had baked the bread herself—round loaves, smaller than the Roman style, more like what Peter’s people had shared.

Gaius cleared his throat. “The centurion at the Antonia Fortress asked why we no longer attend the officers’ sacrifices.”

A beat of silence.

Lydia broke off a piece of bread. “What did you tell him?”

“That we pray differently now.”

One of the soldiers—Marcellus—snorted. “He called you superstitious.”

Gaius’s jaw tightened. “He called us Jews.”

The word hung in the air, heavier than the incense drifting from a neighbor’s house.

Cornelius studied his cup. The wine was thin, more water than fruit. A Jerusalem custom, Peter had said.

Drusilla set her bread aside, untouched. “We could attend the rites but not partake. As we did before.”

Lydia’s hands stilled. “Before?”

“When we only feared the Jewish God. Not when we—” Drusilla gestured vaguely at the circle.

“Belonged to him,” Gaius finished.

The fountain burbled. Somewhere beyond the walls, a lyre played, the notes bright and careless.

Cornelius thought of the Germanian village again, not the fire, but the moment before. The chieftain had offered him a cup in the old way, both hands extended. A guest’s portion. He had taken it, drunk deeply, then given the order to torch the granary.

He set his cup down. “We won’t pretend.”

Drusilla’s eyes flashed. “Then what will we do when the prefect commands your presence at the imperial cult?”

“We’ll decide then.”

“You keep saying that!” Her voice cracked. The kitchen boy flinched.

Lydia reached for another loaf. Her fingers trembled. “Peter said the Spirit would guide us.”

“And where is he?” Drusilla’s nails dug into her palms.

“Enough.” Cornelius didn’t raise his voice. He didn’t need to.

His wife went very still. The others held their breath.

A moth fluttered into the circle, drawn by the lamp. It circled once, twice, then landed on the edge of Cornelius’s cup. Its wings were the color of dust.

He watched it flex its delicate legs. “We keep the watch. We break bread. We tell what we saw.”

Gaius steadied. “And when the orders come?”

The moth took flight, vanishing into the dark.

He picked up his cup. “Then we’ll stand together.”

Drusilla’s lips parted. He thought she might argue. Then, slowly, she reached for her own cup.

The kitchen boy let out a shaky breath. Marcellus nudged the bread toward Lydia. Gaius poured more wine, his hands steady now.

Overhead, the stars emerged, one by one. The same stars that had watched over Germanian forests and Judean hills. The same stars Peter would see tonight, wherever he was.

Cornelius drank. The wine tasted different now, not better, not worse, but alive in a way he couldn’t name.

Lydia began to sing. The others joined, their voices uneven but growing stronger. Not a hymn to Rome. Not a psalm of David. Something new, born in this in-between space.

Drusilla didn’t sing. But she didn’t leave. And when the song ended, her hand brushed his under the table. A fleeting touch, barely there.

Enough.

The olive press stood idle in the corner of the courtyard, its wooden beams dry and silent. Cornelius ran a hand along the worn handle, remembering the rhythmic creak of its labor, the rich scent of crushed fruit that used to cling to the stones. Now it gathered dust. The household bought their oil from a Syrian merchant near the harbor, a man whose wife had been among the first to hear Lydia’s account of the baptisms.

Lydia herself knelt by the herb garden, plucking withered leaves from the rosemary. Her movements were slower these days, her shoulders rounded with a weight that had nothing to do with labor. She glanced up as he approached, her fingers pausing mid-motion. “Dominus.” The old title slipped out less often now, but still, sometimes, when she was tired.

He crouched beside her, the joints in his knees protesting. “The mint is thriving.”

She touched a leaf, her nail tracing its serrated edge. “It’s the only thing that is.”

A breeze carried the salt-and-fish stink of the harbor, mingling with the sharper tang of the herbs. Somewhere beyond the courtyard wall, a street vendor called out prices for figs. The ordinary sounds of Caesarea, unchanged.

Cornelius studied Lydia’s profile, the tightness around her mouth, the shadows beneath her eyes. “Gaius told me about your sister.”

Her hands stilled.

“He said she’s ill.”

Lydia released the tension through her nose, a slow, controlled breath. “Fever. The physician bled her twice. It didn’t help.”

“You should go to her.”

Her head jerked up. “You would permit it?”

“You don’t need my permission.”

She stared at him, her lips parted. The unspoken truth hung between them: she did. Not legally, perhaps, but in every way that mattered. A slave didn’t leave her master’s household without leave, no matter what new words they’d spoken over the baptismal water.

Cornelius plucked a sprig of mint, rolling it between his fingers. The scent clung to his skin. “Take Tertius with you. The boy knows the city’s back ways.”

Lydia’s throat worked. “And if—” She stopped, swallowed. “If she asks for the rites?”

He met her gaze. “Then give them.”

Her breath hitched. She looked away, toward the kitchen where the boy was scrubbing pots, his cough long since faded. “The prefect’s men watch the eastern gate.”

“Then go by the fish market. Marcellus will cover your absence at muster.”

She pressed her palms to her thighs, leaving green smudges on the fabric. “You know what they’ll say if they catch us. That we’re spreading Jewish magic.”

“I know.”

The rosemary shivered in the breeze. A bee hovered near the lavender, its wings a blur.

Lydia wiped her hands on her tunic. “I’ll return before dark.”

He nodded.

She stood, hesitated, then reached for his hand. Her fingers were calloused, warm. “Thank you.”

The words were simple. The weight behind them was not.

Cornelius watched her go, her steps quickening as she neared the kitchen. A boy’s voice rose in question, then fell to a murmur. A moment later, the side gate creaked open and shut.

Drusilla’s shadow fell across the herbs. “You sent her.”

He didn’t turn. “Yes.”

Her sandals scuffed the dirt as she stepped closer. “You know the risk.”

“I do.”

She let out a sharp pause. “And if the prefect hears?”

“Then we’ll answer.”

“With what, Cornelius? The truth?” Her voice was low, edged with something brittle. “Shall we tell him an angel spoke to you? That a Jewish fisherman poured water over our heads and called it salvation?”

He looked up at her. The sun caught the silver in her hair, turning it to wire. “Would you rather I lied?”

Her jaw tightened. “I’d rather you thought.”

“I have.”

The old argument. He rose, his knees protesting. “Is that what you want? For me to command this household as I did the cohort?”

She didn’t flinch. “I want you to keep us safe.”

“Safe.” He tasted the word, bitter as unripe olives. “Is that what we were before?”

A muscle jumped in her cheek. She turned away, her arms crossing over her chest. “You’ve changed.”

He didn’t deny it.

The fountain burbled, the sound too loud in the silence. A lizard darted across the stones, its tail flicking.

Drusilla’s shoulders slumped. “I miss the certainty.”

He touched her elbow felt the tension there. “So do I.”

She didn’t pull away.

The summons came at dusk.

Gaius delivered it, his face grim in the lamplight. “The prefect wants you at first watch.”

Cornelius unrolled the scrap of parchment. The message was brief, the handwriting hurried. Report to the Antonia Fortress. Bring your men.

Drusilla stood in the doorway, her fingers gripping the frame. “Why?”

Gaius shifted his weight. “There’s talk in the barracks. About Lydia’s sister.”

Cornelius folded the parchment. “Who spoke of it?”

“A legionary from the Syrian cohort. He frequents the wine shop near her sister’s house.”

Drusilla’s pause caught. “Then they know.”

Gaius hesitated. “They know she visited. Not why.”

Cornelius touched the eagle buckle at his belt. The metal was cool beneath his fingers. “Ready the men.”

Gaius nodded and left, his footsteps echoing in the colonnade.

Drusilla stepped forward. “You can’t go.”

“I must.”

“They’ll ask you to swear by the emperor’s genius.”

He said nothing.

Her hands clenched. “Cornelius.”

He met her gaze. “Do you remember the Germanian chieftain?”

The question caught her off guard. She blinked. “What?”

“The man whose village I burned. The night before the raid, he offered me a cup. A guest’s portion.” He traced the edge of the buckle. “I took it. Drank deeply. Then I gave the order.”

Her face went very still.

“I’ve spent ten years trying to wash that taste from my mouth.” He let his hand fall. “I won’t take another cup.”

The lamplight flickered, casting long shadows across the floor. Somewhere in the house, the kitchen boy laughed, the sound bright and unburdened.

Drusilla reached for him. Her fingers brushed his wrist, light as a moth’s wing. “Then I’ll go with you.”

He covered her hand with his own. “No.”

“They won’t dare—”

“They will.” He squeezed her fingers. “Stay with the household.”

Her throat worked. He thought she might argue. Then she nodded, once, sharp.

Cornelius turned to the window. The city sprawled beyond, its lamps flickering to life one by one. The Antonia Fortress loomed in the distance, its torches already lit.

He thought of Lydia, kneeling by her sister’s pallet. Of Gaius, standing firm when the others wavered. Of Drusilla, her silvered hair catching the light.

The taste of the Germanian’s cup was gone. In its place, something new, something alive.

He straightened his tunic. “Keep the watch until I return.”

Drusilla’s chin lifted. “We will.”

The word settled in his chest, warm as embers. We.

The city’s noise rose around him, laughter from a nearby tavern, the clatter of cart wheels, the distant cry of a night heron. Caesarea, alive and unyielding.

He walked toward the fortress, his steps steady. The household’s song followed him, carried on the wind.
