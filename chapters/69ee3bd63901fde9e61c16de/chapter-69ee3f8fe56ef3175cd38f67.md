The smell was the first thing, always. It lived in the wood of the stairs, in the plaster of the walls, a sour, organic tang that no amount of lime or vinegar could scour away. Not rot, exactly. A transformation. The smell of hides becoming leather, of life being cured into utility. Peter breathed it in as he descended from the rooftop at first light. It was the smell of his host’s living, and it marked the house as a place apart.

Simon’s courtyard was a shallow rectangle of packed earth, shaded by a single, gnarled fig tree. Along the far wall stood the stone vats, stained a permanent, dark ochre. One was filled with a murky liquid, the soaked hide within barely visible beneath the surface like some drowned creature. A slave, a young man with a shaved head and a leather apron moved between the vats with a long pole, his movements slow with early morning torpor. He didn’t look at Peter. The tanner’s trade, necessary as it was, rendered a man ritually unclean. Touching dead flesh, handling the byproducts of death. Simon navigated this with a pragmatic piety washing and praying with a diligence that spoke of constant negotiation. Peter, as a guest, managed it too. He kept his distance from the workspaces, a habit so ingrained it felt like breathing.

The main room was cool and dim. Simon was already at the low table, tearing a round of flatbread with blunt, stained fingers. His wife, Martha, placed a bowl of olives between them. Morning light, slicing through the high window caught the dust motes dancing above the table like tiny, restless spirits.

“Sleep well, brother?” Simon’s voice was a gravelly rumble, worn down by years of shouting over the scrape of fleshing knives and the chatter of the market.

“Well enough.” Peter took a seat on the woven mat, accepting a piece of bread. It was a lie. He had slept in fits, his dreams a muddle of nets with holes too large to mend, of faces he did not recognize pressing in from the edges of a crowd, their mouths moving in silent appeal.

Martha poured watered wine into clay cups. “Another day the Lord has made,” she said it without looking up, a ritual utterance. A thin scar, pale against her olive skin, traced the line of her jaw. Peter found himself staring at it wondering at its origin—a cooking accident, a childhood fall. The kind of detail a man notices when his own thoughts are a place he doesn’t want to be.

He ate. The bread was good, coarse and nutty. The olives were bitter. He chewed slowly, listening to the sounds of the house waking: the slave’s sandals scuffing in the yard, the distant cry of a fishmonger from the direction of the harbor, the rhythmic creak of the rope from the well next door. Joppa was stirring. His own community, the believers scattered through the city’s Jewish quarter would be gathering soon for morning prayers, debating, sharing, caring for the widows. The practical, tangible work of the fledgling assembly. He should be among them. Yet here he sat, in this liminal house by the sea held by a restless inertia he couldn’t name.

“The shipment from Ashkelon arrives today,” Simon said, wiping his mouth with the back of his hand. “Good quality, they say. Fewer parasite holes.” He spoke of hides the way another man might speak of grain or pottery. It was his world. “I’ll need to be at the quay by the third hour.”

Peter nodded. “I will pray for your business.”

Simon grunted, a sound that could have meant thanks or acknowledgment or nothing at all. He pushed back from the table. “The roof is yours. Quiet up there, once the gulls move off.”

They finished the meal in a silence that was not uncomfortable, just occupied. Martha cleared the plates with efficient clatters. Simon disappeared into the yard, his voice rising in curt instruction to the slave. Peter remained seated, his fingers tracing a deep gouge in the table’s wood. It ran against the grain, a violent, careless mark. Someone had slammed something down, a long time ago. A pot, a tool, a fist. The house was full of such scars, the accumulated history of a difficult craft practiced in a confined space.

He thought of the home in Capernaum, of the clean smell of lake wind and baking fish. He thought of the upstairs room in Jerusalem, the charged silence after the Teacher had gone. Each place had its own texture, its own spiritual scent. This house smelled of process, of a boundary-line occupation. Fitting, perhaps. Where else would he be, when his own thoughts were fixated on boundaries?

The news from Samaria had settled in him like a stone in his gut. It was a joy, of course. A miraculous confirmation. The Spirit moving, the baptism, the breaking down of an ancient, bitter wall between Jew and Samaritan. He had rejoiced with John had felt the surge of holy astonishment. But in the quiet hours since, a colder, more granular unease had taken root. If the wall to Samaria could fall, what other walls were merely waiting for the divine push? The God-fearers in the synagogues, the Gentiles like Cornelius who prayed to the God of Israel gave alms kept the moral law—but who remained uncircumcised, outside the covenant, eternally other. Where did they fit in the new kingdom? Were they a problem to be solved later, or were they, somehow, the point?

A question felt like a betrayal. It tasted like ash in his mouth. He was a son of Israel, a keeper of the Law. The covenant was with Abraham’s children, not with the nations who bowed to stone and empire. The Teacher had come for the lost sheep of the house of Israel. He had said so.

But he had also spoken of other sheep. Of a vineyard where the late-coming workers received the same wage. Of a faith greater than any bloodline.

Peter’s head ached with the contradiction. It was not a theological puzzle to be solved. It was a fault line running beneath the foundation of everything he knew. To even entertain the question felt like standing at the edge of one of Simon’s vats, peering into the murk wondering what it would be like to let go and sink in.

He stood abruptly, the mat rustling under his knees. He needed air that didn’t smell of tannin and salt. He needed the roof.

The stairs were narrow, steep, the wooden treads worn concave in the middle. He climbed them as he had every morning since coming to Joppa, a daily retreat. The rooftop was flat, covered in cracked plaster, bounded by a low parapet. To the east, the jagged silhouette of the city climbed the hill, white walls glowing in the early sun. To the west, nothing but the sea, a vast, breathing plain of green and silver, stretching to the curve of the world.

The gulls were there, as Simon predicted. They wheeled and screamed over the harbor, their cries the sound of the sea given voice. He watched them for a moment, their effortless pivots on the wind. They knew nothing of covenants or cleanliness. They lived on fish and garbage, creatures of the air and the water’s edge, belonging to neither land nor deep.

He walked to the western parapet, his shadow long and thin before him. The tanner’s house was built on the very margin of the city, where the urban noise gave way to the slap of waves against stone. Below, the rock-cut pools used for washing the hides were already flooded with the rising tide, the water sloshing in and out with a weary, rhythmic sound. A stray dog, ribs visible beneath its mangy coat, sniffed along the base of the wall, then lifted its leg against a corner post.

Peter turned his back on the sea, facing toward Jerusalem. He assumed the posture of prayer, standing, his hands open at his sides. The words of the Shema formed in his mind.

They died there. The familiar cadence, the anchor of a lifetime, refused to come. His mind was a jumble of faces instead. The hopeful, anxious face of the Greek-speaking widow they’d provisioned last week. The hardened, closed face of a Pharisee who had challenged him in the synagogue. The joyful, disbelieving face of John when he’d brought the news from Samaria. And beneath them all, like a dark watercolor bleeding through parchment, the face of the Roman centurion from his dream.

It had been the same dream, three nights running. Not a vision, not a clear message. Just a man in the polished armor of a Roman officer, standing at a distance on a clean, ordered street. The man said nothing. He only watched, his expression unreadable—not hostile, not pleading. Waiting. And in the dream, Peter knew he was expected to cross the street, to approach this man who was the very embodiment of the occupation, of the pagan world. His feet would not move. A street between them seemed to widen, becoming a chasm.

He opened his eyes. The sun was higher now, warming the plaster beneath his feet. The gull-cries had faded somewhat, replaced by the distant clang of a harbor bell. He tried again, forcing his thoughts upward. “Lord. Guide us. The way is… unclear.”

The prayer felt small, swallowed by the immensity of the sky. He was a fisherman on a roof, grappling with a net that seemed to grow larger, its mesh finer, designed to catch things he had spent a lifetime learning to throw back. The peace he sought, the clear directive did not come. Only the restless churn of the sea, and the persistent, sour smell rising from the yard below, reminding him where he stood—in a house of impurity, on the edge of the known world, waiting for a wind to fill his sails when he no longer knew the course.

He would stay on the roof. He would wait. It was all he knew to do.

The sun climbed, a white coin hammered into the dome of the sky. Its heat pressed down on the rooftop plaster, pulling up the scent of dried lime and old rain from the cracks. Peter remained standing, but his posture had slackened. His hands, which had been open, now hung at his sides, fingers curling slightly as if around the handles of an invisible oar. The prayer had not come. A memory had, sharp and unbidden.

A fishing net, years ago, torn on a submerged rock off Bethsaida. Not a clean rent, but a ragged constellation of small holes, the cords frayed and weeping. Mending it was tedious, finicky work. You had to tie each break individually, a tiny knot of repair, and the pattern of the damage never made sense. One hole here, another a hand’s breadth away, no logic to it. You’d fix one and see three more you’d missed. His father had worked beside him in silence, his own gnarled fingers moving with a slow, constant patience that felt like a rebuke to Peter’s frustration. “The sea takes what it wants,” his father had said, not looking up. “Our job is to put it back together. Not to ask why it tore.”

The memory faded, leaving the taste of salt on his tongue. This was the same. The net of the covenant was torn. Samaria was a hole, now mended by the Spirit’s own hand. But the fraying continued at the edges, among the God-fearers. Were they another hole to be tied off, or were they a different kind of damage altogether?

His stomach clenched, a hollow, insistent fist. He had eaten little at breakfast, and the bread was gone now, burned away by the churn of his thoughts. Fasting had always cleared his mind, sharpened his focus for prayer. Today it felt like a slow draining, leaving him light-headed and strangely porous. The sounds of Joppa amplified—the metallic shout of a coppersmith from three streets over, the rhythmic thud of a loom, the shrill bargaining of women at the well. Each noise was a needle prick on his skin.

He lowered himself to sit, his back against the warm parapet. The rough plaster snagged his tunic. He closed his eyes, seeking the inner dark, the quiet place where God’s voice sometimes settled. But the faces returned. Not just the Roman’s from the dream. The faces of the Gentiles who lingered at the back of the synagogue in Caesarea, their expressions a mix of yearning and profound confusion as they listened to the Torah readings they could never fully own. He had seen them. He had pitied them. Their devotion was impressive, but it was a devotion from the outside, noses pressed against the glass of a banquet to which they had no invitation.

What did they eat? One question surfaced, absurd and specific. What did a Roman centurion, a man like the one in his dream, eat for his noon meal? Pork, probably. Sausage seasoned with garlic and herbs forbidden by the Law. He would eat it without a thought, without a blessing, the juice of it on his chin. Its image was vivid, repellent. Peter’s own saliva grew thick. His empty stomach lurched.

This was the wall. Not stone or legionary armor, but this deep, raw recoil. Holiness was separation. Clean from unclean. Sacred from profane. Israel from the nations. You could not simply wish that away. You could not theologize a pig clean.

A gust of wind came off the sea, cooler, carrying the damp, briny smell of low tide. It flapped the edge of his head-covering. He opened his eyes. The sea was changing color, the green deepening to a bruised purple where a cloud shadow passed. He watched the shadow move, a stain spreading on the water.

Other sheep. The Teacher’s words spoken in the last confusing days before the end. I have other sheep that are not of this fold. I must bring them also.

Peter had nodded then, thinking he understood. The scattered tribes, perhaps. Or the righteous among the nations in some distant, final reckoning. He had not thought of a living, breathing Roman soldier, his hands calloused from a sword grip, his soul somehow hungry for a God whose laws he could never keep.

“They cannot be brought in unless they become one of us,” Peter said aloud, his voice a dry rasp against the wind. “They must take on the yoke. The circumcision, the law. It is the door.”

But the voice in his head, which sometimes sounded like his own and sometimes like the ghost of a question the Teacher had asked, replied: Who made you the doorkeeper?

He had no answer. Only the tightening in his gut, the ache behind his eyes from squinting at a too-bright sky. The hunger was no longer just an absence. It was a presence, a dull animal pacing inside the cage of his ribs. He felt faint, untethered. The solid rooftop beneath him seemed to sway, just slightly, as if the house itself floated on the tide.

He looked down at his hands. They were broad, scarred across the knuckles from nets and weather. Good hands for hauling, for mending, for holding the familiar weight of a fishing knife. They were not the hands of a philosopher. He was not built for this—for parsing the infinite mind of God, for stretching the covenant like a hide over a frame it was never meant to fit. He was built for doing. Go here. Preach this. Heal that. The clarity of a command.

The lack of one now was a form of torture.

A lizard, its body the color of dust, darted from a crack in the parapet and froze, its throat pulsing. Peter watched it, mesmerized by its perfect stillness. Its tiny black eye seemed to hold a complete and untroubled universe. It knew its place. Insect. Sun. Stone. It did not anguish over the purity of its prey.

A shadow on the water reached the shore, plunging the rock pools below into a sudden, gloomy chill. The wind picked up, whistling through a gap in the rooftop railing. It carried a new smell cutting through the tannin—the smell of rain, still far out at sea. A storm coming.

Peter’s vision blurred at the edges. The white city walls to the east shimmered, losing their definition. He blinked, hard. The prayer he had been unable to form now condensed into a single, desperate word spoken inward, too dry for his throat to give it sound.

Help.

As if in answer, the wind shifted. It came from the south now, from the direction of the desert. It was a different wind, hotter, drier, carrying not the promise of rain but the grit of distant sands. It swirled across the rooftop, lifting a spray of dust and dried fig leaves.

And with it came a sound.

Not a voice. Not yet. A resonance, deep in his bones, like the lowest note from a temple trumpet felt more than heard. The air tightened, pressed in on his eardrums. The mundane sounds of Joppa—the smith, the loom, the gulls—did not cease, but they receded, as if someone had stepped behind a thick curtain.

Peter went very still. The lizard vanished.

His skin prickled, every hair on his arms standing upright. This was not the feeling of prayerful closeness, that warm, assured presence. This was something older, wilder. It was the feeling that came before the thunder on the Lake of Galilee, when the water went flat and leaden and the air tasted of iron. It was the charged silence before the tearing of the temple veil.

He tried to rise, but his limbs were heavy, weighted by the strange, thickening air. His hunger was gone, replaced by a buzzing fullness, as if his very blood hummed.

Before him, the sky over the sea did not darken with storm clouds. The clear blue seemed to bleach, to pale into a terrible, sterile whiteness. It was not a vision of something. It was an unraveling of the ordinary. The green of the sea leached away into a sheet of dull metal. The white walls of the city became bones under a sun.

And then, descending from that white nothingness, he saw it.

A great sheet.

It was not cloth. It had the semblance of cloth, a vast, four-cornered expanse, but it was made of substance that held light and shadow differently. It seemed to be woven from air and intention. It was being let down from the heavens, not falling but lowering with a deliberate, terrifying gentility. It descended toward him, toward this rooftop on the edge of the world, and as it drew nearer, he saw what it contained.

All kinds of four-footed animals, creatures of the earth, wild beasts, reptiles.

And birds of the air.

A chaos of life, a moving, breathing, rustling tumult. He saw the curved horns of a gazelle, the sinuous coil of a snake, the bristled back of a boar. He saw the bright, beady eye of a raven, the strut of a stork. They were not crammed; they existed together in the impossible space of the sheet, a tableau of creation that defied the first chapter of Genesis, where God had separated them all according to their kinds.

His pulse stopped. His mind screamed the categories, the ancient, sacred divisions. Clean. Unclean.

There were sheep in the sheet. And there were pigs, their pink, hairy flanks obvious. There were eagles and vultures alongside doves. A hyena sat, tongue lolling, beside a sleeping lamb.

A voice spoke. It was not in his ear. It was in the center of his skull, clear, and as vast as the sky from which the sheet hung.

Get up, Peter. Kill and eat.

The command was an earthquake in his soul. Every fiber of him, every year of law and custom and identity, recoiled. His body went rigid with refusal. This was not a test of hunger. This was an assault on the core of who he was.

“Surely not, Lord!” The words tore from him, a raw shout into the heavy air. “I have never eaten anything impure or unclean!”

The echo in the air deepened, vibrating in the pit of his teeth. The sheet hung, flickering with its impossible cargo. A voice came again, not angry, not pleading. Final. Like stone being set.

Do not call anything impure that God has made clean.

The words struck him with physical force. He swayed back, as if pushed.

The sheet did not vanish. It remained, a suspended impossibility. The boar rooted at the corner. The vulture preened a feather. The clean and the unclean, together, declared clean by a fiat that rewrote the world.

It happened again. A second time, the command. The same animals, the same terrible, beautiful mingling. Kill and eat. His refusal was weaker now, a whisper of instinct overcome by the drowning tide of divine contradiction.

And a third time. The sheet filled his vision, the whole of creation bundled and offered. Then, as suddenly as it had come, it was drawn back up into the white heaven, vanishing as if through a seam in the sky.

The ordinary sounds of Joppa crashed back in—a donkey braying, a child crying, the harbor bell. The sea was green again. The city walls were solid stone. The hot, gritty wind was just wind.

Peter was on his knees. He did not remember falling. He pressed his forehead against the sun-warmed plaster of the rooftop. His body trembled, a fine, uncontrollable shaking that started in his hands and ran up through his shoulders. The vision had lasted only moments, but it had carved a channel through him, emptying him of every certainty he possessed.

The command echoed in the new, hollow space. Kill and eat.

He understood nothing. And yet, in the devastation of his old understanding, a terrifying, electric clarity began to flicker. This was not about food. The animals were just the sign, the first broken symbol.

This wall was not just damaged. It was not to be mended.

It was to be dismantled, stone by sacred stone, and he was being handed the first crowbar.

Peter stayed there, his knees grinding into the plaster, until the trembling subsided into a deep, cellular exhaustion. The heat of the day had reached its peak. The sun was a white brand on the back of his neck. The vision was gone, but its afterimage pulsed behind his eyes—the boar’s coarse hair, the vulture’s naked head, the terrible togetherness of it all. His mind scrabbled for purchase, for a way to file this event into a category he understood. A test of loyalty, perhaps. A warning against presumption. God would not could not, overturn the Law. It was eternal, the very pattern of holiness. The voice must have meant something else. A metaphor. The animals were… sins. Yes. Sins of the flesh, bundled together, and he was being told to purge them. Or they were the Gentile nations, and he was being shown their spiritual state—a confused mingling. The command to kill and eat was symbolic. It had to be.

He pushed himself up, his joints stiff. His mouth was a desert. He needed water. He needed to talk to John, to James, to someone who could help him unravel this. He turned toward the stairwell, his shadow a puddle at his feet.

A knock sounded from below.

It was a firm, official rap of knuckles on wood, not the casual slap of a neighbor. It came from the street gate, not the house door. Peter froze, one foot toward the stairs. Simon had few visitors of that sort. Tanners did business at the yard, not the residence.

The knock came again, three measured beats.

His heart, which had slowed to a weary thud gave a single, hard contraction. A cold thread of understanding, too awful to acknowledge, spun through the numbness. No. It was a coincidence. A tax collector. A message from the brethren in Jerusalem.

He heard the shuffle of Simon’s sandals in the courtyard below, the creak of the gate hinge. A man’s voice, deep, speaking Greek with the clipped, confident accent of a soldier or an imperial servant. He could not make out the words. Then Simon’s voice, higher, surprised. “Of course. Please. He is at prayer. On the roof.”

Footsteps, more than one set, entered the courtyard. They were heavy, disciplined. Not the soft, scuffing steps of fishermen or farmers.

Peter could not move. He was a statue of a man on a roof caught between a vanished vision and an approaching reality. The wind had died completely. The air was a solid, hot weight. From below, he heard the distinct metallic chime of a piece of equipment—a sword pommel knocking against a buckle, or the edge of a shield. Roman military issue.

The top of a head appeared in the dark square of the stairwell, then broad shoulders blocking the light. A man emerged onto the rooftop. He was not a legionary. He wore the fine linen tunic and cloak of a household steward, but his bearing was upright, his hair cut short in the Roman style. His eyes scanned the roof and found Peter. They held no hostility, only a focused curiosity. Another man, similarly dressed, followed him.

They were not soldiers. They were servants. But they moved with a military bearing, and their gaze was the assessing look of men who represented power.

The first man approached, stopping a respectful distance away. He gave a slight, polite nod of the head. Not a bow. A courtesy between equals, or near-equals. His Greek was flawless, formal.

“I am seeking a man named Simon, also called Peter. He is lodging here with Simon the tanner.”

Peter found his voice. It came out rough, scraped raw. “I am he.”

The man’s expression did not change, but his shoulders relaxed a fraction, as if a minor logistical hurdle had been cleared. “My name is Claudius Lysias. I serve the centurion Cornelius, of the Italian Cohort, stationed in Caesarea.” He paused, letting the name and title hang in the hot air. “Cornelius is a righteous man, one who fears God and is well spoken of by the entire Jewish nation. He was instructed by a holy angel to send for you, to bring you to his house, so that he may hear what you have to say.”

Peter turned from the sea. He went down the stairs to meet them.
