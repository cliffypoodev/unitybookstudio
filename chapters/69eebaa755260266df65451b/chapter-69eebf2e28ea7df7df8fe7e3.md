The radiator’s hiss was the only sound in the apartment. Cassius stared at the blank television screen, his reflection a smudge of pale skin and dark hair against the void. The jeans were still on, the marble a familiar pressure against his hip. He’d worn them for three days. The weight was a fact now, like a heartbeat.

Miller’s knock came at seven. Two short raps, then the jangle of keys. The door swung open.

“You’re not dressed.”

Cassius looked down at his T-shirt, the same one from yesterday. “I am dressed.”

“For a funeral maybe.” Miller tossed his coat over the back of the couch. He carried a six-pack in a paper bag, condensation bleeding through the bottom. “Lena’s bringing the pizza. She’s running late, some client call that wouldn’t end.”

The normalcy of it was a warm blanket. Cassius let out a breath he hadn’t known was tight in his chest. Miller moved through the kitchen, opening cabinets, clinking glasses. The sounds were anchors. This was Friday. Pizza. Beer. Miller complaining about his boss. Lena dissecting a case. The ritual was a wall against the static, and for a moment, Cassius leaned against it, grateful.

Lena arrived ten minutes later, her heels clicking a staccato rhythm in the hall before she shouldered the door open, a large cardboard box in her hands. “It’s sausage and mushroom. Don’t give me that look, Miller, you’ll eat it.”

“I hate mushrooms.”

“You hate joy.” She dropped the pizza on the coffee table, shrugged out of her blazer. Her gaze swept over Cassius, a quick, professional assessment. “You look tired.”

“I’m fine.”

“You’re wearing jeans on your couch. You never do that.” She sat, pulled a slice free. Cheese stretched into long, greasy strings. “How’s the head?”

“It’s attached.”

Miller handed him a beer. The bottle was cold, slick. Cassius took a long pull. The bitter taste was good. Real. He sat in his usual armchair, the one with the dent in the cushion that fit his spine. For twenty minutes, the world held. Miller talked about a problematic intern. Lena recounted a deposition that went off the rails. The pizza dwindled to a single slice congealing in the box.

Cassius felt the marble in his pocket. He rubbed it through the denim with his thumb, a circular motion. The conversation lulled settling into the comfortable silence of people who’d known each other too long. This was the moment. One memory surfaced, crisp and bright—a shared laughter, a specific joke, the warmth of collective belonging. It felt safer than Damien. This was recent. This was them.

“Remember that chicken Marsala?” Cassius said, leaning forward. “At your place, Miller. Two months ago. You swore you’d followed the recipe, but you used cooking wine. It tasted like salted vinegar. Lena threatened to order Chinese.”

Miller took a sip of beer. He didn’t smile.

Lena wiped her fingers on a napkin. “What chicken?”

“The dinner. You made that huge deal about using a real Dutch oven. You burned the first batch of shallots.” Cassius chuckled, the sound thin in the quiet room. “We drank that bottle of Malbec you were saving. The one from your trip.”

Miller and Lena looked at each other. A quick glance, there and gone.

“Cass,” Miller said, his voice careful. “I don’t own a Dutch oven.”

“Yes, you do. The blue one. You bought it for that phase.”

“I had a rice cooker phase. Never a Dutch oven phase.” Miller offered a small, conciliatory smile. “You’re thinking of someone else.”

The warm blanket of normalcy slipped. A cold draft took its place. Cassius kept his voice light. “No, it was you. Lena brought that awful kale salad. We ate on your balcony. It started raining, remember? We had to grab everything and run inside. Your neighbor’s cat got in and stole a piece of chicken right off your plate.”

Lena’s brow furrowed. She folded the napkin into a precise square. “I don’t eat kale. I’m allergic to cruciferous vegetables. It makes my throat itch.”

“You said it was a detox.”

“I would never say ‘detox.’ That’s pseudoscience.” Her tone was gentle, the one she used with confused witnesses. “We haven’t had a dinner party at Miller’s in... years. Not since his apartment flooded. That was 2018.”

The static was back, a high-pitched whine just behind his ears. Cassius’s thumb pressed harder on the marble. “This was two months ago. We talked about it for weeks in the group chat. The planning. Lena, you couldn’t decide what to bring.”

“What group chat?” Miller asked.

“The three of us. On our phones.”

Miller pulled his phone from his pocket, tapped the screen, handed it over. The messaging app was open. The thread named “Cass & Lena” showed their last exchange from three days ago—a link to a news article about a city council vote. No third person. No dinner planning. No history of a group including Miller.

Cassius’s own phone felt like a brick in his other pocket. “No. There’s a separate thread. With all three of us.”

“Show me,” Lena said softly.

He dug out his phone, his fingers clumsy. He opened the app, scrolled. His chats: Miller. Lena. Mom. Work. No group. His heart began a slow, heavy thud against his ribs. He went to his search bar, typed “Miller Lena dinner.” Nothing.

“It’s here,” he muttered, scrolling faster. “It has to be.”

“Cassius.” Lena’s voice was a firm, quiet anchor. “It’s okay. You’ve been under a lot of stress. Memory plays tricks.”

“This isn’t a trick!” The words came out sharper than he intended. Miller flinched. Cassius took a breath, forced his voice down. “I remember it. Vividly. The taste of the wine. The pattern on your plates, Miller—those ugly orange sunflowers. The sound of the rain on the balcony glass. You told that story about your cousin’s wedding, the one where the best man fainted.”

Miller’s face went still. He looked at Lena, a silent plea for support.

Lena leaned forward, her elbows on her knees. “Cass, listen to me. This is what happens. The brain gets tired. It... conflates. It mixes things you read, or dreamed, with real events. It feels real because the details are vivid. That’s how it works.”

“I didn’t read about your ugly plates. I didn’t dream the rain.” He could feel the defensiveness hardening his tone, making him sound like a cornered child. He hated it. He took another swig of beer, but it tasted like aluminum now.

The room was too quiet. The radiator sighed.

Miller stood, collected the empty bottles. “Maybe we should call it a night. You look like you could use some sleep.”

“I’m not crazy.” The word hung in the air, ugly and final.

“Nobody said that,” Lena replied, but her eyes were sad. The look you give a patient before you deliver a bad prognosis.

Cassius looked from one face to the other. Their concern was a solid, impenetrable wall. His certainty was a ghost trying to shove through it. The asymmetry was absolute. He had the memory, the sensory weight of it—the smell of burned shallots and rain, the feel of a wine glass stem between his fingers, the ache in his cheeks from laughing. They had a blank space. A null set.

“Check your photos,” he said, a last, desperate gambit. “You took a picture. Of the cat with the chicken. You said it was the funniest thing you’d ever seen.”

Miller didn’t reach for his phone. “I don’t have a cat picture.”

“Just look.”

With a slow, resigned exhale, Miller pulled his phone back out. He swiped, tapped, scrolled. He turned the screen toward Cassius. A gallery of memes, screenshots, a blurry picture of a broken sink. No cat. No balcony. No friends around a table.

The static in Cassius’s ears became a roar. He nodded, his throat too tight for words.

Lena gathered her things. Miller lingered by the door. “Get some rest, okay? We’ll talk tomorrow.”

The door closed behind them. The latch clicked. A sound of separation.

Cassius sat in the silence, the empty pizza box on the table, the smell of garlic and grease still hanging in the air. His phone was in his hand. He opened the messaging app again, staring at the list of conversations. He typed a new message.

To: Miller, Lena
Remember the dinner? The burned shallots? The cat?

He hit send.

Two bubbles appeared almost immediately.

Miller: Go to sleep, Cass.
Lena: We’re worried about you. Call me in the morning.

His message sat there, a lone island in the thread. Seen. Acknowledged. And utterly, completely alone. He wasn’t remembering a different event. He was remembering a different world. And in this one, he was just a tired man who’d imagined a dinner party.

His thumb found the marble again. He closed his eyes. For a second, he was there—the humid air of the balcony, the taste of sour wine, Miller’s laughter booming over the patter of rain. The memory was perfect. Whole.

It was his alone.

He opened his eyes to the empty apartment. The proof was in his pocket, whispering of other suns, other rains. Of a dinner that never was, with friends who had never been there. The horror wasn’t in their denial. It was in the perfect, vivid reality of the memory they denied. The ghost wasn’t in his apartment. It was in his head. And it was throwing a dinner party.

The phone screen burned a white rectangle into the dark of the room. Cassius didn’t move. The two response bubbles had vanished, replaced by Miller’s final text. A period at the end of a sentence.

He scrolled up. The entire history of the “Cass & Lena” thread was mundane. Work complaints. Article links. A brief debate about the best Thai place downtown. No third party. No plans. No jokes that required a third person to land.

His thumb moved on its own, tapping the search function again. He typed “Dutch oven.” No results. “Chicken.” Nothing. “Balcony rain.” The phone returned a blank stare.

A cold, sharp knot tightened behind his sternum. He stood, the motion jerky, and carried the phone to his desk. The laptop was old, its fan whining to life with a dusty complaint. He opened his browser, logged into his cloud account. His photos loaded in a grid of thumbnails. He navigated to the date two months back.

The weekend in question showed three pictures. A receipt for groceries. A screenshot of a weather forecast predicting sun. A blurry shot of a street sign he’d taken for directions. No people. No food. No blue Dutch oven.

He checked his email. Searched “Miller dinner.” A single result: an automated calendar reminder from last year for a dentist appointment he’d missed.

The evidence wasn’t just absent. It had been replaced. The weekend was full of mundane, solitary data points—the digital footprint of a man who had stayed home. He remembered taking the picture of the street sign. He’d been on his way to Miller’s, running late because the bus was off-schedule. That memory was clear. The sun had been in his eyes. He’d squinted at the phone screen.

But according to the time stamp, he’d taken that photo at 10:03 PM. He was never out that late.

Cassius leaned back in his chair. The leather creaked. A car alarm whooped twice on the street below, then cut off.

Conflates. Lena’s word echoed. Clinical. Neat. It suggested a blender, a smoothie of reality and fiction. This wasn’t a smoothie. This was a surgical removal. A specific, detailed event, with its own sensory texture and emotional weight had been cleanly excised from the world’s record. Only his mind held the scar.

He opened his sent messages folder in his email. Scrolled to the date. He never deleted anything. Digital hoarding was a habit, a way to prove to himself that things had happened.

There it was.

A thread with the subject line: “Re: Dinner this Friday?” Sent from his account to both Miller and Lena. Time-stamped: 7:14 PM, two months and three days ago.

His breath caught. He clicked it open.

His own words stared back at him.

Miller – 7:14 PM
Got the wine. That Malbec you like. Should I pick up bread on the way? Lena says she’s making a salad but we all know that means a bag of pre-washed greens. I’ll get a baguette.

Miller – 7:22 PM
And don’t use that cheap cooking wine again. I’m bringing the real stuff. My taste buds still haven’t forgiven you for last time.

Miller – 8:05 PM
Running late. Bus is a nightmare. Be there by 8:30. Don’t burn the shallots.

The messages were his. The cadence was his. The mild, familiar ribbing was exactly how he talked to Miller. He read them aloud, his voice a dry whisper in the silent room. They were real. They had left his phone. They existed in the digital ether.

Where were the replies?

He scrolled down. The thread showed only his sent messages. No responses from Miller. No responses from Lena. His questions hung in the void, unanswered. It was a one-sided conversation, a man talking to a void that looked like his friends’ email addresses.

According to this record, he had sent planning emails into a black hole. And then, according to every other record, he had never sent them at all.

The dread was a physical thing now, a low vibration in his molars. He checked the recipients. The addresses were correct. Miller’s work email. Lena’s Gmail. He copied Miller’s address, opened a new email window, pasted it. The auto-fill populated Miller’s full name. One system recognized it.

So the messages had gone somewhere. They just hadn’t landed.

Or they had landed, and the replies had been deleted from the universe’s server.

Cassius pushed back from the desk. The chair wheels rattled over a floorboard. He walked to the living room window, looked down at the wet street. A neon sign for a laundromat buzzed pink across the pavement. The world outside continued, solid and indifferent.

He thought of the memory. Not the fact of it, but the feel. The warmth of the apartment. The steam from the Dutch oven fogging the kitchen window. Miller’s face, flushed from wine and heat, grinning as he brandished a spatula. Lena rolling her eyes hiding a smile behind her wine glass. The cat—a fat, orange tabby—darting through the sliding door the moment Miller’s back was turned. The collective shout of laughter. The scramble. The chicken clamped in the cat’s mouth as it fled under the couch.

That memory had texture. It had layers. It had the chaotic, unplanned quality of real life. Dreams didn’t have that. Dreams had logic. Symbolism. This was just an evening. A good one.

His phone vibrated on the desk. A new email.

The sound was like a gunshot in the quiet. He flinched. He made himself walk over made himself look.

It was a shipping notification for printer ink he’d ordered last week.

The relief was sickening, followed immediately by a deeper chill. He was waiting for a correction from the universe. A note from God: Sorry, system error. Here are the missing replies.

He sat down again. He opened the sent message thread once more. He hit “reply all.”

His fingers hovered over the keys. What did you say to a ghost thread? To your own words coming back to you from a void?

He typed: Did you get these?

He deleted it. Too desperate.

He typed: The dinner happened.

He deleted it. A statement of fact to an empty room.

He typed: Hello?

He stared at that one. A single word. A child’s call into a cave. He hit send.

The whoosh sound effect seemed louder than usual. The message appeared below the others, the newest member of a lonely chain.

He waited. One minute. Two.

No bounce-back. No error message. The email just sat there in his outbox sent. Delivered, presumably, to the same silent addresses.

This was the proof. Not of the dinner, but of the rupture. He had evidence of the act of planning something that, according to all other parties and all corroborating data had never been planned. He had a fossil of an intention that left no skeleton in the world.

He minimized the email. Opened his text thread with Lena. His last message to her—Remember the dinner?—was still there. Her response below it.

He had a wild, sudden thought. He went to his phone’s notes app. He kept lists there. Groceries. Books to read. Movies people recommended. He searched “Dutch oven.”

One result. A note from over a year ago titled “Miller’s Birthday.” In the list: Bottle of Scotch, Card, Ask him about that blue Dutch oven he wanted.

He had asked. Miller had wanted one. A memory surfaced, not as a full scene, but as a fragment. Miller pointing to a picture in a catalogue. That’s the one. Holds heat like a dream.

So the Dutch oven had existed as a want. A future possibility. In the memory of the dinner, it had become a reality. A detail had jumped from the realm of potential into the area of lived experience, taking an entire evening with it.

Cassius put the phone down. One screen went black.

The apartment was too quiet. The radiator had stopped hissing. The silence had a weight, a density. It pressed against his eardrums.

He thought about calling his mother. Asking her if she remembered him talking about a dinner party. But he already knew what she’d say. She’d ask if he was getting enough sleep. She’d tell him he worked too hard. Her concern would be another kind of erasure, softer than Lena’s but just as absolute.

The dread wasn’t about the dinner anymore. It was about the mechanism. If this could be removed—something so recent, so concrete, so shared—what else was gone? What other anchors had he lost without knowing? The static wasn’t just noise. It was the sound of pieces of his life being switched off, one by one, like lights in a distant city seen from a plane at night.

He looked at the dark television screen. His reflection was still there, a smudge. A man in a chair, surrounded by the quiet evidence of a life that was missing pieces. The ghost wasn’t throwing a dinner party. The ghost was the party. And he was the only one left in the empty room, holding the invitations.

He needed to move. The stillness was a trap. Cassius pushed himself up from the chair, his knees cracking. The apartment smelled of cold pizza and dust. He walked to the kitchen, filled a glass with tap water, and drank it in one go. The water tasted of chlorine and old pipes. A real taste. He set the glass down too hard on the counter. The clunk was satisfying.

His coping mechanism was documentation. When the world went soft, he made it hard. Lists. Timelines. Physical evidence.

He went to the hall closet, pulled down a banker’s box from the top shelf. It was heavier than he remembered. Dust motes danced in the slatted light from the living room lamp. He carried the box to the coffee table, shoving the pizza box aside. The cardboard flaps made a dry, coughing sound as he opened them.

Inside were notebooks. Not fancy journals, but cheap, spiral-bound things. Dozens of them, arranged by year. He pulled out the current one, black cover, the spine cracked from use. He flipped past pages of cramped handwriting—work notes, grocery lists, dreams he’d recorded upon waking. He found the next blank page.

Pen in hand, the ritual began.

Date: October 23. Time: 9:47 PM. Event: Discrepancy confirmed. Dinner at Miller’s (proposed date: August 14) is absent from all external records. Digital footprint shows sent emails (see printouts attached) but no received replies. Miller and Lena have no memory of event. They deny the existence of a group chat, a Dutch oven, the cat incident.

His handwriting was neat, blocky. An engineer’s script. He forced it to stay that way, even as his pulse thumped in his wrist.

Sensory recall of event remains intact. Vivid. Specific details: blue enamel Dutch oven (Le Creuset brand, 5.5 qt). Pattern on plates (orange sunflowers, chipped rim on one). Taste of Malbec (blackberry, high tannin). Sound of rain on balcony glass (irregular patter, wind from the east). Tactile: warmth of steam from pot, condensation on wine glass.

He wrote until his hand cramped. He filled the page with details, each one a brick in a wall against the void. The act of writing made the memory feel more real, but also more distant. It was a specimen now, pinned to the page.

He needed the digital corroboration. He went back to the desk, printed the email thread. The printer whirred, spat out two pages. The black text on white paper looked official. A record. He stapled the pages, slid them into a clear plastic sleeve, and placed them in the box. Then he photographed the notebook page with his phone, saved it to a folder named “Logs,” and backed it up to the cloud. He created a second backup on an external drive he kept in a fireproof lockbox.

The process was meticulous. It usually brought a grim sense of order.

Tonight, it felt like building a sandcastle below the tide line.

He sat back on the couch, the notebook open on his lap. The apartment was a capsule of yellow light in the dark city. A siren Dopplered past, miles away. He noticed a crack in the ceiling plaster, a hairline thing that branched like a river delta. He’d never seen it before. Or he had, and forgotten.

His left hand rested on the notebook page. A tremor started in his thumb.

Subtle. A fine, rapid vibration, as if a tiny engine was idling deep in the joint. He stared at it. He willed it to stop. The tremor danced to his index finger, a shiver under the skin.

He made a fist. The knuckles turned white. The shaking moved to his wrist, a faint quiver that made his watch strap shift against his skin.

This was new.

This was physical.

He opened his hand, laid it flat on the paper. The tremor continued. It wasn’t fear. Fear was a heartbeat, a clutch in the gut. This was mechanical. A system glitch.

He thought of the erased dinner. The phantom thread. The absolute, compassionate denial from the only two people who mattered. The asymmetry was a pressure, and it was finding release through his flesh.

He stood walked to the bathroom, and flicked on the light. The fluorescent bulb buzzed, flickered once, then steadied. He looked at his face in the mirror. Shadows under his eyes. Stubble coming in gray at the corners of his mouth. A man in his thirties looking worn down to the bone.

He held his left hand up, palm toward the mirror.

It was still.

He lowered it. The tremor started again, a faint buzzing in his muscles. He turned on the cold tap and ran water over his wrist. The shock of cold helped for a few seconds. Then the shaking returned, persistent as a hum.

Drying his hand on a towel, he walked back to the living room. He didn’t sit. He paced. Three steps to the window, four steps to the kitchen doorway, three steps back. The rhythm was another form of documentation. The worn path on the carpet proved he was here, moving through time.

What else was gone?

His left hand wouldn’t stop shaking.
