The lecture hall smelled like chalk dust and overheated radiators. Cassius sat halfway back, notebook open but untouched, his pen tapping against the edge of the desk in a rhythm that didn’t match the professor’s cadence. The man at the front had the kind of voice that flattened everything—even the concept of rebirth—into something dry and procedural.

“The Wheel strips the soul clean between lives,” the professor said. “No memory survives. Only patterns. Karmic imprints.” He adjusted his glasses, the lenses catching the late afternoon light in two flat, white squares. “Imagine a library where every book is pulped at closing time. The paper is recycled. The ink is dissolved. The next morning, new books are printed on the same pages. No trace remains of the stories that came before.”

Something about the metaphor itched at him. Not the idea of erasure—that made sense, in a cold, mechanical way—but the certainty of it. The professor spoke as if this were a known fact, a documented process, something observed and measured. Cassius glanced around. The other students scribbled notes or stared blankly at the ceiling. No one else seemed bothered.

He raised his hand.

The professor blinked, as if surprised anyone was still listening. “Yes?”

“How do we know?” Cassius asked.

A few heads turned. The professor’s eyebrows lifted slightly. “Know what?”

“That nothing carries over. If the slate’s wiped clean, how could anyone verify it?”

The professor smiled, the kind of smile adults reserved for children who asked why the sky was blue. “Because no one remembers. That’s the point.”

Cassius’s left hand trembled. Just a flicker, barely noticeable. He pressed his palm flat against the desk. “But absence of evidence isn’t evidence of absence.”

The professor’s smile didn’t waver. “In this case, it is. The system is self-verifying. If memories survived, we’d have accounts. We don’t.”

A girl in the front row snorted. “Maybe you just didn’t read the right books.”

Laughter rippled through the room. The professor’s smile tightened. “If you’d like to explore unsubstantiated metaphysical claims, I’d recommend the philosophy department. This is religious studies. We deal in documented traditions.”

Cassius didn’t laugh. The itch had spread, crawling up his spine. He opened his mouth to push back—then stopped. What was he arguing for? The idea that some part of you might linger might remember felt viscerally true in a way he couldn’t explain. But the words wouldn’t come.

The professor moved on.

Cassius exhaled, slow and controlled. His hand still pressed against the desk, steadying. The lecture hall’s windows were streaked with autumn rain, the world outside blurred into smears of gold and gray. For a second, the colors shifted—not brighter, but sharper, as if someone had adjusted a lens. The scent of salt air, sudden and vivid, filled his nose. Then it was gone.

He blinked. Chalk dust. Radiators. The professor droning on about karmic debt.

Cassius flexed his fingers. The tremor was gone.

When the lecture ended, he lingered as the other students filed out. The professor packed his notes into a worn leather briefcase, ignoring him until the room was nearly empty.

“You’re the Ashbourne boy aren’t you?” he said without looking up.

Cassius stiffened. “Yes.”

“Your thesis advisor mentioned you. Said you had a”—the professor waved a hand—“a peculiar mind.”

Cassius waited.

The professor snapped his briefcase shut. “Peculiar minds often waste their time chasing questions that don’t matter.”

The dismissal should have stung. Instead, Cassius felt something else—a quiet, stubborn certainty, like a bone-deep bruise he couldn’t stop pressing. “What if the question matters because no one’s asking it?”

The professor sighed. “Then you’ll die frustrated.” He walked out.

Cassius stood there for a long moment, alone in the empty lecture hall. The overhead lights buzzed. Somewhere, a radiator hissed.

He turned to leave—then caught his reflection in the window.

For less than a heartbeat, the face looking back wasn’t his. Older. Thinner. A scar cutting through one eyebrow. The image flickered, static on a bad connection, before resolving back into his own familiar features.

Cassius stepped closer. His breath fogged the glass.

The reflection didn’t change again.

He touched the cold pane with two fingers, half-expecting it to ripple. It didn’t.

Outside, the rain had stopped. The path back to his dorm glittered under the streetlights, wet pavement reflecting fractured gold. Cassius shoved his hands into his pockets and walked, the professor’s words looping in his head like a stuck record.

No memory survives.

The weight in his chest disagreed.

Cassius’s dorm room smelled of paper and the faint metallic tang of the radiator under the window. He sat at his desk, the green-shaded lamp casting a pool of light over his open notebook. The rest of the room receded into shadow, the narrow bed with its military-tight sheets, the bookshelf crammed with texts on comparative religion and philosophy, the corkboard pinned with index cards in precise rows. He had transcribed the professor’s lecture verbatim, his handwriting neat and controlled. Now he underlined key phrases, the nib of his pen pressing hard enough to leave faint grooves in the paper beneath.

He circled strips.

A slash through no.

One system is self-verifying.

A question mark in the margin, dark and deliberate.

Outside, a gust of wind rattled the windowpane. Cassius didn’t look up.

1. Documented cases of past-life recall
2. Verifiable details (names, locations, events)
3.

His pen moved steadily, the ink drying almost before it touched the page. The radiator hissed, a sound like distant radio static. He ignored it.

Halfway through point four, his left hand twitched. The pen skipped, leaving a jagged line across the page. Cassius frowned. He pressed his palm flat against the desk, fingers splayed, and waited. The tremor didn’t return. He exhaled through his nose and resumed writing.

Then the smell hit him, salt, sharp and bracing, layered with something warmer beneath it. Sunscreen. Coppertone. The scent was so vivid his throat tightened. He could taste it, the way salt air clung to the back of the tongue.

Cassius froze.

The desk lamp flickered. Not a power surge, a slow, uneven pulse, as if the bulb were breathing. The light took on a golden hue, the color of late afternoon on sand. For a second, he wasn’t in his dorm room.

Somewhere else.

Waves crashed in the distance, a rhythmic thump like a heartbeat. A gull cried. The air was thick, humid, pressing against his skin.

The vision snapped.

Cassius jerked back, his chair scraping against the floor. The lamp burned steady again, the light cool and artificial. His notebook lay open, the ink of his last sentence still wet. His hands were clean. No sand. No salt. Just the faint, ever-present dust of the dorm.

He swallowed. His throat was dry.

A dream. A waking hallucination. Fatigue. He ran through the possibilities, each one landing with the quiet click of a lock engaging. He’d been up late three nights in a row, chasing references for his thesis. The human brain did strange things under sleep deprivation. He’d read the studies.

He reached for the notebook again, then stopped. His gaze snagged on the corner of the page, where a single drop of ink had bled into the paper. It spread in uneven tendrils, like roots seeking water. He watched it, motionless, until the ink dried.

Wind picked up outside, rustling the dead leaves clinging to the oak beyond his window. Its sound was brittle, a whisper of something ending. Cassius turned the page.

He started a new list:

1. Documented physiological effects of sleep deprivation
2. Hypnagogic hallucinations (frequency, duration)
3.

His handwriting was steadier now, the letters uniform. The radiator ticked. The room settled around him, solid and real.

A knock at the door.

Cassius didn’t look up. “It’s open.”

The door creaked. Miller leaned against the frame, a grease-stained paper bag in one hand. “You missed dinner.”

Cassius kept writing. “I’m not hungry.”

Miller tossed the bag onto the desk. It landed with a soft thump, the scent of fries and overcooked beef escaping through the folds. “Eat anyway. You’re gonna pass out in the library again, and I’m not dragging your ass back this time.”

Cassius glanced at the bag. The grease had already started to seep through, leaving translucent spots on the brown paper. He could picture the burger inside, soggy bun, limp lettuce, the cheese congealing into a single rubbery sheet. His stomach turned.

“Thanks,” he said, and pushed the bag aside.

Miller lingered, arms crossed. He studied Cassius’s notes, his gaze skipping over the dense blocks of text. “You’re really hung up on this reincarnation thing, huh?”

Cassius tapped his pen against the notebook. Once. Twice. “It’s research.”

“For what? Your thesis or your existential crisis?”

“Both.”

Miller snorted. He pushed off the doorframe and wandered over to the bookshelf, running a finger along the spines. “You know what your problem is? You think too much.”

“That’s the point of grad school.”

Cassius watched him. Miller had a way of filling space without trying, his presence loud even when he wasn’t speaking. He was the only person Cassius knew who could make reading look like an athletic event.

“You don’t believe in any of this,” Cassius said.

Miller shrugged. “I believe in beer. And pizza. And not losing my mind over shit nobody can prove.” He snapped the book shut and shoved it back onto the shelf. “Come on. Let’s go to the Rat. You need a drink.”

Cassius looked down at his notes. The words swam slightly, the ink blurring at the edges. He blinked, and they sharpened again.

“I have work to do.”

“You always have work to do.” Miller grabbed his jacket off the bed and tossed it at him. “One beer. Then you can come back and spiral in peace.”

Cassius caught the jacket. The fabric was cold, smelling faintly of cigarette smoke and autumn air. He hesitated.

The radiator snapped. The scent of salt was gone, if it had ever been there at all.

He stood.

“One beer,” he said.

Miller grinned. “That’s the spirit.”

Cassius shoved his notebook into his bag, careful to avoid smudging the ink. His left hand trembled as he zipped it shut. He curled his fingers into a fist, then released them.

The tremor faded.

He followed Miller out, turning off the lamp as he went. The room plunged into darkness, the desk a vague shape in the gloom. The notebook lay hidden in his bag, its pages filled with questions that had no answers.

Outside, the air was crisp, the sky clear. The path to the bar glittered under the streetlights, wet from the earlier rain. Cassius kept his hands in his pockets, his fingers brushing against the smooth, cool surface of a marble he didn’t remember putting there.

He didn’t take it out.

Some things were better left unexamined.

The Rat was loud, sticky, and smelled like spilled beer and fryer grease. Cassius nursed his pint while Miller held court with a group of undergrads, his laugh booming over the jukebox. The noise should have been comforting—just another Thursday night, but it grated against Cassius’s nerves like sandpaper.

He rubbed his left temple. The beer wasn’t helping. The first sip had tasted off metallic, like licking a battery. He set the glass down and wiped his palm on his jeans.

Miller leaned over, shouting to be heard. “You look like someone pissed in your cereal.”

“I’m fine.”

“Bullshit.” Miller shoved a shot glass across the table. “Drink this. It’ll fix your face.”

The liquid inside was clear, with a faint blue tint. Cassius didn’t touch it. “What is it?”

“Medicine.”

Cassius pushed it back. “I’m good.”

Miller rolled his eyes and downed the shot himself. “Your loss.”

The music shifted, something with too much bass, the vibrations thumping against Cassius’s ribs. He stood abruptly, the chair legs scraping against the floor. “Bathroom.”

Miller waved him off, already turning back to the undergrads.

The hallway to the bathrooms was narrow, the walls sticky with decades of accumulated grime. Cassius splashed cold water on his face, the shock of it sharp against his skin. The mirror above the sink was cracked, his reflection bisected by a jagged line. He stared at the left half—his own eye, bloodshot and tired, then the right.

For a fraction of a second, the reflection didn’t match.

The right eye was darker, the skin around it lined, a thin scar cutting through the eyebrow. Cassius blinked. The image resolved back into symmetry.

He gripped the edge of the sink. The porcelain was cold, unyielding.

A glitch. A trick of the light. The crack in the mirror distorting his features. He ran through the explanations, each one landing with less conviction than the last.

Back at the table, Miller was arm-wrestling an undergrad. Cassius grabbed his jacket. “I’m heading out.”

Miller didn’t look up. “You’re a buzzkill, Ashbourne.”

“Yeah.”

The night air was a relief, crisp and clean. Cassius walked fast, his breath fogging in front of him. The streetlights cast long shadows, the pavement still damp from the rain. He kept his hands in his pockets, his fingers finding the marble again. Smooth. Cool. Unaccountably there.

His dorm was quiet when he got back. The hallway smelled like microwave popcorn and stale laundry. He fumbled with his keys, the lock sticking before finally giving way.

The room was exactly as he’d left it, desk lamp off, bed neatly made, the radiator hissing softly. Cassius dropped his bag by the door and headed straight for the bathroom.

The dorm bathroom was worse than the Rat’s. Flickering fluorescent light, tiles stained with rust, a mirror fogged with decades of mineral deposits. Cassius turned the tap, the water rushing loud in the quiet. He cupped his hands under the stream, the cold biting his skin.

When he looked up, his reflection was waiting.

Him. Just him. Same tired eyes, same unremarkable face. He breathed out, the tension in his shoulders easing slightly.

Then the light flickered.

Not a flicker, a pulse. A slow, deliberate dimming, like a bulb about to die. The reflection wavered. Not his face. Older. Thinner. The scar through the eyebrow, faint but obvious. The eyes were different, too, darker, harder, the kind of eyes that had seen things Cassius hadn’t.

He stumbled back, his hip hitting the sink. The image held for a heartbeat, two, then snapped back to normal.

Cassius’s breath came fast and shallow. His hands shook. He pressed them flat against the sink, the cold porcelain grounding him.

“No.”

The word hung in the air, too loud in the small room.

He leaned closer, his nose almost touching the glass. His reflection mimicked him perfectly. No scar. No age. Just Cassius, pale and wide-eyed.

The fluorescent light hummed, steady now. The radiator clanked in the other room. Normal sounds. Real sounds.

He turned off the tap. The silence was worse.

Back in his room, Cassius sat on the edge of the bed, his hands clasped between his knees. The notebook in his bag called to him, but he didn’t move. He stared at the wall instead, at the faint crack running from ceiling to floor, like a fault line.

He could rationalize it. Fatigue. Stress. A trick of the light. But the certainty in his gut—cold and heavy, wouldn’t budge.

Something was wrong.

Not with him. With the world.

The radiator snapped through clenched teeth. Cassius didn’t sleep.

When dawn finally crept through the window, pale and tentative, he was still sitting there, his fingers curled around the marble in his pocket.

Some things, once seen couldn’t be unseen.
