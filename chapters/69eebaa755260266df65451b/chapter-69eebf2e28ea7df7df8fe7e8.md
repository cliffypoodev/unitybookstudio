She didn’t sleep. The cold on her cheek stayed long after the weight on the bed had faded, a phantom sunburn in reverse. Dawn came, a gray smear through the blinds. She got up made coffee, and the routine of it—the scoop of grounds, the click of the machine, the slow drip—felt like a lie she was telling the apartment. A performance of normalcy for an audience of empty rooms. The scent of brewing coffee couldn’t touch the other smell, the one that had lingered in the air beside her pillow. Old paper. Cold stone. In her sinuses now, a permanent background note.

She spent the morning cleaning again. She scrubbed the bathroom tile grout with a toothbrush. She organized her books not by author but by color, creating a meaningless gradient of spines on the shelf. Busywork for her hands, a dam against the static in her head. But the dam was porous. Images leaked through, not as full visions, but as sense-memories without context.

The warmth of sun on bare shoulders. Not the weak, filtered light through her apartment window, but a direct, heavy heat. The grit of sand underfoot. The taste of something sweet and tangy on her tongue—pineapple maybe. A laugh, low and warm cut off by the sound of surf.

She stopped, the toothbrush hovering over a mildew spot. Her own breathing was loud in the small room. These weren’t frightening. That was the problem. The cold breath on her cheek had been terror, pure and simple. This was… nostalgia. A deep, aching homesickness for a place she had never been.

“Stop it,” she said aloud. Her voice was flat, absorbed by the tiles.

The afternoon bled away. She tried to read, but the words swam. She’d get to the end of a paragraph and have no idea what it said. Her mind was elsewhere, tracing the contour of a memory that wasn’t hers. A memory of a face.

She didn’t know her name. Not yet. But she knew the slope of her nose, the way one eyebrow arched slightly higher than the other when she was skeptical. She knew she had a faint dusting of freckles across her cheekbones that only appeared in summer. She knew the exact shade of her eyes—not blue, not green, but the color of sea glass tumbled smooth.

This knowing arrived not as a revelation, but as a stubborn, pre-existing fact. Like remembering her own birthday.

Madness. She knew it was madness. She gripped the arms of her chair, the fabric rough under her palms. A real thing. She was here, in this chair, in this apartment that smelled of cleaning products and dust. Arthur Brindle died here. That was the haunting. Not some sun-drenched fantasy.

But the fantasy had weight. It had texture. It had the smell of salt and sunscreen.

Frustration boiled over. She shoved herself out of the chair and paced to the window. The street below was quiet, a Tuesday afternoon lull. A woman walked a small, prancing dog. A delivery truck idled double-parked. Normal life, carrying on in its normal channel. She was the one receiving interference.

She needed to see it. To make it external. If it was outside of her, she could maybe look at it, analyze it, prove it was just a construction.

She didn’t own any art supplies. She found a blank page in the back of her notebook and a cheap ballpoint pen. She sat at the kitchen table, the bare bulb overhead casting a hard circle of light. She clicked the pen. Hesitated.

This was giving in. This was what crazy people did—they drew the faces of the voices in their heads.

But the alternative was letting it eat a hole in her sanity from the inside.

She put the tip of the pen to the paper. She didn’t sketch. She didn’t plan. Her hand moved, guided by a muscle memory that belonged to a different set of hands. A confident, flowing line for the jaw. A lighter, tentative one for the curve of a smile. She drew the eyes closed, with long lashes fanning downward, because she couldn’t get the sea-glass color right with blue ink, but she knew the shape of the lid, the delicate crease.

She worked in silence, the only sound the scratch of the pen. She didn’t breathe. She was a conduit. The face emerged from the white space not as an invention, but as a recollection. She knew the exact point where her hairline met her temple. She knew the small, almost imperceptible scar through her left eyebrow.

When she finally stopped, her hand was cramping. She leaned back.

A woman looked up from the page. She was sleeping, or resting. Peaceful. She was beautiful, but in a specific, ordinary way. The kind of face you’d see laughing in a grocery store aisle. Real.

A cold nausea, entirely separate from the haunting washed through her. She didn’t recognize her. And she knew her completely.

She shoved away from the table, the chair legs shrieking on the linoleum. She couldn’t look at it. She left the notebook open, the page exposed, and walked to the sink. She ran the water cold, splashed her face. One drop of water dripped from her chin onto her shirt.

When she turned, the drawing was still there. It hadn’t vanished. It was just a drawing. Ink on paper. Her doing.

She approached slowly, as if it were a sleeping animal. She stared at the closed eyes. Who are you?

A name surfaced, not with a bang, but with the soft certainty of a bubble reaching the air. Lilith.

She didn’t know where it came from. It just was. Lilith.

The sound of it in her mind unlocked something. A floodgate of sense-memory, not a vision, but a barrage. The smell of her shampoo—coconut and something herbal. The feel of her calloused fingertips against her palm. The sound of her humming off-key in the kitchen, a song she didn’t know. The weight of her head on her shoulder while they watched a movie, the credits scrolling, forgotten.

It was a life. A dense, detailed, ordinary life. Sunday mornings. Arguments about whose turn it was to do the dishes. The shared, wordless glance across a crowded room.

She gripped the edge of the table. Her knees threatened to buckle. This wasn’t terror. This was grief. A profound, devastating sense of loss, as if someone had died. But no one had died. Because no one had existed.

The contradiction was a physical pain, a crack running down the center of her chest. She mourned a ghost who had never lived. She loved a stranger.

The scent of salt and sunscreen grew stronger, overwhelming the apartment’s stale air. For a few heartbeats, it was all she could smell. She could almost feel the sun-warmed wood of a deck under her bare feet, hear the distant cry of gulls.

Then it faded. The familiar chill seeped back in, the dry paper scent of the other presence, the one that shared her bed. It felt intrusive now. Jealous, almost.

She looked from the drawing of Lilith’s peaceful face to the dark hallway leading to her bedroom. Two different hauntings. One of cold intimacy, one of warm absence.

She carefully tore the page from the notebook. The paper made a soft, final sound. She didn’t know what to do with it. Crumpling it felt like a violence. Burying it in a drawer felt like a burial.

In the end, she propped it against the base of her reading lamp on the side table. She turned the lamp on. The warm yellow light fell over the drawing, making the ink lines seem softer, more alive.

She sat in her chair across the room and watched the piece of paper, waiting, though she didn’t know for what. For the lines to change. For the eyes to open. For the scent of coconut to return.

Outside, a car door slammed. The ordinary world. In here, she kept vigil for a ghost of sunlight, while the ghost of cold stone watched from the shadows of the hall.

The need didn’t go away. It grew. The ballpoint sketch wasn’t enough. It was a shadow of her, a pale carbon copy. She needed color. She needed to capture the freckles, the exact hue of her hair—dark brown with strands of gold bleached by the sun.

Two days after the drawing, she found herself in an art supply store downtown. The fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. The smell of turpentine and paper was alien and sharp. She moved through the aisles like a sleepwalker, filling a small basket with things she didn’t know how to use: a pad of watercolor paper, a set of paints in little tubes, a few brushes with soft bristles. The cost was absurd. She paid in cash, avoiding the clerk’s eyes.

Back in her apartment, she cleared the kitchen table again. She filled a jelly jar with water. She squeezed blobs of paint onto an old china plate—burnt sienna, yellow ochre, a green she hoped might approximate sea-glass.

She stared at the blank, textured paper. One memory of Lilith’s face was already softening at the edges, becoming a feeling more than a photograph. She had to hurry. She had to pin it down before it faded for good.

She wet a brush. She mixed a color for her skin. Her hand, which had been so sure with the pen, shook. The first brushstroke was a clumsy wash, too dark, too orange. She cursed, a harsh sound in the quiet. She was ruining it.

She closed her eyes. Not thinking. Just feeling. The warmth of her cheek under her lips. The way the light caught the fine hairs on her forearm in the afternoon.

She opened her eyes. She didn’t paint the face. She painted the light around it. A wash of gold, fading to the soft blue of a sky just before dusk. She painted the suggestion of a shoulder, the curve of a neck. She left the face itself blank, an empty space in the center of all that warmth.

Better. The absence was more accurate. It was a silhouette burned into her senses, a negative space where a life should be.

She sat back, exhausted. The painting was a mess. Amateurish. But the feeling was there, trapped in the runny pigments. Longing.

From the hallway, the floorboard creaked. A slow, deliberate compression of wood.

She didn’t turn. She kept her eyes on the empty space where Lilith’s face should have been.

“I know you’re there,” she said, her voice hoarse from disuse.

The silence that followed was different. It wasn’t listening. Waiting.

“She’s not yours,” she whispered to the empty space on the paper.

In the hallway, the cold intensified. A draft curled around her ankles. The scent of old paper was suddenly overpowering, blotting out the faint chemical smell of the paint.

It felt like a reply.

She didn’t paint again. The watercolor sat on the table for a day, the paper buckling as it dried, the blank space at its center an accusation. She covered it with a dish towel. The act felt like hiding a body.

But the memory of Lilith didn’t fade. It crystallized. The phantom senses sharpened, becoming more intrusive, more specific. She’d be washing a mug and feel the phantom slickness of sunscreen on her palms. The taste of salt would bloom on her lips when she drank tap water. She found herself staring at her own hands, expecting to see grains of sand caught in the hairs on her knuckles.

The sketch from the notebook, propped by the lamp was a constant peripheral presence. A sleeping anchor. She started talking to it. Not full conversations—just a word, here and there.

“Morning,” she’d mutter, passing by to get coffee.

Or, “Rain today.”

It was the kind of thing you’d say to a spouse. Domestic, mundane. The horror of it was in the normalcy. The haunting had moved in, taken a chair, and was waiting for the paper to be delivered.

On the third evening, the compulsion returned. The sketch was black and white. It lacked dimension. She needed to see her in three dimensions, to map the planes of her face with something more tactile than ink. She didn’t question the urge. It was a need, as basic as thirst.

She didn’t have clay. She had leftover plumbing putty from a long-forgotten sink repair, a gray, oily compound sealed in a foil pouch in a kitchen drawer. It would have to do.

She worked at the kitchen table under the single bulb, the dish towel now a work mat. The putty was cold and resistant at first, stiff under her fingers. She kneaded it, the heat from her hands making it pliable. The chemical, petroleum smell mixed with the ever-present paper-dust chill from the hall.

She didn’t think. She let her fingers remember.

They remembered the high, smooth arc of a forehead. The subtle dip at the philtrum above the lips. The strong line of a jaw that softened just at the chin. Her thumbs found the sockets for eyes, hollowing out two gentle, almond-shaped spaces. She used the rounded end of a pen to press a delicate line for a closed lid, to carve the subtle channel of the upper lip.

She worked for hours. Time dissolved into the rhythm of push and pull, of adding a tiny bit of material here, scraping away a fraction there. The world narrowed to the gray mass taking form under her stained fingers and the precise, internal blueprint she was tracing. It wasn’t creation. Excavation. She was uncovering a shape that already existed, buried in the medium of her own muscle memory.

Sweat beaded on her temple. Her back ached from hunching. She didn’t stop.

When she finally leaned back, her hands were trembling, coated in a fine gray film. On the dish towel sat a face.

It was her. Not a likeness. Not an interpretation. It was the woman from the sketch, rendered in three solemn dimensions. The quiet repose of the features, the slight, knowing tilt at the corner of the mouth that wasn’t quite a smile. It was all there. She had sculpted a stranger’s face with the unthinking accuracy of a woman sculpting her own.

The fascination that had carried her through the work curdled, all at once, into a pure and simple dread.

She hadn’t imagined this. Her hands had known things her mind did not. The proof was physical, sitting twelve inches from her face, smelling of mineral oil and her own sweat. This wasn’t a symptom. It was evidence. Evidence of a life lived in a body that wasn’t hers, in a time that wasn’t now.

She reached out, her finger hovering an inch from the sculpted cheek. She couldn’t bring herself to touch it. The idea of feeling that familiar curve under her fingertip, in cold putty instead of warm skin was a violation of some deep, unspoken law.

“Who are you?” The question was a dry rasp.

The apartment offered no answer. Its hallway was a pool of black silence. The chill there seemed to be holding its breath.

A strange, reluctant acceptance settled over her, heavy as a lead blanket. This was her life now. Not a haunting to be solved, but a reality to be cataloged. She had a ghost of cold stone and old books who shared her bed. And she had a ghost of sunlight and salt who lived in her hands. They were both real. They were both hers.

She got up, her joints protesting. She fetched a clean glass from the cupboard, filled it with water, and drank it slowly, staring at the sculpture from across the room. It looked smaller from a distance. More fragile.

She should destroy it. That’s what a sane person would do. Burn the sketch, smash the putty face back into a formless lump, purge the foreign memory from her environment.

She didn’t move.

Destroying it would be a murder. A second death, for a life that had never been. She couldn’t do it. The grief would be too large. It would fill the apartment and drown her.

So she would keep it. She would live with it. This was the compromise, the reluctant acceptance. She would share her space with these two silent tenants, these echoes of lives that pressed against her own from opposite sides of an impossible barrier.

She washed her hands at the sink, the gray paste swirling down the drain. The water was hot, but she couldn’t get the feeling of the putty off her skin. In the creases of her knuckles, under her nails.

She dried her hands on a towel. The silence in the apartment was absolute. No traffic outside, no hum from the refrigerator. It was the dead hour, deep in the night.

She was about to turn, to face the sculpture again, to decide what to do with it, when she heard it.

A faint, melodic thread of sound. A woman’s voice, humming.

Coming from nowhere and everywhere. Not from the hallway. Not from the sketch. It seemed to emanate from the air itself, a vibration in the stillness. The tune was unfamiliar, a meandering, wistful series of notes that rose and fell without a clear rhythm. Off-key in one spot, just slightly flat.

She knew that hum. She knew the way it caught on that particular note. She’d heard it in a sun-drenched kitchen, accompanied by the clatter of dishes. She’d felt the vibration of it against her shoulder where Lilith’s head rested.

Lilith’s hum. The one she did when she was thinking, or content, or just existing in a moment.

Her breath locked in her chest. The certainty was absolute, a cold spike driven straight through her heart. That sound belonged to the face on the table. It was her voice, bleeding through from a beach that didn’t exist, in a time that had never happened.

She didn’t move. She didn’t dare settle. She just stood at the sink, her damp hands clenched on the edge of the counter, and listened to the ghost of a woman she loved humming in the dark of her apartment.

The melody looped once, softly, then faded into the returning silence, leaving only the echo of its shape in her mind, and the deeper silence of the cold watching from the hall.

The hum didn’t come back. She waited at the sink until her knuckles ached from gripping the counter, until the silence re-established itself as a solid thing, thicker than before. The chill from the hallway remained, a watchful presence. She turned slowly. The sculpture sat on the dish towel, a gray moon in the lamplight. The sketch by the reading lamp seemed to glow.

She couldn’t leave her like that. Propped against a lamp base. A scrap. She deserved a frame.

The thought was absurd, and she clung to its absurdity. It was a concrete task. A problem with steps. It was a dam against the ocean of grief threatening to break behind her ribs. She found a tape measure in a junk drawer. She noted the dimensions of the sketch. She wrote them on the back of an envelope. The numbers were a lifeline.

The next day, she went to a discount store on the outskirts of the city. The fluorescent lights were a different frequency than the art store’s, harsher, buzzing with a headache-inducing pitch. She walked past aisles of plastic bins and cheap cookware. The frame section was at the back, a wire rack of mass-produced options. Gold, black, fake wood grain. She ran her fingers over a simple black wooden frame. Cool and smooth. She checked the size. It would fit, with a little room to spare. She didn’t consider the other options. Black felt right. Solemn. Final.

At home, she cleared the kitchen table once more. She laid the frame face down, pried up the little metal tabs with a butter knife. She removed the cardboard backing and the sheet of thin, cloudy plastic that came with it. She placed her sketch in the center. The drawing looked smaller now isolated in the white matting that wasn’t really a mat, just the cardboard showing around the edges. She hesitated. Should she center it? It was already centered. Her hands were shaking. She fitted the backing in, pressed the tabs back down with more force than necessary. The metal squealed in protest.

She turned the frame over.

Lilith, behind glass.

She was sealed in now. Contained. A specimen. The glass created a distance, a barrier she could feel. She could no longer imagine the texture of the paper, the slight raise of the ink lines. Lilith was untouchable. That, she realized was the point. She couldn’t reach her. She never could.

She carried the frame to the living room. She didn’t have much art on the walls. A generic print of a boat in a harbor left by a previous tenant. She took it down, the nail left exposed in the plaster. She hung the black frame in its place. Too high. She adjusted it, her fingers leaving smudges on the glass. She stepped back.

There. On the wall. A part of the apartment’s landscape.

The grief hit her then, not as a wave, but as a settling. A quiet, total collapse of something internal. It wasn’t the sharp pain of loss. It was the hollow, endless ache of aftermath. She sank into her armchair, her eyes fixed on the framed sketch. She had given a ghost a room in her home. She had made space for her. She wasn’t fighting it anymore.

These weren’t hauntings. They were obituaries.

Every phantom sense, every bleed-through memory was a life she had lived somewhere else. A life that had been real, with its own weight and texture and smell, and was now gone. Not just gone—erased, overwritten canceled by the fact of her existence here, in this chair, in this wrong and silent apartment. She wasn’t being haunted. She was the cemetery. She was the ground where other versions of herself were buried.

The cold presence in the hallway was another grave. A different life, one of stone and silence and old paper. That version of her had loved, or needed, or been bound to that cold thing. And it had died with her. Now it lingered here, a fossil in the wrong stratum.

She thought of the marble in her coat pocket, the blue ribbon swirl. An artifact that didn’t belong anywhere else. That was her. An artifact. A leftover.

Tears didn’t come. Her eyes were dry and hot. The grief was too large for tears. It was a geological fact. A mountain range of loss inside her range after range of lives she could remember but had never lived. She mourned for Lilith, for the feel of her skin in the sun. She mourned for the cold presence, for whatever bleak companionship it represented. She mourned for a hundred other whispers she hadn’t even identified yet, ghosts waiting in the wings of her consciousness.

She would never be believed. That was the final, absolute truth. She could show them the sketch, the sculpture, she could describe the taste of the sea salt and the smell of the old books until she was blue in the face. They would see a talented, troubled woman. They would see delusion given form. The proof was only proof to her. The validation had to come from inside this sealed, silent chamber of her own skull.

It wasn’t solid. It wasn’t immutable. It was a broadcast. And her receiver was the only one picking up the signal drift.
