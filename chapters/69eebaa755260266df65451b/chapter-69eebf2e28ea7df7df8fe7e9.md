The pain woke him.

It wasn’t a thought, not an awareness. It was a raw, cellular signal. His skin screamed. Cassius opened his eyes to the gray morning light of his bedroom. He was lying on his back. The ceiling was the same. The radiator hissed the same low, steady note. Everything was the same except his body.

He moved his arm. The sheet against his forearm wasn’t fabric; it was sandpaper. He hissed, a short, sharp intake of breath. He looked at his arm. The skin was pink. Not pale, not the usual winter pallor of a man who hadn’t seen proper sunlight in months. It was a vivid, angry pink, blistered along the ridge of his forearm where it had pressed against the mattress. He sat up. The movement pulled the skin across his shoulders. A hot, tight ache bloomed there, deep under the surface.

He stood. The floorboards were cold under his feet. He walked to the bathroom, his steps careful, each shift of his torso stretching the burn. The bathroom light was unforgiving. He looked at himself in the mirror.

His face was red. Not flushed. Burned. The redness followed the exact pattern of sunlight exposure—high across his forehead and cheeks, skipping the shadowed hollows under his eyes and chin. His neck was red down to the collar line. He turned, craning to see his shoulders in the reflection. They were worse. The skin was a deeper red, almost purple at the peaks, with a faint, shiny texture like cooked meat.

He hadn’t been outside.

He ran the week in his head. Monday: grocery delivery, unpacked boxes in the kitchen. Tuesday: attempted to read gave up, watched a documentary about deep-sea vents. Wednesday: laundry, the hum of the machine a constant drone. Thursday: the cold spot in the hallway lingered for two hours, he’d measured it with a thermometer. Friday: he’d cleaned the kitchen tiles, scrubbing at a phantom stain that wasn’t there. Saturday: he’d slept late, woke to the smell of ozone and old paper. Sunday: today.

No sunlight. No park walks. No open windows. The weather had been overcast, a solid ceiling of gray since Tuesday. He’d lived under electric light and the dim glow through blinds.

He touched his cheek. The skin was hot, dry, taut. It felt exactly like a sunburn. One memory of sunburn was clear—the summer he was fifteen, a day at the lake with Miller, his shoulders fried to the point of peeling. This was that. But it wasn’t.

He went back to the bedroom. He pulled his shirt off over his head, the fabric dragging across his chest like a rake. He looked at his torso. The burn was there too, a lighter pink across his chest and stomach, but definite. It had a shape. It wasn’t uniform. Patterned, as if he’d been lying under a sun with partial shade. His left side was more burned than his right.

He sat on the edge of the bed. The pain was a constant now, a background hum louder than the radiator. He tried to think of explanations. Some kind of rash. A reaction to laundry detergent. He’d used the same detergent for years. A fever. He didn’t feel feverish, just burned. A dream so vivid it etched itself onto his skin. That was the territory he was in now, the territory where dreams could do that.

He needed to see a doctor.

The thought was flat, practical. A man with a sunburn in a sunless week needed a medical opinion. It was the next logical step. He dressed, choosing a loose, soft shirt that still felt like burlap as he pulled it on. He avoided looking at the framed sketch of Lilith on the living room wall as he passed. Her face was a different kind of burn.

The clinic was a ten-minute drive. He kept the heater off. The cool air of the car was a relief against his face. He watched the streets. The sky was still gray, a dull wool blanket stretched tight over the city. No sun. No chance of sun.

The waiting room smelled of antiseptic and worn carpet. He filled out a form. Reason for visit: severe sunburn with no sun exposure. The nurse, a woman with tired eyes and a quick smile took the form without comment. She led him to an examination room. The room was small dominated by a padded table and a cabinet of supplies. The light was fluorescent, harsh. It made his burn look theatrical, like stage makeup.

The doctor entered five minutes later. A man in his fifties, with a careful demeanor and glasses that magnified his eyes. He introduced himself as Dr. Evans. He looked at Cassius’s face, then at the form.

“Sunburn,” he said.

“Yes.”

“No sun exposure this week?”

“None. I’ve been indoors. The weather’s been overcast.”

Dr. Evans nodded. He asked Cassius to remove his shirt. Cassius did, the movement stiff. The doctor examined his shoulders, his chest. He used a small light to look at the blisters on Cassius’s forearm. He asked about medications, new foods, changes in routine. Cassius gave the same answers. No changes. Nothing new.

“Any itching? Tingling before this appeared?”

“No. I woke up with it.”

The doctor pressed a cool finger against Cassius’s shoulder, testing the skin’s elasticity. He asked Cassius to lie back on the table. He examined Cassius’s eyes, his throat, listened to his heart with a stethoscope. The stethoscope disk was cold against his burned chest. Cassius watched the doctor’s face. He saw the professional curiosity, the cataloging of details. He didn’t see confusion. He didn’t see the dawning realization that this was impossible.

“It’s a classic sunburn pattern,” Dr. Evans said finally. He stepped back, folding his stethoscope into his pocket. “First-degree with some second-degree areas on the shoulders. But without exposure...” He trailed off. He looked at Cassius again, a longer look. “Have you been using any new skincare products? A cream that might have a photosensitizing agent?”

“No.”

“A tanning bed? Even a brief visit?”

The doctor picked up the form again. He tapped the edge against his thumb. “There are rare conditions. Photosensitivity disorders. But they usually present with rash, itching, before the burn manifests. And they require some light source, even artificial.” He looked at the fluorescent ceiling light. “This wouldn’t do it.”

Cassius waited. He felt the hope leaving him, a quiet drain. He wanted the doctor to find a cause. A medical cause, a name, a syndrome he could research. He wanted a box to put this in.

“I could run some bloodwork,” Dr. Evans said. “Check for autoimmune markers. But honestly, Mr. Ashbourne, this presentation without a clear trigger...” He shook his head. “The most likely explanation is that you did have sun exposure. Perhaps you forgot. A walk you didn’t recall, a window left open with strong sunlight.”

“I didn’t forget.”

The doctor’s smile was gentle, professional. “Memory is tricky. Especially with routine. You might have stepped outside to check the mail, stayed a few minutes in a spot of sun you didn’t notice.”

“I didn’t.”

“The body doesn’t lie about a burn like this. It needs UV light.” Dr. Evans wrote something on the form. “I’ll prescribe a topical cream for the pain and to prevent infection. Keep the skin moisturized. Avoid any light source, even indoor lights, for a few days. If it worsens or you develop fever, come back.” He handed Cassius a printed prescription sheet. “The bloodwork is optional. I can order it if you’d like.”

Cassius took the sheet. The paper felt thin, insignificant. “What if the bloodwork shows nothing?”

“Then we’re back to unexplained photosensitivity. Which is rare, but not unheard of.” The doctor’s tone was closing the door. The visit was ending. “The body can do strange things.”

Cassius put his shirt back on. The fabric was agony. He thanked the doctor, an automatic politeness. He walked out of the room, out of the clinic, back into the gray day.

The drive home was slower. He didn’t turn on the radio. The silence in the car was full of his skin’s protest. He parked outside his building. He sat for a minute, watching a pigeon pick at something on the sidewalk. The pigeon was gray, the same color as the sky, the same color as the concrete. Everything was gray except him.

He went inside. He climbed the stairs. Each step jostled his shoulders. He entered his apartment. The familiar space felt different now. It wasn’t a place haunted by sounds or cold spots. It was a place that could rewrite his body. He stood in the living room, looking at the walls, the furniture, the framed sketch.

The doctor’s words hung in the air. The body doesn’t lie.

But his body was telling a story of sunlight that hadn’t happened here. It was wearing the evidence of a day he hadn’t lived. This wasn’t a memory, a sound, a smell. This was flesh. This was pain you could measure with a thermometer, treat with a cream. This was clear.

He walked to the bathroom again. He looked in the mirror. The red face looked back. It wasn’t his face. It was the face of a man who had spent a day on a beach, or a lawn, or a patio somewhere else. A man who had felt sun on his skin. That man had gone to bed in a different bed, in a different room, under a different sky. And he had woken up here, in this bathroom, wearing Cassius’s clothes, Cassius’s life, but carrying his own sunburn.

The burn wasn’t a haunting. It was a transplant.

Cassius leaned close to the mirror. He looked at his own eyes, the familiar eyes in the unfamiliar, burned face. The horror wasn’t that something was invading him. The horror was that he was the invasion. This body, this skin was contested territory. Another life had staked a claim. It had planted its flag in his flesh.

He thought of Lilith’s sketch on the wall. He thought of the cold presence in the hallway. They weren’t ghosts. They were neighbors. They lived in adjacent apartments, and the walls between them were thin. Sometimes their music leaked through. Sometimes their heat leaked through. This was their heat. A sunburn from a window he’d never sat by.

He took the prescription cream from his pocket. He opened the tube. The cream was white, cool. He applied it to his shoulders. The relief was immediate, a dulling of the sharpest pain. He worked the cream into his skin, the motion methodical. He was treating a wound from another world, the nurse for a casualty of a war he couldn’t see.

When he finished, he washed his hands. The water was cold. He dried them on a towel. He looked at his red face in the mirror one more time.

The body doesn’t lie.

His body was telling a truth he couldn’t hear, a truth from a different channel. The burn was the signal, strong enough now to burn through the static and etch itself onto his receiver.

He needed a second opinion. That was the next logical step wasn’t it? When one expert failed, you found another. He drove across town to an urgent care facility, a bright, plastic-looking place with a digital check-in kiosk. The process was the same. The form. The waiting room with a television playing a morning show. A different nurse, younger, with a tattoo of a butterfly on her wrist. She took his vitals. Her fingers were warm. She didn’t comment on the burn.

The physician’s assistant who saw him was brisk, efficient. She examined the burn with a small, pen-like light. She asked almost the same questions. He gave almost the same answers.

“Any history of psychiatric conditions?” she asked, not looking up from her tablet.

The question landed like a stone in still water. “No.”

“Night terrors? Sleepwalking?”

“Because this,” she said, gesturing with her light pen at his shoulder, “this is UV damage. If you’re certain you weren’t exposed, we have to consider parasomnias. You could have gone outside in your sleep. It happens more than people think.”

“I don’t sleepwalk.”

“People who sleepwalk rarely remember.” She tapped on her tablet. “I can refer you to a sleep specialist. And a dermatologist for the photosensitivity workup, though Dr. Evans’s plan is appropriate.” She offered a thin smile. “In the meantime maybe consider installing a latch high up on your door. A simple precaution.”

He left with a referral slip and a brochure about sleep hygiene. The brochure had a picture of a person sleeping peacefully under a tidy blanket. He threw it in a trash can in the parking lot.

The hollow inside him was filling now, not with fear, but with a solid, cold frustration. They were speaking a different language. They were talking about latches and sleep cycles and forgotten walks. He was talking about a theft that happened at the cellular level. He drove to a third clinic, a walk-in place near the university. The wait was two hours. He sat on a hard plastic chair, his skin throbbing in time with the fluorescent lights. A man across from him coughed wetly into his elbow.

This doctor was older, with a weary resignation in his posture. He listened to Cassius’s story, looked at the burn, and sighed. “I’ve seen this once before,” he said. He leaned back in his chair. The vinyl creaked. “Fella swore he was allergic to his own television. Got a burn on his neck, he said, from the light. Turns out he’d fallen asleep in his recliner, sun came through the window, baked him for three hours. He just didn’t remember the nap.” The doctor spread his hands. “The mind protects us from monotony. It deletes the boring bits. You probably sat by a window lost in thought. That sun moved. You got cooked. Your mind filed the whole afternoon under ‘nothing important’ and deleted it.”

“It’s been overcast for a week.”

“A break in the clouds. Five minutes is all it takes.” The doctor wrote something on a notepad. “I’m going to prescribe a mild sedative. Stress can manifest in physical ways. You’re clearly under a lot of it. The sedative might help you sleep, break the cycle of anxiety that’s making you fixate on this.”

Cassius didn’t argue. He took the new prescription, a white square of paper, and folded it next to the first one. The two papers made a stiff little packet in his pocket. He walked out into the late afternoon. The gray sky had darkened to the color of wet slate. A few cold drops of rain hit his forehead, a shock against the heated skin.

He stood beside his car, keys in hand. The frustration was a hard knot under his sternum. It was the wrong feeling. Too small. He should be terrified. He was. But the terror was buried under this thick, stupid anger at their reasonable, wrong answers. They were trying to solve a puzzle with the pieces from a different box. Their pieces made a sensible picture: a forgetful man, a sleeping man, a stressed man. His pieces showed a fissure in the world.

A gust of wind blew down the street, carrying the smell of wet pavement and exhaust. And something else.

For a second, it wasn’t exhaust. Salt. Sharp, marine salt, mixed with the coppery tang of blood. Iron. The scent was so vivid it coated the back of his throat. He could taste it. He turned his face into the wind, searching for its source—a fish market, a broken hydrant, anything. This street was just a street. A bus snapped to a stop at the corner. The smell vanished, replaced by diesel.

But it left a ghost behind. The scent was now on him. It clung to his skin, underneath the clinical smell of the antiseptic soap from the clinics. Salt and iron. In the burn itself, a memory trapped in the damaged cells.

He got in the car. He didn’t drive. He sat with the heater off, the cold seeping in. He brought his wrist to his nose and inhaled.

Cream. Cotton. The faint, clean smell of his own sweat. Nothing else.

He leaned his head back against the seat. The roof of the car was stained with old water spots. They formed a shape like a wide continent. He stared at it. His mind, unmoored, drifted.

The gulls were screaming. Not the faint, distant cries of the gulls at the city dump, but a loud, ragged chorus right overhead. The sun was a physical weight, a hammer on the back of his neck and shoulders. Too bright. The world was washed in white glare. He was squinting, his eyes watering. He was holding a fishing rod. The line was taut, thrumming. Something heavy and angry fought on the other end. The wood of the rod was smooth, worn shiny in the grip. His hands were different. Tanned, leathery, with a thick white scar across the knuckle of his right thumb. Salt spray stung his lips. He could feel the rough, sun-bleached planks of a dock under his feet, the gentle, oily roll of the water beneath it. The smell was overwhelming: dead fish, gasoline, brine, and the hot metallic scent of his own sunburned skin, already beginning to tighten and pulse with heat. A voice, rough and close, yelled, “Don’t horse it! Let him run!”

Cassius jerked forward, his forehead almost hitting the steering wheel. The car was silent. The continent of water stains was just stains. His hands on the wheel were his hands—pale, uncalloused, scarless. The fishing rod was gone. The phantom tension in his arms lingered for a second, a ghost ache.

He was breathing hard. His heart knocked against his ribs. This wasn’t a memory. It was an insertion. It wasn’t a feeling; it was a full sensory broadcast. Sight, sound, touch, smell, the specific texture of pain. It had the hyper-clarity the other flashes sometimes had, but this was different. It wasn’t a scene from a life he might have lived. It was a scene from a life someone else was living, right now, and the signal had bled over so powerfully it had pre-burned his skin in anticipation.

The salt and iron smell. It was the dock. The blood from a gutted fish. The scent had traveled with the memory, a stowaway.

He looked at his red hands on the wheel. They were not the hands from the dock. But they were burned as if they had been. The other man’s sun had reached across whatever boundary separated them and burned this Cassius instead. Or maybe it had burned them both. Maybe they were both sitting somewhere, in different cars on different streets, staring at their own hands wondering why they ached from a fight with a fish they never caught.

The terror arrived then, cold and final. It wasn’t a spike. It was a settling. It filled the hollow and dissolved the knot of frustration. This was beyond doctors. This was beyond forgetting. His body was no longer a closed system. It was a broadcast receiver, and it was picking up physical experiences from other stations. A sunburn today. What tomorrow? A broken leg from a fall he didn’t take? A knife wound from a fight in an alley he’d never walk down?

He started the car and drove home through the drizzle. The world outside the windshield looked thin, like a poorly projected image. The buildings had no weight. The people on the sidewalks moved like echoes.

Back in his apartment, the silence was a presence. He didn’t turn on the lights. He stood in the middle of the living room, the gray twilight seeping through the windows. He took the two prescriptions from his pocket. He looked at them, the neat printing, the official logos. He walked to the kitchen trash can and dropped them in. They fluttered down onto coffee grounds and an empty egg carton.

The cream from the first doctor was on the bathroom counter. He picked up the tube. Real. It would soothe the symptom. It would do nothing for the cause. He unscrewed the cap and squeezed a white worm of it onto his finger. The smell was medicinal, clean. It erased nothing.

He applied it to his face in the dark bathroom, guided by touch. The mirror was a black rectangle. He was glad he couldn’t see himself. The man from the dock had a face. Tanned, lined, squinted against the sun. Cassius could almost feel the shape of that other jaw under his fingertips, a phantom bone structure beneath his own.

When he finished, he washed his hands again. The water ran clear. He lifted his hands to his face, cupping them over his nose and mouth, and breathed in deeply.

There it was. Faint, almost gone, underneath the soap and cream.

Salt. And iron.

Proof. Not for anyone else. For him. The body doesn’t lie. His body was telling a truth that belonged to someone else, a truth that had arrived by a road he couldn’t map. He lowered his hands. The apartment waited, a shell holding a man who was becoming a chorus. Silence wasn’t silence anymore. It was the static between stations, and in it, he could almost hear the distant hungry scream of gulls.

He sat at his desk. The lamp cast a yellow circle on the wood, leaving the rest of the room in deep shadow. He opened a notebook, a fresh one. The first page was blank. He wrote the date at the top. Then he wrote: Event: Sunburn. Location: Shoulders, chest, face, forearms. Degree: 1st-2nd. Pain level: 7/10. No known exposure.

His pen hovered. What else? Associated sensory flash: fishing dock. Salt, iron, heat, gulls, fight with a fish. Duration: approximately 8-10 seconds. Physical residue: scent memory on skin for minutes after.

It looked insane on the page. A clinical log of a hallucination. But the burn wasn’t a hallucination. He touched his cheek. The skin was hot, tight. He wrote: Physical evidence is objective, measurable, and contradicts local environmental conditions.

He needed more. Proof for himself, if no one else. He stood went to the bathroom, and turned on the light. He flinched at the sudden glare, at the red man in the mirror. He took his small digital camera from a drawer—a relic bought for a vacation he never took. He switched it on. The battery icon blinked weakly. He aimed it at the mirror, framed his burned face and shoulders, and took a picture. The flash exploded in the small room, leaving purple afterimages. On the camera’s tiny screen, his face looked like a crime scene photo. Alien. He took another of his shoulder, the blisters on his forearm close-up. The clinical detail was grotesque. Satisfying.

He did not look away.
