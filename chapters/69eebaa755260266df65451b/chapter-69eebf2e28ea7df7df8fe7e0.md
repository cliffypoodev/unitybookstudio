The coffee tasted like burnt paper and regret. Cassius stared into the mug, watching the oily sheen on the surface catch the morning light. Thirty. The number sat in his head like a wrong note in a familiar song.

He’d made a list. A stupid, hopeful list he’d found in a desk drawer last week, scribbled on the back of a gas station receipt when he was twenty-two. By thirty: publish something, own a dog, learn guitar, see the ocean. The receipt was faded, the ink smudged. He’d done none of it. The dog was a landlord issue, the guitar was still in its case under the bed with two broken strings, the ocean was a thousand miles away, and the only thing he’d published was a letter to the editor of the Herald about potholes on Maple Street. They’d printed it, misspelling his name as “Cassidy.”

The radiator ticked. A bus groaned to a stop outside his building, the hydraulic sigh familiar as his own breathing. Normal. Everything was aggressively, boringly normal.

He took another sip of coffee. The burnt taste was gone, replaced by something else—something underneath it. A faint, dry sweetness. Like cedar. Like the inside of an old hope chest.

Cassius set the mug down. He hadn’t bought cedar anything. His apartment smelled of dust, cheap coffee, and the faint chemical lemon of the floor cleaner the super used.

He breathed in through his nose. Just coffee. Just dust.

A jay screamed outside the window, a harsh, scraping sound. The noise drilled into his temple, a single point of pressure. For a second, the light in the kitchen seemed to stutter. Not a flicker of the bulb, but a shift in quality—the pale yellow of a winter sun going softer, warmer, like late afternoon in a different season. He saw, clear as memory, the side of a blue house. Not sky blue. A deep, faded navy, with white trim peeling near the gutter. A sprinkler chattered on a lawn.

Then it was gone.

The kitchen was his kitchen. Beige walls, the crack in the plaster above the stove shaped like a lazy bolt of lightning, the calendar from the hardware store still showing February even though it was April. The light was flat and cold again.

Cassius rubbed his eyes, hard, until colors bloomed behind his lids. Stress. Not enough sleep. He’d been staring at budget reports until two a.m. the numbers blurring into gray static on the screen.

He got up, the legs of his chair scraping against the linoleum with a sound that set his teeth on edge. He needed to get ready for work. Tuesday. He had a meeting at ten about quarterly projections, a thing he both understood and found utterly meaningless.

The shower water was too hot, then too cold. He stood under the spray, trying to rinse the strange morning from his skin. The cedar smell was back, just for a heartbeat, mixed with the steam and the generic citrus of his soap. It brought with it a feeling, not a thought—a hollow, quiet ache behind his breastbone. The kind of ache left by a loss so old it had worn smooth, like a stone in a river.

He shut off the water. Dripped on the bathmat.

Shaving was a mechanical act. Foam, razor, short strokes. The face in the mirror was his. Thirty. A few fine lines at the corners of his eyes, a streak of premature gray at the temple his mother always pointed out. He looked tired. He looked ordinary.

His hand paused, the razor hovering near his jaw. A name surfaced in his mind, unbidden, riding a wave of that same sweet-wood smell.

Damien.

It wasn’t a thought he’d had. It was just there, fully formed, weighted with a grief that felt both fresh and ancient. The hollow ache in his chest throbbed in time with it.

“Who the hell is Damien?” he said aloud. His voice was too loud in the small, steamy room.

No answer. Just the drip of the faucet.

He finished shaving, nicked his chin. A bead of blood welled up, bright and real. He pressed a piece of toilet paper to it, the white blotting red. An anchor. He was here, bleeding in his bathroom. Not remembering some blue house, not smelling some phantom cedar, not grieving for some stranger named Damien.

Dressed, tie knotted badly and re-knotted, he grabbed his briefcase and keys. The apartment door locked behind him with a solid, final click.

The hallway carpet was a pattern of muted greens and browns, worn to a threadbare path down the center. Mrs. Gable’s door across the hall was cracked open, the chain lock on. She always watched. He could feel her eye at the narrow gap.

“Morning, Mrs. Gable.”

Her door eased shut without a sound.

Outside, the air had a bite to it, a last gasp of winter clinging to the spring morning. His car, a sensible sedan the color of dull mud sat at the curb. He unlocked it, slid inside. The interior smelled of stale fast food and the pine tree air freshener dangling from the rearview, a scent so aggressively artificial it made his eyes water.

He turned the key. The engine coughed caught.

He pulled away from the curb, checking his mirror. The blue house flashed again, not a full image—just a smear of color in his peripheral vision, the sensation of a driveway under tires that wasn’t asphalt but crushed white stone.

He jammed the brake. A car lurched to a halt behind him.

The street behind him was just a street. Brick apartments, leafless trees, a kid on a bicycle weaving down the sidewalk.

His heart was doing a slow, hard knock against his ribs. He took a deep breath. The air in the car was all pine chemical and old fries.

“You’re losing it, Ashbourne,” he muttered. “Thirty-year-old breakdown. Classic.”

He drove to work, keeping his eyes fixed on the road ahead, on the tangible world of stoplights and crosswalks and billboards. He turned the radio on found a talk station, let the drone of political argument fill the car. Noise. It blocked out the quiet, and the quiet was where the other things lived.

The office building was a glass-and-steel rectangle that reflected the cloudy sky. He parked in his assigned spot, number forty-seven. He sat for a minute, hands on the wheel, listening to the engine tick as it cooled. The meeting awaited. The projections. The polite, vacant smiles of his colleagues.

He got out, smoothed his tie. He was Cassius. He was thirty. He had a job to do.

As he crossed the parking lot, a breeze kicked up, carrying the city smells of exhaust and damp concrete. And underneath it, so faint he almost convinced himself he imagined it was the dry, sweet scent of cedar. Gone before he could take another breath, leaving behind only the echo of that hollow ache and the silent, unanswered weight of a name.

He pushed through the glass doors into the lobby, letting the sterile, climate-controlled air wash over him. The past was past. This was now. This was real.

He almost believed it.

Cassius watched the second hand sweep around the kitchen clock. Seven o’clock. Dinner at his mother’s. His thirtieth birthday. The air in his apartment felt thin, charged with something he couldn’t name, something that hummed just beneath the surface of his skin. The ghost of cedar maybe. Or the echo of a name.

He drove across town, the familiar streets blurring into a tunnel of sodium lights. His mother lived in the house he grew up in, a two-story colonial with a porch swing and a perpetually overgrown rose bush. It was a solid, comforting place, a bastion against the strange currents that had tugged at him all day.

He parked, the engine cutting out with a final shudder. The porch light cast a warm, yellow glow on the front door. He took a breath, pushed the strange feelings down. Tonight was about steak, cake, and pretending he didn’t feel like a stranger in his own life.

His mother, Eleanor, opened the door before he even knocked. Her smile was wide, crinkling the corners of her eyes. She wore a floral apron over a sensible sweater, her silver hair pulled back in a neat bun. “Cassius! Happy birthday, darling.”

She pulled him into a hug that smelled of lavender and roast beef. He let himself be held for a moment, the familiar warmth a balm against the day’s unease.

“Smells good, Mom.”

“Only the best for my birthday boy.” She stepped back, her gaze sharp, assessing. “You look tired. Still working too hard?”

He offered a weak smile. “Something like that.”

The dining room was set. His father, William sat at the head of the polished mahogany table reading the evening newspaper, a glass of amber liquid in his hand. He grunted a greeting, lowering the paper just enough to show his silver-rimmed glasses perched on his nose.

“Thirty, eh? Time flies.”

“Happy birthday, son.” His father raised his glass a fraction, a small, stiff gesture that passed for affection.

Cassius took his usual seat, across from his mother. The crystal glasses caught the light from the chandelier, throwing tiny rainbows onto the white tablecloth. The clink of silverware against china, the low murmur of the news from the living room, the comforting scent of cooked meat and his mother’s perfume—it was all designed to soothe.

Eleanor bustled into the kitchen, returning with a platter piled high with roast beef, perfectly golden potatoes, and bright green beans. She placed it in the center of the table.

“Eat, eat,” she urged, filling his plate generously.

He ate, tried to focus on the conversation about local politics and the rising cost of property taxes. His father’s voice was a low rumble, Eleanor’s a cheerful counterpoint. It was a familiar rhythm, one he’d heard a thousand times.

But the phantom cedar smell was back, faint but persistent, clinging to the edges of the roast beef aroma. And with it, the hollow ache in his chest, a low thrum beneath the surface of his attention. He found himself looking at the empty chair at the end of the table.

Not really empty. In his mind, a boy sat there. A boy with bright, quick eyes and a gap-toothed grin. A younger boy maybe ten. He reached for a potato, elbowing Cassius’s arm playfully. Cassius saw the faded blue of a T-shirt heard a high, clear laugh. The light from the chandelier seemed to catch the boy’s hair, turning it to spun gold. A faint scar above his left eyebrow, from falling off his bike.

Cassius dropped his fork. It clattered against the plate.

Eleanor looked at him, concern etched on her face. “Cassius? Are you all right?”

He picked up the fork, his hand shaking slightly. “Fine. Just… thinking.”

The boy was still there, a ghost at the table, vivid and real. He was eating a potato, his mouth full, eyes sparkling. He leaned over, whispered something in Cassius’s ear about getting extra dessert.

Cassius felt a wave of affection, so sharp it stole his pause. And then, an equally sharp wave of grief. The ache in his chest intensified, a cold knot tightening.

“Mom,” he began, his voice rough. He cleared his throat. “Mom, I… I had a memory today.”

Eleanor paused, her hand hovering over the gravy boat. “Oh? About what, dear?” she said, gentle, indulgent.

“About… Damien.” The name felt heavy on his tongue, a secret he’d carried his whole life and only just remembered. “My brother.”

The silence that followed was absolute. The clink of his father’s ice in his glass seemed impossibly loud. His father slowly lowered the newspaper, his eyes, usually so distant, now fixed on Cassius.

Eleanor’s face, a moment ago soft with maternal affection, hardened. Not in anger, but in something far colder. A kind of polite, absolute blankness.

“Cassius,” she said, her voice flat, “you don’t have a brother.”

He shook his head, a desperate denial. “But I do. I remember him. Damien. Younger than me. He had… he had a scar, here.” He touched his own eyebrow, tracing the phantom line. “From falling off his bike. We were at the park.”

His mother set down the gravy boat. It landed with a soft thud. She looked at him, her eyes clear, unwavering. “Cassius, you were an only child. We never had another son.”

His father cleared his throat. “Your mother’s right, son. Always just you.” His voice was calm, steady, devoid of any doubt.

The boy at the table vanished, dissolved into the flickering air. The cedar smell faded. The light from the chandelier became just light again, cold and bright. Cassius looked at the empty chair, then back at his mother’s face. It was a wall. Immovable.

“But… I swear…” He trailed off. How could he argue with such certainty? With such quiet, unyielding conviction?

Eleanor reached across the table, her hand covering his. Her touch was warm, firm. “It’s stress, darling. The office. All those numbers you stare at. You need to relax.”

He pulled his hand back, gently. The warmth of her touch felt like a lie. His chest tightened, a cold vice squeezing his ribs. He felt like he was suffocating, trapped between two irreconcilable truths. One, a memory so real it tasted of scraped knees and summer afternoons. The other, his mother’s placid, absolute denial.

“I… I need some air.” He pushed his chair back, the legs scraping against the floor. The sound was loud, jarring.

His mother watched him go, her expression unreadable. His father had already raised his newspaper again, a rustle of paper already dismissing the anomaly.

Cassius walked out of the dining room, the roast beef smell turning cloying. He walked through the living room, past the old grandfather clock ticking away the seconds, each tick a tiny hammer blow against the fragile structure of his sanity. He walked out the front door, leaving the warmth and the light behind him. The air outside was cold, sharp. He stood on the porch, staring out into the dark, familiar street. The rose bush was a dark, thorny silhouette against the neighbor’s porch light. He let the words out in the cold air, trying to clear his head, trying to make sense of the perfect, terrible blankness he had just encountered.

The grief for Damien, the brother who never was felt more real than the chill on his skin.

Cassius drove back to his apartment, the steering wheel cold beneath his hands. The city lights blurred, streaking past his windows like a faulty broadcast. He didn’t remember the drive. Didn’t remember stopping at red lights or yielding to pedestrians. He just found himself parked in front of his building, the engine off, the silence thick and heavy.

He climbed the three flights of stairs, his keys a cold weight in his palm. The muted green and brown carpet of the hallway seemed to absorb all sound, all light. His own apartment door felt alien, the brass knob cool and unfamiliar. He fumbled with the lock, his fingers clumsy, until the tumblers clicked with a hollow sound.

Inside, the apartment was dark and still. He didn't turn on the lights. The faint glow from the streetlamps outside filtered through the blinds, casting long, distorted shadows across the living room. The air felt thick, charged with the lingering presence of his own thoughts.

He walked into his small office, a spare room he’d converted into a study. His desk, usually a model of organized chaos seemed to hum with an unspoken question. He sat down, the worn leather of his chair creaking under his weight. He pulled a fresh legal pad from the top drawer, its clean white pages a stark contrast to the swirling confusion in his head. A pen, heavy and metallic felt good in his grip.

He started writing. Not about the budget reports, not about the quarterly projections. He wrote about Damien.

Name: Damien. He wrote it in bold, precise letters. Relationship: Brother. A pause. He underlined it twice. His mother’s flat voice echoed in his ears: you don’t have a brother. He pushed the thought away.

Characteristics: Younger. Gap-toothed grin. Scar above left eyebrow. He closed his eyes, conjuring the image. The boy was so clear, so vivid. He could hear his laugh, feel the slight pressure of his elbow in his side. The scent of cedar wafted through his mind, sweet and clean, like fresh-cut kindling.

Memory 1: Park. Falling off bike. Scar. He sketched a crude map of a park he couldn’t place, marking an X where the bike fell. Memory 2: Dinner table. Eating potatoes. Whispering about dessert. He drew a small, smiling face next to the empty chair in his mental dining room.

He wrote for a long time, filling page after page with fragments, sensations, snatches of conversation that felt both utterly real and utterly impossible. He documented the blue house, the chattering sprinkler, the crushed white stone of the driveway. He described the way the light caught Damien’s hair, turning it to spun gold. He wrote about the affection, the sharp, sudden love that had stolen his beat.

His pen moved across the page, a steady, rhythmic scratching in the quiet room. He felt a strange sort of calm settling over him, the meticulous act of recording a familiar anchor in a sea of unreality. This was his coping mechanism. This was how he made sense of the world: by breaking it down into data points, by categorizing the inexplicable, by imposing order on chaos.

He had always been good at it. His academic career, brief as it was had been built on his ability to organize disparate facts into coherent narratives. His current job, the endless spreadsheets and projections was just a more mundane application of the same skill. He could take any problem, any mystery, and reduce it to a series of solvable equations.

But this was different. This wasn't a problem to be solved. This was a truth that contradicted all other truths.

He paused, rereading a line: “Mom, you don’t have a brother.” The words stood out, stark and unyielding. A cold, hard fact from his mother’s mouth, pitted against the hot, clear reality of his own memory.

He looked at his notes. They were precise. Detailed. They held the weight of experience. But they were also… alone. Unvalidated. He could show this legal pad to anyone, and they would see a hallucination. A delusion. His own private madness.

He chewed on the end of his pen, the plastic hard against his teeth. He felt for the first time the true isolating horror of his experience. It wasn’t a ghost in the house. It was a ghost in his mind, and he was the only one who could see it.

He pushed the legal pad aside. He needed context. He needed proof.

He opened his laptop, the screen a sudden, harsh rectangle of blue light in the dark room. He typed “Damien Ashbourne brother” into the search bar. Nothing. He tried “Ashbourne family history.” Again, nothing. The internet, that vast, wide archive of human existence offered no trace of the boy with the gap-toothed grin.

He went deeper, searching for old school yearbooks from his elementary school, local newspaper archives from his childhood years. He found pictures of himself, younger, awkward. Pictures of his parents. But always, only himself. Always, an only child.

The lack of evidence was its own kind of evidence. Evidence of absence. Or evidence of something far more unsettling.

The digital clock on his laptop screen read 2:17 AM. He had been at it for hours, chasing a ghost through the cold, unfeeling circuits of the internet. His eyes burned. His head throbbed. He felt a profound weariness settle into his bones, a fatigue that went deeper than lack of sleep. It was the exhaustion of fighting against reality itself.

He leaned back in his chair, running a hand over his face. The meticulous notes on the legal pad mocked him from the desk. The blankness of the search results screamed at him from the screen.

He was Cassius. Thirty years old. An only child. And he had a brother named Damien who existed only in his head, a phantom limb of a life that never was. The truth of it settled over him, cold and heavy.

He reached for his pen again, intending to add a final, desperate note to his growing file of impossible facts. But as he extended his hand, his left hand began to tremble. A subtle tremor, at first, barely noticeable. But it grew, a tiny, uncontrollable vibration that ran through his fingers, up his arm, and into his chest. It wasn’t fear. It wasn’t cold. It was something else. A new kind of static. A new kind of interference.

The pen clattered to the desk. He stared at his trembling hand, a deep unease creeping over him. It was a physical effect, an involuntary response, like the cedar smell, like the stuttering light. A small, insidious crack in the carefully constructed facade of his physical reality. He tried to steady it, clenching his fist, but the tremor persisted, a silent, unsettling proof of the chaos brewing within him.
