The hospital released him with a steroid pack and a referral to an allergist he never called. The referral sat on his kitchen counter for three weeks, the edges softening in the humidity from the sink, until he threw it away. The eggs went next. Then the peanuts, the shellfish, the penicillin prescription from a dental procedure two years back—anything that carried the faintest whiff of potential cross-contamination with the phantom allergy list he’d started compiling in his head. His diet narrowed to plain rice, boiled chicken, steamed vegetables with no seasoning. Food became fuel, not pleasure. A necessary injection of calories to keep the machine running.

That machine felt borrowed.

He sat at his living room table, age fifty-one. That table was a door laid across two filing cabinets, a relic from his last office job. On it sat three notebooks, a stack of index cards, two pencils sharpened to needle points, and a mug of cold tea. The afternoon light through the blinds cut the room into bars of gold and deep shadow. Dust motes swam in the slants of light, lazy and indifferent.

Notebook One was labeled OBSERVATIONS. It contained dates, times, duration of episodes, and sensory details. April 12, 2:17 PM. Auditory: child’s laughter, approximate age 5-7, source: hallway. Lasted 8 seconds. Concurrent scent: cut grass, gasoline. Physical sensation: warmth on left cheek, as if facing sun. His handwriting was small, tight, economical.

Notebook Two was titled CORRELATIONS. This was where the system broke down. He’d tried to group the fragments into what he called ‘channels.’ Channel Blue: associated with Damien, the blue Victorian house, the scent of cedar, a feeling of childhood summer boredom edged with a specific, brotherly affection. Channel Gold: Lilith, the beach, the porch, the taste of salt and lemon, a profound, anchoring contentment. Channel Gray: the cold presence, the antiseptic smell, the scratching, the sense of being watched with a clinical, jealous hunger. There were others. Flickers too brief to categorize. A memory of calloused hands working leather. The smell of ozone and hot metal. The taste of bitter ale.

The correlations refused to hold. A scent from Blue would bleed into a tactile sensation from Gold. The cold of Gray would seep into a Blue memory, frosting the edges of a summer day. The index cards were worse. He’d written key identifiers on them, hoping to physically sort the streams. He laid them out on the table now, a pathetic tarot spread.

Red mitten. Sailboat wallpaper. Spearmint gum. Diesel fumes. Cold doorknob.

He stared at them. They were litter. Sensory litter from a dozen shipwrecked lives washing up on his beach. No narrative. No through-line. Just things. He picked up the ‘cold doorknob’ card. One memory was sharp—a specific brushed-nickel knob, always cold to the touch, on a door painted a gloss dark green. Whose door? Which life? He didn’t know. The knowledge ended at the temperature of the metal.

His pencil tapped the paper, a rapid, nervous staccato. He forced himself to stop. This was the coping mechanism, the meticulous cataloging. Supposed to create order. Supposed to be a dam.

The dam was leaking everywhere.

A prickling started at the base of his skull, a familiar static charge. He closed his eyes, bracing. Not now. He had to finish the entry for Tuesday’s olfactory spike—burnt toast and lilac, a combination that had made him gag over the sink.

The static didn’t crest into a vision. It settled in, a low hum behind his eyes. The quality of the light in the room changed. Not brighter, but… thicker. The gold bars from the window took on a syrupy, amber quality. The dust motes weren’t floating anymore; they swirled, deliberate as galaxies. The air smelled of vanilla and old paper.

This was new. A passive overlay. Not a memory, but a filter applied to the present.

He looked at his hand on the table. His own hand, age fifty-one, a few liver spots, the knuckles slightly enlarged. Under the filter, he saw a younger hand. Tanned. A thin white scar across the knuckle of the index finger. A wedding band he didn’t own.

He turned the hand over. His palm lines were different. A deep, straight fate line that cut clean across. He clenched the fist. His joints ached with a younger, different ache—the phantom pain of a break that never happened to him.

“Stop,” he whispered.

The filter flickered. The scent of vanilla sharpened, then snapped back to the dry dust-and-old-wood smell of his actual apartment. The light flattened. His hand was just his hand again. The scar was gone. The ring was gone.

He exhaled, a shaky sound. He reached for Notebook One, flipped to a fresh page. His pen hovered.

What to log? Passive visual/olfactory overlay. Channel unknown. Duration approximately 40 seconds. Included proprioceptive dissonance (foreign hand-map).

It sounded insane. Because it was.

He threw the pen. It hit the far wall with a plastic tick and clattered to the floor.

The frustration was a hot, sour ball in his stomach. This was the work. This was the only work left. To be the archivist of his own dissolution. And the archive was nonsense. A library where all the books were written in fragments of different languages, shelved at random, and slowly catching fire.

He got up, paced to the window. The view was the brick wall of the adjacent building, a fire escape, a sliver of alley. He’d watched a pigeon raise two clutches of chicks on that fire escape this spring. A real thing. A thing that happened here.

As he watched, the brick wall softened. The hard edges bled. The color warmed from rust-red to a sandy ochre. The fire escape melted, replaced by the swaying fronds of a palm tree. The alley became a strip of dazzling turquoise ocean under a sky streaked with peach and violet.

The coastal sunset. Channel Gold.

It wasn’t a memory. It was a replacement. His apartment window was gone. The hum of the city was gone, replaced by the rhythmic crash of waves and the cry of distant gulls. Warm, salt-heavy air washed over his face. He could feel the grit of sand on a floor that wasn’t his, smell coconut oil and grilling fish.

He didn’t move. He knew if he reached out, his hand would meet glass that wasn’t there. If he turned his head, he’d see the rest of a room he’d never been in. A couch with a faded floral print. A low table holding a bottle of beer sweating in the humid air. Lilith’s sandals by the door.

His breath hitched. He wanted it. God, the want was a physical ache, deeper than hunger, more desperate than thirst. It was the want of a plant bending toward a sun it had only ever dreamed of.

He squeezed his eyes shut. It’s not yours. It’s a broadcast. You are a receiver.

When he opened them, the ocean was still there. The sunset was deepening, painting the underside of scattered clouds in fiery magenta. A figure walked along the water’s edge, silhouetted—a woman with a long, easy stride. Lilith.

She stopped turned toward the house. Toward him. She raised a hand, not a wave, just a lifting of fingers in acknowledgment. I see you. I’m here.

He couldn’t breathe. The sterile, careful distance of his cataloging shattered. This wasn’t a fragment. This was a moment, whole and alive and happening now, somewhere. And he was in it. Or a version of him was.

The figure turned and continued her walk. The sun dipped lower, a molten coin on the horizon.

Then, like a slide being yanked from a projector, the image stuttered. A vertical tear of static ripped through the ocean. The peach sky flickered to the dull gray of his alley. The turquoise bled to dirty asphalt. The crash of waves became the distant wail of a garbage truck.

He was staring at the brick wall. The fire escape. The pigeon, preening on a railing.

He stumbled back from the window, his legs connecting with the edge of his chair. He sat down hard. The wood creaked.

His hands were trembling. Not with fear, but with loss. The loss was absolute, a vacuum in his chest. He’d been offered a sip of water in a desert, and then the cup was dashed from his lips.

He looked at the table. At the notebooks, the index cards, the whole pathetic architecture of his understanding. It was a joke. You couldn’t map a shifting coastline. You couldn’t log a ghost that was also yourself.

The Channel Gold overlay had felt more real, more solid, than the chair beneath him now. His own reality had the consistency of a rumor. A story he was telling himself, badly, while better, brighter stories bled through the pages.

He picked up the ‘cold doorknob’ card. He didn’t just remember the cold metal. He missed the door it belonged to. He grieved for a room he’d never entered.

The war was over. He had lost. But this was worse than deletion. This was being forced to watch, through a thousand keyholes, all the other lives you were supposed to be living instead. And knowing, with a certainty that was its own kind of madness, that they were all more real than this one.

A bed too large. It had always been too large, a queen-size mattress he’d bought during a brief, optimistic phase after the divorce, but now its emptiness was a physical presence. A cold expanse of sheet on the other side. He lay on his back, age fifty-three, and stared at the ceiling. A crack mapped a slow river delta from the light fixture toward the wall. He’d named its tributaries years ago: the Styx, the Lethe, the Cocytus. Tonight, the Lethe branch seemed longer.

The hum was back. Not the aggressive static of a spike, but the low-grade, persistent buzz of a poorly tuned station. It lived in his molars now. He ran his tongue over them, feeling the vibration. His own private frequency.

Sleep was a theory. He closed his eyes. The darkness behind his lids was not empty. It swarmed with afterimages—the turquoise sea, the silhouette of Lilith, the cold nickel doorknob. Sensory ghosts. He focused on the crack above him, using its real, fixed shape as an anchor.

Then the anchor slipped.

The texture of the sheets changed. Not a memory overlay, but a replacement. The crisp, slightly stale cotton of his own bedding became flannel. Soft, worn, pilled with use. The smell of his room—dust, old books, the faint chemical tang of the city air seeping through the window—dissolved. Replaced by the warm, sweet scent of lavender and baby powder. Underneath that, the earthy, comforting smell of dog.

He didn’t open his eyes. He knew the rules. Opening his eyes was surrender.

But his body betrayed him. His hand, moving of its own volition, slid across the flannel sheet. It didn’t find empty space. It found a small, warm, solid form curled against his side. A child. The breath was shallow and even, a puff of air against his rib cage. A tiny hand rested on his chest, fingers splayed.

His heart stopped. Then it hammered, a frantic drum against the small palm.

A weight settled across his feet at the end of the bed. A sigh. The dog. He knew it was a Labrador, golden, without seeing it. He knew its name was Sam. The knowledge was just there, whole and complete, like he knew his own birthday.

This was Channel… he had no name for it. Domestic. Quiet. It was the life that happened in the spaces between the dramatic memories. The laundry-folding, dish-washing, goodnight-kiss life. The life he’d never had.

Tears burned behind his closed eyes. He didn’t dare move. He held his breath, trying to stretch the moment into an eternity. This was the real haunting. Not the cold scratching or the phantom beaches. This. This perfect, ordinary warmth.

A small sound escaped the child—a sleep-mumble, a wet click of the tongue. The little hand flexed against his chest, clutching a handful of his T-shirt. His T-shirt. It was a soft, gray cotton shirt he didn’t own. He felt the child’s hair, fine as cornsilk, tickling his arm.

The dog at his feet readjusted, its collar jingling softly. A single, solid thump of a tail against the comforter.

This was a Tuesday night. In some other world, in some other broadcast, it was a Tuesday night and he was a father and a dog owner and the bed was exactly the right size. The crack on the ceiling was probably fixed. They’d have fixed the crack.

The longing was a bone-deep ache. It wasn’t desire; it was recognition. This was the shape his life was supposed to fill. This warmth, this weight, this soft sound of breathing that wasn’t his own. The hollow in his chest, the one he’d carried for decades wasn’t a hollow at all. It was a mold. And this was the casting that fit it perfectly.

He risked opening his eyes.

The room was dark, but not his dark. A night-light shaped like a star cast a soft, yellow glow from a plug by the door. He could see the shape of a dresser, cluttered with a haphazard pile of children’s books and a digital clock that read 2:14 AM. A framed photo on the wall, too dark to see details. The air was the perfect temperature, slightly cool, ideal for sleeping under a blanket with a child tucked beside you.

He turned his head, millimeters at a time, terrified the motion would shatter everything.

The child was a boy maybe four or five. His face was round and peaceful in sleep, lips slightly parted. One cheek was smushed against Cassius’s side. He had freckles across the bridge of his nose. Cassius knew, without knowing how he knew, that the boy’s name was Eli. Short for Elijah.

The thought trailed off, belonging to another man.

He reached up, his hand moving with a foreign muscle memory, and brushed a strand of hair from the boy’s forehead. The touch was feather-light. The skin was impossibly soft, warm. Real.

Eli stirred, nuzzling closer. “Daddy,” he mumbled, a sleep-thickened whisper.

The word hit Cassius like a physical blow. It stole the air from his lungs. It was a key turning in a lock he didn’t know he had.

He was crying. Silent tears tracked from the corners of his eyes, hot, running into his hairline. He didn’t try to stop them. This was it. The core of the wound. Not the forgotten lives, but the forgotten loves. The people who, in a fair universe would have been his. The dog at his feet, the child at his side, the woman who was probably sleeping down the hall after a long day. A whole ecosystem of connection, humming with quiet, unremarkable life.

He wanted to freeze this. To stop time here, in this dark, lavender-scented room on a Tuesday night at 2:14 AM. He would trade every other memory, every other possible channel, for this one to be his primary signal. He would live in this Tuesday night forever.

The dog thumped its tail again. A car passed outside the window, its headlights painting a slow arc across the ceiling—a smooth, unbroken ceiling, no cracks.

This was more real than the feel of his own, solitary mattress had ever been. This was the truth. The other life, the one with the filing cabinet desk and the dust motes and the pigeon on the fire escape, that was the phantom. That was the bad dream.

He let his head fall back into the pillow. He inhaled the scent of lavender, baby powder dog, child. He felt the solid, trusting weight against him. He listened to the twin rhythms of pause—the boy’s, the dog’s. His own heart began to slow, syncing with the peace of the room.

This was home.

A sharp, electronic chirp sliced through the dark.

Wrong. It didn’t belong here. It was the sound of a digital alarm clock from the 1990s, a cheap, grating beep-beep-beep.

The night-light flickered. The star shape distorted, stretching into a smear of yellow light.

No.

The lavender scent twisted, soured, mixing with the dry, familiar smell of his own bedroom dust. The flannel beneath him thinned became coarse cotton. The weight against his side lightened, faded.

“No,” he whispered, his voice raw. He tightened his arm, a reflexive, protective gesture.

His arm closed around empty air.

The warm, solid form of the child dissolved. It didn’t vanish abruptly; it evaporated, the sensation of his presence lingering for a half-second like the ghost of a limb. The small hand on his chest became a memory of pressure, then nothing. The dog’s weight vanished from his feet. The soft jingle of the collar cut off mid-sound.

The chirping alarm was constant. It was his alarm. On his bedside table. In his apartment.

The star-shaped night-light was gone. The yellow glow was replaced by the sickly green numerals of his own clock: 3:07 AM. The clutter of books, the framed photo, the smooth ceiling—all ripped away, revealing the stark, familiar lines of his own barren room. The crack on the ceiling leered down at him, the Lethe branch now seeming to pulse.

He was alone. The emptiness of the bed was a void, a crater. The silence was deafening, absolute. It pressed against his eardrums.

A sob broke from him, harsh and ugly in the quiet. He curled onto his side, into the space where the child had been, fetal around the absence. The sheets were cold. The loss was a physical sickness, a nausea of the soul. He hadn’t lost a memory. He’d lost a son he never had. He’d lost a dog named Sam. He’d lost a whole, quiet world.

His hand, clenched into a fist, brushed against something soft.

He froze. His beat hitched.

Lying on the sheet, in the indentation where the child had slept was a small, worn teddy bear. Brown, one eye missing, the fur matted from love. It smelled faintly, unmistakably, of lavender and baby powder.

An artifact. A piece of driftwood from a sunken world.

A desperate, wild hope flared in his chest. He reached for it, his fingers trembling.

The moment his fingertips made contact with the rough, plush fabric, the bear pixelated. Not a fade. A violent, digital glitch. It fragmented into a hundred squares of mismatched color and static, like a corrupted image file. Then it was gone. Not even an indent left in the sheet.

His hand closed on nothing.

The alarm clock chirped once more, then fell silent. The green numbers glowed: 3:08.

He lay in the cold, empty bed, in the silent, hollow room. The scent of lavender and baby powder hung in the air for one more moment, a cruel afterthought. Then it too was gone, replaced by the stale, lonely smell of a room where only one person had ever slept.

He climbed out of bed. The floorboards creaked under his weight, a familiar sound, but even that felt thin, stretched. His bare feet met the cold wood. He padded to the kitchen, the chill seeping into his bones. The apartment was quiet, the city outside just beginning to stir with the low rumble of delivery trucks. He flipped on the overhead light. The fluorescent hum was flat, clinical. He blinked, adjusting to the harsh glare.

He needed coffee. The ritual of it, the solid weight of the mug, the bitter taste was one of the few constants left. He filled the kettle, placed it on the stove. The gas burner ignited with a reassuring whoosh. He waited for the water to boil, his gaze drifting to the chipped ceramic of the countertop, then to the window. The brick wall of the adjacent building stood sentinel. No palm trees. No ocean. Just brick.

His left hand still trembled. He watched it, a thing separate from him, a nervous bird trapped in a cage. He tried to steady it, clenching his fist, but the tremor persisted, an echo of the phantom child, the phantom dog, the phantom life. He needed to touch something real. Something undeniably his.

He walked to the small table by the window, the one where he kept his keys, his wallet, the few trivial objects that marked his existence. He reached for the marble. The clear glass orb, with its single blue ribbon swirl was his anchor. His proof. He’d found it in a coat pocket years ago, a relic from a life he could still grasp. It was the only object he knew for sure belonged here, to this Cassius.

His movement snagged. He pulled his hand back, staring at the polished wood surface. Nothing. The marble wasn’t there. A cold knot formed in his stomach, spreading quickly through his chest. He ran his hand over the table again, slowly methodically, pushing aside the old mail, the stray pen. Still nothing.

Panic, cold and sharp, pricked at him. He scanned the floor around the table. Underneath it. He dropped to his knees, peering into the shadows. His heart hammered against his ribs. He checked the small drawer in the table, pulling it open with a scrape of wood on wood. Empty.

The kettle whistled, a shrill, insistent sound that tore through the quiet. He ignored it. His eyes darted around the small kitchen, searching, desperate. Where could it be? He never moved it. Never. He kept it there, always, a small, quiet sentinel.

The hum in his molars intensified, a high-pitched whine. The edges of his vision softened. The kitchen walls seemed to undulate, the faded yellow paint shifting, gaining depth. The light from the window, reflecting off the brick took on a warmer, golden hue. The air grew thick, humid. He smelled faint traces of something sweet and cloying. Lilacs.

His hands, still trembling, scrabbled across the tabletop. He felt a phantom splinter, a familiar, rough grain that wasn’t the smooth oak of his own table. He pulled his hand back, confused. He looked down. His hand was no longer on his kitchen table. It rested on a wide, varnished windowsill. Beyond it, not the brick wall, but a stretch of overgrown grass, a tangled rose bush, and the white-painted trim of a Victorian house. Blue. This was the blue house. Channel Blue.

He was kneeling on a thick, patterned carpet. Not his worn linoleum. The air was cooler here, carrying the scent of damp earth and old wood. He heard the faint, distant sound of a child’s laughter. Damien’s laughter.

A wave of nausea rolled over him. He squeezed his eyes shut, pressing his palms against his temples. “No,” he mumbled, the word tasting like dust. He opened his eyes. The kitchen. His kitchen. The kettle still screamed on the stove. The fluorescent light buzzed overhead. The cold knot in his stomach tightened.

He stood, pushing himself up with hands that still shook. He moved to the counter and turned off the burner. The silence that followed was heavy, oppressive. He stood there, silence between them shallowly, waiting for the bleed-through to recede completely. It didn’t. The scent of lilac lingered, a ghost of a smell, warring with the faint metallic tang of his apartment’s pipes. The light, though still fluorescent seemed to hold a memory of gold.

He needed the marble. More than coffee. More than anything. He went back to the table. He stared at the empty spot. He closed his eyes again, trying to visualize it, to pull it into existence with sheer force of will. A clear glass sphere. A single blue ribbon swirl. Smooth, cool to the touch.

He opened his eyes. And there it was.

The truth settled over him, cold and heavy. He was adrift. The anchor was gone.
