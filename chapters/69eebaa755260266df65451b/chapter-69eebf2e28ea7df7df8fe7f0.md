The street was quiet, the kind of quiet that comes from good insulation and double-paned windows. Cassius stood at the edge of the sidewalk, the marble a hard knot in his pocket. The house was a pale yellow with white trim, the porch swept clean. A ceramic frog sat by the welcome mat, grinning up at him with glazed eyes. He’d seen that frog before—in a kitchen window, sunlight catching the curve of its back. Except he hadn’t.

He flexed his left hand, the tremor starting up again. The notepad in his other hand listed the address, the date, the time. Documentation. Always documentation. The ink had smudged where his thumb had pressed too hard.

The front door opened. A woman stepped out, a watering can in hand. Lilith.

She was older now, her dark hair streaked with gray, but the way she moved—the slight hitch in her step as she bent to water the petunias—was the same. Cassius’s breath caught. He knew the shape of her shoulders under that cardigan, the way she’d hum under her breath when she thought no one was listening. He knew the birthmark on her left shoulder hidden now by fabric.

He shouldn’t be here.

But he was.

Lilith straightened, rubbing at the small of her back. She turned, and for a heartbeat, her gaze swept over him without recognition. Then it snagged. Her fingers tightened around the watering can.

Cassius stepped forward. The words were there, rehearsed, but his mouth was dry. “Lilith.”

She didn’t answer. Her free hand drifted to her collarbone, fingers pressing there like she was checking for a pulse.

“You don’t know me,” he said. A lie. She did. Just not here.

The watering can trembled. “Who are you?”

He could say it. He could tell her about the beach house, the way the salt air tangled her hair, the song she’d hum while sketching at the kitchen table. He could describe the exact shade of blue in her favorite mug, the one with the chip on the handle. He could say, you used to press your cold feet against my calves in bed, and I’d pretend to complain.

But her eyes were wide, her breath shallow. That fear was a living thing between them.

“I’m sorry,” he said instead.

She took a step back, the heel of her shoe catching on the porch step. The watering can tipped, spilling onto the wood. “You need to leave.”

Cassius didn’t move. The marble was warm in his pocket, a grounding point. “I just—I needed to see you.”

“See me?” She lowered the words. “I don’t know you.”

A car door slammed down the street. A dog barked. Normal sounds, ordinary sounds, but they made her flinch.

Cassius reached into his pocket. Not for the marble—for the photograph. The one he’d carried for years, folded and refolded until the creases threatened to split the faces. He held it out. “Do you recognize this?”

She didn’t take it. Her gaze flicked to the photo, then back to his face. “What is this?”

“Us,” he said. “A lifetime ago.”

The photo showed them on a porch, sunlight dappling the wood, her head tilted back in laughter. Except it wasn’t her. Not quite. One woman in the photo was younger, her hair longer, her smile looser. But close enough.

Lilith’s breath hitched. “That’s not me.”

“It is.”

“No.” She shook her head, sharp. “That’s—that’s some kind of trick. Photoshop. I’ve never seen you before in my life.”

The words landed like stones. Cassius let his hand drop. The photo crumpled in his grip.

Lilith took another step back, her shoulder bumping the doorframe. “I’m calling the police.”

He should go. He would go. But not yet.

“Do you ever dream about the ocean?” he asked.

Her fingers froze halfway to the door handle.

“The sound of the waves,” he pressed. “The way the light hits the water in the afternoon. The smell of salt and sunscreen.”

Her throat worked. “Stop.”

“You used to say the horizon looked like a seam. Like the sky was stitched to the sea.”

“Stop.” Her voice was raw. She fumbled for her phone, her hands shaking.

Cassius watched her. The light from the living room window caught her hair, turning the gray strands silver. For a second, just a second, he saw her younger—sunburned, smiling, reaching for him across a blanket of sand.

Then the moment snapped.

Lilith had the phone to her ear. “Yes, hello—there’s a man on my porch. He won’t leave.”

Cassius turned away. The street was still quiet, still ordinary. His shoes scuffed against the pavement as he walked, the sound too loud in the stillness. Behind him, the door slammed shut.

He didn’t look back.

The marble was cold in his pocket. Proof. Anchor. Useless thing.

He’d known hadn’t he? Known she wouldn’t remember. Known it would end like this.

But he’d come anyway.

The streetlights flickered on as he reached the corner. The first few drops of rain hit his shoulders, cold and sharp. Cassius tilted his face up, letting the water bead on his skin.

Somewhere, in another life, Lilith was humming in a sunlit kitchen.

Somewhere, she knew him.

Not here.

The rain came harder now, slanting under the porch roof. Lilith hadn’t moved from the doorway, the phone still pressed to her ear, but the call hadn’t connected yet. Cassius watched the way her fingers curled around the device, the knuckles whitening. He knew that grip—the same one she used when she was bracing for bad news.

“Wait,” he said.

She didn’t lower the phone. The screen lit up with the glow of an unfinished dial.

Cassius reached into his pocket again, slower this time. Not the photo. Not the marble. A key. Small, brass, the teeth worn smooth. He held it up between them. “You keep a spare under the blue flowerpot. The one with the crack.”

Lilith’s breath stuttered. The phone dipped an inch.

“You lost the original key six months after we moved in,” he continued. “You were furious because it had the little seashell charm on it. The one your sister gave you.”

Her free hand twitched toward her collarbone again, a reflex. The birthmark was there, just under the fabric. He could picture it perfectly, a smudge of brown like spilled coffee.

“How do you know that?” Her voice was thin.

Rain hissed against the pavement. Cassius turned the key over in his palm. “Because I was there.”

A drop hit the porch step between them, then another. Silence stretched, taut.

Lilith’s thumb hovered over the call button. She didn’t press it.

Cassius took a step forward. The wood creaked under his weight. “You hate thunderstorms. You always said they sound like someone dragging furniture upstairs. You’d bury your face in my shoulder until they passed.”

Her lips parted. The phone slipped another inch.

“And you hum when you’re nervous. Always the same tune. Daisy Bell.”

A sharp intake of pause. Her fingers tightened around the phone again, but she didn’t raise it.

Cassius pressed on. “You have a scar on your left knee from when you fell off your bike at nine. You burned your right hand on a curling iron in college.”

“Stop,” she whispered. “Just—stop.”

He did. The key was warm in his hand, warmer than it should have been.

Lilith’s gaze flicked to the street, then back to him. The fear was still there, but something else, too, a dawning horror. Recognition of the wrong kind.

“You’re sick,” she said softly. “Aren’t you?”

Cassius exhaled. Rain dripped from his hair onto his jacket. “Not the way you think.”

She shook her head. “You need help.”

“I need you to believe me.”

“Believe what?” she said, quieter now. “That we—what? Lived some other life together? That’s not possible.”

The marble was a weight in his pocket. He could pull it out, show her, make her hold it. But he’d tried that before. With others. It never worked.

“You used to wake up at 3:17 a.m. Every night,” he said instead. “You’d stare at the ceiling for exactly twelve minutes, then roll over and go back to sleep. You never remembered it in the morning.”

Lilith went very still.

“And you bite your lip when you’re concentrating. Right here.” He touched his own lip, just off-center. “You’ve done it since you were a kid. Your mom used to tell you’d chew straight through it one day.”

Her hand rose, unbidden, to her mouth.

Cassius took another step. The distance between them was closing. “You have a mole on your left hip. Shaped like a crescent moon. You used to say it was your lucky spot.”

Lilith’s beat hitched. Her fingers trembled against her lips.

Rain drummed against the roof. A car splashed through a puddle somewhere down the block.

“Let me in,” Cassius said. “Just for a minute. Just to talk.”

She didn’t move.

He could see the war in her eyes, the part of her that wanted to slam the door, call the police, scrub this entire encounter from her memory. And the other part. The part that knew.

The phone slipped from her fingers. It clattered onto the porch, the screen dark.

Lilith exhaled. “One minute.”

She stepped back, holding the door open just wide enough.

Cassius crossed the threshold.

The living room was warm, the air thick with the scent of lavender and something baking, cookies maybe. A lamp cast a yellow glow over the couch, the coffee table, the stack of design magazines fanned out beside a half-finished crossword.

He knew this room. Not the furniture, not the layout, but the feeling of it. The way the light fell. The way the air moved.

Lilith shut the door behind him. The lock clicked.

She didn’t offer him a seat.

Cassius wiped the rain from his face with his sleeve. The tremor in his left hand was worse now, a steady vibration. He pressed his palm flat against his thigh to still it.

Lilith folded her arms. “Start talking.”

He could lie. He could say he was a private investigator, a stalker, a man with too much time and a talent for research. But he was tired of lies.

“We were married,” he said. “In another life.”

Her jaw tightened. “That’s not funny.”

“I’m not trying to be.”

“Then what are you trying to be? Because this—” She gestured between them. “This is insane.”

Cassius reached into his pocket. The marble was there, smooth and cool. He didn’t take it out. “You remember the dream you had last week? The one about the lighthouse?”

Lilith’s arms tightened around herself.

“You told me about it the next morning,” he continued. “You said the stairs kept going, no matter how far you climbed. And the light at the top was the wrong color. Not white. Blue.”

Her moment caught.

“You woke up with sand in your sheets,” he said. “You couldn’t figure out how it got there.”

A shudder ran through her. She took a step back, her shoulder bumping the wall. “Get out.”

“Lilith—”

“Get out!” The words came out raw, ragged.

Cassius didn’t move. Rain lashed against the windows. The room felt smaller suddenly, the walls closing in.

Lilith’s chest rose and fell rapidly. “You’re not real. This isn’t real.”

He reached for her.

She flinched away, her back hitting the wall. “Don’t touch me.”

Cassius let his hand drop. The tremor was worse now, crawling up his arm.

Lilith’s eyes were wide, wild. “You need to leave. Right now.”

He could push. He could tell her about the miscarriage, the way she’d sobbed into his shirt, the way the hospital room had smelled like antiseptic and wilted flowers. He could describe the exact shade of the blanket they’d wrapped her in, the one she’d never let him wash.

But her hands were shaking. Her silence came in short, sharp gasps.

He paused with his hand on the knob. “You’ll dream about the ocean again tonight,” he said quietly. “When you wake up, check your sheets.”

Lilith didn’t answer.

Cassius stepped back into the rain. The door slammed behind him.

The streetlights buzzed overhead, the sound like static. He tilted his face up, letting the water wash over him.

Never here.

The rain needled Cassius’s neck as he walked down the porch steps. Behind him, the deadbolt slid home with a final metallic snick. He flexed his left hand; the tremor had spread to his wrist now, a persistent buzz under the skin. The marble in his pocket pressed against his thigh with each step, its presence both comfort and indictment.

A curtain twitched in the neighbor’s window. Cassius kept his gaze forward, his shoulders hunched against the downpour. The street was a blur of wet pavement and smeared porch lights. Somewhere behind him, Lilith’s voice rose, tight and urgent. He didn’t need to hear the words to know she was giving the dispatcher his description. White male, late sixties, gray jacket, dark hair. He could recite the script himself.

A police siren warbled in the distance, still blocks away but closing fast. Cassius picked up his pace, his shoes slapping against the slick sidewalk. Rain had soaked through his collar, the fabric clinging to the back of his neck. He could turn himself in. Let them take him to some fluorescent-lit station, let some tired cop with coffee air ask him questions he couldn’t answer. Do you know this woman? Have you been stalking her? He’d been through it before. They’d check his ID, run his name, find nothing. No warrants. No priors. Just a man with a head full of ghosts and a pocket full of proof no one could see.

The siren grew louder. Cassius ducked into an alley between two houses, the narrow space smelling of wet garbage and mildew. He pressed his back against the brick, the rough surface scraping through his jacket. The patrol car’s lights strobed against the opposite wall, red and blue fracturing the rain into jagged shards. Tires hissed on the pavement. A car door slammed.

“Ma’am?” a man’s voice called, official and calm.

Lilith’s reply was muffled, but the panic in it carried. Cassius closed his eyes. He could picture her standing there, arms crossed, fingers digging into her elbows. The way she’d tuck her chin when she was scared but trying not to show it.

Footsteps crunched on gravel. A flashlight beam swept the alley entrance, then moved on. Cassius let out a whisper of pause. He waited until the voices faded, until the radio static and the murmur of procedure retreated back toward Lilith’s porch. Then he pushed off the wall and kept walking.

The alley dumped him onto a side street lined with parked cars. Rainwater gurgled in the gutters, swirling around discarded cigarette butts and candy wrappers. Cassius wiped his face with his sleeve. His fingers brushed the marble again, its surface slick with condensation.

He shouldn’t have come.

But he’d had to.

Headlights cut through the rain as a bus approached, its brakes squealing. The doors wheezed open. Cassius didn’t move. The driver gave him a look—You getting on or what?—before shaking his head and pulling away. The taillights blurred into crimson streaks.

Cassius leaned forward, resting his elbows on his knees. Water dripped from his hair onto the concrete between his shoes. He’d left something behind in Lilith’s house. Not the key; he still had that. Not the photo. Something else. A thread maybe. A filament of recognition. He’d seen it in her eyes, just for a second, before the terror swallowed it. She’d known. Not the details, not the facts, but the shape of it.

And she’d rejected it anyway.

The marble was warm against his palm when he finally pulled it from his pocket. Clear glass with a single blue ribbon swirling through its center. He’d found it in his coat twelve years ago, no memory of putting it there. It belonged to a version of him that didn’t exist here. A version who’d picked it up from a beach in 1994, slipped it into his pocket as a keepsake. A version who’d had a brother named Damien, who’d ridden bikes down a street that was now a community garden.

Cassius rolled the marble between his fingers. The blue streak caught the streetlight, glinting like a sliver of sky.

Another siren echoed in the distance, different from the first, higher, faster. An ambulance maybe. Or another patrol car, circling wider. They’d be looking for him now. Lilith would have given them his description, his direction. He seemed off. Not violent, but... wrong. He could hear her saying it, the way her voice would dip on the last word, like she was ashamed to admit it.

Cassius stood. The marble went back into his pocket, its weight familiar. He turned up his collar and stepped out into the rain again.

The sidewalks were empty now, the windows dark. A cat darted across the street ahead of him, a shadow with eyes that flashed green in the gloom. Cassius followed it without thinking, his feet carrying him down a narrow walkway between two houses. The cat vanished into the underbrush, but he kept going, pushing through a tangle of wet branches until he emerged into a small, overgrown park.

A swing set stood in the center, the chains rusted, the seats cracked with age. Cassius walked toward it, his shoes sinking into the sodden grass. He gripped one of the chains, the metal cold and rough under his fingers. The swing creaked as he gave it a push, the sound lonely in the empty park.

He could keep doing this. Keep tracking down the fragments of his other lives, the people who’d loved him in worlds that no longer existed. He could find Damien next, or the version of his father who’d died in a car crash in ’89 instead of a nursing home in ’66. He could collect their confusion, their fear, their pity, like stones in his pockets.

Or he could stop.

The thought was quiet, almost gentle. He could go home. Sit in his apartment with its ticking radiator and its stack of notebooks. Wait for the next memory to bleed through, the next ghost to tap him on the shoulder. He could let the world forget him in increments, let the edges of his existence fray until there was nothing left but the marble and the silence.

The swing slowed, the chains groaning. Cassius let go.

Somewhere, in another life, Lilith was checking her sheets for sand.

The rain let up as he reached the park’s edge, the downpour thinning to a drizzle. Cassius paused under a streetlight, the yellow glow pooling around him. He took the marble out one last time held it up to the light. The blue swirl seemed to pulse, a heartbeat trapped in glass.

Then he slipped it back into his pocket and walked away.

The sirens had stopped. The night was quiet again, the only sound the drip of water from the trees and the distant rumble of a train. Cassius didn’t look back.

He already knew what he’d see.
