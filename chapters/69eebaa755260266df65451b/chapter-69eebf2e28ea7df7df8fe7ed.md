She stopped trying to sleep.

The bedroom was a bad place. It was the junction. The walls were thin, the static loudest here. The mattress was a fault line. She’d lie down and the pressure would start—not in her head, but in the air. A thickening. A waiting.

So she sat in the armchair she’d dragged from the living room. She faced the bed. The notebook was open on her lap, a cheap spiral-bound thing. The pen was a cheap plastic stick. The lamp on the side table cast a yellow circle on the floorboards, stopping just short of the bedframe. She’d positioned it that way. The bed was in shadow. A stage.

She waited.

The first shift wasn’t a memory. It was a texture. The flannel of her pajama pants against her thighs became something else. Coarser. A rough wool blanket, scratchy and smelling of lanolin and woodsmoke. She didn’t fight it. She let the sensation coat her legs. She wrote in the notebook: Channel: rough wool. Scent: sheep, smoke. Temp: warm. Duration: 7 seconds. She underlined the time. Her handwriting was steady.

The wool faded. Her own flannel returned, soft and inert.

She breathed. In. Out. The clock on the dresser ticked. A real sound, mechanical. She focused on it. A pin in the map.

The next was auditory. A voice, male, older, rasping with phlegm. “—don’t care what the schedule says, the coupling’s shot.” Not words meant for her. A broadcast from a machine shop, a garage, someplace with concrete floors and grease. The voice hung in the air of her bedroom, superimposed on the silence. She wrote: Channel: male, 50s?, occupational. Content: mechanical complaint. Ambient: echo metal clang (distant). Duration: 3 seconds.

It vanished. The silence it left behind was different. Richer. It had edges now.

Her knee ached. Her own knee, the right one, arthritis from a fall she remembered taking on an icy sidewalk three winters ago. Or had she? The memory of the fall was clear—the shocking cold of the pavement through her trousers, the sharp crack of bone—but the winter itself was vague. A gray season among many gray seasons. The pain was real. She massaged the joint, her fingers pressing into the familiar sore spot beside the kneecap. An anchor.

The bed creaked.

Not her doing. She was in the chair. That sound came from the mattress, the left side, near the pillow. A single, low groan of springs. As if a weight had just settled.

She didn’t look at the bed. She looked at the notebook page. She wrote: Channel: physical presence. Manifestation: bedspring compression. Location: left side. Intensity: single event. Time: 2:14 AM. She noted the time from the clock. Her hand shook. She made the numbers neat, boxy. The shaking was in the pen, not the letters.

A smell bloomed. Antiseptic. The sharp, astringent tang of hospital-grade cleaner, layered over something sweet and decaying. The scent of the old man, Arthur Brindle, the one who died here. It filled her nostrils, coated her tongue. She’d researched that smell. It was the ghost of a ghost. She wrote: Channel: Brindle. Scent: isopropyl alcohol, necrotic tissue. Persistence: high. Association: cold spot (absent currently).

She breathed through her mouth. The smell clung.

Then cutting through the chemical rot, a different scent. Salt air. Coppertone sunscreen. Hot vinyl. A beach day. The smell was so vibrant, so specific, it felt like a shove. Her lungs hitched. Channel: Gold. Coastal. Lilith. She didn’t write the names. She wrote: sensory spike: olfactory only. Beach signature. Intensity: 9.

It lasted five heartbeats. When it faded, the hospital smell was gone too. The room just smelled like dust and the dry heat of the radiator.

She realized she was leaning forward, elbows on her knees, the notebook almost sliding from her lap. She sat back. The chair creaked, a real sound, her sound.

This was the work. She was not a haunted woman. She was a receiving instrument. A faulty radio, picking up every station on the dial at once. Her job was not to listen to one, but to log the interference patterns. To map the static.

The concept was a cold comfort. It fit in her head, neat and logical. It did nothing for the hollow feeling behind her breastbone, the ache that wasn’t physical.

Another texture. The skin on her back prickled, not with cold, but with a specific, forgotten sensation. The heat of a sunburn on the second day, tight and glowing, under the gentle friction of a cotton sheet. The sunburn from the life that wasn’t hers. She’d had that burn, once. She’d borne the evidence. Now she only remembered the memory of it. A ghost pain. She wrote: Channel: Gold (confirmed). Somatic memory: sunburn day 2. Location: upper back/shoulders.

She was cataloging her own life’s souvenirs. The thought was so ridiculous a laugh bubbled in her throat. She swallowed it. It turned into a cough.

The thumping started.

Not in the living room. Here. At the foot of the bed, in the dark just beyond the lamp’s glow. A slow, rhythmic thump... thump... thump against the floorboards. The dog’s tail. It was here with her now. It had followed the bowl.

She didn’t look. She wrote: Channel: canine. Auditory: tail thump. Location: bedroom, foot of bed. New behavior: migration from living room.

The thumping continued. A metronome. She found her breathing syncing to it. She broke the rhythm deliberately, inhaling sharply on the off-beat.

A taste flooded her mouth. Not memory. Actual taste. The oily, sulfurous aftertaste of eggs. Tuesday eggs. The ones that had tried to kill her. Her stomach clenched. Saliva pooled. She spit into the wastebasket beside the chair. She wiped her mouth with the back of her hand. Channel: biological rejection. Taste: egg residue. Somatic: nausea. Note: no recent ingestion. Cross-contamination.

The thumping stopped.

The sudden silence was a physical void. Her ears rang with the absence of sound. She stared at the dark space at the foot of the bed. Nothing.

Then, the bed again. Not a creak. A sigh. A human sigh. Weary, heavy, the expulsion of breath after a long day. A woman’s sigh. It came from the right side of the bed, her side.

Lilith.

She froze. The pen tip hovered above the paper. She hadn’t heard her, not directly, since the ambulance. Her channel had been silent for so long. This wasn’t a memory. This was a real-time event in a room that overlapped her own.

She didn’t write. She listened.

The sheets rustled. A soft, sliding sound of fabric on fabric. A body turning over. Then, a whisper. Her voice, sleepy, muffled by pillow. “You’re thinking too loud.”

The words were for her. For the Wound in that bed, in that life.

Her own throat locked. She couldn’t answer. She wasn’t there. She was here, in the chair, a ghost in her own bedroom.

Lilith sighed again, a smaller sound. “Come to sleep, Cass.”

It was the nickname that did it. The casual, intimate shortening. No one had ever called her that. Not in this life. The sound of it was a key turning in a rusted lock. Something gave way in her chest, not an emotion, but a structural fatigue, like a beam finally sagging under a weight it was never meant to hold.

The sensory spike didn’t build. It detonated.

The yellow lamplight bleached out, replaced by the soft blue-gray of moonlight through a large window. The shape of the window was wrong—wider, with a view of dark, shifting ocean. The smell of salt and night air replaced the dust. The feel of the armchair vanished. She was lying down. The mattress was firm, different. A body’s warmth pressed along her left side. Lilith’s warmth. She felt the curve of her hip against hers. The weight of a summer quilt, not too heavy.

She was there. In the coastal house. In the bed she shared with her.

She didn’t move. She let the reality settle over her. More real than the chair, than the notebook. The details were granular. The faint, rhythmic crash of waves on sand. A moth bumping softly against the screen outside the window. The taste of mint toothpaste on her own tongue. The slight stiffness in her shoulder from a day of sailing—a stiffness she hadn’t earned, but that her body remembered perfectly.

She turned her head on the pillow.

Lilith was facing her, her eyes closed. Moonlight silvered the line of her cheekbone, the slope of her nose. Her hair was dark against the white pillowcase. Wound knew the exact texture of it between her fingers. She knew Lilith would smell like lavender soap and sea air.

This was not a memory. This was now. For this version of her, it was a Tuesday night in July. For Lilith, she was her wife, lying beside her, thinking too loud.

Wound’s hand, not her decision, lifted from under the quilt. It hovered. She wanted to touch Lilith’s cheek, to confirm the reality. But the touch would be hers. It would be an action. It would collapse something.

Lilith’s eyes opened. They were dark, reflecting the moonlight. She looked at Wound, not through her. She saw her. “Hey,” she whispered. “You okay?”

Wound couldn’t speak. The Wound in that bed would have. She’d have said, “Yeah. Just thinking.” She’d have kissed her forehead. This Wound, the observer was a stone.

A flicker. A line of interference across the image. The blue moonlight stuttered. For a fraction of a second, the yellow lamplight was back, the shape of her own bedroom window—a narrow rectangle of streetlight orange. The smell of salt was cut with dust.

Lilith’s face changed. Not her expression, but its certainty. It softened at the edges, like a station tuning out.

“Cass?” she said, her voice tinged with new concern.

Wound was losing the signal. The static roared in the space between the wave crash and the radiator’s hiss. She clenched her will, a futile muscle. Stay.

But the protocol was breaking. The zero overlap rule. She was not just receiving. She was present. Her presence was causing interference in Lilith’s reality. Wound saw the moment Lilith felt it—a tiny furrow appeared between her brows. She reached for her.

Her hand touched Wound’s arm, the one lying on the quilt between them.

The touch was electric. Real. Warm and solid.

The connection shattered.

Wound gasped. The chair. The lamp. The notebook sliding to the floor with a papery slap. Her bedroom. The taste of eggs was back, sour in her mouth. The thumping was back, frantic now, a rapid thumpthumpthump at the foot of the bed.

She was shaking. Her whole body trembled, a violent, uncontrollable reaction. The phantom warmth of Lilith’s touch still burned on her arm. She looked at the skin. Nothing.

She bent, fumbling for the notebook. Her fingers were numb. She picked it up. The page was blank where she’d stopped writing. She stared at the empty lines.

She couldn’t log that. There was no category for it. Channel: Gold. Event: mutual awareness. Tactile confirmation. Duration: approximately 22 seconds. Result: system error.

She didn’t write it. She closed the notebook. She set it carefully on the side table.

The thumping slowed settled back into its slow rhythm.

She had been a receiver. Now she was a contaminant. She had leaked back.

This thought was quiet, cold. It sat in the center of her. She looked at the bed, at the empty space where Lilith had been. Where Wound had been, with her.

A new detail, one she hadn’t noticed in the spike, surfaced in her mind. On the nightstand in that other bedroom, next to a glass of water had been a small, smooth stone with a white quartz vein running through it. A keepsake. A paperweight.

An anchor.

She stood up. Her knees held. She walked to her own dresser, opened the top drawer. She pushed aside socks and found the marble. The clear glass with its single blue ribbon swirl. She held it in her palm. Cold. It was the only thing that belonged solely to her.

The thumping stopped. The room held its breath.

She wasn’t an instrument anymore. She was a woman in a room with a marble, and she had just touched a ghost who felt real. The map was useless. The lines were all wrong.

She carried the marble to the living room. The apartment felt different, stretched thin, like a worn photograph. She sat on the sofa, the one with the faded floral print. The light from the window was gray, the kind of light that promises nothing. She rolled the marble between her fingers. Smooth. Unyielding.

The dog, unseen began to scratch at the bedroom door. A low whine followed. Wound ignored it. Her stomach felt hollow, not with hunger, but with a strange, unfamiliar ache. A yearning. Lilith. The one from the coast. The ghost wife.

She remembered her. Not as a blur, a phantom, but as a solid presence. She remembered the specific way her hair fell across the pillow, dark against the white. She remembered the curve of her hip against hers. She remembered the faint scent of lavender soap and sea salt that clung to her skin. The memory was so vivid it hurt. More real than the floral sofa. More real than the cold marble in her hand.

She closed her eyes. She tried to call Lilith back. To conjure the soft blue-gray moonlight, the sound of the ocean. She tried to feel her warmth beside her. Nothing. The static was too loud. The broadcast had ended.

But the echo remained. A taste in her mouth, not of eggs this time, but of mint. The ghost of a kiss. The whisper of Lilith’s voice calling her name. Cass. The sound of it resonated in her chest, a deep, mournful chord. No one in this life had ever used that name. It belonged to another Wound. A happier one. A loved one.

She opened her eyes. The living room was exactly as she’d left it. Overstuffed, slightly dusty, filled with the quiet hum of an old refrigerator. The dog was still scratching at the bedroom door. It was a familiar sound, a comforting one in its persistence. But it wasn’t enough to banish the quiet roar of the static in her head.

She picked up the notebook from the coffee table, where she’d tossed it earlier. It fell open to a page she didn’t recognize. Her own handwriting. Neat. Precise. But the words were not hers. Not from this life.

July 17th. Lilith. Dinner at the Anchor. Discussed remodel. She wants the bay window by next spring, before the big one. Said it would be perfect for viewing the migration. Asked if I remembered the old lighthouse. Said she’d show me sometime.

She read it again. The big one. The migration. The old lighthouse. None of it meant anything to her. No dinner at the Anchor. No remodel. She had never seen a bay window in her life. She had never discussed a migration. She knew no old lighthouse.

Yet the words were hers. The ink was dry. The date was yesterday.

She frowned. This wasn’t a bleed-through. This was an insertion. An active interference with her own timeline. The other Wound, the one with the coastal wife had written in her notebook. Had reached across the divide.

A cold dread spread through her. Not physical, not somatic, but a creeping certainty that the lines were blurring. She wasn't just a receiver anymore. She was a canvas. And another hand was painting on it.

She remembered the touch. Lilith’s hand on her arm. The warmth. The clear reality of it. She had felt her. Lilith had felt her. And now, she had this. A tangible piece of another life, written in her own hand.

The dog stopped scratching. Silence fell over the apartment, heavier than before. The refrigerator hummed louder. The gray light seemed to press in on her.

She flipped back through the notebook. Pages filled with her careful logging of the static. The textures, the smells, the voices. All cataloged. All observed. Never authored. Until now.

Her heart thumped a slow, heavy rhythm against her ribs. It wasn’t fear. It was something else. A profound sense of disorientation. Like waking up in a house she thought she knew, only to find a new room, fully furnished, with a stranger’s belongings.

She thought of Lilith. The one from this life. The graphic designer with the sharp wit and the penchant for dark coffee. She remembered her laugh, a bright, clear sound. She remembered the way she’d tilt her head when she was thinking, a small furrow appearing between her brows. Wound had loved that Lilith. Had lost her, years ago, to a mundane accident. A car crash on a Tuesday morning. The one from her memory. The one that was real.

But the Lilith from the coast, the one who called her Cass was real too. Just not in this timeline. And she wanted a bay window. Before the big one. The migration.

It was not the old, familiar ache of losing the graphic designer Lilith, but a fresh, immediate grief for the wife she had never known. The wife who had touched her arm. The wife who had worried about her. The wife who wanted a bay window.

She traced the words with her finger. The big one. What did it mean? A storm? A seasonal event? A calving of icebergs? She knew nothing of coastal life. She had lived her entire life inland, surrounded by brick and concrete. The closest she’d come to an ocean was a documentary on public television.

The marble felt cold in her palm. She squeezed it. A small, physical anchor in a world that was rapidly unmooring.

She stood up. She walked to the window. The street below was quiet. A lone car drove past, its tires hissing on the damp asphalt. The sky was still gray. Unremarkable. This was her reality. This was the steady signal. But the other signal, the one that hummed beneath it was growing stronger. More intrusive.

She looked at the notebook again. The entry, so clear, so specific. She remembered the conversation. She remembered Lilith’s hand in hers. She remembered the taste of the mint. The quiet joy of planning a future event. A bay window. For the migration.

She had lived that moment. She had authored it. And then it had been erased. Or rather, she had been pulled away from it. Back to this. The faded sofa. The insistent hum of the refrigerator. The dog scratching at the bedroom door.

A thought occurred to her: what if the other Wound was also writing in her own notebook? Logging the static of this life? What if she, this Wound was just a phantom memory for another version of herself? A brief, forgotten entry in a journal in a coastal home. July 17th. Slight headache. A strange feeling of being watched. Briefly remembered a drab apartment and a floral sofa. Chalked it up to indigestion.

A mental shiver. The feeling of being the ghost.

She walked back to the sofa. She sat down. The marble was still cold. She placed it on the coffee table, next to the notebook. She stared at the handwritten entry.

The big one.

She picked up her phone. She typed coastal migration into the search bar. She typed old lighthouses, Maine. She typed bay window installation. She was looking for clues. Trying to piece together a life she had lived, but did not remember. A life that was now intruding on her own.

The screen glowed, indifferent. She scrolled through images of whales, seabirds, historic landmarks. None of it felt right. None of it resonated. It was all just data. Not memory. Not feeling.

She looked at the marble. It sat there, inert. A simple glass sphere. It was the only thing that hadn't changed. The only constant.

She closed the phone. She closed her eyes. She tried to remember the graphic designer Lilith. Her face. Her laugh. The way she’d tilt her head. She tried to hold onto her. To anchor herself to the life that was supposed to be hers.

But the image of the coastal Lilith, with her moonlight-silvered cheekbone and her whispered Cass was stronger. More vivid. More immediate. She was the one who had reached for Wound. She was the one who had touched her.

Wound was losing the signal, the one that was supposed to be her own.

She picked up the marble. She held it tight. She looked at the notebook, at the entry she had not written. She felt a deep sense of melancholy. A future she had planned, a future she would never live. A bay window. The migration.

She opened the notebook again, flipping past the unfamiliar entry. She found the last page she had written. Her own words. Her own observations. The logs of the static. The interference. She picked up her pen.

She wrote: Channel: Gold. New manifestation: direct authorship within primary timeline. Content: future event planning (coastal). Emotional state: heavy disorientation, grief. Warning: contamination increasing.

She paused. She added a single word. Lilith.

She underlined it.

She spent the morning at the small table she called her study, the marble a paperweight on the stack of journals. She wasn't looking for ghosts. She was looking for data. The entry about the bay window was an anomaly, a puncture. She needed to map the perimeter of the wound.

She opened the oldest journal, the one from her thirties. The handwriting was tighter, more anxious. She found the first logged phantom: Channel: undefined. Auditory: child's laughter, distant. Duration: 4 seconds. Location: empty park. She flipped forward. Channel: Silver (provisional). Olfactory: pipe tobacco, cherry-scented. Somatic: pressure of eyeglasses on bridge of nose. She had been cataloging symptoms for decades, naming the channels after metals, after colors, giving the static a false order.

She pulled the current notebook closer. She compared entries side by side. The coastal life—Gold—had been the most persistent. It had the most sensory detail: sunburn, salt air, mint, the texture of a summer quilt. It also had the clearest emotional signature: contentment, a low-grade marital warmth. It was a complete life, broadcasting on a strong signal.

The other channels were fragments. A life where she wore eyeglasses and smoked a pipe. A life with a child's laughter in a park. A life that tasted of Tuesday eggs and ended in an ambulance. These were weaker, intermittent.

She started a fresh sheet of paper, drawing a rough timeline of her own life along the top. Below it, she attempted to plot the intrusions of Gold. The sunburn memory at age twelve. The smell of salt air at twenty-four. The first auditory fragment of waves at thirty-one. The pattern was clear: the Gold channel had been strengthening, its intrusions becoming longer, more detailed, more recent. Last night was the peak. Not a memory, but a live feed. Mutual awareness.

She leaned back. The chair groaned. The logical conclusion was that the Gold timeline was somehow winning. Its signal was overriding the others, becoming dominant. It made a terrible kind of sense. A happy life would be a stronger broadcast than a sad one. A life filled with love would resonate more powerfully than a life of quiet isolation.

But the egg channel, the one that killed her was a strong signal too, raw and traumatic. It should have been loud. Yet it only appeared as taste, as somatic rejection. It never offered a scene, a moment of living. Just the death.

The timelines weren't just parallel. They were convergent. And they were hostile. Her documentation wasn't a map. It was a target. And her anchor had just turned cold.
