The street was loud in the right ways. Car horns, a distant siren, the rhythmic thump of bass from a passing sedan with its windows down. Cassius walked with his hands in his pockets, his left one curled around the marble. The sun was a low, warm pressure on the back of his neck. He’d come out for coffee. A simple thing.

He approached the cart on the corner. The vendor, a man with a gray mustache and a faded baseball cap was handing a pretzel to a woman in a blue coat. Cassius waited. The woman took her change and walked away.

The vendor wiped his hands on his apron and looked past Cassius, scanning the sidewalk for the next customer.

“Just a black coffee, please,” Cassius said.

The man didn’t turn. He lifted a thermos, poured a cup for himself, and took a sip. He stared at the traffic light across the street.

Cassius cleared his throat. The sound was lost in the city’s drone. “Excuse me. Coffee?”

Nothing. The vendor shifted his weight, humming a tuneless snippet of something. His eyes slid over Cassius’s shoulder, tracking a cyclist weaving through cars.

A cold prickle started at the base of Cassius’s skull. He took half a step closer to the cart. “Hello?”

The man’s gaze drifted. It passed over Cassius’s face, over his chest, and fixed on a point somewhere behind him. There was no recognition in it, no flicker of annoyance at a customer being too quiet. It was the look you gave a lamppost. A fire hydrant. Empty space.

Cassius raised his voice. “I’d like to buy a coffee.”

A teenager in headphones bumped into Cassius’s shoulder, muttered “Sorry,” and kept walking without breaking stride. He hadn’t looked at Cassius either. The impact was glancing, impersonal, like he’d brushed a trash can.

The vendor turned and began rearranging napkin dispensers.

The cold in Cassius’s skull spread down his spine. He uncurled his fist from around the marble and placed his hand flat on the stainless-steel counter of the cart. The metal was slick with condensation. “Can you see me?”

His own voice sounded thin. Brittle.

The vendor’s hand, holding a rag, passed within inches of Cassius’s fingers. He wiped the counter in a slow, circular motion, working from the far end toward Cassius’s hand. He didn’t pause. He didn’t look down. The rag swiped right over Cassius’s knuckles. The touch was dry, rough, and utterly unintentional. The man was cleaning the counter. The counter had an obstacle on it. He cleaned around it.

Cassius snatched his hand back. He stared at his own fingers. They looked solid. Real.

He took a step backward, then another. The vendor never glanced up. A young couple approached the cart, laughing about something. The man with the mustache smiled at them, a real smile that crinkled the corners of his eyes. “What can I get you folks?”

Cassius stood three feet away. They didn’t look at him. The woman’s shoulder bag swung and would have hit his arm if he hadn’t jerked aside. They ordered two sodas paid, took their drinks, and walked right past him, their conversation a bright, continuous bubble that absorbed no hint of his presence.

He was a stone in a stream. The water parted and flowed around him, leaving no mark.

His heart was doing something wrong. A tight, fluttering rhythm high in his chest, not a pound. He turned and walked. His legs felt distant, like he was operating them by pulling strings. He focused on the sidewalk cracks. One after another. The marble was a hard, warm moon in his palm.

A man walking a dog came toward him. Cassius moved to the right, standard sidewalk etiquette. The man didn’t adjust. He kept a straight course, the dog sniffing at a planter. Cassius had to stop short, his shoe scuffing the concrete. The man and the dog passed so close he felt the breeze of their movement. The dog, a terrier mix didn’t lift its head. Animals always noticed him. Stray cats would follow him for blocks. Pigeons would startle if he got too close. This dog just trotted by, leash slack.

He reached the crosswalk. The walk signal was a white, illuminated man. Cassius stepped off the curb with a small crowd of people waiting for the light. They all moved together, a loose organism. He was in the middle of them. He could smell perfume, sweat, the wax on someone’s jacket. A woman to his left checked her phone. A man on his right adjusted the strap of a messenger bag.

Then the crowd reached the opposite curb. It dissipated, people peeling away in different directions. Cassius stopped. They flowed around him. A man in a suit walked straight at him, eyes on his own phone, and Cassius had to sidestep again to avoid a collision. The suit didn’t look up. Didn’t flinch. Just kept walking, thumbs tapping the screen.

It was a different kind of silence. Not an absence of sound, but an absence of echo. He made no ripple. He cast no shadow in the social world.

A trash can stood by a bus stop bench. Cassius walked to it and leaned against the cool metal. He needed to breathe. He needed to think this through. A system glitch. A temporary thing. He’d been tired lately. Maybe his voice was softer than he realized. Maybe the vendor was hard of hearing. The dog was distracted. This crowd was busy.

He made himself look at his reflection in the darkened window of a closed bookstore next to the bus stop. The glass was streaked with grime. His reflection was a smudge of color—the beige of his jacket, the gray of his hair, the pale oval of his face. He looked like himself. An old man. Tired. But there. Solid.

He lifted a hand. The reflection lifted a hand.

From the corner of his eye, light flickered. He turned.

A digital billboard towered over the intersection, mounted on the side of a brick building. It cycled through advertisements. A car on a mountain road. A soda can beading with condensation. A smiling family holding phones with a specific cellular carrier’s logo.

The family ad dissolved into a public service announcement. A blue background, white text: HAVE YOU SEEN THIS MAN? The text was standard. Below it, a photograph began to pixelate into existence.

It was a black-and-white photo, slightly grainy, like a yearbook picture or a driver’s license photo. The face was younger. Thicker hair. Fewer lines. The eyes were direct, a little wary.

It was his face. His own. From maybe thirty years ago.

Cassius’s breath locked in his throat.

The image held for two seconds. Three. The text underneath flickered, the letters scrambling into nonsense: HAV YUO SEENT HIS AMN.

Then the whole board stuttered. The blue background flashed to black. The image of his younger self fractured into a thousand dancing pixels, a blizzard of white and gray. It collapsed in on itself and was gone.

The billboard went dark for a heartbeat. Then the car ad reappeared. A car sped soundlessly along the mountain road, tires kicking up a plume of golden dust.

Cassius stared. The tight flutter in his chest was gone, replaced by a hollow, airless void. He looked down at his hand, still clenched around the marble. He forced his fingers open.

The glass sphere lay in his palm. The blue ribbon swam in its center, vivid, clear. He closed his fingers over it again, squeezing until the edges bit into his skin.

Proof.

He looked back at the street. The people. The traffic. The world continued, bright and noisy and absolute. He was in it. He could feel the grit under his shoes. He could smell the exhaust, the distant grease from the pretzel cart. He was here.

But the world, it seemed was no longer entirely sure of that fact.

He pushed himself away from the trash can. He didn’t know where he was walking. He just moved, putting one foot in front of the other, his shoulder blades prickling as if expecting a shove that never came. He kept to the edge of the sidewalk, close to the buildings. A man exiting a shop held the door open, looking past Cassius, holding it for the empty space behind him. Cassius caught the door before it swung shut and slipped inside. The man was already walking away.

The shop was a pharmacy. Fluorescent lights hummed overhead. The air smelled of antiseptic and floor wax. He went to the counter at the back. A pharmacist in a white coat was typing at a computer. A woman in line ahead of Cassius picked up a prescription, chatted about the weather, and left.

Cassius stepped up. The pharmacist finished typing, looked up, and gazed at the empty space to Cassius’s left. He blinked, shook his head slightly, and turned to a shelf of pill bottles.

“I need to check something,” Cassius said, his voice too loud in the quiet store.

The pharmacist didn’t react. He counted pills into a vial.

Cassius reached into his jacket pocket and pulled out his wallet. He extracted his health insurance card, a faded plastic rectangle, and slid it across the counter. It scraped on the laminate.

The pharmacist’s eyes dropped to the card. He stared at it. His brow furrowed. He picked it up turned it over, and looked at the back. He held it to the light, then swiped it through a reader attached to his keyboard. He watched the screen. His frown deepened.

He tapped a few keys. Waited. Shook his head.

“Huh,” he muttered to himself. He swiped the card again. More typing. A longer wait. He sighed, a sound of professional annoyance. He tossed the card back onto the counter. It landed where Cassius had placed it.

“System’s glitching,” the pharmacist said to no one. “File’s not there.”

He turned back to his pill bottles, dismissing the empty counter, the ghost card, the unresolved question.

Cassius stood very still. He looked at his insurance card lying on the polished surface. He looked at the pharmacist’s back. The hum of the lights was the only sound.

Slowly, he picked up the card. He put it back in his wallet. He put his wallet in his pocket. His fingers found the marble again. They were trembling.

He walked out of the pharmacy. The door chimed a cheerful goodbye.

Outside, the afternoon light was slanting, long and gold. The city gleamed. Cassius stood on the sidewalk, a man of flesh and bone and memory, holding a piece of glass that weighed nothing and everything. People streamed around him. Cars passed. The billboard across the way showed a new ad for a bank.

He was transparent. A smudge on a lens. A file marked for deletion.

He started walking home. He didn’t hurry. There was no point. The panic was a cold, still thing in his stomach now settled and heavy. He watched his feet move. He counted his breaths. He held the marble so tight he thought it might fuse to his skin.

A child on a scooter veered toward him, laughing, looking over her shoulder at a friend. Cassius didn’t move. The scooter’s front wheel passed through the space his foot had occupied a half-second before. The girl didn’t notice. She kicked off again, her laughter trailing behind her like a ribbon.

Cassius kept walking. The sun was in his eyes. He didn’t blink.

The waiting room smelled of lemon disinfectant and worn magazines. Cassius sat in a vinyl chair that sighed under his weight. A television mounted in the corner played a morning show on mute, the hosts’ smiles too wide and constant. He’d called ahead. Ten o’clock, routine check-up, blood pressure, the usual conversation about his cholesterol. He’d given his name at the window. The receptionist, a young woman with a high ponytail had typed, nodded, and handed him a clipboard without meeting his eyes. He’d assumed she was busy.

Now he wasn’t sure.

He filled out the form. Name. Date of birth. Current medications. He wrote slowly, his handwriting a shaky version of what it used to be. He listed his allergies. He noted his last tetanus shot. He held the marble in his left hand under the clipboard, rolling it against his palm.

“Cassius?” A nurse stood at the open door to the inner offices. She held a tablet. She scanned the room, her gaze passing over his chair, then swinging back. She smiled. It didn’t reach her eyes. “Right this way.”

He followed her down a short hallway. The walls were pale green. A framed print of a sailboat hung slightly crooked. The nurse’s shoes made soft, squeaking sounds on the linoleum. She led him into an examination room.

“Step on the scale for me, please.”

He did. The digital numbers flickered settled. She tapped the tablet. “Blood pressure next.” She wrapped the cuff around his arm. It inflated with a familiar, tight hug. She watched the gauge, her expression neutral. She noted the numbers. “The doctor will be with you shortly. You can have a seat on the table.”

She left, closing the door behind her. The squeak of her shoes faded.

Cassius sat on the edge of the examination table. The paper cover crinkled under him. The room was cold. A diagram of the human heart, cross-sectioned and labeled in cheerful colors was taped to the wall opposite. He focused on the marble, its solid heat. He counted the tiles on the floor. Fourteen across, nine down. One near the baseboard was chipped.

The door opened. Dr. Evans entered, a man in his late fifties with thinning hair and wire-rimmed glasses. He carried a thick paper file, not a tablet. He smiled, a professional, practiced curve of the mouth. “Cassius. Good to see you.” He didn’t offer a hand. He sat on the rolling stool and opened the file on the small counter. “Let’s see how you’ve been.”

Cassius watched him read. Dr. Evans’s smile faded. His forehead creased. He flipped a page, then flipped back. He adjusted his glasses.

“Hmm.”

“Something wrong?”

Dr. Evans didn’t look up. “Just reviewing your history.” He turned another page. He stopped. His finger tapped a line of text. “Says here you had a full hip replacement. 2018.”

Cassius blinked. “No. I’ve never had surgery.”

“Right.” Dr. Evans’s voice was absent, dismissive. He kept reading. “And you’re on... let’s see... insulin therapy? For type one diabetes, diagnosed 2005.”

“I don’t have diabetes.” Cassius’s own voice sounded flat. Hollow.

“Mmm.” The doctor turned another page. His movements were slower now, more deliberate. “Allergy to penicillin. Severe. Anaphylaxis noted in 1992.”

“I’m not allergic to penicillin. I took it for a sinus infection two years ago. You prescribed it.”

Dr. Evans finally looked up. His eyes were polite, confused. “I did?”

“Yes.”

The doctor looked back at the file. He shook his head, a tiny motion. “That’s not what your record indicates. It’s quite detailed.” He closed the folder. The sound was final. “Well. Your vitals today are good. Blood pressure’s a touch high, but given the discrepancies, that’s understandable.”

“Discrepancies.”

“Between what you remember and what’s documented.” Dr. Evans wheeled a little closer. His tone shifted, softening into something Cassius recognized. It was the voice people used for the confused. The forgetful. “It happens sometimes. Memory can be tricky. The file is quite thorough. We have to trust the documentation.”

Cassius felt a hot, sharp pressure behind his eyes. Not tears. Something angrier. “That is not my file.”

“It has your name. Your birth date. Your insurance ID.” Dr. Evans spread his hands. “It’s you.”

“It says I have a metal hip. Do I limp?”

“You could have adapted.”

“Test my blood sugar. Right now.”

The doctor’s patience thinned. A tightness appeared around his mouth. “That’s not necessary for a routine visit. The history is clear.”

Cassius stood. “No, it isn’t.”

Dr. Evans stood as well. He was taller. He looked down at the file, then at Cassius. His confusion was being replaced by professional concern. The kind you note in a chart. “Cassius. I have your file. This is what we have. Perhaps... perhaps we should consider a referral. A cognitive evaluation.”

“I am not demented.” The words came out too loud, cracking on the last syllable. He fumbled in his pocket, pulled out his wallet, yanked out the insurance card. He thrust it at the doctor. “Scan it. Pull up the real file. The one from last year. The one from six months ago.”

Dr. Evans took the card. Looked at it, then at Cassius. He sighed. He walked to the computer on the counter, woke the monitor, and swiped the card. He typed. He waited.

Cassius watched his profile. The doctor’s eyes scanned the screen. His fingers paused on the keyboard. He typed something else. He leaned in. He scrolled. He went still.

“That’s odd.”

“What?”

“The system... it’s showing the same file. The one I have.” Dr. Evans turned the monitor slightly. Cassius saw columns of text, medical abbreviations. “But the accession log... it says this file was created this morning. 9:47 a.m.” He looked at Cassius, his professional calm now frayed with genuine puzzlement. “That’s not possible. You’ve been a patient here for over a decade. Your old files should merge. They don’t just... start fresh.”

Cassius said nothing. The cold, heavy thing in his stomach turned over.

Dr. Evans swiveled back to the computer. He clicked rapidly, opened different screens. “There’s no merge history. No archived data. It’s like... like you were entered into the system for the first time today. But that’s...” He trailed off. He looked from the screen to the paper file in his hand, as if comparing two incompatible truths. He shook his head again, harder. “There must be a system migration error. A glitch in the new software. I’ll have IT look into it.”

He said it to himself, not to Cassius.

He closed the computer window. He handed the insurance card back. His touch was quick. “We’ll use today’s vitals as a baseline. I’ll note the historical... inconsistencies. We’ll sort it out. For now, just keep doing what you’re doing. Your physical exam is fine.” He was moving toward the door, the paper file tucked under his arm. He was ending the visit. “Schedule a follow-up in six months. The receptionist will help you.”

He was gone. The door clicked shut.

Cassius stood in the silent, cold room. The diagram of the heart smiled at him. The chipped tile near the baseboard seemed larger now, a little mouth in the floor. He put his insurance card away. He picked up his jacket from the hook. He moved slowly, each action requiring separate, conscious thought.

He walked back down the green hallway. The sailboat was still crooked. The waiting room was emptier now. The television showed a weather map, swirling masses of blue and green.

He approached the reception window. The young woman with the ponytail was typing. He waited.

She didn’t look up.

“I need to schedule a follow-up,” he said.

Her fingers kept flying across the keys. Click-clack.

“Excuse me.”

Nothing. She took a sip from a water bottle, her eyes never leaving her screen.

Cassius turned and walked to the exit. He pushed the heavy door open. The outside air was a slap, warmer, smelling of cut grass from a nearby park. He stood on the concrete step, blinking in the sunlight.

From behind him, inside, a voice called out, clear and bright cutting through the lobby.

“Mr. Thompson? You forgot your umbrella!”

He froze. The voice didn’t call again. He looked over his shoulder, through the glass door. The receptionist was leaning over her counter, looking toward the empty chairs. She shrugged, then sat back down.

Cassius looked down at his empty hands. He had never owned an umbrella. He hated the way they collapsed in the wind. He always wore a hat.

Thompson.

The name sat in his head, a dead weight. A label for an empty chair. A file created at 9:47 that morning.

He walked down the steps to the sidewalk. He didn’t look back. The sun was high now, bleaching the color from everything. He put his hand in his pocket. His fingers closed around the marble. They were cold. Its glass was warm.

He walked. He didn’t know where he was going. Home was a concept that felt thin, theoretical. A place for a man whose file might not exist. He walked past shops, past cafes. His reflection glided over dark windows, a smudged phantom keeping pace.

A bus roared by, belching exhaust. The gust of air ruffled his hair. It felt real. The grit on his teeth was real. The ache in his knees was real.

But he was starting to understand. Reality was not a negotiation. It was a broadcast. And his signal was fading, replaced by static and by a name that wasn’t his, called out to an empty chair.

The key turned in the lock with a sound like breaking ice. Cassius pushed the door open and stood on the threshold. His apartment lay before him, a still life of a life. The same lamp with the crooked shade. The same rug, worn thin in a path from the door to the kitchen. The same smell of dust and old books and, faintly, the cabbage he’d boiled for lunch two days ago. It was all the same. It felt like a museum exhibit of a place called Home.

He took off his jacket and hung it on the hook by the door. He did it slowly, feeling the weight of the wool, the texture of the lining. He walked to the kitchen. The linoleum was cool under his socks. He filled a glass with water from the tap, drank it all. The water tasted of minerals and pipes. He set the glass in the sink. It clinked against a waiting spoon.

Prove it.

The thought was quiet, insistent. He went to the living room. His bookshelf stood against the wall, shelves bowing slightly under the weight. He ran a finger along the spines. He knew them by touch. The rough cloth of the old history text. The slick, peeling laminate of a paperback thriller. He pulled out a volume at random. The Collected Poems of W.B. Yeats. He opened it. His name was written on the flyleaf in blue ink, the handwriting of his thirty-year-old self, all sharp angles and confidence. Cassius. He traced the letters. The ink was slightly raised. He remembered buying the book from a sidewalk stall, the vendor with a parrot on his shoulder. He remembered the rain starting just as he handed over the cash. The memory was sharp, complete. He put the book back.

He went to the bedroom. The bed was made, the quilt smooth. On the nightstand: a reading lamp, a pair of wire-framed glasses, a novel he’d been halfway through for a month. He opened the top drawer. Socks, folded. A leather box. He lifted the box out set it on the quilt. Inside were the things that had no other place. A medal from a long-ago race he’d run in college. A photograph of his father, young and squinting at the sun. A broken watch. He picked up the photograph. The paper felt light, insubstantial. His father’s face was familiar, but the connection felt thin, like a radio signal from a distant star, full of static. He put it back.

He closed the box. He placed the marble on top of it. He sat in the circle of light, in the silent apartment that felt less like a shelter and more like a fading photograph, and listened to the soft, formless whisper of a world forgetting him.
