The diner smelled like old fryer grease and weak coffee. Cassius traced a finger along the chipped Formica edge of the table, the texture catching on his skin. Across from him, Miller stirred three sugars into his coffee, the spoon clinking against the ceramic in a rhythm that matched the flickering fluorescent light overhead.

“You remember that time Damien tried to ride his bike down the stairs?” Cassius said. He kept his voice casual, like he was asking about the weather.

Miller stopped stirring. The spoon hovered. “Who?”

“Damien. My brother.”

Miller set the spoon down carefully on the napkin. The coffee had gone a shade too dark from the sugar. “You don't have a brother.”

The words landed like a dropped glass—sharp, sudden, impossible to ignore. Cassius's left hand twitched, just once, a tiny spasm against the table. He pressed his palm flat to stop it.

“Yeah, I do. Younger. Blond. Had this scar on his chin from when he wiped out on his bike.” He could see it now—the way Damien had grinned through bloody teeth, proud of the wreck.

Miller's brow furrowed. He leaned back in the booth, the vinyl creaking under his weight. “Cass. You're an only child. Always have been.”

The air in the diner thickened. Cassius could hear the fryer hissing in the kitchen, the low murmur of a radio behind the counter. He picked up his own coffee, but the smell was suddenly wrong—too bitter, like something burnt underneath.

“I have pictures,” he said.

“Of what?”

“Of him.”

Miller exhaled through his nose, slow and deliberate. “Okay. Show me.”

Cassius pulled out his phone, swiped to the album labeled “Family.” Scrolled past his parents, past holidays, past the blurry shots of his childhood bedroom. Nothing. He tapped back, checked again. The photos were all there—same as always—but Damien wasn't in any of them. Not even the edges.

His thumb hovered over the screen. The tremor was back, faint but obvious, vibrating through his fingers.

“Maybe I—” His voice cracked. He cleared his throat. “Maybe I left them at home.”

Miller watched him. Not like he was worried. Like he was waiting for the punchline.

“Cassius,” he said finally. “You good?”

“Yeah. Fine.”

“You're sweating.”

Cassius wiped his forehead. His skin was damp, cold. “It's hot in here.”

Miller glanced at the door, where a draft slipped in every time someone entered. Didn't call Cassius out. Just took another sip of his coffee.

The waitress refilled their waters. Cassius stared at the ice cubes, the way they melted unevenly, the cracks forming under the pressure of the liquid.

“Your mom's still in the same house, right?” Miller said.

“Yeah.”

“Maybe she's got the pictures.”

Cassius nodded, but the motion felt mechanical. His mother's face flashed in his mind—her expression when he'd mentioned Damien at dinner. That flat, absolute denial. The way she'd looked at him like he was a stranger.

Miller tapped the table. “Hey. You're zoning out.”

“Sorry.” Cassius forced a smile. “Just thinking.”

“About?”

“Nothing.”

Miller sighed. “Look, if you're stressed about work or whatever—”

“It's not work.”

“Then what?”

Cassius opened his mouth. Closed it. The words tangled in his throat, knotted too tight to pull free. He could say it—could force it out—but Miller's face was already set in that patient, pitying look people wore when they thought you were losing it.

“Never mind,” he said.

The check came. Miller grabbed it before Cassius could, tossing cash onto the table. “Come on. Let's get some air.”

Outside, the cold bit through Cassius's jacket. The sky was gray, the kind of overcast that made everything feel muted. Miller lit a cigarette, the smoke curling up into the flat light.

“You sure you're okay?” he asked.

Cassius flexed his left hand willing the tremor to stay gone. “Yeah.”

Miller took a drag, exhaled slowly. “You'd tell me if something was up, right?”

“Sure.”

The lie sat between them, heavy as a stone. Miller didn't push. Just nodded, like he'd expected it.

Cassius shoved his hands into his pockets. His fingers brushed against something small, smooth. He pulled it out, a clear marble with a single blue swirl. He didn't remember putting it there.

Miller glanced at it. “What's that?”

“Nothing.” Cassius closed his fist around it. The glass was cold against his palm. “Just something I found.”

Miller shrugged. “You're weird today.”

They stood there for a minute, the silence stretching thin. Finally, Miller stubbed out his cigarette. “I gotta head back. You good?”

“I'm good.”

Miller clapped him on the shoulder, a quick, firm pressure. “Call me if you need anything.”

Cassius watched him walk away, his footsteps echoing on the pavement. The marble weighed nothing in his hand, but it felt like the only real thing left.

He knew where he had to go next.

The drive to his mother's house was too quiet. The radio static hissed, a low, constant whisper. Cassius turned it off. The silence was worse.

He parked in the driveway, the gravel crunching under his tires. The house looked the same as always, white siding, black shutters, the oak tree in the front yard swaying slightly in the wind. Normal. Solid.

Cassius gripped the steering wheel. His hands were steady now.

He got out of the car.

The front door was unlocked.

The living room smelled like lemon polish and the faint mustiness of old books. His mother's knitting basket sat by the armchair, a half-finished scarf spilling out in shades of navy and cream. Cassius stood just inside the doorway, his fingers flexing at his sides.

“You're early,” she said, looking up from her book. The lamp cast warm light over her face, softening the lines around her eyes. “I thought you were seeing Miller tonight.”

“We did. I just—” He swallowed. “I needed to ask you something.”

She marked her page with a finger and set the book aside. “Alright.”

The couch creaked under him as he sat. He could see the photo albums on the shelf behind her, their spines worn from years of handling. He'd looked through them a hundred times. A thousand. He knew every picture by heart.

“Do you remember Damien?”

The words hung in the air. His mother blinked.

“Who?”

“Damien.” His throat tightened. “My brother.”

For a second, she just stared at him. Then her expression shifted—not confusion, not anger. Concern. The kind that made his stomach drop.

“Cassius,” she said gently. “You don't have a brother.”

The room tilted. He gripped the edge of the couch, the fabric rough under his fingers. “Yes, I do. Younger than me. Blond. He had this scar—” He touched his own chin. “Right here. From when he tried to ride his bike down the stairs.”

His mother leaned forward, her brow creasing. “Honey, you're an only child. You always have been.”

“No.” The word came out too sharp. He forced his voice lower. “No, he was real. He shared my room. We had bunk beds.”

She reached for his hand. Her skin was warm, familiar. “Cassius, look at me.”

He did. Her eyes were clear, certain. No flicker of recognition. No hesitation.

“There was never a Damien,” she said.

The air left his lungs. He pulled his hand back. “You're lying.”

“Why would I lie about that?”

“I don't know.” His pulse was too loud in his ears. “But he was there.”

He could see it. The blue Victorian house. The cedar porch. Damien's laugh, loud and unguarded, as he balanced on the railing. The way the wood had splintered under his weight when he fell.

His mother's voice cut through the memory. “Cassius.”

He looked up.

“You've been under a lot of stress,” she said. “Work, the move, all of it. Maybe you should talk to someone.”

The words were careful. Kind. They made his skin crawl.

“I'm not making this up.”

“I didn't say you were.” She hesitated. “But sometimes our minds… fill in gaps. Especially when we're lonely.”

He stood abruptly. The photo albums called to him, a silent plea. He crossed the room in three strides, yanked the first one off the shelf.

“Here,” he said, flipping pages. “Look. Right here.”

The photos were exactly as he remembered. His parents holding him as a baby. Birthday parties. Vacations. Him, always him, alone. No second child. No Damien.

His hands shook. He turned another page. Another. Nothing.

“Cassius.” His mother's voice was soft behind him. “Please.”

He slammed the album shut. The sound echoed.

“You're gaslighting me.”

The word felt clumsy in his mouth, too dramatic, but he couldn't think of another one.

His mother's face crumpled. “I would never do that.”

“Then where is he?”

“There is no ‘he.’”

The certainty in her voice was a knife. He stared at her, searching for any sign of doubt, any flicker of uncertainty. There was none.

The room was too small suddenly. The walls too close. He needed air.

“I have to go.”

“Wait.” She stood, catching his arm. “Stay. Let's talk about this.”

Her touch burned. He pulled away. “There's nothing to talk about.”

“Cassius—”

“I'm fine.”

He wasn't. His vision blurred at the edges. The marble was still in his pocket, a cold, solid weight. Proof. Of what, he didn't know.

His mother's hand hovered between them, like she wanted to reach for him again but didn't dare. “You're scaring me.”

Good, he almost said. The thought shocked him. He bit it back.

“I just need some time,” he managed.

She didn't try to stop him as he walked out. The door clicked shut behind him, too quiet for the way it felt, like a seal, like a verdict.

The driveway gravel crunched under his shoes. The oak tree swayed, its branches scraping against the siding. Normal sounds. Normal things.

He fumbled for his keys, his fingers clumsy. The car door slammed louder than he meant it to.

The engine roared to life. He didn't look back.

The road stretched ahead, dark and empty. The radio hissed static again, a low, insistent whisper. He turned it off. The silence was worse.

His phone buzzed in his pocket. Miller, probably. He ignored it.

The marble was still in his hand. He didn't remember taking it out. The blue swirl caught the streetlights, glinting like an eye.

He tightened his grip.

There had to be proof somewhere. A hospital record. A school photo. Something.

He just had to find it.

The streetlights flickered as he drove. His reflection in the rearview mirror looked hollow, his eyes too dark.

He didn't recognize himself.

That thought came unbidden, unwelcome. He shoved it down.

Focus.

Damien was real. He was.

Cassius just had to prove it.

The apartment smelled like stale coffee and the faint metallic tang of the radiator hissing in the corner. Cassius kicked the door shut behind him, the deadbolt clicking into place with a sound too final for the chaos in his head. He dropped his keys on the counter. They skidded across the laminate, one landing in the sink with a dull clink.

His desk was buried under stacks of paper, bills, half-finished work reports, a grocery list from three weeks ago. He swept it all aside with one arm, sending a pen rolling off the edge. It hit the floor and vanished under the couch.

The notebook was where he'd left it, wedged between two psychology textbooks he'd bought on a whim last year and never opened. Black cover, college-ruled pages, the spine cracked from being carried in his back pocket too many times. He flipped it open to the first blank page. The paper was slightly yellowed at the edges.

He uncapped a pen. Pressed the tip to the page. Stopped.

What was he supposed to write?

Damien is real.

The words looked childish. Desperate. He scratched them out, the ink bleeding into the paper.

The radiator coughed, a wet, rattling sound. Outside, a car alarm warbled for a few seconds before cutting off abruptly. Cassius breathed out through his nose, slow and controlled. His left hand twitched against the desk. He flattened it, pressed his palm down until the tremor stilled.

He started again.

Timeline Discrepancies

Better. Clinical. Like he was documenting a case study instead of his own crumbling sanity.

1. Damien. Younger brother. Blond. Scar on chin from bicycle accident (1994). Shared bedroom in blue Victorian house (current address: 2217 Maple, now a community garden).

His handwriting was too neat, the letters cramped together like he was afraid the page would run out of space. He forced himself to slow down.

2. Mother's denial. No photos, no records. Claims I'm an only child.

He turned the page.

Evidence to Collect:

- School records (check elementary yearbooks)
- Hospital visits (bike accident, stitches)
- Neighbors' accounts
- Property records for 2217 Maple

the list grew, each item a lifeline. If he could find just one—just one piece of proof, then he wasn't losing it. Then he wasn't alone.

His phone buzzed on the desk. Miller's name flashed on the screen. Cassius ignored it. The screen went dark.

The marble was still in his pocket. He pulled it out and set it on the desk beside the notebook. The blue swirl caught the light from his desk lamp, a tiny, perfect spiral. He didn't remember where it came from. That should have bothered him more than it did.

He picked it up, rolled it between his fingers. The glass was cool, smooth. Familiar in a way he couldn't place.

The radiator snapped again. Cassius set the marble down and reached for his laptop. The screen flared to life, casting a blue glow over his hands. He typed 2217 Maple into the search bar.

The results loaded. A satellite image of a vacant lot overgrown with weeds. A rusted chain-link fence. No house. No porch. No trace of the blue Victorian that lived so vividly in his memory.

His throat tightened. He clicked on the property records.

Current Owner: City Parks & Recreation
Previous Use: Residential (demolished 1998)

1998. He would have been twenty-three.

He stopped.

There was no Damien.

The thought slithered in, unwelcome. He shoved it aside, opened a new tab. Searched for his elementary school's yearbook archives.

The site loaded slowly. He drummed his fingers on the desk, the rhythm uneven. His left hand twitched again.

Fifth grade. His class photo. He scanned the rows of faces, the too-wide smiles, the awkward haircuts. There he was, front row, second from the left. His stomach lurched.

No Damien.

He clicked through the grades. Sixth. Seventh. Eighth. Nothing.

The room was too warm suddenly. He yanked his sweater over his head, tossed it onto the couch. The fabric caught on the edge and slid to the floor in a heap.

He tried the hospital next. Called the records department, waited through the automated menu. A woman's voice, crisp and professional, answered on the third ring.

“I'm looking for records of a childhood injury,” he said. “My brother. Damien Cassius. Bicycle accident, mid-nineties. Probably needed stitches.”

A pause. Keyboard clicks.

“I'm not finding anything under that name.”

“Try just Cassius. Maybe they filed it under mine.”

More clicks. A longer pause.

“I'm sorry, sir. There's no record of any bicycle-related injuries for a Cassius in that timeframe.”

His grip on the phone tightened. “You're sure?”

“Yes, sir.”

He hung up. The phone slipped from his fingers, landed face-down on the desk.

The notebook taunted him. All those neat, careful lists. All that nothing.

He grabbed the pen again.

3. No records found. No yearbook photos, no property records, no medical history.

The words blurred. He blinked. His vision cleared.

Possible explanations:

- Mass gaslighting (unlikely)
- False memories (source?)
- Alternate timeline bleed-through (???)

The last one looked insane. He almost crossed it out. Left it.

The marble glinted at him. He picked it up held it to the light. The blue swirl twisted, endless.

He didn't remember buying it. Didn't remember finding it. It had just appeared. Like Damien had vanished.

His phone buzzed again. Miller. Again. Cassius flipped it over, silenced it.

The clock on his laptop read 1:17 AM. The street outside was quiet now, the occasional car passing in a slow, distant hum. He should sleep. He knew he wouldn't.

He turned back to the notebook.

Next steps:

- Interview neighbors from childhood (who's left?)
- Check local newspaper archives (bike accident?)

The last line felt like surrender. He closed the notebook set the pen down. His left hand trembled against the desk, a steady, insistent thrum.

He didn't know what he was documenting yet. Only that if he didn't write it down, it might vanish entirely, leaving him utterly alone in a world that remembered nothing.
