The pen hovered over the blank page, the cheap motel notepad he’d bought for this purpose. The apartment smelled of old coffee and the faint, persistent ghost of lemony floor cleaner. He’d opened every curtain. The late afternoon sun cut a sharp, dusty rectangle across the table, illuminating the empty lines.

He wrote at the top: Core Self – Preferences.

The first entry was supposed to be easy. Coffee: black.

His hand twitched. The scent of steamed milk, sweet and rich, flooded his sinuses. The phantom warmth of a ceramic mug pressed against his palm, a different shape, heavier. A voice, not his, murmured in his inner ear: extra shot of vanilla, light foam. He blinked. The sunlight on the paper was just sunlight. His own mug sat to the left, cold, the dregs oily and black.

He scratched out black. He wrote with cream. A sour taste coated his tongue, the aftertaste of cheap powdered creamer from a roadside diner that existed in a state he’d never visited. He crossed that out too. He left the line blank.

Music. He wrote jazz, instrumental.

A driving bassline thumped behind his sternum. Guitar chords, distorted and angry rang in the hollows of his teeth. He saw a callus on his left index finger felt the phantom ache of a guitar strap digging into his shoulder. A memory carried the smell of beer and sweat, a sticky floor. He pressed the heel of his hand against his temple until the noise receded to a tinny echo. He didn’t cross it out. He drew a question mark next to it. One question mark looked pathetic.

Favorite season: autumn.

This one held for a full three seconds. Crisp air, the smell of leaf mold and woodsmoke. Then a wave of humid, oppressive heat pressed down on him. The blinding white light of a beach at noon seared his vision. A craving for the salt-sting of ocean water, for the relief of diving into a cool pool. His skin prickled with the memory of sunburn. He dropped the pen. It rolled to the edge of the table, teetered fell to the floor.

He didn’t pick it up. He stared at his hands. They were his hands. Age spots, a small scar on the knuckle from a long-forgotten cut. But the muscle memory in them was a lie. One set of nerves remembered splitting firewood in a chill October dusk. Another remembered the precise tension needed to tune a guitar’s high E string. A third recalled the gentle pressure of troweling mortar between bricks on a sun-baked wall.

He wasn’t a person. He was a shelf.

The realization arrived not as a shock, but as a dull, cold settling in his gut. A library shelf where different librarians kept placing books by different authors. The books didn’t belong together. They argued in the silence. A biography of a quiet academic sat next to a pulp novel about a rock guitarist, next to a technical manual for masonry, next to a romance novel set on a coast. They were all checked out to the same card. His skull was the return chute.

He got up. His knees popped. The floorboards under his feet were just floorboards. He walked to the kitchen, filled a glass with tap water. He drank it, looking out the window at the brick wall of the adjacent building. A pigeon landed on a rusted fire escape, pecked at nothing, flew off.

Back at the table, he turned to a fresh page. He wrote: Core Self – Memories.

Earliest memory:

His mind served up a slideshow. A red ball rolling across a green lawn. The rough texture of his grandfather’s wool cardigan against his cheek. The taste of a specific brand of orange sherbet from a small plastic spoon. The sting of iodine on a skinned knee, his mother’s voice humming a tuneless song. The dizzying height of an oak tree he’d climbed, the world swaying.

Which one was his? The lawn was from a house his parents never owned. The grandfather died before he was born. He’d never liked sherbet. He was afraid of heights.

He closed his eyes. He tried to force it, to dig underneath the clutter. He visualized a shovel breaking through layers of sedimentary rock—childhood, adolescence, adulthood. Each layer was compressed fragments from different places. He dug until the mental shovel hit something hard and unyielding. Not a memory. A void. A blank spot where the foundation should be.

His earliest verifiable memory was from a school photograph in second grade. He could describe the itchy sweater he wore. But the emotional content—the feeling of standing there under the bright lights—was borrowed. It felt like watching a film of someone else’s life, with his own face poorly superimposed.

He opened his eyes. The page was still blank under the heading.

Frustration was a dry heat at the back of his throat. This was supposed to be an exercise in reclamation. He was the archivist. He would catalog the primary source material of Cassius. Except the primary source was a palimpsest, scraped clean and written over so many times the original vellum had worn through.

He picked up the pen from the floor. The plastic was warm from the sun.

He tried a different angle. What do I want?

To finish the dissertation. To hear the crowd roar. To build a wall that lasts a hundred years. To watch the sunset with Lilith. To be left alone. To be remembered. To be erased.

The static peaked, a white noise scream inside his skull. He gripped the edge of the table. The wood grain dug into his fingers. He focused on that single, solid sensation—the pressure, the slight splintery texture. He rode the sensation back to silence.

When the noise faded, he felt empty. Not peaceful. Drained. The frustration had evaporated, leaving behind a fine, cold ash of resignation. He couldn’t define a core. There was no core. There was only the shelf, and the books, and the quiet, desperate noise they made when you tried to read them all at once.

He looked at the two pages. The first was a mess of scratches and question marks. The second was pristine, empty. A perfect record of his identity.

A strange sound escaped him. Not a laugh. Not a sigh. A short, dry exhalation of air. It was the sound a machine might make powering down.

He wasn’t distressed. That was the most peculiar part. The cold dread, the panic, the grief for Lilith—those belonged to specific books. The man on the shelf, the shelf itself felt only a deep, tectonic fatigue. The exhaustion of a structure holding incompatible weights. It wasn’t sadness. Physics.

The sun had moved. The rectangle of light now climbed the wall, leaving his notepad in shadow. The air in the apartment cooled by a degree. He became aware of a new sensation, soft and insidious, unspooling at the edges of his awareness.

It wasn’t a memory of an event. It was a memory of a state.

A feeling of quiet domesticity. The soft thump-thump-thump of a dog’s tail against a braided rug. The low hum of a refrigerator in a different kitchen. The scent was faint—lavender laundry detergent, yes, but underneath it, the warm, sweet smell of a sleeping child’s hair.

It had no edges. It offered no faces, no names. It was just a texture of existence, a blanket of mundane peace so profound it felt alien. It was the opposite of the vibrant, sun-drenched longing for Lilith. This was gray and gentle and complete. A life where nothing was wrong because nothing was extraordinary. A life of small, quiet satisfactions that left no scars.

It terrified him more than any ghost.

Because he didn’t want it. The Cassius at this table, in this apartment, with this marble in his pocket felt no pull toward that gentle haze. It felt like suffocation. But the feeling was in him, seeping into his bones like a low fever. It wasn’t a broadcast he was receiving. It was a broadcast he was becoming. A new book, quietly sliding into place on the shelf. A boring, peaceful, horrifying book.

He pushed back from the table. The chair legs shrieked against the floor.

He needed air that wasn’t filtered through someone else’s life. He needed a street, strangers, noise that wasn’t in his head. He stood, his body feeling borrowed and clumsy. He reached for his jacket on the hook by the door. As he shoved his arms into the sleeves, his fingers brushed against a hard, round shape in the right pocket.

The marble.

He closed his hand around it. The glass was cool. The single blue ribbon swirl inside was just a flaw in the material, a trapped thread of color. It wasn’t a memory. It was an object. It existed here, in this room, on this timeline. It was the only thing on the shelf that hadn’t been published elsewhere.

He held it tight until the feeling of the child’s hair and the braided rug faded back into the general static. He opened the door. The hallway air was stale and still. He stepped out, pulling the door shut behind him. The lock clicked with a finality that echoed in the empty hall.

He didn’t know where he was going. The destination didn’t matter. The movement did. He was a shelf going for a walk.

The hallway carpet was a pattern of muted browns and greens, worn to a ghost of itself down the center. Cassius focused on the path, one foot in front of the other. The marble was a hard, cool sphere in his fist. He made it to the stairwell, pushed through the fire door. The concrete steps echoed. Each echo was a clean, singular sound. His.

He reached the lobby. The clerk behind the desk, a young man with headphones on didn’t look up. The glass doors slid open with a hydraulic sigh. The city evening hit him—a wall of sound and smell. Exhaust, fried food, the damp concrete smell of recent rain. It was all generic, anonymous. It belonged to no one’s specific memory. Perfect.

He walked. He didn’t pick a direction. He let the crowd’s current nudge him along the sidewalk. He passed a bookstore, a bail bonds office, a shuttered theater. The faces around him were a blur of preoccupation. He tried to match his breathing to his steps. In. Out. The rhythm was his own.

His stomach growled. A basic, physical signal. He needed to eat. The thought was clear, uncomplicated. He would get food. He would choose it. He would eat it. It would be a thing he did.

He saw a diner ahead, light spilling from its wide windows onto the wet pavement. The sign was simple: EAT. He pushed the door open. A bell jingled. The air inside was warm, thick with the smell of grease and coffee and something sweet maybe pie. A few patrons sat in booths. A waitress leaned against the counter, writing on a pad.

“Sit anywhere, honey,” she said without looking up.

He took a booth by the window. The vinyl seat creaked under him. He picked up the laminated menu, sticky at the corners. He scanned the list. Burgers. Sandwiches. Meatloaf. The words were just words. He felt a flicker of something like hope. A choice.

The waitress approached. She had a kind, tired face. “What can I get you?”

He opened his mouth. “I’ll have the—”

The taste of a greasy spoon cheeseburger, loaded with grilled onions and a specific, tangy sauce, flooded his mouth. His salivary glands clenched. His fingers remembered the heft of a different menu, the texture of newsprint instead of plastic. He heard the clatter of a different kitchen, a different voice calling order up.

He swallowed. “The chicken club. On wheat toast. No mayo.”

“Fries or coleslaw?”

“Fries.” The word came out before the sensory memory could hijack it. He held onto it. Fries. Golden, salted. He pictured them.

“Drink?”

“Coffee. Black.” He said it firmly, defying the ghost of steamed vanilla.

She nodded, scribbled, tore the ticket from her pad. She clipped it to a wheel above the pass-through window. The action was crisp, professional. It had no echo.

He watched the kitchen through the window. A cook in a stained apron moved with economical grace. He focused on the man’s hands, the flip of a patty on the grill, the shake of a fry basket. Real hands. Real now.

His coffee arrived. He took a sip. Bitter, thin. Exactly what he’d ordered. He held the taste on his tongue, a small victory.

When the plate came, the sandwich looked correct. Toasted wheat, bacon visible at the edges. The fries were a pale yellow, not the golden brown he’d imagined. He picked one up. Limp, lukewarm. He ate it anyway. He took a bite of the sandwich. The chicken was dry. The bacon was soft. It was a profoundly mediocre meal.

He ate all of it. He chewed each bite deliberately, noting the texture, the lack of flavor. This was his choice. This was his experience. Unsatisfying. It was his.

He paid at the counter, leaving cash on the Formica. The bell jingled again as he left. The evening had deepened, the sky a bruised purple. Streetlights hummed to life. He felt steadier. The walk back to the apartment was a straight line. He retraced his steps, a creature of habit. His habit.

Back inside his apartment, the silence felt different. Not empty. Watchful. He took off his jacket and hung it up. The marble went back into the pocket. He stood in the middle of the living room. The notepad still sat on the table, a monument to failure. He ignored it.

He was tired, but it was an honest tiredness from walking, from chewing. He decided to make tea. A simple, before-bed ritual. He filled the kettle at the sink set it on the burner. The blue flame popped to life with a whump. He opened the cupboard. A small box of assorted tea bags sat next to a can of soup. He pulled the box down.

Earl Grey. Chamomile. Peppermint. Green tea.

He reached for the chamomile. His hand bypassed it, fingers closing instead around the Earl Grey. He didn’t like Earl Grey. He found its bergamot scent cloying, perfumed. But his hand brought the bag to his nose. The smell was immediate, vivid. Not just the tea. A memory of a chipped blue teapot, a drafty English flat, the sound of rain against a single-pane window. A feeling of focused solitude, of working late on a paper, the tea steaming beside a battered laptop.

He dropped the bag as if it had burned him. It fluttered to the counter. He stared at his traitorous hand.

The kettle began to whistle, a sharp, rising scream. He snatched it off the burner. The whistle died into a hiss of steam. His heart thudded against his ribs. He poured the boiling water into a mug, sloshing it onto the counter. He grabbed the chamomile bag, shoved it into the water. He didn’t wait for it to steep. He carried the mug to the living room set it on a coaster on the side table. He wouldn’t drink it. The act of making it was enough. He had made a choice.

He sank into his armchair. The fabric was scratchy against his neck. He looked at the blank television screen. He should read. He had a book on the end table, a history of urban planning. He’d chosen it himself from the library last week. He picked it up, opened it to the dog-eared page. The words swam. The sentences about zoning laws and public transit corridors bled into a detailed, technical knowledge of load-bearing stresses in Victorian brickwork. The paragraph he was trying to read became a manual he hadn’t studied. He saw the diagrams in his mind’s eye, clear as blueprints.

He snapped the book shut. The sound was too loud.

Music. He could put on music. Something without words. Something instrumental. He stood went to the small shelf where he kept a few CDs. He had a recording of ocean sounds. He had a classical guitar album he never listened to. He had a jazz compilation from a discount bin.

His fingers didn’t reach for the ocean sounds. They went unerringly to the classical guitar. He didn’t remember buying it. The case was dusty. He opened it, slid the disc into the cheap player. He pressed play before he could stop himself.

The first notes filled the room. A slow, complex melody. Beautiful. And it was utterly, completely foreign. It invoked no emotion in him. It was just sound. But his left hand, resting on his knee began to move. The fingers twitched, pressing against an invisible fretboard. The muscles in his forearm tightened with the memory of a specific chord change. His foot tapped a rhythm that wasn’t in the music, the rhythm of a metronome from a practice room a lifetime ago.

He was a puppet. His body was remembering a concert he’d never given.

He lunged for the player, hit the stop button. The silence that followed was absolute, ringing. He stood there, breathing hard, his traitorous hand now clenched into a fist.

He was in his apartment. He was surrounded by his things. None of them were his.

The desperate need for a simple, unambiguous action seized him. He would take a shower. Water was water. Soap was soap. Physical, sensory, clean.

In the bathroom, he turned the taps. He stripped off his clothes, let them fall in a heap on the floor. He avoided the mirror. He stepped under the spray. The water was hot, almost scalding. He let it beat against his scalp, his shoulders. He picked up the bar of soap. Plain, unscented. He worked it into a lather. The smell was neutral, chemical.

He washed his arms, his chest. The routine was mindless. For a few minutes, there was only the heat, the sound of the water hitting the tub, the slick feel of soap. Then, as he rinsed his hair, a different scent cut through the steam.

Not lavender. Not the lemony cleaner from the floor. This was dry, woody, sweet.

Cedar.

So strong he could taste it. The porch of a cabin. The wood warm from a day’s sun, the grain rough under his bare feet. The scent mixed with the smell of pine from the forest beyond and the faint, mineral tang of a lake. He saw the specific way the late light slanted through the trees, dappling the planks. He felt the comfortable ache in his shoulders from splitting wood that afternoon. He heard the distant, lonely cry of a loon.

He was no longer in the shower. He was on that porch, looking out at the darkening water, a feeling of deep, uncomplicated peace settling in his bones. This was a place of silence. Of solitude that wasn’t lonely. A life of manual labor and early nights and no ghosts.

The water began to run cold. The shock of it brought him back. He fumbled for the taps turned them off. He stood dripping in the sudden quiet, shivering. The cedar scent was gone. The bathroom smelled of damp tile and his plain soap.

He toweled himself dry with rough, angry strokes. He pulled on clean pajamas. His hands were shaking. He went back to the living room. His chamomile tea was cold, a pale scum forming on its surface. He picked up the mug, carried it to the kitchen, poured it down the drain.

He needed to sleep. To shut down. The shelf could go dark for a few hours.

He turned off the lights moved toward the bedroom. As he passed the armchair, a tune drifted into his head. It was a simple melody, a little wistful, carried by a single, clean voice. Beautiful. It made his chest ache with a longing that was not his own.

He didn’t realize he was humming it until the sound reached his own ears.

He stopped, his breath catching in his throat. He knew that melody. He had never heard it before in his life, but he knew it. It was the song Lilith hummed while she painted. The one she said her grandmother taught her. It belonged to her quiet moments, to the slant of light in her studio, to the smell of turpentine and linseed oil.

Hers. Not his. It was a ghost song, and he was the haunted house it lived in now.

He stood in the dark living room, the unfamiliar melody dying on his lips, the phantom scent of cedar porch wood still clinging to the back of his sinuses. He was a room where the radio was always on, tuned to stations he couldn’t name, playing songs for people he had never been.

The tremor started in his left hand around five in the morning. Cassius watched it from the chair by the window, his coffee cooling on the sill. A faint, constant quiver in the muscle between his thumb and forefinger. It wasn’t fear. More like a low-voltage current, a signal from a distant tower. He pressed the hand flat against his thigh. The denim of his jeans was rough under his palm. The tremor buzzed against the fabric, a trapped insect.

He’d slept in fits. The bed had felt too large, the pillows all wrong. He’d woken twice with the taste of lake water in his mouth, once with the phantom weight of a cat curled in the hollow behind his knees. There was no cat. He got up before dawn, drawn to the window by the gray light seeping around the edges of the buildings. The city was quiet, just the low rumble of a garbage truck two streets over. He made coffee. The process was his. Grind the beans, boil the water, pour it slow. The smell was a dark, bitter anchor. He carried the mug to his chair and sat, waiting for the sun.

The hand kept shaking.

He looked at it, this thing attached to him. It had written school essays held Lilith’s hand turned the pages of a thousand books. Now it vibrated with stolen histories. A mason’s hand, calloused from trowel work. A pianist’s hand, remembering arpeggios. A surgeon’s hand, steady as a rock. All those layers of muscle memory, short-circuiting.

He finished the coffee. The bitterness was a clean line drawn on his tongue. He stood, the movement stiff. His knees ached—his own ache, from a fall on ice decades ago, or maybe from years of kneeling in a garden that never existed. It didn’t matter. The pain was here.

He went to the small desk wedged in the corner of the living room. The journal was there, a simple black notebook. He’d bought it yesterday. A final attempt at a log. Not of memories, but of the static itself. He pulled out the chair sat down. The wood creaked. He opened the notebook to the first blank page. He picked up a pen. The tremor transferred to the tip, a tiny, frantic dance over the white paper.

He took a breath held it. He willed the shaking to stop. He focused on the weight of the pen, the smooth barrel. He pressed his left forearm hard against the desk, pinning it. The shaking lessened to a faint shimmer. He put the pen to paper.

Log. Morning. The hand is shaky today.

The letters were slanted, a bit too large. His handwriting. He was sure of it. He continued.
