The hand stopped shaking when he held the marble.

He’d been in the kitchen, staring at the pattern of water stains on the ceiling—a shape like a horse’s head, he’d decided three years ago, but tonight it looked more like a bent key. The tremor had started up again, a fine vibration in his fingers that made the spoon against the coffee mug sound like a tiny bell. He put the mug down. A sound kept going, a ring in his teeth. That’s when he remembered the coat.

It hung in the hall closet, a heavy wool thing he hadn’t worn in a decade. The closet smelled of mothballs and dust, a dry, ancient scent that made the back of his throat itch. He pushed past other coats, his hands finding the rough texture of the wool. The right pocket was empty. The left held a crumpled tissue, a receipt from a gas station so faded the numbers were ghosts, and the marble.

His fingers closed around it. The glass was cool, smoother than he remembered. The tremor in his hand dampened, then stilled. Just like that.

He brought it back to the living room sat in his chair, and held it up to the lamplight. The blue ribbon inside wasn’t a perfect swirl. One end was slightly thicker, with a tiny bubble trapped in the glass like a frozen breath. He’d never noticed the bubble before. He turned it slowly. The ribbon seemed to move, a lazy twist in the amber light.

For a week, the apartment hadn’t been his own. Not a full replacement, not like the beach or the porch. Worse. A layering. He’d be reading and the text would blur, the letters swimming into a different font on a different page—a technical manual for an air compressor, a child’s picture book about trains. The smell of his coffee would shift mid-sip to hot chocolate, then to bourbon, then to the flat metallic tang of a public water fountain. He’d reach for the light switch and his hand would be wrong. Younger. Older. A scar across the knuckles he’d never earned.

He hadn’t slept in his bed for three nights. He dozed in the chair, the marble a hard weight in his fist. The thumping from the dog channel had stopped entirely. The silence it left was worse. An expectant silence. A stage waiting for the next actor.

He lowered the marble, rolling it between his palm and fingers. The motion was rhythmic, almost compulsive. Grounding. That’s what the therapists on daytime TV called it. A grounding object for anxiety. But this wasn’t anxiety. This was a topological problem. It was a map that had been folded too many times, and now all the layers were visible at once.

The marble was the one point that didn’t shift.

He’d tried testing other things. The photograph of his parents on the bookshelf—in one bleed-through, the frame was silver, not wood. The chip on the edge of the kitchen counter—sometimes it was repaired, a smooth line of beige epoxy. His own face in the bathroom mirror—that was a lottery every morning. But the marble held in this hand, under this light was constant. The blue ribbon, the tiny bubble. The cool, perfect weight of it.

Why?

A memory surfaced, not as a vision, but as a dry fact, like something read in a footnote. He’d found it in this coat pocket. Chapter Four. Age thirty-two. He’d been looking for evidence of the blue house, of Damien. He’d found this instead. An object with no origin story. No memory of receiving it as a gift, of winning it in a game, of picking it up from the ground. It had just been there, a perfect, closed loop.

He’d assumed it was from one of the other channels. A piece of Damien’s world, or Lilith’s, somehow slipped through. A souvenir. But souvenirs were supposed to feel foreign. This didn’t. It felt native. It felt like his.

He sat up straight, the chair creaking under him. The apartment was quiet, just the hum of the refrigerator cycling on in the kitchen. He looked from the marble in his hand to the room around him. The worn beige carpet. That water-stained ceiling. The bookshelf with its mismatched books. All of it was contestable. All of it had been replaced, overlain, contradicted by other versions of here.

Except this.

He closed his eyes, tightening his grip. He thought of the beach—the feel of hot sand, the smell of salt and Lilith’s sunscreen. He waited for the marble in that memory. He sifted through the sensory data. Her hand in his. The cry of gulls. The cool interior of a rented cottage. No marble. He thought of the porch with the swing, the feeling of a child leaning against his leg. Lemonade in a glass. The rough paint on the railing. He pushed further, into the darker, fragmented channels—the cold room with the scent of antiseptic, the clatter of a factory floor, the dizzying height of a ladder against a brick wall. Not in any pocket. Not on any shelf. Not in any hand.

His eyes opened. The living room snapped back, dull and steady.

It wasn’t from them. It wasn’t a bleed-through. It was a thing that existed only here, in this timeline. His original timeline. The one that was being written over.

The realization was quiet. It didn’t feel like a revelation, more like a simple, dreadful alignment. A single equation finally solving itself at the bottom of the page. He was holding a fossil. Not of a different life, but of his own. The only piece of evidence that hadn’t been edited.

He brought it close to his eye, peering into the glass. The blue ribbon seemed to curve away into a miniature sky. The bubble was a planet. A whole cosmos in a sphere of glass. His cosmos. The only one that belonged solely to him.

A sound escaped him—not a laugh, not a sob. A short, dry exhalation. The irony was absolute. All these years chasing validation, wanting someone to confirm the memories, the people, the houses. And the only thing that could confirm anything was this. An object with no history. Proof of existence in the form of a hole.

He got up, his knees protesting. He went to the desk, opened the black journal. He didn’t sit. He uncapped a pen with his teeth and wrote at the top of a fresh page, the letters blocky and urgent.

The marble is mine.

He stared at the sentence. It looked childish. Obvious. He added another below.

It does not appear in any other channel. It is unique to this broadcast.

He put the pen down. He picked up the marble again. He needed to be sure. Not just a feeling. A test.

For the next hour, he moved through the apartment, holding the marble in different places. By the window where the streetlight bled in. In the dark of the bathroom with the door closed. In his bedroom, standing where the bed was in other versions. He waited for a flicker, for the glass to warm or cool, for the ribbon to change color. Nothing. It remained stubbornly, perfectly itself.

He ended up back in the living room, by the bookshelf. His parents smiled from their photograph, frozen in some long-ago afternoon. He looked from their faces to the marble. Did it come from them? Had his mother given it to him, a forgotten trinket from a fair? Had his father won it at a carnival game? He couldn’t ask. They were gone. Even if they were here, they’d deny it. They’d give him that gentle, worried look. What marble, sweetheart?

The marble’s origin was as erased as Damien. But the object itself remained. Physical. Clear.

He sank into his chair, exhausted. The fatigue was a cold sediment settling in his bones. He wasn’t a library shelf. He was an archaeological dig, and every other layer belonged to someone else. He was just the dirt. But this—this was a relic from the first civilization. The one that had been paved over.

He curled his hand around it, the glass warming to his skin. The tremor didn’t return. The overlapping scents of the week—hot chocolate, bourbon metal—faded, leaving only the dust and the lingering ghost of mothballs. The silence in the apartment softened lost its waiting edge.

For the first time in a week, the space felt like a container. And he was inside it, holding the one thing that proved he’d always been here.

He opened the black journal to a random page from years ago. The handwriting was tighter, more frantic. Damien’s favorite color was green. He hated mushrooms. He could whistle through his teeth. He laid the marble on the page, covering the words. He closed his eyes and tried to summon the memory of writing it—the feel of the pen, the lamplight, the hope that cataloging would make the brother real. He pushed further, trying to feel Damien’s presence in the room that night. The ghost of a laugh, the smell of rain on a denim jacket.

Nothing shifted. The marble was just a weight on paper.

He tried another page. A description of the blue house’s porch swing. The chains needed oil; they squeaked in a B-flat. He placed the marble over the description. He summoned the sensory bleed—the feel of worn wood under his thighs, the taste of lemonade, the weight of a small head against his shoulder.

That memory came, vivid and thick. The lemonade was too sweet. The child’s hair smelled of grass. But there was no marble in the memory. No shape in the pocket of those borrowed shorts, no glint on the porch floorboards. A memory played out around the absence.

He went through ten pages. Twenty. Each time, he used the recorded detail as a gateway, letting the phantom timeline surge up around him. The factory floor with its smell of hot metal and old grease. The cold clinic room, the crinkling sound of sterile paper. The beach, the grit of sand in the bed sheets. He immersed himself in each one, a deliberate dive, feeling for the marble. A pocket check. A glance at a shelf. A memory of receiving it.

Every channel was rich with detail, a full sensory broadcast. And every one lacked this specific frequency. The marble left no echo. It cast no shadow in those other worlds.

His head began to ache, a low, drilling pressure behind his eyes. The cognitive strain of holding multiple realities in his mind at once, of comparing them was a physical tax. His vision blurred at the edges. He blinked, and for a second the desk was a different desk—metal, bolted to a floor, a schematic pinned under his left hand. He gasped and slapped his palm down on his own wooden desk. The solid knock of real wood brought him back. The marble hadn’t moved.

Proof. Not the kind he’d wanted for decades, not a shared truth. A private one. The asymmetry was total. The marble validated nothing but its own existence, and by extension, his. It was a lighthouse that only he could see, signaling a shore only he inhabited.

He leaned back, the chair groaning. The apartment at night was a capsule of yellow light in a sea of dark windows. He looked at his hands. They were just hands. The right one, holding the marble was steady. The left, resting on the journal showed the beginning of liver spots, the veins more prominent than they’d been a year ago. This was his body. This aging, specific vessel. For a week, it hadn’t felt like his. It had felt like a rental, like a suit that didn’t fit. Now, with the marble’s cool pressure in his palm, the boundaries of his skin felt definite again. The itch of wool socks, the ache in his lower back from poor posture, the dry taste of old coffee on his tongue—these were his sensations. They belonged to this broadcast.

A dangerous idea, thin and sharp as a needle, slipped into his mind.

If the marble was native to this timeline, if it was an anchor point in the chaotic overlay of his consciousness... could it be used? Not just as a passive touchstone, but as a tool? A wedge?

He’d spent his life being acted upon. Memories invaded. Reality shifted. He was the receiver, faulty and overwhelmed. What if he could transmit? What if he could push back?

One thought was so audacious it made his breath catch. It felt like arrogance. Like touching a live wire. That system—the cosmic cycle, the karmic machine, whatever it was—had a glitch. He was the glitch. But the marble... the marble was a glitch within the glitch. A piece of the original code that hadn’t been overwritten. What happened when you introduced stable code into a corrupted system?

He didn’t know. The metaphor broke down, his mind veering into static. He was a man with a marble, not a technician.

But the instinct remained. A raw, human impulse. You find a rock in a landslide, you brace yourself against it.

He stood up, needing to move. He paced the short length of the living room, from the bookshelf to the window and back. The marble was a hard knot in his fist. Three steps. Turn. The carpet was matted in a path he’d worn over years. His path.

What would pushing back even look like?

He stopped at the window. His reflection was a ghost over the dark street. A pale man in a lit room, holding something close. When a bleed-through began, it was usually sensory—a smell, a texture, a sound. Then the visual layer would try to impose itself. The thumping from the dog channel. The glimpse of a different wallpaper. The sense of a different body.

What if, at the first sign of invasion, he focused everything on the marble? Not just holding it, but forcing it into the center of his perception. The exact coolness of the glass. The precise curve of the blue ribbon. The microscopic bubble. Making it the loudest signal in his head. A beacon of here and now and mine.

It might do nothing. It might just be a mental trick, a fancy version of counting backwards from ten.

Or it might be a declaration of war. On the other channels. On the other versions of himself. They were all fighting for bandwidth. What if he used the one piece of hardware that belonged solely to him to jam their signal?

The reflection in the window grimaced. It was a terrifying thought. Peace, even a fractured, horrible peace was still peace. Actively fighting the intrusions might provoke them. It might make the system try harder to correct him, to overwrite the anomaly. He could lose whatever fragile equilibrium he had.

He thought of the beach. Of Lilith’s hand. The memory was a phantom, but the loneliness it echoed was real. If he fought, he might lose even the ghosts. He’d be utterly alone in this, his original, crumbling timeline.

But wasn’t he already?

He turned from the window. The journal lay open on the desk, a record of hauntings. A proof of passivity. He looked at the marble in his hand. It was just a piece of glass. It had no power. He was assigning it power because he had nothing else.

That was the truth. It wasn’t a magic amulet. It was a paperweight. A fossil. A piece of evidence in a trial where he was the only witness, judge, and jury.

Yet.

He walked to the desk and sat down heavily. He picked up the pen. He didn’t write about memories. He wrote an instruction.

Protocol: Anchor.

He underlined it twice.

At first sign of channel interference (olfactory, auditory, tactile, visual), focus physical and mental attention on the marble. Sensory catalog: temperature, weight, visual detail (ribbon, bubble). Verbal anchor: “This is mine.” Repeat. Do not engage with the invasive signal. Starve it.

The words looked clinical, absurd. Like instructions for a machine. He was giving an order to himself.

He put the pen down. His heart was beating a quick, dull rhythm against his ribs. He felt like he’d just set a trap in his own mind. The bait was his only piece of truth. The thing he couldn’t afford to lose.

He placed the marble carefully on the desk, in the circle of lamplight. It gleamed, innocent. A child’s toy. He stared at it, waiting for the fear to subside. It didn’t. It crystallized into a cold, sharp resolve.

He wouldn’t try it now. He was too tired, his mental borders too thin. It would be like performing surgery on himself while dizzy.

But tomorrow. When the light was different. When he’d slept, if sleep came. He would carry the marble in his pocket. And when the world next tried to change, he would not just witness it. He would say no.

The silence in the apartment changed character. It was no longer waiting. It was holding its breath.

Morning light through the blinds was the color of weak tea, striping the carpet. Cassius woke in the chair, his neck stiff. The marble was in his hand. He’d fallen asleep clutching it. He uncurled his fingers. The skin of his palm was marked with a perfect shallow circle.

He made coffee. The routine was a series of checks. The canister was red, not blue. The spoon had the correct, slightly bent handle. The water from the tap smelled of chlorine, not iron. He held the marble in his left hand through it all, rolling it against his palm. A baseline.

He showered with it on the edge of the sink, watching it through the foggy glass door. A dark pupil in the porcelain. Dressed, he put it in the front pocket of his trousers. A tangible weight swinging against his thigh with every step. A reminder.

The first test came while he was buttering toast. The smell of the butter changed, mid-swipe. It went rancid, then vanished, replaced by the greasy, savory scent of fried bacon. A phantom kitchen. A different breakfast. His stomach turned. He dropped the knife. It clattered on the plate.

He grabbed the marble through the fabric of his pocket, squeezing so hard the glass dug into his skin. He focused on the pressure, the specific, cool hardness. He yanked it out and held it before his eyes. The blue ribbon. The bubble. The world around the marble shimmered, the edges of the kitchen counter going soft. The bacon smell intensified, mixed with the sound of a radio playing low, a weather report. He didn’t look at the counter. He stared into the glass.

“This is mine,” he said. His voice was a dry scrape.

He cataloged. The glass was room temperature now, warmed from his body. The ribbon’s thicker end pointed toward ten o’clock. The bubble was a perfect sphere, not a teardrop. This is mine. This is here.

The bacon smell wavered. The radio report became static, then a high whine in his ears. The counter snapped back into focus, beige laminate, not white tile. The scent dissolved, leaving only the neutral smell of his own kitchen, of dry toast.

He let out a shuddering breath. His heart hammered. It had worked. Not perfectly. The invasion had gotten a foothold—the smell, the sound. But he’d pushed it back. He hadn’t been pulled all the way in.

A fragile triumph, bitter as aspirin. He ate the toast. It tasted like dust.

He moved to the living room, intent on cleaning. Busywork to stay in his body. He was dusting the bookshelf when his hand passed over a particular volume—a thick history of the city. His fingers, brushing the spine felt not the cheap textured paper of the dust jacket, but cool, pebbled leather. A different book. A different shelf. One memory surged, a full-body pull: a library, oak tables, the dry, sweet smell of old paper.

He didn’t just hold it. He forced his mind toward it, a mental shove. He imagined the marble was a physical object in the path of the memory, a rock in a stream. The library image hit it. The sensation broke. The smell of old paper ripped in two, replaced by the flat odor of furniture polish from his rag. The feeling of the leather cover evaporated. His hand was on the paper jacket, slightly damp from the spray.

He was breathing hard, as if he’d run up stairs. A headache, not a drill now but a vise, tightened across his temples. He leaned against the shelf. The victory felt violent. He hadn’t remembered. He’d rejected.

The rest of the morning was a series of skirmishes. A flicker at the edge of his vision—a different lamp in the corner. The muffled sound of a television laugh track from another apartment that didn’t exist. The fleeting taste of mint on his tongue. Each time, he grabbed the marble. Each time, he focused on its concrete reality, repeating the phrase like a spell. Each time, the invasion stuttered and faded.

Each time, the headache deepened, and a crushing fatigue followed, as if the act of refusal burned some vital fuel his body couldn’t replenish.

By early afternoon, he was on the couch, the marble on his chest, rising and falling with his pause. The apartment was quiet. Too quiet. The silence had a new quality—not peaceful, but wary. Repelled. He felt like he’d shouted in a sacred place and was now waiting for the echo to return as punishment.

He must have dozed. The shift was sudden, a total replacement. No warning scent or sound.

He was in a car. Driving. Night. Rain streaked the windshield, smearing the red taillights of the car ahead into bleeding galaxies. The steering wheel was cold leather. A woman’s voice was talking from the passenger seat, low and anxious, about a mortgage rate. He knew her. Eleanor. His wife. The weight of a wedding ring was on his finger. Its memory was dense, layered, a complete life pressing down on him.

He tried to move his hand to his pocket. His hand, in this memory was on the wheel at ten and two. It wouldn’t obey. He was Cassius-in-the-car, not Cassius-on-the-couch. The overlay was complete.

Panic, hot and metallic, flooded his throat. He was losing the thread. He was being written over.

He couldn’t reach for the marble. So he thought of it. He visualized it on his chest, not here in this dark car. He poured every shred of will into the image. The clear glass. The blue ribbon. The bubble. He screamed it inside his own skull. THIS IS MINE.

The car wavered. The sound of the rain lessened became the hum of his refrigerator. The feel of the steering wheel softened became the nubby texture of his couch cushion. Eleanor’s voice cut off mid-sentence about interest points.

He gasped, bolting upright on the couch. The marble tumbled onto the floor with a sharp tock against the wood. He scrambled for it, his vision swimming. His fingers closed over it. He held it to his forehead, the glass cool against his feverish skin. He was shaking again, a full-body tremor.

That had been different. Not a bleed, but a takeover. The system wasn’t just sending stray signals anymore. It was trying to lock in a new channel. And it had nearly succeeded.

The cost of the anchor was written in his body. Nausea churned in his gut. His mouth was parched. The headache was a solid thing, a helmet of pain. He felt scraped raw inside, his mind aching like a muscle torn from the bone.

He sat on the floor, back against the couch, for a long time. The tea-colored light had faded to gray. Evening was coming.

This was the resistance. The system pushed. He pushed back. The pushback had a tax. How much could he pay before he broke? Could his heart take this pounding dread every day? Could his mind withstand this deliberate, wrenching dislocation?

He thought about putting the marble in a drawer. Going back to being a passive receiver. Enduring the ghosts, the overlays, the confusion. It was a kind of suffering, but it was a familiar one. It didn’t demand this brutal expenditure of self.

If he put it away, he was surrendering the only piece of ground he’d ever reclaimed. He was admitting that the other channels had a right to this space, this body, this consciousness. He was voting for erasure.

The tremor in his hands slowly stilled. The pain in his head settled into a dull, manageable throb. The resolve from the night before hadn’t evaporated. It had been tempered made heavier. This wasn’t a war he could win. He understood that now. The system was cosmic. He was one sticky soul. But it was a war he could choose to fight. Refusal was a stance. A stance was a shape. A shape was an identity.

He got up, every joint protesting. He went to the desk. The journal was open to his Protocol: Anchor note. He picked up the pen. His hand was steady. He wrote below it, the letters slow and deliberate.

The cost is high. The alternative is dissolution.

I choose the cost.

He placed the marble on the page, covering the words. He would carry it. He would use it. Every pushback would be a statement: I am here. This is mine. Even if here was a shrinking island. Even if mine was just a piece of glass and a fading mind.

The apartment darkened around him. He didn’t turn on the lights. He sat in the gathering gloom, the marble a cold, hard star in his hand. The silence held its beat. So did he. Waiting for the next wave, his anchor ready, his will a thin, frayed line holding fast to the last true piece of himself. Letting go meant losing everything. So he would not let go.
