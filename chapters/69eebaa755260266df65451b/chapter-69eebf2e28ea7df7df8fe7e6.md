The hum was back. Cassius stood in the middle of his living room, the ledger from the college archive open on his coffee table. Eleanor Voss, 24. The name was just ink. It explained nothing. The afternoon light through the blinds cut the room into dull, dusty stripes. He should make coffee. His throat was dry.

A flicker, like a dropped frame. A light didn’t just brighten—it changed quality. The air lost its apartment smell of old carpet and radiator dust. It became salt and warm pine and something floral, something like gardenia.

He blinked. The coffee table was gone.

He was standing on wide-plank hardwood the color of honey, worn smooth in a path from a sunlit kitchen. A sea breeze lifted the white curtains at a wall of windows. Beyond them, the ocean was a flat, brilliant blue under a morning sky. The light was liquid, golden, pouring over a deep, comfortable sofa piled with linen pillows. A fat orange cat dozed in a square of sun on the rug.

His heart didn’t pound. It swelled. A fullness in his chest, warm and heavy and right. This was his living room.

“You’re blocking the light, love.”

She tossed the words to him from the kitchen, a low, amused alto. He turned.

Lilith stood at the granite counter, slicing a mango. She wore a simple blue sundress, her dark hair twisted up with a pencil. The sun caught the fine hairs on her forearms, turning them to gold dust. A bead of juice ran down her wrist. She caught it with her thumb and brought it to her mouth.

The sight of her—the specific slope of her shoulder, the faint line between her brows as she focused on the fruit—unlocked a vault in him. A decade of mornings. Her cold feet seeking his under the covers. Her laughter at his terrible impressions. The quiet solidarity of reading on opposite ends of this same sofa while rain tapped the windows. It was all there, not as memory, but as present fact. The bedrock of his life.

“Cass?” She looked up, knife paused. “You’re zoning. Bad night?”

He opened his mouth. No sound came out. The warmth in his chest was curdling, a sweetness going sharp. This was the phantom. This was the static. But the cat was real, its flank rising and falling. The salt air was real, coating his tongue. Her face was real, the slight concern in her eyes now as she set the knife down.

“I’m fine,” he heard himself say. His voice was wrong here. Too tight. “Just… admiring the view.”

She smiled, a slow, private thing. “The ocean’s that way, goofball.” She wiped her hands on a towel and came around the counter. Her bare feet were quiet on the floor. She stopped in front of him, reached up, and pressed the back of her hand to his forehead. Her skin was cool, faintly sticky from the fruit. “No fever. You just have that thousand-yard stare. Like you’re reading fine print on the horizon.” She let her hand slide to his cheek. Her thumb brushed his cheekbone. “Still thinking about the Brindle file?”

The name was a splinter of ice. It didn’t belong here. In this world, he was… what? He grasped for the context. It came, effortless. A freelance researcher. Historical anomalies. A dry, quiet job he did from the sunroom. The Brindle file was a recent contract, a family genealogy with some puzzling gaps. Boring work.

“Yeah,” he managed. “Just… stuck on a date.”

“It’ll come.” She dropped her hand, laced her fingers with his. Her palm was warm, calloused from gardening. “Come eat. I got those croissants from the bakery. They’re still warm.”

She tugged him toward the kitchen. He followed, his hand in hers an anchor point. The details assaulted him. A pottery bowl full of lemons on the counter. A child’s drawing—a lopsided whale—held to the refrigerator by a seashell magnet. The specific squeak of the third cabinet hinge as she opened it for plates. This wasn’t a memory. Too dense, too layered. Memory was a highlight reel. This was the mundane, ignored middle. The life between the big moments.

He sat at the scrubbed pine table. She set a plate before him, a perfect croissant, a slice of mango fanned out like a sunset. She poured him coffee from a French press. The smell was rich, earthy.

“You’re quiet,” she said, leaning against the counter with her own mug. She studied him over the rim. “It’s the static isn’t it?”

The word landed like a stone in the sunlit room. Static. Here, it meant something else. A tinnitus he’d had for years, a minor nuisance. A high-pitched ring in his left ear when he was tired. She’d bought him a white noise machine for his office.

“A little,” he said.

“You need to log off for the weekend. No more digging through dusty probate records.” She took a sip, her eyes on the ocean. “We could drive up the coast. Get that fish stew you like. Walk on the cold sand.”

He could feel it. The crunch of shells underfoot. The wind cutting through his jacket, her leaning into his side for warmth. The way she’d tuck her cold nose against his neck later, in the car, and laugh.

A desperate hungry want choked him. It was a physical ache. This. He wanted this. Not as a vision, but as the steady, boring truth of his days.

The light stuttered.

Not a flicker. A stutter. Like a film strip catching, burning a hole in the image. For a fraction of a second, the ocean outside the window was a flat gray wall. The smell of salt and gardenia thinned, replaced by the faint, stale odor of his own apartment.

He gripped the edge of the table. The wood grain under his fingers was real.

Lilith didn’t notice. She was rinsing her mug at the sink, her back to him. The cat stretched, its claws scraping the rug. A normal sound.

But the hum was back. Underneath the sound of the water, underneath the cry of a distant gull. It was the low, electrical hum from his apartment. It was here, bleeding through.

Panic, cold and slick, coiled in his gut. No. Not yet.

He stood up so fast his chair scraped.

Lilith turned, water dripping from her hands. “Cass?”

“Don’t go,” he said. The words were raw, stripped.

Her brow furrowed. “I’m just doing the dishes.”

“Not you.” He took a step toward her, his hand outstretched. Not the dishes. Not this room. This world. Don’t fade. Don’t become static. “Just… stay here. With me.”

Understanding softened her features. She dried her hands on her dress, crossed the space between them. She took his face in both her hands. Her touch was solid, anchoring. “Hey. I’m right here. I’m not going anywhere.” She searched his eyes. “You’re really not okay are you?”

He shook his head, a minute movement. He was trembling. He could feel it in his jaw under her palms.

“Okay,” she whispered. She leaned in, rested her forehead against his. Her breath was warm, smelled of coffee and mango. “Then we stay. We stay right here all day. We’ll build a fort on the sofa. We’ll ignore the phone. We’ll be boring together.”

He closed his eyes. He committed it to memory—the exact pressure of her forehead, the scent of her, the sound of the sea as a constant backdrop. He tried to weld it into his cells. Proof.

The hum rose in pitch. Became a whine.

The sunlight dimmed. Not like a cloud passing. Like a dial being turned down.

“Lilith,” he breathed.

“I’m here.”

But her voice had an echo to it. A hollow quality, as if coming through a long tube.

He opened his eyes.

The vibrant blue of her dress was desaturating, graying at the edges. The kitchen behind her was losing resolution, the details of the pottery bowl, the child’s drawing, melting into vague shapes. The cat was a blur of orange, then gone.

“Cassius.” Her voice was faint. Her hands on his face were losing substance, becoming pressure without texture, like a memory of touch.

“Hold on,” he begged, clutching her wrists. “Please.”

She smiled. Sad, full of a love that knew it was ending. “It’s just the static, love. It’ll pass.”

Then she was particles. Then she was light. Then she was the afterimage you see when you close your eyes against the sun.

The honey-colored floor dissolved under his feet.

He was falling. Not through space, but through layers of signal. The crash of waves became the hiss of a bad connection. The smell of gardenia became the odor of dust heated by a sunbeam. The warmth became the stale, enclosed air of a room that hadn’t been properly aired in weeks.

His knees hit thin carpet.

He was on the floor of his apartment living room. The afternoon light was the same dull stripes. The ledger was on the coffee table. The hum of the fluorescent light in the kitchen was the only sound.

His hands were empty. They were clenched into fists in his lap. He slowly uncurled them.

His right palm held the ghost of her touch. A phantom warmth, already fading. He brought it to his face, to the place where her forehead had pressed against his.

Nothing. Just his own clammy skin.

A sound escaped him. Not a sob. Something drier. The scrape of air over a hollow place.

The apartment was silent. That silence wasn’t empty. It was the silence after a voice you love has stopped speaking. It had shape. It had the exact contours of the life that had just been ripped away.

He sat on the floor, in the dust motes dancing in the weak light, and felt the warmth on the other side of the static bleed out of him, leaving a cold so complete it felt like a new kind of gravity.

The cold gravity of the carpet held him. Cassius counted the stripes of light on the floor. One. Two. Three. The fourth one was bisected by the shadow of the coffee table leg. He focused on that imperfection, the way the light bent. He could still smell gardenia. In his nose, in the back of his throat, a sweet poison. He breathed in through his mouth, trying to catch the apartment’s true scent—dust, old books, the ghost of last week’s spaghetti. The gardenia persisted.

He pushed himself up. His body felt borrowed, the movements clumsy and too loud in the hollow silence. The ledger on the table was an affront. Eleanor Voss, 24. What did a dead student matter? He had a wife. He had a cat. He had a mortgage on a house by the sea.

This thought was a trap. He recognized its shape. It was the phantom trying to overwrite him, to argue its case for reality. He couldn’t afford to think in terms of had. He had to think in terms of is. He is in his apartment. He is forty-two. He is alone.

His coping mechanism clicked in, a rusty gear finally catching. Document. Corroborate. Anchor.

He went to the small desk shoved against the wall, a laminate thing from a big-box store. His laptop was there, a thick, obsolete brick. He opened it. The screen flared to life, the desktop a generic image of a mountain range he’d never seen. He opened a blank document. His fingers hovered over the keys. The title came automatically: Event Log – 10/17/2016 – 3:14 PM.

He typed the date. The time was an estimate. Time didn’t work the same over there. Had he been gone seconds? An hour? The light in the room hadn’t changed.

Primary sensory intrusion initiated at approximately 3:10 PM, he typed. The clinical language was a life raft. Visual: full environmental replacement. Apartment replaced with residential interior, coastal setting. Wide-plank hardwood flooring, honey tone. White curtains. Ocean view. Orange domestic shorthair cat. Individual designated “Lilith” present.

His fingers faltered. Individual designated “Lilith” present. Female. Approx. mid-30s. Dark hair. Blue sundress. Engaged in food preparation (mango).

It was a police sketch. It said nothing. It didn’t capture the way the sun lit the gold dust on her arms. The specific, trusting weight of her hand in his. The cadence of her voice saying goofball. He forced himself to continue.

Tactile: confirmed. Individual made physical contact. Hand-holding. Forehead contact. Sensation was persistent and detailed, including temperature differential (cool hand vs. warm face) and texture (slight stickiness from fruit juice).

Auditory: full dialogue exchange. Individual referenced “Brindle file,” “static,” proposed a trip up the coast. Individual used term of endearment “love.”

Olfactory: dominant scent of gardenia and salt air, undercut by coffee and baked goods.

Duration: subjective experience approx. 8–10 minutes. Objective time elapsed unknown.

Termination: sensory fade initiated with auditory “hum” increasing in pitch. Visual desaturation. Tactile dissolution. Full reversion to baseline environment followed.

He sat back. The words were a cage he’d built around the experience. See? It was just data. A list of symptoms. A psychotic episode with high sensory fidelity. He read it over. It was the driest, most insane thing he had ever written.

A cramp seized his right hand. He looked down. He was still clutching an invisible hand, his fingers curled so tight the knuckles were white. He forced them open. The phantom warmth was gone, but the muscle memory of her grip remained. His palm ached.

He needed a physical anchor. The marble. He swiveled in his chair, scanned the cluttered bookshelf. On the middle shelf, between a geology textbook and a broken radio. The clear glass with its single blue swirl. He’d found it in a coat pocket years ago. It belonged only here. None of the phantoms recognized it. Native to this timeline, to Cassius Prime.

He fetched it. The glass was cool. He rolled it between his palm and his fingers, feeling the solid, unchanging curve of it. This was real. This object had continuity. He’d had it at thirty-two. He had it now at forty-two. It existed in this apartment, on this shelf, in this city with no ocean.

The cat. The cat had been orange. A specific shade, like a pale pumpkin. It had a nick in its left ear. He hadn’t typed that detail. He added it now. Feline: orange domestic shorthair, nick in left ear. He stared at the sentence. Why would his brain invent a nick in a cat’s ear? Such a pointless, specific thing. The brain in crisis was supposed to deal in broad strokes—fear, longing, archetypes. Not the squeak of a specific cabinet hinge. Not the pattern of calluses on a gardening hand.

Dread, a cold slurry began to fill the hollow spaces the warmth had left. This wasn’t like the flickers of Damien, the brother-that-wasn’t. That had been echoes, shadows, a face in a crowd. This was infrastructure. This was a complete, functioning world with its own boring minutiae. A world with a history—the Brindle file, a white noise machine for his office static, a favorite fish stew up the coast. A world with a future—a weekend plan, a fort of sofa cushions.

A world where he was loved.

The horror wasn’t in the loss. The horror was in the plausibility. The haunting wasn’t a monster in the corner. It was a better life in the sun, and the terrible, creeping suspicion that it might be the truer one. That this—the dust, the silence, the dead—was the phantom.

He put the marble down on the desk beside the laptop. It looked like a paperweight. A trinket.

From the kitchen, the fluorescent light hummed. It was the same pitch as the hum that had preceded the fade. His breath hitched. He listened, straining. Just the light. Just a bad ballast.

Then, underneath it, so faint it was at the edge of hearing, a melody.

Two notes. A pause. Three notes in a descending line. A lilting, simple tune. Unfamiliar.

He froze.

It wasn’t in the room. It was in his head. But not like a remembered song. It had presence. It occupied acoustic space, a tiny, distant music box playing in a faraway room of his own skull.

Lilith. She had hummed it. Standing at the sink, rinsing her mug, her back to him. A wordless, absent-minded little tune. He hadn’t registered it then, his mind too full of panic. Now, in the silence, it echoed.

He pressed the heels of his hands against his ears. The hum of the light was muffled. The melody continued, unimpeded. Clear. Delicate. Terrible.

He stood up, chair scraping. That sound should have drowned it. It didn’t. The melody looped. Two notes. Pause. Three descending notes.

He walked to the window, yanked the cord for the blinds. They clattered up. The late afternoon sky was a uniform gray, the color of old television static. The building across the street was a grid of lit and unlit windows. A real place. His real view.

The melody reached the end of its loop and began again.

This was new. The phantoms had never left residue before. They came, they tortured him with their presence, they left. They didn’t leave souvenirs behind. This was an infection. A sensory shard lodged in the fabric of his reality, playing on a frequency only he could hear.

He leaned his forehead against the cool glass. His breath fogged a small circle. The melody played on. A lullaby from a world that didn’t exist. A love song from a ghost who was, in some other timeline, just doing the dishes.

The cold from the window seeped into his skin. It didn’t touch the dread. The dread was internal, a core of ice that had nothing to do with the temperature outside. The documentation was worthless. The marble was just a rock. The phantom wasn’t retreating. It was broadcasting.

And he was the only receiver, tuned forever to a station that played, on a loop, the warmth on the other side of the static.

The melody followed him out of the apartment. It was there under the rattle of his keys, the thud of the door, the squeak of his sneakers on the stairwell’s linoleum. Two notes. Pause. Three descending notes. A tiny, perfect engine of madness whirring in the center of his head.

He needed noise. Crowds. The mundane clatter of the real.

The grocery store was three blocks away, a fluorescent-lit cavern that always smelled of disinfectant and overripe bananas. He pushed a cart with a wobbly wheel, the metallic screech a welcome counterpoint to the lullaby. He focused on the list in his mind. Milk. Bread. The kind of coffee that tasted like burnt dirt. He moved through the bright aisles, a man performing an errand.

He picked up a gallon of milk. The cold condensation felt good on his palms. He checked the date. His eyes scanned the carton, but his brain was parsing the store’s ambient soundscape—the Muzak cover of a pop song, the beep of a scanner, a child whining about cereal. He was listening for the melody to be echoed, for the world to somehow pick up the transmission.

It didn’t. The tune remained his private curse.

He turned into the cereal aisle. A woman was there, her back to him, reaching for a box of granola on the top shelf. She was tall. Dark hair pulled into a messy knot at the nape of her neck. A blue cardigan.

Cassius stopped. His cart bumped the shelf. The sound was a gunshot in his ears.

It wasn’t her. It couldn’t be. The hair was wrong—Lilith’s was longer, straighter. The shoulders were different. This was just a woman maybe thirty-five, in comfortable jeans and sneakers. She got the granola, dropped it into her basket with a soft thump. She hummed.

A wordless tune.

Cassius’s blood turned to slurry in his veins.

It wasn’t the same melody. It was something else, some Top 40 riff. But the quality of it—the low, off-key alto, the way she whispered through the notes—was a direct punch to his sternum. He knew that hum. He’d heard it in a sunlit kitchen, accompanied by the splash of dishwater.

The woman turned, sensing his stare. Her face was pleasant, ordinary. Pretty in a forgettable way. No recognition in her eyes. She offered a polite, closed-lipped smile for the man blocking the aisle, then looked past him to the oatmeal.

“Sorry,” Cassius mumbled. He shoved his cart forward, the wobbly wheel shrieking in protest. He fled the aisle, his heart a frantic bird in a cage of ribs. He didn’t look back.

Coincidence. That’s all. The human brain was a pattern-matching machine gone haywire. It found faces in clouds heard voices in static. He was primed, that was all. He was a loaded gun pointed at the world, and anything that even vaguely resembled the trigger was going to get shot.

He tried to focus. In through the nose, out through the mouth. The technique from a book. It didn’t work. His lungs felt shallow, tight. He leaned on his cart in the baking goods aisle, staring at a wall of chocolate chips until the letters blurred.

He needed to finish. Get out.

He grabbed a loaf of wheat bread without looking. The coffee was in aisle seven. He walked there, his eyes on the scuffed floor tiles. Don’t look up. Don’t see anyone. Just get the can and go to the checkout.

A laugh cut through the air from two aisles over. A warm, rolling chuckle that ended in a soft snort.

Cassius froze. His hand, outstretched toward the coffee hung in the air.

The sound came again, followed by a man’s lower chuckle. A couple, sharing a joke by the frozen pizzas.

It was her. It had to be. The hum was a fluke, but the laugh—that was a fingerprint. A vocal fingerprint his cells recognized before his mind could argue.

He abandoned his cart. He walked, then jogged, the bread tumbling from the child seat onto the floor. He rounded the corner into the frozen food section.

A man and a woman stood by the glass doors, examining a box of garlic bread. The woman was shorter, blonde, wearing a puffy vest. She laughed again at something the man said, covering her mouth. It wasn’t the same laugh. Not even close. Higher, tinkling.

A laugh Cassius had heard had been hers. But it wasn’t coming from this woman. It had come from somewhere else, from another aisle, or maybe from the speakers, or maybe from the hollows of his own skull. He stood there, panting slightly, a man lost among the frozen peas.

A store employee, a kid with acne and a red vest, eyed him cautiously. “You okay, sir?”

Cassius nodded, a stiff, jerky motion. He turned and walked back to aisle seven. His cart was gone. Someone had moved it, thinking it abandoned. His bread was on the floor near the muffin mixes. He left it there.

He found the coffee. He carried the can to the front, his arms wrapped around it like a life preserver. The express lane was open. The cashier was a woman in her sixties with kind eyes and a name tag that read DOLORES.

She scanned the coffee. “That it today, honey?”

The term of endearment was a razor sliding between his ribs. Love. “Yes,” he said. He sounded like a stranger.

“Paper or plastic?”

“What?”

“Your bag. Paper or plastic?”

He was not being haunted. He was being erased.
