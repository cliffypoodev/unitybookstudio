The tremor in her left hand lasted three days. It faded by the fourth morning, leaving behind a dull ache in the tendons, like she'd been gripping something too tight for too long. She didn't tell anyone. Who was there to tell? Miller's calls had tapered off, polite check-ins that felt more like duty. Lena had emailed a link to a therapist specializing in “memory dissonance and stress-related cognitive events.” She'd deleted it.

The job went six months later. Not fired, exactly. “A restructuring.” Her position was deemed redundant. The severance package was decent, a financial airbag for a fall she'd seen coming. She'd been making errors—small, inexplicable lapses. Sending a report to the wrong vendor. Forgetting a meeting she'd confirmed an hour before. Once, she'd looked at a quarterly spreadsheet and the numbers had refused to resolve into meaning, swimming on the screen like black tadpoles. She'd blamed the tremor, the sleeplessness, the static in her head that never quite went away.

The apartment lease was up two months after the severance ran out. She couldn't afford the old place. The new one was a third-floor walk-up on the south edge of the city, where the buildings shed their brick facades for cheap aluminum siding. The number 8 on the door was crooked, screwed in too high on the left side. She'd noticed it immediately, the asymmetry like a visual itch she couldn't scratch.

Now Wound stood in the middle of the living room, a space that smelled of industrial lemon cleaner and, underneath, the ghost of old cigarettes. Three cardboard boxes formed a pathetic archipelago on the beige carpet. That was all she'd brought. The rest she'd sold or given away, a ruthless paring down that felt less like moving and more like preparing for an evacuation.

Her coping mechanism was order. She attacked the nearest box, slicing the tape with a key. Dishes. She carried them to the kitchenette, a narrow galley with linoleum the color of stale mustard. She opened a cupboard. A previous tenant had left a single can of tomato paste, its label faded to a pink blush. She left it there, a sentinel on the empty shelf. She stacked her plates, her bowls, the movements precise. Each item placed was a point of stability. A fact. This bowl is here. This is my bowl. The process was a wall against the static.

But the wall had cracks. As she lifted a coffee mug, a memory surfaced, unbidden and hyper-vivid: a different kitchen, morning light the color of honey pouring over blue-checked curtains. The smell of bacon, someone humming off-key. A man's voice, warm and sleep-rough. “You burn that coffee again, Cass, and I'm divorcing you.” A laugh in response, not her own. A woman's laugh. The memory had the texture of real life, the grain of the wooden countertop under her palms, the chill of the ceramic mug. More solid than the lemon-scented emptiness around her now.

It lasted three heartbeats. Then it dissolved into the hum of the refrigerator, a low, unhealthy rattle.

She set the mug down too hard. The clunk was loud in the empty room.

She finished the kitchen box. The next one was labeled OFFICE. Books, a few framed prints she never hung, the fireproof lockbox with her external drive. She left it sealed. The third box was marked MISC. She didn't remember packing it. The label was in her own handwriting, blocky capitals with a dying marker.

Inside, on top, lay a photograph.

It was a standard 4x6, glossy. The colors were slightly oversaturated, the way prints from the nineties often were. It showed a backyard. A younger version of herself maybe fifteen, squinting at the camera with a half-smile was holding a garden hose, spraying the grass. Next to her, leaning against a birch tree was a boy. Dark hair, a gap-toothed grin maybe ten years old. He was holding a red plastic shovel.

Wound didn't recognize the backyard. She didn't own a red shovel. She didn't have a younger brother.

She turned the photo over. Nothing written on the back. Just the white, slightly textured paper.

Her breath felt thin. The static in her ears rose from a hum to a whine, the sound of an old tube television tuned to a dead channel. She stared at the boy's face. The features were generic—a kid's face. But the posture, the way he leaned into the tree as if it were a friend felt specific. Known.

This was new. The marble, the drawing, the sweater—those were artifacts from her own past, twisted. This was an intrusion. A scene she did not remember living.

She placed the photo on the carpet, face up. She dug through the rest of the MISC box. Old cables. A broken desk lamp. A stack of takeout menus from her old neighborhood. No other photos. No context.

She sat back on her heels. The afternoon light through the single window was failing, thickening to a dull orange. The crooked 8 on the door seemed to glare at her.

She needed a system. A way to categorize this. She fetched her notebook from her jacket pocket, the same one she'd used to log the erased dinner. She opened it to a fresh page. At the top, she wrote: EVIDENCE OF INTRUSION. She drew a line down the center. On the left, she wrote OBJECT. On the right, ORIGIN MEMORY.

Under OBJECT, she wrote: Photograph, backyard, circa 1990? Under ORIGIN MEMORY, she left a blank. She stared at the empty space. The whine in her ears pulsed.

She picked up the photo again. She studied the details. The birch tree had a peculiar bend about six feet up, a kink like an elbow. The grass was lush, green. In the far corner of the frame, a corner of a wooden swing set was visible, bright blue paint. She closed her eyes, trying to force a memory. The smell of cut grass. The chill of spray from the hose. One sound of the boy's laugh.

Nothing. Just the blank wall of her own history, and this perfect, impossible window punched through it.

A sound from the hallway.

Her eyes snapped open. It was a laugh. A child's laugh, high and clear cut short as if a hand had been clapped over a mouth. It came from right outside her door.

The air in the apartment went still. The refrigerator's rattle stopped, as if holding its breath.

Wound didn't move. Her muscles locked. The photo was a cold rectangle in her hand.

Silence stretched for five seconds. Ten.

Then a shuffle, a soft scuff of shoes on the cheap hallway carpet. Moving away. A door down the hall opened and closed with a solid, final click.

She let out a breath she hadn't meant to hold. Her heart was a frantic bird against her ribs. It was just a neighbor. A kid from another apartment. This building probably had families. It was nothing.

She looked down at the photo. The boy in the picture grinned his gap-toothed grin, forever leaning against the bent birch tree.

The static in her ears didn't fade. It settled into a steady, whispering hiss, the sound of a place where no signal was meant to be. She placed the photo carefully in the back of her notebook. She closed the cover. This room was almost dark now. She hadn't bought any light bulbs.

She sat there in the growing gloom, the three unpacked boxes around her like tombstones, and listened to the silence. It wasn't empty anymore. It was full of the hiss.

She didn't buy light bulbs. She sat in the dark until the streetlights outside the window buzzed on, casting a jaundiced glow across the carpet. The hiss in her head became the room's soundtrack. She finally moved when her knees ached from kneeling. She stood, joints protesting, and went to the window. The street below was quiet, just the occasional car hissing on wet pavement. A normal evening. A normal street.

Her stomach growled. She hadn't eaten since morning. The thought of food was a distant, theoretical concern. She went to the kitchen, opened the empty refrigerator. The light inside was a shocking, clinical white. It illuminated nothing but clean shelves and that single can of tomato paste. She closed the door. The darkness returned, softer now, her eyes adjusted.

She needed to sleep. The bedroom was just an alcove off the living room, a space barely large enough for the mattress on the floor. She'd set it up that morning, the fitted sheet straining at the corners. She didn't undress. She lay down on top of the comforter, shoes still on. She stared at the ceiling. A hairline crack ran from the corner toward the center, fork-tongued.

The laugh replayed in her mind. High, clear cut off. A kid playing, then shushed. It was nothing. It was everything.

She focused on the crack in the ceiling. She traced its path with her eyes, over and over. A meditation. Her breathing slowed. The static receded to a background hum, almost peaceful. Her eyelids grew heavy. The streetlight glow through the thin curtains blurred into a diffuse halo.

She drifted.

The smell reached her first. Not lemon cleaner. Popcorn. Buttered, salty, with that faint scorched note from kernels that cooked too long. And underneath, the waxy, fruity scent of crayons.

Sound followed. The low murmur of a television, a sitcom laugh track. The crinkle of a paper bag. A soft, rhythmic thumping.

Wound opened her eyes.

She was on a couch. A plaid, corduroy couch, worn soft on the arms. A knitted afghan was tucked around her legs. The light in the room was warm, lamplight, pooling on a shag carpet the color of wheat. A coffee table sat before her, littered with coloring books and a tumbler of something amber. Ice cubes melted in it.

The thumping was a dog's tail. A large, reddish-gold dog lay on the floor by the hearth of a brick fireplace. Its tail whumped steadily against the baseboard in its sleep. A Labrador maybe. Its flank rose and fell.

This was not her apartment. This was a living room. A lived-in one. The walls were covered in bookshelves, crammed with paperbacks and knickknacks. Photographs in wooden frames. A clock shaped like a black cat, its tail ticking.

She tried to sit up. Her body felt heavy, languid saturated with a deep domestic contentment. It was a foreign sensation, a warmth in her chest that had no correlate in her memory. It terrified her.

A child ran into the room. A boy, about seven, in dinosaur-print pajamas. His dark hair was damp from a bath. He skidded on the carpet, a crayon gripped in his fist.

“Dad. The blue's all gone.”

The word landed in Wound's gut like a stone.

She couldn't speak. She looked at the boy's face. It was the boy from the photograph. Older now, the gaps in his teeth filled in, but the same. The same eyes.

“You used it all on that sky,” a woman's voice called from the kitchen. “I told you it was a big sky.” She appeared in the doorway, wiping her hands on a dish towel. She was pretty in a tired, comfortable way. Brown hair in a loose ponytail, smile lines around her mouth. She wore a college sweatshirt over leggings. She looked at Wound, and her smile deepened, crinkling her eyes. “He's blaming his artistic excess on a crayon shortage.”

Wound stared. She knew her. She didn't know her. The knowledge was a physical ache, a ghost limb of a life. Her name was on the tip of Wound's tongue, wrapped in the butter-and-crayon smell.

“I'll get more tomorrow,” she heard herself say. Her voice was different. Rougher, warmer. It came from her chest without her permission.

“See?” The woman—her wife—tossed the towel over her shoulder. She walked over and ruffled the boy's hair. “Dad to the rescue. Now finish up. It's almost time.”

The boy dashed back out. The dog's tail thumped once, twice.

She sat on the arm of the couch, her hip pressing against Wound's shoulder. A familiar weight. She smelled like soap and popcorn. She looked down at Wound, her expression softening. “Long day?”

Wound nodded, the motion automatic. She could feel the scratch of the corduroy against her neck, the warmth of the woman through the fabric. So detailed. The split ends in her ponytail. A tiny chip in the polish on her thumbnail. This wasn't a memory. It was happening.

“You're a million miles away,” she said. Her hand came to rest on Wound's head, her fingers gently scratching her scalp. The touch sent a bolt of pure, unadulterated belonging through her. It was a key turning in a lock she hadn't known was empty.

Panic surged, cold and bright, underneath the warm, imposed contentment. This was the intrusion. This was the bleed-through. She was a passenger here. She tried to move her hand, to push the woman away, to break the contact. Her fingers twitched on the afghan.

“Just tired,” her voice said.

“Mmm.” She didn't move her hand. “Well, the weekend's almost here. We can all be tired together.”

The television laugh track swelled. The dog sighed in its sleep. The clock's tail tick-tick-ticked. Wound wanted to scream. To tear this whole warm, impossible scene apart. This was the life the photograph promised. This was the boy grown older. This was the marriage, the dog, the popcorn-scented peace.

It was a trap. A beautiful, suffocating trap.

She squeezed her eyes shut. She focused on the static. She dug for it in the back of her skull, past the sitcom sounds and the tail-thumping. She found it—a thin, high wire of noise beneath the domestic symphony. She grabbed onto it. She imagined pulling it forward, amplifying it, letting it shred this false signal.

The popcorn smell wavered. Became chemical, lemony.

“Cass?” Her hand stopped moving. Her voice held a note of concern. Real concern.

Wound pushed. She envisioned the static as a wave, crashing over the lamplight, dissolving the bookshelves, silencing the laugh track. The thump of the dog's tail became erratic, a stuttering metronome.

“Dad?” the boy's voice came from the hallway, farther away.

Wound opened her eyes. The warm light flickered, like a power dip. The woman's face above her swam, her features blurring at the edges. For a second, Wound saw not a wife, but a stranger wearing a mask of concern. Then it snapped back.

“I'm fine,” her voice said, but it was thin, strained. “Just a headache.”

She had to get out. She tried to stand. The afghan tangled around her legs. The heaviness in her limbs was cement. She was sinking into the couch, into this life.

She gasped, bolting upright.

Cold darkness. The smell of lemon cleaner and dust. The mattress beneath her was hard, unforgiving. She was in her bedroom alcove. The streetlight cast the long, lean shadow of the window frame across the floor.

Her heart hammered against her sternum. She was drenched in a cold sweat, her shirt stuck to her back. The warmth of the afghan, the weight of the woman's hand—they were gone, leaving a phantom imprint so vivid it felt like amputation.

She clutched at her own scalp. Her hair was short, thinner. Not the hair the woman had touched.

The static was back, full force, a roaring in her ears. Underneath it, layered like an afterimage on her retinas, she could still hear the tail.

Thump.

Faint. Distant.

A solid, wooden knock from the living room, behind her closed bedroom door.

She stopped breathing. The sound was obvious. The slow, contented thump of a large dog's tail hitting a baseboard. She'd just heard it for what felt like an hour. She knew its rhythm, its weight.

It came again. Thump.

Then silence.

The static screamed. Wound sat frozen, her fingers digging into her knees. There was no dog. The apartment was empty. She had no dog. She was allergic to dogs.

The sound did not return. The silence that followed was absolute, a vacuum sucking all other sound out of the world. Worse than the thump. It was the silence of something listening back.

She didn't move until the gray light of dawn bleached the darkness from the room. She sat and stared at the crack in the ceiling, now a sharp black line in the morning gloom, and waited for the world to decide which version of her was real.

She didn't go into the living room. She sat on the mattress until the gray light turned yellow, until she could hear the distant grind of a garbage truck two streets over. The normal sounds of a Monday morning were an anchor. She clung to them.

Her coping mechanism was order. Facts. Lists. She stood, her body stiff and cold. She opened the bedroom door.

The living room was empty. Just the three boxes, the beige carpet, the slant of morning sun highlighting the dust in the air. No dog. No baseboard worn from tail-thumping. She walked to the spot where the sound had seemed to originate, near the wall shared with the hallway. The carpet was flat, undisturbed. She knelt and ran her fingers over the baseboard. Cheap, painted MDF. No marks.

See? Nothing.

She made coffee. The ritual was mechanical: scoop, pour, wait. The gurgle of the machine was a real sound. The smell was bitter, familiar. She drank it black, standing at the kitchen counter, and stared at the crooked 8 on her front door. The sun hit it now, making the brass glare.

A dream. A stress-induced auditory hallucination. Her sleep was garbage, her diet was worse. The brain, under sufficient strain could fabricate anything. She'd read about it. She'd researched it after the first tremor. Hypnopompic hallucinations. Sleep paralysis. The mind stitching together fear and memory fragments into a convincing lie.

That's all it was. A lie.

She washed the mug. Dried it. Put it in the cupboard beside the sentinel tomato paste can. The action was precise. A fact: the mug is here. The dream is over.

But her hands were shaking again. A fine, constant tremor. She shoved them into her pockets.

She needed to document it. Not to prove anything to anyone else—there was no one else—but to impose a grid on the chaos. To translate the feeling into data. Data could be analyzed. Data could be contained.

She retrieved her notebook from the floor where she'd left it. She sat at the small, wobbly table she'd bought at a thrift store. She opened to a new page. She wrote the date, the time she'd woken. She titled the entry: AUDITORY EVENT.

She described the preceding dream in clinical terms. “Vivid, multi-sensory narrative involving a domestic setting, unfamiliar family unit.” She did not write wife. She did not write son. She wrote “adult female, perceived as spouse” and “juvenile male, perceived as offspring.” She listed the sensory details: smell of popcorn and crayons, texture of corduroy, visual of dog (canine, large, reddish-gold).

Then she described the transition. “Upon forced awakening, auditory perception persisted. Rhythmic thumping sound, low-frequency, wooden resonance. Approx. 3-4 repetitions. Originated from living area. Duration: 10-15 seconds total.”

She paused. Her pen hovered over the page. The next part was the threshold.

She wrote: “Possible explanations, in descending order of probability.”

1. Hypnopompic hallucination, auditory component. Dream echo.
2. Plumbing noise. Building settling.
3. Neighbor activity (unlikely given hour and character of sound).
4. Psychological break. Incipient psychosis.
5. External, non-ordinary phenomenon.

She stared at the fifth entry. It looked absurd on the page. It was a trapdoor. To write it was to acknowledge the trapdoor existed. She drew a careful box around it.

She needed more data. She needed to know if the environment held any trace. She pushed back from the table and conducted a survey.

She checked the window latches. Secured. The front door deadbolt. Locked. She examined the baseboard again, this time with the morning light from the window. She got on her hands and knees. The dust was a uniform layer. No scuff marks, no scratches that looked new. She pressed her ear to the wall. A faint electric hum from somewhere maybe a fridge in another unit. No tail-thumps.

Nothing.

She sat back on the floor. The relief was a weak, tepid thing. It meant the fifth explanation was receding. It meant her mind was the source. A broken source, but an internal one. Contained. She could handle broken. She'd been handling broken for years.

She returned to the table. Under the list, she wrote: “Physical inspection negative. No corroborating evidence.” She underlined negative twice.

The act of writing was calming. The letters were sharp, black, definitive. They pinned the experience to the page. Here. This happened. Now it is here, and not in the room.

She flipped back to the earlier page, to the EVIDENCE OF INTRUSION log. The photograph entry stared back at her, the ORIGIN MEMORY column blank. She looked at the closed notebook cover, where the photo was tucked. She didn't take it out. She knew what she would see. A boy, the tree, the blue swing set.

A thought, cold and clear: the boy in the dream was older than the boy in the photo. The dream was a continuation. A later chapter.

Her stomach turned over. She shut the notebook.

The apartment was too quiet. The hum was gone. The silence had a texture, a thick, velvety quality that seemed to absorb sound. She could hear the blood moving in her own ears. A high, thin whistle, the tail-end of the static.

She got up. She needed to move. To do something normal. She decided to unpack the last box, the OFFICE box. She needed shelves. She'd go to the hardware store later, buy some brackets and boards. A project. A physical, real-world project with measurable steps.

She sliced the tape on the OFFICE box. Books first. She stacked them on the table: a history of urban planning, a few old tech manuals, a novel she'd never finished. Then the lockbox with the external drive. She left it. Then, at the bottom, wrapped in an old sweater was the stapler from her old desk. A heavy, steel thing. Beneath it, her fingers brushed against paper.

Not a book. A sheaf of papers, folded in thirds.

She pulled them out. They were photocopies. Faded, the text blurry. Architectural schematics. She unfolded them. The header read: “Pine Ridge Residential Development – Phase 2.” A site plan. She recognized the street names. It was a subdivision on the west side of the city built in the late eighties. She'd worked on a permit review for a renovation there, years ago. These were leftover copies. Why had she kept them?

She went to toss them back into the box when a detail snagged her eye. Lot 14. The shape of the backyard was familiar. A deep rectangle, with a notation for a “mature white birch (to be preserved).”

She spread the paper flat. Her pulse, which had just settled began a slow, heavy drumming.

In the dream, the backyard in the photograph had a birch tree. A birch with a kink like an elbow.

She traced the lot lines with a trembling finger. The schematic showed the house footprint, the driveway. In the back, near the property line, a small square was marked “SWINGSET/PLAY AREA.”

She dropped the paper. It fluttered to the floor.

Coincidence. It had to be. A generic backyard. Birch trees were common. Swingsets were common. Her brain, dreaming had cobbled together a generic childhood backdrop from fragments of forgotten paperwork. That's all. The brain was a magpie. It collected junk.

She forced a pause. It shuddered in her chest. She bent to pick up the schematic, and as she did, the air changed.

A new smell reached her.

It cut through the lemon cleaner and dust. It was the smell of cut grass, sharp and green. And underneath it, the dry, aromatic scent of cedar. Like a hedge, or a chip mulch bed. It was the smell of a well-kept yard on a summer afternoon. It was the smell from the photograph, from the dream.

Not from inside the apartment. From under her front door. A faint, distinct draft carrying the scent of a different place, a different home.

She froze, half-crouched. The schematic was limp in her hand.

The smell grew stronger. Not overpowering, but clear. It didn't belong in this building of lemon cleaner and old cigarettes. It belonged to lawns and gardens. To the Pine Ridge subdivision, Lot 14.

She stayed there, motionless, for a full minute. The scent didn't fade. It lingered, a ghost of a season, a ghost of a life.

Then, slowly, it began to dissipate. The green sharpness softened, blended with the dry cedar, and then both faded, leaving behind only the stale apartment air.

She straightened up. Her legs were numb. She walked to the front door, her steps silent on the carpet. She put her eye to the peephole. The hallway was empty, lit by a flickering fluorescent fixture. Beige walls, beige carpet. No grass. No cedar chips.

She unlocked the deadbolt. The click was loud. She opened the door a crack.

The hallway air was cool, slightly stuffy. It smelled of generic carpet cleaner and, from another apartment, the greasy tang of yesterday's fried food. No cut grass. No cedar.

She closed the door. Locked it. She leaned her forehead against the cool wood.

She had documented the sound. She had explained it away. But this... this was a new sense. Olfactory. Harder to dismiss as a dream echo. It had happened while she was fully awake, standing in her sunlit, empty living room.

She turned and looked at the schematic on the floor, at the notebook on the table. Her grid was failing. The data was leaking.

She walked back to the table. She sat down. She opened the notebook to the AUDITORY EVENT entry. At the bottom, below her neat list of explanations, she wrote a new line.

“Post-inspection, 10:17 AM. Olfactory event. Smell of cut grass and cedar wood. Origin: hallway under door. Duration approx. 60 seconds. No visible source. No logical environmental cause.”

Her pen hesitated. Then, beneath it, she wrote a single word, circled it, a desperate, silent concession to the trapdoor swinging wide.

“Corroboration.”
