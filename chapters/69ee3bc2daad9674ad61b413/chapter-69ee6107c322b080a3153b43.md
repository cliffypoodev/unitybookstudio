The silence after Decimus’s headshake stretched. It wasn’t the quiet of discipline, but the hollow, airless quiet of a vessel cracked open. The normal morning sounds of Jerusalem—the cart, the cock, the murmur—felt like a painting on a far wall, something flat and irrelevant.

Gaius broke it. He pushed himself up from the ground, his movements stiff, like a man twice his age. He didn’t look at the tomb. He walked to where his helmet had rolled, picked it up, and knocked the dirt from its crest with three precise, mechanical raps against his thigh. That sound was obscenely loud.

“Report,” Gaius said. The word was a stone dropped into a well.

Decimus’s tongue was a dry scrap in his mouth. Report. The word had structure, procedure. It demanded facts. The seal shattered without cause. One stone door became light as chaff. A light that was not light filled the chamber. The ground moved but made no sound. The burial linens were empty and folded. Each fact was a stepping stone into madness. He opened his mouth. A croak came out. He swallowed, tasting blood and dust.

“The tomb,” he managed. “It’s… vacated.”

“We have eyes,” Marius said, but the usual bite was gone. His voice was frayed. He still faced the dark entrance, his pilum now more a walking stick than a weapon. “By who? How?”

“Not by who,” Lucius whispered. He was staring at his own feet, as if confirming they still touched the earth. “Not by any who.”

Felix wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. His face was the color of old cheese. “We were all thrown. The ground… it wasn’t a quake. It was a pulse.”

Proculus cradled his injured arm against his chest. “The light came from inside. I saw it under the door, before it… before it moved.”

“The door moved because the ground shook,” Marius insisted, the argument weak, automatic. A last twitch of a dying reflex.

“My mother,” Lucius said, to no one. “She told me stories. Of messengers. Of empty tombs.”

“Shut your superstitious mouth,” Marius snapped, but it lacked heat. His eyes darted to the entrance again, then away, as if the darkness had grown teeth.

Decimus’s thumb found the edge of his breastplate again. The bronze was cool, the join seamless. His fingers traced the curve. Fix this. But there was no flaw to correct, no dirt to scour. The rupture was in the world itself. He looked at his men. Felix, pale and shivering. Proculus, cradling his arm, his clever sarcasm evaporated. Lucius, lost in childhood fears. Gaius, a statue waiting for an order. Marius, a defiant shell.

A report. Pilate’s seal was broken. The governor’s guaranteed tomb was open. The body was gone. Those were the facts. The causes—the light, the pulse, the impossible geometry of folded cloth—those were poetry. And discipline was obedience. The rest was poetry.

“We secure the site,” Decimus said. The voice that came out was not his own; it was flat, a commander’s voice borrowed from a man who no longer existed. “No one enters. No one leaves. Gaius, you and Proculus take the eastern garden wall. Watch the path from the city. Felix, the western perimeter. Lucius, with me at the entrance. Marius.”

Marius finally turned from the tomb. His expression was naked, stripped of its usual cynical armor. He just looked tired.

“Search the immediate area,” Decimus continued. “For… disturbances. Footprints. Tools. Anything.”

“There won’t be any,” Marius said, but he nodded. It was a task. A concrete thing.

The men moved, a slow, disjointed scattering. They avoided looking at the open maw of the tomb. Gaius helped Proculus up, supporting his good side, and they moved toward the low stone wall that bordered the garden’s east side. Felix stumbled toward the olive trees, one hand on his stomach. Marius began a slow, wide circle, his eyes on the torn earth, his pilum held loosely.

Decimus was left with Lucius by the entrance. The younger soldier’s breath hitched in short, shallow gasps. He kept glancing at Decimus, waiting for a cue, for a sign that the world had snapped back into its familiar shape.

“Steady,” Decimus murmured. The word was ash.

He made himself look into the antechamber. The dawn light reached about a pace inside before fading into black. He could just make out the edge of the archway leading to the burial chamber. Nothing moved. The cold air still seeped out, carrying that faint, clean scent of cut stone and myrrh. It smelled like a market stall. Like commerce. The absurdity of it caught in his throat.

Lucius shifted his weight. “Sir? What do we… what do we tell them?”

Them. The centurion. The tribune. Pilate. The priests. A cascade of authority waiting for a story that would not start a war.

“We tell them the facts,” Decimus said.

“They’ll say we slept.”

“They will.”

“They’ll have us flogged. Or worse.”

Decimus knew the math. Eight guards. A broken imperial seal. A missing body of political significance. The least messy solution was to execute the guards for dereliction. A lesson in discipline. He saw his father against the wall again, not as a memory, but as a premonition. He refused an order. I lost a seal. Different sins, same wall.

“Then we find better facts,” Decimus heard himself say.

From across the garden, Marius called out. His voice was sharp, confused. “Decimus.”

He and Lucius turned. Marius was standing near the base of the gnarled olive tree where Decimus had spent the watch. He was staring at the ground.

Decimus walked over, Lucius at his heels. The other soldiers paused, looking.

“Look,” Marius said. He pointed.

At first, Decimus saw nothing. Scuffed dirt, a few fallen olive leaves. Then he saw the pattern. The soil was disturbed in a perfect, wide circle around the tree, as if a giant hand had pressed down and twisted. But within that circle, radiating from the trunk, were lines. Dozens of them, etched into the firm earth as if by a fine tool. They swirled outward, a silent, concentric explosion frozen in dirt.

“They weren’t here before,” Marius said. His certainty was absolute. “I leaned against this tree at third watch. This was just flat dirt.”

Decimus crouched. He didn’t touch the lines. They were deep, precise. They made no sense as footprints, as tool marks. They looked like the grooves in a potter’s wheel. Or like ripples in water, if water could solidify in an instant.

“What in Hades…” Proculus whispered, coming up beside them, Gaius supporting him.

Lucius made a sign against evil, his fingers fumbling near his chest.

A cold that had nothing to do with the morning settled in Decimus’s joints. This wasn’t evidence of a theft. This was a signature. A mark left by whatever force had passed through. Proof, and it was utterly damning. You couldn’t take this to a tribune. You couldn’t say, the earth rippled like water, sir. They’d be declared insane on the spot.

“Cover it,” Decimus said, straightening up.

Marius stared at him. “What?”

“Kick dirt over it. Now. All of you.”

The men hesitated, caught between the strangeness of the marks and the flat command in his voice.

“Do it!” The crack of authority finally spurred them. They began scuffing their boots through the complex lines, destroying the pattern. Dust rose in small clouds. The perfect ripples vanished under chaotic, human tracks.

As they worked, Decimus turned his back on them. He looked toward the city. The sun was higher now, bleaching the color from the stone walls. A hawk circled over the Temple Mount, a tiny, distant speck. Normal. Everything was relentlessly, insultingly normal.

His thumb went back to his breastplate. He found a smudge—a faint streak of the white stone dust from the tremor. He rubbed at it with his thumb. The powder smeared but didn’t vanish. It ground into the bronze, a pale ghost of a stain.

A sound began.

It wasn’t a sound you heard with your ears first. You felt it in the soles of your feet, a low, resonant hum coming up from the bedrock. It vibrated in the fillings of his teeth. It was the note a giant bowstring might make, drawn to its absolute limit.

The men stopped scuffing at the dirt. They went still.

That hum deepened, gathering weight. It wasn’t loud. It was everywhere. In the air, in the stone of the tomb, in the bones of the olive trees. The leaves on the tree above him began to shiver, though there was no wind.

Lucius made that small, choked sound again.

The hum intensified, vibrating up through Decimus’s legs, into his spine. It promised something. It was the drawn breath before a shout. The coiled tension in a muscle before it strikes.

He looked at the tomb entrance. The darkness inside seemed to thicken, to pulse in time with the submerged frequency shaking the world.

Coming back.

Gaius was the first to move. He didn’t speak. He simply turned and started walking, stiffly at first, then faster, toward the garden gate that led to the city path. He didn’t run. It was a deliberate, urgent retreat.

Felix watched him for two heartbeats, then bolted, crashing through a rosemary bush in his haste to get away from the tomb, the tree, the humming earth.

“Stop!” Marius roared, but the command was swallowed by the rising vibration. Proculus was already backing away, his good hand held out as if to ward off the air itself.

Lucius looked at Decimus, his eyes wide with a silent plea. Order us to flee. Please.

The hum reached a pitch that seemed to press against Decimus’s eardrums. A fine spray of dirt trickled from the tomb’s lintel. The ground was no longer still; it trembled, a continuous, subtle shudder.

Marius grabbed Lucius’s arm. “We stand! We are soldiers of Rome!”

But his grip was tight with fear, not conviction. He was holding Lucius for his own anchor as much as to restrain him.

Decimus’s own legs trembled, a sympathetic resonance with the earth. Every instinct screamed to run, to follow Gaius and Felix into the bland, ignorant safety of the waking city. To abandon the post. To live.

He saw the folded cloth in his mind. The perfect, empty fold.

He planted his feet wider, bracing against the tremor. He drew his gladius. The steel blade caught the sun, a tiny, defiant flash of mundane light in the face of the growing, soundless thunder.

“Hold the line,” he said, though he could barely hear his own voice over the hum filling his skull.

It wasn’t an order for them. It was a last, desperate pact with himself. He would see it. Whatever was coming, he would not turn his back. He would have one true fact to report, even if it was the last thing he ever did.

The humming crest broke.

Not into sound, but into pressure. A wall of silent force slammed out from the tomb entrance. It didn’t throw him this time. It pressed him down, driving the air from his lungs, forcing his head toward the shuddering ground. He fought to look up, his vision blurring at the edges.

From the darkness within the tomb, a light began to grow. It was not the violent white flash of before. This was slower, deeper—a cool, blue-white radiance that spilled from the archway like water, pooling in the antechamber before flowing out into the garden.

It touched the trampled dirt where the strange ripples had been. It touched the roots of the olive tree.

And where it touched, the earth ceased its trembling.

The light did not illuminate. It clarified.

The world Decimus saw through the pressure crushing his chest was not the garden he knew. The stone of the tomb was no longer rough-hewn limestone; every pit, every fossilized shell in its surface was carved in impossible detail, a map of a million years rendered in an instant. The olive leaves above him were not green, but a vibrating fabric of silver and charcoal, each vein a river on a leaf-shaped continent. He could see the sap moving inside the twigs, a slow, golden pulse. This was not seeing. It was being forced to know.

The pressure relented.

It didn’t fade. It snapped off, as if a giant hand had simply lifted. Decimus gasped, his lungs burning as they refilled with ordinary air. The hyper-clarity vanished, leaving the garden looking dull, fake, like a cheap theater set.

Silence rushed in. The deep foundational hum was gone. The tremor in the earth had stopped. The only sound was the ragged chorus of men breathing, and a high, thin ringing in his own ears.

He pushed himself up onto his elbows. Lucius was curled on his side a few feet away, retching dryly into the dirt. Marius was on his knees, head bowed, one hand splayed on the ground as if to confirm it was solid. Proculus had fallen against the garden wall, clutching his injured arm, his face a mask of pure confusion. Of Gaius and Felix, there was no sign.

One light from the tomb persisted. It filled the antechamber with that cool, silent blue-white radiance, but it went no further. It lapped at the threshold like a tide pinned by an unseen shore. It was not the light of a lamp or the sun. It was the light of a moon that didn’t exist, cold and sourceless.

Then the light moved.

Not the glow itself, but something within it. A shadow. A form.

It resolved in the archway of the burial chamber, standing where the burial bench would be. It was not a man. It had the suggestion of a man’s shape—a verticality, a presence—but it was wrought from the light itself, and from the darkness behind it. It was a silhouette cut from reality, edged with a corona of blinding silver. Decimus could not look at it directly; his eyes skittered away, watering, capturing only impressions: a suggestion of height that seemed to change, a stillness more potent than any motion.

No one made a sound. Lucius stopped retching. Marius stopped breathing.

The form did not speak. It turned its head—or the space where a head would be—and looked out through the antechamber, past the threshold, into the garden. Into Decimus.

The gaze was not eyes. It was an assessment. A recognition. It saw the dirt ground into his knuckles, the smear of white dust on his breastplate, the terrified Roman mind trying and failing to categorize it. It saw his father against the wall. It saw the quiet farm in Campania where his mother waited. It saw the folded linens.

He was a single, raw point of awareness, stripped of rank, of history, of language. The fear did not vanish; it transformed. It was no longer terror of death or punishment, but the dizzying, nauseating fear of being known so completely by something so utterly other.

Then it was gone.

Not that it walked away. The form simply was not there. The light in the burial chamber dimmed, fading from a radiance to a gentle glow, like the memory of light on a white wall after sunset.

The ordinary darkness of a sealed rock chamber did not return. The space within was simply empty, and profoundly black.

The silence that followed was different. It was the silence of a house after a great wind has passed through, leaving everything standing but fundamentally rearranged. Decimus heard a sparrow chirp somewhere down the hill. Its sound was obscene.

Marius let out a long, shuddering breath. It was the sound of a man surfacing from deep water. He climbed to his feet, movements clumsy. He did not look at the tomb. He stared at his own hand, flexing his fingers as if he’d never seen them before.

“What…” Proculus’s voice was a dry rasp. He didn’t finish.

Lucius sat up, wiping his mouth. He was crying, silent tears cutting tracks through the dust on his cheeks. He made no effort to hide them.

Decimus stood. His legs held him. He still gripped his gladius. The weight of the familiar hilt felt absurd, a child’s toy against what had just passed. He sheathed it, the click of the latch deafening in the quiet.

His eyes were drawn back to the tomb entrance. The great, circular stone door still lay where it had been flung, half in, half out of its track, tilted at a drunken angle. The black opening yawned. The strange light was gone from the antechamber now too, leaving only the natural shadow of rock.

A thought, dry and procedural, surfaced in the wreckage of his mind. The seal is broken. The tomb is open. The body is gone. The facts remained. They were now the least important truths in the world, but they were the only ones that had a language.

He took a step toward the entrance. Then another.

“Decimus.” Marius’s voice was a warning, but it had no force.

He kept walking. He passed the threshold. The chill of the rock enveloped him, that market-stall scent of myrrh and cut stone. The antechamber was empty. The archway to the burial chamber was a portal into pure dark.

He did not enter. He stood at the edge, looking in.

From within the blackness, a detail emerged. Not seen, but remembered from the brief, hyper-real vision the light had forced upon him. The burial bench. The folded linens. They were not tossed aside. They were arranged. The cloth that had been around the head was not with the others; it was set apart, folded in a neat square, placed where the head would have rested.

This was not theft. This was a message left in linen. I do not need this anymore.

A scrape of stone on stone echoed from outside.

Decimus turned. Marius was backing away from the entrance, his pilum raised again, not in attack, but in a warding gesture. Lucius had scrambled to his feet. Proculus was pressed against the garden wall, pointing with a trembling hand.

The great stone disc was moving.

It was not rolling. It was righting itself. The several-hundred-pound mass of carved limestone, tilted in its track, began to level. It made a low, grinding grumble, the sound of immense weight settling. Then, with a sigh of dust, it began to roll backward.

Not from a push. Not from any force Decimus could see. It moved along its stone track with a smooth, deliberate, impossible momentum. It rolled back along the path it had torn when it was flung inward, back across the threshold, out of the antechamber.

Decimus stood frozen in the doorway, watching it come. It should have crushed him. It rolled past him with the quiet precision of a servant closing a door, missing him by the width of a hand. The cool, damp air of the tomb washed over his back.

The stone rolled out into the garden morning. It didn’t stop at its original, closed position. It continued, rolling cleanly along its track until it cleared the entrance completely, until it reached the small stone curb meant to halt it. It settled there with a final, soft thunk.

Now parked completely aside, a perfect, open doorway between the world of sunlight and olives and the world of cut rock and folded cloth.

The tomb was not just open. It was offered.

Decimus stood in the shadow of the arch, looking out at the garden. The sun was fully up now. It painted the olive leaves in green and gold. It sparkled on the dew not yet burned away. It lit the faces of his men—Marius’s stark disbelief, Lucius’s tear-streaked awe, Proculus’s dumbstruck horror. It glinted off the bronze of the discarded helmet Gaius had left behind.

The world had not ended. The hawk still circled. The sparrow still chirped. The city of Jerusalem, just down the hill, was waking to its bread and its prayers and its politics, utterly unaware that the bedrock of everything had just sighed and shifted a fraction of an inch.

He stepped out of the tomb. The sunlight felt thin, insubstantial.

Marius lowered his pilum. “Why?” he asked, the word torn from him. Not how. Why.

Decimus had no answer. He looked at the perfectly rolled stone, now a silent monument to an impossible physics. He looked at the open doorway, a mouth that had spoken in light and stone and was now quiet. He looked at his men, broken not by violence, but by witness.

The report was due. The facts were waiting. But the only truth that mattered was the one vibrating in his marrow, a truth with no words: the door had been closed, and now it was open. And it had been opened from the inside.

The sparrow chirped again, a bright, stupid sound. It was the only thing that felt real.

Marius broke first. He took three stumbling steps backward, his pilum dropping from his hand to clatter on the rocky path. He didn’t look at it. His eyes were fixed on the stone, parked so neatly to the side, a servant dismissed. He turned and vomited into a clump of sage, the heaves violent and empty.

Lucius just sat where he had fallen, his arms wrapped around his knees, rocking slightly. A low, continuous moan hummed in his throat, the sound a wounded animal might make. He stared at nothing, his eyes wide and unblinking, seeing the form in the light over and over.

Proculus slid down the garden wall until he was sitting in the dirt, cradling his injured arm. He kept shaking his head, a short, sharp denial. “No,” he muttered to the ground. “No, no, no.” It was a chant, a ward against the memory.

Decimus stood between the tomb and his men. The air felt different in his lungs—thin, used up. He became aware of a dozen irrelevant details: the way a vein throbbed in his temple in time with his heartbeat, the exact pattern of mud spatter on his left greave, the sharp, medicinal smell of the crushed sage where Marius was sick. His mind was a shattered mosaic. He tried to fit the pieces into a report. At approximately dawn, the stone door, which had previously been displaced inward, righted itself and rolled to its open position of its own accord. The sentence was insanity. He could already hear the centurion’s derisive snort, see the cold calculation in the tribune’s eyes.

He made himself look at the tomb. The opening was a black rectangle framed by rough-hewn stone. The strange light was entirely gone. It was just a hole in a hill. But it was a hole that had whispered out a silent thunder and a being of light. It was a hole that had seen him.

His thumb went to his breastplate, to the smear of white dust. He rubbed at it. The stain was part of the metal now.

“Gaius and Felix are gone.”

Marius’s voice was raw. He’d wiped his mouth on his sleeve. He wasn’t looking at Decimus; he was scanning the garden paths, the city gate. His face had settled into a grim, familiar mask, but it was cracked. A muscle in his jaw jumped like a trapped thing.

Decimus nodded. It was a fact. A manageable one. Two men had abandoned their post. That was a crime with a known punishment. It was a piece of the world that still functioned by Roman rules.

“We go after them,” Marius said. It wasn’t a question. It was a lifeline. A pursuit. A tangible mission with steps: find the deserters, subdue them, bring them back. It would give them all something to do, a script to follow until their minds caught up.

“No.”

The word left Decimus’s mouth before the thought was fully formed. Marius stared at him.

“They saw,” Decimus said. His own voice sounded distant. “They saw it, and they ran. Let them run.”

“They’ll talk,” Marius insisted, a flare of his old pragmatism. “They’ll run to the first wine shop and babble about ghosts and lights. The story will be all over the Lower City by noon. You think the priests won’t hear? You think the tribune won’t?”

“Let them babble.” Decimus turned his head, his neck stiff. “Who will believe them? Drunken guards, fleeing their post, telling tales of earth spirits and moving stones. It’s the ravings of cowards. It discredits them. It might even,” he said, the logic coiling cold and clear, “discredit anything else that is said about this place.”

Marius’s eyes narrowed. He was listening now, the soldier in him latching onto tactics.

“If we drag them back in chains,” Decimus continued, “they become part of an official inquiry. Their testimony gets recorded. Their… ravings become part of the legion’s problem. This way, they’re just two deserters. A separate matter.” He was building a wall, stone by stone, between the impossible thing that had happened and the official report that had to be written. The deserters were loose mortar, best swept away.

Proculus let out a wet, shaky laugh. “Ravings. Right.” He looked at his trembling hands. “What do we say, then? We stood our ground against a… a light that pressed us into the dirt? We saw a man made of moonbeam? We’ll be joining my father in the mines by sundown.”

“We say we were attacked,” Lucius said, low. He’d stopped rocking. His eyes were clear now, focused on Decimus with a desperate intensity. “By followers of the prophet. A large group. They overpowered us. They rolled the stone.”

It was the obvious lie. The expected one. It was the story the priests would already be hoping for, the story Pilate would find most convenient. A political crime, not a theological cataclysm.

The lie would save their skins. But the truth, the silent, shattering truth, had already begun its work. It was a seed of ice in his gut, and he knew, with a certainty that had nothing to do with reason, that it would never melt. It would only grow, pressing against the walls of the lie he was about to build, until something cracked.
