The garden gate swung shut behind Mary and Joanna with a soft, final thud. That sound seemed to echo in the unnatural quiet they left behind.

Decimus stood where the women had been, his back to the open tomb. The sun was higher now, bleaching the color from the olive leaves. It baked the dust on his greaves. He could hear the others behind him, a low murmur of panic held in check by sheer habit. Marius was breathing through his mouth, short, sharp puffs. Proculus muttered a prayer to Mithras, his injured arm cradled against his chest. Lucius hadn’t moved from where he’d sunk to the ground, his face the color of old ash.

One world had not ended. That was the strange, flat fact of it. A rooster crowed. A breeze carried the smell of thyme from somewhere. A stone was still rolled away, the black mouth of the tomb still yawning. Nothing had changed, yet everything had.

“Report.”

The word came from Gaius. He stood apart, near the fig tree, his posture rigid. He hadn’t fled with the others. He’d retreated, a tactical withdrawal to the edge of the garden, and now he’d returned. His face was a closed door. Decimus turned to face him. The eight men—those who hadn’t bolted into the city—were watching. Their eyes were wide, whites showing.

What was there to say? The seal broke. One stone moved. A light that was not light. A pressure that was not sound. An empty space where a corpse should be. Linens folded. Women seeing angels. It was a list of impossibilities. To speak it aloud was to invite madness, or a charge of it.

Decimus cleared his throat. It felt full of grit. “The tomb is empty. The burial cloths remain inside. Arranged.” He kept his voice low, monotone. A field report. “The civilian women arrived shortly after. They witnessed the… vacancy. They received a message from a male figure at the site. They departed in a state of agitation.”

Gaius stared at him. The optio’s eyes were the color of flint. He said nothing for a long count of ten. A bee droned past his ear and he didn’t flinch. “Displaced.”

“Yes.”

“By what agency?”

That question hung in the air. Decimus could feel the weight of the other men’s silence. They were waiting for him to say it, to give voice to the thing humming in the garden like a struck bell.

“Unknown,” Decimus said.

Gaius’s jaw worked. A tiny muscle flickered under his ear. “Your assessment. Theft? Decoy?”

“No.” The answer was immediate, too quick for a proper soldier. Decimus forced himself to slow down. He looked at the ground, at a crack in the flagstone that cradled a single, perfect ant. “The linens. They were… collapsed. As if the body simply… vacated them. The face cloth was folded. Set apart. No thief takes the time. No conspirator bothers with the laundry.”

“You are a soldier, Valerius, not a philosopher of linens.” Gaius took a step forward. The distance between them crackled. “You are telling me a corpse vanished from a sealed tomb. You are telling me your men”—his gaze swept over them—“saw lights. Felt tremors. Now you cite neatly folded cloth as evidence of the supernatural.”

“I am telling you what is.” Decimus’s voice didn’t rise. It felt scraped raw. “The body is gone. It was not taken by human hands. That is the fact.”

A snort came from Marius. He’d found his footing again, anger rushing in to fill the hollow fear. “A fact. He says it’s a fact. I saw a light, I’ll grant you. I felt the ground shake. I’ve seen a man’s guts spill in the sand and he saw lights too, before he died. It means nothing. Someone moved the body. The rest is panic. Night-fear.”

“The stone, Marius,” Lucius whispered from the ground. He was staring at his own hands as if he didn’t recognize them. “You felt it move. You said it was like a sigh.”

“I said shut up.”

Gaius held up a hand. The movement was precise, final. The argument died. He looked at Decimus, through him. “You entered the tomb alone.”

“I did.”

“And you saw no sign of forced entry from within? No tunnel? No secondary aperture?”

“It is a single chamber. Carved from bedrock. There is no other way in or out.”

“And the official seal?”

“Shattered. As documented at the change of watch.”

Gaius nodded once. He turned on his heel, his gaze taking in the garden, the tomb, the huddle of men. His mind was visibly working, discarding the impossible, sorting the actionable. Decimus had seen it before, at the siege of Jotapata. When the report came of a wall section collapsing from the inside, no enemy sapper found, Gaius had dismissed the talk of demons, ordered the breach filled, and doubled the watch. A problem, not a portent.

“This is a political incident,” Gaius said. His voice was different now. Cool. Administrative. “The corpse of a publicly executed agitator, interred under Roman authority is missing. The location was under Roman guard. This is a failure of duty. A breach of security.” He looked at each of them in turn. “You will not speak of lights. You will not speak of tremors. You will not speak of… folded cloth. You will report the facts as they pertain to the chain of command. The tomb was found open. The body was not present. You apprehended civilian mourners at the scene who were likewise surprised by the absence. Is that understood?”

A low murmur of assent. It was a lifeline. A story they could hold onto that didn’t involve the fabric of the world unraveling.

“But the women,” Proculus blurted. “They saw the man. The one in white. They’ll tell someone.”

“Let them,” Gaius said. “They are hysterical mourners. Their testimony is grief, not evidence. Ours is the only testimony that matters to the tribune and to the Temple priests, who will be screaming for answers before noon.” He pointed a finger at Decimus. “You. With me. The rest of you, secure this site. No one enters. No one touches anything. If any of those… disciples… return, you detain them. Politely. You are rocks. You are statues. You feel nothing, you saw nothing but an empty hole. Understood?”

The men nodded, a jerky synchronization of relief. A simple order. A tangible duty. Marius straightened his helmet, his face settling into his familiar lines of grudging competence.

Gaius started walking toward the city gate, not looking back. Decimus fell into step beside him, his legs moving stiffly. The garden receded behind them, the tomb’s shadow shrinking in the rising sun. The normal sounds of a Jerusalem morning grew louder—the distant clatter of a cart, a vendor’s call, the bleat of a goat. Each sound felt like a slap.

They walked in silence for a hundred paces along a dusty path lined with rough stones. Decimus focused on the back of Gaius’s neck, on a patch of sunburned skin just above his armor’s edge.

“You believe it,” Gaius said, not turning his head.

Decimus didn’t answer.

“I don’t care what you believe,” Gaius continued. His voice was low, for Decimus alone. “But if you carry that… that look… into the Antonia, they will see it. The tribune will see it. He will think you part of it. Complicit or incompetent or mad. Any of those is a death sentence for your pension. Maybe for you.”

“I know.”

“Do you?” Gaius finally glanced at him. His flint eyes were unreadable. “You saw something. Fine. Lock it in a box. Bury the box. What matters now is what we say next. The story is simple. The tomb was robbed. We failed to prevent it. We will accept the punishment. The punishment will be a fine maybe a flogging. It will end. This other thing…” He shook his head, a minute twitch. “This other thing does not end. If you give it words, it will grow. It will swallow you and your mother’s hope of a farm in Campania whole. Do you understand?”

The words were not kindness. They were a calculation. Gaius was protecting the unit, minimizing damage. But the truth of them was a cold stone in Decimus’s gut. He saw his mother’s face, not as a memory but as a ghost on the road ahead, her expression crumbling as a stranger told her his ashes would not be coming home.

“I understand,” Decimus said.

“Good.” Gaius faced forward again. The walls of Jerusalem loomed ahead, Herod’s Gate a dark maw. “When we report, you will say only what I told the men to say. The tomb was open. The body gone. The women were present. Nothing more.”

“And if they ask what I think happened?”

“You are a soldier of Rome. You do not think. You observe and report.” Gaius’s pace didn’t falter. “The tribune will ask the questions. You will give the answers. Then you will be silent. Your silence is your only shield now, Valerius. Do not drop it.”

They passed under the shadow of the gate. The coolness was a shock. The smell changed—dust and dung to packed earth and sweat and cooking oil. The city closed around them. Decimus kept his eyes on the cobbles, on the worn-smooth path that led to the fortress. He locked the box. He buried it deep.

But in the dark of his mind, an image remained, clearer than any memory of his mother’s face: a small, neat square of linen, folded upon stone. A deliberate leaving. It did not feel like an end. It felt like a door swinging open onto a dark, impossible room. And he was being ordered to stand in the hallway and pretend he heard nothing saw nothing knew nothing at all.

A guard post was a cube of shadow and stale air built into the thickness of the city wall near the Damascus Gate. It smelled of old smoke, sour wine, and the tang of men who’d slept in their armor. A single, high slit of a window cut the gloom with a bar of white morning light, full of swirling dust. Gaius shut the heavy wooden door behind them. The outside world muffled to a murmur.

He didn’t take the stool behind the rough plank table. He remained standing, his back to the door, blocking it. He set his helmet on the table with a deliberate click. This sound was an anchor in the quiet.

“From the beginning,” Gaius said. “The watch change.”

Decimus stood at attention, his eyes fixed on a water stain on the mud-brick wall behind Gaius’s head. It resembled a galloping horse, or maybe a hound. He’d never decided. He recited the facts, the same ones he’d given in the garden, stripped even barer. The intact seal at fourth watch. The routine patrol. The moment the first guard—Lucius—noticed the stone’s position. The investigation. The emptiness.

He did not mention the quality of the silence before the stone moved. He did not describe the way the folded face cloth had looked in his torchlight, a small, white island of terrible order. He made it a logistics report. An inventory discrepancy.

Gaius listened without moving. When Decimus finished, the only sound was the distant cry of a water-seller in the street. Gaius’s fingers tapped once, twice, on the table’s edge. The rhythm was irregular. Irritated.

“Lucius,” Gaius said. “He’s from Cyrene. A wool-merchant’s son. Sees omens in spilled salt.”

“He’s a reliable soldier.”

“He was on first watch. Alone.”

“The post is a two-man watch. Standard procedure. He was relieved by Marius and Proculus at the change.”

“And between the change and the discovery, the tomb was unobserved.”

“For minutes. Yes.”

“Minutes are enough.” Gaius pushed off from the door and paced the narrow width of the room, three steps each way. His movements were compact, efficient. He stopped by the window, the light etching the lines of his profile. “A trained team, working in the dark. They could have rolled the stone, extracted the body, re-set it to appear sealed from a distance. The seal breaks from the inside if the stone moves. You know this.”

“The track was clean,” Decimus said. His voice felt too loud in the small space. “No drag marks. No chocks. No debris. A stone that size moved by men in a hurry leaves gouges, chips. There was nothing. The track was smooth, as if it had never been used.”

“Or it was swept.”

“In the dark? Under watch?”

“You said the watch was changing. Confusion. A moment of inattention.” Gaius turned from the window. His face was in shadow again. “It is more plausible than your theory.”

“I have no theory.”

“You have a preference. Your silence on the matter is a shout, Valerius.” Gaius leaned on the table, his palms flat. The wood creaked. “You want it to be a mystery. A divine intervention. It absolves you of failure. A god is harder to court-martial than a band of grave-robbers.”

A hot knot tightened behind Decimus’s breastbone. He kept his gaze on the water stain. The horse had a broken leg. It was just a stain. “I am reporting conditions. The body is gone. The means of its removal lack a human signature. That is all.”

“Lack a human signature.” Gaius repeated the phrase like he’d found a rotten nut in his meal. “You’ve been listening to the Greek philosophers. Next you’ll be quoting Epicurus on the nature of voids.” He straightened. “The means are not our concern. The consequence is. A politically sensitive corpse is missing from Roman custody. The Temple aristocracy demanded the guard. Pilate authorized it. This,” he said, gesturing vaguely toward the door, toward the city, “is a diplomatic incident wrapped in a professional disgrace. The priests will use it to bludgeon the Prefect. The Prefect will need a head to roll. Several heads.”

He let the words hang. The dust motes danced in the light bar. Decimus could hear the faint scrape of a mouse in the wall. A real sound. A mundane one.

“The women’s testimony is useless,” Gaius continued, thinking aloud now. “Hysterics. Visions. They are peasants. The Temple will dismiss them. Our testimony is the only one that carries weight. And our testimony must be… manageable.”

Decimus finally looked at him. “Manageable.”

“A story that contains a culprit. A story that ends.” Gaius’s eyes met his. They held no malice, only a cold, appraising clarity. “The tomb was robbed. One guard was overcome—by stealth, by numbers, it doesn’t matter. We failed. We accept the punishment. The matter is closed. The alternative is to report that the earth shook and a dead prophet walked out, leaving his linens behind. That story has no end. It invites inquiry. It inspires fanatics. It turns a contained failure into an uncontainable problem.”

“It is the truth.”

“It is a version of events witnessed by frightened men in the dark.” Gaius’s voice dropped, becoming almost conversational. “Do you know what I saw at Bethsaida? After the storm? Men swore they saw a figure walking on the water. They described a glow. A presence. They were half-drowned, salt-blind, and terrified. I saw a piece of wreckage, a spar from our own ship, riding the waves. The mind makes its own truth when it’s afraid. Your men were afraid. You are afraid.”

Decimus’s hands clenched at his sides. He felt the grit under his nails from the garden soil. A physical fact. “I was not afraid. I was… observing.”

“And what did you observe, truly? In the cold light of reason, stripped of the hour and the panic?” Gaius challenged. “You observed an absence. Everything else is interpretation. Poetry. We are not poets. We are soldiers. We deal in facts that can be acted upon. The fact is the body is gone. The actionable fact is that we must provide a reason for its disappearance that does not set the city on fire.”

He moved to a small, locked chest in the corner. He produced a key from a thong around his neck, opened it, and took out a wax tablet and a stylus. He brought them to the table and sat, finally, on the stool. The sunlight fell across his hands as he smoothed the wax.

“I am sending a preliminary report to the tribune at the Antonia,” Gaius said, not looking up, “and a separate, discreet message to the High Priest’s household. A courtesy. A show of cooperation. It is better they hear a manageable version from us first, before the wild tales from the women reach them.”

Decimus watched the stylus move. Gaius wrote in quick, abrupt strokes. He didn’t write much. He was not a man of many words. Each one would be chosen, weighed.

“What are you writing?” The question left Decimus’s lips before he could stop it.

Gaius didn’t pause. “That during the last watch of the night, the tomb of the Nazarene was breached by persons unknown. The Roman guard detail, though vigilant was unable to prevent the theft of the corpse. The site is secured. One guard awaits further instructions and stands ready to accept appropriate disciplinary measures for the security lapse.” He looked up. “Is that inaccurate?”

It was a lie of omission. A lie of framing. It was a clean, surgical lie that cut out the heart of the night and left a hollow, explainable shell. Decimus felt the box in his mind, the one he’d locked, rattle. “It is… incomplete.”

“It is complete enough to be filed. To be resolved.” Gaius scored a final line. “The tribune will be angry. There will be a fine. A flogging, perhaps, for appearance’s sake. Then it will be over. We will rotate back to Caesarea. In a year, no one will remember the name of the executed peasant, let alone that his grave was robbed.” He held Decimus’s gaze. “If we report the rest, they will never stop asking questions. They will interrogate each man separately. They will find inconsistencies—and there will be inconsistencies, because what you saw makes no sense. They will decide it is a conspiracy. A plot. A mutiny. And then, Valerius, the floggings will not be for appearance. They will be for truth. And they will not stop until the truth they want is the only one left breathing.”

He said it calmly. A tactical assessment. Decimus saw it with a horrible clarity: the damp room beneath the Antonia, the leather straps, the questions that circled back to the light, the folded cloth, the impossible vacancy. He saw Gaius enduring it, his jaw tight, giving them nothing but the manageable story. He saw himself breaking. He saw his mother waiting for a letter that would never come.

Gaius saw the understanding in his face. He gave a single shallow nod. He broke the seal on a small clay message cylinder, rolled his tablet slip inside, and pressed his ring into the clay. The High Priest’s symbol was already marked on the outside. He had it prepared. This had been his plan from the moment he left the garden.

“Take the main report to the Antonia yourself,” Gaius said, handing Decimus a second, sealed cylinder. “Deliver it to the tribune’s aide. Wait for a response. I will send the other message through a… discreet channel.” He stood. “The guard is yours until I return. Maintain the cordon. Admit no one. Not even priests. Tell them they must have authorization from the Prefect or the tribune. Stone faces, Valerius. You saw nothing but an empty hole.”

Decimus took the cylinder. The clay was cool, slightly rough. It weighed nothing. It felt like the heaviest thing he’d ever held.

Gaius moved past him to the door. He paused, his hand on the latch. He didn’t look back. “A man can carry the truth, or he can carry his life. Rarely both. Choose the burden that lets you keep walking.”

He opened the door. The noise of the city rushed in—a donkey braying, a child shrieking, the smell of bread baking. A world, ordinary and constant. Gaius stepped into it and was gone, melting into the stream of people heading toward the Temple Mount.

Decimus stood alone in the guard post. A bar of sunlight had crawled across the table, now illuminating the empty space where Gaius’s helmet had been. He looked down at the message cylinder in his hand. His thumb rubbed over the tribune’s seal.

Outside, the water-seller cried his price again. The mouse scratched in the wall. A fly buzzed against the window slit. Normal sounds. Real sounds.

He lifted the latch and stepped out into the blinding street, the lie tucked against his palm, the image of the folded cloth burning in the dark behind his eyes. The two burdens were already pulling him apart.

The Antonia Fortress loomed at the northwest corner of the Temple Mount, a squared-off malignancy of pale stone. Decimus crossed the courtyard, the eyes of sentries on his back. He focused on the feel of the cobbles through his sandals, on a chip in the second step of the administrative stair, on the way his own breath tasted of dust and thyme.

He delivered the cylinder to a bored clerk in an antechamber that smelled of ink and lamp oil. The man took it without a word, gesturing for him to wait on a wooden bench worn smooth by countless waiting soldiers. Decimus sat. He stared at a patch of sunlight on the opposite wall, watching it tremble as a breeze stirred a tree outside the high, barred window.

In his mind, the garden was silent. The tomb was dark. And the linen square, folded with a care that spoke of infinite leisure sat upon the cold stone bench, waiting for someone to understand its message.
