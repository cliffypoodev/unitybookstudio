The ache in her finger became a compass point. Every throb oriented Roman back toward the server bank, toward the code she’d left open on the terminal. She hadn’t saved it.

“Come see.”

She started from the top reading each function aloud in a whisper. The process was mechanical, numbing. It drove out the throb in her finger, the tightness in her neck. Code was pure. It either worked or it didn’t. There was no subtext, no hidden meaning, only bug or feature.

An hour in, she found it.

Not a flaw in her work. A foreign string, six lines long, nestled in a subroutine for power-flow diagnostics. It was elegant. It wouldn’t cause a failure. It would simply… observe. Record the system’s response to a simulated fault and then erase itself. The fence flicker.

Her stomach went cold. Someone had inserted this after her compile. They’d needed her new protocol as a carrier.

She traced the string’s origin point in the logs. Access ID: L_RAINE.

Lyra.

Roman pushed back from the terminal. The chair wheels squealed. She stared at the ID on the screen until the letters blurred. Then she deleted the query, wiped the log trace. The action felt automatic, her fingers moving before her mind had fully processed the why.

Confronting her meant taking it to Voss. Voss would have her dismantled, piece by piece, until she gave up a reason. Roman had seen his methods before. They were efficient. They were also permanent.
