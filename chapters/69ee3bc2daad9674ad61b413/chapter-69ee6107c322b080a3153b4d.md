Nathaniel's stylus hovered over the papyrus, the ink threatening to drip. The words he'd been ordered to write—the disciples came by night and stole the body while the guards slept—sat like a weight on his tongue. He could taste the lie, metallic and sour. His fingers twitched. A drop fell, blotting the edge of the resh in g'nuvim. Thieves.

Elazar leaned over his shoulder, close enough that Nathaniel caught the stale mint on his breath. "Again? You're wasting good parchment."

Nathaniel pressed his thumb to the smudge, spreading it into a deliberate streak. "The fibers are uneven. It won't take the ink."

A lie for a lie.

Elazar exhaled through his nose. "Scrape it clean. The commander expects this before midday."

The bronze scraper bit into the papyrus. Nathaniel worked methodically, removing every trace of the botched letter. The motion steadied him—the physical act of erasure. He'd done this before, revising minor discrepancies in tax records. Never a fabrication of this magnitude.

A fly buzzed against the window grating. Outside, the Temple courts hummed with ordinary commerce: the clink of coins, the bargaining cries of merchants, the lowing of sacrificial beasts. Life, uninterrupted.

The second attempt went smoother. His hand knew the shapes, even if his mind recoiled. The Roman guards, having partaken of wine, fell into a deep slumber. At first light, they awoke to find the stone rolled away and the body missing. They pursued the thieves but were unable to apprehend them.

He paused at unable. Too weak.

Elazar tapped the table. "Change it to were overpowered. Makes them sound less incompetent."

Nathaniel adjusted the phrasing, his stomach tightening. Every edit coiled the lie tighter, more plausible. He sanded the fresh ink, blew away the grit.

"Better." Elazar took the scroll, scanned it with the scrutiny of a man who'd built a career on bureaucratic precision. "Add a line about the guards being disciplined. Flogged. It sells the story."

Nathaniel dipped his stylus. "How many lashes?"

"Enough to sound credible, not enough to kill them. Twenty-five."

The number felt obscenely specific. Nathaniel wrote it.

A knock at the door. A junior scribe entered, balancing a stack of wax tablets. "The list from the High Priest."

Elazar took the tablets, dismissed the boy with a flick of his fingers. "We'll need ten copies. Five for the Temple archives, three for the Romans, two for the synagogues."

Nathaniel's wrist ached at the thought. "I'll need more ink."

"Then get more." Elazar set the tablets on the corner of the desk. "And hurry. Caiaphas wants this in circulation before the rumors spread."

Nathaniel stood, his knees protesting. The room was too warm, the air thick with the scent of cedar and lamp oil. He needed—what? A moment to breathe? A way out?

One corridor outside was marginally cooler. He pressed his forehead against the limestone wall, letting the roughness ground him. From a nearby chamber, two priests argued in hushed tones about the price of doves.

The ink storeroom was down two flights, tucked beneath the stairs. The keeper, an old man with milky eyes, didn't look up as Nathaniel entered. "Red or black?"

"Black."

The keeper pushed a ceramic jar across the counter. "That'll be two shekels."

Nathaniel fumbled for his purse. His fingers brushed the emergency coin his mother had sewn into the lining years ago. For when the world turns, she'd said. He'd never needed it.

Back in the scriptorium, Elazar was gone. The wax tablets lay open, revealing names—prominent elders, wealthy patrons, the garrison commander. Nathaniel traced the first name with his fingertip. Ananias ben Nedebaios. A man who'd once donated a gold lampstand to the Temple. A man who'd believe whatever Caiaphas told him.

He unstopped the fresh ink, stirred it with his stylus. The liquid was thick, almost viscous. It clung to the metal.

The first copy took the longest. By the third, his hand moved automatically, the words spilling onto the papyrus without conscious thought. The disciples came by night... the guards were overpowered... twenty-five lashes...

A shadow crossed the doorway. Nathaniel glanced up, expecting Elazar. A young girl stood there, balancing a tray of bread and olives. "My master sent this."

"Your master?"

"The scribe Elazar." She set the tray on the edge of the desk. "He said you'd be hungry."

Nathaniel stared at the food. His stomach turned. "Thank you."

The girl hesitated. "Are you well? You look pale."

"I'm fine." He forced a smile. "Just tired."

She nodded and withdrew. The bread smelled of cumin and honey. He couldn't touch it.

The fifth copy was nearly perfect. No smudges, no uneven letters. A professional job. He sanded it, rolled it tight, tied it with a scarlet cord. The cord was new, expensive. A detail that would lend credibility.

Elazar returned as Nathaniel finished the sixth. "Good. Keep going." He picked up one of the completed scrolls, examined the seal. "The Romans will want their copies by dusk."

Nathaniel's hand cramped. "I'll need another scribe to help."

"You're the only one I trust with this." Elazar's voice dropped. "You understand what's at stake."

Nathaniel did. The alternative—an empty tomb, a missing body, whispers of resurrection—was unthinkable. It would unravel everything.

He reached for the next blank sheet. The papyrus was smooth, unblemished. A fresh lie waiting to be written.

The tenth copy dried in the late afternoon light. Nathaniel capped the ink, wiped his stylus clean. His fingers were stained black.

Elazar gathered the scrolls into a leather satchel. "Well done. The High Priest will be pleased."

Nathaniel flexed his stiff fingers. "What happens now?"

"Now?" Elazar slung the satchel over his shoulder. "Now the story becomes truth."

Outside, the first stars appeared. The Temple gates would close soon, the merchants packing up their wares, the pilgrims retreating to their lodgings. Tomorrow, the official narrative would spread—whispered in courtyards, proclaimed in synagogues, etched into records.

Nathaniel watched a lone dove circle the pinnacle. It banked sharply, wings catching the last of the light, and vanished into the gathering dark.

The guard quarters smelled of sweat and hot iron. Decimus stood at attention in the centurion's office, the muscles of his back rigid beneath his tunic. The report—freshly inked, still damp in places—lay on the desk between them. Lysias hadn't touched it yet. He was sharpening a stylus, the blade scraping against the bronze with a sound like a whetstone on steel.

Gaius leaned against the wall by the door, arms crossed. His face gave nothing away, but his thumb tapped a slow rhythm against his bicep. The tell was subtle. Decimus had seen it before battles, when the optio was calculating odds.

Lysias set the stylus down. "This is the official account?"

"Yes, sir," Gaius said, pushing off the wall. "Temple scribes drafted it. We're to distribute copies to the garrison."

Lysias picked up the scroll, unrolled it with deliberate slowness. His eyes skimmed the lines. "Twenty-five lashes."

Decimus's jaw tightened. The number was a fiction, but the sting on his back wasn't. He'd taken five once, for tardiness. They'd split the skin.

Lysias looked up. "You were there, Valerius."

The words lodged in Decimus's throat. He'd rehearsed them on the walk over, shaping the lie until it fit his mouth. "Yes, sir."

"And?"

"The report is accurate." The lie came out flat, toneless. A statement of fact.

Lysias's gaze didn't waver. He knew. Not the truth, but the stink of a cover-up. Decimus had seen that look before, in the eyes of officers who'd been handed impossible orders and made them work.

Gaius cleared his throat. "The priests are offering compensation. A month's wages for each man on the detail."

Lysias rolled the scroll shut. "Generous."

"It's hush money," Decimus said before he could stop himself.

The room went still. Gaius's thumb stopped tapping.

Lysias set the scroll aside. "Of course it is." He leaned back in his chair, the wood creaking under his weight. "But it's also smart. The last thing we need is a bunch of legionaries babbling about stolen corpses and divine intervention. Bad for morale. Worse for discipline."

Decimus stared at a chip in the desk's edge. He'd seen the tomb. That stone hadn't been rolled away—it had been moved, as if plucked from its track by a hand the size of a siege engine. The linen wrappings were collapsed, undisturbed, like a shed skin. No drag marks. No footprints. Just that impossible emptiness.

Gaius shifted. "The priests want this buried. So do we."

Lysias grunted. "And the men on the detail?"

"Taken care of." Gaius's voice was smooth. "They'll keep their mouths shut."

A fly buzzed against the window grating. Outside, the clang of training swords echoed from the courtyard. Decimus focused on the sounds, anchoring himself.

Lysias picked up the stylus again, tapped it against the desk. "Valerius."

Decimus met his eyes.

"You're a good soldier. Reliable." Lysias's tone was casual, but the weight behind the words wasn't. "Don't start thinking now."

The words landed like a blow. Decimus's fingers twitched at his sides. He forced them still. "No, sir."

Lysias held his gaze a moment longer, then waved a hand. "Dismissed."

Gaius pushed off the wall and opened the door. Decimus turned on his heel and marched out. The corridor was cooler, the air thick with the scent of oiled leather and damp stone. His footsteps echoed too loud in his own ears.

Gaius fell into step beside him. "That went well."

Decimus didn't answer. His palms itched. He rubbed his thumb against the calloused ridge of his left hand, the motion automatic.

Gaius glanced at him. "You're not having second thoughts."

It wasn't a question. Decimus kept walking.

Gaius grabbed his arm, pulled him into an alcove. The sudden move jarred Decimus's shoulder. He shook free. "What?"

"Look at me." Gaius's voice was low, urgent. "This isn't the time to develop a conscience."

Decimus exhaled through his nose. The alcove smelled of mildew and old wine. "I know what I saw."

"And I know what's at stake." Gaius's eyes were hard. "You think the priests are the only ones who want this buried? The legate doesn't need rumors of Jewish messiahs stirring up the locals. Neither do we."

Decimus flexed his fingers. One image wouldn't leave him—the tomb, the linen, the way the dawn light had pooled on the floor like spilled water. "It wasn't stolen."

Gaius's jaw tightened. "It doesn't matter."

"It matters if it's true."

"Truth?" Gaius barked a laugh. "You've been in Jerusalem too long. Truth is what keeps us alive. Today, that's a stolen body and twenty-five lashes we'll never take." He leaned in. "Collect your pay, keep your head down, and go home to your mother. That's the job."

Decimus looked past him, at the patch of sunlight on the corridor floor. It wavered, distorted by the uneven stone. For a heartbeat, he wanted to say it—to voice the thing clawing at his ribs. But the words wouldn't come.

Gaius clapped him on the shoulder, the gesture just shy of too hard. "Come on. Let's get a drink."

Decimus let himself be steered toward the mess. The weight of the lie settled over him, heavy as a cloak. He'd carried worse.

The wine was sour, the bread stale. Decimus sat at the end of a long table, nursing his cup while Gaius held court with the others. The optio was in fine form, spinning the official story with just enough embellishment to make it sound plausible.

"—swarmed us. Dozens of them. Had to've been prearranged."

A younger legionary frowned. "Why not just kill you?"

Gaius shrugged. "Who knows how these people think? Maybe they wanted witnesses." He took a swig of wine. "Point is, it's handled. The priests paid, we keep quiet, everyone wins."

Decimus traced the rim of his cup. The clay was chipped. He wondered, idly, how many mouths it had touched before his.

Gaius caught his eye and raised his cup in a mock toast. Decimus didn't return it.

The mess hall buzzed around him—laughter, the clatter of dice, a bawdy song from the far corner. The noise grated, every sound too sharp, too bright.

He set the cup down and stood. Gaius raised an eyebrow but didn't stop him.

Outside, the sun was low, the air cooling. Decimus walked the perimeter, his boots scuffing the packed earth. The routine soothed him: left, right, left, the rhythm of patrol.

At the eastern wall, he paused. Jerusalem sprawled below, the Temple gleaming in the late light. Somewhere in those streets, the disciples were hiding. Or running. Or telling a story of their own.

He touched the dagger at his belt, the metal warm from his body. A practical weapon. A soldier's tool.

The truth was a blade too, he realized, and just as dangerous.

He turned his back on the city and walked on.

The Lower City market thrummed with its usual chaos, but the rhythm was off. Vendors hawked their wares with half-hearted cries, their eyes darting to the clusters of Temple guards moving through the crowd. A fishmonger wiped his hands on his apron, leaving streaks of silver scales. "Heard they flogged the whole detachment," he muttered to the baker beside him.

The baker kneaded dough with unnecessary force. "Flog Romans? Since when do priests have that authority?"

A woman haggling over lentils paused, her copper coin hovering midair. "My cousin cleans the High Priest's chambers. Says they paid the soldiers to say they slept."

The fishmonger snorted. "And I'm Herod's bastard. Those legionaries wouldn't take a whipping for all the silver in Tyre."

Near the spice stalls, a cluster of women bent their heads together. One clutched a bundle of myrrh to her chest, her fingers trembling. "Miriam saw the tomb," she whispered.

"Quiet." The oldest woman gripped her wrist. "Not here."

Two Temple guards shouldered past, their spears glinting. The market's murmur died in their wake, then surged louder behind them.

In the shadow of the Temple's western wall, a scribe named Ezra adjusted his phylacteries and pretended to study a scroll. The courtyard was quieter here, the air thick with the scent of crushed thyme from the nearby gardens. A man in a patched cloak sidled up to him, keeping his eyes on the ground.

"You heard?" the man breathed.

Ezra didn't look up. "Heard what?"

"The guards' story. That the disciples stole the body."

A muscle twitched in Ezra's jaw. He'd copied enough legal codes to know a fabricated testimony when he heard one. "And you believe that?"

The man's fingers plucked at his cloak. "My sister was at the tomb at dawn."

"Keep your voice down." Ezra rolled the scroll tighter. "Meet me at the Fuller's Field tonight. Third olive tree from the north wall."

A priest emerged from the colonnade, his robes whispering against the stones. The man in the cloak melted into the crowd. Ezra bowed his head respectfully as the priest passed, but his knuckles whitened around the scroll.

At a wine shop near the Dung Gate, a Roman supply clerk slammed his cup on the table. "Twenty-five lashes? Bullshit. Lysias wouldn't let his men take that for a bribe."

The shopkeeper refilled the cup without being asked. "Maybe they deserved it."

The clerk leaned in, his breath sour with cheap wine. "You ever seen a legionary after a flogging? They don't walk straight for a week. Those guards were on duty this morning. No limps. No fresh scars." He tapped the side of his nose. "It's a cover-up."

In the corner, a weaver set down his loom weights with deliberate care. His daughter had been among the women at the tomb. She'd come home wide-eyed, whispering of angels and absence.

Nathaniel walked the perimeter of the Temple courts, the official scrolls heavy in his satchel. He'd delivered five already, two to the archives, one to the garrison commander, two to prominent elders. Each handoff had been the same: a solemn nod, a murmured assurance of the story's truth, a purse changing hands.

A group of pilgrims from Galilee clustered near the money changers, their accents thick as they argued. "But why would they steal him?" a young man demanded. "They were hiding like frightened hares last night!"

His companion shook his head. "The priests say—"

"The priests say what suits them." An older woman spat on the ground. "I saw Yeshua raise Lazarus."

Nathaniel quickened his pace. The words nipped at his heels.

In a narrow alley behind the potters' quarter, Leah pressed her back against the cool clay wall. The alabaster jar of myrrh weighed heavy in her sash. She'd meant to anoint a body. She carried proof of an absence.

Footsteps approached. She tensed, but it was only Tova, her face flushed from running. "They're saying it in the squares now," she panted. "That the disciples stole him."

Leah's fingers found the jar's smooth surface. "They didn't."

"I know." Tova wiped sweat from her brow. "But the Temple guards are telling everyone who'll listen. And the Romans aren't contradicting them."

A potter emerged from his workshop, squinting at the fading light. The women fell silent until he passed.

Leah breathed out. "Where's Miriam?"

"Gone to Bethany. Peter and John are hiding near the olive press." Tova's voice dropped. "They saw the linens."

The image rose unbidden, those empty wrappings, collapsed like a shed snakeskin. Leah shuddered. "We need to tell the others."

"Tell them what?" Tova's hands fluttered, restless. "That we saw nothing? That's not a story people follow."

Leah gripped her wrist. "We saw everything. The stone. The angels. The absence."

A donkey brayed in the distance. The potter's wheel creaked as it spun. Ordinary sounds in a world that had cracked open at dawn.

Tova's eyes gleamed. "Then we tell it. All of it."

As dusk settled over Jerusalem, the city pulsed with competing truths. In the Upper City, wealthy merchants repeated the official story over roasted lamb and imported wine. In the Lower City, whispers slithered through the streets like smoke. Near the Pool of Siloam, a group of laborers passed a skin of wine and debated whether a dead man could walk.

At the Antonia Fortress, Decimus stood watch on the ramparts. Below, a Temple herald addressed a growing crowd, his voice carrying in the still air.

A rock arced from the crowd, striking the herald's shoulder. He staggered, and the guards surged forward. The crowd scattered like startled pigeons.

Decimus turned away. To the west, the last sliver of sun gilded the Temple's pinnacle. Somewhere in the gathering dark, the truth was loose.

And it wouldn't stay buried.
