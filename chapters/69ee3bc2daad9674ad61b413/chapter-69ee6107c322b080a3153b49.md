The guard quarters smelled of old sweat and lamp oil. A fly buzzed against the high, narrow window, a frantic, trapped sound. Decimus stood at attention, his eyes fixed on a crack in the plaster wall opposite. It ran from the ceiling to the height of a man’s shoulder, forking like a river delta. He counted the tributaries. Seven.

Gaius leaned against the doorframe, arms crossed. He hadn’t spoken since they’d entered the stale room. His silence was a different texture than the garden’s. This was the silence of a man waiting for an axe to fall.

Boots sounded in the corridor outside. Not the measured tread of a legionary—lighter, quicker. A clerk.

The door opened. A Temple servant, his tunic too clean for the hour stood with a wax tablet. He didn’t enter. “The centurion Gaius and the legionary Decimus Valerius. You are summoned.”

“By whom?” Gaius’s voice was flat.

“The captain of the Temple guard. And others.” The servant’s eyes flickered over Decimus, then back to Gaius. “Immediately.”

“Where?”

“The Hall of Polished Stones.”

Gaius pushed off the frame. A muscle jumped in his jaw. He gave a single, sharp nod.

The servant vanished. The fly found the window again.

“You will say nothing,” Gaius said, still looking at the empty doorway. “You will stand. You will answer only the question put to you, with the fewest words possible. You will not volunteer detail. You will not describe light. You will not mention folded cloth.” He turned. His eyes were the color of a winter sky, empty of warmth. “Do you understand?”

“Yes, sir.”

“The story is theft. The body was stolen by the dead man’s followers while you slept. You were negligent. You accept the punishment. That is the only story that leaves that room alive.”

“They will ask how.”

“They will tell you how.” Gaius picked up his helmet from a bench. He didn’t put it on. He turned it in his hands, his thumb tracing the brim’s edge. A habitual, smoothing motion. “Your memory is faulty. The shock of the discovery. It was dark. You saw shapes. You pursued, but they scattered into the city. The stone was already moved when you returned.”

“That isn’t—”

“It is now.” Gaius looked up. “Your mother’s farm in Campania. Six years. Think of the stone walls. Think of the olive trees. That is the only thing in your head when they speak to you. Not the garden. Not the linen. The olive trees.”

Decimus saw them. Gnarled trunks, silver leaves. A lie for a grove. The trade sat in his stomach like a lump of cold porridge.

“Move.”

***

The streets of the Upper City were waking. The smell of baking bread drifted from a courtyard. A woman shook a rug from a balcony above, sending a cloud of dust motes swirling into the slanting morning light. The ordinary world, continuing. Decimus felt severed from it, a spectator behind thick glass.

They passed through a gate manned by Temple guards, not Romans. These men wore polished scale armor and held themselves with a rigid formality that seemed borrowed. Their eyes tracked Gaius and Decimus with cold assessment. No salutes were exchanged.

Three men waited.

One sat behind a heavy table of dark wood: the captain of the Temple guard, a man named Malchus. Decimus knew him by sight—a compact, bearded man with a network of fine scars across the knuckles of his right hand. He was examining a scroll, his face expressionless.

To his right stood a priest. Tall, thin, his robes a stark, expensive white. His hands were clasped before him, the fingers long and pale. He didn’t look at the newcomers; his gaze was fixed on a point on the wall, as if reading an invisible text.

To the left, near a brazier that held no fire was Claudius Lysias, the Roman tribune of the Antonia Fortress. He was out of armor, wearing a simple white tunic with a narrow purple stripe. He held a stylus, tapping it softly against his thigh. Tap. It was the only sound.

“Centurion. Legionary.” Malchus did not look up from his scroll. “Report the incident at the garden tomb.”

Gaius stepped forward. He gave the version he had crafted in the guard room. Terse. Factual. A failure of watchfulness. Suspected followers of the crucified prophet. An empty tomb discovered at first light. A brief, futile pursuit.

Malchus listened, unblinking. When Gaius finished, the captain set the scroll aside. “You slept.”

“A moment of inattention, sir. The men were weary from the previous day’s duties.”

“The seal was broken.”

“The stone was found rolled aside. We assume the thieves broke it.”

“Assume.” The priest spoke. His voice was softer than expected, almost melodic. It made the word sound like a curse. He turned his head slowly. His eyes were a pale, watery brown. “You assume. The women who came at dawn did not assume. They claim to have seen a messenger. An angelic being. They claim the tomb was empty because the man called Jesus lives.”

Silence. The tribune’s stylus stopped tapping.

“The hysterical claims of grieving women, sir,” Gaius said, his tone carefully neutral. “We saw no such being. We saw an empty hole.”

The priest’s lips thinned. “What did you see, legionary?” He was looking at Decimus.

Decimus felt the weight of the room press down on his shoulders. The polished stones seemed to lean in. He saw the olive trees. Silver leaves trembling in a breeze. “I saw the tomb open, sir. The burial bench empty.”

“And the burial linens?”

The air grew thinner. Gaius didn’t move, but Decimus felt the warning like a hot wire against his skin. “They were there, sir.”

“In what state?”

“Discarded.”

“Were they torn? Scattered? Stained?”

Decimus’s mouth was dry. He saw the neat fold of the face cloth, the collapsed shell of the shroud. A quiet, impossible order. “They were… as if the body had been removed.”

“As if.” The priest took a slow step forward. His sandals whispered on the tile. “You entered the tomb. You alone.”

“Describe what your eyes told you. Not what you assume. The evidence.”

The olive trees blurred. In their place was the dark mouth of the tomb, the cool air, the linen shell holding the shape of a man who was not there. The truth was a hook. It pulled. “The large cloth was lying flat. Collapsed. The smaller cloth for the head was separate. It was folded.”

“Folded.” The priest let the word hang. He glanced at Malchus. “Not tossed aside. Not stolen with the body. Folded.”

Claudius Lysias sighed, a faint, weary sound. He tossed his stylus onto the table. “A fastidious thief.”

“A thief does not fold grave clothes,” the priest said, his melodic voice now edged. “A thief does not leave the value of fine linen behind. A thief does not roll a stone of that weight from its groove without waking eight professional soldiers twenty paces away.” He turned his full attention to Decimus. “What else did you see? Before? The other guards spoke of a light. A tremor in the ground.”

Gaius shifted his weight, a barely perceptible movement. “Sir, the men were confused.”

“I asked the legionary.”

Decimus kept his eyes on the crack between two tiles at his feet. He saw the ripple patterns in the garden soil, the complex whorls they had scuffed away. He heard the hum that was not a sound. He felt the light that clarified the veins in a leaf. The words piled up behind his teeth, a dangerous, truthful weight. He bit down on them. “It was dark, sir. There was… confusion.”

“Confusion.” The priest’s whisper was like dry leaves. “You are a soldier of Rome. Your duty is observation. Your report is ‘confusion’?”

Malchus leaned forward, his scarred knuckles resting on the table. “The story of theft is insufficient. It raises more questions than it answers. It invites… speculation. Of a different kind.” He looked at Claudius Lysias. “Speculation that destabilizes.”

The tribune rubbed the bridge of his nose. “What is it you require, then?”

“A better story.” Malchus’s eyes were black and depthless. “One that is plausible. One that leaves no room for angelic messengers or folded shrouds. One that places the blame squarely, understandably, on human agents.”

The priest nodded. “The disciples. They stole the body. They must have. It is the only logical conclusion.”

“But the guard detail—” Claudius began.

“Was overpowered.” Malchus cut him off. “While they slept. All of them. A deep, collective sleep. Perhaps aided.” He let the implication hang—wine, a bribe, a whispered threat. “They awoke to find the stone moved, the tomb empty. They gave chase, but the thieves escaped into the city. The body is gone. The disciples will, of course, claim he is risen. But we will have the testimony of the guards. Roman testimony. That the body was stolen by night.”

The room was very still. The tribune looked from Malchus to the priest, then to Gaius. His expression was that of a man adding a column of figures and finding a sum he did not like. “You are asking my men to perjure themselves before a potential imperial inquiry.”

“We are asking them to provide the only testimony that prevents a riot,” the priest said. “Jerusalem is a tinderbox. Passover crowds remain. A story of a risen messiah will ignite it. A story of a stolen corpse is a criminal matter. It is contained. It ends with a search that finds nothing.”

“And if the disciples produce the body?”

“They cannot produce what they do not have. Wherever they have hidden it, it will decay. It will become bones. Bones prove nothing but death.”

Claudius Lysias was silent for a long moment. He looked at Decimus. “Can your men hold to this? Under questioning?”

Gaius answered. “They will hold to it, sir. They understand the alternative.”

The tribune’s gaze didn’t waver from Decimus. He was looking for something—a crack, a hesitation. Decimus made his face stone. He thought of olive trees. He thought of his mother’s hands, rough from weaving.

“Very well,” the tribune said, the words heavy. “You have your testimony. See that it is consistent.”

Malchus stood. “The guards will be compensated for their… cooperation. A substantial sum. For the trouble, and for the disciplinary marks that will, regrettably, be recorded for their failure.”

Bribery, dressed as wages. The cold lump in Decimus’s stomach grew.

“Dismissed,” Malchus said. “The centurion remains. Details of the transfer.”

Decimus turned on his heel, the movement automatic. He walked toward the door, the polished walls a blur. The priest’s soft voice followed him, a final, murmured thread.

“See that the folded cloth does not trouble your memory, legionary. Some stones are better left unturned.”

He stepped into the sun-washed corridor. The door closed behind him with a solid, final click. He stood alone in the bright, empty hall, the taste of citrus oil and lies thick in his throat. Somewhere, a real fly buzzed against a real window. He stared at his own shadow, pooled darkly on the gleaming floor. It held no shape he recognized.

Boots echoed behind him. Gaius emerged from the room, his face a closed door. He didn’t look at Decimus. He walked past, his stride measured and hard. “With me.”

They retraced their steps through the silent hall, past the expressionless Temple guards. The black and white tiles seemed to shift underfoot, an optical trick. Decimus focused on the centurion’s back, the precise swing of his arms. A fly, a different one, orbited a sconce. Its buzz was the only life in the place.

Outside, the Upper City’s noise hit him like a wall. Donkeys brayed. A vendor shouted the price of lentils. A child laughed, a sharp, bright sound. The ordinary world was an assault. He felt raw, skinless. Every smell—dung, incense, frying fish—was too sharp. Every color hurt.

They didn’t head back toward the Antonia. Gaius turned south, into a wider street that sloped upward. Toward the Temple Mount.

“Sir?” The word was out before he could stop it.

Gaius didn’t break stride. “We are summoned again. Deeper in.”

“The Temple?”

“The priestly chambers. The high priest’s circle.” Gaius’s voice was low, stripped of inflection. “Malchus was the appetizer.”

The street narrowed as it climbed, the buildings pressing in. The shadows were cooler here, smelling of wet stone and old urine. Decimus’s hand went to the pommel of his gladius, a habitual check. The leather was warm from the sun. The weapon felt absurd suddenly. A toy. What was a short sword in the house of a god he didn’t know?

They passed under a grand arch. The atmosphere changed. The commercial clamor faded, replaced by a low, reverent murmur. They were on the outer courts of the Temple Mount. A sea of people moved in slow, ritual currents—men in prayer shawls, women with offerings, pilgrims staring upward with wide, awestruck eyes.

A squad of Temple guards was waiting, Malchus at their head. He had changed his tunic. This one was darker, simpler. He looked at Gaius, then jerked his chin. “This way. Leave the mob behind.”

The guards formed a box around them, not an escort but a containment. They moved through the crowd, the Temple men using their spears as prods, parting the sea of pilgrims. Decimus saw faces turn, eyes widening at the sight of Roman armor in the inner precincts. Expressions flickered—curiosity, fear, hatred. A man spat, the globule landing on the white stone between Decimus’s feet. He kept walking.

They passed through the Court of the Gentiles, a vast plaza of noise and commerce. Money changers clinked coins. Doves cooed in wicker cages. Then another gate, another set of guards. The signs were in Hebrew and Greek: No foreigner may enter beyond this point on pain of death.

Malchus didn’t pause. The guards at the gate stepped aside.

Decimus’s breath hitched. He was crossing a line not just of stone, but of law and faith. A Roman soldier, armed, in the inner courts. It was a profound violation, a calculated display of who held real power here today. The murmur of the crowd fell away, replaced by a deeper, more solemn quiet. The ground here was paved with larger, finer stones. The walls soared, their tops lost in glare. Priests in layered vestments moved with unhurried purpose, their faces set in masks of distant piety. None looked at the Romans. The pointed ignoring was more unnerving than any stare.

The coping mechanism came unbidden. His right hand began tracing the seams of his armor, the pad of his thumb seeking the junction between chest plate and shoulder guard. He found the familiar ridge, the slight imperfection in the bronze where a dent had been hammered out years ago. He followed its contour, a silent, repetitive prayer to the god of discipline. I am here. I am armored. I am Roman.

It was a lie. Here, he was a pollutant.

They ascended a wide staircase, then turned into a covered colonnade that ran along the inner wall. The shade was sudden and cool. The sound of their footsteps changed, echoing off the high ceiling. From an open doorway ahead, the scent of cedarwood and myrrh wafted out, expensive and funereal.

Malchus stopped before a door of dark, oiled wood. No insignia, no guard. Its very plainness spoke of supreme authority. He knocked once, a dry tap of knuckles.

A voice from within. “Enter.”

Malchus pushed the door open and stood aside. He looked at Gaius, then at Decimus. His scarred knuckles were white where he gripped his spear. “You will stand where indicated. You will speak only when addressed.”

Gaius entered first. Decimus followed, his helmet feeling suddenly too tight, a metal bowl squeezing his thoughts.

Decimus knew them immediately: the high priest, Joseph Caiaphas, and his father-in-law, the former high priest Annas. Even out of ceremonial vestments, their authority was a physical pressure. Caiaphas was younger, with a sharp, intelligent face and eyes that missed nothing. He had a scribe’s hands, long-fingered, stained with ink. Annas was older, his posture stooped, but his gaze was like a hook. It pulled at you, searching for a soft place to sink in.

The door closed behind them. Malchus did not enter. They were alone with the two most powerful Jewish men in Judea.

The silence stretched. It was not the waiting silence of the hall. This was an active, assessing silence. Annas leaned forward slightly, his knuckles resting on the table. The grain of the wood was very fine, swirling around a dark knot. Decimus fixed on that knot. It looked like an eye.

“The report from the captain of the guard is… unsatisfactory.” Caiaphas spoke first. His voice was calm, reasonable, the voice of a man used to being listened to. “It lacks conviction. It is a story for the marketplace, not for the Sanhedrin.”

Gaius stood rigid. “It is the story as it happened, sir.”

“Is it?” Annas’s voice was a dry rasp. He didn’t look at Gaius. He looked at Decimus. “You are the one who entered the tomb.”

Decimus felt the hook set. “Yes, sir.”

“Describe the linen. Again.”

The order was flat. A test. Decimus’s mouth was dust. He saw the olive trees. Their leaves were still. No breeze. “It was collapsed. Empty.”

“And the cloth for the face?”

“It was… set apart.”

“Folded.”

“Yes.”

Annas exchanged a glance with Caiaphas. Something passed between them, a language of slight shifts in posture, a tightening around the eyes. Caiaphas gave a minute nod.

“There is a new story,” Caiaphas said. He picked up a stylus from the table, turning it end over end. Bone, polished smooth. “You slept. All of you. A deep sleep. You were not merely negligent. You were overcome. When you awoke, the stone was moved. The tomb was empty. You saw figures fleeing. You pursued. You failed.”

It was the same story Malchus had crafted, but the emphasis was different. The weight had shifted from failure to victimhood. Overcome.

“By whom?” Gaius asked, the question tight.

“Does it matter?” Annas’s rasp cut the air. “The disciples of the Galilean. Or others who wish to see unrest. The point is that you, Rome’s finest were rendered helpless. Asleep on watch. It is a disgrace. But it is a comprehensible disgrace. Men can be drugged. Men can be bribed. Men can be threatened.” He paused. “What men cannot explain is a folded shroud. A stone rolled uphill by no human hand. A missing body with no decay. These things are not comprehensible. They are dangerous.”

Caiaphas set the stylus down with a precise click. “You will be questioned by the governor. Pilate is a practical man. He understands civic disorder. He does not understand… mysteries. You will tell him of your deep, unnatural sleep. You will accept the disgrace. In return, the Temple will not press for your execution for dereliction. And you will be compensated for your public shaming.” He looked directly at Decimus. “Your memory of that night is now the memory we have given you. The folded cloth is a detail from a dream. You will forget it.”

Decimus’s thumb was tracing the seam on his armor so hard he felt the bronze edge bite into his skin. The pain was a clean, sharp point in the fog. He saw his father’s face, not drunk but weary, the night he spoke of sanded-down truth. They make it fit the hole.

“Do you understand?” Caiaphas’s tone allowed for only one answer.

“Yes, sir,” Gaius said.

“Yes, sir,” Decimus echoed. The words tasted of citrus oil and myrrh.

Annas leaned back, seemingly satisfied. But his old, hooked eyes stayed on Decimus. They held a cold, professional curiosity, the look a physician gives a puzzling symptom. “You have the look of your father,” he said, the words so unexpected they seemed to drop into the room like stones.

Decimus’s blood turned to ice. He stopped breathing.

“A centurion in the Tenth was he not? During the rebellion. A man who understood orders.” Annas tilted his head. “Until he didn’t.”

The high window’s light seemed to brighten, bleaching the color from the room. Decimus’s coping mechanism shattered. His hand fell from his armor, hanging numb at his side. They knew. They had pulled his service record, dug into the past he kept buried. The wound was not private. It was a ledger entry for them, a point of leverage.

“A tragedy,” Caiaphas added, not unkindly. Worse than kindness. “To die for a moment of confusion. To leave a son alone. Stability is precious. A pension. A farm. A mother cared for in her old age. These things can be secured. Or they can be… lost. In the chaos of unrest.”

The threat was velvet-wrapped, exquisitely aimed. It wasn’t about him. It was about the olive grove, the stone walls, his mother’s hands. They weren’t offering a bribe. They were holding a knife to the world he wanted.

Gaius was staring straight ahead, a statue. He had known. Or he had guessed.

“You are dismissed,” Annas said. “Remember the sleep. Forget the fold. The safety of the city depends on it. Your family’s safety echoes that larger peace. See that you guard it.”

The words were a final, gentle pressure on the hook setting it deep.

Decimus turned. His movements were mechanical, a puppet’s. He followed Gaius out of the room, back into the colonnade’s shade. The door shut, sealing the two old men and their calculated peace inside.

Malchus was gone. The corridor was empty. The bar of light from the high window lay across their path, a bright, impassable river. Gaius stood at its edge, not crossing. He finally looked at Decimus. His winter-sky eyes held no comfort, only a grim acknowledgment. The mask had slipped, for a fraction of a second, and beneath it was the same cold porridge of complicity.

He said nothing. He simply stepped into the light, his shadow swallowing the gleaming stone. Decimus followed, the taste in his throat now obvious: the iron of blood, and the sour, lingering dregs of a truth he had already agreed to bury.

Malchus was waiting for them in the courtyard, a leather pouch in his hand. Small, but it hung with a specific, dense weight. He didn’t speak. He pressed it into Gaius’s palm. The centurion’s fingers closed around it, his knuckles bleaching white for an instant before he thrust it into the fold of his belt. A second, identical pouch was offered to Decimus.

He stared at it. The leather was finely tanned, smooth. He imagined the coins inside, freshly minted Tyrian shekels, their edges sharp. The price of a folded cloth. The price of a dream. His father had died for refusing a different order. He had died for sanding down a different truth. They make it fit the hole. This pouch was the mortar for that fitting.

“Take it,” Gaius said, his voice low. It wasn’t a suggestion.

Decimus’s hand lifted of its own accord. The pouch was heavier than it looked. The weight was wrong—too solid, too final. It didn’t clink. The coins were packed tight, muffled. He slipped it into the small pouch on his own belt, where he kept a whetstone and a spare buckle. The leather bulged, an ugly tumor on his hip.

The Temple guards reappeared, forming their box once more. The return journey was a blur of descending steps and shifting light. The murmur of the outer courts rose to meet them, a wave of mundane sound that now felt like a mockery. They passed back through the death-threat gate, through the commerce of the Gentiles, the money changers’ cries now sounding like a taunt. Change your truth. Exchange your memory. We give good weight.

Out in the open street, the guards melted away. The sun was high, hammering down on the white stone, bleaching the shadows into thin, gray lines. Gaius started walking, not toward the Antonia Fortress, but east, toward the quieter residential streets of the Upper City. Decimus followed, the pouch at his hip thumping against his thigh with every step. A physical metronome counting his complicity.

They walked in silence for a hundred paces. The street was empty but for a slave sweeping a doorstep, the bristles whispering against the stone. The sound was like the priest’s voice.

Gaius stopped in the lee of a high wall, shaded by a twisted fig tree. He turned. His face was sheened with sweat, but his eyes were dry and cold.

“You will take the coins,” he said. It was an order, stripped bare. “You will spend them. Slowly. On nothing remarkable. A better grade of oil for your mother. A new cloak before winter. You will not bury them. You will not try to give them back.”

Decimus’s tongue was a lump of clay. “They knew about my father.”

“Of course they knew.” Gaius didn’t blink. “They know the price of donkeys in Damascus and the name of every Roman soldier’s first whore. Information is their currency. Now it is ours. They have shown their hand. They want peace. We want to live. The transaction is clean.”

“It’s not clean.”

“It is now.” Gaius’s voice dropped, a low, hard rasp. “Listen to me, Valerius. This is not a philosophical exercise. Annas did not threaten you. He described a reality. The city is a pot at a rolling boil. A story of a stolen body is a lid. A story of a risen one is a fist pounding that pot until it shatters. Which story gets your mother her pension? Which story lets you walk out of this province alive?”

Decimus looked past him, at the fig tree. A single, misshapen fruit hung from a low branch, pecked at by birds, oozing a clear, slow sap. Life went on in its ugly, persistent way.

He walked out into the evening to give his false testimony, the lie a cold, solid mass in his gut and the memory of the folded cloth a silent, screaming ghost behind his eyes.
