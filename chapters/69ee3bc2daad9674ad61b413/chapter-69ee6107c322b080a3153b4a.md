The pouch at his hip held its shape against his thigh, a hard, rounded lump that moved with him. Decimus kept his eyes on Gaius’s back, on the sweat-darkened line of his tunic between the shoulder plates. A street climbed turned, descended into a narrow lane where the houses pressed close. The smell of baking bread and sewage warred in the still air. A child’s wail cut through a shuttered window, sharp and sudden. Gaius stopped before a plain wooden door set into a limestone wall. No insignia. No guard. He knocked twice, paused, knocked once more.

The door opened inward. A Temple guard, face impassive, stepped aside.

The room was small, windowless, lit by a single clay lamp on a low table. The air tasted of old wax and dust. Two men waited. Decimus recognized the priest from the hall—Annas’s man, the one with the dry voice. The other was younger, slender, with a scribe’s stoop and ink-stained fingers. Nathaniel. He held a stylus but no tablet.

“Close the door,” the priest said.

The guard outside pulled it shut. The latch clicked, a final sound.

Gaius stood at a modified attention, his weight balanced. Decimus mirrored him, the pouch a dead weight. He focused on a crack in the plaster behind the priest’s head. It forked like a lightning bolt, one branch reaching for the ceiling.

“The matter is simple,” the priest began. He did not sit. “A story is spreading. We require a better one. You will provide it.”

Gaius gave a single shallow nod. “Understood.”

“The story is this.” The priest’s hands were clasped before him, still. “During your watch, in the deepest hour of the night, you slept. All of you. The disciples of the Galilean came, rolled away the stone, and stole the body. You awoke too late to prevent it. You gave chase but lost them in the darkness of the garden. This is what occurred.”

Silence pooled in the low room. The lamp flame guttered, stretching shadows long across the floor.

Decimus’s throat tightened. He saw the linen cloth, the precise fold. A thief did not stop to tidy a death shroud.

“That is not what happened,” he said. The words were out before he could catch them, flat and toneless.

The priest’s gaze shifted to him. It was not angry. Appraising, like a man judging the grain of a piece of wood. “It is what happened now.”

Nathaniel’s stylus tapped once, softly, against his own thumb.

“The tribune has been persuaded of the… plausibility of this account,” the priest continued. “A failure of duty is a military matter. A disciplinary one. It contains no… theological complications. It is a lid, as your centurion so aptly put it, for a boiling pot. You will attest to it. Before the proper authorities. When asked.”

Gaius spoke, his voice a low rumble. “And for this attestation?”

A faint smile touched the priest’s mouth. It did not reach his eyes. “For your cooperation, and for the… hardship of the disciplinary marks you will doubtless receive, the Temple wishes to express its gratitude.” He gestured to Nathaniel.

The scribe set his stylus on the table. From beneath it he drew a small, heavy-looking leather bag, identical to the one now burning against Decimus’s hip. He placed it beside the lamp. It settled with a dense, muffled thud.

“Fifty denarii,” the priest said. “For each man who stood the watch. A supplement to your pay. Compensation for the… embarrassment.”

Fifty. A year’s wages for a laborer. Decimus could see the olive grove in Campania, the roof repaired, his mother’s hands smooth and uncracked by winter. That image was vivid, sweet. It turned to ash as soon as it formed.

Gaius didn’t look at the bag. “The men will need to be convinced. They saw… things. Felt things. That story leaves out the light. The tremor.”

“They saw nothing.” The priest’s voice hardened, just a fraction. “They felt a minor earth tremor, common in these hills. They were tired men, frightened by shadows and their own superstitions. The dawn revealed a crime. That is all.”

“Proculus’s arm is broken,” Decimus heard himself say.

“A fall in the dark. During the failed pursuit.” The priest shrugged, a minute lift of one shoulder. “These details align. They must.”

Gaius was silent for three long breaths. Then he stepped forward took the bag from the table, and tucked it into his own belt. The movement was efficient, practiced. “They’ll align.”

“Decimus?” The priest turned the name into a question.

All the air had left the room. Decimus’s thumb found the callus on his palm, the one from the sword grip. He rubbed it. The ritual was empty now, the familiar ridge of skin offering no anchor. He saw the folded cloth. Then he saw the fig tree’s oozing fruit, the pecked-out hollow. Life in its ugly, persistent way.

“Yes,” he said. The word was dry, a leaf crushed underfoot.

“Good.” The priest’s clasped hands relaxed. “The silver is for your silence. But understand… it is also for your safety. This story protects you. A story of a risen prophet… it makes you liars twice over. Either you lied about the theft, or you lie now about what you saw. Either way, the mob finds a reason. We are giving you the reason that lets you live.”

It was not a threat. It was a diagnosis.

Nathaniel cleared his throat, a soft, papery sound. He picked up his stylus again, not to write, but to hold it. “There is a… consistency required. A guard detail will be broken up. Transferred to different units. To prevent the story from… evolving in retelling. You will not speak of it to each other again.”

Gaius’s jaw worked. “When?”

“The orders are being cut now. You return to the Antonia. You will receive them before sunset.”

Decimus thought of Marius’s defiant glare, Lucius’s terrified twitch, Proculus’s pained grimace. They would become ghosts. Men he had shared a fire with for three years vanished into the machinery of the legion. Cleaner that way.

“Is that all?” Gaius asked.

“One more thing.” The priest’s eyes settled on Decimus. “The folded cloth. The women will speak of it. Some already have. If you are asked, you saw nothing of the sort. The tomb was ransacked. The linens were thrown aside. In your panic and the poor light, you may have been… mistaken.”

Decimus’s thumb stilled. The hook in his mind, the one holding the image of that neat square of linen apart from the rest, twisted.

“I was not mistaken.”

“You are now,” the priest said, his voice gentle. “That is the nature of this transaction. You trade a memory for a future. A good trade.”

Nathaniel looked up. For the first time, his eyes met Decimus’s. They were a pale, watery brown, full of an uneasy intelligence. He gave a slight, almost imperceptible shake of his head. It wasn’t a warning. It was something worse—a shared weariness, an acknowledgment of the mechanism they were all now inside. Then he looked down at his empty hands.

The priest gestured to the door. “Go. Spend the silver on life. Not on guilt. Guilt is a luxury for safer men.”

Gaius turned and knocked on the door. The guard outside opened it. The daylight in the lane was blinding, a white-hot shock after the dim cell. Decimus blinked, stepping over the threshold. The door closed behind them with a solid, muffled thump, sealing the silence inside.

They walked. The pouch of silver Gaius had taken thumped against his thigh in a different rhythm than the one on Decimus’s hip. A discordant beat. The lane opened onto a broader street where a water-seller chanted his price. A donkey laden with pottery shuffled by, its ears twitching at flies.

“Don’t think about it,” Gaius said, not looking at him. “Just do it.”

“He shook his head,” Decimus said. “The scribe.”

Gaius stopped. He turned, his face carved into harsh planes by the overhead sun. “What?”

“Nathaniel. When the priest told me to forget the cloth. He shook his head. Not ‘no.’ Just… he knew.”

Gaius stared at him for a long moment. Street noise swirled around them—a woman bargaining, the clatter of the potter’s wares, the distant bleat of a sheep. Ordinary life, rushing past the still point they occupied.

“Then he’s a fool,” Gaius said, his voice low. “Knowing changes nothing. It just makes the weight harder to carry. Now move.”

He walked on. Decimus followed, the two pouches of silver a twin condemnation, paying for the silence that now stretched ahead of him, vast and empty as the tomb he had entered in the dawn.

The antechamber they were ushered into next felt like a parody of the first. This one had a high, narrow window that let in a bar of dusty light, illuminating motes that swirled like agitated sand. A bench sat against one wall, its wood worn smooth by countless waiting backsides. The smell here was different—faint incense, the kind used to mask mildew, and underneath it, the ghost of roasted meat from some nearby kitchen. Decimus’s stomach turned. He could not remember when he had last eaten.

Gaius dropped onto the bench with a grunt, the leather bag hitting the plank beside him with a solid thunk. He leaned his head back against the wall and closed his eyes. The line of his throat was exposed, the pulse there steady and slow. A scar, pale and ropey, curved from behind his ear into the hairline. Decimus had never noticed it before.

He remained standing. The pouch at his hip was a lodestone, pulling his stance uneven. He focused on the window. Through it, he could see a sliver of Jerusalem’s sky, a hard, untroubled blue. A single white cloud, frayed at the edges, drifted across the opening. He tracked its progress until it vanished.

“Sit down,” Gaius said, eyes still closed. “You’re making the air nervous.”

Decimus didn’t move. “Fifty denarii.”

“That’s what he said.”

“For a lie.”

Gaius’s eyes opened. They were the flat grey of river stones. “For a story. One that keeps our heads on our shoulders and our names off a crucifix. You heard the man. It’s the safer fiction.”

“It’s not a fiction. It’s a… a painting over a window. The view is still there behind it.”

“Then don’t look at the window.” Gaius sat forward, elbows on his knees. He picked up the bag from the bench, hefted its weight in his palm. The coins inside slid against each other with a soft, liquid rustle. “This is real. The bread you’ll buy with it is real. The mob with stones, that’s real too. You choose your reality, Decimus. This one pays better.”

Decimus’s thumb went back to the callus. He pressed down until he felt the burn. “They want us to forget the cloth. The fold.”

“So forget it.”

“I can’t.”

“You can.” Gaius’s voice lost its lazy edge. “You walk out of here, you go to the baths, you get drunk. You find a woman. You spend one of these coins. By morning, the memory will have softened. In a week, it’ll be a dream. In a month, you’ll wonder if you ever saw it at all. That’s how it works.”

A fly buzzed into the bar of light, circled twice, and flew back out the window. Decimus watched it go. He thought of the silence in the tomb, a quality of stillness that felt less like absence and more like a held breath. You could not bribe a silence like that. You could only pretend not to hear it.

“What about the others?” he asked. “Marius. Lucius. Will they be convinced so easily?”

Gaius shrugged. “Marius is smart. He’ll see the sense. Lucius is scared. He’ll cling to any story that lets him sleep. Proculus is in a surgeon’s tent. He’ll be grateful his career isn’t over.” He set the bag down again. “They’ll take the silver. They’ll tell the tale. People do, for a lot less.”

He said it as a simple fact, a law of nature. Decimus had seen it himself—a farmer swearing his boundary stone had always been ten paces to the west, a merchant forgetting the origin of a smuggled amphora. Memory was clay. Pressure reshaped it.

“And the light?” Decimus heard the stubbornness in his own voice, childish. “The tremor?”

“Earth tremor. Dawn light playing tricks.” Gaius stood up, stretching his shoulders back until something cracked. “You’re making it complicated. It’s not. We slept. The body was stolen. We failed. We’re being punished and paid to be quiet about our failure. It’s clean. Military. No ghosts, no gods.”

He walked to the window, looked out at the slice of sky. His profile was sharp against the brightness. “My father was a fisherman on the Mare Nostrum. He told a story once, about hauling in a net so heavy he thought it was full of tuna. It was a Roman anchor, old, crusted with barnacles. He and his brothers struggled with it for an hour, trying to get it over the gunwale. Dead weight. Pulling them under. You know what he did?”

Decimus waited.

“He cut the net. Let it go. He lost the net, the day’s catch, everything. But he sailed home.” Gaius turned from the window. “That’s this. That anchor in your head—the folded cloth, the quiet—it’s pulling you under. Cut the net.”

The analogy was almost convincing. But an anchor was inert. What Decimus carried felt alive, a seed buried in dark soil. He said nothing.

Gaius misread his silence as assent. He nodded, coming back to the bench. “Good. Now, the transfer. They’ll split us up. You’ll probably get sent to Caesarea, or maybe up to Syria. Fresh start. New unit. You tell the story once, to your new centurion when he asks about the mark on your record. Then you never speak of it again. You become the soldier who slept on watch once, learned his lesson moved on. It becomes a joke you tell about yourself after too much wine. ‘The time I napped through a resurrection.’” He smirked, a thin, humorless stretch of his lips.

Decimus did not smile. He looked at the pouch on the bench. The leather was good quality, oiled, the stitching tight. A quality purchase for a quality lie. He imagined the scribe Nathaniel purchasing a dozen such bags, the transaction recorded in some ledger under “Administrative Expenses.” The sheer, mundane bureaucracy of corruption was somehow more chilling than any dramatic threat.

“He was weary,” Decimus said quietly.

“Who?”

“The scribe. Not afraid. Weary. Like this was the tenth time today he’d bought a man’s memory.”

Gaius let out a short, sharp breath. “You have a poet’s disease, Decimus. You see meaning in dust. One man has a job. He does it. We have a job. We do it. The poetry is what gets you killed.”

Footsteps echoed in the hall outside, sandals slapping against stone. They both turned. A door opened, and the same Temple guard stood there, holding two small, sealed scrolls. He held them out without a word.

Gaius took them. He broke the seal on his own, scanned the lines, his eyes moving left to right. His face showed nothing. He handed the second scroll to Decimus.

The papyrus was crisp. The ink was fresh, a deep black. The language was formal, clipped Latin. Decimus Valerius, legionary of Cohors I Italica is hereby reassigned to the garrison at Fortress Antonia, Jerusalem, effective immediately. Report to Centurion Quintus Drusus for duty assignment. He read it twice. Not Caesarea. Not Syria. Jerusalem. The heart of it.

“Jerusalem?” he said.

Gaius had finished his own orders. He crumpled the scroll into a tight ball. “I’m for Caesarea. They’re keeping you here.” He looked at Decimus, a new calculation in his gaze. “That’s interesting.”

“Why?”

“Proximity.” Gaius tossed the ball of papyrus into a corner. “The story is freshest here. The women are here. The… whispers are here. Maybe they want a friendly face on the wall, one who knows the official version by heart.” He stepped closer, his voice dropping. “Or maybe it’s a test. To see if you can live beside the truth and still swear to the lie. A sharper knife.”

The guard at the door cleared his throat. “You are to depart separately. You—” he pointed at Gaius, “—leave now, by the south gate. You—” the finger swung to Decimus, “—wait a quarter hour, then report to the Antonia armory. Your kit has been moved.”

Gaius picked up his silver. He fastened the bag to his belt, adjusting it until it sat right. He gave Decimus a long, final look. All the earlier pretense of camaraderie was gone, stripped away. What remained was the centurion’s mask, impenetrable and cold.

“Remember the anchor, Valerius,” he said. Then he leaned in, close enough that Decimus could smell the sour wine on his breath. The words were a whisper meant for no one else. “And remember this. The legion’s discipline for lying to a tribune is a flogging. Its punishment for betraying a tribune’s trust is crucifixion. You’ve taken the silver. You’re in the story now. The only way out is feet first.”

He straightened up, nodded to the guard, and walked out. His footsteps faded down the corridor, measured and even, until there was only silence.

Decimus stood alone in the antechamber. One bar of light from the window had crept across the floor, now touching the toe of his sandal. He looked at the crumpled scroll in the corner. He looked at his own orders, still in his hand. The words blurred. Report to Centurion Quintus Drusus. A relative of the tribune, known for his rigid adherence to protocol. A man with no patience for poetry or for doubts.

He lifted the pouch of silver from his belt. Heavier than it had any right to be. He untied the thong, loosened the opening, and tipped a single coin into his palm. A denarius. The silver was cool, slightly greasy. He turned it over. Caesar’s profile on one side, a proud, hooked nose and a laurel wreath. On the reverse, a goddess seated, holding a branch. Pax. Peace.

He closed his fist around it. The metal bit into his palm. He thought of the fig tree again, the furious wasteful life of it. He thought of the clean, empty fold in the dark of the tomb.

The coin bought silence. But the memory it was meant to erase was not a sound. It was a shape. A shape folded into the void where a body should have been. You could not spend a coin to unsee a shape. You could only learn to walk around the hole it left in your vision.

He dropped the coin back into the bag, pulled the thong tight. The guard at the door watched him, impassive. A quarter hour. He had fifteen minutes before he had to step back into the world and begin the work of forgetting.

He sat on the bench Gaius had vacated. The wood was still warm. He placed the pouch beside him, then folded his hands in his lap. He watched the motes dance in the sunbeam, a frantic, invisible current stirring them. He did not think. He simply waited, measuring the slow, sinking weight of the silver in the room, and the heavier, colder weight of the lie now settling into his bones.

The armory of the Antonia Fortress smelled of old iron, linseed oil, and the sour tang of men who slept too close to their own equipment. Decimus stood at his assigned locker, a niche in the stone wall barely wider than his shoulders. His kit was already there, as the guard had said. His helmet, his mail shirt, his gladius and pugio, his marching pack. All of it arranged with a neatness that felt accusatory. Someone had gone through it.

He ran a hand over the rings of his lorica. The metal was cold. He always oiled it after a night watch, a ritual to prevent the rust that crept in from Jerusalem’s damp dawns. He had not done it this morning. Yet the rings showed no bloom of orange, only a thin, fresh sheen. Someone had oiled it for him. The kindness was a violation.

He heard the scrape of a hobnailed sole on stone behind him. He didn’t turn.

“Valerius.”

The voice was a rasp, like stone grinding on stone. Centurion Quintus Drusus. Decimus turned and saluted, his arm snapping to his chest. The centurion was a whipcord of a man, all tendon and bone. His eyes were the color of a winter sky set deep under a bony shelf of brow. He held a wax tablet in one hand.

“Your transfer is processed,” Drusus said. He did not look at the tablet. He looked at Decimus’s face, as if reading a dispatch written there. “You are reassigned to the Third Watch, the western battlement from the Herodian Gate to the corner tower. Your duty roster is here.” He held out the tablet.

Decimus took it. The wax was smooth, the writing a stark, angular script. It listed times, gate numbers, relief schedules. Normal. Utterly normal.

“The incident at the garden tomb is noted as a disciplinary lapse,” Drusus continued. His voice gave no hint of what he thought of the incident. It was a statement of record. “You will receive ten lashes at the next disciplinary muster. A formal mark on your record. Do you understand?”

“Yes, sir.”

“The tribune has chosen to be lenient. The theft was not, in the end, your direct failure. The punishment is for sleeping on watch. The story ends there. You will not discuss the details with your new contubernium. You will not speculate. You will not answer questions. If asked, you refer the question to me. Is that clear?”

“Yes, sir.”

Drusus took a step closer. He was shorter than Decimus, but he carried a dense, compact energy that made the air feel thinner. “I do not care what you think you saw. I care what you say you saw. The official report says the body was stolen while you slept. That is the reality of this fortress. Any other reality is a figment. It does not exist here. Cultivate it, and you will be digging latrines in Gaul by the next full moon. Or worse.”

He didn’t elaborate on the worse. He didn’t need to. The cold certainty in his voice was more effective than any threat.

“I understand, sir.”

“Good.” Drusus’s gaze flicked to the locker, to the oiled mail. “Your gear has been serviced. Consider it a reminder. The legion takes care of its own. But it expects its own to maintain order. Internal and external. See that you do.”

He turned and walked away, his caligae striking the flagstones with a precise, rhythmic tap that echoed long after he’d turned the corner.

Decimus let out a breath he hadn’t realized he’d been holding. His hands were steady. He noted that with a distant curiosity. Inside, a cold tremor had taken root just below his ribs. Outside, he was marble.

He busied himself with his kit. He checked the edge of his gladius—still sharp. He tightened the straps on his pack. All the while, the pouch at his hip was a live coal. He could feel the individual coins through the leather, their hard, round presence an obscene secret.

Other soldiers moved through the armory, a low murmur of voices and the clank of metal. A man two lockers down cursed softly as he struggled with a stuck buckle. Another laughed at a crude joke. The ordinary sounds of barracks life. Decimus was a ghost among them. He had crossed a line, and the world on this side looked the same, but it was not. Its air was different.

He finished with his locker and slung his pack over one shoulder. He had time before his watch began. He left the armory and climbed a narrow staircase that led to the outer battlements. The sun was high now, beating down on the pale Jerusalem stone. Heat shimmers rose from the rooftops of the Lower City. He walked along the wall-walk, the city wide to his right, the Temple Mount rising in gleaming majesty to his left.

He stopped at a section where the parapet was slightly wider, a small alcove formed by a buttress. It was a blind spot, out of view of the sentry towers. Below, the ground fell away steeply into a narrow, trash-strewn gully that ran along the fortress wall. This was his spot. He’d found it months ago, a place to steal five minutes of solitude when the press of men and duty grew too thick.

He set his pack down and leaned his forearms on the sun-warmed stone. A lizard, painted the same dusty grey as the rock, darted into a crack. In the gully, a dog nosed at a pile of refuse. The air smelled of dust, dung, and the distant, sweet scent of baking bread from the city.

He unlooped the pouch from his belt. The leather was already softening with his body heat. He loosened the thong and poured the coins into his cupped hand. A cascade of silver, catching the sun with a hard, bright gleam. Fifty denarii. A fortune. A condemnation.

He counted them. He didn’t know why. The number was fifty. He counted them anyway, stacking them into little towers of five on the parapet. The face of Tiberius stared up from each one, aloof, imperial. The goddess Pax on the reverse seemed to mock him with her branch.

This money was for his future. For his mother’s roof. It was also a lock, snapping shut on his tongue. He could not spend it. Not yet. To buy a drink with one of these coins would be to accept the transaction, to make the lie a part of him, digested and spent.

His eyes scanned the alcove. The stones were tightly fitted, but time and weather had worked on the mortar. Near the base, where the parapet met the wall-walk, a crack had opened, a dark, vertical line about the width of his thumb, packed with old dirt and bits of wind-blown chaff.

He looked left, then right. The wall-walk was empty. The only sound was the distant cry of a street vendor and the buzzing of flies in the gully.

He scooped the coins back into the pouch. He pulled the drawstring tight, making a compact, heavy bundle. He knelt, his knees pressing into the gritty stone. He worked the pouch into the crack. It was a tight fit. He had to push, using the heel of his hand to force the leather down into the darkness. The mortar crumbled a little at the edges, a fine dust coating his knuckles. He pushed until the pouch was completely swallowed, until his fingertips touched only cool, rough stone.

He sat back on his heels. A small, ridiculous act. If anyone saw him, they’d think he was hiding stolen goods. Perhaps he was. He had stolen this silver from his own conscience. The payment was in his possession, but it was not in his life. Buried in a wall, a silent metallic secret.

He scooped up a handful of dust and dirt from the wall-walk and rubbed it over the crack, obscuring the fresh marks his efforts had made. He spat on the stone and worked the mud into the crevice with his thumb until it looked no different from any other weathered seam.

He stood up, brushing his hands on his tunic. His knees ached. The pouch was gone. The weight on his belt was gone. A different weight now resided in his gut, a cold, solid ball.

He looked out over Jerusalem. The city baked in the afternoon silence. Somewhere down there, in the warren of streets, the women who had seen the empty tomb were telling their story. Somewhere, the disciples were hiding, terrified. And here, on this wall, a soldier who had seen the folded cloth was now pledged to call them liars.

He heard the tread of the relief watch approaching, the rhythmic crunch of sandals on stone. He picked up his pack and slung it over his shoulder. He took his position at the parapet, his back straight, his eyes scanning the horizon as he’d been trained to do. He was Decimus Valerius, legionary of the Cohors I Italica, assigned to the western battlement. He had slept on watch. He would take his lashes. He would not speak of it again.

The relief watch passed him with a nod. He acknowledged it with a grunt.

The sun beat down. The lizard did not reappear. In the crack in the stone, fifty pieces of silver rested in the dark, a hidden heart of the lie, waiting for a future he could not yet bring himself to buy.
