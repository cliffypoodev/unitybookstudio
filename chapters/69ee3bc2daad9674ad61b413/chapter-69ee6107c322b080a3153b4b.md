The relief watch’s footsteps faded down the stone stairs. The heat on the battlement was a physical weight, pressing the stink of the city up from the streets—cooking oil, sewage, dust, the sweet-rot smell of the market’s refuse pile. Decimus stood at his post. His back was straight, his eyes scanned the horizon. The posture was automatic, drilled into his spine. Inside, the space where the pouch had hung on his belt felt obscenely light. The new weight in his gut was a lodestone.

He finished his watch. The sun crawled. When the horn sounded the next watch change, he moved with the other men, a piece of a machine. Down the steps, through the arched gate, into the dim cool of the barracks block. The air inside smelled of sweat, leather oil, and the flat, stale scent of old bread. His contubernium’s room was empty. Marius and Proculus were probably still at the Temple, collecting their own pieces of silver. Felix and Lucius would be at the baths, trying to wash the garden dust from their skin. Gaius was wherever Gaius went to think—some quiet corner of the fortress where a man could calculate without interruption.

Decimus sat on the edge of his bunk. The straw stuffing crackled. He unlaced his sandals, placed them neatly beside the bedframe. He unbuckled his belt, laid it across his knees. The empty spot glared at him.

His fingers found the rough wool of his tunic, the linen of his subligaculum beneath. He dug into the small hidden pocket sewn into the waistband. His mother’s doing. “For your emergency coin, Decimus. Don’t let the others see.” He pulled out the single silver denarius he kept there, warm from his skin. He turned it over. The profile of Tiberius was worn smooth on one side. He pressed the edge into the ball of his thumb until the skin blanched white.

The folded cloth.

It wouldn’t leave him. It was there, behind his eyes, when he blinked. Not the empty bench. Not the strange, clarified light. Just the cloth. The way it lay, collapsed in on itself, the hollow where a head had been. And beside it, the other one. Neat. Deliberate. A square of linen folded once, then again set apart.

A thief in a panic does not fold laundry. A thief with a dead weight to move, with guards possibly stirring outside, with the risk of being caught desecrating a tomb—he grabs. He yanks. He might drop the spices. He might leave a sandal behind in his hurry. He does not pause to make a tidy pile.

Decimus put the denarius back. The pressure in his gut tightened.

The door scraped open. Lucius stepped in, his hair damp, his face pink from scrubbing. He smelled of cheap soap and anxiety. He didn’t meet Decimus’s eyes. He went to his bunk and started polishing a greave that was already gleaming.

“Get it all off?” Decimus asked.

Lucius jumped. The cloth in his hand slipped. “Sir?”

“The dust. From the garden.”

“Oh.” Lucius rubbed at the bronze. “Tried. Water’s tepid. Makes the soap clot.” He fell silent, his polishing becoming a frantic, circular motion.

“Proculus and Marius back?”

“Haven’t seen them.”

Decimus watched him. Lucius’s shoulders were hunched, his neck tendons tight. He was waiting for an order, a reassurance, something to tell him how to feel. Decimus had nothing to give him. A silence stretched, filled only by the whisper of cloth on metal.

“Do you think they’ll…” Lucius started, then stopped. He changed direction. “My mother. She always said the dead rest. That it’s a kindness. A final kindness.”

“She’s right.”

“But if they don’t.” Lucius looked up, his eyes wide and young. “If they… get up. Where does the kindness go?”

Decimus stood. The movement was too sharp. Lucius flinched. “The tribune’s orders stand. We slept. The body was stolen. That’s the report.”

“I know the report. I can say the words.” Lucius’s voice dropped to a whisper. “But what if the words are wrong?”

They are. The thought was clear and cold, a shard of ice in his chest. He didn’t say it. He walked to the small, high window. A slice of white-hot sky. A fly buzzed against the iron grate, a frantic, tiny percussion. “The words are what keep you paid. What keep you out of a disciplinary pit. The words are your shield, Lucius. Hold them up.”

“Yes, sir.”

He heard the bunk creak as Lucius lay down, turning to face the wall.

Decimus remained at the window. The fly gave up fell to the sill, and lay on its back, legs twitching. He watched it. A small, stupid death. He thought of the lizard on the wall, the quick dart of its tongue. Life was hungry. Life took what it could, and then it stopped. That was the order of things. A body in a tomb stayed a body. Spices slowed the smell. Stone kept out the animals. Eventually, bones. That was the contract.

The folded cloth broke the contract.

It was a small thing. A ridiculous thing. In the grand ledger of a soldier’s life—the battles, the orders, the silences you bought with coin or blood—a square of linen meant nothing. It hooked him. It was the pebble in the sandal that blistered the heel, the crack in the cup that made the wine taste of clay.

He left Lucius to his pretended sleep. The corridor outside was cooler, the stone floor sweating a faint damp. He walked without purpose, his footsteps echoing. He passed the mess hall, empty now between meals, the long tables scarred and sticky. He passed the armory, the smell of iron and linseed oil sharp behind its barred door. He ended up in the latrine, the reek of ammonia and lime so strong it made his eyes water. He wasn’t there to piss. He stood over the channel of running water, hands braced on the stone partition, and stared into the dark flow.

The money was in the wall. A secret. His secret. Gaius thought he’d spent it, buried it in his pack. The priests thought it was buying his voice. But it was sitting in a crack, gathering mortar dust. It was a placeholder. A token for a debt he hadn’t decided if he would pay.

He thought of his mother’s hands, kneading dough. The way she’d press the heels of her palms into the soft mass, fold it over, press again. A rhythm as old as wheat. She believed in the household gods. She left offerings—a bit of wine, a crust of bread—by the little shrine in the corner of their kitchen. She said it kept the balance. She never asked for riches. Just for the sun to rise, for the olives to swell on the trees, for her son to come home.

What would her hands do with thirty pieces of silver? Buy a new millstone? Repair the roof? More coin than she’d see in five years of harvests. Safety. It was the farm paid off, a cushion against drought, a guarantee of warm wool in winter.

It was also the price of calling her son a liar. Not that she would ever know. But he would.

He straightened. A soldier at the far end of the latrine coughed, spat into the channel. Decimus left.

Back in the room, Lucius was snoring softly, a fake, uneven rhythm. Decimus lay on his bunk. He stared at the smoke-stained ceiling. A crack ran diagonally across the plaster, forked like a lightning bolt. He followed it with his eyes, from one end to the other, then back again.

It wasn’t just neat. Placed. There was an intention to it. A courtesy. That was the word that came, unbidden and absurd. A courtesy to whom? To the women who would find it? To the man who had worn it?

He closed his eyes. Darkness. Then the image, brighter. The tomb interior in that first gray light. The stillness. The linen shell, empty. And beside the head-place, that small, square fold.

Why?

A practical man would find a practical reason. A thief, interrupted might drop the face cloth. He might even toss it aside. But he would not fold it. A follower, reclaiming the body for a proper burial elsewhere would take the linens with the body. They were expensive. They were part of the rites.

No reason fit. Except one.

The one reason that made no sense. The one the women had babbled about in the garden, their faces wild with a terror that looked too much like joy. “He is not here. He is risen.”

The words in his mind had no sound. They were just shapes, like letters carved on a stone he couldn’t read.

He turned on his side, facing the wall. The rough plaster was cool against his forehead. He breathed in the smell of old straw and his own sweat. A memory surfaced, not from the garden, not from the tomb. From his boyhood. His father, home from a campaign washing in a basin in the courtyard. Decimus maybe six, watching him. His father’s back was a map of scars—the pale, ropey welts of a lashing, the slick pucker of a pilum wound. He’d turned saw the boy staring. He hadn’t smiled. He’d said, “A man’s truth is what he carries. Not what he says.” Then he’d splashed water on his face, and the moment was gone.

Decimus had never understood it. Now, lying in the barracks, the weight of the hidden silver a cold stone in his belly, he thought he might. His father had carried the truth of that massacre he refused to order. He carried it to his execution. He spoke the words of refusal, but the truth was the thing he’d carried in his bones.

What was Decimus carrying?

Not the truth of a resurrection. That was too vast, too impossible. He carried a smaller, sharper truth. A square of linen. An absence that was full of intention. A hole in the world where a body should have been.

It was a truth that had no use. It wouldn’t buy his mother a new roof. It wouldn’t spare him the lash for sleeping on watch.

He must have slept. When he opened his eyes, the light from the high window had softened to amber. The room was fuller. Marius sat on his own bunk, counting coins from a pouch into his palm. He stacked them in little towers on the wool blanket. His face was calm, focused. The transaction was complete. Proculus lay on his stomach, his injured arm tucked beneath him. He was awake, watching Marius stack the silver. His expression was hollow.

Nobody spoke.

Decimus sat up. His mouth tasted of dust and metal. He reached for his waterskin and took a long pull. The water was warm, stale.

Marius finished his counting, swept the stacks back into his pouch. He looked up caught Decimus watching. He didn’t look away. He gave a slow, deliberate nod. A nod between men who had shared a dirty job. A nod that said, “We are alike now.”

Decimus held his gaze for a count of three. Then he looked down at his own hands. They were clean. He rubbed the thumb of his right hand over the calloused palm of his left. A small, circular motion. He could still feel the grit of the mortar dust.

Gaius entered. He didn’t slam the door, but it closed with a finality that made Proculus jump. Gaius surveyed the room—Marius with his pouch, Proculus curled in on himself, Lucius pretending to sleep, Decimus on the edge of his bunk. He didn’t ask if they’d been paid. He didn’t need to.

“The hearing is at dawn,” Gaius said. His voice was flat, stripped of all inflection. “The Temple court. You’ll be called one at a time. You will tell them you fell asleep. You will say you woke to see figures—shadowy figures—carrying something wrapped in linen from the tomb. You gave chase, but they vanished into the city. You found the tomb empty. That is all.”

“How many figures?” Marius asked, not looking up from tying his pouch to his belt.

“Doesn’t matter. Two. Three. You didn’t get a good count. Dark. You were groggy from sleep.”

“You didn’t see them move it. Already moved when you woke. They must have done it before you nodded off.”

Proculus pushed himself up on his good elbow. “What if they ask why all eight of us slept?”

“They won’t.” Gaius’s tone brooked no argument. “But if they do, you say you shared a skin of wine against the chill. You regret it. You accept your punishment.”

A shared skin of wine. A simple, human failing. Better than earthquake lights and empty linens. Better than a folded cloth.

Gaius’s eyes settled on Decimus. “The optio of the watch will confirm the story. The tribune has been… appeased. This ends with the lash, and then it ends. We put it behind us. We do not speak of it again. To anyone.”

Decimus gave a single, sharp nod. It was the nod of a soldier accepting an order. It meant nothing.

Gaius seemed satisfied. He turned to leave. His hand was on the door latch when he paused. He didn’t look back. “Valerius. A word.”

Decimus followed him out into the corridor. Gaius walked a few paces away from the door, stopping where the wall sconce held a sputtering torch. The light carved deep shadows under his eyes, in the grooves beside his mouth.

“The money,” Gaius said, low. “Where is it?”

“Safe.”

“Is it spent?”

“No.”

Gaius studied him. “You understand the… arithmetic of this. Yes? The silver is for your silence. If you keep the silver and break silence, that is theft. They will crucify you for it. If you give the silver back and speak, you are a madman. They will break you for it. The only move that keeps you whole is to keep the silver and keep quiet.”

“Do you?” Gaius took a step closer. The smell of him was leather and cold sweat. “I saw your face in that garden. You saw something. Something that got under your skin. Forget it. It doesn’t matter. What matters is the story we all tell tomorrow. The story that becomes the fact. In a month, it will be the only truth that’s left. The other thing… it will fade. Like a dream.”

“Dreams don’t leave evidence,” Decimus said. The words were out before he could stop them.

Gaius went very still. “Evidence?”

“The cloth. Folded.”

A long silence. The torch hissed. Down the hall, someone laughed, the sound swallowed by stone.

“I didn’t see a folded cloth,” Gaius said, each word precise as a nail being hammered. “I saw an empty tomb. I saw guards who failed in their duty. I see eight men who will be flogged for it. That is what I see.” He leaned in. He spoke quieter now, to a whisper, harsh and dry as sand. “What do you see, Decimus?”

Decimus looked past him, at the dancing shadow the torch threw on the opposite wall. It had no fixed shape. It writhed. “I see my mother’s farm.”

Gaius leaned back. He nodded once, a curt satisfied motion. This answer was correct. It was the answer of a practical man. “Good. Keep seeing it.”

He walked away, his footsteps echoing into the gloom.

Decimus stood alone in the flickering light. He saw the farm. The olive trees in neat rows. The whitewashed wall of the house. The patch of herbs by the door. He saw his mother’s hands.

And over it all, like a watermark on parchment, he saw a square of linen, folded neatly, placed aside.

A courtesy.

He went back inside. The room was silent. Marius was sharpening his pugio, the scrape of iron on stone rhythmic, hypnotic. Proculus stared at the ceiling. Lucius breathed the ragged breath of true sleep.

Decimus lay back down. He closed his eyes. He did not see the farm. He saw the crack in the battlement wall, the dark seam where the mortar had crumbled. He saw the pouch pushed deep into that darkness, a secret swallowed by stone. He saw the future as a forked path. Down one road, the lie was told, the lashes were taken, the silver was dug up and spent. Life went on.

He didn’t know what was down the other. Only that it began with a folded cloth.

He held the image in his mind until sleep took him, not as an escape, but as a practice. A rehearsal for the silence to come.
