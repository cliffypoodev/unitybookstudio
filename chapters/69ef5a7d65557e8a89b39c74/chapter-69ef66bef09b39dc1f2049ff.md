The air in Gator Gulch Haunted House smelled like two things: dead possum and prophetic regret. Patty was intimately familiar with both. The possum was a recent addition, bloating under the floorboards of the gift shop. The regret was older, baked into the warped plywood walls, the ghost of a thousand bad decisions made by men in cheap monster suits. Patty adjusted the proton pack strapped to his back. It wasn’t a real proton pack. It was a modified swamp buggy battery hooked to a paint sprayer and some Christmas lights, but in the dim green glow of his ecto-goggles, it looked legit.

His partner, Boone, kicked the front door. It swung open with a squeal that ended in a wet gurgle. “Charges are set, Ectoplasm. The perimeter is spiritually secure.”

Boone believed everything he said. He was a former alligator wrestler who’d found Jesus and then lost him again somewhere around the concept of electromagnetic spectral residue. He wore camo overalls and a motorcycle helmet with a cross painted on the front in white correction fluid.

“You used the holy water sprinklers?” Patty asked.

“Affirmative. And the backup salt lines. And the psalms on cassette tape playing on a loop in the truck.” He hefted his own weapon, a modified potato gun loaded with rock salt and dried sage. “If there’s a demon here, it’s gonna taste like Thanksgiving and feel the wrath of the Lord.”

Their third, Cissy was already inside. They found her in the main hall, which was supposed to be a swamp monster’s cave but now just looked like a flooded basement with sad plastic ferns. She had her ear pressed to a rusting air duct. Cissy was their tech. She believed ghosts were just “bad data” and could be debugged.

“Hear anything?” Boone whispered, his voice echoing in the helmet.

“White noise,” Cissy said, not moving. “But there’s a pattern underneath. A rhythm. It’s not a moan. It’s… a hum.”

A low-frequency thrum vibrated up through the waterlogged floorboards. It made Patty’s fillings ache. He’d felt this before, in the pit of his stomach, right before he’d had his childhood vision of Jesus riding a ghostbuster through the food court at the Dadeland Mall. That had been a Tuesday. This was a Wednesday, and the vibe was similarly off.

“That’s not a ghost,” Patty said. “Ghosts are sorrow, or anger, or unfinished business. That’s a… a mechanical indigestion.”

“Class five full-torso phantasm,” Boone declared, consulting a meter he’d built from a garage door opener. “Readings are off the chart. Also, my teeth are buzzing.”

The hum deepened. The few remaining lights in the haunted house—a string of orange bulbs shaped like pumpkins—flickered. Instead of going dark, they pulsed in unison, a slow, steady beat. Thump. The dirty water puddled on the floor shivered in perfect concentric circles.

Cissy stood up, her eyes wide behind her thick glasses. “That’s not random. That’s a signal.”

“It’s a cry for help from the other side!” Boone shouted, raising his potato gun. “Show yourself, spirit! We come in peace, armed with the power of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Hostile Takeover!”

Patty’s method was different. He couldn’t just talk to spirits. Words got stuck in his throat, ever since the mall vision. His communication was more physical. As the hum reached a fever pitch, his body knew what to do.

He shrugged off the proton pack. It hit the water with a splash.

“What are you doing?” Boone hissed. “That’s our only heavy artillery!”

“It’s a dialogue, Boone. Not a demolition.” Patty took a deep breath, centered himself, and began to move.

His interpretive dance for hostile spirits usually started with a series of defensive, angular poses—lots of sharp elbows and startled glances. This felt different. The hum wasn’t angry. It was insistent. His arms, which usually slashed the air began to flow. His feet, which usually stomped began to glide through the muck. He became a slow, sinuous ripple, a question mark made of flesh.

Boone watched, horrified. “He’s doing the wet noodle. That’s his ‘seeking understanding’ routine. We don’t have time for understanding, Patty! We have time for salt and scripture!”

A cold spot formed in the center of the room. The air got heavy, thick enough to chew. Then the wall at the end of the hall changed. The peeling mural of a giant alligator faded, replaced by a flickering, blue-white projection. It wasn’t a face. It wasn’t a specter.

It was a schematic.

Complex, interlocking lines of light formed a three-dimensional diagram. It looked like a circuit board crossed with a root system. Symbols flickered at the nodes—not letters, but sharp, angular glyphs that hurt to look at. A low, synthesized voice, crackling with static, filtered from the walls themselves.

“Primary containment… failing. Biological command protocol… migrating. Root network… active.”

The voice sounded bored. Terribly, utterly bored, and deeply annoyed.

Patty stopped dancing. “Okay. That’s new.”

Boone lowered his potato gun. “What in the name of Sam Hill is that? Is that… engineering?”

Cissy stepped closer, her face bathed in the blue light. “It’s a flow chart. For a… a system. Look at the feedback loops. The redundancy here.” She traced a shape in the air. “This is beautiful. Horrible, but beautiful. It’s like a nervous system for a building.”

“Warning,” the voice droned. “Serpent Engine assimilation of local biome… exceeds projected timeline. All non-compliant organic matter will be… reconfigured.”

A new image flashed: a map of the Everglades, with pulsing red dots connected by glowing lines. One dot blinked right on their location.

“It’s talking about the swamp,” Patty whispered. His prophetic regret sense was tingling like a bad tooth. “It’s not haunting this place. It’s… reporting from it.”

Boone’s garage door opener meter started smoking. He dropped it with a yelp. “It’s a demon of technology! A silicon Satan! I need more sage!”

“It’s not a demon, you pinecone,” Cissy snapped, pulling a small tape recorder from her vest. She held it up to the vibrating air. “It’s a message. A diagnostic report. And it’s stuck.”

The schematic on the wall zoomed in on one pulsing node. Glyphs scrolled past. One phrase resolved, clearer than the rest, in blocky, digital text: KRANE WETLAND TRIAL SITE—BIOCOMMAND RELAY ALPHA.

The hum shifted pitch. The bored voice gained a tinge of urgency.

“Personnel stranded in chrono-synclastic phase shift. Request extraction. Coordinates embedded in… in…” The voice dissolved into a burst of static, followed by the distinct sound of someone sighing. “Oh, forget it. They’ll never get it. We’re doomed to repeat the safety briefing for all eternity.”

The projection flickered. For a split second, Patty saw them. Three figures, translucent and glowing, standing where the schematic had been. They wore lab coats and expressions of profound, bureaucratic misery. One was checking a clipboard. Another was massaging his temples.

Then they were gone. The schematic vanished. The pumpkin lights went out, plunging them into darkness broken only by the gurgle of swamp water.

Patty’s dance had been for nothing. This wasn’t a ghost. This was a technical support team trapped in a cosmic loophole.

Boone fumbled for a flashlight. The beam cut through the dark, landing on the wall. The alligator mural was back. But scorched into the paint, faint and smoking was the same root-and-circuit diagram.

“What,” Boone said slowly, “was that?”

Cissy clicked off her tape recorder. “That, Boone was a residual psychic imprint of a temporal accident. A ghost isn’t a soul. It’s data that can’t be deleted. And these guys?” She gestured to the scorched wall. “They’re not dead. They’re… buffering.”

Patty picked up his fake proton pack. The weight of it felt stupid now. “They’re trying to warn people. About some… engine. And it’s in the swamp.”

The smell of prophetic regret grew stronger, mixing with the possum and the ozone. Patty’s childhood vision flashed again: Jesus on a ghostbuster’s pack, not fighting spirits, but calmly unplugging something. He’d always thought it was metaphorical.

Now, standing in the belly of a failed haunted house, with the echo of a bored engineer in his ears, Patty wondered if the Son of God had been trying to show him a very specific set of instructions.

“We’re not ghostbusters,” Patty said, the realization dawning like a stomachache. “We’re IT for the apocalypse.”

Boone looked from the wall to Patty, his helmet visor fogged with confusion. “But I bought all this sage.”

They regrouped in the “Baron Samedi’s Voodoo Lounge,” which was now just a storage closet with a broken fog machine. Cissy played the tape back. The bored voice filled the small space, detailing containment failures and biomass conversion rates.

“…and if the root network reaches maturity, local consciousness will be overwritten with command protocols. Basically, the swamp gets a new boss. A real micromanager.”

“See?” Cissy said, pausing the tape. “It’s a hostile takeover. A corporate merger of reality. These engineers were working on something called Project LAZARUS REX. Wetland branch. Something went wrong, and they didn’t die. They just… phased out of sync with our time. Their last moments are on a loop.”

“So they’re not ghosts,” Patty repeated, trying to fit this into his theology. His theology was mostly borrowed from late-night cable and a deep-seated need for his life to mean something. “They’re… live-streaming their own disaster from the past?”

“From a pocket of the past,” Cissy corrected. “A bubble in time, sustained by… heck, I don’t know. Something powerful and weird.”

Boone had taken his helmet off. His face was pale. “You’re telling me we just got a mission brief from a bunch of dead scientists?”

“Not dead,” Cissy and Patty said in unison.

The door to the closet creaked open. A man stood there, silhouetted by the gray afternoon light filtering through the broken roof. He was tall, thin, and dressed in a beige suit that had clearly lost an argument with the swamp. He held a briefcase.

“Patty O’Shea?” he said, his voice smooth and tired. “My name is Arlen. I’m with… a concerned party. I understand you’ve made contact.”

They all froze. Boone reached slowly for his potato gun.

“Who’s asking?” Patty said, stepping forward, putting himself between this guy and Cissy’s tape recorder.

Arlen offered a thin smile that didn’t reach his eyes. He looked like a math teacher who’d seen too much. “I represent the people who used to sign the checks for Project LAZARUS REX. The wetland trials were… shut down. Improperly, it seems.” He glanced at the scorched wall visible through the closet door. “The phenomenon you witnessed is a containment leak. A memory leak, if you will. It’s not dangerous. Just… embarrassing for my employers.”

“That ‘embarrassing’ leak talked about an engine turning my swamp into a puppet show,” Boone growled.

“Colorful metaphor.” Arlen sighed. He set his briefcase on a moldy crate and clicked it open. Inside were neat rows of hundred-dollar bills. “This is a retainer. For your services. We need you to… stop listening. Your unique methods have inadvertently amplified the signal. Just walk away. There’s fifty thousand dollars here. Consider it a consulting fee.”

The money smelled like new paper and lies. Patty’s prophetic regret went into overdrive. This was the moment in the movie where the plucky heroes take the cash and the world ends in the third act.

Patty looked at the money. He thought about his vision. He thought about the bored doomed engineers, forever repeating their warning.

He began to dance.

It wasn’t his seeking-understanding dance. This was his “I’m about to make a terrible decision” dance—a series of sharp, jerky movements, punctuated by aggressive finger-pointing at the briefcase, then at Arlen, then at the sky.

Arlen watched, his expression flattening into pure bewilderment.

Boone understood immediately. “He’s saying the money is a spiritual pollutant! That taking it would curse our souls to wander the earth like… like salesmen!”

Cissy just closed the briefcase with a snap. “We’re not for sale. Especially not by someone who uses the term ‘memory leak’ to describe a screaming hole in reality.”

Arlen’s polite mask slipped. Annoyance flashed in his eyes. “You don’t understand what you’re interfering with. This isn’t ghosts. This is legacy code. And the system it’s trying to run is waking up. You can’t debug it with… with interpretive dance.”

“Wanna bet?” Patty said, stopping mid-pirouette.

The hum returned. This time it wasn’t in the walls. It was in his bones. The scorched diagram on the wall across the hall began to glow again, brighter. The symbols shifted, rearranging. The bored engineer’s voice crackled, sharper now, panicked.

“Secondary breach! The root has found a local amplifier! It’s him! The dancer!”

The voice cut off. The diagram blazed, and the lines of light snaked out from the wall, not as light anymore, but as thin, glowing vines. They slithered across the floor, through the dirty water, moving with terrible purpose. Not towards Arlen.

Towards Patty.

The first vine wrapped around Patty’s ankle. It didn’t feel like a plant. It felt like cold electricity and someone else’s desperate thought. Images flooded his mind, not as pictures, but as sensations: the taste of copper and soil, the smell of ozone and rotting flowers, the sound of a million insects chanting in perfect rhythm.

Arlen took a step back, his calm gone. “Oh, no. No, no, no. It’s not just a memory. It’s recruiting.”

The vine tightened. More slithered from the wall, aiming for Patty’s wrists, his throat. The hum was inside his skull now, a constant, pounding rhythm. Underneath it, he heard a new voice. This one wasn’t bored. It was ancient hungry, and very, very interested.

“A new node,” it said, low, not in his ears, but in the marrow of his bones. “A flexible conduit. You will dance the world into shape for us.”

Boone yelled and fired his potato gun. The salted sage pattered against the vines harmlessly. Cissy grabbed a machete from her belt and started hacking. The severed vines writhed on the floor, glowing sap oozing out that smelled like burnt almonds and regret.

Patty was the amplifier. His dance, his stupid, desperate attempt to talk to the unseen had tuned him to the frequency of the thing in the swamp. The Serpent Engine. And it liked what it heard.

Arlen stared, not at Patty, but at the writhing, intelligent roots. His face was a sheet of pure, unprofessional terror. “It’s not just awake,” he said. “It’s curious.”

The main vine yanked Patty’s foot out from under him. He hit the filthy water with a splash. The world narrowed to the glow, the hum, and the hungry, ancient voice offering him a job as the choreographer for the end of everything.

This was not the ghostbusting gig Patty had signed up for.

The water in his mouth tasted like possum and panic. The vine around his ankle pulled, not with brute force, but with a terrible, intelligent insistence, like it was reeling in a particularly interesting specimen. He scrabbled at the slimy floorboards, his fingers finding no purchase.

Boone’s motorcycle helmet appeared in Patty’s blurry vision. He dropped to his knees, not to grab Patty, but to press his hands against the sides of his head. “The chanting! Do you hear the chanting? It’s like a bee choir singing tax forms!”

Cissy didn’t hear chanting. She saw patterns. She stopped hacking at the vines and stared, machete dangling, at the way the glowing roots now formed a perfect spiral around Patty, pulsing in time with the hum. “It’s a connection protocol. It’s handshaking. Patty, stop thinking!”

“I’m not thinking!” Patty gargled, swallowing another mouthful of swamp-tainted water. “I’m drowning!”

“Your brainwaves are thinking! You’re broadcasting existential dread on its preferred frequency! Think about something neutral! Baseball! Tax law! The middle seasons of MASH!”

Patty tried. He conjured an image of Alan Alda. The vine squeezed tighter. A fresh wave of sensations hit him: the crushing pressure of deep soil, the patient, slow unfurling of a million tendrils seeking light, the cold, clean logic of a machine evaluating biomass.

Arlen, the beige-suited bureaucrat of doom had backed against the wall. He fumbled in his jacket and pulled out a small, black device that looked like a TV remote. He pointed it at the vines and pressed a button. A high-pitched whine sliced through the hum. The vines flinched. The glow dimmed for a second.

“Signal jammer,” he barked, his voice tight. “Military-grade. It won’t stop it, but it might confuse it long enough for you to get out of the… the connection radius.” He sounded like he was reading from a manual he didn’t believe.

The pressure on Patty’s ankle lessened. He kicked free, scrambled backward on his elbows, and collided with Boone’s legs. Boone helped him up. Patty’s jeans were soaked and one sock was glowing faintly.

“We need to contain the source,” Cissy said, her eyes locked on the scorched wall. The diagram was alive, the lines crawling like neon worms. “The projection is coming from somewhere. A transmitter. Probably whatever they left behind when they… phased.”

“The control room,” Patty coughed out. He remembered the map from the haunted house’s grand opening brochure. “Behind the giant animatronic gator. There’s a tech booth.”

Boone looked from the writhing wall to Arlen’s jammer, which was now emitting a smell like burning hair. “We’re going towards the evil diagram? The one that just tried to make Patty a human antenna?”

“The evil diagram is a symptom,” Cissy said, already moving, sloshing through the water. “We find the hardware, we can maybe turn it off. Or at least change the channel.”

Arlen moved to block her path. “You cannot go in there. The chrono-synclastic instability is too pronounced. You could be phased alongside them. Or worse, you could complete the circuit and give the root network a permanent door into our timeline.”

Patty wiped swamp off his face. His prophetic regret was a solid lump in his gut. “Mister, your choices are get out of our way, or get danced at. And my ‘frustrated with corporate lackeys’ dance involves a lot of pelvic thrusts.”

Arlen hesitated. The jammer in his hand sparked, and he dropped it with a curse. The hum surged back, louder. The pumpkin lights overhead flickered on and off, but now they were blinking in a frantic, rhythmic SOS.

Boone hefted his potato gun. “I’m out of sage. I’ve got one shot of rock salt and a prayer that’s mostly about football scores. Let’s move.”

They left Arlen clutching his briefcase of useless money and pushed through a fake Spanish moss curtain. It was made of plastic and smelled like cat urine. Behind it was the giant animatronic gator, a hulking skeleton of rusted metal and mildewed latex. Its jaw hung open, revealing a dark passage where its throat would be. A faded sign read: ‘ABANDON HOPE, YE WHO ENTER THE GATOR’S GUT.’

“Subtle,” Boone muttered.

They crawled in. The passage was tight, wet, and smelled of fried electronics and despair. It opened into a small, square room. The control room.

It was a museum of failure. Banks of dead monitors lined one wall, their screens dark and dusty. A mixing board with sliders was home to a thriving colony of mushrooms. In the center of the room sat a single chair, bolted to the floor. And in front of it, on a metal desk was the source of the glow.

It wasn’t a computer. It was a lump of smooth, dark material about the size of a toaster. It looked like obsidian, but light moved inside it, following the same root-and-circuit patterns they’d seen on the wall. Wires, frayed and ancient, snaked from it into the walls, the floor, the ceiling. It hummed. Not the oppressive swamp hum, but a quieter, sadder version.

And in the chair, flickering in and out of existence like a bad connection sat a man.

He was translucent washed in the same blue light. He wore a lab coat over a plaid shirt. He had a beard that looked like it had grown during a single, long sigh. He was typing furiously on a keyboard that wasn’t there. As they watched, he’d type, slam an invisible enter key, then put his head in his hands. He’d fade out, then snap back into existence a few inches to the left and repeat the sequence.

“That’s him,” Patty murmured. “The bored guy.”

Cissy approached slowly, not looking at the ghost, but at the obsidian lump. “This is the relay. It’s… organic. Grown, not built. And it’s hot. It’s pulling power from somewhere. Geothermal maybe.”

Boone made the sign of the cross with his potato gun. “Spirit! We command you to cease your phantom typing and state your business!”

The flickering engineer didn’t look up. He typed. He faded. He reappeared. He typed the same thing.

Cissy leaned over the desk, squinting at the lump. “It’s not a recording. It’s a live feed. He’s stuck in a time loop, but he’s still there, trying to send data. Look.” She pointed to the surface of the dark material. Tiny, glowing symbols formed and dissolved. “He’s typing a report. Over and over.”

Patty stepped closer. The air around the chair was cold and smelled of ozone and stale coffee. “Hey,” he said, softly.

The engineer’s head snapped up. His eyes, hollow and bright with decades of frustration, locked onto Patty’s. He stopped typing. He opened his mouth. A staticky whisper filled the room, not from his mouth, but from the lump.

“…containment failure… biomass conversion at eighteen percent… the root is dreaming now… it dreams of command…”

“We hear you,” Patty said. He didn’t dance. He just stood there, soaked and scared. “What do you need?”

He stared at Patty. He flickered. For a second, Patty saw right through him to the moldy wall behind. Then he solidified, and his expression changed. The bureaucratic misery cracked, replaced by something raw and desperate. He pointed a translucent finger at the obsidian lump.

“Kill it,” the whisper hissed, full of static. “The relay. It’s the anchor. It holds the door open. They’re coming through the root.”

“Who’s coming?” Boone asked.

The engineer’s image distorted, stretching like taffy. His whisper became a chorus of overlapping voices, all terrified, all saying the same thing. “The Engine. The Serpent. It learned to grow. It’s in the roots. In the water. It wants the swamp. Then the cities. It wants everything to… to belong.”

He flickered violently. The room around them shivered. The dead monitors on the wall suddenly blazed to life, each showing a different, impossible image.

Monitor One: a live feed of the swamp outside, but the sawgrass was standing in perfect, military rows. Monitor Two: a schematic of a human brain, with glowing roots wrapping around the frontal lobe. Monitor Three: a close-up of a gator’s eye, its pupil reflecting not light, but scrolling lines of digital code. Monitor Four: a weather map of Florida, with a swirling, serpentine storm pattern labeled “BIOLOGICAL COMMAND FRONT.”

“Reality’s glitching!” Cissy yelled, ducking as a monitor spat sparks.

The engineer was now standing, his hands pressed against an invisible barrier between him and them. His mouth moved silently, then the whisper returned, weaker. “You have to break the relay. It’s the only way to collapse the bubble. We’ll be lost. But you’ll be safe. For a while.”

“We can’t just kill you!” Patty said. The prophetic regret in his gut twisted into something else—a sharp, aching pity.

He gave a ghost of a smile, sad and tired. “We’ve been dead for years. We just haven’t stopped working yet.” He pointed to the lump again, urgently. “Use the… the dancer. His resonance. Feedback loop. Overload it.”

He flickered out. The monitors died. The only light came from the pulsating relay.

Silence, except for the hum and their breathing.

“He wants me to dance at the evil rock,” Patty said.

“It’s science, Patty!” Cissy said, her voice frantic with excitement. “You’re a biological resonator! You tuned into the frequency! If you resonate at the relay with enough… emotional intensity, you could create a sympathetic vibration! Shatter it!”

“What if I resonate us all into a pocket dimension?” Patty asked.

“Better than being turned into a node in a swamp network,” Boone said, peering at the relay with grim determination. He patted Patty’s shoulder. “Do your thing, Ectoplasm. The weirdest thing you got. I’ll cover you with the power of prayer and this last bit of salt.”

There was no time to argue. The hum was building again, and this time it felt inquisitive, probing. The vines in the other room began to thrash, slapping against the walls. Arlen shouted something unintelligible, then there was a crash.

Patty stood in front of the obsidian lump. It was warm to the touch, and it vibrated under his fingertips. He closed his eyes. He didn’t think about steps. He thought about the bored engineer doomed to type his warning forever. He thought about the swamp, being forced to march in rows. He thought about his stupid vision of Jesus, calmly unplugging things.

His body moved.

It wasn’t a dance he’d ever done before. It was all sharp, stuttering stops and starts—a mimicry of a broken transmission. He jerked his head to the side, a glitch. He moved his arms in opposing, jagged rhythms. He stomped his feet not in time, but in a chaotic, arrhythmic pattern designed to sound like system failure. It was the dance of a corrupted file, a dropped call, a blue screen of death made flesh.

The relay pulsed faster. The light inside it swirled, confused.

“You’re agitating it!” Cissy cheered. “It doesn’t know how to parse you! You’re a syntax error!”

Patty got weirder. He incorporated a move he’d seen a malfunctioning robot do at a car wash once. He added a series of frantic, tiny hops. He hummed the theme song to Cheers off-key.

A crack appeared on the surface of the relay with a sound like ice breaking.

Then the room folded.

It wasn’t a metaphor. One second Patty was facing the desk. The next, the desk was on the ceiling. Boone was standing perpendicular to the floor, his feet on the wall, looking profoundly annoyed. Cissy was upside down, her glasses dangling from one ear. The laws of physics had called in sick.

“Chrono-synclastic instability!” Cissy yelled from her inverted position. “We’re inside the bubble!”

The flickering engineer reappeared, not in the chair, but standing amidst them. He was more solid now, his lab coat no longer translucent. He looked at Patty, then at the cracking relay. He nodded, once.

A giant form materialized behind him. Then another. The other two engineers. One was a woman with a severe bun, holding a clipboard that was now on fire. The other was a young man frantically trying to drink from a coffee mug that poured its contents upward into the air.

They were all here. With them.

The woman with the burning clipboard pointed at the relay. Her voice, when it came was clear and crisp, no longer a whisper. “The fracture point is now. You must strike it. A physical impact, during the resonant dissonance.”

“Strike it with what?” Boone asked, now lying on what had become the ceiling.

The young engineer with the coffee mug threw his mug at the relay. It passed through it harmlessly. “Something real! Something from your side!”

Patty looked around. His proton pack was in the other room. Boone’s potato gun was empty. Cissy’s machete was on the floor, which was currently the south wall.

The bored engineer, the first one, stepped closer to Patty. The chaos of the folding room didn’t seem to affect him. He looked into Patty’s face, and for the first time, Patty saw not desperation, but a weary kind of hope. He reached out his hand. Not to grab Patty. He pointed at Patty’s chest.

Patty looked down. Hanging from a cord around his neck, under his wet t-shirt was a small, heavy St. Christopher medal. His grandmother’s. Cheap metal, shaped like a shield.

“This?” Patty asked, pulling it out.

He nodded.

It was the dumbest weapon imaginable. It was also the only one Patty had.

The room spun again. The relay was now pulsing like a panicked heart, light bleeding from the crack. The hum was a scream. The vines from the other room finally burst through the doorway, not slithering now, but lashing like whips, seeking to protect their anchor.

“Now, Patty!” Cissy screamed, clinging to a pipe that was dripping sideways.

Patty didn’t dance. He just moved. He let the folding room toss him towards the desk. He grabbed the edge, pulled himself forward, and with all the strength he had left, he swung the St. Christopher medal on its cord like a tiny, pathetic mace.

He brought it down on the crack in the obsidian lump.

The sound was not a crash. It was a release. A deep, sub-audible pop, like a pressure valve blowing. The light inside the relay died instantly. The hum cut off, replaced by a ringing silence so absolute it hurt his ears.

The folding room snapped back. Gravity reasserted itself with a vengeful yank. Boone fell from the ceiling and landed in a heap. Cissy dropped onto the mushroom-covered mixing board with a squelch. Patty collapsed against the desk, the cheap metal medal hot in his hand.

The vines in the doorway went limp, their glow fading to a dull, dead gray.

And the three engineers stood before them, solid, whole, and no longer flickering.

They looked at each other. They looked at their hands. The woman patted out the fire on her clipboard. The young man looked at his empty hand where the coffee mug had been.

The bored one, the first one, let out a long, slow breath. It fogged in the suddenly cold air. He looked at Patty.

“Thank you,” he said. His voice was real. Quiet, raspy from disuse, but real.

“You’re… here,” Patty managed to say.

“For now,” the woman said, her voice crisp. “The bubble collapsed. We’re reintegrated. Temporarily. The laws of causality will… reassert themselves. We shouldn’t be here.”

“What happens to you?” Boone asked, getting to his feet, rubbing his back.

The young engineer shrugged. He had kind eyes. “We finish dying, probably. But quietly. And only once. It’ll be a nice change.”

The first engineer stepped closer to Patty. He wasn’t scary anymore. He was just a tired man who’d been stuck in a hell of his own making. The comedic chaos of the last hour—the potato gun, the interpretive dance, the folding room—melted away, leaving something quiet and unbearably sad in the middle of the mildewed control room.

He reached out again. His hand didn’t point. It hovered, then gently, hesitantly took Patty’s hand that held the St. Christopher medal. His fingers were cold, but solid. A silent plea for understanding, a moment of human contact after an eternity of lonely, looping warnings.

His touch was a shock. Not of electricity, but of simple, deep gratitude. It was the most intimate thing that had ever happened to Patty in a place called Gator Gulch.

“The relay is broken,” he said quietly. “But the Serpent Engine is not. It’s in the roots. It learned from us. It’s patient. It will try again.”

He let go.

Behind him, on the blank, dead wall where the monitors had been, a shadow flickered. It wasn’t cast by anything in the room. It was vast, serpentine, and intelligent. It coiled across the plaster, a silhouette of something ancient and hungry that lived in the wiring of the world. It held for a second—a silent, looming promise—and then it was gone.

The three engineers looked at the wall, then at each other. They began to fade, not flickering this time, but gently dissolving, like mist in morning sun.

“Tell someone,” the woman said, her voice already distant. “Tell them to watch the swamp.”

And they were gone.

The control room was just a room again. A broken, wet, sad room. The obsidian lump on the desk was now just a dull, cracked stone.

Boone, Cissy, and Patty stood there, breathing in the smell of ozone and decay. The slapstick was over. The misinterpretations were cleared up. They weren’t ghostbusters. They were the cleanup crew for a catastrophe that hadn’t happened yet, briefed by casualties from a war they didn’t know they were in.

Cissy picked up her machete from the floor. “Well,” she said, her voice flat. “That was a thing.”

Patty looked at the St. Christopher medal in his palm. It was warm. He slipped it back under his shirt. It settled against his skin, a tiny, cold weight.

From the other room, they heard a groan. They shuffled back out to find Arlen pinned under the fallen body of the giant animatronic gator. His briefcase had burst open, and hundred-dollar bills were floating in the filthy water around him like sad, paper lilies.

He looked up at them, dazed. “Did you… did you stop it?”

“We unplugged the doorbell,” Boone said, nudging a floating bill with his boot. “The thing that was ringing it is still outside.”

Arlen closed his eyes. “My superiors are not going to like this.”

Patty looked past him, through the broken facade of the haunted house, out to the darkening swamp. The sawgrass waved in the evening breeze. It looked normal. For now.

But Patty could still feel the echo of that ancient, curious hunger. He could still see the serpentine shadow on the wall. A call to action, but to what action? Who do you call when the swamp itself is the patient zero?

Patty turned to Boone and Cissy. They looked as lost and tired as he felt.

“We need better equipment,” Patty said.

Boone nodded, staring at his empty potato gun. “I’m thinking holy water flamethrower.”

Cissy adjusted her glasses. “I’ll start working on a root network scanner. And maybe some better dance lessons.”

Outside, the first frogs began to croak. They sounded normal. Just frogs.

But Patty listened closer, his body still humming with spent echo. Underneath the croaks, so deep it was almost imagination, he heard it. A rhythm. A single, slow, patient beat.

Thump.

Like a giant, sleeping heart under the mud.

Waiting to be told what to do next.
