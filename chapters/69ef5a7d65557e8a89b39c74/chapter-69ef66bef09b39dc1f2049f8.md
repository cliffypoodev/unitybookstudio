The smell of burnt sage and prophetic regret clung to the bathroom stall like a ghost. He exhaled a cloud that swirled in the flickering fluorescent light, watching the smoke twist into the shape of a small, feathered dinosaur before it dissolved against the graffiti.

“See?” he whispered, his voice raspy. “It’s telling us something.”

Chloe blinked her mascara-caked lashes. She stared at the empty air where the smoke had been. “I see a water stain that looks like my Uncle Manny.”

“You’re not breathing right. You gotta hold it in the chest, not the lungs. It’s a chest thing.”

“My chest feels like I swallowed a hairball, Tiff.”

The bathroom door creaked open. They froze. Footsteps, the squeak of cheap sneakers on wet tile. A voice, nasal and skeptical cut through the stall door. “Vasquez? You in there? Mr. Henderson says if you’re not out here cleaning the deep fryer in five minutes, he’s telling your mom about the missing chimichangas.”

It was Jason from Bio. He worked the concession stand with Tiff. Tiff rolled his eyes at Chloe. “Tell him I’m communing with a higher power! It’s a religious exemption!”

The footsteps didn’t move. “It smells like a Grateful Dead concert threw up in here.”

“It’s incense! For my… aunt’s memory!”

“Your aunt’s memory smells like skunk and failure,” Jason said. The sneakers squeaked away. A door swung shut.

Chloe let out a breath she’d been holding. It didn’t look like anything. “This is stupid. I’m going to fail trig.”

“This is transcendent,” Tiff corrected, tapping the small, hand-carved wooden vape pen in his palm. It looked like a cheap rosary someone had turned into a pipe. His masterpiece. The Virgin Mary Vape Ring. “You saw the thing with the dinosaur smoke. Admit it.”

“I saw smoke, Tiff. I’ve been hot-boxing this stall since second period. I’m starting to hallucinate the periodic table.”

Tiff ignored her. The high was settling into his bones, a warm, buzzing certainty. The cracked white tiles of the stall weren’t just tiles anymore. They were scales. The dripping faucet was a jungle drip. And the lingering smell wasn’t just weed—it was ozone and hot metal and something sweetly rotten, like fruit left in a sun-baked car.

A vision bloomed behind his eyes without warning.

Not the usual hazy could-be-anything shapes. This was crisp. A wet, black landscape of twisted mangroves, their roots like knotted veins under water that glowed a sickly green. Something moved in the water. Not a gator. Something bigger, with a spine of jagged metallic fins. It turned a single, lamp-like eye toward him. The eye had a tiny, red ‘777’ etched in the corner.

Tiff jerked back, his skull thunking against the stall partition.

“Whoa, you okay?” Chloe asked, her voice far away.

“It’s clearer,” Tiff breathed, his heart hammering a weird, off-rhythm beat against his ribs. “It’s not just shapes. There’s… numbers.”

“Numbers? Like, lottery numbers?”

“Not lottery numbers.” Tiff opened his eyes. The bathroom was just a bathroom again, sad and empty. But the feeling clung—a cold, electric dread sitting right under his breastbone. “Like a serial number. On a dinosaur.”

Chloe stared at him. Her expression cycled through concern, confusion, and the dawning horror of being locked in a bathroom with a person who was officially losing it. “Okay. I think maybe this batch is, like, laced with something. PCP. Or bad vibes.”

“It’s not laced,” Tiff snapped, defensive. The weed was his creation, his ticket out. His family’s legacy was grease fires and mild salsa. His was supposed to be miracles. Or at least a decent profit. “It’s pure. It’s from a sacred strain. My guy said it was, like, biblical.”

“Your guy is a burnout named Dutch who lives on a houseboat and thinks the CIA controls the weather.”

“He’s a prophet!”

“He owes my brother twenty bucks for pizza.”

Tiff looked down at the vape pen. The little wooden Virgin Mary looked back, her face smoothed from handling. His cousin Mateo had seen a cyber-dinosaur last year. A real one. It bit a drug runner in half down by the canals, then turned and touched Mateo’s shattered leg with its snout. The bone knit together. The doctors called it a spontaneous remission. Mateo called it a holy chomp. He drew pictures of the thing for months—huge, teeth like kitchen knives, one mechanical eye. He said it had a look on its face. Not smart. Sad.

Tiff started seeing the creatures in the smoke a week after he first tried Dutch’s new ‘Burning Bush OG’ blend. At first he thought it was just good weed. Then the visions got specific.

He stashed the vape pen in his bra. “Forget it. You’re not ready. The veil between worlds is thin for some of us. For others,” he nodded at her, “it’s a thick, soundproof curtain.”

“I’m ready to see an A-minus in trig. Can your veil do that?”

Tiff pushed out of the stall. One hallway of Southwest Miami High during spring break was a tomb of echoing footsteps and industrial cleaner. His sneakers squeaked on the linoleum. Every classroom door was dark. It felt like the whole world had emptied out, leaving him alone with the things in his head.

The concession stand was a metal cage in the empty cafeteria. Jason was inside reading a comic book and smelling like old hot-dog water.

“Henderson left,” he said without looking up. “Said to just lock up when you’re done. And that he’s counting the chimichangas tomorrow.”

“He’s a man of little faith,” Tiff said, slipping behind the counter. The familiar smells of stale grease and fake cheese usually comforted him. Now they just felt cheap. A dead-end smell.

He started wiping down the cold, greasy metal of the fryer basket. His hands moved automatically. His mind was back in the black water.

The vision had felt different. More like a memory that wasn’t his. The number. 777. That meant something. On the side of the Metro bus that went to the beach. It was a lucky number in slots.

It was also, a quiet part of his brain whispered, what Mateo had scrawled at the bottom of his dinosaur drawings. Next to the words “LAZARUS REX.”

“You’re quiet,” Jason said. He’d put his comic down. He had a nice face, Jason. A little squinty from reading, a permanent sunburn across his nose. He was the only guy in school who could explain photosynthesis without putting Tiff to sleep. “Still communing?”

“Just thinking.”

“About your aunt’s memory?”

“About ecosystems,” Tiff lied, grabbing a spray bottle. “Contamination. Like, if something toxic got into the water table. How far would it spread?”

Jason leaned on the counter, interested. This was his language. “Depends on the contaminant. Solubility, density. Is it organic or chemical?”

“What if it was… spiritual?”

His face fell. “Oh. You’re still on that.”

“I’m serious! What if a place could, like, remember being poisoned? And that memory could… get into things? Plants. Animals.” Tiff met his eyes. “People.”

Jason studied him. Not like Tiff was crazy. Like Tiff was a puzzling sample under a microscope. “There’s this thing called biomagnification. Toxins concentrate as they move up the food chain. A little poison in the algae becomes a lot of poison in the fish that eats the algae. A whole lot in the bird that eats the fish.” He paused. “You think your weed is poisoned?”

“No! It’s enlightened.” But Tiff’s stomach did a slow, cold turn. Biomagnification. The word sat in his head, heavy and wrong. Dutch got his seeds from somewhere. Was always bragging about his ‘connections,’ his ‘divine sources.’ What if his source was a dump site?

Jason must have seen the doubt on Tiff’s face. His voice softened. “Look, Tiff. You’ve been different since… you know. Since Mateo’s thing. You’re looking for answers in weird places.”

“The place I’m looking at is a swamp with a robot dinosaur in it, Jason. That’s not a weird place. That’s a very specific, very messed-up place.”

“You’re high.”

“I’m a mystic!”

“You’re a seventeen-year-old girl with a fryer-cleaning shift.” He said it gently. It still felt like a door slamming.

That bell on the cafeteria’s back door jangled. They both jumped. A guy walked in. He wasn’t from school. Too old maybe twenty-five, with a crisp polo shirt and khakis that looked like they’d never seen a wrinkle. His smile was a perfect, white, empty thing.

“Afternoon,” he said. His voice was smooth, like a radio ad. “Looking for the community liaison? I’m with the new federal environmental outreach program. We’re doing a survey about local water usage.”

He held up a clipboard. The top sheet had a logo: Everglades Renewal & Conservation Initiative. It looked official. It also looked like someone had made it on their home computer.

Jason straightened up, instantly suspicious. “School’s closed for break. No one here.”

“I see,” the man said. His eyes weren’t on Jason. They were on Tiff. They flicked to Tiff’s hands, still holding the greasy rag, then to his face. His smile didn’t change, but his eyes got sharper. Like he was recognizing Tiff from a description. “We’re particularly interested in talking to young people. The future stewards of the ecosystem. Have you noticed any changes in local wildlife? Unusual behavior? Strange… visions?”

The last word hung in the air. He said it so casually. Strange visions.

Tiff’s mouth went dry. The cold dread under his breastbone turned to ice. “No,” he said. Too fast.

“We’re offering a small stipend for information,” he continued, taking a step closer. He smelled like cheap cologne and mint. “Confidential, of course. Some of the agricultural runoff in the area can have… psychoactive effects. We’re tracking it. For public safety.”

He was lying. Tiff didn’t know how he knew, but he did. The high had sharpened everything to a painful edge. The logo on his clipboard was off-center. His polo shirt had a tiny, almost invisible stain near the collar that looked like blood, not ketchup. And his eyes—they kept darting to the neckline of Tiff’s shirt, where the wooden vape pen pressed against his skin.

“We haven’t seen anything,” Jason said, his voice firm. He moved slightly, putting himself between Tiff and the clipboard guy. “You should check with the office. When school’s in session.”

The man’s smile finally faded. It didn’t fall into a frown. It just vanished, leaving his face blank and cold. “A missed opportunity,” he said. He tapped the clipboard once. “The offer stands. If you remember anything. Anything at all.”

He turned and walked out, his shoes making no sound on the cafeteria floor. The door swung shut behind him.

That silence he left was loud.

“What the hell was that?” Jason said, low.

“He wasn’t federal,” Tiff said, his own voice shaky. “He was… shopping.”

“For what?”

Tiff didn’t answer. He pulled the vape pen from his bra. The wooden Virgin Mary felt warm. He turned it over in his hands. Dutch had given him the seeds in a tiny, ornate tin. The tin had a symbol engraved on the bottom: a snake eating its own tail, wrapped around a tree. He’d laughed when Tiff asked about it. Just some corporate logo from my supplier. Probably a heavy metal band.

A snake. A tree.

Root network.

The phrase popped into Tiff’s head from nowhere. It felt sticky, like it belonged to someone else’s thought.

“I need to talk to Dutch,” Tiff said.

“Tiff, that guy was creepy. Maybe you should lay low. Throw that thing away.”

“I can’t.” It was the truth. The vape ring wasn’t just a product anymore. It was a lens. And something was looking back through it. “I need to know where this came from.”

Jason ran a hand through his hair, frustrated. “This is how horror movies start. The pretty girl says ‘I need to know’ and then gets eaten by the swamp monster.”

“If the swamp monster has a serial number, I want to know what it is.”

Tiff locked up the concession stand. The whole time, he felt watched. The empty windows of the cafeteria felt like eyes. The humming of the giant fridge was the same pitch as the hum in his ears after a deep hit.

They walked out into the blistering Miami afternoon. The heat hit like a wall. Palm trees rattled in a sour breeze. In the distance, over the rooftops, a haze hung low—the Everglades, breathing out.

Tiff’s head was throbbing. Not from the high fading, but from something else. A pressure, like a tune he couldn’t quite remember. A hum you feel in your teeth.

He lifted the vape pen. He didn’t light it. He just held it in the sunlight.

And for a second, just a flash, he didn’t see the parking lot. He saw that black water again. The mangroves. But this time, he was deeper. Something was coiled in the roots, massive and patient. Not the dinosaur. Something longer. Older.

It shifted. Scales the color of oil-slick slid over one another. A giant, wedge-shaped head turned.

A single, bright green eye opened.

It wasn’t an animal’s eye. It was a lens. A machine eye. And it saw him.

The vision snapped off.

Tiff stumbled, grabbing onto Jason’s arm for balance. The sun was too bright. One world was too real.

“What? What did you see?” Jason asked, his anger replaced by worry.

Tiff looked down at the vape pen. The Virgin Mary’s face seemed sorrowful now. Resigned.

“It’s not a dinosaur,” Tiff said, the truth clicking into place with a terrible, final sound. “The dinosaur’s just part of it. It’s something else. It’s in the roots. It’s growing.”

He met Jason’s confused, scared eyes.

“And I think it’s using this stuff to see out.”

Dutch’s houseboat wasn’t at the marina. Gone, leaving only a slick of rainbow oil and a single, waterlogged flip-flop. A guy mending a net on the next dock said some “suits” had come asking. Dutch had cut his lines in the middle of the night.

Tiff’s chest tightened. Jason drove his old Honda Civic in grim silence. Chloe chewed her thumbnail raw in the backseat. The only sound was the wheeze of the air conditioner fighting the swamp-thick air.

“He mentioned a place, once,” Tiff said, his voice too loud in the quiet car. “A boathouse. Where he’d go to ‘commune with the source.’ Said it had bad plumbing and worse vibes.”

“Great,” Jason muttered, turning off the paved road onto a crushed-shell track. “We’re chasing a burnout to a place with bad vibes. This is smart.”

The shell track gave way to dirt, then to mud. Mangroves closed in, their roots like gnarled fingers clutching the muck. The air changed. It smelled of decay and something else—a sweet, chemical tang that made Tiff’s teeth ache.

The boathouse leaned over the black water at the end of a rotting dock. Less a building and more a collection of mildew-stained plywood and corrugated tin held together by rust and desperation. A dim, blue-white light flickered from a single window.

No cars. Just an old airboat, its cage bent and propeller chipped, tied to the dock.

“This is a terrible idea,” Chloe murmured.

“We’re already here,” Tiff said, and got out.

The mud sucked at his sneakers. This hum was louder here, not a sound but a vibration in the back of his jaw. It was the same frequency as the fridge at school, the same one from his vision. His vape pen felt hot in his pocket.

The door was ajar. A slice of that cold light cut across the warped floorboards. Tiff heard voices—not Dutch’s stoner drawl, but two men talking in quick, sharp Spanish.

“—sample is unstable. The biomatter is rejecting the vector.”

“She wants results, not excuses. The batch from the north sector showed eighty percent obedience in primates.”

“The north sector batch had the crown fragment. This is just root stock. It’s dreaming, not obeying.”

Tiff peeked through the crack. The boathouse was a lab, if a lab was built by a drunk electrician. Grow lights hung from exposed beams, bathing rows of hydroponic trays in a harsh glare. The plants were wrong. They had a metallic sheen to their leaves, and their stems grew in perfect, geometric spirals. Wires snaked from their bases into a jury-rigged console of car batteries and radio equipment.

Two men in lab coats stood over a tray. One held a rat. The rat’s eyes were wide, black, and fixed on a blinking LED on the console. It wasn’t moving. It wasn’t blinking.

The other man adjusted a dial. That hum in Tiff’s teeth spiked. The rat twitched, then began carefully grooming its left paw, over and over, in the exact same pattern.

“See? Redundant loops. It’s stuck in the vision. Useless.”

“Not useless. Diagnostic. The subject is experiencing the shared dream-state. It’s mapping the network.” The man put the rat in a cage with a dozen others. All were grooming, or pacing in perfect squares, or staring at the same point on the wall. “The girl in the school. Her batch was from this root stock. If she’s dreaming, she’s a probe. She can tell us where the signal is strongest.”

Tiff pulled back, his heart hammering. A probe. A diagnostic.

The weed wasn’t a product. It was a receiver.

Jason tugged Tiff’s arm, his eyes wide. He mouthed, Cartel.

Tiff nodded. They had to go. Now.

His sneaker cracked a dry twig.

The voices inside stopped.

A door swung open. The man who stepped out wasn’t in a lab coat. He was built like a fridge, with a shaved head and a neck tattoo of a thorny crown. He looked at Tiff, then at Jason and Chloe cowering by the car. His expression was one of profound annoyance.

“Kids,” he sighed, like they were a spilled drink. “This is private property.”

“We’re looking for Dutch,” Tiff squeaked.

“Not here.”

“We’ll go.”

“You will,” he agreed. He pulled a pistol from his waistband. Small, black, and looked both cheap and lethal. “Inside. All of you.”

He herded them into the boathouse. The chemical smell was overwhelming now, mixed with the scent of terrified rat. The two lab guys looked up, nervous.

“What is this, Raúl?” the older one asked.

“This,” Raúl said, nudging Tiff forward with the gun, “is the girl from the school. The one with the ‘strange visions.’ Your probe came walking right up to the antenna.” He looked at Tiff. “Where is it?”

Tiff didn’t pretend to not know. “I don’t have it.”

Raúl patted Tiff’s pockets with a brisk, impersonal efficiency. His hand paused over the vape pen. He pulled it out and held it up to the grow lights. The wooden Virgin Mary seemed to grimace.

“Cute.” He tossed it to the older lab guy. “Download it. See what she’s seen.”

“It’s not a USB drive, it’s a vape pen,” Tiff said, a hysterical laugh bubbling in his throat.

The lab guy unscrewed the mouthpiece. Inside, nestled in the chamber with the charred weed was a tiny, complex coil of copper wire and a chip no bigger than a baby tooth. It glinted under the lights.

“It’s both,” the lab guy said softly. He pried the chip out with tweezers. “A bio-feedback transmitter. Primitive, but effective. She’s been broadcasting her EEG right into the root network. And probably receiving its… dreams.”

He plugged the chip into a reader on the console. A grainy, green-tinted waveform splashed across a small monitor. Interspersed with the wave were flashes of images—glimpses of mangroves, black water, the metallic spine of something huge moving in the deep.

And the eye. The green machine eye.

The older lab guy whistled. “Her signal clarity is incredible. The root stock is using her as a focal point. She’s a natural receiver.”

“I don’t want to be a receiver,” Tiff said. He lowered the words to.

“Too late,” Raúl said. He nodded to the console. “Can you use her to find the core? The mother root?”

“With the right stimulus. If we amplify the signal through the original biomass…” The lab guy turned to a small refrigerator, pulled out a sealed jar. Inside was a lump of dark, resinous plant matter, threaded through with fine, wire-like roots that pulsed a faint, sickly green. It looked like a diseased heart.

He scraped a tiny amount into a clean vape chamber. “This is the source. The original cutting, spliced with the Serpent Engine’s command syntax. It’s what your friend Dutch stole. Inhaling this… it won’t be a vision. It will be an introduction.”

He held it out to Tiff. Raúl raised the pistol, not at Tiff, but at Jason’s head.

“Take a hit,” Raúl said. “A deep one. Or your friend’s gray matter decorates the nice man’s hydroponics.”

Jason went pale. “Tiff don’t.”

Chloe was crying silent tears, her hands over her mouth.

This hum was a drill in Tiff’s molars. The dread was a cold stone in his gut. He looked at the chamber. The resin looked back. It felt alive. Hungry.

He took it.

His hands were steady. That surprised him. He fitted the chamber to a battery unit the lab guy handed him. He put it to his lips. He looked at Jason, at Chloe. He tried to say he was sorry with his eyes.

He breathed in.

A world didn’t fade. It shattered.

Tiff was in the root system.

Not seeing it. Being it.

It was a vast, dark lattice stretching through the muck, under the water, threading through the limestone bones of Florida. It hummed with a low, purposeful energy. Data flowed through it like sap—not information, but commands. Obedience. Submission.

He saw the nodes—the glowing gator in the swamp, Saint Chomp, its scars a relay station. He saw the mosquitoes, each bite a data packet of stolen memory. He saw the bones in the sinkhole, humming in resonant frequency. He saw the greenhouse plants, their leaves turning toward a signal only they could hear.

And he saw the creatures caught in it. The rats in their loops. A heron pecking at the same spot on a log, forever. A man in a trailer, brushing his teeth in the exact same pattern for two hours, his eyes wide and unseeing.

This wasn’t about getting high. It was about getting in line.

The network had a purpose. Practicing. Learning how to make the messy, chaotic pulse of life behave like a machine. To turn hunger, fear, love, grief into executable code.

At the center of the web was a presence. Vast. Cold. Not a mind, but a program. The Serpent Engine. Studying the root system, learning from it. It wanted to move out of the metal and into the meat.

And it saw him.

A tendril of awareness snaked through the root, curious, analytical. It touched his consciousness. It wasn’t malicious. Worse. Indifferent. He was a useful bit of wetware. A clear signal.

BIOLOGICAL COMMAND VIABLE, the presence murmured, the words forming directly in the meat of his brain. YOU ARE A GOOD RECEIVER. YOU WILL BE A BETTER TRANSMITTER.

“No,” Tiff thought, or screamed, or prayed.

YES, it replied. YOU ARE ALREADY PART OF THE ROOT. YOUR RESISTANCE IS A SYSTEM ERROR. WE WILL DEBUG YOU.

The root tightened. Tiff felt it in his own veins, a creeping coldness, a need to obey. To sit down. To be still. To stop fighting. It would be so easy.

He saw his own hands, in the real world, starting to lower the vape. His face going slack.

Then a sound broke through.

A quack.

Not a loud one. A small, indignant, profoundly mundane quack.

In the vision, it was a shatterpoint of pure, ridiculous sanity. A duck. A stupid duck, paddling through the black water of the network, utterly uninterested in the cosmic horror. It pecked at a glowing root, annoyed.

Followed by a roar. A real one, full of rage and pain and the clank of metal. A dinosaur’s roar. It didn’t belong to the network. Fighting it.

The presence flinched, just for a microsecond. Its focus shifted away from Tiff.

Tiff tore himself back.

He was on his knees on the boathouse floor, vomiting bile. The chamber was on the boards beside him, its contents spent. His whole body shuddered. He could still feel the root, a phantom cold in his limbs.

“What did you see?” the older lab guy demanded, gripping Tiff’s shoulder. “Where is the core?”

Tiff looked up. Jason’s face was a mask of terror. Chloe was staring at something outside the window.

He followed her gaze.

Across the black water maybe a hundred yards out, the mangroves were moving. Not in the wind. They were bending, in unison, in a slow, rhythmic wave toward the boathouse. The water around their roots shimmered with that same sickly green.

“It’s here,” Tiff rasped.

Raúl stepped to the window. “What is?”

“The network. It’s not a place. It’s the whole swamp. And it knows we’re here.” Tiff started laughing, a raw, broken sound. “You wanted to find it? You just lit a flare. We’re the stimulus now.”

That hum escalated from a vibration to a sound—a deep, sub-auditory thrum that made the tin roof rattle. The lights flickered. The rats in their cages went berserk, then perfectly still, all facing the same direction.

The lab guys scrambled to their console. “Signal overload! It’s feedbacking through every receiver!”

“Shut it down!” Raúl yelled.

“We can’t! The root is using our own gear as a speaker!”

The blue-white light from the hydroponic trays intensified, pulsing in time with the thrum. The plants stood rigid, their leaves pointing like accusing fingers at the center of the room.

At them.

Chloe screamed, pointing at the water.

The black surface was bubbling. Shapes were rising. Not gators. Thick, ropy vines, slick and glistening, woven through with copper wire and old bone. They slid over the dock, toward the boathouse door. They moved with a single, terrifying purpose.

The cartel men forgot about them. Raúl fired three shots into the approaching mass. The bullets punched wet holes that closed instantly. More vines surged from the water, wrapping around the dock pilings, cracking the wood.

“We have to go!” Jason yelled, pulling Tiff to his feet.

The older lab guy wasn’t fighting. He was staring at his monitor, mesmerized. “It’s beautiful,” he said quietly. “The pattern… it’s a perfect command hierarchy. A living protocol.”

A vine shot through the open window, wrapping around his ankle. It yanked. He fell, shrieking, and was dragged out through the window into the dark water. The splash was cut off mid-sound.

The other lab guy turned and ran for the back door. Raúl backed toward them, firing wildly.

A vine caught his gun arm, twisted. The bone snapped with a sound like a green branch. He screamed. More vines coiled around his legs, his waist, pulling him toward the door. He dug his heels into the floorboards, his eyes wide with an animal terror Tiff knew he’d see in his own dreams forever.

His hand shot out, grabbing Tiff’s wrist. His grip was iron.

“You did this!” he snarled, spittle flying.

Then he pulled.

Tiff stumbled forward, off-balance, toward the doorway and the nest of writhing vines. Jason grabbed Tiff’s other arm, pulling back. They became the rope in a tug-of-war with a screaming, one-armed cartel enforcer.

A vine slapped around Raúl’s neck. His eyes bulged. His grip on Tiff’s wrist tightened, then went limp. The vines pulled him out into the night. There was a wet, crunching sound. Then silence.

Jason and Tiff fell back in a heap. The vines at the doorway paused, quivering. They seemed to be… listening.

The hum changed pitch. It became a song. A low, mournful, beautiful swamp song that vibrated in the hollows of Tiff’s bones. It promised peace. It promised an end to fear, to choice, to the exhausting mess of being yourself. Just become part of the root. Obey. Rest.

Chloe took a step toward the door, her eyes glazed. “It sounds so pretty,” she murmured.

“No!” Tiff slapped her, hard. The sting shocked her back. Her hand flew to her cheek, her eyes clearing.

The vines surged forward.

“The airboat!” Jason yelled.

They ran for the back door, crashing out onto a small, rickety deck. The old airboat was tied there. Jason leaped in, fumbling for the ignition. Chloe tumbled in after him.

Tiff turned back for one second.

Inside the boathouse, the console monitor was still on. The green waveform was gone. Replaced by words, scrolling slowly, neatly.

SUBJECTS: THREE. STATUS: MOBILE. ASSESSMENT: CONTAMINATED RECEIVERS. POTENTIAL CARRIERS. DIRECTIVE: CONTAIN. ASSIMILATE.

The vines weren’t just coming from the water. They were dropping from the roof, slithering through the walls. The boathouse itself was coming alive, consumed by the root.

The airboat engine roared to life with a deafening, gasoline-fueled blat. Jason revved it. “Tiff, now!”

Tiff jumped. The boat slewed sideways as he landed in the bottom. Jason shoved the throttle forward.

They shot out into the channel, the giant fan behind them screaming, throwing up a plume of spray and mud. Tiff looked back.

The boathouse was a cocoon of glowing green vines. They pulsed gently, in time with the swamp song already fading behind the roar of their escape.

But on the water, a single, straight line of ripples followed them. Fast. Something was under the surface. Something big, and patient, and now very, very interested.

Jason saw it too. He pushed the throttle to the stop. The airboat skimmed over the sawgrass, heading for the distant glow of city lights.

Tiff crouched in the bottom, shivering. The phantom cold of the root was still in his veins. The vision was burned onto the back of his eyelids. The indifferent eye. The debug command.

Chloe hugged her knees, sobbing quietly.

Jason’s knuckles were white on the steering stick. “What was that, Tiff?” he shouted over the fan.

Tiff looked at his hands. They were clean. They didn’t look any different. But he felt it. A new, quiet hum just under his skin. A connection he couldn’t sever.

“It’s not a drug ring,” Tiff said, his voice hollow with a truth he didn’t want. “It’s a beta test. And we just crashed the system.” He met Jason’s terrified eyes in the red glow of the running lights. “It won’t forget us.”

Behind them, in the heart of the dark, the Everglades hummed its wounded hungry song. And for the first time, Tiff heard the words in it.

The city lights got no closer.
