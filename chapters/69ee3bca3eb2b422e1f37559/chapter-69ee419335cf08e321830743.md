The pain in Miriam's shoulder burned like a brand, but the words burned deeper. Peter's voice—her brother's voice—echoed in her skull even as the stones bit into her cheek. Around her, the crowd surged like a storm-tossed sea, sandals slapping against pavement, voices rising in a dozen tongues. A sandaled foot caught her ribs. She curled tighter, fingers digging into the cracks between stones.

Elior's grip on her wrist had vanished in the chaos. She lifted her head just enough to see the Cyrenian merchant shoved against a pillar, his face slack with something beyond fear. Beyond awe. The look of a man who'd seen the veil between worlds torn open.

Peter was on his knees now, blood streaking his temple, but his voice cut through the din—clear, unshaken. Not his voice. Not quite. The cadence was wrong, the vowels too precise. It was the voice of her childhood tutor reciting Homer, the voice of the spice merchant who'd taught her Parthian curses behind his stall.

A hand grabbed her uninjured arm, yanking her upright. Tavi's face swam into focus, his lips moving, but the words dissolved before they reached her. The Temple interpreter's mouth shaped warnings she couldn't hear over the roar of the crowd, the metallic clang of Roman armor, the high, keening wail of a woman singing in perfect Latin three paces away. A Galilean girl, barefoot and sunburnt, who'd never set foot beyond the Jordan.

Miriam's fingers found the rhythm against her thigh—tap, tap, tap—a frantic counterpoint to the chaos. Her mind scrambled for purchase. This wasn't translation. This was possession. The rules were shattered.

A stone flew past her ear, striking a legionary's shield with a hollow thud. The optio barked an order. The soldiers tightened formation, shields locking, but the crowd pressed closer, their confusion curdling into something hotter. A dark-skinned pilgrim from Kush fell to his knees, hands raised toward Peter, babbling in the dialect of Meroë. A Cappadocian wool merchant clutched his chest as if struck, whispering words Miriam had only heard in the back alleys of Alexandria's harbor.

Tavi dragged her backward, his free hand slicing through the air in the sharp, chopping gestures of Temple sign language. Useless here. The crowd wasn't watching for signals. They were listening.

Peter staggered to his feet. Blood dripped into his beard, but his smile was fierce, unbroken. He spread his arms—a gesture Miriam had seen a hundred times in the agora, the universal stance of a man with nothing to hide. His next words weren't Aramaic. Not Greek. Not any language she could name, yet the meaning unfolded in her mind like a scroll: "You already know the answer."

The crowd stilled. Even the Romans hesitated.

Then the Levite who'd accused Peter of drunkenness earlier let out a choked cry. "That's—that's my father's blessing. From Sepphoris. He said those words to me on my wedding day. No one else was there! How do you know them?"

John stepped forward, his voice weaving through the sudden silence like a needle through linen. "Not us." He touched his own chest, then pointed upward. The gesture was simple. Devastating.

Miriam's breath hitched. Her fingers stilled mid-tap. The Levite's shock mirrored her own—this wasn't just language. Memory. Intimacy. The kind of words that passed between lovers in the dark, between parents and children in moments too tender for witnesses.

Tavi's grip tightened. "We need to—"

A horn blared from the Temple steps. The crowd recoiled as one. The shofar's cry meant blasphemy. Meant lockdown.

Miriam twisted free. The movement sent white-hot pain lancing through her shoulder, but she barely registered it. Peter was looking at her again. Not at the crowd, not at the Romans. At her. His lips moved.

The words weren't Parthian this time. They were the nursery rhyme her mother had hummed while kneading dough, the one Miriam had forgotten until this moment. The one her brother had whistled the morning he died.

Her knees buckled.

Tavi caught her before she hit the ground, his arms locking around her waist. "Move!" He hauled her toward the colonnade, but the crowd had become a living thing—a single body with a hundred grasping hands. A woman in Phoenician blue clutched at Miriam's sleeve, babbling in a dialect so ancient even the scholars in Alexandria argued over its roots. A Roman decurion stood frozen, his helmet askew, mouthing words that belonged to his dead centurion father.

The shofar sounded again. Closer this time.

Miriam's vision tunneled. The pain in her shoulder, the heat, the press of bodies, it all narrowed to Peter's face, to the impossible certainty in his eyes. He knew. He knew the rhyme. Knew the riddle. Knew the last words her brother had whispered.

Tavi swore, dragging her behind a pillar as the first wave of Temple guards shoved into the square. "We can't stay here." His breath was ragged against her ear. "They'll lock the gates."

A child's laugh cut through the noise. Clear. Bright. Unafraid.

Miriam turned.

A girl no older than six stood at the edge of the crowd, her bare feet dusty, her tunic patched at the elbows. She wasn't looking at Peter. Wasn't looking at the guards. She was staring at the sky, her small hands outstretched as if to catch something only she could see.

"Look," the girl said in flawless Egyptian, the dialect of the Delta, the one Miriam's mother had spoken. "It's raining fire."

And then the screaming began.

The child's words struck Miriam like a blow to the chest. She jerked her gaze upward, expecting smoke, expecting chaos, but the sky was a pitiless blue, the sun hammering down on the Temple's gold-plated roof. No fire. No rain. Yet the girl kept reaching, her fingers splayed as if tracing invisible threads.

The screaming wasn't from panic. Recognition.

A woman near the colonnade clutched her head, rocking back and forth, her lips moving in perfect sync with Peter's next sentence, though Peter spoke in the guttural consonants of Germania, and the woman's reply was purest Cyrene. A Roman soldier dropped his pilum; the iron clang should have been lost in the din, but Miriam heard it like a bell. His mouth shaped words too soft for the distance between them, yet they reached her anyway: "Forgive me."

Tavi's fingers dug into her arm. "Miriam. Breathe."

She wasn't. Her lungs refused to expand. The air itself had thickened, pressing against her skin like the humid stillness before a storm. Peter's voice coiled around her, slipping past her ears, past her thoughts, straight into the marrow of her bones. The words weren't just heard. They were known.

A man in priestly white shoved past, his face contorted. "This is sorcery!" His shout should have carried authority, but the syllables fractured midair, dissolving into a dozen echoes, each in a different tongue, each landing with the weight of a verdict.

Miriam's fingers found their rhythm again, tapping against her thigh—tap-tap-tap—but the pattern stuttered, outmatched. Her mind scrambled for the familiar refuge of translation, the buffer of parsing meaning through structure and syntax. But there was no parsing this. No distance. The Greek that poured from Peter's mouth now wasn't the polished Attic of scholars or the marketplace pidgin of the docks. It was the thick, honeyed dialect of her childhood, the one she hadn't spoken aloud in fifteen years. The one her mother used when telling stories by lamplight.

"Little sparrow," Peter said, except he didn't. The words were Greek, but the voice was her brother's. The exact timbre, the slight rasp at the edges from the lung fever he'd never quite shaken. The cadence of his last words to her before the mob dragged him into the alley.

Her knees gave out. Tavi caught her, but his grip felt distant, insubstantial. The stones beneath her were no longer sun-warmed limestone but the rough cobbles of Alexandria's Jewish quarter, slick with blood she'd spent five years trying to forget.

Peter stepped forward, his shadow falling across her. The crowd rippled, a collective intake of breath. He wasn't looking at her now, but the words kept coming, her brother's voice woven into his, speaking the secret names they'd invented for the constellations, the nonsense rhyme they'd chanted while skipping stones across the harbor. Things no one else could know. Things she'd buried.

A hand seized her wrist. Elior. His lips moved, but the sound arrived seconds late, out of sync, as if the world had broken along some unseen seam. "Not safe here."

The shofar blared again, closer now. Temple enforcers shoved through the crowd, their batons rising and falling in brutal arcs, but the blows landed without sound, without consequence. The pilgrims didn't scatter. They turned, faces upturned, tears cutting through the dust on their cheeks. A Parthian trader clutched a Roman soldier's forearm, babbling in flawless Latin about a daughter lost to fever. The soldier didn't pull away.

Miriam pressed her palms to her ears. It didn't help. The words weren't outside her anymore. They were in her pulse, in the spaces between her ribs. Peter raised his hands, and the crowd stilled, not in obedience, but in the awful, rapt silence of a truth too large to deny.

"This is what was spoken," he said, in Aramaic now, but the meaning unfolded inside her skull in Greek, in Parthian, in the fragmented Coptic of the Nile Delta. "You will hear in your own tongues."

Not translation. Not possession. Something worse. Something that bypassed language entirely and planted meaning directly into the soil of her mind, where it took root with terrifying speed.

The Levite who'd accused Peter earlier staggered forward. "You, you're quoting Joel. But the text says 'dreams,' not 'tongues.'" His voice cracked. "I've studied the scrolls my whole life. I know the verse."

John answered without moving his lips. The words simply were, blooming in Miriam's mind like ink in water: "The scrolls are fixed. The Spirit is not."

A sound tore from the Levite's throat, half sob, half laugh. He sank to his knees.

Miriam's vision blurred. The crowd swayed around her, a single organism breathing in unison. She wanted to run. Wanted to bury her face in Tavi's shoulder and scream until her throat bled. But her body refused to obey. The Greek kept coming, each syllable a key turning in a lock she'd welded shut years ago.

Peter turned. His gaze pinned her where she stood.

"You hid your voice so well, even you forgot where you left it."

Not an accusation. A lament.

The stones beneath her feet trembled, or maybe she did. The crowd's murmurs built to a roar, but one voice cut through, high and clear: the little girl again, laughing as she spun in circles, her arms outstretched to a sky only she could see.

"Not fire," the girl corrected, giggling. "Feathers."

Miriam looked down.

A single white feather lay at her feet, pristine against the dust.

Then the world shattered.

Not the ground. Not the sky. The careful, curated distance between thought and word, between memory and meaning. The walls she'd built around her grief, around her fear, around the part of herself that still believed in things unseen.

Peter's next words weren't in Greek. Weren't in any language at all.

They were her brother's last pause given sound.

And for the first time in five years, Miriam answered him.

The feather dissolved into dust as Miriam's heel ground against it. The air smelled of sweat and crushed mint and the metallic tang of fear.

She forced her fingers still. Merchant's instincts reasserted themselves, slicing through the fog of memory. The Levite on his knees. The Roman clutching his pilum. The Cappadocian weeping into his hands. Every face told a different story, but the math was the same: too many witnesses, too much raw emotion. This wouldn't stay contained. Couldn't.

Peter raised his hands again. The movement should have been theatrical, but the stillness that followed felt inevitable, as if the world had been waiting for this exact angle of his wrists. His sleeves fell back, revealing scars that mirrored the ones on Miriam's brother, the pale, ropey texture of lash marks.

"Men of Judea," he said. He lowered the words without strain, though he wasn't shouting. "And all who dwell in Jerusalem."

The words landed differently this time. Not in her bones, not in her blood, but cleanly in her ears. Aramaic. Normal Aramaic, with the Galilean flattening of vowels she'd come to expect.

Miriam exhaled. The relief lasted half a heartbeat.

Because the crowd didn't hear Aramaic.

The Phoenician woman gasped, hands flying to her mouth. The Kushite pilgrim let out a low moan. The Roman decurion stiffened, his fingers twitching toward his sword hilt before falling away. They weren't reacting to the meaning. They were reacting to the sound, each hearing the same words in their own mother tongue, their own childhood dialect.

Tavi's breath hitched beside her. "It's still happening."

She knew that tone. The Temple interpreter had heard something he couldn't explain, something that bypassed his professional detachment. His thumb tapped against his thigh in a staccato rhythm, not the even meter of her own nervous habit, but the erratic pulse of a man recalculating.

Peter stepped forward. Dust puffed around his sandals. "Let this be known to you, and give ear to my words."

The Levite lurched upright. "You're drunk."

"These men are not drunk, as you suppose." John cut in, his voice mild but his stance widening, shoulders squaring. A fisherman's posture, braced for a wave. "This is what was spoken through the prophet Joel."

Miriam's pulse jumped. Quoting scripture was dangerous. Quoting Joel more so; the text spoke of dreams and visions, of blood and fire. Of judgment.

Peter didn't pause. "'And in the last days it shall be, God declares, that I will pour out my Spirit on all flesh.'"

A sharp cry came from the crowd's edge. Miriam turned in time to see a woman in Essene white collapse, her fingers clawing at her chest as if trying to dig the words out. A man beside her—a Greek from the look of his chiton—caught her shoulders, his own face slack with shock. She shouldn't have understood. Essenes spoke a dialect closer to Hebrew than Aramaic; the Greek's Ionic accent marked him as an Ephesian. Yet their reactions mirrored each other perfectly.

Miriam shook off the thought. Not yet. The merchant in her needed to see the pattern, needed to trace the contours of this thing before it exploded.

Peter's voice rose. "'Your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, and your young men shall see visions, and your old men shall dream dreams.'"

The little girl who'd spoken of fire and feathers giggled, spinning in a tight circle. Her patched tunic flared around her knees. No adult touched her, no family called her back. She danced alone in the eye of the storm, her laughter threading through the growing tension like gold through sackcloth.

Miriam's throat tightened. The quote was wrong. Not wrong, shifted. Joel's original spoke of "your young men seeing visions" first, then daughters prophesying. Peter had reversed them. A small change. A deliberate one.

The Levite noticed too. His face darkened. "You twist the text!"

Peter met his gaze. "Does the potter not shape the clay as he wills?"

A hush fell. Even the Romans stilled. The metaphor was too pointed, too loaded—Jeremiah's words turned back on the Temple elite. Miriam's fingers itched for her ledger, for the neat columns of profit and loss where the world made sense. This wasn't commerce. This was wildfire.

Elior appeared at her shoulder, his olive skin gone ashen. "The priest's guard is mobilizing near the Hulda Gates." His Greek was clipped, precise. The voice of a man reporting market fluctuations, not impending violence. "They'll declare this a disturbance. You know what comes next."

She did. The Temple authorities tolerated many things, but not challenges to their interpretive monopoly. Not in the shadow of Pilate's fortress.

Peter seemed oblivious to the danger. Or indifferent. "'And I will show wonders in the heavens above and signs on the earth below, blood, and fire, and vapor of smoke.'"

The words landed like struck bronze. The sun climbed higher, bleaching the courtyard stones, but a sudden chill raced down Miriam's spine. Peter wasn't just quoting now. He was weaving—Joel's prophecy threaded through with phrases from Isaiah, from the Psalms, from texts no Galilean fisherman should know so intimately. The effect was dissonant and seamless, like hearing a symphony played on a single string.

The crowd swayed forward as one. Not attacking. Not fleeing. Drawn.

Miriam saw the moment come faster. She'd seen this before, not in synagogues or temples, but in Alexandria's markets when a particularly gifted storyteller held court. The moment when the crowd's attention shifted from individual listeners to a single, pulsing organism. The point of no return.

The Levite lunged, his robes flapping. "Blasphemer! You'd invoke the Day of the Lord here? Now?"

Peter didn't flinch. "The sun shall be turned to darkness, and the moon to blood, before the day of the Lord comes, the great and magnificent day."

A Roman trumpet sounded from the Antonia. The crowd rippled, but no one ran. The little girl stopped spinning. For the first time, she looked at Peter, her head cocked like a bird's.

Peter lowered his voice, but the words carried anyway. "And it shall come to pass that everyone who calls upon the name of the Lord shall be saved."

Silence.

Not the absence of sound, but the kind of quiet that comes before a storm breaks. Miriam's skin prickled. The quote was Joel again, but the weight behind it—the terrible, tender certainty—made it something else entirely. A promise. A threat.

The Levite's face twisted. He turned to the nearest Temple guard. "Arrest them."

The guard hesitated.

Peter smiled. Not in triumph. Not in defiance. The expression was softer than that, sadder. The smile of a man who'd already seen how this ended.

Miriam knew that look. She'd seen it on her brother's face seconds before the stones flew.

Her stomach lurched. The merchant in her screamed to leave, to melt into the crowd before the violence began. But the girl who remembered lullabies stood rooted, transfixed.

The little girl darted forward, her dusty feet flashing. She grabbed Peter's hand, her small fingers barely circling his wrist. "Will there be more feathers?"

Peter knelt. The movement brought him eye level with the child, his knees grinding into the dirt. "More than you can count."

The Levite made a strangled noise. "You see? He admits it! Sorcery!"

John stepped between them, his voice calm. "Fellow Israelites, hear these words."

Miriam didn't wait for the rest. She knew a diversion when she heard one. Knew a market tipping point when she saw it.

Tavi was already moving, his body angled toward the colonnade's shadow. Elior followed, his merchant's instincts mirroring hers.

She took one step. Then another. The crowd parted around her, too focused on the confrontation to notice three more bodies in motion.

Peter's voice followed her, clear as a knife drawn from its sheath:

"You crucified him."

Miriam froze.

Not an accusation. A statement. The kind of fact you built a ledger around.

Behind her, the world held its silence.

Then the screaming started again.
