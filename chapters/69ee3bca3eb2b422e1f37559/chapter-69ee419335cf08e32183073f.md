The alley behind the Temple courts smelled of crushed mint and donkey dung. Miriam counted the amphorae a second time—twelve, all intact—while the oil merchant’s apprentice shifted from foot to foot. The boy’s sandals were too large, the straps cutting into his ankles. She noted the detail without meaning to, then forced her focus back to the transaction.

"From a mercantile perspective," she said, tapping her thumb against her index finger in a slow, steady rhythm, "Damascus olive oil trades at sixteen drachmae per amphora this season. Given festival tariffs, one might expect a ten percent premium."

The merchant, a squat man with a scar bisecting his left eyebrow, wiped his hands on his apron. "Twelve."

"Fourteen."

"Thirteen, and I throw in the rope."

Miriam glanced at the frayed coil at his feet. The fibers were sun-bleached but serviceable. She could sell it in Alexandria for half a drachma. "Done."

The coins changed hands. The merchant’s fingers were slick with oil, leaving smudges on the silver. Miriam wiped each piece against her sleeve before dropping them into her purse. A petty habit, but one her brother had taught her—always clean the money before it touches your skin.

The alley narrowed as she moved toward the main thoroughfare. A pair of Levites in white linen pushed past, their voices low and urgent. "…not just the fishermen. The weavers’ quarter too."

Miriam angled her body to let them pass, her gaze fixed on the uneven cobbles. The rhythm of her thumb against her finger quickened.

A hand caught her elbow.

She jerked back, her free hand already at the knife hidden in her sash.

"Peace." The man released her immediately. He wore the blue-trimmed robe of a Temple interpreter, his beard neatly trimmed, his palms raised. "You dropped this." He held out a single drachma, the edge dented from a long-ago strike.

Miriam exhaled through her nose. One hand stayed where it was. "My thanks."

"Tavi bat Nadav." He inclined his head. "You’re the Alexandrian merchant. The one with the Damascus contacts."

She could have lied. Should have, by every rule that kept her alive these past five years. But his Aramaic had the crisp cadence of a scholar, and his eyes—dark, sharp—missed nothing. "Miriam."

"Your Greek is better than your Aramaic."

"And your Latin is better than your Hebrew."

A flicker of surprise. Then a grin. "You heard me with the centurion yesterday."

She had. A quick exchange by the Antonia Fortress, Tavi translating some priestly complaint into fluid, unaccented Latin. That soldier hadn’t even blinked.

A donkey brayed somewhere behind them, the sound swallowed by the rising murmur of the crowd. Tavi stepped closer to let a wine-seller pass, his shoulder nearly brushing hers. "You should avoid the Essene Quarter today. The prefect’s men are checking lodgings."

Miriam’s thumb stilled. "I’m contracted at the Caravanserai."

"Good." He hesitated, then lowered his voice further. "They’re looking for Galileans. Anyone connected to the prophet."

The words hung between them. Miriam adjusted the strap of her satchel, the leather digging into her collarbone. "I trade in oil and spices. Not prophets."

"Of course." Tavi’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. "But you have ears. And the wrong question overheard at the wrong time…" He let the thought trail off, his gaze sliding past her to the cluster of priests now emerging from a side gate. Their robes were finer, their voices louder. One gestured sharply toward the Court of the Gentiles.

Tavi took a half-step back. "Be careful with your coins, Miriam of Alexandria. Even the clean ones leave traces."

Then he was gone, melting into the crowd with the ease of a man who knew every shadow in the city.

Miriam counted her breaths. Five in, seven out. Her knife was still there, cool against her ribs. She turned toward the Caravanserai, her pace measured, her face calm.

Behind her, the priests’ voices rose in heated debate. One word, clear above the rest, sent a ripple through the crowd:

"Galilean."

The Court of the Gentiles hummed with the uneasy energy of a beehive prodded too often. Miriam chose a spot beneath the shaded portico where the marble columns cast striped shadows across her face. A good place to watch without being seen. The stone bench was warm beneath her thighs, the heat leaching through her linen robe. She counted the exits—three archways, two staircases—and noted the Roman soldier lounging by the westernmost column. His helmet was off, his fingers drumming against the hilt of his gladius. Not relaxed. Waiting.

A group of priests swept past in a rustle of fine wool and tinkling bells. Their voices carried, sharp with the clipped Hebrew of the Temple elite.

"—not just the fishermen. The tanners’ guild is whispering about signs."

"Contain what?" The older priest, his beard streaked with white, snapped the words. "A handful of illiterate peasants babbling in the marketplace? The prefect wants order, not your hysterics."

Miriam traced the edge of her satchel strap with her thumb. The leather was worn smooth in one spot, a ridge her finger found without thought.

"Their so-called prophet died screaming like any other criminal. Let them rot with his memory." The white-bearded priest adjusted the embroidered sash at his waist. The fabric was dyed Tyrian purple, the fringe perfectly even. A man who noticed details. "The festival is what matters. The offerings. The tithes."

A younger priest, his cheeks flushed, cut in. "Tell that to the crowd when they start shouting about resurrection in the middle of the Court."

The older priest’s hand twitched toward his sash. A tiny motion, but Miriam caught it—the way his fingers grazed the fabric as if checking for something hidden beneath. A knife? A scroll? The gesture was too practiced to be casual.

"Then handle it." The words were ice. "Or do I need to remind you what happened to the last man who let a mob form in my Temple?"

The younger priest paled. The group moved on, their sandals clicking against the stone, their voices dropping to murmurs once more.

Miriam exhaled. The air smelled of incense and sweat, the metallic tang of the sacrifice fires mixing with the sour note of overcrowded humanity. A fly landed on her knee. She watched it crawl across the weave of her robe, its wings iridescent in the slanted light. An irrelevant detail. She noted it anyway.

To her left, a Roman officer leaned against a pillar, his cuirass gleaming. He was listening to a Temple scribe rattle off numbers—something about grain stores and festival allocations. The officer’s Latin was rough but functional. "Double the patrols at the Damascus Gate. And keep your zealots away from the Antonia."

The scribe bobbed his head. "Of course, Prefect."

"I don’t pay you for rumors." The officer pushed off the pillar. "I pay you for names. Locations. The rest is your problem."

Miriam’s thumb tapped against her leg. Once, twice. The rhythm stuttered when the officer’s gaze flicked in her direction. She looked down, pretending to adjust her sandal strap. The leather was fraying at the buckle. Another thing to repair before the journey home.

When she glanced up, the Romans were gone. The portico was quieter now, the shadows lengthening as the sun climbed. A pair of doves pecked at crumbs near her feet, their heads jerking with mechanical precision. One had a crooked tail feather. It stuck out at an angle, quivering with each peck.

A hand touched her shoulder.

She didn’t startle this time. One hand stayed hidden, but her muscles coiled ready. She turned slowly.

Tavi stood there, his interpreter’s robe slightly rumpled, a sheen of sweat at his temples. "You’re still here." It wasn’t a question.

Miriam shifted to put the column between herself and the nearest cluster of pilgrims. "The Court is public space."

"It is." His eyes—dark, too perceptive—skimmed over her face. "But the Caravanserai is safer for merchants with sharp ears."

She could have played dumb. Could have shrugged and walked away. But his tone held a challenge, and the part of her that still remembered how to be reckless lifted its head. "I don’t know any Galileans."

"Good." Tavi plucked a stray thread from his sleeve. The motion was casual, but his fingers were precise. A man used to handling fragile things. "Because the priests are offering silver for information. And the Romans are offering less pleasant incentives."

The dove with the crooked feather took flight, its wings beating the air inches from Miriam’s cheek. She didn’t flinch. "I trade in oil."

"Of course." Tavi smiled, but it was the smile of a scribe copying a well-worn text, empty of conviction. "But a woman who speaks four languages is a rare thing in Jerusalem. People notice."

A chill prickled the back of her neck. She hadn’t told him about the Latin, the Greek, the Aramaic she’d let slip. "People should mind their own business."

"Jerusalem is business." He stepped closer, his voice dropping. "You have three days until the festival ends. Use them wisely."

The implication hung between them. Leave. Before someone asked the wrong questions.

Miriam stood, her satchel brushing against the bench. The marble had left a damp patch on her robe, the fabric clinging to her thighs. "I have contracts to fulfill."

Tavi’s gaze flicked to her hands—the tapping thumb, the white-knuckled grip on her strap. "Then fulfill them quickly." He turned to go, then paused. "Oh. You might find the southern spice market more profitable this afternoon. Fewer patrols."

He walked away without waiting for a response. Miriam watched him disappear into the crowd, his blue-trimmed robe blending with the sea of pilgrims. The Court was louder now, the voices rising in a dozen languages, the air thick with the scent of roasting meat and crushed herbs. Somewhere, a child wailed. A donkey brayed. The ordinary chaos of a holy city.

The crooked-feathered dove returned, pecking at nothing. She stared at it, at the absurd, stubborn angle of that one misplaced feather. A flaw. A vulnerability. Something that made it different from all the other doves in the Temple.

She stood abruptly. The dove startled, wings flapping as it fled.

Time to check the southern market.

The southern market sprawled like a drunkard against the Temple’s outer walls, its awnings sagging under the weight of midday heat. Miriam wove through the press of bodies, her sandals sticking to the cobbles where spilled wine had dried into a syrupy film. The spice stalls were tucked behind the money changers, their tables shielded from the worst of the sun by faded linen canopies. She counted the patrols—two Romans at the corner, a Temple guard leaning against a pillar—before approaching the nearest merchant.

The man’s fingers were stained yellow with turmeric. He wiped them on his apron, leaving streaks like old bruises. "Alexandrian?"

Miriam inclined her head. "You have myrrh."

"Best in the city." He jerked his chin toward a row of clay jars. The seals were intact, the wax imprinted with a mark she recognized—Cyrene. Elior’s work. She’d know that clumsy "E" anywhere.

She tapped her thumb against her index finger, slow and deliberate. "From a mercantile perspective, Cyrene myrrh trades at a discount. The overland route is less reliable than sea transport."

The merchant’s smile didn’t reach his eyes. "This isn’t Cyrene myrrh."

"Of course not." She traced the seal with her fingertip. The wax flaked under her nail. "But the amphorae from Alexandria have a wider mouth."

A shadow fell across the table. Tavi.

Miriam’s hand stilled.

He picked up one of the jars, turning it in his palms as if weighing more than clay and resin. "Strange place for an oil merchant."

The spice vendor stiffened. His gaze darted to the Temple guard, then back to his wares. "Interpreter."

Tavi set the jar down with a click. "Leave us."

The man hesitated, his fingers twitching toward his apron pocket—where the knife would be, where Miriam’s already was—but he ducked his head and retreated into the crowd.

Miriam adjusted her satchel strap. The leather bit into her shoulder. "You’re disrupting commerce."

"And you’re lying about your trade." Tavi plucked a dried fig from a nearby basket, rolling it between his fingers. "Cyrene myrrh is half the price of Judean. But you knew that. Just like you knew the seal."

A child darted past, chasing a scrap of parchment caught in the hot wind. Miriam watched it skitter under a money changer’s table. "I have diverse interests."

"Mm." He bit into the fig, chewing slowly. "You also have an accent. Not much of one. Just enough to tell me you didn’t grow up speaking Aramaic in some Alexandrian slum."

One knife pressed against her ribs. She could feel the outline of the blade through the fabric. "You’re mistaken."

"Am I?" He licked fig juice from his thumb. "The word you used earlier—‘mercator.’ That’s Latin. But you didn’t learn it from some dockside trader. Your vowels are too clean. You had a tutor. Which means family money."

"Nothing." Her voice was sharper than she intended. The tapping started again, faster now. "You’re overreaching."

Tavi studied her hands. "Maybe. But here’s what I know for certain. You speak four languages without thinking. You notice details most people miss. And you’re afraid."

A donkey brayed somewhere to the left, the sound jagged and sudden. Miriam forced her breath to stay even. "I’m cautious."

"There’s no difference." He stepped closer, his voice dropping. "Not in this city. Not now."

The sun was directly overhead, baking the stones beneath their feet. Miriam could feel the heat through her sandals. An irrelevant observation. She noted it anyway.

Tavi’s sleeve brushed her arm as he reached past her to straighten a jar of saffron. His fingers lingered near the rim. "The priests are offering thirty pieces of silver for information about the Galileans. The Romans are offering less. But more permanent."

"You don’t have to." His breath smelled of figs and anise. "You just have to look like you might. That’s enough these days."

A fly landed on the saffron jar. Its wings flickered, a rapid twitch of iridescence. She focused on that instead of the pulse hammering in her throat.

Tavi straightened. "Three days, Miriam. Then the festival ends. Then the real hunting begins."

He walked away without looking back.

The market noise rushed in to fill the silence he left behind—the clink of coins, the haggling voices, the rhythmic thud of a mortar grinding cardamom. Miriam breathed out. Her fingers found the satchel strap again, the worn spot smooth under her touch.

A voice cut through the din. Parthian.

Her head snapped up.

An old woman haggled with a spice vendor, her words sharp and guttural. Nothing unusual. Nothing out of place. But Miriam’s skin prickled as if brushed by a cold wind.

No.

She turned abruptly, nearly colliding with a wine-seller balancing a tray of cups. A man cursed, liquid sloshing over the rim. Miriam muttered an apology and pushed through the crowd, her pace quickening.

Her knife was still there. The coins were still there. The contracts were still there.

But something else was, too.

A feeling.

Not fear. Not caution.

Something with sharper teeth.

She glanced back once. Tavi stood at the edge of the market, watching her. His expression was unreadable from this distance, but his posture was relaxed, his arms crossed, his weight shifted slightly to one side. A man waiting.

Miriam turned the corner and let the crowd swallow her.
