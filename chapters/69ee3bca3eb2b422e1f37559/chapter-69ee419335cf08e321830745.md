The stone bench was hard against his thighs. Tavi traced the edge of a worn mosaic tile with his sandal, following the faded blue curve of a stylized wave. The antechamber smelled of cedar oil and old parchment, a smell so familiar it had long since stopped registering as a smell at all. Simply the air of authority. From beyond the heavy cedar door, the murmur of voices rose and fell in the measured rhythm of men who believed time was a currency they controlled.

The summons had come an hour ago, delivered by a junior Levite whose face was slick with more than the morning’s heat. The boy’s words had been efficient, stripped of context: the Prefect of the Temple Guard required an interpreter’s assessment of a disturbance in the outer courts. A Galilean faction causing a scene. Possible drunkenness, possible sorcery, definite public disorder.

Tavi had asked only one question. “Languages?”

The Levite had blinked. “Many. That’s the problem. Pilgrims are claiming… understanding.”

Now, he waited. He let his mind idle on the mosaic. The wave was part of a larger, mostly destroyed seascape. A fisherman’s net, a fragment of a boat. Poor work, rushed. He’d always meant to ask which prefect’s renovation it was from, and then never had.

The door opened. Not the junior Levite, but Malchus, the captain of the high priest’s household guard. His face was a closed fist. “They’re ready for you.”

The chamber beyond was smaller than the formal hall, a room for practical matters. Annas, the former high priest, sat in a high-backed chair near a slit window, the light cutting his profile into sharp planes. He did not look up from the scroll in his hands. Caiaphas, the current high priest, stood by a table, rolling a wax tablet between his palms. A third man, a Roman in a simple tunic and military boots, leaned against the far wall. Pilate’s man. Tavi cataloged the posture: observant, deliberately non-participatory. The air was warm, close.

“The interpreter,” Caiaphas said. Not a greeting. A label.

“You observed the gathering in the square earlier,” Annas said, his voice dry as dust. He still did not look up. “The one centered on the fisherman from Galilee. Peter.”

It wasn’t a question. Of course they knew he’d been there. The Temple had eyes in every wall. “I was in the vicinity, lord. On other business.”

“Describe what you witnessed.” This from Caiaphas. He set the tablet down with a precise click.

Tavi kept his hands loose at his sides. The professional mask settled over him, comfortable as a worn cloak. “A crowd formed around a group of Galileans. The principal speaker was Peter, formerly a follower of Yeshua of Nazareth. His initial address provoked a division. Some listeners accused him and his companions of drunkenness.”

“And the others?” Annas’s eyes flicked to him, pale and assessing.

“The others claimed to understand him. In their own languages.”

“Which languages?” the Roman spoke from his corner. His Greek was educated, but the accent was from the Italian peninsula. Not a junior officer.

Tavi turned his head slightly, including the man in his answer without appearing to address him directly. “I identified Cappadocian Greek, a dialect of Latin from the western provinces, the Aramaic of Judea, of course. Also Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Parthian. A Berber tongue from North Africa. Others I could not place at distance.”

“A circus,” Caiaphas muttered. He began to pace, the hem of his robe whispering against the polished stone floor. “A festival sideshow. Drunken babbling that fools the credulous.”

The Roman pushed off the wall. “My centurion reports the opposite. He says a woman from Cyrene fell to her knees weeping. Claimed the man spoke of her stillborn child, using a name only she and her husband knew. In her own dialect.” He looked at Tavi. “Is that possible? For a man to speak a language he has never learned?”

All three men looked at him now. The weight of the question was a physical thing. It was not a query about possibility. It was a request for a category: miracle or fraud. The official answer would determine the Temple’s response. Suppression or containment.

Tavi felt the familiar internal shift into analysis. The sanctuary of linguistics. “It is impossible under known pedagogical principles. True fluency requires immersion, practice. The accent alone…” He paused. The face of the woman from the square flashed behind his eyes—not the Cyrene woman, the other one. The Alexandrian merchant. Her shock had been absolute, a bone-deep recognition that had nothing to do with credulity. “However,” he continued, choosing each word like a step on a slippery path, “the reactions I observed were not those of people hearing approximate translations or lucky guesses. The affect was one of… personal recognition. As if the speaker had accessed not just vocabulary, but intimate memory.”

A silence followed. Annas laid the scroll aside. “Sorcery, then.”

“Or mass hysteria,” Caiaphas countered. “The festival, the crowds, the heat. A charismatic speaker and a predisposed audience. It is a known phenomenon.”

“The Pythia speaks Greek,” the Roman said flatly. “She does not make Scythians weep in their mother tongue.” He focused on Tavi again. “You are the expert. You heard it. Your professional opinion. Is it a trick?”

Tavi’s mouth was dry. He thought of the merchant woman’s fingers, tapping a frantic, silent rhythm against her leg. A coping mechanism for a mind under assault. He had seen scholars do similar things when confronted with a text that upended all their assumptions. “If it is a trick,” he said slowly, “it is one that bypasses all known mechanisms of deception. It operates at the level of the individual listener simultaneously. A street magician cannot achieve that. A sorcerer would need intimate knowledge of hundreds of strangers’ lives.” He shook his head, the gesture minute. “I lack a framework to classify it as deception.”

Caiaphas stopped pacing. “Then you lack usefulness.”

“My usefulness,” Tavi said, the words out before he could measure them, “is in telling you what it is not. It is not drunken babbling. The syntax reported from multiple witnesses is coherent. It is not conventional oratory. The effect is too specific, too personal. Dismissing it as wine or madness will not make it stop. The crowd is already moving.”

“Moving where?” Annas’s voice was a razor.

“To the ritual baths. They are asking for baptism in the name of the Nazarene.”

The reaction was immediate. Caiaphas’s face darkened. Annas went very still. The Roman’s expression didn’t change, but his posture did—a slight tightening of the shoulders. A military assessment.

“A purification rite,” Caiaphas said, his voice low with fury. “On Shavuot. In our Temple courts, they invoke a dead criminal’s name for ritual cleansing.” He turned to Annas. “This is no longer about a noisy gathering. This is a rival cult forming at our gates. During the pilgrimage. Under the prefect’s nose.” He shot a glance at the Roman.

The Roman ignored the provocation. “A mass movement is a public order issue. My concern is the stonework, not the theology. Can you control your people, or will this require garrison intervention?”

“We will control it,” Annas said. His eyes were on Tavi. “You will go to the Court of the Gentiles. You will observe this ‘baptism.’ You will listen. Your task is to provide a linguistic analysis that allows us to discredit it. Find the flaw. The inconsistency. The source of the trickery. A man cannot know what he does not know. Therefore, he is receiving information. How? From whom? Is there a confederate in the crowd feeding him cues? Is there a system of signals?”

Tavi understood. They did not want the truth. They wanted the blueprint of a conspiracy. They needed a villain, a method, a plot they could arrest.

“And if there is no system?” he asked.

Annas leaned forward. The light from the window gleamed on the fine wool of his robe. “Then you will devise one. You are an interpreter. Your craft is building bridges of meaning between disparate tongues. Build one for us. Between this… phenomenon… and a charge of seditious fraud that will satisfy the prefect and disperse the crowd.” He settled back. “The festival concludes in three days. This must not survive it. Your reputation for objectivity is your credential. Use it.”

Malchus opened the door. The dismissal was clear.

Out in the corridor, the cooler air felt like a reprieve. Tavi’s heart beat a dull, stubborn rhythm against his ribs. He walked, his sandals making soft clicks on the stone. He passed a descending row of niches holding oil lamps, their flames unwavering in the still air.

He thought of Peter’s voice. Not the words, but the timbre. It had not sounded like a man receiving signals. It had sounded like a man who was himself a signal, a conduit for something else. A terrifying thought. A more terrifying thought: what if the conduit was genuine?

A servant hurried past with a jug of water, avoiding his eyes.

Tavi’s own mind, his prized analytical instrument, felt like one of those flawed mosaics. The picture it was trying to assemble had pieces that did not fit. The woman from Alexandria—Miriam. Her understanding had been a wound ripped open. That was not the effect of a clever trick. Tricks produced amazement, then suspicion. They did not produce grief so acute it bent the body double.

He pushed through a heavy curtain into the brighter, noisier arcade that led to the outer courts. The sounds of the city rushed in—vendors, animals, the distant murmur of a thousand conversations. The smell of incense was overpowered by dust and animal dung and humanity.

He had his orders. Find a conspiracy. Construct a lie the authorities could use.

But his training, the core of him that respected the sacred, maddening complexity of language, rebelled. His profession was built on a fundamental belief: true meaning could be found, could be translated, if one worked with enough skill and patience. To deliberately mistranslate a phenomenon, to force it into a false category, was not just a duty. It was a desecration of his entire life’s work.

He stopped at the edge of the stair leading down into the swirling chaos of the Court of the Gentiles. The baths were to the south. He could already see an unusual flow of people in that direction, a current against the normal festival eddies.

He would go. He would listen.

But as he descended into the noise and the light, he knew, with a certainty that chilled him, that he was no longer just an observer sent by the Temple.

He was a man walking into a sentence for which he did not yet have the words.

The Court of the Gentiles was a sea of noise, a chaos of commerce and devotion that usually followed predictable currents. Today, a new tide pulled against the grain. Tavi stood on the bottom step, letting the press of bodies flow around him. He focused on the specific quality of the sound.

It wasn’t just loud. It was layered, a dissonance that scraped at his nerves. The hawkers still shouted their prices for doves and lambs, the moneychangers still clinked coins on their tables. But underneath that, a different energy hummed. Excitement, yes, but also a raw, unvarnished shock. He heard it in the breaks in conversation, the sharp inhalations, the low, urgent questions that trailed off without answers.

He moved south, toward the ritual baths. The crowd thickened. He saw a Phrygian merchant, his face pale beneath his sun-darkened skin, clutching the arm of a companion. “He knew my father’s name,” the man was saying, over and over in heavily accented Greek. “My father, who died in the mines. He said ‘Elpis’—that was his dog. No one here knows that.”

Tavi cataloged the detail without breaking stride. A proper noun, culturally specific, affectively charged. Not something a confederate could easily discover or signal.

He found a vantage point near a column, its base worn smooth by generations of hands. From here, he could see the broad, sun-bleached steps leading down to the mikva’ot. The baths were crowded, but not with the usual solitary, ritual-minded men. Clusters of people stood in the waist-deep water, surrounded by onlookers on the steps. At the center of the largest group stood Peter. The fisherman’s tunic was soaked, plastered to his broad frame. He held a man by the shoulders, speaking directly into his face.

Tavi couldn’t hear the words. The distance was too great, the ambient noise too high. But he saw the man’s reaction. He went limp, then shuddered, a convulsion that looked less like fear and more like the release of a terrible weight. When Peter lowered him into the water, the man did not struggle. He came up gasping, but his gasp turned into a shout—a wordless, joyous sound that was cut short by a sob.

No signals. No subtle glances toward accomplices in the crowd. Peter’s attention was total, locked on the person before him. His companions—other Galileans, men and women both—moved through the other baths doing the same. Their movements were inefficient, unpracticed. This was not a staged ritual. It was something being invented moment by moment, with a frantic, desperate sincerity.

A woman pushed past Tavi, her eyes fixed on the water. She was middle-aged, her dress the plain weave of a provincial. She muttered under her breath, a repetitive, rhythmic plea in a dialect from the Decapolis. Tavi caught the words for shame and well and dry.

“You understand her?”

The voice came from his left. A young Levite, one of the Temple’s many minor functionaries, stood watching him. The boy’s face was a mask of professional disdain, but his eyes were wide, unsettled.

“Enough,” Tavi said.

“It’s a madness. They’re dunking anyone. A Celt just went under. He came up shouting about oak trees.” The Levite shook his head. “The captain says to watch for the trick. The wires and the mirrors. Where are they?”

Tavi didn’t answer. He was listening to a different sound. It came from a group of Parthians seated on the ground nearby. They were not looking at the baths. They were looking at each other, speaking in turns, their voices thick with emotion. One man held his hands out in front of him, turning them over as if seeing them for the first time.

Tavi edged closer, using the column as cover. His Aramaic was good, but their dialect was archaic, laced with Persian loanwords. He caught fragments.

“…not the prophecy of Cyrus, but the sound of the river where my sister…”

“…a knot in my chest, the one I carried from the battle at…”

“…said the name of the valley where the lambs are lost. My valley.”

They weren’t quoting scripture. They weren’t even discussing Peter’s sermon. They were sharing secrets. Personal, geographic, emotional secrets that the preacher’s words had apparently dislodged from some hidden chamber within them. The ‘tongues’ were not translating a public speech into private language. They were performing a reverse alchemy, turning public words into a private key.

His professional mind recoiled. Language didn’t work that way. Meaning was negotiated, contextual, shared. It couldn’t contain unique, biographical data unknown to the speaker. Unless the meaning wasn’t coming from the speaker at all.

The thought was a door swinging open onto a void. He slammed it shut.

A commotion erupted closer to the baths. A Roman soldier—not the garrison, but one of Pilate’s men detailed to festival crowd control—was arguing with a Judean man. The Judean was gesturing wildly toward the water.

“He called me by my childhood name!” the man shouted in Greek, for the soldier’s benefit. “No one here knows it! Not my wife, not my brothers here with me!”

“Your name is not my concern,” the soldier snapped. “The blockage of the thoroughfare is. Move this assembly along.”

“You don’t understand! He knew!”

Tavi watched the soldier’s face. He saw not skepticism, but a profound, professional annoyance. The supernatural was a nuisance. It disrupted traffic flow. This, Tavi realized, might be the only analysis that mattered to Rome. Not fraud or miracle. Obstruction.

He needed air that didn’t smell of wet wool and dust and fervor. He turned, intending to retreat back to the arcade, and almost collided with Miriam of Alexandria.

She stood perfectly still, a statue in the river of movement. Her face was turned toward the baths, but her eyes were unfocused, seeing nothing in the present scene. A single tear had tracked a clean line through the dust on her cheek. Her fingers were moving again, not the frantic tapping from before, but a slow, deliberate sequence—tapping thumb to each fingertip in turn, over and over. A counting rhythm. A grounding rhythm.

She was speaking, so softly he almost missed it. Not Greek or Aramaic. The liquid, vowel-rich language of the Egyptian countryside. He knew enough to recognize a lament.

“The reeds by the water… the sound of his laugh in the reeds…”

Then her eyes focused, landing on him. The connection was instant and electric. She didn’t startle. She simply looked at him, and in that look was a shared, terrifying comprehension. She had seen him in the square that morning. She knew he was an official. And she didn’t care.

“You hear it,” she said in Greek. Not a question.

He could have denied it. He should have. He gave the smallest nod.

“It is not in his mouth,” she whispered. The words seemed to cost her something. “It is in the ear. The fire… it sits on the ear, not the tongue. It translates…” She struggled, searching for a concept her languages lacked. “It translates the heart. Not the words. The heart of the speaker into the… the memory of the listener.” Her hand rose, almost touching her own ear, then fell. “My brother. He spoke of my brother. By the canal. In our words.”

She wasn’t explaining it to him, Tavi realized. She was assembling the explanation for herself, using him as a witness to make it real. Her coping mechanism had shifted from internal counting to external articulation. She needed to speak the madness to believe she wasn’t alone in it.

“The authorities,” Tavi said, his voice low. “They will say it is a trick. Drunkenness.”

A faint, sorrowful smile touched Miriam’s lips. It was the most unsettling expression he had seen all day. “You cannot fake a memory you do not own. You cannot steal a grief you have not carried.” She looked past him, toward the arguing soldier and the distraught Judean. “They will call it madness because the alternative is to admit the world is not a closed scroll. It is a door.” Her eyes returned to his. “You are a man of words. You know a locked door when you see one.”

Then she moved, melting back into the crowd, heading not toward the baths, but away, toward the city gates.

Tavi stood rooted. The noise of the court collapsed into a single, high-pitched ring in his ears. It translates the heart.

His orders were to find a system, a conspiracy. But the only system he could deduce was monstrously, elegantly simple. The speaker’s intent was being filtered through the listener’s life, rendered not into equivalent vocabulary, but into the specific images, names, and wounds that constituted that listener’s secret history. Perfect, untraceable communication. And it was utterly impossible to disprove, because its evidence was locked inside the subjective experience of hundreds of people.

A cold knot tightened in his stomach. He had his analysis. The phenomenon was authentic. It was also, from the perspective of the Temple and the Empire, infinitely more dangerous than fraud. Fraud could be exposed. Madness could be contained. This? This could only be silenced.

And to silence it, they would need a lie sturdy enough to bear the weight of all these shattered, weeping, joyful people. They would need a translator willing to commit the ultimate betrayal of his craft: to take a truth that transcended language and chain it down with false, careful, deadly words.

He looked at his own hands. The tools of his trade. They felt alien.

From the baths, a new sound rose—a hundred voices, maybe more, singing. No single melody, no unified language. A ragged, dissonant, glorious chorus of separate songs that somehow, in the bright, cruel Jerusalem sun, did not clash. They wove together. For a moment, Tavi heard not the noise of a disturbance, but the first, fractured note of a translation too vast for any one tongue to contain.

He knew then he could not build the bridge Annas demanded. The materials they wanted him to use—fraud, sedition, madness—would not hold. The truth was a solvent, and it was already eating away at the foundations of everything he knew.

He turned and walked away, not back toward the Temple’s quiet, accusing chambers, but deeper into the singing crowd. He had to hear more. He had to understand the grammar of this impossible sentence, even if it meant becoming a part of it.

The room smelled of cedar oil and old parchment. A single high window cut a sharp rectangle of white light onto the floor, bisecting the faded rug. Tavi stood just inside the door, waiting. The official, a man named Jotham from the Temple’s administrative wing, did not look up from the scroll he was reading. He was a compact man with a scribe’s permanent stoop, his fingers stained with ink. The silence was a test.

“Sit,” Jotham said finally, still not looking up.

Tavi sat on the stool opposite the desk. Too low, forcing him to look up. A design choice, not an accident.

Jotham finished his paragraph, rolled the scroll with precise, economical motions, and set it aside. He laced his fingers together on the desk’s bare surface. No stylus, no wax tablet. This conversation was to be unrecorded. “You observed the disturbance.”

“I observed the event in the Court of the Gentiles,” Tavi corrected. The words sounded pedantic even to him.

“Semantics are your profession. Not mine.” Jotham’s eyes were the color of dry clay. “The High Priest’s father requires an analysis. A preliminary one. Is it a threat to public order?”

“It is a crowd. Crowds are inherently a threat to order.”

“Do not play the sophist with me.” The tone remained flat, devoid of heat. This was worse than shouting. “You were sent to assess the mechanism of the deception. Provide your assessment.”

Tavi’s mouth was dry. He thought of the Parthians sharing secrets on the ground. The Judean shouting his childhood name. Miriam’s tear-tracked dust. It translates the heart. He could not say that. The sentence would dissolve in this dry air, and he would be marked as either a fool or a sympathizer. His mind, trained for precision, began to construct a report. It was the most difficult translation of his life: truth into acceptable fiction.

“The phenomenon is not conventional oratory,” he began, echoing his earlier words to Annas. “The speaker, Peter of Galilee, exhibits no signs of classical training. His delivery is… direct. Unadorned.”

“We are not critiquing his rhetoric. We are investigating a fraud.”

“The fraud, if it is one, is sophisticated.” Tavi chose each word as if stepping onto rotten wood. “It operates on two levels. The first is the glossolalia—the speaking in many tongues. This could be achieved through prior study, or through the use of multilingual confederates in the crowd creating a chorus effect. The second, more complex level is the personalization. The specific names, memories.”

Jotham leaned forward an inch. “Explain.”

“A confederate system is theoretically possible.” Tavi heard his own voice, clinical, detached. It was like listening to another man speak. “A network of individuals dispersed through the crowd, gathering biographical details from pilgrims in the preceding days. In the chaos of the festival, people talk. They share stories of home, of loss. A skilled organizer could compile a ledger of such details. Signals could then be passed to the speaker—a hand gesture, a cough, a specific position in the crowd—indicating which detail to deploy and to whom.”

It was a monstrous, convoluted construction. It would require an army of covert listeners, a flawless communication system, and a speaker with a prodigious, instantaneous memory. It was the kind of plot only someone who had never organized anything more complex than a dinner party would believe possible. Perfect.

Jotham’s expression did not change. “You witnessed such signals?”

“I witnessed intense focus on the part of the speaker and his associates. Such focus could be misinterpreted as receiving instruction. The crowd’s emotional state is high. People are predisposed to see meaning in coincidence.” He was weaving the lie with threads of truth, a fabric of plausible deniability. His stomach turned.

“So. A pre-planned conspiracy. Seditious in intent, as it appropriates a holy festival for its own ends. Designed to provoke unrest and challenge Temple authority by manufacturing false divine endorsement.” Jotham summarized it neatly, packaging the chaos into a familiar box. “This is your professional opinion?”

Tavi’s throat tightened. He saw the door the lie would open. Arrests. Scapegoats. The quiet, efficient violence of the Temple guard. He saw Miriam’s face, the absolute absence of guile. You cannot fake a memory you do not own.

“It is one plausible interpretation of the observable facts,” he said. The evasion was a coward’s moat around his crumbling integrity.

Jotham stared at him for a long count. The rectangle of light on the floor had crept forward, now touching the leg of Tavi’s stool. A bar of heat against his ankle.

“Plausible is not sufficient. The prefect’s office deals in certainty. Or its convincing facsimile.” Jotham unclasped his hands and tapped a single finger on the desk. “Your reputation is for objectivity. For… intellectual purity. This is a currency. We wish to spend it. You will draft a formal statement, for the record, outlining the strong likelihood of a coordinated deception. You will cite the logistical requirements—the confederates, the signaling. You will avoid definitive claims of miracle or divine action. You will frame it as a threat to the Pax Romana as well as to religious decorum.”

He did not move. He sat on the stool, listening to the silence in the room, and wondered what the first word of his statement should be. Whatever he chose, it would be a betrayal. The only question was whether the betrayal was of his masters, his profession, or something else he could not yet name.
