The oil lamp flickered when Miriam set it down, the flame bending toward the wax tablet as if drawn by the names scored into its surface. Her own stood out—Miriam, daughter of Eleazar—the letters precise, the strokes deliberate. She traced the grooves with her thumb. Five years of anonymity, erased in one act of surrender.

The room smelled of rosemary and the faint metallic tang of the ink she'd used. A moth battered itself against the lamp's clay rim, wings whispering against the hot surface. She should shoo it away. She didn't.

Outside, the city murmured—footsteps on stone, a distant argument, the occasional rise and fall of prayer from the direction of the upper room. The sounds layered over each other, none distinct enough to parse. She exhaled through her nose. The list was a beacon now. A target.

Her fingers found the knife at her belt. One blade slid free with a soft hiss. She turned it in the lamplight, watching the flame fracture along the edge. A practical tool, this. Not a weapon. She'd told herself that for years.

The tablet's surface caught the light at an angle, throwing her reflection back at her—a smudged impression of cheekbone and shadow where her eyes should be. She pressed her thumb into the wax again, harder this time, distorting the letters beneath.

A knock at the door.

She went still. The knife stayed in her hand.

"Miriam?" Eliana's voice, low and frayed at the edges.

She sheathed the blade. "Come in."

The door creaked open. Eliana carried a basket of bread, the cloth covering it stained with oil. She looked at the tablet, then at Miriam's hands. "You're still awake."

"I was reviewing the lists."

Eliana set the basket on the table. The bread smelled of barley and ash. "They're accurate. You made sure of that."

Miriam rubbed her thumb against her fingers, feeling the wax residue. "Accuracy isn't the problem."

Eliana pulled the cloth back, revealing a loaf split unevenly down the middle. "We saved you half."

"I'm not hungry."

"You need to eat."

The words hung between them, ordinary and impossible. Miriam picked up the smaller piece. The crust gave way under her fingers, crumbling at the edges. She took a bite. It tasted like nothing.

Eliana watched her chew. "They'll come for the lists tomorrow."

"I know."

"They'll want to add more names."

Miriam set the bread down. "They can't keep it in one place. Not now."

"We won't." Eliana touched the edge of the tablet. "Copies will be made. Distributed."

"Like the bread."

The moth brushed the flame again. A wingtip caught, flaring bright for an instant before the insect spiraled to the floor, smoking. Miriam ground it under her sandal.

Eliana didn't flinch. "You're afraid."

"I'm practical."

"You're both."

Miriam wiped her hands on her robe. The wax smeared. "They'll use this to find people."

"Yes."

"And you're not afraid?"

Eliana picked up the other half of the loaf. "I was. The first time they came for my husband. The second time, when they took my son. By the third time, I had nothing left to lose." She broke the bread in two. "Fear needs something to hold onto. You have to give it a place to land."

Miriam looked at the tablet. Her name stared back.

Eliana held out a piece of bread. "Eat."

This time, Miriam took it.

The lamp burned lower. Shadows stretched across the walls, long fingers reaching for the ceiling. Somewhere in the city, a dog barked, the sound sharp and sudden. Miriam listened for footsteps beneath it. None came.

Eliana brushed crumbs from her lap. "They'll start at dawn."

"Who will?"

"The ones making copies. The ones carrying them." She nodded to the tablet. "That one stays here. The others go to the corners."

Miriam touched the knife again. "And if they're found?"

"Then they're found." Eliana stood, gathering the empty cloth. "The names are already written."

The words settled between them, heavy as stone.

Miriam blew out the lamp.

Darkness swallowed the room, but the afterimage of the flame lingered behind her eyes—a bright, persistent ghost. She closed her hand around the tablet's edge. The wax was warm where the light had touched it.

Eliana's sandals whispered against the floor as she moved toward the door. "Will you sleep?"

"No."

A pause. Then the door opened, a slice of moonlight cutting across the threshold. "Then I'll see you at first light."

The latch clicked shut.

Miriam sat in the dark, her fingers still curled around the tablet. The names were there, even unseen. Her brother's voice echoed in the silence, the words he'd spoken before the soldiers came, words she'd buried so deep she'd forgotten their shape until Peter dragged them into the light.

She drew the knife again. One blade gleamed faintly in the moonlight.

A choice, then. To carve her name deeper, or to let it stand as it was.

She set the knife down.

The tablet lay before her, a dark mirror awaiting the dawn.

The rooftop of the Shops of Annas offered an unobstructed view of the Temple courts, the marble gleaming like bone under the moon. Tribune Cassius Longinus braced his hands against the parapet, the stone rough under his palms. Below, the last stragglers of the festival crowd drifted through the Court of the Gentiles, their shadows pooling around the colonnades. The night should have been quiet. Should have been still.

The air hummed.

He'd heard the reports, whispers of fire without burn, tongues without translation. Nonsense, of course. And yet.

A centurion cleared his throat behind him. "No disturbances, sir. Just the usual pilgrims."

Cassius didn't turn. "And the Galileans?"

"Scattered. A few still gathered in the upper rooms near the Essene Quarter."

"Watching them?"

The centurion shifted his weight. "Not closely. They're praying, mostly."

Cassius breathed out through his nose. Prayer he could tolerate. It was the other thing, the sound they claimed had filled the house, the light that wasn't fire, that set his teeth on edge. He'd seen enough strange things in Judea to know the difference between superstition and something sharper.

"Double the watch at the Antonia," he said. "Quietly."

The centurion hesitated. "Sir, the prefect said—"

"I know what the prefect said." Cassius finally turned. The man's face was half in shadow, the lamplight carving deep grooves under his eyes. "But I won't have another riot on my hands. Not so soon after the last one."

The centurion nodded and left, his footsteps precise on the stairs.

Cassius looked back at the Temple. A lone figure moved along the far colonnade, hooded, carrying something small and flat. A tablet, perhaps. The figure paused at the edge of the torchlight, then melted into the shadows.

He rubbed his thumb along the edge of his signet ring. The metal was warm from his skin.

Somewhere in the city, a dog barked. Then another. The sound skittered across the rooftops, fading into the murmur of the night.

Miriam's fingers ached.

She flexed them, watching the tendons shift under her skin. The stylus had left a groove in her middle finger, a pale line that would linger for days. The wax tablet lay beside her, the names now firm and clear. No more blurring. No more hesitation.

From the street below, a snatch of Aramaic floated up—a woman's voice, low and urgent. "—not safe to carry them all at once—"

A man answered, the words too muffled to parse.

Miriam didn't move to the window. Didn't strain to hear. The lists were moving. That was enough.

She touched the edge of the tablet. The wax had cooled, but the memory of the flame lingered in its surface.

A knock at the door, softer this time.

She didn't reach for the knife. "Come in."

Tavi slipped inside, his robe dusty from the streets. He carried a bundle under one arm, the cloth stained with something dark. Ink, maybe. Or dirt. He looked at the tablet, then at her hands. "You're still working."

"Finished." She nodded to the tablet. "It's ready."

He set the bundle on the table. The cloth fell away, revealing a stack of thin wooden boards, each barely larger than her palm. "So are these."

She picked one up. The wood was smooth, the grain tight. Names scored the surface in neat, even lines. Not wax. Carved. Permanent.

"Who—?"

"Elior." Tavi rubbed his thumb along the edge of one board. "He had tools. And time."

Miriam traced the letters. The strokes were deeper than wax allowed, the edges sharp. No smudging. No erasing.

Tavi watched her. "They'll want you to speak tomorrow."

Her thumb stilled. "I know."

"Are you ready?"

She set the board down. The names stared back at her, unblinking. "Does it matter?"

He didn't answer.

The lamp flickered. Outside, the city's murmur shifted, a swell of voices from the direction of the Temple, then silence.

Tavi turned his head slightly, listening. "They're still out there."

"Who?"

"All of them." He moved to the window, pushing the shutter open a fraction. Cool air slipped in, carrying the scent of smoke and damp stone. "The ones who heard. The ones who didn't. The ones who'll hear tomorrow."

Miriam joined him. The street below was empty, but the shadows between the buildings seemed to settle. A figure moved at the far end of the alley—hooded, quick, then vanished around a corner.

Tavi let the shutter fall closed. "They'll come for us, you know."

"And you're not afraid?"

She looked at the tablet. At the boards. At the lamp, still burning. "I was."

He studied her face. Then, quietly: "Good."

A silence stretched between them, not quite comfortable, not quite strained.

Somewhere in the city, a child laughed, a bright, sudden sound that dissolved into the night.

Tavi picked up one of the boards. "We should go."

Miriam nodded. She took the tablet and slid it into the pouch at her belt. The wax was cool against her hip.

The lamp burned on as they left, the flame bending toward the empty space where the tablet had been.

Cassius stood at the edge of the rooftop, the first light of dawn staining the sky behind the Temple. The night had been long. Uneasy.

Below, the city stirred, shutters opening, voices rising, the smell of baking bread cutting through the lingering scent of smoke. Normal. Familiar.

A centurion approached, his helmet tucked under one arm. "No incidents, sir."

"Gathering in the upper rooms again. More of them this time."

Cassius steadied. "Keep watching."

The centurion hesitated. "Sir, the prefect wants a report."

"Tell him it's handled."

The man left.

Cassius flexed his hand. The ring bit into his finger.

Across the city, a door opened. Then another. Figures emerged, men, women, some carrying bundles, some empty-handed. They moved with purpose, their paths converging toward the Temple.

A breeze kicked up, carrying the scent of rosemary and dust.

The city held its breath.
