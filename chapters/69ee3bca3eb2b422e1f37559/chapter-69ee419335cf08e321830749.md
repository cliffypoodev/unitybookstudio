The clerk’s voice followed them up the street, a dry recitation of numbers and names. Miriam’s shoulder ached where Tavi’s grip had tightened. The legionary’s knife scraped against his nail again—a small, precise sound that cut through the muffled noise of the festival.

The Pool of Siloam was already crowded when they arrived. Pilgrims clustered along the stone steps, their robes hiked up to their knees, feet dangling in the water. A man with a sunburnt neck was arguing with a woman over a clay jug. Someone’s child wailed. The air smelled of wet stone and unwashed bodies.

Miriam counted seven distinct languages before she stopped herself. Old habit.

A woman stepped forward from the edge of the crowd. Her linen headscarf had slipped back, revealing streaks of gray in her dark hair. She moved like someone who had already made up her mind—no hesitation, no glancing around for approval. Just the steady, deliberate pace of a person walking toward something inevitable.

Eliana. The name surfaced from the murmur of the crowd. A widow from the Lower City. No one important.

She didn’t wait for instruction. Didn’t ask for permission. She walked into the water until it reached her waist, then turned to face Peter. The hem of her robe floated around her like a second skin, translucent with moisture.

“Do it,” she said. Not a request. A statement.

Peter’s beard was dust-streaked, his forehead shiny with sweat. He looked exhausted. But when he spoke, his voice carried over the water. “Do you repent?”

Eliana didn’t flinch. “Yes.”

“Do you believe?”

“Yes.”

No elaboration. No bargaining. Just the bare, unadorned truth.

Peter cupped his hands and poured water over her head. It streamed down her face, dripped from her chin. For a heartbeat, nothing happened. Then Eliana shuddered—a full-body tremor that made the water ripple around her. Her knees buckled. She caught herself on the stone edge of the pool, fingers digging into the cracks between the rocks.

Miriam’s breath caught. She’d seen ecstatics in Alexandria, men and women who claimed divine inspiration, who shook and wailed and spoke in tongues. But this was different. Eliana wasn’t performing. Wasn’t seeking attention.

Alive.

The word came unbidden. Alive in a way Miriam hadn’t been in years.

Eliana pushed her wet hair back from her face. Her eyes were red-rimmed, but she was smiling. Not the careful, measured smile of a merchant closing a deal. Something raw. Unpracticed.

Tavi shifted beside her. “We should go.”

Miriam didn’t move.

A boy no older than twelve splashed into the water after Eliana, his tunic clinging to his skinny frame. Then an old man with a bent spine. A young mother clutching a baby to her chest. One by one, they stepped forward. No grand speeches. No elaborate rituals. Just water and a single, unshakable yes.

Miriam’s fingers found the edge of her sleeve and twisted the fabric. The motion was automatic, a silent tally against some invisible ledger. How many? How much? What did it cost?

But there was no calculation here. No bargaining. Just surrender.

One thought should have been comforting. It wasn’t.

A drop of sweat slid down her temple. The sun had dipped lower, painting the pool in gold and shadow. The water shimmered where the pilgrims disturbed it, breaking the light into fragments.

Eliana waded back to the steps, her soaked robe dragging behind her. She paused in front of Miriam, close enough that Miriam could see the water beading on her lashes.

“You’re from Alexandria,” Eliana said. Not a question.

Miriam nodded.

Eliana studied her for a long moment. Then she reached out and brushed her damp fingers against Miriam’s wrist. The touch was fleeting, almost accidental. But it burned.

“You don’t have to understand it,” Eliana said softly. “You just have to want it.”

Then she was gone, melting into the crowd.

Miriam stared at the spot where she’d been. Her skin tingled where Eliana had touched her. A stupid, physical reaction. Nothing more.

Tavi exhaled through his nose. “Miriam.”

She knew that tone. Knew what came next. The rational argument. The cold assessment of risk. The unspoken plea: Don’t do this.

But for the first time, the words didn’t land. They slid off her like water off stone.

The pool’s surface stilled. The last of the sunlight gilded the edges of the ripples. Somewhere in the distance, a shofar sounded—low and mournful.

Miriam flexed her fingers. The ghost of Eliana’s touch lingered.

Want.

Such a small word. Such a dangerous one.

The shofar’s echo faded into the murmur of the crowd. Miriam rubbed her wrist where Eliana’s fingers had brushed it. The skin there felt too tight, as if branded. She flexed her hand, then curled it into a fist. The gesture did nothing to dispel the sensation.

Tavi cleared his throat. “We’re drawing attention.”

He was right. A few pilgrims lingered near the pool’s edge, their gazes flickering toward her and Tavi with open curiosity. One man—thick-necked, with a Cyrenian accent—leaned toward his companion and muttered something. The companion’s eyes darted to Miriam’s face, then away.

She turned her back to them, feigning interest in the pattern of cracks in the stone steps. Old habit: when observed, become unremarkable. Blend into the architecture. But the cracks refused to hold her focus. Her mind kept circling back to Eliana’s face—the way her expression had fractured open, unguarded, when the water hit her. No mask. No calculation. Just naked, unshielded joy.

A gust of wind carried the scent of roasting meat from the festival stalls. Miriam’s stomach clenched. She hadn’t eaten since dawn. The thought of food turned to ash in her mouth.

“You’re thinking about her,” Tavi said, his voice low.

Miriam didn’t answer. She didn’t need to.

He exhaled through his nose, a sound she’d come to recognize as his version of a sigh. “She’s a widow from the Lower City. No status. No protection.”

“I know.” The words came out sharper than she intended. She softened them with a quick, mercantile pivot. “From a logistical standpoint, public displays are unwise. They invite scrutiny.”

Tavi’s thumb tapped against his belt—once, twice. His tell. He was holding something back.

A child’s laughter rang out from the pool. Miriam glanced over her shoulder. The boy who’d followed Eliana into the water was splashing his companions, his face alight with a grin that reminded her, suddenly and painfully, of her brother. A memory hit like a misplaced step in the dark: her brother’s laughter, bright and unguarded, moments before the shouting started. Before the stones flew.

She looked away.

The crowd near the pool had thickened again. A knot of women in Galilean headscarves clustered around Eliana, their hands fluttering like birds as they spoke. One pressed a dry robe into her arms. Another touched her shoulder, a gesture so casual it made Miriam’s chest ache. No one in Alexandria had touched her like that since the pogrom. No one dared.

Tavi shifted his weight. “We should return to the upper room. The others will be waiting.”

Miriam nodded absently. Her feet refused to move.

A Roman patrol emerged from the alley beside the pool, their segmented armor glinting in the late afternoon light. Three legionaries, their helmets casting shadows over their faces. One carried a wax tablet. He tapped it with the tip of his stylus as he scanned the crowd.

Miriam’s pulse jumped. She’d seen that look before—the bored, methodical assessment of a man compiling a list.

The legionary with the tablet paused near the Galilean women. His gaze slid over them, dismissive, then settled on Eliana. He made a note.

Eliana, still damp-haired, still smiling, didn’t notice.

Miriam’s breath caught. She took a step forward before she realized she’d moved. Tavi’s hand closed around her elbow, pulling her back.

“Don’t.” His grip was firm. “You can’t help her.”

The legionary moved on, his boots scraping against the stone. The other two followed, their spears slung casually over their shoulders. One of them laughed at a joke Miriam didn’t catch.

She wrenched her arm free. “They’re making lists.”

Tavi’s jaw tightened. “They always make lists.”

A breeze stirred the surface of the pool, distorting the reflection of the sky. The water looked darker now, the gold leaching out of it as the sun dipped lower.

The legionaries disappeared around a corner. The crowd’s noise swelled again, oblivious.

Tavi touched her shoulder—brief, impersonal. “Miriam.”

She shook her head. The movement dislodged a drop of sweat that had gathered at her temple. It slid down her cheek like a tear.

Eliana was laughing now, her head thrown back, the sound bright and unguarded. One of the Galilean women handed her a cup of wine. Another adjusted the fold of her new robe. Small, effortless acts of care.

Miriam turned away. Her throat ached.

A scrap of parchment fluttered near her foot, caught in a draft. She bent to pick it up automatically, her merchant’s instincts overriding the moment. The parchment was blank except for a single, smudged line of Greek: Whoever seeks to save his life will lose it.

She crumpled it in her fist.

Tavi was watching her, his expression unreadable. “We should go.”

Miriam opened her hand. The parchment fell to the ground, where the wind caught it again, carrying it toward the pool.

For a heartbeat, she considered letting it drown.

Then she walked away.

The streets of the Lower City twisted like a gutted snake, narrow and reeking of old fish and spilled wine. Miriam kept her pace measured, her gaze skimming the crowd without settling. Too many faces. Too many eyes. The legionaries had vanished, but their presence lingered in the way her shoulders tensed at every raised voice, every sudden movement.

Tavi matched her step for step, his silence more pointed than words. She could feel the weight of his disapproval like a second shadow.

A group of pilgrims jostled past, their laughter too loud, their robes brushing against Miriam’s arms. She sidestepped, her fingers twitching toward the hidden pocket where she kept her coins. Old habit. The touch of strangers still set her teeth on edge.

“You’re going the wrong way,” Tavi said.

Miriam didn’t slow. “I know.”

The admission hung between them, stark and unadorned. She should have turned east at the last intersection, toward the Essene Quarter and the relative safety of the upper room. Her feet had carried her deeper into the Lower City’s maze, where the buildings leaned close enough to blot out the fading light.

Tavi caught her elbow, steering her into the shallow alcove of a shuttered baker’s stall. The scent of yesterday’s bread clung to the wood. “Explain.”

She could have lied. Could have spun some mercantile excuse—a debt to collect, a contact to meet. The words lined up neatly in her mind, polished and plausible. But when she opened her mouth, what came out was: “They marked her.”

His grip tightened. “And?”

“And nothing.” She pulled free. “It’s just an observation.”

A donkey brayed somewhere down the alley, the sound raw and grating. A child shrieked in response, high and delighted. Normal sounds. Unremarkable sounds. The kind of noise that should have been comforting.

Miriam rubbed her wrist again. The skin still burned. “I’m not doing anything.”

“Yet.”

The word landed between them like a thrown stone.

A woman shuffled past, her arms laden with bundles of dried herbs. The sharp scent of thyme and hyssop cut through the alley’s stench. Miriam watched her go, noting the way the woman’s shoulders hunched, the way her steps quickened when a pair of Temple guards rounded the corner.

Tavi followed her gaze. His voice dropped lower. “You think you’re invisible because you’re careful. But you’re not. Not to them. Not if you start—” He broke off, jaw working.

“Start what?”

“Caring.”

The word was an accusation.

Miriam looked away. The alley’s walls were pocked with old nail holes and charcoal graffiti. Someone had scratched a crude fish into the plaster. Another had drawn a stick-figure legionary with an oversized spear. The art of the powerless—small, defiant acts of mockery that changed nothing.

A gust of wind funneled through the narrow space, carrying the distant echo of a shofar. The sound was thinner now, mournful. A call to evening prayers.

Tavi’s thumb tapped against his belt again. Once. Twice. “They’ll come for her first. You know that.”

Miriam did. Widows were soft targets. No husband to protest. No sons to retaliate. Just a woman alone in a city that had always favored the protected.

She flexed her fingers. “I’m not going to do anything.”

“But you want to.”

It wasn’t a question.

That truth coiled in her chest, hot and unfamiliar. She could lie to herself—could dress it up as curiosity, as professional interest in a novel phenomenon. But the pulse jumping in her throat betrayed her.

Such a dangerous word.

A door creaked open farther down the alley, spilling lamplight onto the uneven stones. A man’s voice called out in Aramaic, summoning someone inside. The cadence was ordinary. Domestic. The kind of moment that should have passed unnoticed.

Miriam’s breath hitched.

Tavi studied her face, his expression unreadable. Then he stepped back, yielding the path. “You’ll get us both killed.”

She almost laughed. The words were so close to what she’d told herself every day since Alexandria. Safety lay in anonymity. In silence. In never, ever standing out.

And yet.

Eliana’s face flashed in her mind again—the way she’d looked coming out of the water, radiant and unafraid. The way she’d touched Miriam’s wrist, as if she already knew the shape of the hunger coiled beneath her ribs.

Miriam took a step forward. Then another.

Tavi didn’t follow.

The alley curved, then split. Miriam hesitated, her mercantile memory supplying a rough map of the quarter. Eliana had mentioned the Lower City. Had implied she lived nearby. But Jerusalem was a warren, and dusk was settling fast, turning the air blue and indistinct.

A child darted past, nearly colliding with her. Miriam caught the boy by the shoulder, steadying him. He grinned up at her, gap-toothed and unafraid, before wriggling free and vanishing into the gloom.

Her hand tingled where she’d touched him.

A voice drifted from an open window above her—a woman singing softly in Greek. A lullaby. The melody was familiar, one Miriam’s mother had hummed when she was small. A memory rose unbidden, sharp as a knife between the ribs.

She quickened her pace.

The street widened slightly, opening onto a cramped courtyard where a handful of women clustered around a communal oven. The firelight painted their faces in gold and shadow. They spoke in low tones, their words punctuated by the rhythmic slap of dough against stone.

Miriam slowed, scanning the group. No Eliana.

One of the women noticed her standing there. She wiped her hands on her apron and called out in Aramaic. “You lost?”

Miriam shook her head. The motion felt stiff, unnatural. “Looking for someone.”

The woman’s gaze sharpened. She said something to her companions, too low for Miriam to catch. Two of the women exchanged glances. A third frowned.

Miriam’s pulse thudded in her throat. She should leave. Should turn around and walk away before the questions started. Before they remembered her face.

But then the first woman stepped forward, wiping flour from her fingers. “You’re the Alexandrian.”

Not a question.

Miriam’s mouth went dry.

The woman studied her for a long moment. Then she jerked her chin toward a narrow passage on the far side of the courtyard. “Try the house with the broken hinge. Third door.”

Miriam’s breath caught. She hadn’t said Eliana’s name. Hadn’t described her.

The woman turned back to her dough, her message delivered. The others followed suit, their postures easing back into the familiar rhythm of work. As if Miriam’s presence was unremarkable. As if they hadn’t just handed her a secret.

The passage was darker than the alley, the walls close enough to brush her shoulders. Miriam counted doors. One. Two.

The third hung slightly askew, its hinge rusted through. A faint glow seeped through the cracks around the frame.

Miriam raised her hand. Hesitated.

This was the moment to turn back. To return to the upper room, to the safety of silence and strategic distance. To be smart.

Her knuckles brushed the wood.

The door swung inward before she could knock.

Eliana stood there, her hair still damp at the ends, her face illuminated by the flickering light of a single oil lamp. She didn’t look surprised.

“Took you long enough,” she said.

Behind her, the lamplight guttered.

Somewhere in the city, a dog barked.

Miriam stepped across the threshold.
