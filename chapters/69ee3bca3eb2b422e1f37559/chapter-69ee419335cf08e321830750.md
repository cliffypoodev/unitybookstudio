The wax tablet lay open on the table, its surface crowded with names in Miriam’s precise hand. The priest traced a finger down the first column, lips moving silently as he counted. The oil lamp flickered, casting long shadows across the austere chamber. Cedar and dust. The scent of old scrolls and older power.

“Seventy-three,” he said.

The scribe in the corner shifted his weight. “From the first day alone. More have come since.”

The priest tapped the tablet. “This is the woman’s work? The Alexandrian?”

“She writes a clean hand. Multiple scripts.”

A dry chuckle. “Useful.”

Beyond the chamber, the distant murmur of the Temple courts carried through the stone—pilgrims, vendors, the occasional shout of a guard. The priest leaned back, fingers steepled. The list was more than names. It was a map. A man could disappear into a crowd, but a name on wax? That was a hook. That was a leash.

“Cyrene, Pontus, Cappadocia.” He read the origins aloud like a merchant assessing inventory. “Not just Galileans. Not just the poor.”

The scribe cleared his throat. “There’s a pattern.”

“I see it.” The priest cut him off with a raised hand. He didn’t need the scribe’s halting analysis. The pattern was obvious—deliberate decentralization. No single location to raid, no leader to isolate. A hydra strategy.

He pushed the tablet aside and reached for a fresh sheet of papyrus. The reed pen scratched as he wrote, the ink dark and sure.

“Copy the names. Separate lists by origin. Note any with ties to the Temple or the Greek synagogues.”

The scribe hesitated. “The High Priest—”

“Will want this before the evening sacrifice.” The priest didn’t look up. “And send word to the Antonia. Discreetly.”

The scribe’s sandals whispered against the stone as he retreated. The priest exhaled, rolling the stiffness from his shoulders. The lamp guttered, the flame drowning in its own wax. He watched it die.

A knock at the door.

“Enter.”

A young Levite stepped inside, his robe still damp at the hem from the purification baths. “They’re gathering again. Near Solomon’s Portico.”

The priest didn’t move. “How many?”

“More than yesterday. Fewer women. More—” The Levite swallowed. “More Romans.”

That got his attention. “Legionaries?”

“A centurion. Two of his men. They stood at the back. Listened.”

The priest’s fingers tightened around the pen. A misstep. The wrong kind of attention. He’d seen it before—a spark in dry tinder, a riot, and then the inevitable crackdown that spared no one.

“Find Tavi.”

The Levite blinked. “The interpreter?”

“Tell him to watch the centurion. Note every interaction. If the Romans are asking questions, I want to know which ones.”

The Levite bowed and slipped out. The priest stared at the half-written letter. The ink had bled, blurring the word “containment.” He crumpled the papyrus and reached for another sheet.

Outside, the shofar sounded, calling the faithful to prayer. The priest ignored it. Let them chant their psalms. Let them bow their heads. He had work to do.

The courtyard of the gathering house hummed with low voices. Miriam sat on the edge of the cistern, her feet dangling above the water. The list was finished, the names recorded, but her fingers still hitched for the stylus. Old habits.

Eliana knelt beside her, grinding herbs in a stone mortar. The scent of rosemary and myrrh sharpened the air.

“You’re quiet,” Eliana said.

Miriam watched a beetle crawl across the sun-warmed stone. “They’ll use the list.”

“Who will?”

“The ones who make lists.”

Eliana’s hands stilled. “You think they’re watching?”

Miriam didn’t answer. The beetle had reached a crack in the stone. It hesitated, antennae twitching, then vanished into the shadow.

A burst of laughter from the far side of the courtyard. Peter stood with a group of men, his gestures broad, his voice carrying. The Roman centurion was gone, but his presence lingered—a tension in the shoulders of the men, a too-loud joke, a hand straying to where a weapon might hang.

Miriam rubbed her thumb against the splinter wound. A tiny scab. A tiny pain.

“They’re always watching,” she said.

Eliana followed her gaze. “Then we give them nothing to see.”

“You don’t hide.”

“No.” Eliana’s smile was small, private. “But I don’t flaunt.”

The mortar scraped against stone as she resumed her work. The rhythm of it—crush, turn, crush—matched the pulse in Miriam’s temples.

A child darted past, chasing a scrap of cloth caught in the wind. The women at the well called to each other in a dialect Miriam didn’t know. Ordinary sounds. Ordinary moments.

The list was out of her hands now.

She stood, brushing the dust from her tunic. “I should check the storeroom.”

Eliana caught her wrist. “Later.”

Her grip was firm, her palm rough with calluses. Miriam could have pulled away. She didn’t.

“Sit,” Eliana said. “The work will keep.”

The beetle reappeared, crawling out of the crack. It hesitated at the edge of the sunlight, then scuttled back into the dark.

Miriam sat.

The runner found Tavi at the Damascus Gate, haggling with a spice merchant over the price of saffron. The merchant’s hands fluttered like startled birds, his voice rising in protest.

Tavi didn’t turn as the runner approached. “I’m occupied.”

“The priest wants you.” The runner shifted from foot to foot. “At the Shops of Annas. Now.”

Tavi sighed. He tossed a coin to the merchant and took the small bundle without waiting for change. The merchant’s outrage followed him down the street.

The Shops of Annas loomed ahead, their upper windows overlooking the Temple courts. Tavi took the stairs two at a time. The priest waited at a table by the window, a cup of wine untouched at his elbow.

“You took your time,” the priest said.

Tavi set the saffron on the table. “I was securing your bribes.”

The priest ignored the jab. He pushed a sheet of papyrus across the table. Names. Dozens of them.

“You know these people.”

Tavi scanned the list. “Some.”

“The centurion?”

“Longinus. Tribune. Not a man to cross.”

“Is he sympathetic?”

Tavi laughed. “To us? No. To order? Absolutely.”

The priest steepled his fingers. “And the woman? The Alexandrian?”

Miriam’s name leapt from the page. Tavi kept his face blank. “A scribe. Useful.”

“Is she one of them?”

“Does it matter?”

The priest’s smile was thin. “It always matters.”

Outside, the sun dipped toward the western hills. The first stars would soon appear. Tavi rolled the papyrus and tucked it into his belt.

“I’ll watch the tribune,” he said.

“And the woman.”

Tavi met the priest’s gaze. “Of course.”

The wine cup sat between them, untouched. The priest finally reached for it, his sleeve brushing the surface. A ripple. A tremor.

“Go,” he said.

Tavi went.

The Upper Room hummed like a beehive at dusk, bodies pressed close, voices layered in a dozen tongues. Miriam stood near the wall, her back to the cool plaster, and let the noise wash over her. A Parthian woman laughed, the sound bright as a copper bell. A Cyrenian man argued good-naturedly with a Cappadocian over the proper pronunciation of some Aramaic phrase. The air smelled of sweat and lamp oil and the flatbread someone had brought up from the market. Ordinary. Alive.

Her thumb tapped against her index finger, a silent count.

Peter stood at the center, his fisherman’s hands gesturing as he spoke. The others leaned in, their faces alight. Miriam caught fragments—something about Moses, something about prophecy—but the words blurred together. She focused instead on the way the lamplight caught the silver in Peter’s beard, the way his tunic stretched tight across his shoulders when he raised his arms. A man who had once mended nets now wove something far more fragile.

A hand touched her elbow.

She turned. Tavi stood beside her, his face carefully neutral. He held out a scrap of papyrus, folded small.

“For you,” he said.

She didn’t take it. “From?”

“The priest.”

Her stomach clenched. The beetle scuttling back into shadow. She unfolded the note. Her name. Her origin. A single line beneath: Your services are required at the Temple archives tomorrow. Dawn.

No request. No please.

She crumpled the papyrus in her fist. “I’m not his scribe.”

Tavi’s gaze flicked to the room, to the faces turned toward Peter. “He thinks you are.”

“Then he’s mistaken.”

A muscle jumped in Tavi’s jaw. He stepped closer, his voice dropping. “They have the list, Miriam. Every name. Every origin. Yours is on it.”

The tapping of her thumb stilled. She had known this would come. Had prepared for it.

Peter’s voice rose above the murmur. “—and the Lord added to their number daily—”

A cheer went up. Hands clapped. The Parthian woman wiped her eyes.

Miriam exhaled. “What does he want?”

“Translation work. Official records.” Tavi’s tone was light, but his eyes were dark. “He’ll pay.”

She almost laughed. As if coin could cover this. As if she were still the woman who had stepped off the Alexandrian ship two months ago, her brother’s death a fresh wound, her only goal to pass unseen.

Peter was praying now, his voice a rumble. The others bowed their heads. Miriam kept hers up.

“Tell him I’ll come,” she said.

Tavi didn’t move. “You don’t have to.”

“I know.”

The prayer ended. The room stirred, people shifting, stretching, breaking into smaller groups. A Galilean woman began singing, her voice clear and strong. Others joined in.

Tavi leaned in. “Be careful.”

Miriam watched the singers. The way their hands touched their hearts, their throats. The way their bodies swayed, unselfconscious. “I always am.”

He left without another word, slipping through the crowd like a shadow.

The song changed, the rhythm quickening. Someone clapped. Someone laughed. Miriam stayed by the wall.

A child bumped into her, a little girl with olive skin and wild curls. She grinned up at Miriam, her teeth small and white. “Dance with me!”

Miriam shook her head. “I don’t—”

The girl grabbed her hand and pulled. “Come on!”

The room spun. The music swelled. Miriam let herself be dragged into the circle, her feet stumbling at first, then finding the rhythm. The girl’s hand was warm and sticky in hers. The Parthian woman winked at her across the circle.

Just for a moment, she forgot the list.

Then the door creaked open.

The music faltered. The dancing slowed. A hush rippled through the room.

Two men stood in the doorway, their robes the fine linen of Temple servants. They scanned the crowd, their faces impassive.

Peter stepped forward. “Brothers—”

“The High Priest requests your presence,” the taller one said. His voice carried. “Tomorrow. After the morning sacrifice.”

No please.

The room held its breath. Peter’s shoulders tensed, then relaxed. “We’ll be honored.”

The men nodded and left. The door closed behind them with a soft click.

The silence stretched.

Then, from the back of the room, a voice: “They’re counting us.”

A murmur. A rustle. The joy of moments ago had drained away, replaced by something sharper.

John touched Peter’s arm. “We should—”

“Not here,” Peter said.

Miriam looked down. The little girl still clung to her hand, her grip tight.

“Are they bad men?” the girl whispered.

Miriam hesitated. “No,” she said at last. “Just men.”

The girl frowned, unsatisfied.

Across the room, the apostles had drawn together, their heads bent. James said something low and urgent. Andrew shook his head. Peter rubbed his beard, his gaze distant.

Miriam knew that look. She had seen it in the markets of Alexandria, in the faces of men weighing risk against profit.

The Parthian woman sighed and picked up her shawl. “I should go.”

Others began gathering their things, murmuring excuses. The room emptied by degrees, the laughter replaced by whispers, the music by the shuffle of sandals on stone.

The little girl tugged Miriam’s hand. “Will you come back?”

Miriam looked at the apostles, at their tense shoulders and lowered voices. At the way Peter’s hand curled into a fist, then released.

“Yes,” she said.

She wasn’t sure if it was a lie.

The girl beamed and skipped away, her curls bouncing. Miriam watched her go, then turned toward the door.

Eliana intercepted her, a bundle of linen in her arms. “Here. The Pella merchant’s goods.”

Miriam took the bundle automatically. The fabric was smooth under her fingers, the weave tight. “Thank you.”

Eliana studied her face. “You’re going to the Temple tomorrow.”

A statement, not a question.

Miriam adjusted her grip on the linen. “Yes.”

“Be careful what you translate.”

Eliana’s smile was thin. “Do you?”

Outside, the first stars pricked the sky. The city sighed and settled into evening. Somewhere, a donkey brayed. Somewhere, a child cried.

Miriam shifted the linen in her arms. The weight of it, the reality. “I’ll see you tomorrow.”

Eliana touched her wrist, just a brush of fingers. “If the Lord wills.”

A chill skittered down Miriam’s spine. She stepped into the street, the bundle held close.

Behind her, the door of the Upper Room closed with a soft, final sound.

The streets of Jerusalem at dawn smelled of baking bread and night soil. Miriam walked with her head down, the bundle of Pella linens tucked under one arm. The Temple loomed ahead, its gold-capped pinnacles catching the first light. She counted her steps—a habit from childhood, a way to measure distance when the streets of Alexandria grew too loud.

A pair of Levites passed her, their robes whispering against the stones. One glanced at her, then away. She knew that look. The assessment. The calculation.

The archives chamber was cooler than the street, its stone walls holding the night’s chill. A scribe she didn’t recognize sat at the far table, his stylus scratching across a wax tablet. He didn’t look up when she entered.

The priest was waiting.

He stood by the window, his back to her, watching the first worshippers trickle into the courts. “You’re late.”

Miriam set the linens on a bench. “Dawn is dawn.”

He turned. His fingers tapped against a scroll case—a slow, deliberate rhythm. “The High Priest wants a record of all diaspora pilgrims. Names. Origins. Length of stay.” He pushed the case toward her. “You’ll transcribe the Greek and Latin petitions first.”

She didn’t reach for it. “I’m not a Temple scribe.”

The unspoken words hung between them. The ones whose names were on the list. The ones gathering in the Upper Room.

She took the scroll case.

The work was mindless. Petition after petition, each one a variation of the same plea—for justice, for healing, for a son’s safe voyage home. She copied them without reading, her hand moving automatically. The scribe at the far table coughed, then coughed again, a wet sound.

Outside, the city woke. The murmur of the crowd swelled, punctuated by the occasional shout of a vendor or the bleat of a sacrificial lamb.

The priest returned at midday. He unrolled her work, his eyes skimming the lines. “Adequate.”

She set down the stylus. Her fingers ached. “Is there more?”

He rolled the scroll again. “The centurion. Longinus. Have you seen him among them?”

She kept her face still. “I don’t know the man.”

“He was at the Portico yesterday. Listening.”

“Many people listen.”

The priest’s gaze sharpened. “Not like that.”

A shadow crossed the doorway. Tavi leaned against the frame, his arms crossed. “The Syrian caravans are arriving. You wanted the tax rolls updated.”

The priest sighed. “Later.”

Tavi’s eyes flicked to Miriam, then away. “The Egyptian envoy is waiting.”

“Fine.” The priest tossed the scroll onto the table. “Finish the petitions. I’ll send for you when I need you.”

He left without waiting for a reply.

Tavi lingered. “You should go.”

She rubbed her thumb against the stylus groove on her finger. “I have work.”

“Not here.”

She looked up. His jaw was tight, his shoulders tense. A warning.

She stood.

The streets were louder now, thick with pilgrims and merchants. A Roman patrol moved through the crowd, their shields glinting in the sun. Miriam kept her pace even, her gaze forward.

The Essene Quarter was quieter, the houses smaller, the streets narrower. She turned a corner and stopped.

Eliana sat on a low wall, a basket of herbs beside her. She didn’t look up as Miriam approached. “You’re late.”

Miriam sat next to her. “I was detained.”

Eliana plucked a sprig of thyme, rolling it between her fingers. “They’re watching the house.”

Miriam followed her gaze. Two men loitered near the well, their robes too fine for the neighborhood. Temple servants.

Eliana’s voice was low. “Peter left at first light. James and John with him.”

“For the High Priest?”

“Yes.”

Miriam watched the men by the well. One laughed, the sound too loud for the quiet street.

Eliana tucked the thyme into her basket. “You should go. Back to Alexandria.”

“No.”

“They have your name.”

“They’ve always had my name.”

Eliana turned to her. “This is different.”

The sun was high now, the shadows short. Somewhere, a child laughed. A door creaked open.

Miriam stood. “I’m staying.”

Eliana studied her face. Then she nodded once and stood with her. “Then we don’t have much time.”

They walked together, the basket between them, the herbs scenting the air. The men by the well watched them pass.

Miriam didn’t look back.

The Upper Room was empty when they arrived, the floor strewn with crumbs and discarded cups. The air still held the ghost of last night’s gathering—oil and sweat and song.

Eliana moved to the far wall, pressing her palm against a loose stone. It shifted with a soft scrape. “Here.”

Miriam knelt beside her. The hollow behind the stone held a small bundle of letters, a purse, a knife.

Eliana took the knife and pressed it into Miriam’s hand. “For the road.”

Miriam looked at it. “I’m not leaving.”

Eliana’s grip tightened. “Then for when they come.”

The metal was cool against her palm. The edge sharp.

Outside, a shout. A crash. The sound of running feet.

Eliana’s breath hitched. “Too soon.”

Miriam stood, the knife hidden in her sleeve. She stepped to the window.

The street below was chaos. Temple guards shoved through the crowd, their clubs out. A woman screamed. A child cried.

And at the center of it all, Peter, his arms raised, his voice carrying above the noise. “Peace! Brothers, peace!”

A guard grabbed his arm.

Miriam’s hand tightened around the knife.

Eliana touched her shoulder. “Wait.”

She didn’t.

She was out the door before Eliana could stop her, down the stairs, into the press of bodies. The crowd surged around her, a living thing, all heat and panic.

Peter saw her. His eyes widened. Then he shook his head, sharp, a warning.

She stopped.

The guards dragged him away, his followers scattering before them. The street emptied as quickly as it had filled, leaving only scattered sandals, a trampled basket, the distant echo of shouts.

Miriam stood alone in the sudden silence.

The sun climbed higher, the light sharpening. Somewhere, a shofar sounded.

She turned her face toward the Temple Mount and didn’t flinch.
