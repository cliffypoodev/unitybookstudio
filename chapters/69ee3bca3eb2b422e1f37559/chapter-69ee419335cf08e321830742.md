The Levite recoiled as if struck. His mouth opened, but no sound came out—only a thin, reedy wheeze. A fire above Peter didn’t flicker. It pulsed, steady as a blacksmith’s forge.

Miriam’s fingers found the wall behind her. A stone was warm. Not from the sun; it shouldn’t be this warm, not in the shade of the pottery cart. Her thumb tapped against her knuckle, a silent count. One. Two. Three. The rhythm stuttered when Peter spoke again, his voice lower now, the Parthian syllables rolling like river stones.

“You call this dishonor?” The words weren’t his. They couldn’t be. The accent was too precise, the vowels rounded in a way no Galilean fisherman could mimic. “You stand in the court of thieves and call the spirit of prophecy a drunkard’s babble?”

The Levite’s hands fluttered to his chest, clutching at the embroidered hem of his robe. His lips moved, shaping silent words, a prayer or a curse. The crowd pressed closer. A woman near the front, her headscarf slipping, reached out as if to touch the air between Peter and the Levite. Her fingers trembled.

John’s voice cut through, clear and bright in Greek. “This is what was spoken by the prophet Joel.” Not recitation. Not memory. The words unfolded like a scroll inside him, fresh ink still gleaming. “‘In the last days, God says, I will pour out my spirit on all people.’”

A man in the crowd gasped. “That’s—that’s not the text. The Septuagint says ‘servants,’ not ‘people.’”

“Does it?” John turned toward him, and the fire above his head flared white. “Or have you been reading a copy that forgot the daughters?”

Miriam’s breath caught. The air tasted metallic, like the moment before a storm. The Roman soldiers had straightened against the wall, hands drifting toward their swords. Not alarm yet, just the instinctive tension of men who sensed a crowd tilting toward something they couldn’t control.

Peter took another step toward the Levite. One man stumbled back, his heel catching on the uneven stones. No one moved to help him. The crowd was a single held breath.

“You.” Peter pointed at the Levite, his calloused finger inches from the man’s face. “You who count the coins in the Temple treasury—do you know whose face is stamped on them? Caesar’s. You dare call me unclean?”

A murmur rippled through the crowd. Too close to sedition. Too close to the kind of talk that got men crucified. The Levite’s face drained of color. He looked past Peter, toward the Romans, but their faces were unreadable.

Then, movement at the edge of the crowd. A figure pushing forward, robes dusty from the road. Elior ben Levi, the Cyrenian merchant, his beard streaked with gray, his eyes wide. He stared at Peter as if seeing a ghost.

“You.” Elior’s voice cracked. “You’re speaking Berber. My mother’s tongue.”

Peter turned. The fire above him flickered, threads of gold weaving through the orange. When he answered, the words were guttural, unfamiliar, but Elior staggered as if struck. He clutched at the man beside him, his mouth working soundlessly.

The crowd erupted. Questions, shouts, a wail from somewhere near the back. A child’s high-pitched cry. The Roman soldiers shoved away from the wall, blades half-drawn.

Miriam didn’t realize she’d moved until her shoulder hit the pottery cart. The jars rattled. Her fingers were numb. The tapping had stopped.

Across the square, Tavi appeared at the mouth of an alley, his interpreter’s sash askew. His gaze locked onto Peter, then darted to the Romans, to the Levite, to the seething crowd. His lips shaped a single word, too far to hear, but Miriam knew it.

Run.

Peter didn’t run. He raised his hands, and the fire above him swelled, casting long shadows across the stones. When he spoke again, the voice wasn’t his alone. It was a chorus, a convergence, a hundred tongues woven into one.

“Hear this, all you who dwell in Jerusalem.”

The Levite fainted.

The alley stank of urine and crushed herbs. Miriam pressed herself into the shadow of a doorway, her pulse hammering in her throat. The shouts from the square had faded, but the air still hummed, a vibration just beneath hearing.

Footsteps. Quick, light. She tensed, her hand finding the knife at her belt.

Tavi rounded the corner, his sash gone, his hair damp with sweat. He skidded to a stop when he saw her. For a heartbeat, they stared at each other.

“You understood him.” Not a question.

Miriam’s thumb twitched against the knife hilt. “You didn’t.”

“I speak seven languages. That wasn’t one of them.” Tavi wiped his mouth with the back of his hand. His fingers shook. “But you—you knew. I saw your face.”

The memory rose unbidden: Peter’s voice shaping the name only she knew. Her brother’s name, in the dialect of their childhood. The one he’d whispered as the mob closed in.

She swallowed. “What happens now?”

Tavi glanced over his shoulder. The distant roar of the crowd surged, then broke into scattered cries. “Now?” A bitter laugh. “Now they arrest him. Or the crowd tears him apart.”

A sound cut him off. Not shouts. Not screams.

Singing.

It spilled from the square, raw and joyous, in a dozen languages at once. No, not a dozen. More. A hundred. A thousand. Voices layered over voices, each distinct, each harmonizing with the others in a way that shouldn’t have been possible.

Tavi’s breath left him in a rush. “Or that.”

Miriam’s knees gave out. She caught herself against the wall, the rough stone scraping her palm. The singing wrapped around her, through her, the words slipping past meaning into something deeper.

Her brother’s voice was in it. She couldn’t hear him, not clearly, but she knew.

Tavi grabbed her arm. “We have to go. Before the Romans lock down the quarter.”

She let him pull her up. The knife was a cold weight against her hip. The singing followed them, winding through the alleys, rising above the rooftops.

Somewhere ahead, a door slammed. A child laughed. The city held its breath.

The singing clung to the stones as Miriam and Tavi emerged onto the Street of the Bakers. The morning sun glared off white plaster walls, turning the air to liquid gold. A donkey brayed somewhere, the sound swallowed by the rising tide of voices from the Temple courts. Not just singing now, shouting, arguing, the rhythmic chant of a psalm breaking apart into a dozen dissonant melodies.

A woman dropped her basket of figs. The fruit burst against the cobbles, pulp bleeding into the cracks. She didn’t stoop to gather them. Her hands hovered near her ears, fingers splayed as if trying to catch something invisible.

Miriam’s sandal slid in fig juice. She caught Tavi’s sleeve. “That’s Cappadocian.”

He frowned. “What?”

“Her.” Miriam jerked her chin toward the woman. “She’s repeating a Cappadocian nursery rhyme. About—about swallows and stolen bread.” The words curled in her throat, familiar and alien at once. She hadn’t heard that rhyme since Alexandria, since the Cappadocian spice merchant’s children taught it to her brother in exchange for candied dates.

Tavi’s grip tightened on her wrist. “You understand that too?”

A boy darted past them, his tunic flapping. “They’re drunk!” he crowed, skidding to a stop near a cluster of pilgrims. “The Galileans—they’ve been at the wine since dawn!”

Laughter rippled through the group. An older man clapped the boy on the shoulder. “Go on, then. Tell us what they’re saying, if it’s just drunkards’ babble.”

The boy opened his mouth. Closed it. The laughter died.

Because the voices weren’t slurred. They weren’t the loose, rambling cadence of intoxication. They were sharp. Precise. The syllables landed like knife strikes, each word obvious to those who knew the tongue, and utterly incomprehensible to those who didn’t.

Miriam’s thumb tapped against her belt. One. Two. Three. The rhythm faltered when a man near the well threw back his head and wailed. Not in pain. In recognition. He clutched at the woman beside him, babbling in thick, guttural syllables—Phrygian maybe, or some northern dialect Miriam couldn’t place. The woman recoiled, but the man kept speaking, his voice rising, cracking, his hands sketching shapes in the air between them.

“He thinks she understands him,” Tavi murmured.

She didn’t. Miriam could see it in the woman’s panicked glance toward the Temple, in the way her fingers twisted the fringe of her shawl. But the man kept talking, his face alight with a desperate hope.

A potter’s stall collapsed as three men shoved through, their arms linked, their voices raised in a hymn Miriam had last heard in the Great Synagogue of Antioch. The melody was the same. The words weren’t. These weren’t the formal liturgical phrases; they were something wilder, freer, a river breaking its banks.

Tavi dragged her sideways as a Roman patrol rounded the corner. Eight men in polished helmets, their shields slung, their hands resting on sword hilts. The optio at the front scanned the crowd, his mouth a flat line.

“Not good,” Tavi muttered.

Miriam’s pulse jumped. She counted exits without meaning to: the alley to the left, too narrow; the baker’s doorway, barred; the steps leading up to the roof walkways—

A hand seized her elbow.

Elior ben Levi, his beard streaked with dust, his eyes red-rimmed. “You.” His breath smelled of olives and grief. “You heard it too. The—the—” He fumbled for the word, his Greek fracturing into Berber. “The voice. The one that knew my mother’s name.”

Miriam tried to pull free. His grip was iron.

“They’re not drunk,” Elior hissed. “They’re—”

The optio barked an order. The patrol split, four men moving toward the Temple, the other four fanning out through the crowd. A soldier grabbed the wailing Phrygian man by the shoulder, shaking him. The man blinked, dazed, then pointed toward the Temple courts.

“No.” Elior released Miriam, lurching forward.

Tavi yanked her back. “We’re leaving.”

The Street of the Bakers dissolved into chaos. A wine merchant overturned his own stall rather than let the Romans confiscate his amphorae. A girl no older than twelve stood on a milling stone, singing in perfect, unaccented Latin about a lamb and a thorn tree. The optio froze, his head snapping toward her.

Miriam’s knees threatened to buckle again. The girl’s song wasn’t just Latin. It was the specific, rolling cadence of the Praetorian barracks, the optio’s own dialect, the one he’d carried from the hills of Umbria.

Tavi hauled her into a side street. The shadows hid them, but the voices followed, twisting through the hot air.

“They’ll lock down the city,” he panted. “If this spreads—”

Miriam’s sandal caught on a loose stone. She stumbled, her palm scraping the wall. The rough plaster bit into her skin. The pain was sharp, clean, an anchor in the maelstrom.

A new sound cut through the din. Not singing. Not shouting.

A horn.

The Temple alarm.

Tavi went very still. “That’s the signal for blasphemy.”

Miriam’s mouth dried. She’d heard that horn once before, in Alexandria, when the mob dragged the Hellenist philosopher through the streets. A memory rose like bile: the way the man’s voice had cracked on the first syllable of his defense, the way the stones had been warm with sun and blood by the time they finished with him.

The horn blew again. Closer now.

Then the screaming started.

The horn’s second blast scattered the crowd like startled pigeons. Pilgrims surged toward the Temple gates, toward the alleys, toward any escape from the soldiers now forming a bristling line across the street. Miriam’s shoulder struck a water carrier; the jar shattered, soaking her tunic to the knee. The cold shock of it grounded her for half a pause before Tavi dragged her sideways into the lee of a date seller’s awning.

“Not that way.” His breath was hot against her ear. “The Portico.”

She resisted. “That’s where they’re herding people.”

“Exactly.” His fingers dug into her wrist. “In a stampede, hide in the stampede.”

The logic was sound. The fear in his eyes wasn’t. Miriam let him pull her into the current of bodies flowing toward Solomon’s Portico. The towering columns loomed ahead, their shadows striping the chaos. A woman clutched a screaming infant to her chest. A merchant abandoned his tray of honey cakes. The air reeked of sweat and crushed coriander.

Then, a break in the crowd. A pocket of stillness.

Peter stood at the base of the steps, flanked by John and a handful of others. No fire above them now. No visible sign at all. Just ordinary men in dust-streaked tunics, their faces turned toward the approaching soldiers.

Miriam’s thumb tapped against her thigh. One. Two. Three. The rhythm stuttered when Peter raised his hands. Not in surrender. In benediction.

Peter spoke.

The words weren’t Aramaic. They weren’t Greek.

Miriam’s knees buckled.

Parthian. But not the trade dialect she’d heard in the caravanserais. This was the thick, honeyed accent of the eastern provinces, the one her brother had mimicked to make her laugh when they were children. The one he’d used that final morning, whispering a joke as the mob gathered outside their door.

The memory struck like a fist. She couldn’t pause. Couldn’t think. The Portico tilted around her.

Tavi caught her elbow. “What? What is it?”

She couldn’t answer. Peter’s voice wrapped around her, the cadence perfect, the inflection exact. He was telling a story—no, not a story. A riddle. The same stupid, childish riddle her brother had teased her with the week before he died.

What’s always in front of you but can’t be seen?

The answer rose in her throat, unbidden. Tomorrow.

Peter’s gaze swept the crowd. Landed on her. His lips curved, just slightly, as if he’d heard her silent reply.

The Roman optio barked an order. Four men advanced, their short swords glinting. John stepped forward, his voice ringing out in flawless Latin. “You’re making a mistake.”

The lead soldier froze. Not at the words. At the accent. The pure, aristocratic lilt of the Palatine Hill, the kind no Galilean peasant could mimic.

A murmur rippled through the crowd. The optio’s face darkened. “Seize them.”

Peter didn’t move. He switched languages again—Egyptian this time, the dialect of the Delta. The words were simple. Innocuous. The river remembers its true course.

Miriam’s vision blurred. She knew that phrase. Knew it in her bones. It was the last thing her brother had said to her, pressed into her ear as he shoved her toward the cellar hiding place.

A soldier grabbed Peter’s arm.

John lunged. The crowd erupted.

A stone flew from somewhere to Miriam’s left, striking a legionary’s helmet with a hollow clang. The man staggered. His comrades closed ranks, shields locking. The optio drew his blade.

Tavi yanked her down as another stone whizzed overhead. “We need to—”

A hand closed around Miriam’s other wrist.

Elior ben Levi, his face streaked with tears. “You heard it.” His Berber was thick with emotion. “The river. You understood.”

She tried to pull free. His grip was fever-hot.

“They’re not speaking languages,” Elior whispered. “They’re speaking hearts.”

The truth of it shuddered through her. The Parthian riddle. The Egyptian phrase. Not random words, private ones. Intimate ones. The kind that lived in the quiet spaces between people who loved each other.

Peter’s voice rose above the din, clear and bright in Aramaic now. “You want signs? You want wonders? Look to your own hearts!”

A soldier swung his shield. Peter went down hard. John cried out, reaching for him. The crowd surged forward, a wordless roar building in their throats.

Miriam didn’t realize she’d moved until she was halfway to the steps.

Tavi’s shout faded behind her. The world narrowed to Peter’s crumpled form, to the soldier raising his sword hilt for another blow, to the sudden, searing certainty in her chest.

She didn’t pray. Didn’t think. Just acted.

“Run,” she gasped.

He blinked at her, dazed. Blood trickled from his temple. When he spoke, the words were in her brother’s voice.

“You were always the brave one.”

A sword hilt came down on her shoulder. White pain exploded across her vision. She fell, the stones rushing up to meet her.

The last thing she heard was Elior screaming, not in Greek, not in Berber, but in the raw, wordless cry of a man who’d just seen something impossible.

Then the darkness took her.
