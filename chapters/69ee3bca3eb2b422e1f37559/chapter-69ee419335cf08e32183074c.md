The curtain shifted again.

This time it was Eliana, carrying a wide shallow bowl that steamed in the cool air of the upper room. The widow’s face was flushed from the climb, her movements efficient. She did not look at Miriam, not directly. Her focus was on the low table, clearing a space among the shards with a gentle sweep of her arm. The pottery scraped. That sound was a small violence in the quiet.

“They’re bringing the rest,” Eliana said, her voice pitched for the room, not for Miriam. She set the bowl down. It held a mash of lentils, glistening with a film of oil and speckled with cumin. The smell unspooled—earthy, sharp, a thread of garlic. It was the smell of a hundred common meals in a hundred poor homes. It did not belong here, among the records of a rebellion.

Miriam’s stomach tightened. She kept her eyes on the last shard she’d inscribed, her stylus poised. The arrival of food was a logistical problem. It meant bodies, proximity, the end of her useful isolation. She could hear them on the stairs now—a clatter of feet, the murmur of voices layered like felt. One room, which had held only the ghost of the two latecomers and the chill of her own vigilance, began to fill.

They came in clusters. That woman from Cyprus with sun-creased eyes. The Phrygian wool-carder, his fingers still stained blue. The young couple from Perea who held hands as if afraid the floor would give way. They carried clay pots, a basket of flatbreads, a skin of wine. They arranged themselves on the floor, on stools, against the walls. A space that had felt cavernous with moonlight shrank under the press of them. The air grew warm, thick with the scent of unwashed wool and human breath and lentils.

Miriam did not move from her table. It was an island. The lists were her anchor.

Peter entered last, ducking through the curtain. He looked older in the dim light, the lines around his eyes cut deep. He surveyed the gathering, his gaze passing over Miriam as it passed over the wall behind her—an acknowledgment of presence, nothing more. He lowered himself to the floor near the center, his joints cracking. A space opened around him, not from reverence, Miriam thought, but from the sheer gravitational weariness the man carried.

“We break bread together,” Peter said. No preamble. His voice was hoarse, stripped of its public thunder. It was just a statement of fact, worn smooth. “As he did with us.”

A rustle passed through the room. Not excitement. A collective settling, like birds on a branch adjusting their weight. The Phrygian wool-carder began to pray aloud in halting Aramaic, thanking God for the food, for the fellowship, for the spirit that had gathered them. His grammar was terrible. He used the wrong preposition for “with.” Miriam’s translator’s mind caught the error, filed it, then tried to dismiss it. The prayer was not for grammatical review.

She watched hands reach. Not a chaotic grab, but a slow, deliberate passing. The basket of bread went from the Cypriot woman to the Perean boy, who tore a piece with a soft crackle and handed it on. He did not take for himself first. He gave. The observation lodged in Miriam’s chest, a small, hard stone of discomfort.

This was not how it was done. In Alexandria, in any city, you secured your portion. You negotiated. You calculated the social debt of accepting. This silent, trusting transfer was alien. Vulnerable.

Eliana stood, wiping her hands on her robe. She picked up the wine skin and a single, coarse cup. She moved toward Miriam’s table.

The island was about to be breached.

Miriam’s fingers found the stylus again, her thumb rubbing the smoothed groove where her grip had worn it. A useless gesture. There was nothing to write.

“You must eat,” Eliana said, stopping an arm’s length away. Not a question. Her eyes were on the lists, not on Miriam’s face.

“The work isn’t finished.”

“The work is feeding the workers.” Eliana poured wine into the cup. The liquid was dark, almost purple in the shadowed light. It sloshed, a single wave against the clay. “The bread is for all. Even the scribes.”

A flicker of annoyance, hot and sudden. Miriam pressed it down. “I am not hungry.”

“That is a lie.” The widow’s voice was soft, matter-of-fact. She said it the way she might note a crack in a pot. She held out the cup.

The refusal formed on Miriam’s tongue, clean and professional. I do not drink from a common cup. The words died. To say them would be to name the wall, to make it visible. It would be another kind of vulnerability. She took the cup. The clay was warm from Eliana’s hand. She brought it to her lips without drinking, a pantomime of participation. The smell was sour, tannic.

Eliana didn’t move. She waited, her gaze finally lifting to Miriam’s. It was a patient look, empty of challenge. It simply held space.

Miriam drank. The wine was rough, scraping her throat. She handed the cup back.

Eliana took it, her fingers brushing Miriam’s. A fleeting, dry contact. “The bread,” she said, and turned toward the basket now making its slow circuit toward their corner of the room.

Miriam exhaled. The taste of the wine lingered, astringent. She watched the basket’s progress. It came from the wool-carder, to a girl with a patched tunic, to a stooped man Miriam recognized from the list as a Levite’s servant named Seth. Each person tore. Each passed. No one measured. No one eyed the remaining size.

When the basket reached her, it was half empty. The flatbreads were stacked, uneven, their edges broken. She looked at them. This was the moment. To take was to enter the economy of the room. To refuse was a declaration of separation.

Her hand moved without her mind’s permission. She took the top piece. Cooler than she expected, pliant. She did not tear it. She held the whole piece, awkward, too large.

Eliana took the basket from her. The widow tore a fragment for herself, small as a coin, and placed the basket on the edge of Miriam’s table. Then she sat on the floor, right there, her back against the table leg. She ate her tiny piece, her eyes closing for a second.

Trapped. Miriam was trapped by the woman’s quiet, unassuming presence. She could not stand and walk away without stepping over her. She remained on her stool, the bread a weight in her hand. Around the room, the soft sounds of eating. The scrape of a bowl. A low murmur of conversation. The Phrygian man was laughing at something, a short, surprised bark.

Peter was speaking again, his voice a low rumble. He was not preaching. He was telling a story. About a hillside, and a crowd, and a boy’s lunch of barley loaves. He spoke in words plain and direct. He described the texture of the barley bread—coarse, dry. He described the way the boy had offered it, not to the teacher, but to the disciple Andrew, as if Andrew were the safe intermediary.

Miriam looked down at the bread in her hand. Barley. The cheaper grain. The peasant’s loaf.

She brought it to her mouth. Tore a piece with her teeth. Stale, chewy. It required work. She focused on the mechanical act—jaw moving, saliva mixing with the crumb. It was a thing to be processed. Not a symbol.

But the story seeped in anyway. Peter was talking about the fragments left over. Twelve baskets. “Not because there was magic in the bread,” he said, his voice dropping. “But because sharing makes the scarcity a lie. The lie is that there is not enough. The truth is we hoard what we think is only ours.”

A man near the curtain coughed. Someone shifted, their sandal scraping plaster. Miriam’s throat tightened. The bread was a dry lump. She could not swallow.

She had hoarded her name, her story, her grief. Kept them in a separate account, a ledger no one else could audit. This room, this passing of a basket, was a forced audit.

Eliana leaned her head back against the table, looking up at the ceiling. “The lentils are good,” she said, to no one. To the room. To Miriam. “Adele from Cyprus knows her spices.”

Such a mundane thing to say. So utterly irrelevant to prophecies and spirits and fragments. Miriam stared at the profile of the woman’s face, the line of her neck where a tendon stood out. Eliana was not wrestling with cosmic truths. She was tasting lentils.

The simple normality of it was a greater pressure than any sermon.

Miriam forced the bread down. She reached for the wine skin Eliana had left on the table, poured a scant mouthful into the cup, and drank it to clear her throat. The second taste was softer. Less sour.

“He is right, you know,” Eliana said, her eyes still on the ceiling. “About the hoarding.”

“I have hoarded nothing,” Miriam said. The defense was automatic, sharp.

Eliana turned her head. Her smile was faint, tired. “You have hoarded yourself. It is the most exhausting thing to keep.”

The words landed with the quiet precision of a physician’s probe. Miriam had no answer. She looked away, toward Peter. He had finished his story. He was sitting with his hands loose in his lap, watching the people eat. His face held no triumph. Only a profound, unguarded fatigue. The mask was gone. Here, in this crowded, stuffy room, he was just a tired fisherman surrounded by strangers sharing his food.

The basket on the table beside her seemed to pulse in her peripheral vision. An accusation. An invitation.

Her hand moved again. She tore her piece of barley bread in two. The inside was whiter, softer. She held out the smaller half toward Eliana, who still sat on the floor.

Eliana looked at the offered bread, then at Miriam’s face. She did not take it immediately. Her head tilted, as if studying a text in an unfamiliar script. Then she reached up and accepted it. Her fingers did not brush Miriam’s this time. They closed around the bread with a deliberate care.

“Thank you,” Eliana said. Not for the bread. For the breaking.

Miriam put the other half in her mouth. She chewed. She tasted the grain—a faint, nutty sweetness beneath the dust. She swallowed. That stone in her chest was still there, but it had changed shape. It was no longer just discomfort.

It was the terrifying, specific weight of a shared loaf.

The meal settled into a rhythm of quiet sounds. The scrape of spoons against clay, the wet click of chewing, the low hum of conversations that didn’t need to carry. Miriam watched the patterns. The Cypriot woman, Adele, leaned toward the Phrygian wool-carder and said something that made him grin, showing a missing tooth. He replied in a mash of Aramaic and his own dialect, his hands sketching shapes in the air—a sheep, a cloud, a wide gesture that might have been a valley. She nodded, understanding not the words but the shape of the story. They were building a bridge of mismatched stones, and it held.

This was the thing that unnerved her. Not the doctrine, but the fluency of this other language. The grammar of shared food. The syntax of proximity. She had spent her life mastering the rules of separation—the correct distance in a marketplace, the precise tone for a negotiation, the careful veil of the eyes that said I see you, but we will not truly meet. Here, the rules were inverted. Every broken piece of bread was a clause in a sentence she couldn’t parse.

Eliana remained against the table leg, a solid warmth against Miriam’s calf. The widow finished her lentils, set the bowl aside, and began to clean her spoon with the edge of her shawl. A practical, unhurried motion. The simplicity of it was a mirror, and in it Miriam saw her own complexity as a kind of gaudy, useless ornament.

Peter had not moved from his spot on the floor. A young man with a patchy beard—one of the Pereans—leaned in and asked him a question too low for Miriam to hear. Peter listened, his head bowed. He didn’t answer immediately. He picked up a crumb from the floor, rolled it between his thumb and forefinger. When he spoke, his voice was so quiet the room seemed to lean in to catch it.

“He told us to love our enemies,” Peter said. “I argued with him about that. I said it was foolish. A strategy for defeat.” He paused, flicked the crumb away. “He asked me what victory looked like. I had no answer. Only a list of people I wanted to see humiliated.”

A ripple through the listeners. Not agreement, not disagreement. A recognition. The Perean boy looked at his own hands, clenched in his lap.

Miriam’s mind supplied the translation: Love your enemies was a catastrophic business model. It dissolved the ledger. It turned a debt of hatred into a gift with no return expected. It was the opposite of every calculation that had kept her alive since the riots. To love the men who had smashed her brother’s skull would be to surrender the last piece of him she owned—her righteous, ferocious grief. That grief was a sanctuary. To leave it would be a second death.

Her breath hitched. She disguised it by reaching for the wine skin again. Her hand trembled. A single, betraying shake. She poured, spilling a drop that bloomed purple on the pale wood.

Eliana saw it. The widow didn’t comment. She just began to hum, a tuneless thread of sound, as she folded her shawl into a neat square. The humming was a privacy screen. A bizarre, merciful act of pretending not to notice.

The conversation around Peter deepened, voices weaving together. A woman from Joppa spoke of a Roman centurion who had paid for the repair of her roof after his men damaged it during a riot. “I wanted to hate him,” she said, her voice rough. “It would have been easier. But he looked ashamed. A soldier, ashamed. It confused me.”

“What did you do?” someone asked.

“I thanked him.” She let out a short, sharp breath. “It tasted like vinegar in my mouth. But I said the words.”

Miriam’s brother’s face flashed behind her eyes. Not as he was in life, smiling and holding a reed pen, but as he was in death—a configuration of angles and shadows on a stone floor. The men who did it had not looked ashamed. They had laughed. Their laughter had been a sound without language, a pure, animal noise of triumph. There was no transaction possible with that sound. No thanks to give. Only the eternal, silent column of her loss.

The room was too warm. The air, thick with food and breath, pressed against her skin. She needed the list. The clean, sharp authority of names and numbers. She turned on her stool, pulled a fresh shard toward her, dipped her stylus. The ink was drying, gummy. She scratched a mark. It blobbed. A failure.

“You keep a different record.”

Peter’s voice. He wasn’t looking at her. He was speaking to the room, but the words landed at her table with perfect aim.

She froze, stylus poised. “Someone must,” she said, her own voice sounding strange, too formal.

“Yes.” He finally turned his head. His eyes in the lamplight were the color of worn slate. They held no accusation. Only a deep, weary curiosity. “But which tally matters? The one that counts what is taken, or the one that counts what is given?”

It was not a rhetorical question. He waited. The room’s attention, a soft, collective presence, settled on her. She felt it like a shift in atmospheric pressure. This was visibility. This was the exact thing she had sworn to avoid.

“A merchant must know both,” she said, the merchant’s mask snapping into place. It was a good mask. It had worked in Alexandria, in Antioch, in the marble halls of wholesalers. “Or she goes bankrupt.”

Peter considered this. He nodded slowly. “Bankrupt. An empty purse.” He looked at his own hands, calloused and broad. “What if the currency changes? What if the market is not for goods, but for grace? How does one account for that?”

Laughter bubbled up from the wool-carder. It was a startled, joyful sound. “You cannot put grace in a purse, fisherman! It leaks out the seams!”

A few smiles, a murmur of agreement. The tension broke, but the question hung in the air, a hook in Miriam’s mind.

Peter’s gaze never left her. He seemed to be listening to a conversation happening just beneath the one in the room. “You have lost someone,” he said, quiet.

The statement was a blade slipped between her ribs. Clinical. Precise. The air left her lungs. She could not hide the physical reaction—the slight jerk of her shoulders, the widening of her eyes. Her mask, so reliable, showed a crack.

Eliana, still on the floor, went very still. She stopped folding.

Miriam’s translator’s mind scrambled. Denial was the protocol. I have lost nothing. I am here on business. The words were ash in her mouth. She could not force them out. One truth, named aloud in this room of strangers, had a monstrous solidity. It sat between her and the man across the room, a third presence.

“We all have,” the woman from Joppa said, her voice gentle, trying to throw a blanket over the exposure.

“Yes,” Peter agreed. But he was not talking to the woman. He was still talking to Miriam. “But some losses become a house we live inside. We think the walls protect us. They just keep out the light.”

The metaphor was clumsy. A fisherman’s metaphor. It shattered something in her. Not grief, not yet. The polished shell she had built around it. Her hand went to the amulet at her throat, a smooth stone worn from years of unconscious touch. Her brother had given it to her. A stupid thing, a river pebble he’d found. For luck, he’d said.

She never took it off. It was her secret ledger entry. The one no tax collector could find.

Peter saw the gesture. His eyes tracked the movement, then returned to her face. He didn’t smile. He didn’t look away. He simply witnessed. Unbearable.

Miriam stood up. The stool legs shrieked against the floor. The sound was a violence in the close room. Every face turned to her.

“I need air,” she said. It was a whisper, but it cut through the murmur.

She didn’t wait for permission. She stepped over Eliana’s legs, avoiding the widow’s upturned face, and moved toward the curtain. A path through the seated bodies felt endless. Knees and shoulders pulled back to let her pass. She felt their eyes on her back, a physical warmth. Not judgment. Curiosity. Concern. Worse.

She pushed through the heavy cloth into the antechamber, then onto the small, open landing of the stairs. The night air was cool, a shock after the room’s closeness. She gripped the rough stone parapet, her knuckles bleaching white. Below, the city was a geometry of shadows and distant, flickering oil lamps. A dog barked. A cart rattled far away.

She had expected relief. She felt unmoored. That room behind her, for all its terrible exposure, had a gravitational pull. Her island had been a fiction. That truth was in the shared loaf, in the clumsy prayer, in Peter’s quiet blade of a question.

She heard the curtain rustle. She didn’t turn. Heavy, slow footsteps. Peter.

He didn’t come beside her. He stopped in the doorway, a bulk against the light from within. He said nothing for a long moment. She could hear him breathing, a slight wheeze in his chest.

“I am sorry,” he said finally. The words were not soft. They were blunt, like stones placed on a grave. “I have a fisherman’s touch. Too heavy.”

She kept her eyes on the city. “You saw a wound. You poked it. It is what people do.”

“No.” The syllable was firm. “It is what I do. I see a thing, I must name it. It is my failure. He tried to teach me otherwise. To see, and sometimes, to be silent.”

This admission, this naming of his own failure, disarmed her more than any wisdom could have. She risked a glance at him. He was looking out at the same darkness, his profile etched against the yellow glow from the room. The public thunder was entirely gone. Here was just a man, old for his years, carrying a weight of regret.

“The loss you spoke of,” she said, the words coming before she could cage them. “Did you live in that house?”

He was silent so long she thought he hadn’t heard. Then he let out a breath that was almost a laugh. “I built it stone by stone. After I denied him. The walls were very high.” He turned his head toward her. “They fell down. It was loud, and it was messy, and I am still sweeping up the dust.”

Inside, someone began to sing. A psalm, one of ascent. The tune was tentative, a single thread of melody. Then another voice joined, a fifth below. Then a third. It was not beautiful. Uneven, human.

Peter listened. A deep sadness softened his features. “They are learning,” he said. Not about the song. About something else entirely.

Miriam understood. They were learning the new currency. The market for grace. Her merchant’s mind recoiled, then went still, baffled. How did you assess the value of a thing that increased only when you gave it away?

The song swelled, filling the upper room, spilling out onto the landing. The words were about going up to the mountain of the Lord. Miriam stood on a stone landing, holding a parapet, a world away from any mountain. Yet the longing in the voices was a thick thing in the night air. It was not her longing. Not yet. But it was near enough to touch.

Peter pushed himself off the doorframe. “The bread was good,” he said, as if ending a formal negotiation. He dipped his head once, a gesture that was neither dismissal nor blessing, and went back inside.

Miriam stayed at the parapet. The cool stone was real under her palms. The city below was real. The ledger in her mind, with its columns of loss and caution, was real. But the song at her back was real, too. And the taste of barley bread, shared, was still on her tongue.

She was no longer observing from an island. She was in the water. And she did not know how to swim.

The singing from inside did not stop. It found its rhythm, a slow, rolling tide of voices that were not trained for harmony but reached for it anyway. Miriam listened, her back to the curtain. The cool air had done nothing to settle her. It had only made the warmth behind her more distinct, a living pulse of sound and beat.

She could leave. The stairs were right there, leading down to the street, to the familiar anonymity of crowded festival lanes. Her sandals were dry. Her cloak was folded inside. She could walk out and never return, and the song would continue without her. It was the logical choice. The safe choice.

Her feet did not move.

The psalm ended. A moment of shuffling quiet, then a new one began—slower, more somber. A lament. The melody was a winding path through a minor key. She knew this one. Out of the depths. The words rose in her memory in Hebrew, then in the Greek translation she’d learned as a girl. Out of the depths I cry to you, O Lord. Her lips moved, shaping the Hebrew syllables without sound.

The curtain stirred. Not Peter this time. Eliana emerged, holding Miriam’s folded cloak. She offered it without speaking. Miriam took it, the coarse wool scratchy in her hands. She did not put it on.

“They are praying now,” Eliana said, her voice barely above the murmur of the song. “For the festival. For the city. For each other.” She leaned against the parapet, a hand’s breadth away. “You do not have to go in. You can listen from here.”

Permission. An exit presented as an option. Miriam looked at the widow’s profile, at the way the lamplight from the doorway caught the fine down on her cheek. This woman had lost a husband, a future. Yet she sat on floors and tasted lentils and hummed tunelessly to grant a stranger dignity. What house did she live in? It seemed to have many windows.

From inside, a single voice rose in prayer—the woman from Joppa. What she said was simple, stumbling. She prayed for her son’s fever to break. For the centurion who had shamed her with his decency. The specificity of it was shocking. This was not the elevated, formulaic prayer of the Temple. This was a woman talking to the divine as if to a neighbor over a courtyard wall. Miriam’s translator’s mind dissected it: unadorned syntax, direct address, concrete petitions. It was the opposite of poetry. Raw data.

And it was followed by another. The Phrygian wool-carder, his voice thick with accent, prayed in a clumsy mix of Greek and Aramaic for his sister back home, for the health of his ewes. He named them. The spotted one, with the torn ear. A laugh hiccupped through the room, fond, not mocking.

Miriam found herself holding her breath. This was not a performance. It was an unfolding. A laying-bare. Each voice added another thread to a fabric that was being woven in real time, uneven, full of knots. She was a master of languages, and she had no grammar for this.

Eliana pushed off the parapet. “I’m going back in.” She paused, her hand on the curtain. “The air is better in here now. Less… calculated.” She slipped inside.

Miriam stayed where she was, the cloak heavy in her hands, the prayer voices rising and falling behind her like waves against stone.
