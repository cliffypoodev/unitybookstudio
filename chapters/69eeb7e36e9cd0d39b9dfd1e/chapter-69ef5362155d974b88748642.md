The jello shots tasted like regret and cheap vodka. Cletus knew this because he had licked a few off his fingers after mixing another batch of “Repentance Revivers” for the Purgatory Palace pop-up booth. The fog machine coughed up something between dry ice and what he suspected was actual purgatorial mist. A neon sign flickered overhead, its cursive script promising “Eternal Rest (Timeshare Available).”

Bartholomew chose that moment to manifest as a chihuahua wearing tiny aviator sunglasses. He yapped directly into Cletus’s left ear.

“Not now, Barty.” Cletus adjusted his clip-on tie, the one with the tiny golden scales of justice pattern. Across the velvet rope, a middle-aged couple squinted at their brochure. The husband smelled like greed and golf course sunscreen. The wife reeked of envy and Chardonnay. Perfect marks.

The chihuahua leapt onto the sales counter, knocking over a display of “Soul Upgrade” brochures. “The plaintiff contends that your current enterprise constitutes fraudulent misrepresentation of—”

Cletus snatched him mid-bark and stuffed him into the “Lost & Found” drawer. The couple blinked.

“Demo model,” Cletus said, flashing teeth he had paid good purgatory scrip for. “Now, folks—ever dreamed of owning a slice of the afterlife?”

The wife’s eyes glazed over the way they do when someone mentions timeshares or colon cleanses. The husband scratched his neck where his golf tan ended. “This legit?”

“Legit as Judgment Day.” Cletus slid the glossy brochure across the counter. “Premium purgatory experience. No hidden fees—except the mandatory soul cleansing, but that’s more of a feature than a fee.”

The Lost & Found drawer rattled. A tiny paw emerged, flipping Cletus off.

The wife gasped. Cletus kicked the drawer shut. “Interactive brochure.”

Bartholomew burst out as a parrot this time, feathers ruffled, one wing clutching a scroll. “Article 37, subsection—”

Cletus grabbed a jello shot and lobbed it. Bartholomew caught it midair downed it, then hiccuped blue flames.

The husband’s eyes lit up. “That a party trick?”

“Standard soul purification process,” Cletus lied. “Now, for a limited time only—”

The parrot landed on Cletus’s shoulder, whispering in his ear like a feathery Satan. “They’re Methodists, Cletus. Methodists don’t believe in purgatory.”

Cletus elbowed him into a potted plant. “Special ecumenical discount today.”

The wife squinted at the fine print. “Says here we’d share the booth with other souls.”

“Think of it as a cosmic timeshare,” Cletus said. “You get every third millennium.”

The parrot emerged from the foliage, now wearing a tiny lawyer’s wig. “Misrepresentation!”

Cletus punted him into a passing tour group.

The husband frowned. “That bird said ‘plaintiff.’”

“Auto-correct,” Cletus said. “Celestial wifi’s spotty.”

Bartholomew returned as a raccoon with a briefcase. He served Cletus papers. Cletus used them to light a cigar.

The wife tugged her husband’s arm. “Honey maybe we should—”

“Wait!” Cletus pulled out the big guns—a laminated photo of the purgatory lounge. “Free margaritas for eternity.”

The raccoon notarized a document with his paw. “The defendant will note that Section 9 expressly prohibits—”

Cletus stomped on his tail. Bartholomew yowled and morphed into a capuchin monkey in a waistcoat.

The husband paled. “Okay, what the hell is—”

“Purgatory, baby!” Cletus threw confetti. Some of it burst into hellfire on contact with the carpet. “Sign today, get a free set of steak knives.”

The monkey climbed the chandelier, screeching legalese. The couple fled.

Bartholomew dropped onto the counter, now back to his original form—a three-foot-tall parrot with the eyes of Cletus’s ex-partner and the voice of a disgraced televangelist. “You’re out of compliance, Cletus.”

Cletus poured himself a jello shot. “Barty, we’ve been through this. I serve my time, I get to die. That was the deal.”

Bartholomew unfurled a scroll longer than Cletus’s rap sheet. “The original contract specifies ‘good behavior.’”

“I’m selling salvation!”

“You’re running a timeshare scam in the antechamber of eternity.”

Cletus gestured to the booth’s neon sign. “It’s called capitalism, featherbrain. Even saints need passive income.”

Bartholomew’s eyes gleamed. “Remember the parrot?”

Ice slid down Cletus’s spine. “That was an accident.”

“You shot him mid-heist.”

“He was a witness!”

The parrot’s beak curved into something too smug for the animal kingdom. “Turns out parrots have souls. And lawyers.” He tapped the contract. “My client seeks punitive reincarnation.”

Cletus lunged for the scroll. Bartholomew flapped out of reach, cackling.

“Your next incarnation,” he announced, “is a sea cucumber.”

The fog machine died with a whimper. The neon sign flickered out. Somewhere, a slot machine paid out in souls.

Cletus grabbed the last jello shot. “There’s gotta be a loophole.”

Bartholomew adjusted his tiny wig. “There is.” He pointed to a clause written in what looked like dinosaur blood. “You can void the contract… if you sell one legitimate timeshare.”

Cletus stared at him. “To who?”

The parrot smiled. “Me.”

The chandelier exploded in a shower of sparks. The jello shots melted into something resembling holy water.

Paperwork.

The monkey stole Cletus’s cigar. The parrot drafted terms. Somewhere, a choir of damned telemarketers sang “Despacito.”

Cletus signed in triplicate.

Bartholomew notarized it with a flaming stamp. “Congratulations. You’ve sold your first honest timeshare.”

Cletus waited for the lightning strike. The heavenly choir. The sweet release of death.

The parrot handed Cletus a receipt. “Your next incarnation will be processed in 6-8 business millennia.”

Cletus choked. “What?”

“Processing delays,” Bartholomew said. “Purgatory’s backed up.”

The fog machine sputtered back to life. The neon sign flickered: “NOW HIRING.”

Bartholomew fluffed his feathers. “Want to earn overtime?”

Cletus poured another jello shot. It tasted like bankruptcy and bad decisions.

“Commission?” Cletus asked.

The parrot grinned. “Straight to hell.”

The receipt in Cletus’s hand burst into hellfire, leaving behind a singed Post-it note that read “VOID IF TAMPERED.” Cletus squinted at Bartholomew. “Six to eight millennia? That’s longer than my original sentence.”

The parrot preened under the flickering neon. “Divine backlog. Turns out your little timeshare scheme clogged the system.”

Behind them, a spectral line was forming. A woman in a muumuu waved a flaming timeshare certificate. “I was promised waterfront views!”

Cletus snatched a megaphone from the counter. “All grievances must be submitted in triplicate to our complaints department.” He pointed to a flaming trash can labeled “COMPLAINTS.”

Bartholomew pecked Cletus’s ear. “You’re missing the bigger loophole, Cletus.”

“Oh, I see it.” Cletus cracked his knuckles. “The contract says I’m free if I sell one legitimate timeshare. It doesn’t specify to whom.”

The parrot’s feathers puffed up. “Don’t you dare—”

Cletus whirled toward the growing mob of disgruntled souls. “Ladies, gentlemen, and legally ambiguous entities! For a limited time only, Purgatory Palace is offering buyback options!”

The muumuu ghost gasped. “You can undo this?”

“Absolutely!” Cletus grabbed a stack of contracts from the drawer. “Sign here, and your immortal soul reverts to its original owner—you!”

Bartholomew dive-bombed the paperwork. “That’s not how buybacks work!”

Cletus ducked. “Says who?”

“Says the celestial small print!” Bartholomew flapped onto the chandelier, which was now listing dangerously to one side. “Section 14-C: ‘All soul transfers are final, no refunds, no exchanges, unless otherwise noted in a separate rider signed in the blood of a sinless accountant.’”

The crowd groaned. A man in a tracksuit threw a jello shot at Cletus. It passed through his translucent hand and splattered against the “NOW HIRING” sign.

Cletus wiped green sludge off his forehead. “Okay, new plan. Class-action lawsuit.”

Bartholomew choked on a seed. “Against who?”

“Management.” Cletus jabbed a finger upward. “If purgatory’s a timeshare, then Someone upstairs is the HOA president.”

The parrot’s beak clacked shut. His tiny eyes dilated. “You can’t sue the Almighty.”

“Watch me.” Cletus yanked a legal pad from behind the counter. “First count: breach of implied warranty. Second count: emotional distress.”

“Blasphemy,” Bartholomew hissed.

“—false advertising.” Cletus scribbled furiously. “Where’s my waterfront view, Barry?”

“It’s metaphorical.”

“So’s my foot in your—”

The chandelier crashed down.

For a glorious second, Cletus thought he had won. Then the dust cleared, revealing Bartholomew unharmed and now wearing a tiny judge’s robe. The parrot cleared his throat. “Motion denied.”

The crowd erupted.

A teenager with a soul-patch threw a chair through the fog machine. It coughed up a cloud shaped like the Virgin Mary giving two thumbs down. The muumuu woman started a chant: “FIX. THIS. PLACE.”

Bartholomew flapped onto Cletus’s shoulder. “You’ve incited a riot.”

“I’ve incited a class-action.” Cletus grabbed the megaphone again. “Who here wants compensatory heaven?”

Every hand shot up.

The parrot sighed. “Fine.” He snapped his wings, and a filing cabinet materialized midair. It landed with a thud, spewing paperwork like a possessed copy machine. “But we’re doing this by the book.”

Cletus peered at the top document. “This says ‘Release Form 9B-6R: Petition for Provisional Salvation (Pending Review).’”

“Yes.”

“The review period is ten millennia.”

“Standard processing.”

Cletus threw the stack into the air. “We’re streamlining.” He turned to the crowd. “Who here has ever lied on a timeshare application?”

Silence.

“Bull. Every one of you fudged your net worth.” Cletus pulled out a pen. “New policy: full confession gets you bumped to the fast lane.”

The muumuu woman raised a hand. “I said I’d read the terms and conditions.”

“Fast-tracked.”

The tracksuit guy coughed. “I might’ve exaggerated my golf handicap.”

“VIP heaven.”

A small child piped up. “I told my mom I didn’t eat the last cookie.”

“Saint status.”

Bartholomew screeched. “You can’t just—”

“Article 12, subsection ‘Hail Mary,’” Cletus said. “Any soul demonstrating spontaneous contrition may petition for immediate—”

“That’s not real!”

Cletus pulled out a jello shot. “Wanna bet?”

The parrot hesitated. Then, with the grace of a creature who had spent three lifetimes as a lawyer, he folded. “Fine. But only if you handle the paperwork.”

The crowd surged forward.

For six straight hours, Cletus processed souls like a damned DMV clerk. Bartholomew notarized. The monkey brewed coffee that tasted like regret. At some point, the fog machine started pumping out the smell of fresh-baked salvation.

By the time the last ghost floated away clutching a “Get Out of Purgatory Free” card, Cletus’s hand was cramping, his tie was askew, and the parrot looked like he had aged a millennium.

Cletus slumped against the counter. “Loophole achieved.”

Bartholomew tossed Cletus a flaming scroll. “Not quite.”

Cletus unrolled it. The words “PROMOTION: PERMANENT MANAGER” glowed in hellfire.

Cletus blinked. “What?”

“You voided your original contract by selling a legitimate timeshare, yes.” The parrot adjusted his tiny wig. “But you just processed 437 buybacks under Purgatory Palace letterhead. That constitutes acceptance of employment terms.”

The neon sign flickered. The “NOW HIRING” now read “NOW OWNED BY CLETUS (CONGRATS/APOLOGIES).”

Cletus grabbed the parrot by his ridiculous cravat. “You set me up.”

“Correction.” Bartholomew pried himself free. “You played yourself. Classic Cletus.”

The monkey handed Cletus a business card:

Cletus
CEO, Purgatory Palace Time Shares
“Eternity Starts Now (Terms & Conditions Apply)”

Cletus poured himself a jello shot. It tasted like defeat. And possibly asbestos.

Bartholomew fluffed his feathers. “Look on the bright side—no more reincarnation.”

“Yeah.” Cletus stared at the empty booth, the broken fog machine, the piles of unsigned contracts. “Just eternal middle management.”

The parrot perched on Cletus’s shoulder. “Welcome to hell, boss.”

The phone rang.

Cletus picked up.

A voice like melted caramel said, “Hi, we’ve been trying to reach you about your soul’s extended warranty—”

Cletus hung up.

It rang again.

Bartholomew grinned. “Should’ve read the fine print.”

The last of the coffee was cold. Cletus stared at the oily black surface, then dumped it down the drain. The sink gurgled, a wet protest. Outside the kitchenette’s single porthole, the station’s maintenance arm swung past, a skeletal shadow against the gas giant’s banded face.

His terminal chimed. A priority ping, source: Central Life Support, Sector G-12.

He didn’t move. The chime repeated, sharper. Protocol demanded acknowledgment within ninety seconds. A log entry. A dispatch. He watched the counter on his wrist-comp tick down from eighty-eight.

At forty-five, he slapped the acknowledge key. The alert expanded. Atmospheric pressure fluctuation. Minimal. Borderline negligible. Probably a faulty sensor in one of the old hydroponics bays. The station was a patchwork of new tech grafted onto fifty-year-old bones. It sighed. It creaked. It gave false readings.

Another chime. Central was requesting a physical diagnostic. Estimated time: two hours, fifteen minutes. His shift had seven hours left.

“Override,” he muttered, typing. “Sensor calibration error. Recommend remote diagnostic cycle before physical dispatch.”

The response was immediate. Request denied. Physical inspection required. Acknowledge assignment.

The words glowed. He could picture the duty officer on the central ring, some kid who had never spent more than a cycle on the outer sectors, tapping the denial with a clean, manicured finger.

He acknowledged.

The access tunnel to G-12 was unheated. His breath plumed. The only light came from the bioluminescent strips running along the conduit overhead, a sickly green that made everything look faintly decayed. His boots echoed, a solitary rhythm against the grating.

Halfway there, he stopped. Listened.

The station had its own music. The deep thrum of the core reactors. The whisper of recycled air through vents. The distant metallic chatter of a cargo loader somewhere far above. Now, underneath it all, a new note: a thin, reedy whistle.

He followed it. The sound led him off the main access way down a secondary maintenance shaft so narrow his shoulders brushed the walls. The whistle sharpened into a hiss.

The panel at the shaft’s end was marked with faded yellow hazard stripes. The seal was compromised. Not a sensor error. A hairline fracture in the primary atmospheric feed line. Pressure was bleeding out, slowly, into the interstitial space between the hulls.

He ran a hand-scanner over the seam. The readout confirmed it. A stress fracture. It wouldn’t blow. Not today. But it would weep. For months. Until someone else bothered to look.

His wrist-comp chimed again. Central. Status update?

He ignored it. From his kit, he pulled a tube of emergency sealant. The industrial glue was cold and thick. He applied a bead along the fracture, his movements methodical, precise. The hiss faded, choked off by the gray paste. It was a temporary fix. It would hold for a few cycles maybe a week. Long enough for him to file the report. Long enough for Central to debate the resource allocation for a proper weld.

The silence that followed felt heavier than the noise.

His comp chimed a third time. He looked at the screen. Update required. Non-compliance will be noted.

He keyed the mic. “Inspection complete. Minor seal degradation. Temporary patch applied. No immediate hazard.”

“Specify location of degradation.” The voice from Central was tinny, bored.

“Secondary feed line, junction seven-beta, interstitial access shaft off G-12 main.”

A pause. Keys clicking. “That shaft is not on the standard inspection grid.”

“I followed an auditory anomaly.”

“Procedure requires adherence to the designated grid. Your deviation is noted.”

He leaned his head against the cold bulkhead. The metal drank the warmth from his skin. “The leak is patched.”

“The deviation is noted,” the voice repeated. “Return to your primary patrol route.”

The channel cut.

His primary route took him past the observation blister on D-7. It was empty. Most of the viewports on the station faced the gas giant, Vey’al, with its hypnotic, swirling storms. This one looked out into the black. Nothing but stars and the endless dark between them.

He wasn’t alone for long.

The door hissed open. Footsteps, light and careful. He didn’t turn.

“They said you went off-grid.”

Silas. His voice was always quieter than Cletus expected, a low rumble that seemed to come from the deck plates.

“Found a leak.”

“And got a mark for your trouble.” Silas came to stand beside him, not looking at him, looking at the same empty star field. He held two steaming bulbs. He offered one. “Here. It’s tea. Actual leaves. Not the recycled algae swill.”

Cletus took it. The heat seeped through his gloves. “Where’d you get it?”

“Traded a favor to one of the logistics runners. Don’t ask.” Silas sipped his own. “A leak, huh?”

“A weep. In the old hull layer. Central would’ve missed it for months on their remote scans.”

“But you heard it.”

“I heard it.”

Silas nodded, as if that explained everything. Maybe it did. Silas had been on the station longer than anyone. He remembered the first retrofit. He had known the engineers who had built the place, most of them dead or transferred now. He operated on a different set of protocols, ones written in memory and instinct.

“They’re nervous,” he said after a while.

“Who?”

“Central. The new overseer. She’s from corporate. Thinks in spreadsheets. Every anomaly is a liability. Every liability is a cost.” He gestured out the viewport with his bulb. “They see the dark out there and all they think about is how much it would cost if it got in.”

“It’s always been about cost.”

“Not like this.” Silas finished his tea. “The deviation will go on your review. It’ll be a point against your efficiency rating.”

“I know.”

“Worth it?”

Cletus thought of the hiss, now silent. The slow bleed of air, of heat, of life. “Yes.”

Silas took his empty bulb. “Next time, log a ‘routine auditory check’ first. Then go looking. Gives you cover.”

“That’s not procedure.”

“It’s my procedure.” Silas tapped the side of his head. “Older and wiser. Try it.” He moved toward the door, then stopped. “The new overseer is making a round of the outer sectors tomorrow. A ‘hands-on assessment.’ Be on your best behavior.”

“Or what?”

“Or she’ll decide hands-on is too expensive and replace us all with sensor drones.” The door slid shut behind him.

The next shift started with a system-wide alert: Overseer’s inspection. All non-essential personnel were to maintain assigned posts. ‘Demonstrate optimal operational efficiency.’

Cletus was polishing an already-gleaming console in the main junction of his sector when the overseer arrived. Dr. Aris. She was younger than expected, sharp-edged in a crisp grey uniform without a single stain or fray. Two junior officers flanked her, datapads ready.

“Technician.” Dr. Aris didn’t smile. Her eyes scanned the junction, missing nothing.

“Ma’am.”

“Your sector has the highest rate of minor incident reports in the last quarter.”

“It’s the oldest sector, ma’am. The systems require more frequent attention.”

“Attention, or intervention?” Dr. Aris stepped closer, her gaze on the console readouts. “My data shows a pattern of unsanctioned diagnostics. You logged a ‘routine auditory check’ in G-12 yesterday before discovering a seal fracture. That specific check is not on the maintenance schedule for that cycle.”

Silas’s cover. It had been flagged anyway.

“I heard something during my patrol. The log was retrospective.”

“Retrospective logging is a violation of data integrity protocols.” Dr. Aris turned her full attention on Cletus. “Your file shows aptitude. Your practical scores are high. But your adherence to procedural workflow is…” She paused, selecting the word. “Interpretive.”

One of the junior officers coughed.

“The station’s systems are interconnected, Technician. A procedural workflow exists for a reason. It allows for predictive modeling, resource allocation, risk assessment. When one element operates on… intuition… it introduces chaos into the model. It becomes a variable that cannot be accounted for.”

“The leak would not have been found by the model.”

“Perhaps. Or perhaps the next scheduled sensor sweep would have detected the pressure loss before it reached a critical threshold. Your intervention, while well-intentioned may have cost more in procedural disruption than it saved in potential repair.”

Cletus felt his jaw tighten. His hands, clasped behind his back, pressed together until the knuckles ached.

Dr. Aris saw it. A faint flicker in her eyes, like a scientist noting a reaction. “Efficiency is not merely about fixing what is broken. It is about maintaining a system where nothing breaks unpredictably. Your record will be annotated. Consider this a clarification of expectations.”

The overseer moved on, her retinue following. The junction felt colder after they left.

His wrist-comp buzzed. A direct message, not through Central. Silas.

Heard you met the spreadsheet. You still breathing?

Cletus typed back, his fingers stiff. He says intuition is a variable. Chaos.

The reply came fast. Chaos is just data you haven’t sorted yet. Finish your shift. Meet me at the blister after. I have something.

The final hours dragged. Every minor alert felt like a test. Cletus followed procedure to the letter. He logged every step. He requested Central authorization for a flickering light panel that normally he’d just smack with his palm.

The authorization took twenty minutes. The panel sputtered and died while he waited.

When his shift finally ended, his head throbbed with a dull, steady rhythm. He made his way to D-7. Silas was already there, a bulky tool crate at his feet.

“You look like you’ve been wrestling with the procedural manual,” Silas said.

“It won.”

Silas grunted, popping the latches on the crate. Inside, nestled in foam was a device Cletus hadn’t seen in years. An old-style acoustic amplifier, a heavy thing with a dish receiver and a set of over-ear monitors. “Found it in deep storage. Belonged to the first chief engineer. Used it to listen to the station’s bones.”

“It’s antique.”

“It’s not connected to the network. It doesn’t log. It just… listens.” Silas handed Cletus the headphones. “Try it.”

Cletus put them on. Silas powered up the unit. A low hum, then sound flooded in. But it was sound stripped of the station’s usual filters. Cletus heard the groan of the hull as it expanded minutely in the sun’s weak light. The gurgle of fluid in pipes three decks away. The resonant ping of a loose bracket spinning slowly in a zero-g conduit. It was the station’s raw, unfiltered voice.

“It’s all data,” Silas said, his voice muffled by the cans on Cletus’s ears. “Just not the kind their models understand.”

Cletus listened. Beneath the groans and pings, he heard it again. A new whisper. Not a hiss. A faint, rhythmic tap-tap-tap, like metal on metal. Coming from the direction of the old storage pods in Sector F.

He took off the headphones. The ordinary station sounds rushed back, flat and familiar.

“Well?” Silas asked.

Cletus looked at the antique amplifier. A variable. Chaos.

“I need to check something,” he said.

“Now? Your shift ended.”

“It’s a retrospective auditory check.”

A slow smile spread across Silas’s face. He closed the crate and hefted it. “Then I’m coming with you. To observe proper procedure.”
