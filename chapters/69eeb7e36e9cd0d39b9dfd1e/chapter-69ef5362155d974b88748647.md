The morning heat in Barcelona smelled of diesel, baking stone, and the ghost of incense that Sister Margaret’s industrial mop could never fully erase. Today, it also smelled of fresh spray paint. She leaned on her aluminum crutch at the edge of the Sacred Grind’s main bowl, the titanium in her knee clicking with a sound like a disapproving rosary bead. Her other hand, the one not white-knuckling the crutch, traced the new mural on the convent’s ancient wall. It depicted St. Francis of Assisi, but he was mid-ollie over a pack of bewildered-looking wolves. The spray can work was immaculate. The saint’s expression was one of serene concentration.

“Again,” she said to the empty park.

Her voice echoed off the polished concrete of the half-pipe that had once been the refectory. The park was silent, abandoned in the post-dawn lull before the local kids trickled in. A single skateboard lay overturned near the coping, its wheels still spinning with a lazy, mocking whir. It wasn’t one of the park’s rentals. The deck was custom, inlaid with what looked like mother-of-pearl forming a complex Chi-Rho symbol. The trucks were polished to a mirror shine, weirdly ornate.

Margaret sighed. The sigh turned into a wince as she shifted her weight. The doctor had said six weeks. The doctor had not accounted for heretical graffiti artists with a flair for aerial saintography.

This was the third mural in a week. First, St. Teresa of Ávila in a full grind along the edge of the baptismal font-turned-skate ledge. Then, St. Sebastian, not pierced by arrows, but by snapped skateboard trucks, his expression one of mild inconvenience. And now, airborne St. Francis. They always appeared overnight, accompanied by the faint, sweet smell of ozone and the lingering impression that the laws of physics had been politely ignored. The coping on the deep end showed fresh scuff marks that arced too high, curves no local skater could land without a ladder and a death wish.

Her walkie-talkie crackled on her belt. “Sister M? You seeing this?”

Brother Tim’s voice was all adenoidal panic. He was nineteen, a novice, and his primary monastic contributions were an encyclopedic knowledge of obscure pop-punk bands and a mortal fear of confrontation.

“I’m standing in it, Tim,” she said, thumbing the button. “Any sign of them?”

“Negative. Front gate’s locked. But the security feed from the cloister… you should just come look.”

The cloister was supposed to be off-limits. It was her project, her sacred want. She was turning the serene quadrilateral of garden and arched walkways into a technical street course, a meditation on flow and gravity. The perfect half-pipe chapel. She’d even sketched stained-glass windows depicting various skateboard saints—a private joke. Now it felt like the joke was on her.

She crutched her way across the main hall, her sneakers squeaking on the polished stone. The sound mixed with the distant, eternal hum of the city. A pigeon watched her from a high window, its head cocked. She remembered, irrelevantly, that her grandfather had called them ‘flying rats.’ This one looked judgmental.

The security monitor in the tiny office showed a green-tinted loop of the cloister. For a second, she saw only the shadowed arches, the piles of lumber and bags of Quickrete for the new quarter-pipe. Then a figure rolled into frame.

He moved with a liquid grace that made her good knee ache with envy. He wore what looked like a modified monk’s habit, the robes cinched at the waist with a belt of tooled leather, the hem cut away to reveal heavily padded knees. His head was shaved, a tattoo of celestial navigation charts visible on his scalp. He carved a smooth line around the central fountain, his board whispering on the stone. He wasn’t just skating. He was performing a liturgy.

“He’s been doing that for twenty minutes,” Tim whispered, as if the man on the screen could hear them. “Also, he… watch.”

The skater approached a stack of lumber. Instead of stopping or ollieing, he simply went up. The board adhered to the vertical wood as if magnetized, carrying him in a smooth, vertical curve before he kicked out and rolled back down, landing with a soft click.

Margaret stared. Her mouth had gone dry, a familiar precursor to holy dread. “That’s not possible.”

“Right? The grip tape alone—”

“Not the physics, Tim. The audacity.” She grabbed her crutch. “Lock the door after me.”

“What are you going to do?”

“Interfaith dialogue.”

The cloister air was cooler, smelling of damp earth and cut stone. The skater heard her crutch-tap approach and came to a halt, one foot on the tail of his board. He wasn’t breathing hard. His eyes were a pale, unsettling gray, like a winter sea.

“Peace be with you,” Margaret said, her voice tighter than she intended.

“And also with you,” he replied, automatic. His accent was strange, clipped and musical at once. He glanced at her crutch, her knee brace over her jeans. “You are the custodian?”

“I’m Sister Margaret. This is my convent. You are defacing it.”

He looked genuinely puzzled. “Defacing? We are venerating. This is the locus. The point of convergence.”

“Convergence of what? Spray paint cans?”

“Of the sacred kinetic line.” He said it with the weary patience of a scholar explaining basic theology to a child. “The path the board takes. The arc. It’s a prayer written in motion. Your walls… they remember the old prayers. They amplify the new ones.” He nodded at her brace. “You have been inscribed, I see. A holy wound from the grind.”

She instinctively touched the tattoo on her forearm, visible below her rolled-up sleeve: a crowned Virgin Mary dropping in on a vert ramp, surrounded by roses and the words Ave Maria, Full of Grace, Send Me a New Deck. Her ‘holy roller derby’ phase. A flush of heat climbed her neck. “That’s private.”

“Nothing here is private. It’s a broadcast tower.” He kicked his board up into his hand. The underside wasn’t decorated with a brand logo or punk art. It was inlaid with a complex, geometric pattern of metals and what looked like bone. “The relics in the trucks… they resonate. We’ve been searching for a locus this pure for cycles.”

The word ‘relics’ landed in her brain and stuck there, absurd and terrifying. “What relics?”

A second skater dropped from the cloister roof.

She didn’t roll down the sloping tiles. She simply stepped off the edge, two stories up, and landed in a silent crouch on her board, which then rolled forward as if she’d been carrying speed. She was dressed similarly, her habit-tunic bleached white. Her scalp tattoo was of a flowering vine. “Ambrose,” she said. “The harmonics are spiking. We need to finalize the Catherine glyph before the window closes.”

“Window?” Margaret’s head swiveled between them. The woman’s board also had the weird inlay. Up close, Margaret saw the bone fragments were tiny, carved into minute saints. A finger bone of St. Christopher? A tooth of St. Lucy? The sheer, grotesque specificity of the thought made her dizzy.

Ambrose gestured to Margaret. “Thérèse, this is the custodian. Sister Margaret. She bears the wound-mark of the grind.”

Thérèse’s gray eyes appraised her. Not unkindly. Like a mechanic evaluating a well-used tool. “The knee? A brutal initiation. The old paths were less forgiving.” She skated a slow circle around Margaret. “You feel it don’t you? The hum in the stone. This place… it was never about stillness. It was about potential energy. The paused breath before the drop.”

And the terrible thing was, Margaret did feel it. She always had. It was why she’d pushed to convert the dying convent, why she sketched ramps between vespers, why she prayed not on her knees but rolling in a smooth, fast carve. She’d called it flow. They were calling it prayer. It was the same thing.

“You can’t just paint over a thousand-year-old wall,” she said, but the protest sounded weak, administrative.

“We’re not painting over,” Ambrose said. “We’re painting through. The images are focal points. They help stabilize the line through the chaos.” He looked past her, toward the main hall. “The others are beginning the morning rite. You should observe.”

“The others?”

In the main bowl, four more skaters moved in a complex, interweaving pattern. They wore the same adapted habits, their scalps a canvas of star maps and botanical illustrations. They didn’t speak. The only sounds were the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of tails hitting concrete as they set up for tricks, the zipper-whir of wheels, the occasional scrape of a perfectly executed slide. They moved like a murmuration of starlings, a single organism.

And they were doing things that made Margaret’s surgeon’s warning scream in her head.

One skater launched from the deep end, spun in a slow, impossible 720, and landed not on the downward curve, but on the vertical wall, skating parallel to the floor for ten feet before dropping back in. Another rode up the transition, onto the railing of the old choir loft, and grinded the entire length—a rail that was, by any sane measure, too narrow and too high. The relics in their trucks glinted under the LED lights she’d installed to replace candles.

They weren’t showing off. There was no audience but her and a gawking Brother Tim, peeking from the office door. This was ritual. Each impossible aerial, each gravity-defying grind was a deliberate, precise motion. A word in a sentence she couldn’t read.

Thérèse stopped beside her. “You see the Catherine line forming?”

Margaret didn’t. She saw chaos. Beautiful, terrifying chaos.

Then Ambrose took a run at the full-pipe. He hit the transition with explosive speed, flew up, up, past the coping, into the open air above the lip. For a heart-stopping second, he hung suspended against the backdrop of a stained-glass window depicting the Annunciation. He flipped his board—a kickflip of such casual, effortless grace it looked like a shrug—and as he descended, he pulled a can of spray paint from his belt.

He landed, rolling away smoothly, and on the vertical surface of the pipe, right where he’d been upside-down, a perfect, spray-painted image bloomed into existence. St. Catherine of Alexandria, her spiked wheel replaced by a skateboard wheel, her hand raised not in benediction but in a classic ‘gnarly’ shaka sign. The lines were clean, the shading photorealistic. It had been created in less than a second of hang time.

The paint was still wet. It smelled like ozone and lavender.

Margaret’s crutch clattered to the floor. She hadn’t felt it slip from her hand. She stood, weight on her good leg, staring at the impossible mural. The twist wasn’t just what they were doing, or why. It was the dawning, gut-punch realization that they were right. This place was sacred to them. More sacred, perhaps, than it had ever been to her order, who saw the skatepark as a necessary, mildly embarrassing fundraiser. These lunatics with their relic-embedded trucks worshipped here. They were pilgrims. She was the janitor.

“The window is closing,” Thérèse said, her voice urgent. “The confluence lasts only eighty-seven more seconds. We must complete the sequence.”

Ambrose skated over, stopping before Margaret. He held out his board. “You feel the line. You bear the wound. You are part of the locus. The Catherine sequence needs a final anchor. A ground point.”

Her knee throbbed in agreement. The titanium clicked. The Virgin Mary on her forearm seemed to watch her, waiting for the drop.

Brother Tim’s voice echoed, tiny and far away. “Sister M don’t! Your meniscus!”

Margaret looked at the board. Looked at the perfect, impossible Catherine on the wall. Looked at the expectant, serene faces of the saints of the skate park.

“Okay,” she said, her voice a stranger’s. “But I’m not doing anything above the coping.”

“Above the coping is where the prayer happens,” Ambrose said, his tone gentle, as if explaining why the host couldn’t be toast.

“My prayer happens at physical therapy,” Margaret shot back. She bent, a clumsy, lurching motion, and retrieved her crutch. The board he offered hovered between them, an accusation. “You’re telling me you’re from the future.”

“A future,” Thérèse corrected. She was watching the others, who continued their silent, interweaving patterns. The thump-thump-thump of their boards was starting to feel like a heartbeat. “One of the quieter timelines. We’re archivists. Kinetic theologians. We recover lost forms of sacred motion.”

“Skateboarding is a lost form?”

“The pure form is. The form that channels the telluric currents through focal points like this.” Thérèse gestured to the walls, the floor, the very air. “Your century reduced it to sport. Commerce. You lost the grammar. We’re here to recover the original texts.”

Margaret’s head swam. The smell of ozone was stronger now, mixed with the scent of her own sweat. “By drawing on my walls.”

“By inscribing,” Ambrose insisted. “The paint is a conductive medium. The relics in the trucks provide the… call it faith. The images focus the energy, stabilize the temporal aperture.”

Brother Tim finally crept from the office, his sandals whispering on the stone. “Temporal aperture? Like a time door?”

“A window,” Thérèse said. “It’s already narrowing. Fifty-three seconds. The Catherine sequence must be anchored, or the resonance will collapse into dissonance. It could un-write this locus from the kinetic record.”

Margaret looked at the perfect, impossible Catherine on the wall. Looked at her own tattoo. She thought of the thousands of hours she’d spent here, the silent joy of a clean line, the way the world fell away during a perfect carve. It had always felt like a secret prayer. These lunatics were just saying it out loud.

“What happens if it collapses?” she asked.

Ambrose’s serene face finally showed a flicker of something else. Alarm. “Best case? A localized gravity surge. Your beautiful park becomes a very deep hole. Worst case? A chronological recoil. This building, and everyone in it, becomes a theological paradox. A footnote.”

The other skaters were slowing, their patterns tightening. The air in the bowl grew thick, pressurized. The LED lights flickered, then began to pulse in time with the rhythmic thump of the boards. A low hum vibrated up through the soles of Margaret’s sneakers, into her bad knee. The titanium sang.

Tim squeaked. “A footnote?”

“Okay!” Margaret barked. The word tore out of her. “Okay. What’s the anchor? What do I have to do?”

“Take the board,” Ambrose said, thrusting it forward. “The sequence needs a ground point. A still center in the storm. You must hold the line. Just… hold it.”

She let her crutch fall again. This time it was deliberate. Her hands closed on the offered deck. Warm. Not from the sun, but from the inside, like a living thing. The inlaid pattern seemed to shift under her fingers. She felt a jolt, clean and sharp, up her arms. A memory that wasn’t hers flashed behind her eyes: a vast, star-dusted plain, and figures moving across it on lines of light.

“Don’t think,” Thérèse whispered. “Just stand. Be the pivot.”

Margaret put the board on the ground. She placed her good foot on it, over the bolts. She kept her weight on her other leg, the brace straight and awkward. She felt ridiculous. A nun with a busted knee, posing on a magic skateboard in the middle of a heretical light show.

Ambrose and Thérèse exchanged a nod. They pushed off, joining the others. The pattern changed instantly. They began circling her, a tight, fast orbit. The thump-thump-thump became a staccato drumroll. A hum rose in pitch. The air crackled.

The stained-glass windows began to glow from within, not with colored light, but with moving images. She saw St. Francis ollie, again and again. St. Teresa grind in an endless loop. Sebastian wince.

“Hold the line!” Ambrose shouted over the building roar.

She was trying. The board was vibrating under her foot, wanting to move. She clenched her teeth. Her knee screamed. She focused on the stupid, irrelevant detail of the pigeon from earlier. It was still there, on the high window ledge. Now puffed up to twice its size, staring at the chaos with an expression of profound avian disapproval.

You and me both, she thought.

The circling skaters moved faster. They became a blur of brown and white habits. Then, one by one, they launched themselves into the air above her, performing their impossible tricks. A 900 spin. A backside noseblunt slide on a surface that was pure air. A varial flip that seemed to hang for minutes. With each trick, a new spray-painted saint bloomed on the walls, the ceiling, the floor. St. Michael doing a stalefish grab. Clare holding a skateboard like a monstrance. The air filled with the scent of ozone, lavender, and the distinct, sharp smell of panic sweat from Brother Tim.

A light was blinding. The sound was a physical pressure. Margaret’s vision tunneled. The only thing clear was the board under her foot, and the bone-deep certainty that if she moved, if she so much as twitched, it would all come crashing down.

Then Ambrose, at the peak of a launch so high his head nearly brushed the forty-foot ceiling, shouted a single word. It wasn’t in any language she knew. It sounded like the crack of a board snapping, and the ringing of a bell.

Everything stopped.

The skaters landed, perfectly, in a circle around her. The lights stabilized. The humming faded to a faint, pleasant buzz, like a refrigerator in a distant room. The new murals were dry. The air smelled clean, like after a thunderstorm.

Margaret let out a breath she hadn’t known she was holding. She immediately hated herself for the cliché. Her leg buckled. She would have fallen, but Thérèse was suddenly there, a steadying hand under her elbow.

“The anchor held,” Thérèse said. She sounded impressed.

“The locus is secure,” Ambrose announced, brushing dust from his habit. “The Catherine sequence is now part of the permanent record. This timeline’s kinetic sanctity is… banked.”

The other skaters were nodding, smiling at each other with the quiet satisfaction of a job well done. One of them, a younger man with a scalp tattoo of interlocking gears, pulled an orange from a pocket and began to peel it. The ordinary sound was deafening.

Margaret shook off Thérèse’s hand. She hopped on her good leg, retrieved her crutch, and stabbed it into the ground for emphasis. “Banked. Okay. So you’re done? No more unauthorized murals? No more midnight mass with a half-pipe?”

Ambrose looked at her, then at his crew. “The primary mission is complete. The aperture is closed. We have… several hours before the next viable confluence opens to return.”

“Several hours.” Margaret’s voice was flat.

“We are, effectively, stranded,” Thérèse confirmed. She didn’t seem bothered.

The gear-headed skater offered Margaret a section of his orange. She stared at it.

Brother Tim finally shuffled over. “So… you’re just gonna hang out?”

Ambrose surveyed the park. His gaze lingered on the quarter-pipe construction in the cloister. “Your transitions are inefficient. The radius here is too tight for a proper carve prayer.” He pointed to the main bowl. “And the coping on the deep end is inconsistent. It chatters the soul.”

A hot, familiar indignation burned through Margaret’s fatigue. “I designed that coping. It’s perfect for street-style tech.”

“It’s hesitant,” said the gear-headed skater around a mouthful of orange. “It apologizes.”

“It does not apologize!”

“It whispers ‘excuse me’ before it grinds,” Thérèse said, a faint smile touching her lips.

Margaret’s face flushed. She’d spent two years, countless grant proposals, and a significant portion of her personal savings on this park. She’d fought the diocese, the historical preservation board, and three separate neighbors who claimed the sound of wheels was “demonic clicking.” She hobbled forward, jabbing a finger at Ambrose’s chest.

“You want to critique? Fine. Put your relics where your mouth is. Show me.”

Ambrose’s pale eyebrows rose. “A demonstration?”

“A skate-off,” Margaret said. The words were out before her common sense could catch up. “Your future-flow prayer-grammar whatever, versus my grounded, historical, meniscus-tearing reality. Best line wins.”

Silence. The future skaters looked at each other. The gear-head finished his orange.

“What are the terms?” Ambrose asked.

“If I win, you spend your stranded hours fixing what you ‘improved.’ You repaint the St. Teresa mural to something less… mid-grind. And you leave the chronology. Quietly.”

“And if we win?”

Margaret spread her hands, encompassing the convent, the park, the still-judgmental pigeon. “You get to inscribe one more mural. Anywhere you want. And I’ll… I’ll acknowledge the grammatical superiority of your coping.”

Thérèse laughed. It was a light, unexpected sound. “She’s bargaining with the sacred. I like her.”

Ambrose nodded slowly. “A kinetic debate. Very well. The format?”

Margaret’s mind raced. She couldn’t do flips. She couldn’t grind. She could barely walk. But she knew this park. She knew every chip in the concrete, every subtle warble in the stone floor where the old foundation had settled. She thought of the straight man, the reader’s proxy. She was the pivot. The anchor.

“A line,” she said. “One continuous line through the entire complex. Cloister, main hall, bowl, chapel, back. No stopping. Most… devout line wins. Judged by Brother Tim.”

Tim made a sound like a stepped-on mouse. “Me?”

“You’re the only neutral party,” Margaret said. “And you’re scared of everyone here. That’s as neutral as it gets.”

The future saints conferred in low, musical tones. After a moment, Ambrose turned. “We accept. Thérèse will skate for us.”

Thérèse gave a slight bow.

Margaret’s stomach dropped. Thérèse moved like water. This was suicide.

But she’d started it. She crutched over to the rental rack by the office and selected a board. Cheap maple, with worn grip tape and soft, slow bearings. She rolled it back. The contrast with their relic-embedded works of art was pathetic. Perfect.

“Ready when you are,” she said.

Thérèse went first. Her line was a thing of beauty. She started in the cloister, weaving through the construction materials, her board tracing curves that seemed to calculate the golden ratio. She popped onto the fountain’s rim, grinded its circumference, and dropped into a silent roll through the arched walkway. In the main hall, she used the pillars as slalom gates, her white habit flowing behind her. She entered the bowl not from the stairs, but by ollieing from the floor directly onto the vert wall, riding it like a wave. Her tricks were minimal—a shove-it here, a small kickflip there—but every movement was fluid, intentional, connected. She ended at the chapel entrance, stopping with her board’s nose pointing at the old altar. She hadn’t broken a sweat.

It was, Margaret had to admit, a prayer. A really, really good prayer.

Brother Tim’s jaw was on his chest. “That was… I mean… the fountain grind…”

“Your turn, Custodian,” Ambrose said. There was no malice in it. Just curiosity.

Margaret took a deep breath. The park was silent again. All eyes were on her. Her knee throbbed a steady drumbeat of no. She looked at her cheap rental board. She looked at the long, daunting path ahead. She thought of her grandfather’s judgmental pigeons, and the crack in the cloister ceiling shaped like a question mark, and the way the morning light hit the St. Catherine mural just so.

She didn’t do a run-up. She just placed the board down, put her good foot on it, and pushed off with her crutch.

“Order me a new deck. And some better bearings. I think I’ve got a new line to learn.”
