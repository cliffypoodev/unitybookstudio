Casey’s compass pointed to regret. It always had. Right now, the needle quivered, stuck fast on the single word etched into its brass face, ignoring the tracks that unspooled into a perpetual dusk outside his cab window. The smell of coal and something older, something like a library of unsigned apologies, filled the engine car.

He kicked the firebox door shut with a practiced, weary swing of his boot. The train, the Endless Haul, thrummed beneath him, a vibration that traveled up through the soles of his feet and into the fillings of his teeth. It was a sound he knew better than his own heartbeat—the low, grinding groan of steel on steel, the hiss of steam, the rhythmic clack-clack-clack that wasn’t a rhythm at all but a chaotic, living pulse. Through that pulse, if he listened just behind it, he heard things. Whispers. Fragments. The old man in Car Three still dreamed of the dog he’d left on a porch in ’72. A woman in Car Seven hummed a lullaby for a child she’d never named.

Casey preferred the secrets of strangers. His own were bad enough company.

The new cargo sat behind him, a lone crate lashed to the wall of the tender with thick hemp rope. Unremarkable—pine slats, iron bands, no bigger than a steamer trunk. Its label was the problem.

CONTAINMENT VESSEL – HUMANITY’S FINAL ACCOUNTING
CONTENTS: REDEMPTION / DOOM (NON-MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE)
HANDLE WITH ABSOLUTE EXISTENTIAL CARE.

It had been loaded at the last stop, a waystation that was just a platform and a single flickering gas lamp in an ocean of grey wheat. The stationmaster, a man with a face like a wrinkled apple left in the sun hadn’t spoken. He’d just pointed a bony finger at the crate, then at Casey, then back at the crate. His meaning was clear: your problem now.

Casey poured the dregs of his coffee, thick as tar, into a tin mug. The liquid caught the endless twilight, reflecting a sky that couldn’t decide between day and night. “Handle with care,” he muttered to the steam. “Where’s the label for that?”

The train gave a lurch. Not a normal sway. This was a deeper, raw shudder, as if the tracks themselves had flinched.

The compass needle spun wildly for a single second before snapping back to REGRET.

“Wonderful.”

A new whisper threaded up through the rails. It wasn’t a passenger’s secret this time. Colder, drier, a sound like pages turning in a ledger.

Casey’s hand clenched on the throttle. He didn’t need the rails to remind him. He remembered the smell of crushed incense and splintered pews, the way the stained-glass saints had exploded into a million colored shards, raining down like hard candy. He’d walked away from that. The railway company, in its infinite and dubious mercy had given him this route. The infinite route. A moving prison sentence.

He turned from the window, his back to the constant grey landscape. The crate sat there. Quiet. Innocuous. Mocking him.

“Redemption or doom,” he said. The words hung in the oily air. “Couldn’t just be spoons. Or mail.”

He took a step toward it. The floorboards creaked a complaint. The crate did nothing. He circled it, a mechanic inspecting a faulty valve. The wood was ordinary, slightly warped. The iron bands were cool to the touch. He crouched, peering at the joinery. No seams glowed. No hum emanated from within. It was just a box.

The whisper from the rails came again, clearer. …the sound of the whistle you didn’t blow…

“Stop it,” Casey growled, not sure if he was talking to the train, the crate, or his own memory.

He stood, knees popping, and reached for his oil can. A distraction. He’d oil the linkage rods. A sensible, mechanical task. He turned back to the engine, to the great, wheezing heart of the machine he understood. As he did, his hip brushed against the corner of the crate.

It was a nothing touch. A bump.

From inside the crate came a sound.

Faint, muffled by wood and straw packing, but obvious. A long, low, mournful whistle. Not a train whistle. A human whistle. The kind a man makes when he’s let all the air out of his lungs after a profound disappointment. It was the exact sigh Casey had made when he’d stepped from the wreckage of the 4:15 to Nowhere and seen what he’d done to the Chapel of the Everlasting Hope.

His own sigh. Bottled in a box.

Casey froze, the oil can dripping a black tear onto his boot. The sound faded, leaving a silence that felt thicker than before. The clack-clack of the tracks seemed to recede, replaced by a ringing in his ears.

This was new. The railway dealt in oddities—a car of perpetual snowfall, a passenger who paid in teeth, a dining car that served only foods you’d forgotten—but this was personal. This was pointed.

He stared at the crate. “Alright,” he said, his voice quieter. “What’s the game?”

The crate offered no answer. The label stared back, bureaucratic and cruel.

A theory, absurd and unwelcome began to form in the back of his mind. It was the kind of thought that started small, like a single drop of water in a dry basin, but you knew with a sick certainty the basin would eventually fill. It didn’t punish you. It just gave you exactly what you deserved, wrapped in irony and tied with a bow of consequence.

What if the cargo wasn’t for a destination?

He took a step back. The compass on the console gave a violent rattle against its glass. The needle strained, bent, for a moment pointing directly at the crate, before springing back to its true north: REGRET.

“No,” Casey said, to the compass, to the crate, to the whole stupid, endless system. “You don’t get to do that. My job is to drive the train. To the last station. That’s the deal.”

The train whistle blew then, the real one, a blast of steam that shredded the quiet. Time for the Viaduct Crossing. A routine marker. Casey moved to the controls on muscle memory, his hands pulling levers, his eyes checking gauges he couldn’t read. All he could see was the crate in the reflection of the glass.

The viaduct was a slender thread of iron over a canyon so deep the bottom was lost in purple shadow. The Endless Haul crept onto it, the sound of its wheels changing to a higher, tense ping against the bridge rails. Halfway across, the train shuddered again. A different shudder. A hesitant one.

The whisper from the tracks wasn’t a whisper this time. It was a clear, cold sentence spoken in a voice that was both his and not his.

You never checked the cargo manifest.

Casey’s blood went still. The manifest. The thick, leather-bound ledger in the conductor’s booth. He never checked it. The stationmasters loaded and unloaded. His job was the engine. The machinery. That was the division. That was the rule.

The train inched forward. The far side of the viaduct seemed to slide farther away.

He left the throttle locked, a reckless act, and stumbled back through the tender, past the mountain of coal, to the small, windowless booth. The manifest lay on the tiny desk, its cover damp with condensation. He flipped it open. The pages were dense with spidery script, listing barrels of pickles, crates of clockwork, bundles of unread newspapers. He thumbed to the most recent entry made at the last stop.

Item #4479. Consignor: The Stationmaster of Regret. Consignee: The Conductor. Description: One (1) soul, used, moderate wear. Pending final audit. Special Instructions: Must not arrive before the conductor understands the terms of delivery.

The words swam on the page. Casey’s breath fogged the air in front of his face. The temperature in the booth was dropping. He heard the faint whistle again, from the crate just outside the thin wall. It was the sound he’d made when they’d handed him his new schedule, the one with no terminus. That sound he made every morning when he saw the same dusk out the same window.

He was transporting his own soul. For judgment. And the railway, in its infinite, twisted wisdom had decided he had to deliver it to himself.

The train gave a sudden, violent lurch.

A coupling screamed. A world tilted.

Casey was thrown against the desk, the manifest fluttering to the floor. Through the open booth door, he saw the crate’s ropes strain. One snapped with a sound like a gunshot. The crate slid six inches across the tender floor, teetering toward the edge, toward the mile-long drop to the canyon floor.

His soul. In a box. About to be dashed on the rocks.

He didn’t think. He moved.

He lunged from the booth, his boots slipping on coal dust. The train swayed on the viaduct, a pendulum of steel and bad ideas. He grabbed for a handhold, missed, and fell forward, his arms wrapping around the rough pine of the crate just as it tipped over the lip of the tender.

His weight arrested its fall. He hung there, half over the edge, the abyss yawning beneath him, the crate heavy in his arms. The cold of the iron bands seeped through his jacket. Inside, the thing—his soul—let out another sound. Not a whistle this time. A low, resonant thrum, like a tuning fork struck against a rib.

It felt familiar. It felt like the vibration in his chest when the train was running smooth and true on a straight stretch of track, that fleeting, impossible moment of peace.

He clung to it. To the box. To himself.

With a grunt that started in his toes, he hauled the crate back from the edge, collapsing with it onto the gritty floor. He lay there, gasping, the coal-smell and the promise-smell filling his nose, the clack-clack-clack of the wheels a frantic heartbeat.

The train eased off the viaduct onto solid ground. The crisis passed.

Casey sat up, his back against the wretched box. He looked at his hands, black with grime, shaking. He’d saved it. He’d saved himself. Or he’d postponed the drop. Hard to know which.

The compass, visible through the cabin door had gone mad. The needle spun in frantic, dizzying circles, unable to settle on regret, or hope, or fear, or anything at all.

From the crate, pressed against his spine, a new sensation bloomed. Not a sound. A warmth. A faint, gentle heat, like a stone left in the sun.

The crate was silent now. The warmth against his back felt almost like forgiveness. Or maybe it was just the latent heat of deep, ridiculous embarrassment.

He started to laugh. A dry, hacking sound that hurt his throat. It wasn’t a happy laugh. It was the laugh of a man who’d just realized the universe’s punchline, and found he was the butt of it.

The train whistle blew again, long and loud, eating the sound.

The laugh died in Casey’s throat, replaced by the metallic taste of panic. The warmth from the crate was a trick. It had to be. The railway didn’t do forgiveness. It did paperwork.

He scrambled to his feet, putting a foot of coal-dusted distance between himself and the box. His soul. In a crate. The absurdity of it hit him in waves, each one funnier and more terrible than the last. He’d spent years carefully not thinking about his soul, the way a man avoids a creaky floorboard in a familiar hallway. Now it was here, a physical object with a shipping label. Had they weighed it? Was there a form for damage incurred during transit?

The train, as if sensing his existential spiral gave a sudden, enthusiastic surge of speed. The clack-clack became a frantic clatter-clatter. The field outside the window blurred into a grey-brown smear. Casey stumbled back to the engine cab, grabbing the brake lever more out of instinct than intent. He pulled.

Nothing happened.

He pulled harder. The lever moved with a sick, greasy smoothness all the way to its housing. No resistance. No answering hiss of compressed air. The train wasn’t just moving. It was accelerating, chasing its own tail light down the infinite track.

“Oh, come on,” Casey muttered. He slapped the pressure gauge. The needle was buried in the red, trembling at a number that would mean a boiler explosion if this were a normal train on a normal line. This was not that. The boiler would probably just sigh in heavy disappointment.

A new sound joined the roar of the wheels—a low, resonant vibration coming from the tender. He turned. The crate was humming. Not a tune. A single, sustained note, the auditory equivalent of a raised eyebrow.

Fine, Casey thought. You want an audit? Let’s audit.

He marched back to the crate, his fear momentarily burned away by a surge of pure, professional irritation. He was a conductor. A railroad man. He handled cargo. So he’d handle this.

He crouched, examining the knots on the remaining ropes. Good hemp, nautical grade. The railway spared no expense on irony. He dug his fingernails into the first knot, a stubborn tangle that had tightened with the viaduct incident. He picked at it. He pulled. He swore at it, using a word that would have cost him a week’s pay in his old life.

The crate’s hum shifted pitch, becoming slightly… amused.

After a minute of futile struggle, Casey stood, kicked the crate lightly, and went to find a knife. His toolbox yielded a rusty shim, which snapped. A coal pick was too unwieldy. Finally, in the back of a greasy drawer, his fingers closed around the handle of his official railway-issue butter knife, its edge rounded from years of scraping carbon off toast. Perfect.

He set to work on the rope sawing with grim determination. The butter knife was worse than useless. It frayed the outer fibers but couldn’t cut the core. He sawed harder. The vibration from the crate intensified, syncopating with his frantic movements. Scritch-scritch-hummm.

He was sweating. A drop landed on the crate’s label, smudging the word “REDEMPTION.”

“See?” he panted, talking to the box. “Effort. I’m making an effort. That’s got to count for something in the ledger, right? A good-faith attempt at unpacking?”

The train shot into a tunnel.

The world went black. Not the soft dark of night, but an absolute, velvet nothing that swallowed the engine’s light whole. The sound condensed, the roar of their passage becoming a deafening, enclosed thunder. The air turned cold and damp, smelling of wet stone and forgotten corners.

Casey dropped the butter knife. It clattered on the floor, the sound tiny and ridiculous against the tunnel’s roar. He fumbled for the cab’s lantern, his hands shaking. He lit it. The feeble yellow flame pushed back the darkness just enough to show the walls of the tunnel streaming past—smooth, featureless stone that went on and on. There were no lights, no markers. Just the endless throat of the mountain.

And the crate was glowing.

A soft, pearlescent light seeped from between the pine slats, illuminating the tender in a sickly, moonlit glow. The label now read, in letters that seemed to pulse: IN-TRANSIT AUDIT IN PROGRESS.

“Audit of what?” Casey yelled over the noise. He lunged for the crate, not to open it, but to shake it. His hands gripped the iron bands. “What’s the criteria? Is there a form? A multiple-choice? Do I get a representative?”

He stared at it. This was worse than judgment. This was itemization.

The train’s whistle blew, a shrill, desperate scream that echoed in the tunnel. Its sound seemed to startle the crate. The glow flickered. The vibration returned, stronger now, a buzzing that climbed from the floor into Casey’s teeth. The crate began to shimmy sideways across the tender floor, inching toward the edge again.

“No, no, no!” Casey threw himself on top of it, wrapping his limbs around the box like a man hugging a temperamental bomb. The glow illuminated his face, his wide, desperate eyes. “Stop it! You can’t jump. You’re cargo! I’m responsible for you!”

The crate bucked under him, trying to throw him off. His own soul, it seemed had a self-destructive streak. The irony was so thick he could choke on it.

He made a decision. It was a stupid decision, the kind that only makes sense when you’re lying on top of your soul in a pitch-black tunnel. If he couldn’t control the train, and he couldn’t reason with the cargo, he’d have to motivate it.

He pushed himself up, still straddling the box. He pointed a finger at the glowing wood. “You listen to me,” he shouted, his voice raw. “I am the conductor of this train. You are consigned to me. That means you’re mine. And I am not delivering a damaged good! You got that? If you go over the edge, the paperwork will be endless. They’ll make me fill out a loss-in-transit form. In triplicate. With a handwritten explanation of the existential circumstances. Do you want that for me? Do you?”

The crate went very still. The glow dimmed to a sullen ember.

Considering the paperwork.

Encouraged, Casey pressed his advantage. “That’s right. Think about the forms. The carbon copies. The notary stamps. Now,” he said, patting the wood as if soothing a spooked horse. “We’re going to work together. You stop trying to leap into oblivion, and I’ll… I’ll try to figure out what you want. A little quiet reflection. Some light reading. What’s your preference?”

A hysterical giggle bubbled up in his chest. His soul was a smart-ass.

He knocked back: the two bits. Tap-tap-tap-tap-tap.

The crate’s glow warmed a degree. The vibration softened into something almost purring. The train, as if responding to this fragile détente began to slow. The deafening roar of the tunnel lessened. A pinprick of light appeared far ahead.

Casey slid off the crate, exhausted. He sat with his back against it, the two of them facing the growing light together. The warmth through his jacket was back. It felt less like forgiveness now, more like a grudging truce. He’d bargained with his own soul using the threat of bureaucratic hell. It was the most railroad solution imaginable.

The train emerged from the tunnel into the perpetual dusk. But the terrain had changed. The grey wheat was gone. Now, a flat, featureless plain stretched to a horizon that was a straight, unwavering line. The sky was the same color as the ground, a muted taupe. It was like traveling through a blank page.

The crate gave a final, soft thrum and fell silent. The glow faded completely.

Casey stood, his joints aching. He walked to the front of the cab, looking out at the nothingness. The train was coasting now, slowing to a crawl. There were no tracks visible ahead. Just the plain.

He knew, with a certainty that settled in his stomach like a cold stone, that this was it. Not a station. The end of the line.

The Endless Haul gave one last, exhausted sigh of steam and shuddered to a complete stop. This silence was absolute. No wind. No insects. No distant sounds of anything at all. It was the quiet of a held breath.

Casey stepped down from the engine, his boots landing on hard, packed earth. The sound was obscenely loud. He turned, looking back at his train. It sat there, immense and silent, a fossilized metal worm on an empty slate.

Then he saw the figure.

A hundred yards ahead, standing alone in the expanse was a man. He wore a conductor’s uniform, identical to Casey’s, though it looked crisper, less stained with coal and regret. As Casey watched, the man turned.

Him. His own face, but smoother, the lines of weariness less deep, the eyes clearer and utterly, terrifyingly empty of recognition. This other Casey held a crate. An empty crate, its lid open, its interior a dark void.

The reflection didn’t speak. It just stood there, waiting, holding the empty container with the patient expectation of a bellhop.

Casey looked back at his train, at the tender where his own crate, his humming, tapping, obstinate soul sat waiting. He understood. This was the delivery. The consignee meets the consignor. He had to take his soul from its crate and place it into the one his reflection held. An internal transfer. The final audit was self-administered.

He started to laugh again. It was a quiet, broken sound that didn’t carry in the vast silence. Of course. You had to package yourself up. You had to hand yourself over. The railway just provided the box.

He climbed back into the tender. He stood before his crate. He put his hands on the wood, now just ordinary, warm pine.

“Okay,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “Last stop.”

He didn’t reach for the ropes. He didn’t need the butter knife. He simply leaned his forehead against the slats and closed his eyes.

“I’m sorry,” he said, not to the crate, but to the memory of colored glass shattering on stone, to the dog on the porch, to the lullaby for no one. “I’m sorry I didn’t look at the manifest sooner.”

Inside the crate, something settled. A quiet, final click, like a key turning in a well-oiled lock.

The train, the Endless Haul gave a soft metallic creak behind him. When Casey opened his eyes and turned, his reflection was gone. The empty plain remained. But the train’s whistle, the real one gave a single, short, questioning toot.

Time to go. The route, it seemed wasn’t endless after all. It just had a different destination than he’d thought.

He walked back to the engine, not looking at the empty space where the reflection had been. He climbed into the cab. He placed a hand on the throttle. He didn’t know where the tracks would appear, or what the next station would be. He only knew the cargo was lighter.

With a gentle push, he opened the throttle. The train began to move, carving a new line into the blank earth behind it.

In the tender, the empty crate sat where he’d left it. A single, glowing sliver of wood, the one with the bureaucratic footnote, rested inside. It pulsed once, softly, and then went dark, just as the train disappeared into the endless, waiting dusk.
