Felix had never seen a kitchen where the grease fires were sentient. He adjusted the wax plugs in his ears—custom molded from melted-down cymbals—and watched a two-foot-tall imp in a scorched chef’s toque flip a pancake directly into the flames. The pancake screamed.

His left eyebrow twitched upward on reflex. The imp froze mid-flip. The entire line of fry cooks stiffened like a brass section missing their cue. Felix didn’t notice. He was too busy counting the number of limbs on the dishwashing staff.

A gargoyle with dishpan hands lumbered past, trailing suds and the distinct scent of burnt rosemary. Felix tapped it on the elbow—elbow?—and mimed scrubbing a plate. The gargoyle’s nostrils flared. It dropped to one knee with a sound like a timpani collapsing.

“No, no, up—” Felix fluttered his fingers upward. Sixteen dishwashers threw their sponges into the air with military precision. One hit the ceiling fan and came down as confetti.

The gargoyle pressed a clawed hand to its chest. “Thy will be done, O Prince.”

Felix blinked. The words were just shapes moving behind the creature’s tusks, but he caught the last one. Prince? He pointed to himself.

The dishwashers nodded as one.

A clatter of hooves behind him. Felix turned as a goat-headed sous chef slid into the station, balancing a tray of sausages that wriggled like they were trying to escape the skewer. The goat demon’s eyes locked onto Felix’s face.

Felix sneezed.

The demon recoiled as if struck. One cloven hoof slipped in a puddle of something that was definitely not marinara.

Felix raised both eyebrows in alarm.

Every demon in the kitchen snapped to attention. The sausages froze midair. The grease fires held their breath.

Then the goat demon misread his panic as a command and breathed a stream of hellfire across the tray. The sausages ignited like napalm.

Felix lunged for the fire extinguisher. Shaped like a medieval mace and hissed at him when he grabbed it.

The kitchen doors burst open. A seven-foot-tall demon with arms like a spider’s legs and a cleaver the size of a cello case strode in. A room temperature dropped twenty degrees.

Felix recognized the posture—this was a bandleader. The way the creature held its cleaver, the tilt of its horns, the way the other demons shrank back. He’d seen it a hundred times in the pit at Carnegie Hall.

Barbatos, according to the stitching on its apron. The head chef.

The demon surveyed the flaming sausages, the kneeling dishwashers, the imp now attempting to juggle three steak knives while sobbing. It turned its furnace-red eyes on Felix.

Felix wiped his hands on his apron and gave a little jazz-hands wave.

Barbatos produced a scrap of parchment from its sleeve and thrust it at Felix’s chest. The handwriting looped and sputtered like a dying candle:

By order of the Infernal Musicians’ Local 666, you have been designated strike mediator. Do not fuck this up.

Felix stared at the note. Then at the cleaver. Then at the sausages, which had begun singing a mournful tune in Yiddish.

His right eyebrow arched.

The entire kitchen held its breath.

Felix pointed at the note, then at himself, then made the universal sign for What the hell?

Barbatos sighed—a sound like a steam whistle giving up—and handed him the cleaver.

The dishwashers cheered. The goat demon fainted.

Felix looked down at the cleaver. Looked up at the expectant faces of two dozen demons. Looked back at the cleaver.

Somewhere, a grease fire giggled.

He raised the blade—The kitchen staff inhaled—and brought it down on a block of suspiciously groaning cheese.

The demons erupted in applause. Felix wiped his brow.

The cheese let out a relieved moan.

By midnight, Felix had learned three things:

1. The dishwashers were all former members of a cursed mariachi band. 2. The “sausages” had union grievances. 3. He was, apparently, the only one who didn’t know demons had been on strike since the invention of the food processor.

Barbatos loomed over him as Felix attempted to interpret a contract written in what looked like burnt cork and chicken blood. The head chef tapped one claw against the cleaver’s edge.

Felix rubbed his temples. He missed his old band. At least when the trombonist had set the stage on fire, it was just regular fire.

Outside the kitchen, the nightclub’s patrons were getting restless. Something heavy smashed against the wall. A chorus of muffled shouts. Then the distinct sound of a piano being pushed down a flight of stairs.

Barbatos didn’t flinch. Just handed Felix a pen.

It bit him.

Felix sucked on his finger and scanned the contract again. Clause 14-B caught his eye: Management agrees to cease all transmutation of damned souls into appetizers.

His eyebrows shot up.

The dishwashers gasped. The goat demon, now revived, clutched its chest.

Felix pointed at the clause, then at the still-smoldering sausages.

Barbatos looked away.

Felix stood—well, wobbled—and grabbed the nearest skillet. He banged it against the grill like a gong. Every demon in the room snapped to attention.

He pointed to the contract. Pointed to the sausages. Pointed to the door where the nightclub’s owner—some Wall Street ghoul in a pinstripe suit—was currently screaming into a candlestick phone.

Then Felix did what any good conductor would do.

He cued the revolution.

The dishwashers advanced on the owner with a wall of suds and very sharp serving forks.

The goat demon lit the reserve napkins.

Felix gave a little flourish—and the entire kitchen charged into the dining room, howling a tune that sounded suspiciously like Solidarity Forever.

Barbatos watched them go. Then it reached into its apron and pulled out a tiny pair of sunglasses.

The head chef put them on. Nodded.

Somewhere, a grease fire sighed like a groupie.

Felix was already at the piano, smashing out chords with one hand and conducting the riot with the other. His ears rang—not with damage, for once, but with the glorious, deafening sound of demons finally in key.

The sausages sang backup.

The negotiation table was a repurposed butcher block still sticky with what Felix hoped was barbecue sauce. Barbatos loomed at one end, cleaver planted in the wood like a ceremonial sword. At the other end, the nightclub owner—a skeletal thing in a three-piece suit that smelled like old stock certificates—tapped a fountain pen against a contract written in what looked like dried tears.

Felix wiped his hands on his apron. The pen had bitten him three times already.

“Let's recap,” he said, pointing to the sausages writhing on a serving platter. “You want to stop turning souls into hors d'oeuvres.”

The dishwashers nodded so vigorously their sponges flew off.

The owner adjusted its cufflinks. “And you want dental.”

The goat demon slammed its hooves on the table. “Vision!”

Felix rubbed his temples. A grease fire nearby coughed politely. He turned to Barbatos. “What's your ask?”

The head chef produced a scroll from its sleeve. Unrolled it. The parchment kept going, unfurling across the grill, over a stack of pans, and into the dish pit. Felix caught phrases like “mandatory smoke breaks” and “no more exorcisms during prep.”

He blinked. “You want a… grievance committee?”

Barbatos nodded. The cleaver wobbled.

The owner scoffed. “Absurd. Next you'll want overtime for damnation.”

The goat demon gasped. “We can get overtime?”

Felix's left eyebrow twitched. The dishwashers immediately formed a picket line, waving serving forks like protest signs. One of them had scrawled UNFAIR on a napkin in ketchup.

The owner's jaw unhinged. “You can't unionize damnation! It's in the bylaws!”

The demons took it as a declaration of war.

A meat tenderizer flew past his ear. The goat demon lit a stack of invoices on fire. Barbatos unsheathed a second cleaver from its apron.

Felix grabbed a ladle and banged it against a stockpot. “Encore!”

The kitchen froze.

He pointed to the owner. “Counteroffer.”

One creature's eye sockets narrowed. It reached into its breast pocket and produced a single bullet. “This is my final offer.”

Felix stared at the bullet. Stared at the owner. Stared at the bullet again.

The goat demon whispered, “Is that a metaphor?”

Barbatos snatched the bullet and popped it into its mouth like a breath mint. Crunched. Swallowed. Then it grabbed the owner by the lapels and hissed something in Enochian.

The owner went pale.

Felix didn't know Enochian, but he knew intimidation when he saw it. He'd played enough jazz clubs to recognize a shakedown.

The owner straightened its tie. “Fine. No more soul-based appetizers. But we're cutting the 401(k) match.”

The dishwashers erupted in cheers. The goat demon hugged a sausage.

Felix frowned. “What's the catch?”

The owner smiled—a slow, terrible thing. “You're now Hell's permanent labor liaison.”

Felix's stomach dropped. “I—what?”

Barbatos clapped him on the back hard enough to dislodge a filling. The demons cheered. Someone handed him a contract and a flaming shot of something that smelled like regret.

He looked down at the paperwork. The signature line read Prince of Picket Lines.

The pen bit him again.

By closing time, Felix had negotiated exactly one concession: a lifetime supply of slightly-burnt sausages. They came with a side of existential dread, but hey—free food.

He slumped against the piano, watching the demons celebrate. The dishwashers had formed a conga line. The goat demon was teaching Barbatos the macarena.

A grease fire sidled up to him. “You're pretty good at this,” it crackled.

Felix sighed. “I'm a jazz pianist.”

That fire nodded sagely. “Same thing.”

Outside, the first shift of damned souls clocked in. Felix watched them shuffle past the kitchen, their eyes hollow, their aprons stained with eternity.

One of them caught his gaze and mouthed help.

Felix's right eyebrow arched.

The dishwashers stopped dancing.

Barbatos adjusted its sunglasses.

The goat demon cracked its knuckles.

Felix took a deep breath. Cued the revolution.

The sausages cheered.

The sausages cheered. Its sound was a wet, greasy slap of noise that made the air in the kitchen feel thick. Grease popped on the griddle. Lena watched it, her knuckles white around the spatula handle.

Her phone buzzed on the counter. A text from her brother, Mark. You coming tonight?

She didn't answer. She flipped a sausage. It landed with a hiss.

“Mom?”

“I did.”

“Then look again.”

Chloe didn't move. Her eyes were on Lena's hands. “You're squeezing that really hard.”

Chloe vanished. The sausages continued to sizzle. Lena picked up her phone. She typed Maybe, then deleted it. She typed I don't think so, then deleted that too. One screen went dark.

Mark would be at the bar by now. The same bar where their father had spent every Friday for twenty years. A tradition of absence. Mark called it honoring his memory. Lena called it something else, but she never said it out loud.

Lena forked the sausages onto a paper towel. The grease bloomed in dark circles. She put two on Chloe's plate.

Lena scraped the griddle clean, the metal edge shrieking against the steel. She washed it, dried it, put it away. The routine of it was a cage. She checked her phone again. No new messages. That clock on the stove read 7:42.

The school bus was a yellow smear at the end of the street. Lena stood on the porch, coffee going cold in her hand. A morning was gray, the sky the color of old dishwater.

Her neighbor, Mr. Henderson was out getting his newspaper. He raised a hand. Lena nodded back, then went inside before he could start a conversation about the weather.

The house was too quiet. She poured the coffee down the sink. She should call work, tell them she'd be late. Had sick days. She had personal days. She had a stack of days she'd never used.

Her phone buzzed. Mark again. Just for one drink. An hour.

One bar was called the Anchor. It smelled of pine cleaner and stale beer, even at three in the afternoon. Lena pushed the door open, the dim light swallowing her.

The hardware store was a cathedral of possibility. Aisles of pipes and fittings washers and O-rings. Lena stood in front of a display of faucet repair kits, holding the old, corroded cartridge she'd wrestled from the sink.

At home, she spread a towel under the sink and got to work. It was a fight. The old fittings were calcified, stubborn. She strained, her shoulder protesting. With a final, gritty twist, the old cartridge came free. Water dribbled, then stopped.

A silence was complete. No plink in the background. She hadn't realized how much she'd been listening for it.
